# We have built-up resentment / We're not entirely happy with each other anymore

## What Someone Says

The presenting story arrives as a narrative about time. The person describes a beginning that was good and a present that has slowly turned. They cannot point to the rupture; there was no rupture. The relationship just gradually became what it is.

- "We got married and it was really great, we were happy."
- "Over time there's this kind of built-up resentment."
- "We're walking on eggshells around each other now."
- "We're not entirely happy with one another."
- "It's been a slow burn for a couple of years."
- "I don't want to go home."
- "We used to talk about everything and now we don't."
- "I don't know when it changed."
- "We have this thing we can't talk about and now everything is around it."

Joe specifically names this as a recurring story. The players change but the shape stays the same. He hears it about marriages, between business co-founders, inside long-running jobs. The setting varies; the underlying dynamic is the same.

"It's basically, hey, we got married, it was really great, we were happy. And over time there's like this kind of built-up resentment. There's this like walking on eggshells. There is this like we're not entirely happy with one another, that whole thing. And sometimes I hear that story in marriages, sometimes I hear this story between founders, sometimes I hear the story inside of a job." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## Detection Cues

- The story is told in the language of time and gradual change rather than specific events
- The word "resentment" or its softer synonyms ("frustrated," "tired of," "fed up with") appears, especially as a chronic state rather than a recent feeling
- The person describes "walking on eggshells" or similar containment language
- The person can name that the beginning was good but cannot name what specifically changed
- The relationship has a workaround structure -- topics that cannot be discussed, behaviors that have to be managed around
- The person frames the issue as "we have grown apart" or "we have just become different people"
- A sense of low-grade dread about being in the relationship that the person cannot make specific
- The dynamic has spread to adjacent domains -- the same low-grade pissedness about work, about family, about life generally

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this presentation is almost always [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing|conflict avoidance]]. The keywords -- built-up resentment, walking on eggshells, slow burn -- are the signature of a relationship that has accumulated unaddressed tension for years.

"Usually there's a couple of keywords in this that will point me to the idea that this is all created by conflict avoidance." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The mechanism: two people who started by being themselves with each other slowly learned where the other's triggers were. Each began editing what they said to prevent the triggers. The editing became the relationship. The parts of each person that did not survive the editing went underground and reemerged as resentment. The resentment was never addressable because the editing was never explicit. The thing they would have had to talk about to fix it was the thing they had built the whole relationship around not talking about.

See [[walking-on-eggshells]] for the pattern in full and [[avoided-conflict-goes-internal]] for what happens to the unaddressed material.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same presenting story branches in several directions depending on what is happening underneath.

- If [[walking-on-eggshells]]: both partners have been managing each other's triggers and have built the relationship around the management. Joe will move toward naming this and toward [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] as the first practice.
- If the person is in [[fight-or-roll-over-binary]] about the relationship: the only options on the table are to leave or to keep absorbing the dissatisfaction. Joe will surface the third option that the binary is hiding.
- If [[you-think-they-cant-handle-it]]: the person has decided the partner is too fragile for the conversations that would matter. Joe will check whether this is real or a self-justifying story.
- If the person is using "we have grown apart" as a fixed identity story: Joe will not buy the story. The growing-apart is what conflict avoidance produces; the people themselves did not get incompatible.
- If [[resentment-as-caretaking-diagnostic]] is active: the resentment is the marker of caretaking that has gone on too long. Joe will move toward the caretaking work.
- If the same fight has been repeating for years and both partners are usually focused on what the other did rather than on their own contribution: a [[shame-hot-potato]] is running. Joe will name the dynamic and move toward [[drop-the-hot-potato]] and [[state-no-wish-for-their-shame]] as the entry into the work.

## What This Concern Looks Like in Different Domains

The same dynamic appears across relationship types:

- **In a marriage:** the topics that cannot be raised, the chores that are silently resented, the affection that has gradually thinned.
- **Between co-founders:** the strategic disagreements that have been smoothed over, the role boundaries that were never clearly negotiated, the political maneuvering that fills the space.
- **In a long job:** the things you have stopped raising in meetings, the boss whose mood you manage, the slow-growing sense that the job is heavier than the work justifies.
- **In a friendship:** the friend you start avoiding for reasons you cannot quite name, the chronic small-irritation that has displaced the easy connection.

The pattern recognition is the same in all cases. The conversation that would have addressed the original tension was not had. The cost has accumulated.

## Coaching Path

The path is not to start with "tell me about the resentment." The path is to name the pattern as conflict avoidance and to introduce the question of what the person has not been saying.

1. Name the pattern. Joe is direct: "this is all about conflict avoidance." Naming the pattern shifts the orientation from "we have a bad relationship" to "we have a treatable dynamic."

2. Surface the avoided territory. The diagnostic question: what topics or feelings have you been not bringing up? The list itself is the data. See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]].

3. Acknowledge the wisdom of the pattern. The conflict avoidance developed for a reason, usually a childhood reason. It probably saved the person's life at some point. The work is not to shame the pattern; it is to update it. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]].

4. Show what has been costing the person. Specifically: the love that cannot land because they have been hiding. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]]. The exhaustion of carrying years of unaddressed tension. The aliveness that has gone out of the relationship.

5. Introduce the small-and-frequent practice. The repair is not heroic. One conversation a week, said kindly, about something that has been not-said. See [[weekly-tension-inventory]] and [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]].

6. Address the fear of the aftermath. The person is usually afraid that the conversations will end the relationship. Joe's frame: the relationship as it currently exists is already ending. The conversations are the only thing that might save it.

## The 4-Keys Compressed Path

When the presenting concern is "we have built-up resentment" and the listener needs a complete, actionable structure rather than a single starting point, Joe offers the [[4-keys-to-save-your-relationship]] framework. The four keys map onto the four most common forms the dynamic takes:

1. **The blame autopilot** (key 1: [[turn-blame-into-wants]]). The blame has been running on autopilot for years; converting it back to wants reestablishes the self-ownership that the relationship lost.
2. **The eggshells around difficult conversations** (key 2: [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]]). The conversations that were never had are the structure of the resentment; the practice surfaces them gradually.
3. **The mutual responsibility-for-happiness contract** (key 3: [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]]). The terminal agreement that has been quietly draining both partners.
4. **The compromise as self-amputation** (key 4: [[dont-compromise-find-both-needs-met]] plus [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]]). The slow disappearance of both selves into the negotiated peace.

The 4-keys framework is the public-teaching version. It does not replace the deeper work but provides a structured entry point when the client needs to know what to do this week rather than what to understand about the dynamic.
