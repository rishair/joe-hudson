# How do I love myself?

## What Someone Says

The defining feature is a question about self-love presented alongside a specific behavioral report: the person dismisses their accomplishments, ignores their needs, or treats themselves as not entitled to what they want. The framing is often that loving themselves *more* would look like the opposite of these behaviors.

- "How do I love myself?"
- "Why do I tend to dismiss my accomplishments?"
- "Why do I ignore my needs, or feel I'm not right to have them?"
- "I think one of the ways I'd love myself more is to acknowledge my accomplishments."
- "Another way would be acknowledging that I have needs and not dismissing them."
- "I have a hard time articulating my needs."
- "I want to be seen, recognized — not just accepted but welcomed."
- "I try a lot. It's sort of my [way of being]."
- "I'm not allowed to have it."
- "I'm thinking about how often I'm in a comparative mindset."

The "maybe" is often present. "Acknowledging that I have needs and not dismissing them, *maybe*." The qualifier itself is the pattern firing — the person cannot fully claim the want even in the act of describing it.

## Detection Cues

- The person opens with "how do I love myself?" framed as a question to be answered, often expecting a technique
- They list specific behaviors they think loving themselves *would* look like (acknowledging accomplishments, having needs, being recognized)
- They report dismissing or downplaying themselves, sometimes with the language "I'm not allowed to have it"
- They describe themselves as trying a lot or working hard at self-improvement
- They are intelligent and self-aware — they can describe their own self-criticism in detail
- They reference a "comparative mindset" or comparing themselves to others as part of the problem
- They report being good at this work alone but doing "false humility" in front of others
- They are visibly tender when describing it — eyes wet, voice softer than usual
- They have done this work before — "I wouldn't know anything about that" said sarcastically about beating themselves up

The texture is different from [[how-do-i-love-myself-and-express-my-emotions]]. There, the paired structure is self-love AND emotional expression, with a backstory of being taught emotions were bad. Here, the question is about self-love specifically, framed through the behaviors of self-dismissal. The emotional channel may be open — the person feels things — but the channel of letting themselves *have* things is closed.

## What Joe Hears

The person already knows how to love themselves. They have been in the state of self-love before, often many times. What they call "I don't know how" is more accurately "I know how and something pulls me out of it." The work is not to install a new capacity. It is to make conscious the pattern that pulls them out — and to bring welcoming to that pattern too.

Underneath the question is usually a specific structural truth: the person has learned that being big, spacious, and self-loving is shameful or dangerous. Someone in their past was jealous or threatened by their bigness and shamed them out of it. The system then installed the self-criticism to do the shaming preemptively, before anyone else could. See [[shame-after-spaciousness]].

The "I dismiss my accomplishments" report is the visible surface of this structure. The accomplishments are not being dismissed because the person doesn't notice them. They are being dismissed because notice-and-claim would put the person back into the territory where they got shamed. The dismissal is protective.

The "comparative mindset" report points at the same root from another angle. The comparison is not the problem — it is what the system uses to organize the dismissal. "If I rank myself against others, I have a structure that justifies why I shouldn't claim what I have."

The operational answer is not new behaviors. It is welcoming. Welcoming whatever arises — including the welcoming itself, the trying, the resistance, the shame, the comparative mind, the false humility. The path through is not getting rid of these. It is welcoming each one as it shows up. See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] for the live form.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people can present this concern and Joe goes different directions based on what he reads.

- Within the first few exchanges, Joe reads the body cues for which layer of being-seen shame is active. See [[body-cues-of-shame-about-being-seen]]. Looking down and to the right signals the inadequacy layer; straight-down head tilt when asked about wanting signals the deeper wanting-shame layer. The cluster of hedges around the want-statement signals the wanting-shame. Joe coaches into the layer that is active. See [[two-layers-of-shame-about-being-seen]].

- If the person already knows how to welcome themselves and demonstrates it within seconds when asked: Joe acknowledges this directly. See [[you-know-this-place-this-is-not-your-first-time-here]]. Then he shifts the work from teaching to noticing what pulls them out. See [[name-the-pattern-that-pulls-them-out]].

- If the person welcomes themselves and then immediately stops, repeatedly: the read is that the work is about the stopping, not about the welcoming. Joe sends them back to welcoming and watches the next stop. Each stop is named. See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]].

- If the person reports "I try a lot" while doing the welcoming work: Joe doesn't fight the trying. He welcomes the trying. "Welcome the trying" is the move. See [[welcome-the-trying]]. The trying is not the obstacle to welcoming. Treating it as the obstacle is.

- If shame or self-criticism arises after the person has been spacious for a moment: Joe names the pattern explicitly. "You go to that place, then you feel shame, then you beat yourself up." This is the [[shame-after-spaciousness]] pattern. The shame after the spaciousness is the lockout mechanism. Welcome it too.

- If the person identifies a behavior they think is the problem (the comparative mind, the trying, the dismissing) and asks how to get rid of it: Joe inverts the approach. He welcomes it instead. "I welcome my comparative mind. It helps me be good in work. There's a brilliance in it." See [[welcome-the-pattern-by-naming-its-brilliance]]. The work isn't to remove the pattern. It's to receive it.

- If the person is good at welcoming themselves alone but does "false humility" in front of others: the read is that the social-facing self is where the wound shows. Joe names this and re-prompts the welcoming with people present. See [[the-good-alone-false-humility-with-others-read]].

- If the person tells themselves a story that self-criticism is required for performance ("if I didn't criticize myself, I wouldn't have the drive to improve"): Joe asks what need the self-criticism is serving and what would be lost if the welcoming replaced it. See [[whats-the-need-that-wouldnt-get-met]].

## Coaching Path

The path Joe followed in the source session, generalized:

1. **Refuse to answer the question on its surface.** Instead, ask what answering it would get them. "How is the answer going to help you? What's the strategy of getting that question answered get you?" The answer usually reveals that the question is itself a request for a specific felt experience (love, welcome, permission).

2. **Have them demonstrate the thing they're asking how to do.** "Show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now." Most people who ask "how do I love myself" already know how, briefly. The demonstration produces the access. See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]].

3. **Watch what stops the welcoming and send them back.** When the welcoming stops, name what just happened. "What'd you do?" Then send them back. The work is in the loop: welcome, stop, name, return. Each return is the practice.

4. **Welcome each pattern that arises by speaking the welcome aloud.** When the person identifies the comparative mind as the problem, model the move: "I welcome my comparative mind. It helps me be good in work. There's a brilliance in it." The spoken welcome with the named brilliance is the operational technique. See [[welcome-the-pattern-by-naming-its-brilliance]].

5. **Name the shame-after-spaciousness pattern.** "The pattern is that you know that place, but after you're in that place, somehow you have a learning that you need to feel shame after that place." Then send them back with the explicit instruction: when the shame arises, welcome it too. See [[shame-after-spaciousness]].

6. **Ask what need wouldn't get met under welcoming.** "What's the need that wouldn't get met if you were welcoming?" The person usually surfaces the function the self-criticism is serving (drive, performance, safety). Then ask whether that need would actually go unmet — usually not. See [[whats-the-need-that-wouldnt-get-met]].

7. **Distinguish welcoming alone from welcoming in front of others.** "You're really good at welcoming the thing when you're in your own space. But when you get in front of somebody else, there's some way in which you do that false humility thing." Then prompt the welcoming with people present. See [[the-good-alone-false-humility-with-others-read]].

8. **Close with the deservingness question.** "Tell me, what would make it that you don't deserve this?" The answer, when the work has landed, is "nothing." See [[what-would-make-it-that-you-dont-deserve-this]].

## Dialogue Example

The pivot from question to demonstration:

- **Guest:** "Why do I tend to dismiss my accomplishments and also more than ignore my needs? I feel like I dismiss them, or feel like I'm not right to have them."
- **Joe:** "How is the answer to that question going to help you? What's the strategy of getting that question answered gets you what?"
- **Guest:** "I mean, my immediate reaction is some way of loving myself more."
- **Joe:** "So the question isn't, how do I love myself?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah, it's literally that."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

The work then moves into demonstration:

- **Joe:** "Show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now. Oh, you did it for a second. What happened?"
- **Guest:** "It's in here for a second, and then it [pulled away]."
- **Joe:** "Let's go back. Go back to welcoming yourself and notice what makes you stop."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

The closing exchange:

- **Joe:** "Tell me, what would make it that you don't deserve this?"
- **Guest:** "Nothing."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not a request for new techniques. The person already has the access. The techniques don't fail because they're wrong — they fail because the underlying pattern that pulls the person out of the access is still operating.

- It is not the same as [[how-do-i-love-myself-and-express-my-emotions]]. There, the system is configured to feel emotion and has been taught to suppress the expression. Here, the system is configured to dismiss itself and has been taught to do so because expansiveness was unsafe.

- It is not solvable by deciding to love yourself. The deciding is the same gear as the dismissing — both are head-level commitments that don't reach the body. The work happens in the live welcoming, in the body, in the moment.

- It is not solvable by getting rid of the comparative mind, the trying, or the self-criticism. Each of these is doing a job. The work is welcoming them, naming the brilliance in them, and noticing that the welcoming dissolves their need to be in charge.
