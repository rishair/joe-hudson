# I have all my eggs in one basket

## What Someone Says

The specific phrasings people use when this concern surfaces. Often in the context of long-term partnership, marriage, or moments of deepening commitment (engagement, wedding, child birth, illness, near-loss).

- "I have all my eggs in one basket."
- "All my eggs are in this basket."
- "What if I lose them?"
- "What if they die first?"
- "I don't know what I would do without them."
- "They're my person and that scares me."
- "We've made each other our whole world."
- "I feel like nothing could compare to this. This is it. This is the thing. And also, that's kind of scary."
- "I'm scared something is going to happen to them."

Often paired with a partner who has the inverse fear: the partner says, "I'm scared of being responsible for your devastation." The eggs-in-one-basket fear and the responsibility-for-the-devastation fear are the two sides of the same configuration.

## Detection Cues

Surface-level signals:
- The person mentions an upcoming or recent commitment milestone (wedding, child, anniversary, illness).
- The person describes feeling closer to their partner than ever and immediately names a fear underneath that closeness.
- The person uses the eggs-in-one-basket metaphor or close variants.
- The person describes a friend or partner who died and connects it to fear about the current partner.
- The person describes extreme sports, dangerous work, or risk-taking and the fear of that risk being borne by the partner.
- The person describes opening their relationship or seeking other connections as a way to spread the risk ("I wanted there to be somebody else you felt connected to in case I leave you").

Indirect signals:
- A new wave of caretaking behavior toward the partner.
- A new wave of pulling away from the partner to "protect" them from future loss.
- Conversations about wills, life insurance, advance directives done with anxiety rather than care.
- A reluctance to fully receive love because receiving it means having more to lose.
- Engineering distance or alternative attachments to dilute the felt intensity.

## What This Concern Hears Underneath

Underneath the eggs-in-one-basket framing is several stacked things:

**The reality the fear is correct about.** Love is finite. The relationship will end -- if not by breakup, then by death. The fear is sensing something true. This is not a distortion to be reframed. See [[finite-love-is-precious-love]] and [[feared-stories-about-emotions-sense-something-real]].

**The avoided grief.** Underneath the fear of losing the person is the actual felt grief of the loss. The fear is what shows up when the grief has not been allowed in. The fear is a defense against pre-feeling the loss. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] and [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]].

**The shame about wanting/needing this much.** "I shouldn't need them this much" or "I've made them too important" is often woven through the fear. The person treats the depth of their love as evidence of weakness or codependence.

**The reactive impulse to manage the risk.** The fear produces strategies to reduce the eggs-in-one-basket exposure: pull away, find other primary attachments, suppress the depth of feeling, refuse to fully arrive in the relationship. Each of these strategies enacts the loss they are designed to prevent. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]].

**The partner-mortality projection.** Often the "I'm scared they'll die" fear is also "I'm scared I'll die and devastate them." The person carries both ends of the loss in advance and feels responsible for protecting the partner from it.

## What Joe and Tara Look For (Reads)

The branching point. Two people saying "I have all my eggs in one basket" go very different directions based on what is read.

- If [[the-thing-not-being-said-as-the-fear-signal]]: the person hints at the fear but skitters away from it. The move is the invitation. See [[lets-go-there]] and [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]].
- If the person is mid-feeling already (tearing up, body softening): stay with them, do not analyze. See [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]].
- If [[rehearsing-how-to-say-it]] or intellectualizing the fear (talking about polyamory as a strategy, talking about evolutionary attachment theory): the head is doing the work the heart needs to do. See [[what-does-your-heart-say]].
- If the partner is in the room and projecting fear onto the other ("I'm scared of leaving them"): the work is for each person to feel their own version of the fear, not to manage the partner's version. See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]].
- If the person is ping-ponging between "I'm responsible for their grief if I die" and "fuck this responsibility": [[dont-ping-pong-on-responsibility]] is operating. The move is to go down to the bottom of the fear and let the apology emerge. See [[apologize-for-trying-to-control]].

## Coaching Path

A typical flow when this concern surfaces:

1. **Invite the fear in directly.** "Can you hang out with the fear?" See [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]]. This is the first move because the fear is what is generating the strategy-thinking the person is in.

2. **Let the body sequence what wants to move.** Often the fear becomes sadness becomes grief becomes a felt wanting to be more fully present with the partner. The sadness about future loss collapses into the aliveness of present love. The sequence does not happen by talking about it. It happens by hanging out with it.

3. **Have the partner be witness, not rescuer.** When the partner is in the room (as in couples coaching), the partner's job is to stay present with their own emotion, not to fix the fear. See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]] and [[stay-centered-while-holding-space]].

4. **Notice what the fear, fully felt, produces.** Often the moving-through of the fear produces a vow, an apology, or a felt clarity about what the person wants in the relationship. From the transcript: after the fear moved, the person turned to her partner and said, "I want you to want to be here with me as much as I want to be here with you" -- and the coach named, "I think we just found some of your vows."

5. **Land on the principle.** "Love is finite. Our time here is finite. That's the amazing thing." The finitude is not the problem. It is what makes the love precious. See [[finite-love-is-precious-love]].

6. **Address the responsibility-and-safety strategies that emerge.** Often the person realizes they have been suppressing themselves to "create safety" for the partner. The work then is to apologize for the suppression and stop creating false safety. See [[creating-safety-suppresses-self]] and [[apologize-for-trying-to-control]].

## What Not to Do

- **Don't reassure.** "You'll be okay. There are other people." This collapses the work. The fear was right that this person is irreplaceable. The reassurance denies the truth.
- **Don't try to dilute the love.** Coaching toward "have more friends, build a wider network" as a solution to the eggs-in-one-basket fear treats the love as the problem. The love is not the problem. The unfelt fear is the problem.
- **Don't intellectualize.** "Statistically, you'll have years together." This is using the head to manage what the heart needs to feel.
- **Don't make it about codependence.** The eggs-in-one-basket fear is sometimes (but not always) a marker of codependence. More often it is a marker of a deep, real love that has accumulated the courage to be felt as deep and real. Treating it as a pathology to be reduced misreads what is actually happening.
