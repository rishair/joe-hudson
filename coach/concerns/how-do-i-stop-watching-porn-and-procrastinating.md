# How do I stop watching porn? / How do I stop procrastinating?

## What Someone Says

- "How do I stop watching porn? How do I stop procrastinating?"
- "I've been trying it for my whole life. I've tried it really, really hard."
- "I feel so much shame about it. I've gotten better at not feeling as much shame, but I still feel shame."
- "I'm so good at making myself unhappy."
- "Other people or other circumstances in my life don't have as much impact on my happiness as I have on myself."
- "I don't act the way I want to act in so many circumstances of my life."
- "I keep doing the thing I don't want to do."
- "I keep failing at the thing I want most."

The presentation often combines a specific behavior (porn, scrolling, procrastination, gaming, food) with a global self-story about being one's own obstacle. The behavior is named as one instance of a broader self-character pattern: "I'm the kind of person who keeps doing this to themselves."

## Detection Cues

The concern arrives with a specific architecture that distinguishes it from related concerns like [[i-cant-be-disciplined]]:

- A long history of trying to stop ("I've been trying for my whole life")
- The behavior is specific and named (porn, procrastination, scrolling) rather than the abstract "I lack discipline"
- The shame about the behavior is foregrounded ("I feel so much shame")
- A self-character framing is layered on top of the behavior ("I'm so good at making myself unhappy")
- A premise of self-responsibility for happiness ("I have more impact on my happiness than other people or circumstances")
- An intellectualized inner life around the behavior — the person has thought about it extensively, read about it, tried many approaches
- A vague sense of failure as a person, not just a failure at the behavior
- An undertone of "I should be able to figure this out and I haven't"

The framing "I don't act the way I want to act" is a particularly reliable signal. The person is reporting a gap between who they intend to be and who they observe themselves being. The gap is being treated as a discipline problem when it is actually a cascade problem.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the words is the cascade described in [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]]:

1. The essential self arrives in the body and feels overwhelming
2. Thinking takes over to manage the overwhelm
3. The thinking turns into shame-thinking ("I'm bad / I'm not enough / I'm responsible for my own happiness and I'm failing")
4. The compulsive behavior arrives to escape the shame-thinking

The behavior the person is asking about is stage 4. The shame story they describe is stage 3. The two stages they cannot see are stage 1 (essential self) and stage 2 (the thinking-as-defense). The work is not on stage 4.

The self-story the person presents — "I'm so good at making myself unhappy, I don't act the way I want to act" — is itself part of the cascade. It is the shame-thinking talking. Coaching the story directly will not produce change because the story is downstream of what is being avoided. Joe explicitly strips the story before re-engaging the question: "let's try the question again without the story" (see [[strip-the-story-and-re-ask-the-question]]).

The deeper hearing: the question is asked as "how do I stop the behavior?" The real question, when the story is stripped, is closer to "how do I become myself?" — the question Joe surfaced when he asked the man to re-ask without the self-story attached. The compulsive behavior and the self-attack are both ways of not being the self the person actually is.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person is heavily in their self-story:** the first move is to strip the story and re-ask the question. See [[strip-the-story-and-re-ask-the-question]]. The question that survives the stripping is the real question, and it is almost never "how do I stop the behavior."
- **If the person retreats into thinking when asked to feel:** the read is [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]] in its specific addiction form — the head is the second stage of the cascade firing in the coaching room. The move is to name the thinking as the avoidance and redirect to the body. Joe: "Your head is trying to figure something out. Let it fucking just dissolve for a second."
- **If the person briefly touches the essential self and then cuts it off:** the read is [[cutting-off-your-own-authenticity-in-real-time]] in its self-inquiry form. The person had contact and then suppressed it. Joe names the suppression: "Oh, you did it, but then you stopped doing it." The naming makes the suppression visible.
- **If the person tries to feel rather than letting it arise:** the read is [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]. The instruction has been heard as "produce a feeling" rather than "permit what is there." The redirect is to drop the trying. "Don't try to feel. You are feeling. It's happening whether you like it or not." See also [[trying-prevents-the-feeling]].
- **If the person frames the behavior as a discipline problem:** the read is that the discipline framing is the trap. The willpower approach has been failing for years; doubling down on it is the loop. See [[i-cant-be-disciplined]] for the related concern, but the move for this specific cascade-driven presentation is the cascade-naming, not the discipline reframe.
- **If the childhood pattern is visible:** when the person describes the addiction, listen for what got shut down early. The exuberant boy in the restaurant, the angry kid put in the car — the suppression that became the cascade was learned somewhere. Naming the connection between the childhood suppression and the present-tense self-suppression is often the move that lands the work.

## Coaching Path

1. **Strip the story and re-ask the question.** The opening framing is wrapped in the self-shame story. The story is part of what is being avoided. Joe's first move is "let's try the question again without the story" (see [[strip-the-story-and-re-ask-the-question]]). The question that survives the stripping is the real question — often something closer to "how do I become myself?" or "how do I find my potential?"

2. **Name the cascade.** Give the person a frame for what is happening: essential self → thinking → shame thinking → behavior. The naming is not the work, but it gives the head a story so the body can come back to the front. See [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] for the cascade and [[name-the-avoidance-cascade-from-essence-to-addiction]] for the move.

3. **Redirect to felt contact with essential self.** Ask the variant of [[what-am-i]] that points at what has never changed and never been hurt: "What are you essentially that has never changed, never been hurt, never been touched by all these stories?" The question is not asked for an answer. It is asked to point.

4. **Catch the live cascade firing.** When the person retreats into thinking, name it. When they cut off felt contact, name it. The coaching session is the laboratory; the same cascade is firing in the room.

5. **Honor what the childhood suppression installed.** When the connection between the present cascade and the childhood pattern is visible, name it. The adult is doing to himself what the parents did to the child. The recognition opens the door for the part that learned to suppress to receive love rather than more correction.

6. **Reframe the work.** Stop coaching the behavior. Coach contact with what the person essentially is. Joe's compression: "If you want it to go away, stay focused on being with what you are, essentially all of it."

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not validate the "how do I stop the behavior" framing on its terms. The framing is part of the problem.
- He does not validate the self-shame story ("I'm so good at making myself unhappy"). The story is the third stage of the cascade firing in the room.
- He does not prescribe habit-stacks, accountability systems, or willpower techniques. The behavior is downstream. Working at the downstream level produces white-knuckle abstinence and recurrence at best.
- He does not pathologize the behavior or the person. The cascade is described as what the brain does, not as evidence of being broken.
- He does not promise that contact with essential self will eliminate the behavior. He observes that focusing on the contact is what tends to dissolve the behavior, in contrast to focusing on stopping the behavior, which tends not to work.
