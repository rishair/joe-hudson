# I need to set boundaries / How do I know if I'm setting good boundaries?

## What Someone Says

This is one of the most common questions presented to Joe across coaching contexts. The wording varies:

- "How do I set good boundaries?"
- "What's the difference between a boundary and an ultimatum?"
- "I think I need to set a boundary, but I don't know how."
- "I tried to set a boundary and it didn't go well."
- "I keep being walked over. I need to be better at boundaries."
- "My boundary keeps getting ignored."
- "Is this a good boundary?"
- "I'm not sure if I'm being too harsh or not harsh enough."
- "I don't want to hurt them, but I need to draw a line."
- "I've been told I need to have better boundaries."

In the original episode, listeners had submitted both forms of this question repeatedly. The two main forms were "what is the difference between a boundary and an ultimatum?" and "how do I know if I'm setting good boundaries?"

"It takes two forms. The one, the question that rose to the top was, what is the difference between a boundary and an ultimatum? And the other one is just, how do you know if you're setting good boundaries? Which I think are basically the same thing." -- Alexa Anderson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## Detection Cues

The concern is often presented explicitly using the word "boundary." But it also shows up indirectly:

- The person describes a relationship where they feel walked over but cannot say what they would do about it
- The person uses the language of "I need to be firmer" or "I'm being too nice"
- The person describes a recurring conflict with a specific person and reports trying various approaches that have not worked
- The person says "I keep wanting them to change but they won't"
- The person describes a household, romantic, or work relationship where they feel chronically resentful but cannot locate why
- The person hedges every request with "I don't want to be a difficult"
- The person says they "have been told" they need better boundaries (often by a therapist, a partner, or a book)
- The person reports having drawn a boundary that "didn't work" or "caused more conflict"

## What Joe Hears

The presenting question is "how do I do this technique well?" The underlying question is "do I deserve to have my truth respected, and am I willing to act as if I do?"

The boundary itself is downstream of the deserving. Most people who struggle with boundaries do not struggle with the mechanics; they struggle with the underlying belief that it is okay for them to refuse what is happening. Without that belief, the boundary either does not get drawn or gets drawn in a constricted way that produces more conflict.

Underneath that is the fear of consequences. The person believes that if they set the boundary, the other will leave, be angry, withdraw love, retaliate. The fear is usually wrong (see [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]]), but it is felt as near-certain.

Underneath that is often the fear that the world will respond well to the boundary, which would invalidate the entire model the person has been operating from. This is the scariest possibility, because it requires grieving the cage that was never there. See [[boundaries]] for this dynamic.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point is what kind of boundary trouble the person is in. Different troubles need different responses.

- If the person has never drawn the boundary at all and the question is intellectual: Joe usually starts with the underlying principle of [[boundaries]] (what makes a boundary good) and the [[does-saying-it-increase-your-love|love test]] for whether their planned boundary qualifies.

- If the person has drawn boundaries but they keep coming out as ultimatums: Joe moves toward [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums]] and the fear that produced the constriction. The work is on the fear, not the language. See [[empowered-vs-power]].

- If the person is in [[aggressive-victim-stance]] and using "boundary" language as a power move: Joe surfaces the dynamic by name. The work is on the underlying anger or fear that the stance is covering.

- If the person is in [[fear-triangle]] dynamics and trying to use boundaries to escape: Joe redirects to the role dynamic itself, since boundary work from inside the triangle stays inside the triangle.

- If the person is at the early stage of a long-arc boundary (e.g., a parent with an addiction): Joe supports the strict version, even if it looks excessive from outside. See [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]] and [[joes-dad-drinking-boundary]].

- If the person is at the late stage and trying to refine an existing boundary: Joe supports the refinement and the discrimination it represents.

- If the person is in resentment about not setting a boundary: Joe explores whether the boundary is needed or whether the underlying want is what needs naming. See [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]].

## Coaching Path

The typical flow:

1. **Establish what makes a boundary good.** It increases your capacity to love the person no matter how they respond. It says what you will do, not what they have to do. See [[boundaries]].
2. **Distinguish boundary from ultimatum.** Use the [[does-saying-it-increase-your-love|love test]]. If the imagined statement opens the heart, it is likely a clean boundary. If it closes the heart, it is likely an ultimatum being mistaken for a boundary.
3. **Locate the fear.** What is the person afraid will happen if they draw the boundary? Often the fear is what is producing the constriction. See [[empowered-vs-power]].
4. **Address the underlying belief about deserving.** Sometimes the boundary work is actually self-worth work. The person does not yet believe they are allowed to refuse. Until that work is done, the boundary will either not be drawn or will be drawn in a distorted form. See [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]] for the developmental arc.
5. **If the relationship is collaborative, explore creative solutions.** Sometimes the boundary is not the right tool. The right tool is a conversation about what each party wants and a creative solution that honors everyone. See [[rubbermaid-dish-solution]].
6. **Practice and apologize.** The expectation is not to get it right the first time. The expectation is to draw the boundary, notice if it landed as an ultimatum, and apologize cleanly. See [[apologize-for-trying-to-control]].
