# How do I deal with a narcissistic parent?

## What Someone Says

This concern usually arrives after the person has already tried the connection tools and seen them fail or backfire. The framing often references VIEW or similar relational practices and asks how to apply them in this specific case.

- "How do I apply the VIEW principles when interacting with a narcissistic parent?"
- "I keep being vulnerable with my mom and it gets used against me."
- "Every time I try to be empathic with my dad, he just escalates."
- "I want to maintain a relationship but the tools that work everywhere else don't work with him."
- "Is it okay to limit contact with my parent?"
- "How do I stay open-hearted with someone like this?"
- "My parent is impossible. What do I do?"

The question often has a hopeful undertone: the person believes that if they just learn the right way to apply the tools, the relationship will improve. The hidden assumption is that the right technique will produce the connection they have not been able to find. This is the assumption that needs to be addressed first.

## Detection Cues

How to recognize this concern from text:

- The word "narcissist" or "narcissistic" applied to a parent, often with some clinical framing.
- A history of trying relational tools that worked elsewhere but failed here.
- A description of vulnerability being exploited or used against the person.
- Reference to escalating dynamics: the more the person opens, the more the parent attacks.
- A question framed as "how do I do X with a narcissistic parent" rather than "should I have a relationship with this parent" — the person is still in the relationship and seeking a way to make it work.
- A note of guilt or moral framing: "I should be able to..." "Good people don't cut off their parents..." "I'm trying to be a better child..."

Indirect signals:

- Mention of a parent who alternates between grandiosity and shame, between contempt and neediness.
- Description of dynamics where the parent does not feel emotion but instead deploys behaviors that seem designed to test how far they can push.
- The person already knows the parent is hard to be with and is asking for permission to do what they have not yet allowed themselves to do.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the question is usually a stack of assumptions to address before the practical answer can land.

**Assumption 1: VIEW will work if applied correctly.** With this 5% of people, VIEW directed at them does not work. Not because the practice is broken; because their emotional machinery cannot receive the vulnerability that VIEW depends on. The vulnerability lands as a leverage point and gets exploited. See [[contain-the-narcissist]] and [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]].

**Assumption 2: A good child stays in the relationship.** There is no rule that requires interaction with a narcissist to be a good person. Separating from a narcissistic parent is one of the available, valid moves. The work is making the choice from clarity, not from obligation.

**Assumption 3: They are a category-of-person separate from me.** Everyone has narcissism. The label-as-separation is itself a narcissistic move. The trigger is information about your own pattern. See [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]] and [[every-trigger-with-your-kid-is-projection]].

The real work is twofold: (1) the internal work of containment — loving your own anger, passive aggression, and manipulation enough that the parent's versions do not destabilize you; (2) the choice about what level of contact serves the actual relationship with love (which can persist independent of the contact level).

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point. Different reads send the conversation in different directions even when the words sound similar.

- If the person is **trying to fix the parent** through better technique: Joe interrupts the technique question. The tools that fix most relationships do not fix this one because the receiving apparatus is not there. The shift is from "how do I get them to be different" to "what is the relationship I actually want, and what level of contact serves it."

- If the person is **projecting the narcissism cleanly outward** (the parent is the narcissist, I am not): Joe surfaces the spectrum framing. "We all have narcissism inside of it." The work is not just about the parent. It is about what gets triggered in the person when the parent shows up.

- If the person is **collapsed into guilt about wanting to limit contact**: Joe gives explicit permission. Separation is fine. Limiting contact is fine. There is no virtue requirement to stay in a damaging dynamic. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] for the concern that often follows.

- If the person has **already separated and is now feeling broken about it**: this is a different concern. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]]. The work shifts to feeling through the guilt to the love underneath.

- If the person is **performing vulnerability with the parent as strategy**: Joe names the dynamic. The vulnerability as strategy is not vulnerability. It is being read by the parent as the lever it has become. See [[view-as-technique]].

- If the person is **stuck on "I should be able to love them"**: Joe distinguishes internal love from external contact. Love can persist while the contact is limited. The boundary does not require ending the internal relationship with love.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow from this concern. Not a script. A map of likely moves with branches.

1. **Acknowledge the trap.** The relational tools that produce connection elsewhere fail here. Not because the person is doing them wrong. Because the receiving system cannot receive them. This dismantles the failure-of-self framing that often runs alongside the question.

2. **Surface the spectrum.** We all have narcissism. The separation-as-label is part of what keeps the relationship stuck. The work includes seeing where the trigger lives in oneself. See [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]].

3. **Reframe the tool.** Boundaries, not VIEW. Containment, not connection. Loving your own anger enough that theirs does not destabilize you. See [[contain-the-narcissist]] and [[boundaries]].

4. **Give permission for the full range of options.** Stay and hold the boundary. Limit contact. Separate entirely. All are valid. The choice is calibrated to your capacity, not to a virtue rule.

5. **Separate internal love from external contact.** You can love them while not being in the room with them. The internal love is not the same as the external relationship. Both can be tended.

6. **Use the trigger.** Whatever they evoke in you is information about your own work. The relationship, even at a distance, can be a source of efficient inner work if the person is willing to look at what shows up.

7. **Address the guilt or grief that follows.** Whatever choice gets made (stay, limit, separate), feelings follow. The guilt of choosing self-protection over family obligation, the grief of the parent you wanted but did not have, the anger at the years lost. Each gets felt. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] and the concern's coaching path.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

- Does not try to teach a refined version of VIEW that will work this time. The technique is not the issue.
- Does not encourage reconciliation or staying.
- Does not pathologize the parent at length. The narcissism is named as a structural pattern, not a moral verdict.
- Does not skip the projection check. The person's own narcissistic patterns are part of what is happening.
- Does not let the person stay in the "what's wrong with my parent" frame. The work is about what the person can do, not about what the parent is.
