# I keep getting in my own way

## What Someone Says

The opening framing is almost always meta and almost always abstract. The person is not bringing a specific problem; they are bringing the pattern of their own avoidance.

- "How do I get myself out of my own way?"
- "I just keep going back to that and then it's like, okay, I recognize that's the problem."
- "We resort to distraction to avoid actually getting our hands dirty in the fucking problem."
- "I go to what I know how to do instead of doing what I want to do."
- "I'm having a hard time stepping into the identity of somebody who can [do the thing]."
- "I recognize the pattern. I just can't seem to stop doing it."
- "Every time I get close to the thing I want, I find some way to derail."

The presentation has a specific texture that distinguishes it from straight procrastination. The person has watched themselves do this enough times that they have built a frame around it. They have named it "getting in my own way." They have noticed the substitution pattern (doing the comfortable thing instead of the wanted thing). They have observed the distraction mechanism. They have done some reading or watching of self-help material and have absorbed the vocabulary for it.

What they have not done is the thing.

The self-awareness is what they bring. They show up already knowing what they are doing wrong. They are asking how to stop. The asking-how-to-stop is itself the pattern firing — one more way to engage with the problem of themselves rather than do the thing.

## Detection Cues

- The concern is presented in abstract, pattern-language rather than concrete particulars. "I keep getting in my own way" rather than "I haven't called the customer back."
- The person uses the word "I" a lot — focused on themselves as the problem rather than on the thing they are avoiding doing.
- Self-aware language: "I recognize," "I see the pattern," "I know I keep doing this," "I'm doing it again."
- A meta-frame ("getting in my own way") that has been used multiple times to describe many different specific situations.
- A relationship to self-help or coaching content. The person has been watching videos, reading books, or working with the framework of personal development long enough to have absorbed pattern-vocabulary.
- A specific dream or aspiration that has been chronically un-pursued — usually creative, entrepreneurial, or identity-changing. Starting a business. Writing a book. Becoming a different kind of professional. Moving to a different city.
- When asked "what specifically?" the person can produce a concrete thing (start the business, do the work, take the step) but their first frame was the pattern, not the thing.
- Phrases like "what's wrong with me," "I'm so good at this," or "I've been doing this for years."
- The person is competent in other domains. They can execute work, manage relationships, hold down a job. The getting-in-own-way is bounded to one specific area where the change would require a new identity.

A diagnostic phrase that almost always shows up somewhere in the opening minutes: "I have a hard time stepping into the identity of [the person who would do the thing]." This phrasing exposes the underlying structure — the avoided action would require becoming someone different, and the system is protecting the current identity by not doing the action.

## What Joe Hears

The abstract pattern-framing is itself part of the pattern. See [[abstract-framing-as-defense]]. The person is engaging with the problem at the meta-level — analyzing how they avoid — because the meta-level is one more layer away from the doing. Each iteration of the analysis is another iteration of the avoidance.

The "what's wrong with me, why do I do this" line of inquiry is also part of the avoidance. See [[complicating-it-as-the-distraction]]. The person has built a sophisticated apparatus of self-analysis that functions as a distraction from the comparatively simple action that would actually move them.

Underneath the abstract framing is usually a concrete avoided thing — an action that would require stepping into an unfamiliar identity. The system has a stake in the current identity. The unfamiliar identity feels dangerous because it is unfamiliar. The body has been organizing the entire architecture of distraction, complication, and self-analysis around preserving the known.

The deeper layer underneath the identity-resistance is usually contained excitement. See [[contained-excitement-is-the-built-architecture]]. The person has, somewhere in their history, learned that excitement is unsafe — they were told to settle down, calm down, not get ahead of themselves, not be too much. The pattern of "getting in my own way" is the adult-form of the system that contains excitement before it can produce action.

The disappointment underneath the contained excitement is also operative. See [[everything-goes-away-anyway]]. The system has correctly observed that wanted things end. It has incorrectly concluded that the way to manage the future loss is to not have the thing in the first place. The not-having is the felt loss in advance.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the framing is abstract from the first sentence:** the read is [[abstract-framing-as-defense]]. The opening move is to interrupt the abstraction by asking for the specific. "It's very abstract. It's too abstract for me. Tell me, what's the — what's — okay, you're getting in your own way. How?" The interruption is the first act of refusing to engage the pattern at the level the person has been engaging it at.

- **If the person has lots of stories about why they get in their way:** see [[reject-all-the-stories]]. The brain has been generating reasons for years; engaging with the reasons would be another iteration of the pattern. The move is to explicitly reject the stories for a moment and look directly at what is being avoided.

- **If the person frames the problem as "I have to figure out what's wrong with me":** the read is [[complicating-it-as-the-distraction]]. The psychological self-analysis is the new distraction. The move is to name this directly: "the psychological 'what's wrong with me, I'm distracting myself' — all of that is also a way to not start your business."

- **If the person uses "I should" anywhere in the opening minutes:** the should is the place to enter. See [[run-the-experiment-of-saying-i-want-instead-of-i-should]]. The live substitution exposes the shame mechanism that is locking the behavior in place.

- **If the person reports having watched a lot of self-help videos or read a lot of books:** the conceptual familiarity has become a substitute for the doing. The opening of the work is not more concept; it is the felt experience the concept points to.

- **If the person uses identity-language ("I'm having a hard time stepping into the identity of"):** the identity-protection is operative. The avoided action would require becoming someone different, and the system is organizing around preventing that.

- **If the person says "but why does that feel so hard?" once the actual simple action is named:** the move has arrived at the real material. See [[now-were-at-the-real-thing]]. The "hard" is the contained excitement, the anxiety about wanting, the disappointment-in-advance. The work is no longer on the pattern; it is on the feeling underneath.

## Coaching Path

1. **Refuse the abstract opening.** "It's too abstract for me. Tell me what specifically." Force the person to name a concrete avoided action. See [[abstract-framing-as-defense]] and the move [[interrupt-the-abstract-with-a-specific]].

2. **Reject the stories.** "I notice your brain has a lot of stories as to why it's true, but I want to reject all those stories for just a second." See [[reject-all-the-stories]]. The stories cannot be the route through because the stories are the pattern's preferred medium.

3. **Restate the problem in concrete form.** "You want to start your own business. You do some things to start your own business, and then you get distracted, and then you go to what's comfortable. Is that generally what's going on?" Get the simple, concrete loop into the room.

4. **Confront the "complicating" move.** "What's wrong with the question, how do I start my own business? What makes it more complicated than that?" The question forces the person to either own the complication as the avoidance or simplify. Usually they will defend the complication ("but I get in my own way") which is the diagnostic that the complicating is the work.

5. **Name the recursive psychological-analysis defense.** "Even the psychological 'what's wrong with me, I'm distracting myself' is also a way to not start your business." See [[complicating-it-as-the-distraction]]. The naming makes the meta-level avoidance visible.

6. **Ask the structural what-stops-you question.** "If you just started doing your business today, right now, what would stop you?" See [[whats-the-thing-you-dont-know-how-to-do]]. The question surfaces the actual obstacle — almost always something small and concrete that the apparatus of self-analysis has been distracting from.

7. **Catch the first "should" and run the substitution.** "Now say I want to [do that]. Which feels better?" See [[run-the-experiment-of-saying-i-want-instead-of-i-should]]. The substitution is fast and produces an immediate felt difference. Use the felt difference as the demonstration that the should is the lock.

8. **Distinguish the thing they know how to do from the thing they don't.** "You know how to stock shelves. What's the thing you don't know how to do?" The question exposes that the person has been working on the wrong thing — building the comfortable apparatus around the actual unfamiliar thing.

9. **Produce the concrete action and remove the permission-asking.** "How hard would it be right now to be like, I will [do the thing] for free?" Most identity-protection collapses when the unfamiliar thing is reduced to a small concrete action that requires no special status to take. See [[just-go-do-the-thing-stop-asking-permission]].

10. **When the person says "but why does that feel so hard?":** stop. Arrived at the real material. See [[now-were-at-the-real-thing]] for the move that follows. Direct the person into the feeling — usually anxiety — that the entire apparatus has been organized around avoiding.

11. **Breathe into the feeling to enjoy it, not to get rid of it.** See [[breathe-into-the-emotion-to-enjoy-it-not-to-get-rid-of-it]]. The instruction has to be explicit and counterintuitive. The person has been trying to make the feeling go away for years; the instruction is to do the opposite.

12. **Name the contained excitement.** See [[contained-excitement-is-the-built-architecture]]. The whole architecture of getting-in-own-way has been built to contain excitement that was, at some point, made unsafe.

13. **Meet the impermanence objection if it arises.** "It's going to disappoint me" / "It's going to go away" — see [[everything-goes-away-anyway]]. The move is to assert that everything ends regardless, that the question is not whether to feel the thing but whether to let yourself feel it now.

14. **Close with the simple action and the lightness.** "Stop distracting yourself. Just go put out ads and tell all your friends." The closing is small, specific, and free of further analysis. The work is to leave the session into the doing, not into more thinking about the doing.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not engage with the meta-frame. The "getting in my own way" framing is the avoidance. Engaging with it as the problem would be participating in the pattern.
- He does not ask "why do you think you get in your own way?" This is the question the person has already been asking themselves for years. The asking is the production mechanism of the pattern.
- He does not offer techniques to overcome the avoidance. Techniques would be more of the same — apparatus added to apparatus, complication added to complication.
- He does not validate the difficulty of the pattern. Naming it as hard would license another iteration of the analysis.
- He does not offer a deep dive into the childhood origins of the pattern. The origins are usually present (excitement-containment from a parent telling the person to settle down) but the move is to work the present-moment feeling, not the past story.
