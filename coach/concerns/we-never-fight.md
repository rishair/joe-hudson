# We never fight / Our relationship is great

## What Someone Says

The presenting concern that is not framed as a concern. The person describes a relationship that is going smoothly, and the words have a slight evenness to them.

- "We never really fight"
- "It's been three years and I've never been triggered in this relationship"
- "We're really compatible"
- "Things have been great for the first couple of years"
- "We don't have any of those problems"
- "Honestly, our relationship is easy"
- "We've never had a big argument"
- "I don't know what people are talking about with all the relationship drama"

Sometimes the person says it directly. More often it shows up as a side comment in a conversation about something else, or as a contrast to other people's relationships.

## Detection Cues

This is one of the harder presentations to catch because the person is not asking for help. The cues are tonal and structural:

- A slight flatness or evenness in how the person describes the relationship
- Pride about not fighting that has a faint quality of bracing
- The relationship is described in objective terms ("we get along well") rather than felt terms ("we love each other deeply")
- Contrast with how the person describes other parts of their life that have aliveness
- A pattern, on closer inquiry, of the person not saying things they think because of how the partner might react
- The realization, when asked, that they cannot remember the last time they felt fully themselves with the partner

The classic case: Brett's reflection in the transcript -- two years of "no fights, this is amazing," followed by realizing both partners had been suppressing anger to protect the other's freeze response. "Everything was great from a number of objective perspectives. But you know, just something, something wasn't quite there until until the trigger actually was allowed to rise and then brought into awareness."

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this presentation, Joe hears one of three things:

**1. [[Walking on Eggshells]] in disguise.** Both people have learned to manage each other's triggers so well that the relationship feels frictionless. The cost is hidden as resentment, low-grade pissedness, or the slow erosion of aliveness. The relationship works the way a well-managed prison works.

**2. Pre-trigger phase.** The relationship is in its early window where the trauma material has not yet surfaced. This is genuinely common in the first 3-5 months when oxytocin masks the configuration. See [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] -- the triggers are coming because the trauma surfaces match, but they have not yet activated.

**3. A real "we're here for our own freedom" agreement.** Rarer, but possible. Two people who are doing their own work, who can be fully themselves with each other, and who consequently do not produce the friction that comes from suppression. The diagnostic is whether each person feels alive and fully themselves, not whether they fight.

The first two are the much more common readings. The third is hypothesizable but should not be assumed.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If [[the-happiness-filter]]: the person describes the relationship with a constant low-grade smile that does not match the flatness underneath. The relationship is being performed as fine.
- If the person mentions, when pressed, that there are topics they cannot bring up: the answer is [[walking-on-eggshells]]. Joe moves to [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] as the diagnostic practice.
- If the relationship is new: Joe may name [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] gently and warn that what feels easy now is also what will produce the triggers later.
- If [[im-not-an-anxious-person]] is also present in some other domain: the person may have a generalized pattern of not noticing internal states. The "no triggers" report is an extension of that pattern.

## Coaching Path

The typical move is not to take the "we're fine" report at face value, and also not to insist that something is wrong. The move is the diagnostic question Joe offers:

1. Joe usually starts with [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] as the diagnostic. The question is not "are you triggered?" but "what are you not saying?" Even people in genuinely healthy relationships have some answer to this. People walking on eggshells have many answers.

2. Based on the response, Joe moves toward [[walking-on-eggshells]] (if there are many things being suppressed), toward [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] (if the relationship is new and the triggers are coming), or toward affirmation (if the person genuinely does not have a list of suppressed things and is alive in the relationship).

3. The follow-on: a practice of starting to say one suppressed thing per day, kindly. Joe is explicit that this is edgy. "Yeah, I mean, take it slow and maybe one thing a day."

4. If triggers surface from this practice, the arc moves into [[trigger]] work proper.

## The Trajectory Variant: "We Started Great and Now It's Heavy"

A close cousin of this concern is the version where the person no longer reports the relationship as fine, but tells the same story shape: "We got married and it was really great, we were happy, and over time there's this kind of built-up resentment, this walking on eggshells, we're not entirely happy with one another." Same trajectory, same mechanism. The difference: the person presenting the trajectory has already noticed the cost. See [[the-built-up-resentment-story]] for the read.

For the coaching, the same set of moves applies, but the sequence runs faster because the diagnostic does not have to overcome the person's belief that the relationship is fine. The mechanism (avoidance produces accumulation, accumulation produces stagnation, stagnation produces resentment and then disconnection) is already partially visible to them.

This same trajectory shows up in founder partnerships and in long jobs. The relational form is identical; the venue changes.
