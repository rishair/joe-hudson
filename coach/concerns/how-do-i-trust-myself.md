# How do I trust myself? / How do I tell intuition from fear?

## What Someone Says

The presenting form varies, but the underlying shape is consistent:

- "How can I trust myself again?"
- "How can I trust my intuition?"
- "How do I tell the difference between intuition and fear, or projection, or reaction?"
- "I have less confidence in what feels right for me."
- "I'll have a gut reaction and then another just as strong one will contradict it. And then another one from a different angle."
- "Am I making decisions out of fear, or out of coming back into my power?"
- "How do I know if this is intuition or just anxiety?"

A common second layer: the person has been doing some kind of inner work (parts work, IFS, meditation, therapy) and the work has multiplied the voices in their head rather than clarifying them. They went in expecting to gain access to themselves and came out aware of so many parts that no single signal feels reliable. See [[parts-work-made-things-worse]].

## Detection Cues

- Explicit framing: "how do I trust myself / my intuition / my gut?"
- The diagnostic question is presented as the actual problem: "I've been reading about it, and some people say it's how you feel in your body, and if your body is tense then it's fear and not intuition unless your intuition is about something scary..." -- the elaborate epistemology around the question is the tell.
- Multiple equally-strong felt impulses contradicting each other.
- The person reports having gotten "more aware" of internal voices and now being less able to act.
- A history of being "in flow" or making confident decisions, followed by a period where decisions feel paralyzing.
- Often comes with a recent hurt: someone broke trust, an opportunity didn't work out, the person was hurt for going after something they wanted. The trust-in-self collapse often dates from there.

## What Joe Hears

The question itself contains a fear assumption. "How do I tell intuition from fear?" presupposes that intuition is reliable and fear is unreliable, and that the work is to sort one from the other. That entire frame is fear's frame. The person is asking how to use their head to validate what their body is saying, which is itself the move that erodes self-trust.

Underneath the question is usually a different question: "How do I feel certain enough to act?" The desire is not for trust but for certainty. The pursuit of certainty is what produced the paralysis. The thinking that produces certainty has filled the field with so many "parts" that no signal feels clean.

The deeper layer: the person is making "decisions" and second-guessing them because they are afraid of an outcome. They want to be sure they will get the result they want. See [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]]. The trust question is not about intuition. It is about fear of outcome.

The deepest layer often surfaces last: a recent hurt taught the person that going after what they wanted got them hurt. They put themselves in a box to feel safe. The fire went out. The trust collapse is the protective response to the unfelt hurt. See [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]] and [[what-we-fear-we-invite]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **The intellectual question with the emotional body underneath:** the person opens with a precisely formulated question but the body is signaling something else entirely. See [[intellectual-question-with-emotional-body]]. The coaching move is to name the gap and ask what they actually want to work on.
- **The happiness filter:** a constant low-grade smile or social pleasantness that doesn't drop even when the topic is hard. See [[the-happiness-filter]]. The filter often only drops when the person touches something real, which makes the drop itself the diagnostic.
- **The "am I doing it right" loop:** when the person reaches a moment of spaciousness or arrival, the next thought is "am I doing this correctly?" See [[am-i-doing-it-right]]. This itself is the resistance, and the resistance produces the very second-guessing the person came in worried about.
- **Decision-language:** when the person says "I have to decide," they are reporting fear. Most of what they did in the last five minutes (what to say, where to look, how to breathe) were not decisions. The "decisions" that feel like decisions are the ones loaded with fear of outcome.
- **The narrative-then-emotion shift:** the person tells a clear story about a past hurt while staying composed; the somatic content shows up only when Joe asks what happened in the body when they said it.

## Coaching Path

1. **Notice the intellectual question vs. the emotional body.** "That's a really intellectual question. Your emotional body is not having an intellectual conversation with me." See [[intellectual-question-with-emotional-body]]. Often the stated topic isn't the topic.
2. **Pivot to what would actually shift.** [[whats-the-most-profound-positive-shift]] -- "What's the thing that if it shifted in your life would be the most profound positive movement?" This produces the real concern, which is often "trusting myself again."
3. **Invert the question.** [[how-do-you-not-trust-it]] -- "How do you NOT trust it? You just spoke. You just made a hundred decisions in the last five minutes. How does that happen?" This makes visible that the person is trusting themselves continuously; what they are not doing is trusting the outcome.
4. **Name the fear-of-decision dynamic.** [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]] -- "As soon as you think there's a decision, you're in fear." This reframes the entire problem. The work is not to sharpen the decision-making process. The work is to feel the fear under the felt need to decide.
5. **Excavate what makes them care about the result.** "What makes you care about the outcome? If you're going to be who you're going to be and the outcome's going to do what it does, what makes you concerned?" The person usually surfaces a value they fear losing (connection, integrity, authenticity), then sees that all of those live in how-they-are, not in the result.
6. **Find the hurt underneath the trust collapse.** "What got hurt? I just watched you in your essence and there was no hurt there. So what is it that got hurt?" The person finds the place in the body where the hurt lives. See [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]].
7. **Welcome the abyss they're avoiding.** [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]] -- "There's an abyss in you that you're avoiding, and your fear is that if you go into it, you won't come out." The session typically arrives here. Without resistance, the abyss turns out to be peace, not annihilation. The person was resisting peace. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]].
8. **Resolve the original question by dissolving it.** "So from here, how do you trust yourself? How does the question even make sense?" The question dissolves rather than being answered.

## Tone

Patient, persistent, slightly playful. Joe doesn't engage the intellectual machinery of the question. He keeps redirecting from "how do I know" to "what is happening right now in your body." He treats the elaborate question-frame with mild dismissal -- not contempt, but the friendly indifference of someone who is not going to follow the bait into the head.

## Related Concerns

The trust question often surfaces as the deeper layer underneath other presenting concerns. Most notably:

- [[i-need-a-scorecard]] — when a person whose identity has organized around external scoreboards is asked what could replace the scorecard, "trusting myself" often surfaces as the internal substrate. The path runs in the opposite direction: from scorecard concern down into the trust question. The two concerns are deeply linked because the absence of a scorecard exposes the absence of self-trust that the scorecard had been substituting for.

The trust question also serves as a cover for other concerns it is upstream of:

- [[im-afraid-ill-revert-to-my-old-pattern]] (emotional-state variant) — when the person has come through a difficult emotional state (depression, anxiety, dark period) and is now afraid that trusting their intuition will lead them back. The trust frame ("I can't trust my voice") covers an underlying recurrence fear ("if I trust my intuition I'll go back to making the same mistakes"). The session that surfaces the underlying concern treats the trust question as downstream and addresses the recurrence-fear directly via [[press-the-button-thought-experiment]] (past-suffering form) and [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. When the welcoming of the feared state is real, trust returns automatically; there is no longer a state the person is trying to steer themselves away from.
