# I make money when I hate what I'm doing / I can't make money doing what I love

## What Someone Says

- "When I love what I'm doing, I don't make any money. And when I hate what I'm doing, I've made lots and lots of money."
- "How do I make money doing what I love?"
- "I left the corporate job to do this meaningful work and now I can't make ends meet."
- "I'm a coach now and it's a bit of a struggle."
- "I love this work, but the cash flow is killing me."
- "I need clients. It's just cash flow."
- "I'm doing the right work but no one's buying."
- "I keep telling myself the money will come if I keep doing what I love."

The concern often arrives with relief and despair in the same breath. Relief at having found work that matters; despair that the work that matters doesn't seem to pay. The person often has years of evidence that the inverse pattern is true for them: the soul-killing jobs paid, the soul-feeding work doesn't.

## Detection Cues

- A direct statement of the inverse: "love → no money / hate → money"
- A description of having made a meaningful pivot (left corporate, started coaching, became an artist, opened the studio) followed by cash-flow distress
- The presenting problem framed as a market problem ("no one's buying," "nobody hires anymore," "the market is bad") when the person has the skill and the time and the ability
- A specific surface explanation that doesn't fully account for the pattern ("nobody public-speaks anymore," "AI is taking over my field," "people don't pay for this kind of work")
- Self-deprecation about the work itself dressed as humor or realism

The most reliable detection cue is the structural shape. The person is not asking how to do their work better. They are asking how to keep doing it without going broke. That gap — between "I can do the work" and "I can't get the work" — is the diagnostic.

## What Joe Hears

The inverse pattern is rarely about money or about the work. It is about the cost of being seen wanting the thing you love. The corporate job paid because the person could be ruthless about asking to be paid for it — they didn't love it, they didn't care, the asking was easy. The meaningful work pays poorly because the person cannot ask cleanly for it. Asking would expose how much they want it, and what they want is loaded with shame.

The "no one's buying" frame is almost always a cover for an avoidance of marketing. And the avoidance of marketing is almost always shame. See [[obligation-as-management]] for why making money "more important than you" pushes money away; the same mechanism is running here in reverse — making shame more important than the work pushes the work's revenue away.

Underneath the inverse pattern is usually a childhood imprint that says: when you wanted something cleanly and visibly, love was withdrawn. Wanting got wired to unlovability. See [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]]. The corporate job did not require wanting in the open. The work you love requires wanting in the open — and the body has been trained that this is dangerous.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person frames it as a market problem:** the read is that they are protecting themselves from naming the avoidance of marketing. Joe will not engage the market frame. He will name the avoidance and ask what makes the marketing hard. The answer is almost always shame.
- **If the person has a specific childhood story that explains "X is more important than me":** the work goes to [[childhood-imprinting]] and [[trauma-mapping]]. The pattern in the present (avoiding marketing) is the pattern in childhood (some parental dynamic was more important than the kid). Apply the original template to the current avoidance: how much are you making [the original X] more important than [the current Y]?
- **If the person collapses into shame in the room:** the move is [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]]. Joe will refuse the moral frame. "It's embarrassing." "It's human." See the public speaking coach example for the rapid version.
- **If the person already has the relevant skill in a different form:** name it. The marketing required to grow a coaching practice often uses the same skills the person teaches. The public speaking coach who can't market is being asked, by the work itself, to apply the work to themselves. See [[skill-transfer-redirect]].
- **If "I just need a paycheck" is in the air:** see [[i-dont-have-time-for-this]] for the related concern that uses material urgency to avoid the work that would make the material urgency dissolve.

## What's Actually Happening

The inverse pattern reveals something specific. When the work is meaningful, the asking required to monetize it touches the shame that the meaningful work is *about*. The coach who teaches authenticity in speaking cannot ask for clients because asking would require the authenticity they teach. The teacher who teaches abundance cannot price their workshop because pricing it would require the abundance they teach. The pattern is structural: the meaningful work is exactly the work the person hasn't yet done on themselves, and the asking is what would force them to do it.

This is also why the inverse pattern often comes with a story about the market. The market story protects the person from having to notice that the obstacle is internal. If the obstacle is internal, the only path forward is through the shame. If the obstacle is the market, the person can wait, complain, or pivot — none of which require them to feel what they have been avoiding.

## Coaching Path

1. **Refuse the market frame.** Joe does not debate whether the field is contracting or whether AI is eating the work. The person's adult competence is not in question. The blocker is internal.
2. **Locate the original "X was more important than me."** If the person can name a childhood scene where they understood that something (a parent's drinking, a parent's shame, a parent's career, a parent's relationship) was more important than they were, get the specific feeling. The feeling installs the rule. See [[trauma-mapping]].
3. **Find the surrogate underneath the surrogate.** The first answer is usually the surface answer ("money was more important," "booze was more important"). Joe asks what was really underneath. "What did the booze represent?" "Avoiding shame." The deeper answer is usually an avoidance of an emotion the parent could not bear. See [[what-was-really-more-important-than-you]].
4. **Apply the original template to the current avoidance.** "How much are you making [the original deeper thing] more important than [the current thing you want]?" "How much are you making shame more important than your work / your income / yourself?" This applies the childhood pattern to the adult presenting problem in one move. See [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]].
5. **Name the marketing avoidance directly.** Joe's binary works here: "Either you're really a crappy coach, or you are not marketing yourself. I'm going to assume it's the second." The binary forces the avoidance into the room. See [[binary-that-forces-the-avoidance-into-view]].
6. **Test the shame in the room.** If the person agrees the avoidance is the issue, Joe checks whether the shame is actually in the room: "If you look at me, do you see that I'm ashamed of you?" "If you look at all the people, do you see that they're ashamed of you?" The somatic check usually surfaces that the shame is internal, not relational. See [[is-the-shame-in-the-room-or-in-you]].
7. **Surface the want in marketable form.** "How do you want to market yourself? Give me three adjectives that would make you fucking excited to let people know what you do." The question converts the avoidance into a positive frame. The person's own language for what they actually want to be known for usually arrives quickly and cleanly.
8. **Name the skill-transfer.** If the person teaches what they cannot do (the public speaking coach who can't market themselves, the relationship coach who can't have a relationship, the abundance teacher who is broke), name it. The same principles apply. They already know how. See [[skill-transfer-redirect]].
9. **Close with normalization.** When the person collapses into "that's embarrassing," refuse the shame frame. "It's human." See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]].

## What Joe Will Not Do

Joe will not give business advice. He will not help craft the marketing plan, suggest content strategies, or recommend pricing. The presenting problem is not a business problem.

Joe will not validate the inverse-pattern story as if it were a real economic principle. "Loving the work means it won't pay" is a cover story. The work pays when the person can let themselves be seen wanting to be paid for it.

Joe will not engage the market frame as if it were the actual obstacle. The market is not what's blocking the cash flow. The shame about asking is.

## The Authenticity-Specific Frame

In some presentations, the inverse pattern is named precisely in terms of authenticity. The person can make money — they have done it before — but only by abandoning themselves. The current concern is not whether the work can pay. It is whether the work can pay while the person stays themselves.

The form Joe surfaces this in one session: "I know I can get it, but I can't get it in the way that I want. Also kind of like, I know I can get it, but I can't get it if I'm being authentic and being myself." The first career paid because the person could ruthlessly self-abandon for it. The current career touches the self directly, and the self-abandonment that paid before is now precisely what cannot be done.

This is the same inverse pattern, named with the precision the body actually feels. The shame is not about asking for money in the abstract. It is about asking for money while being seen as the person you actually are.

## The Comedian-to-Coach Variant

A specific common shape: the person was the kid who made the parents laugh / performed / achieved / held the family together. The career they now love is structurally a continuation of that childhood connection-strategy. The financial dysfunction is because the work is still aimed at the parents, not at the clients in the room. See [[performing-for-love-becomes-savior-career]] for the full pattern article, and [[comedian-to-coach-money-shame-session]] for the canonical illustration.

The diagnostic Joe surfaces in this variant: "How much of the coaching is actually just you still trying to save your parents?" Once the recognition lands, the obstacle to making money has a specific name. The career is not the problem. The childhood wiring still aiming the career at absent parents is.

## The Inverse Form

The same mechanism produces the opposite presentation: the person makes lots of money doing work they hate. See [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] for the version where someone has been monetarily successful but the should is older than the work. The current concern is the post-pivot form: the person already saw through the old should, did the work, made the meaningful change — and discovered that the new arrangement reveals a deeper layer of the same wiring.
