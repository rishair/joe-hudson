# My inner critic won't stop / The voice in my head is constant

## What Someone Says

The constant critic shows up in many phrasings:
- "I can't get my mind to stop."
- "My inner critic is brutal."
- "I'm so hard on myself."
- "I replay conversations for hours."
- "My brain never shuts up."
- "I keep telling myself I'm an idiot."
- "I can't stop second-guessing everything I say."
- "I rehearse what I'm going to say a hundred times."
- "I'm tired of the loop in my head."

Often people do not name it as the inner critic. They describe its effects: trouble sleeping because the mind won't stop, anxiety before social events, exhaustion after work, rumination that won't release.

## Detection Cues

Direct cues:
- The person uses the word "critical" or "critic" about themselves.
- The person describes self-talk: "I tell myself..."
- The person reports rumination, replaying, rehearsing, looping.
- The person says "I can't get out of my head."

Indirect cues:
- A person describing the same problem from the same angle repeatedly without arriving at action — the recursive thought loop is one of [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]].
- A person who second-guesses what they just said in conversation, mid-sentence.
- A person who tells a story about themselves with the cadence of a complaint.
- Sleep disturbance attributed to "racing thoughts."
- High-functioning people who report exhaustion that does not match their workload.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the concern is the [[voice-in-the-head]] — the recurring, repetitive, critical voice that runs in the background of most people's experience. The complaint is functionally a description of low-level chronic depression (dysthymia) — see [[voice-in-the-head]] for the clinical mapping.

Underneath that: the person has likely been at war with the voice for a long time, which has made the voice more entrenched, not less. See [[dont-fight-the-voice]]. They probably believe the voice is necessary to motivate them, keep them safe, or improve them. The voice has earned its place by appearing to perform a function it does not actually perform.

The deeper read: the voice was installed in childhood (see [[childhood-imprinting]]), is operating with care underneath crappy strategies (see [[voice-as-care]]), and is producing the outcomes it claims to prevent (see [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]]).

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point depends on what is underneath the surface complaint.

**If the person is fighting the voice and exhausted:** the work is to drop the fight. The voice cannot be defeated by force; force is what it eats. Joe will go to [[dont-fight-the-voice]] and offer [[voice-as-care]] as the reframe. The move is [[apply-view-to-the-voice]].

**If the person is rehearsing conversations:** see [[rehearsing-conversations]]. The pattern needs to be named for its self-defeating mechanics. Joe may use [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] to show that the rehearsal produces the bad outcome it tries to prevent. The path is to feel the underlying fear (see [[fear-is-about-feeling]]) rather than continue the rehearsal.

**If the person is using the voice as a motivator:** they will defend it. Joe will name the should-as-threat dynamic ([[should-as-threat]]) and show that the voice has produced years of the same stuck behavior, which means it is not working as a motivator. He may use the screw-gun analogy: if a tool consistently strips the screws, you stop using it.

**If the person is locked in [[perfectionism]]:** the voice is the critical parent internalized. Joe will go to [[perfectionism]] and [[connection-over-perfection]]. The voice is not the problem; the standard it is trying to satisfy is.

**If the person has done a lot of self-work and the voice is more subtle:** Joe will name [[trying-to-be-right-is-the-voice]]. The voice has moved from crude criticism to dressed-up integrity questions. Same voice, new costume.

**If the person is judging others heavily:** the voice is leaking outward. See [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]] and [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]].

**If the person is rumination-locked:** the loop is a sign of an unfelt emotion. See [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]]. The work is to find what the loop is avoiding feeling, not to fight the loop directly.

**If the inner critic has the texture of self-attack:** the critic is often the inward form of anger that has nowhere else to go. See [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]]. When the energy of anger cannot move outward (because of childhood injunction, because it was punished, because it was tied to love removal), it turns inward as self-abusive talk. The work in this case is releasing the anger somewhere external. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]]. As the held charge drops, the inward attack often loosens on its own.

**If the person has strong external boundaries against asshole behavior but tolerates the same from themselves:** the work is to extend the no-asshole rule inward. See [[extend-the-no-asshole-rule-inward]]. The asymmetry between what the person will tolerate from others vs. from themselves is the diagnostic.

**If the critic has become an internal figure the person describes as a demon, monster, dragon, or "the thing inside me":** the figure has hardened from voice into entity. The work is the chase-and-hug move. See [[chase-it-around-the-room]]. Fighting the figure has produced more figure; the inverse relationship is pursuit-with-love. The "demon" is just looking for hugs.

**If the person is ready to move from softening the voice to using it:** introduce [[voice-as-guide-to-unhealed-places]]. Once the voice no longer feels like an enemy, its content becomes a high-specificity map of what is still unhealed. The recurring themes are not noise; they are coordinates for the next layer of work.

**If the person keeps automatically agreeing with the voice's claims:** the work is to withdraw the acolyte's nod. See [[the-politician-and-the-acolytes]]. The voice's authority depends on the listener's automatic validation; refusing the validation is the prerequisite for any other practice to land.

## Coaching Path

1. Name the voice. Often the person has not separated the voice from themselves. Naming creates a useful distance: "you are the one hearing this voice, not the voice itself." See [[voice-in-the-head]].

2. Stop the fight. Whatever they have been doing to combat the voice is making it worse. Introduce [[dont-fight-the-voice]] and the thief-security-guard frame.

3. Reframe the voice as care with bad strategies. See [[voice-as-care]]. This shifts the relationship from adversarial to compassionate.

4. Introduce a practice. Different practices fit different moments:
   - For someone who has not yet seen how constant the voice is: [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]]. The entry-point experiment that makes the voice audible to the person.
   - For someone ready to play with multiple ways of meeting the voice: [[menu-of-responses-to-the-self-critical-voice]]. The four-response set — ouch, I-see-you're-scared, I-wonder-if-that's-true, love-and-compassion — each doing different work for different moments.
   - For someone who needs lightness: [[experimenting-with-the-voice]].
   - For someone who needs analytical rigor: [[lawyer-the-voice]] and [[untruth-in-every-thought]].
   - For someone who needs depth: [[whats-looking-out-behind-my-eyes]].
   - For someone who needs relational repair: [[apply-view-to-the-voice]].
   - For group work: [[excavating-the-inner-critic-in-group]].

5. Set expectations. The voice has been operating for decades. The change is gradual. Quick wins are sometimes possible but should not be the bar. The work is a long, gentle, repeated re-relating.

6. Connect to the broader work. The voice is the immediate concern. Under it are larger patterns — [[shame-drives-behavior]], [[perfectionism]], [[i-need-to-improve-myself]], [[i-should-be-doing-this]]. The voice is the symptom layer that opens onto the structural layer.
