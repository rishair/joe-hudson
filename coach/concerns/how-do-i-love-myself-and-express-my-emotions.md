# How do I love myself and express my emotions?

## What Someone Says

The defining feature is a paired question — how to love themselves AND how to express their emotions — usually with a backstory of having been taught that emotions are bad. The person has been working on changing this. They are not refusing to feel; they are trying to feel and finding the trying does not work.

- "How do I love myself and express my emotions properly?"
- "How do I learn to express my emotions?"
- "How do I accept myself and reconnect with my inner child?"
- "I've always been told not to let my emotions shine through, and that it's a bad thing."
- "I should just let my emotions roll off my sleeve and not pay attention to them."
- "I did that for so long, but it's not really who I am."
- "I'm a very emotional guy, but I was taught to hide it."
- "I'm trying to express my emotions."
- "I live a lot of my life in my head."
- "I've been isolated through a lot of different aspects of my life."
- "I want to not listen to all the people throughout my life who have told me I'm worthless."
- "I'm very good at masking my emotions."
- "I don't know who I am truly anymore."

The person often delivers the report while visibly emotional — eyes wet, voice tight — while saying they are trying to express emotions. The visible emotion and the report of being unable to express emotion are happening simultaneously.

## Detection Cues

- The person opens with a paired question about self-love AND emotional expression, not just one or the other
- They identify themselves as "an emotional person" who was taught to hide it (distinct from the head-dominant person who reports no emotional access — they have access, they were taught to suppress it)
- They use the word "properly" or "the right way" about expressing emotions — the framing implies there is a correct technique they haven't learned
- They are visibly emotional while reporting that they cannot express emotion
- They live with their parents, recently moved back, or are otherwise in active proximity to the original family environment
- They describe themselves as introverted, isolated, or living in their head
- They reference a history of judgment, bullying, or trauma from when they were younger
- They are working on this — they have read, watched videos, gone to therapy — but the trying isn't producing the result

The presentation has a specific texture. The person is not numb (like the [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] concern). They are flooded with emotion that has nowhere to go because the channels were closed during childhood. The flooding produces dissociation as the only available management strategy. The person sees themselves dissociating, doesn't want to be dissociating, and tries to stop — which is more dissociation.

## What Joe Hears

The system was originally configured to express emotion. The expression was taught to be wrong. The person learned to mask the emotion — to perform a flatter, less-emotional version of themselves. The original emotional capacity is still there. It was buried, not removed.

The current adult work has two simultaneous tasks:
1. Stop the masking that has become automatic
2. Let the original emotional capacity surface

The two tasks interfere with each other when done from the head. The person tries to express emotion and the trying is the mask in a new costume. The person tries to stop masking and the trying is masking. The system has learned to convert all effort into the suppression pattern, even effort aimed at the opposite.

This is structurally different from [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]]. There, the wires from body to awareness have been dimmed or cut. Here, the wires are intact and active. The signals are arriving. The system has built a layer of muscular control on top of the signals to prevent them from being expressed. The work is not to rebuild the wires (they work) but to release the muscular control (which the trying maintains).

The "I don't know who I am" report often comes alongside this concern. The masked self is who the person knows. The emotional self underneath has been hidden so long that its emergence feels foreign. The unknowing is accurate — the person genuinely does not know the unmasked version of themselves yet — and is also a structural inevitability of the work, not a sign of failure. See [[the-head-cant-know-who-you-are]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people can present this concern and Joe goes different directions based on what he reads.

- If the person is visibly emotional while reporting being unable to express emotion: the read is [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]]. The trying to express is the suppression operating. Joe names the live evidence. "It's happening right now. The trying is trying to stop it from happening right now."

- If the person is dissociating in real time — looking away, going flat, leaving the body: the read is [[disassociating-in-the-coaching-room]]. Joe may name it directly ("you're isolated right now, bro"). The dissociation is the management strategy.

- If the person says "I'm trying" repeatedly while the thing they're trying for is visibly happening: Joe interrupts the descriptive pivot and points at the present-tense fact. The "I'm trying" pivot is the same as the masking pivot — both are the head taking over from the body.

- If the person describes the masking as their inner-child wound and asks how to repair it: Joe pivots to the depersonalization work. What were the parents scared of (see [[what-were-your-parents-scared-of]])? The repair starts with releasing the personal frame, not with techniques.

- If the person has named the wound but is in obligation about the repair ("I need to sit with my emotions"): Joe deploys the [[mangy-cat-eating-from-the-hand]] example to replace "need to" with "get to." The wounded part cannot be repaired through demand.

- If the person says "I don't know who I am" after describing the masking: Joe deploys [[the-head-cant-know-who-you-are]] and redirects attention to the body. The unknowing is correct at the head layer and resolvable at the body layer.

- If the person attempts to do "non-personal observation" but does it by dissociating: Joe names [[non-personal-with-body-in-vs-out]]. The non-personal observation is a genuine capacity. The dissociation as its vehicle is the wound. The work is to keep the capacity while putting the body back in.

## Coaching Path

The path Joe followed in the source session, generalized:

1. **Ask for the question that the rest hinges on.** "The one that's scariest to ask, the one that's making you almost cry right now." See [[ask-for-the-scariest-question]]. This finds the load-bearing question rather than the first one the person produces.

2. **Refuse the advice to follow the suppression teaching.** The person reports having been told to "let emotions roll off my sleeve." Joe's answer: "To not listen to any of that bullshit." The refusal positions emotional expression as the natural state, not as a remedial skill the person has to acquire.

3. **Name the live trying as the pattern.** When the person says "I'm trying" while crying, name that the trying is the suppression in real time. See [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]].

4. **Name the live dissociation.** "You're isolated right now, bro. You're isolating the sadness that's happening right now." See [[disassociating-in-the-coaching-room]] and [[point-at-the-present-evidence]].

5. **Run the amplify-then-opposite move on the dissociation.** Ask the person to 10x their disconnection, then release the tension, then go to the opposite end without effort. See [[amplify-the-dissociation-then-opposite-with-no-effort]]. This produces an embodied slingshot back into felt connection.

6. **Refuse intellectual identity work.** When the person reports "I don't know who I am," name that the head can't know. Redirect to the body. See [[the-head-cant-know-who-you-are]].

7. **Deploy the parent-fear question.** "What were your parents scared of?" When the person produces an answer about themselves, correct: "It's not you. It's not about you." Then ask again. See [[what-were-your-parents-scared-of]] and [[the-pattern-is-not-personal]].

8. **Run the body-catch-up move.** When the person says "deep down I know that," walk the recognition through the body. "Let it seep into every cell." Ask for the updated felt report. See [[let-the-body-catch-up]].

9. **Refine to embodied non-personal observation.** Once the body is back in, demonstrate that seeing oneself from outside can be done without leaving the body. See [[non-personal-with-body-in-vs-out]].

10. **Replace "need to" with "get to" through the cat example.** When the person asks how to do the ongoing work, deploy [[mangy-cat-eating-from-the-hand]] to convert the work from obligation to patient relationship.

## Dialogue Example

The opening sequence from the source session:

- **Guest:** "Um, I got a lot."
- **Joe:** "Um, I don't even know which one to choose, but the one that the rest hinges on. The one that's scariest to ask, the one that's making you almost cry right now."
- **Guest:** "I mean, how do I — how do I love myself and express my emotions properly, or learn to express my emotions?"
- **Joe:** "What do you mean properly?"
- **Guest:** "Um, I don't know, I'm a very emotional guy, as you can see, but I've always been told and taught to not let my emotions, uh, shine through, and it's a bad thing. And, um, you know, I should just let my emotions roll off my sleeve and not pay attention to them. Um, and I did that for so long, but it's — it's not really who I am."
- **Joe:** "Cool. Yeah. So my answer to your question is, how do you do it properly? Is to not listen to any of that bullshit."
- **Guest:** "I'm trying, I'm trying."
- **Joe:** "Well, it doesn't sound like you need to try. What makes you try?"

The exchange shows the structural moves in compressed form. Joe asks for the load-bearing question, declines the framing of "properly" (which assumes there's a correct technique), refuses the suppression teaching the person was raised on, and immediately interrupts the "I'm trying" pivot.

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not the same as [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]]. The person feels — sometimes intensely. The problem is that the feeling has been taught to be wrong and the system has built suppression on top. The work is releasing the suppression, not building emotional access from scratch.
- It is not solvable by learning emotional expression techniques. The person likely already knows the techniques. The techniques fail because the underlying suppression is still operating. The work is upstream of technique.
- It is not solvable by self-talk affirmations about being lovable. The wound has a specific shape (specific parents, specific moments, specific messages). Generic affirmation doesn't reach the specific shape.
- It is not solvable by isolating to do "the work." The pattern was installed in relationship; the repair happens in relationship. Withdrawal to work on it alone usually maintains the original wound. See [[point-out-theyre-already-doing-it]].
