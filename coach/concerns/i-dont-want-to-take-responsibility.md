# I don't want to take responsibility / I'm tired of taking responsibility

## What Someone Says

The concern arrives sounding like a riddle the person is asking themselves out loud. They want to know why they don't want what they "should" want, and the trying-to-figure-it-out is itself another instance of the pattern.

- "I don't want to take responsibility. And I want to know why."
- "I have so many opportunities, and I don't want to take any of them."
- "I either think I should or would like to deliver on it for my husband."
- "I'm tired of taking responsibility for everything."
- "I'm responsible for not wanting to be responsible." (the recursive frame)

The presenting is often paired with an "Oroboros" framing — the person notices they are taking responsibility for not wanting to take responsibility, and brings that observation in as if the loop itself were the problem to solve. The loop is not the problem. The loop is the pattern.

## Detection Cues

Listen for the specific combination of:
- Articulated opportunities (career, relationships, projects) the person can name but feels no pull toward.
- Language of "should" or "deliver" attached to a specific other person (often a spouse, parent, business partner).
- Self-aware noticing of the resistance — the person already knows they're resisting and is bringing the resistance itself for analysis.
- A meta-frame in the presenting ("I'm being responsible for not being responsible") that signals the person is intelligent, articulate, and stuck *because* of the articulation, not in spite of it.

Indirect signals:
- The person reports being constantly tired without being able to name what exhausts them.
- The person describes life as a series of obligations even when most of those obligations are nominally chosen.
- The person uses the word "have to" about almost everything, including things they wanted.
- The person can describe what they "should" want in detail and cannot describe what they actually want at all.

This concern is adjacent to [[i-should-be-doing-this]] but presents from the opposite side: that concern is "I should and I'm not doing it"; this concern is "I'm doing it and I don't want to be." The first is failure-of-compliance; this is exhaustion-from-compliance.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the words is a body that has been holding itself together for so long that the holding is now the felt experience of being-alive. The person is not actually choosing the opportunities — the opportunities are arriving inside the same composure-grip that the person learned to apply to themselves long ago, and the system is now refusing the latest application of the grip even when the content is good.

The "responsibility" the person is tired of is not external obligation. It is the internal composure: the constant, low-grade muscular and energetic effort of [[composing-yourself-as-self-suppression|composing yourself]]. That composure is so total that it includes the responsibility for the coaching session itself, the responsibility for the spouse's experience, the responsibility for not falling apart in any setting. The refusal of opportunities is the system's last available signal that the composure is killing it.

The Oroboros framing — "I'm responsible for not wanting to be responsible" — is not a paradox to solve. It is a description of how thoroughly the composure has colonized the inner life. Even the resistance to composure is composed.

The deeper read: the composure was originally a response to being yelled at for not being composed. The voice that demands composure is now the person's own. See [[what-was-done-to-you-youre-now-doing-to-yourself]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person is composing themselves in the session itself:** this is the central read. The person laughs and immediately tightens; expresses a feeling and immediately contains it; describes the problem in articulate, organized prose while the body holds rigid. The session itself becomes the demonstration. Joe will name it live: "right now feels like you're taking responsibility for something. What is it that you're taking responsibility for right now?" See [[composing-in-the-session-itself]].
- **If the person reports getting yelled at for not being composed as a kid:** Joe goes to the [[what-was-done-to-you-youre-now-doing-to-yourself]] principle. The external voice has become the internal voice. The "fuck those people" reaction is now self-directed without the person noticing.
- **If the person can intellectualize the pattern but cannot drop it:** the intellectualization is itself another instance of composure. Joe will refuse to engage at the level of analysis and pivot to a body-level move — [[visualize-the-kid-composing-themselves]] or [[tell-me-to-compose-myself]].
- **If the person's body is unusually still or contained:** Joe reads constitutional sensitivity that was locked down. The work then includes the reframe — [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]] — so the person can stop treating their own system as the defect.
- **If the person's bodied energy briefly "pops out" and gets pulled back:** Joe will catch it in real time. "I saw you, you just popped out for a minute. It was really sweet." The catch makes the suppression visible by contrast with what was being suppressed.

## Coaching Path

1. **Refuse the external framing.** The person presents the husband, the opportunities, the responsibilities. Joe redirects: "right now feels like you're taking responsibility for something. What is it that you're taking responsibility for right now?" The work is in the room, not in the inventory of obligations.
2. **Name the live composure.** Once the person laughs and re-composes, Joe names it. "So that you just did it. You just laughed. Like, I got you. You laughed and then you're like, nope, composure." This is [[composing-in-the-session-itself]] caught live.
3. **Move the source.** Ask what's wrong with the unmasked part. "What's wrong with this thing, this less composed part of you?" The answer typically reveals the original imprint — being yelled at, being told to settle down, being made wrong for too-muchness.
4. **Externalize the introject.** Have the person do to Joe what is being done internally — [[tell-me-to-compose-myself]]. The instruction is uncomfortable on purpose. The discomfort makes the absurdity of the internal practice visible.
5. **Use the developmental absurdity.** [[visualize-the-kid-composing-themselves]]. Ask when a child should start composing themselves. The mind says "as soon as possible"; the body shudders at the image of a five-year-old containing themselves. The split between mind and body is the opening.
6. **Reverse the apology direction.** [[apologize-to-the-other-for-what-you-do-to-yourself]]. Have the person apologize to the spouse for all the ways they have told the spouse to compose themselves. The apology often lands as relief even though the spouse is not in the room — because the apology is actually addressing the self.
7. **Reframe the sensitivity.** Once the composure begins to soften, the person will encounter a wave of feeling. Name the gift: "your system is incredibly sensitive and you learned to lock it down because it was so unsafe to have that sensitive of a system." See [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]].
8. **Hand them re-entry.** As composure releases, the person will need a way to ground. [[notice-pleasure-and-sensation-as-grounding]] is the practice: feel the pleasure and expansiveness in the body as the way to stay with the opening rather than re-composing. "Take it slowly because it's a lot. You are really gonna be able to feel a lot. It's an incredible gift. But just take it slow in it."
