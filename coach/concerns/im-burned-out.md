# I'm burned out / I'm running on fumes

## What Someone Says

- "I'm burnt out."
- "I've been running so hard for so long I don't even know how to stop."
- "I have nothing left to give."
- "I'm running on fumes."
- "I'm running on adrenaline."
- "I can't think straight anymore."
- "I dread getting up to do this again."
- "I used to love this and now I just resent it."
- "I'm exhausted but I can't seem to rest."
- "When I do rest I just beat myself up about resting."

Sometimes the concern arrives wrapped in pride: "I've been pushing hard for ten years and I think I'm finally hitting a wall." The pride and the depletion are both real.

## Detection Cues

- The person describes a long stretch of effort (months or years) with no real recovery in it.
- Caffeine consumption is high. Sleep is poor or unrestorative.
- Coffee, energy drinks, or stimulants have become structural rather than occasional.
- The person reports having been more excited or alive about the work in the past and a flatness now.
- The person is in a leadership role (CEO, founder, executive, head of household) and treats burnout as a personal failure rather than a structural signal.
- Self-attack language is present: "I should be handling this better," "I shouldn't be this tired," "Other people manage."
- The person reports the brain "caving in on itself" -- they can't see opportunities clearly, can't take pleasure in the work, the optimism necessary for the role has drained out.
- They describe doing things they "have to" do, not "get to" do. The whole frame is obligation.
- They run on urgency: nothing gets done until it becomes urgent, and then the urgency itself produces the next adrenaline hit.
- Adjacent to [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] (the fear that without the pressure nothing would get done) and [[i-should-be-doing-this]] (the language of shoulds dominating).

## What Joe Hears

The burnout is not the central thing. The way the person has been operating -- the running-on-adrenaline frame for living -- is the central thing. The burnout is the body presenting the invoice. See [[burnout-cycle]] and [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]].

Underneath the report of exhaustion are usually three concurrent layers:
- **Nervous system:** the adrenals have been firing for so long that they are depleted, and the body has dropped into the recovery state that follows depletion.
- **Psychological:** the person is living in constant fight-or-flight -- shoulds instead of wants, obligations instead of desires, deadlines instead of inspiration. The brain reads this as ongoing attack.
- **Emotional:** there is an emotion the person has been running from. Avoiding the emotion generates adrenaline. The avoidance is what makes the system burn hot all day.

See [[burnout-cycle]] for the three-layer frame.

The most important read is whether the person is on the "running hard" side of the cycle or the "collapsed on the couch beating themselves up" side. The work is different in each phase, but the self-attack is the locking mechanism either way. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person is mid-run, not yet collapsed:** the work is to look at what they are running from. What emotion are they avoiding? What shoulds are running them? The intervention is to find ways to do the work that feed them rather than drain them. See [[how-could-you-do-this-and-gain-energy]].

- **If the person has already collapsed and is in the bathrobe phase:** validate the recovery as biology, not character. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]]. Address the self-attack that is extending the recovery. The CEO who doesn't beat himself up recovers in 3-4 months. The one who does beats himself up takes 2-5 years.

- **If the person is using urgency as motivation:** name the pattern. See [[urgency-as-motivation]]. The investor who only acts under urgency teaches his whole team to manufacture urgency for him. The cost shows up in the body.

- **If the person treats adrenaline as aliveness:** name this. Adrenaline feels like aliveness the way coffee feels like energy. By the third cup you're not getting energy, you're avoiding the crash from the second cup. The thrill of running on fear has a cost.

- **If the person is in their first rest period and immediately got busy with ideas:** name the [[post-rest-second-wind-trap]]. The first wind of energy on the recovery side is not the recovery completing. It's a stage of it. The deeper rest is past it.

- **If the person believes rest is laziness:** see [[rest-is-an-accomplishment]]. Resting well is a skill, not a default. Flip the frame from "wasting time" to "becoming better at recovery."

- **If the person reports the recovery is "taking too long":** they are adding shoulds to the recovery. The shoulds extend the recovery. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]].

- **If the person frames burnout as something outside them caused (the job, the kids, the company):** Joe will gently distinguish lifestyle from how the person is relating to the lifestyle. Two people can live the same life and one will burn out and one will not. The difference is what is happening internally.

- **If the person regularly does scary activity (climbing, jumping, racing) and reports feeling settled after it:** consider [[adrenaline-discharge-via-fear-activity]]. The activity may be working as a discharge mechanism for chronic background anxiety. The person is not the adrenaline-junkie stereotype; they are using the activity to manage a backlog they have no other way to clear. The work is not to take the activity away but to add direct contact with the anxiety so the activity is one option among several rather than the only release valve.

- **If a normally-adrenalizing activity has stopped producing adrenaline:** the adrenals are exhausted. Stop using the discharge activity and actually rest. See [[burnout-cycle]] for the diagnostic.

## Coaching Path

1. **Name which side of the cycle they are on.** Mid-run or post-collapse. The intervention differs. See [[burnout-cycle]].

2. **Locate the emotion being avoided.** What feeling does the running prevent? Often grief, fear, helplessness, or shame. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[what-we-fear-we-invite]].

3. **Examine the shoulds.** Each should is an adrenaline cost. Where is the person operating from "have to" rather than "get to"? See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

4. **For the things they really do need to do, ask how they could do them with energy gained rather than energy lost.** See [[how-could-you-do-this-and-gain-energy]]. This is the highest-leverage move with executives.

5. **If they are resting, address the self-attack on the rest.** "What if you were trying to be the best at resting?" See [[rest-is-an-accomplishment]].

6. **Address the post-rest second-wind trap.** If they have been resting and now feel ready to jump back in -- that is not the recovery completing. Sit with it longer. The deeper peace and joy on the other side of the second wind is where the real restoration is. See [[post-rest-second-wind-trap]].

7. **Reframe energy management as the lever, not time management.** See [[energy-management-over-time-management]].

8. **Treat tiredness as a signal to rest, not a problem to push through.** Tiredness is the body's recovery mechanism. Resisting tiredness extends burnout.

## Where People Get Stuck

- They try to solve burnout with willpower, which is more adrenaline. See [[overwhelm-reduces-productivity]].
- They try to rest but bring the same self-attack into the rest, extending the recovery indefinitely. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]].
- They believe the burnout is caused by the external situation (work, family, company) and miss that the same situation produces no burnout in someone with a different internal relationship to it.
- They feel the second wind on day 10 of vacation, declare themselves recovered, jump back in, and end up burned out again within months.
