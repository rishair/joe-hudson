# How do I navigate this uncertain time? / Everything feels uncertain right now

## What Someone Says

This concern usually walks in dressed in current events. The specifics shift with the year: a pandemic, an election, a market crash, a war, AI, geopolitics. The substrate is constant: too much change happening too fast in too many domains to feel ground anywhere.

- "We live in really uncertain times."
- "I don't know how to make decisions right now."
- "Everything is changing so fast."
- "I just want some stability."
- "I keep doomscrolling and I can't stop."
- "How do you move forward with conviction in all this?"
- "I can't predict anything anymore."
- "I'm scared about [AI / the economy / the war / the next election]."
- "It feels like the world is falling apart."
- "I feel paralyzed."
- "We're all fucked."
- "I'm trying to stay positive but it's a lot."

The presenting question is always some version of "how do I find more certainty?" The implicit assumption: certainty is a thing that exists, and the problem is that the current moment has less of it than usual.

## Detection Cues

The concern can show up as the explicit ask ("help me navigate uncertain times") or indirectly:

- A person whose conversations keep looping back to current events
- A person who is making decisions much more slowly than usual, or refusing to make them
- A person who has stopped planning more than a few weeks out
- A person who is consuming news compulsively
- A person who has frozen in their business, their career, or their relationship and is attributing the freeze to external chaos
- A person whose body language tightens when the topic of the future comes up
- A person who has retreated into a tight loop of safe activities and is calling it "waiting it out"
- A person who is rapidly oscillating between catastrophizing and forced positivity
- A person whose anxiety has become a chronic background hum that they have stopped registering as anxiety
- A person who keeps asking the coach what is going to happen, what to invest in, what the world will look like in five years

The concern can also surface in coaching as a meta-frame around almost anything else: "I would normally just decide this, but with everything going on, I don't know."

## What Joe Hears

There is no such thing as certainty. There never was. The brain runs a prediction routine, gets pretty good accuracy on the daily patterns ("I wake up, I get breakfast, I go to work"), and labels that level of prediction "certainty." Then a period arrives where the prediction routine fails more often than usual, and the person experiences this as the world becoming uncertain. The world did not become uncertain. The illusion of certainty got harder to maintain. See [[uncertainty]].

The cultural framing — "these are uncertain times" — is therefore subtly wrong. Better framing: this time is bringing a mirror to the reality of the situation, which is that everything is always changing. Nobody sat down in 2010 and accurately predicted 2020. Nobody is sitting down today and accurately predicting 2030. The current moment is not exceptional in this regard; it is just less successful at hiding the fact.

What is also underneath: a refusal to face what is actually real. Most of the suffering in the presenting concern is not coming from the actual changes happening. It is coming from one of three artificial collapses of uncertainty — optimism, pessimism, or realism that pretends to neutrality while still collapsing the future to a fixed outcome. All three are forms of refusing to be in what is actually here, which is not-knowing combined with the capacity to be a certain way inside the not-knowing. See [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]].

The other thing underneath: massive transformation happens precisely in moments of rapid change. The window of tolerance is where life is comfortable. Transformation almost never occurs in the window of tolerance. The opportunity in this kind of moment is enormous; the person is treating it as only threat. See [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point depends on which artificial collapse the person is currently running and which mechanism inside them is doing the collapsing.

- **If the person is in chronic catastrophizing** ("we're fucked"): they are not facing reality, they are imagining a fixed bad outcome. The body is usually clenched and they have been in this state for so long that they have stopped registering it as a choice. The path is toward noticing that the catastrophe has not actually arrived in the way they have been imagining for years, and toward letting the underlying fear actually be felt. See [[catastrophizing-vs-visualizing]] and [[dont-be-pessimistic-in-uncertainty]].

- **If the person is in forced positivity** ("it's going to be fine, hope and faith"): they are using optimism as a way to not feel the fear or grief that is here. The optimism is sugar. It tastes good briefly and produces a crash. Long-term, the person becomes less effective, not more. The path is toward letting them feel what they have been outrunning. See [[dont-be-optimistic-in-uncertainty]].

- **If the person is in armchair realism** ("I'm just being realistic"): they are collapsing the uncertainty into a fixed negative outcome and dressing it as neutrality. This is the most subtle of the three because it sounds like maturity. The flying-through-the-crash example points at the difference. See [[dont-be-the-realist-who-collapses-uncertainty]].

- **If the person is frozen and dysregulated**: they likely have been cortisol-soaked for long enough that the problem-solving capacity has gone offline. The first work is regulation, not insight. The three-moves practice applies: face the reality (notice the catastrophe hasn't actually arrived), feel the emotion you've been avoiding, find the certainty that is actually here. See [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty]].

- **If the person comes from a chaotic family of origin**: their stance toward the current chaos is being shaped by an old imprint. Children of alcoholics often went one of two ways: extremely adaptive to chaos, or extremely controlling. Both are responses to the same imprint and both can be operating now. See [[alcoholic-family-chaos-vs-control-lineage]].

- **If the person is treating the chaos as something being done to them**: this is the most common configuration and the most consequential. The shift is from "this is being done to me" to "this is an adventure I am taking ownership of." The shift is not optimism. It is the only stance that actually produces both better outcomes and a livable interior. See [[take-the-adventure-stance]].

## Coaching Path

The path is not a script. It is a map.

1. **Decline the premise that this time is uniquely uncertain.** The framing is the first thing to loosen. The person believes they are dealing with exceptional uncertainty; the work begins with the recognition that what they are dealing with is the reality of being alive, foregrounded.

2. **Diagnose which artificial collapse is running.** Are they optimistic, pessimistic, or realist? Each one requires a slightly different intervention. See [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]].

3. **Make the situation specific.** Not "the world" — what specifically is uncertain in their life? The job? The relationship? The kids? Generic uncertainty is harder to be with than the specific uncertainty that is actually theirs.

4. **Bring the body in.** If they have been cortisol-soaked for months, the work is not more thinking. It is regulation. The three-moves practice applies: face what's actually real, feel what's been avoided, notice the certainty that is here. See [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty]].

5. **Reframe the question.** From "what's going to happen?" to "how do I want to be in this?" The first question has no answer. The second one always does. See [[how-do-you-want-to-be]] and the uncertainty-specific application [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]].

6. **Offer the adventure stance.** Not as a positive thinking exercise. As a structural recognition: the only stance that works in genuine uncertainty is ownership of one's own being inside it. See [[take-the-adventure-stance]].

7. **Name the opportunity.** Massive transformation happens in moments of rapid change. The fire that is here is also the soil for the next thing. See [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]].

8. **Bring wonder.** Long-horizon questions ("what does this look like in 300 years?") and short-horizon presence ("what is certain right now?") both bypass the trap of trying to predict the unpredictable middle. Wonder and fear do not coexist well in the brain. See [[wonder]].

The arc is not linear. Most people will cycle through all three artificial collapses before the adventure stance becomes available. The job is to keep returning them to what is actually real and what is actually theirs to choose.

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not [[i-feel-stuck]] — that concern is about a chronic inner pattern. This concern is about an external situation that is intensifying inner patterns.
- It is not [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] — that concern is about a missing direction. This concern is about a present-tense overwhelm.
- It is not [[i-have-all-my-eggs-in-one-basket]] — that concern is about a specific concentrated risk. This concern is the general experience of the ground feeling unstable in many directions at once.
- It is not [[someone-i-love-is-dying]] — though it can co-occur and the work overlaps. The dying-of-a-loved-one concern has a specific known loss arriving. This concern is the chronic unsettled state of not knowing what is arriving.

The discrimination matters because the work differs. This concern's specific work is to dissolve the false expectation of certainty and recover ownership of how to be inside what is actually always uncertain.
