# Why am I always trying to fix things?

## What Someone Says

The person names a pattern of compulsive fix-it activity that covers self, others, and often the world. The pattern is so pervasive that they cannot find ground outside it.

- "I want to fix everything."
- "I see something I believe is wrong or some injustice and I have to fix it."
- "If people don't like the way I've shown up, I have to justify it."
- "I'm trying to fix myself. That's probably the biggest thing."
- "I'm trying to prove I've healed by taking somebody else's issue away."
- "I see something discriminatory and I'm going to stand up and make a stand even if that person didn't want me to."
- "Maybe it's obsessive caretaking behavior."
- "There's this underlying anger and fear and I feel stuck."
- "How do I stop fixing people?"
- "How do I stop fixing myself?"

The presentation often includes a meta-layer: the person knows they are doing it, has labeled it as a pattern, and is asking how to stop. The asking-how-to-stop is itself part of the pattern. See [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]].

## Detection Cues

- The person describes fixing as a *whole way of being*, not a single behavior. Fixing the self, fixing others, fixing injustice — all in the same description.
- The person reports that others reject or resent the fixing ("people don't like how I show up").
- The person justifies the fixing morally — there's an injustice, someone is hurt, it's the right thing to do.
- An underlying anger and fear runs alongside the fixing. The fixing has not produced peace; it has produced agitation.
- A felt sense of obligation that overrides what the other person wants ("even if that person didn't want me to").
- The fix-stance often surfaces as activism, as parenting, as caretaking a struggling family member, as managing a partner, as workplace problem-solving, as self-improvement projects — all at the same time.
- The person describes a sense that they have to *prove* something through the fixing. Often "I've healed" or "I'm a good person."
- The presenting frame is meta: "how do I stop being a fixer?" rather than "I have this specific situation."

The concern is broader than [[i-caretake-people]] (which is relational) and broader than [[i-need-to-improve-myself]] (which is interior). The fix-everything concern is the *whole posture* — the person who can't look at the world without seeing a list of things to fix, including themselves.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the fixing is the inability to be with anything. The fixer is reaching for the only thing they know how to do because being-with is unfamiliar. The fixing is the activity that holds the avoidance of presence in place.

A particular shape often surfaces underneath: a chronic, low-grade sense that everything the person touches fails. The fixing is driven by this sense. See [[everything-i-touch-fails-drives-fixing]]. The harder the person fixes, the more the failure-sense is reinforced, because nothing the fixing actually addresses is the source of the feeling.

The fixing often functions as a defense against silence. If the person stops fixing, they encounter the silence underneath — and the silence is intolerable because they have not built the capacity to be in it. The anxiety that arises when fixing stops is the gateway to the territory they have been avoiding. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]].

"You can't fix anything if you're not here like this with me. You're trying to fix something without being able to be with it." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

There is also a judgment layer underneath the fixing. The person who fixes injustice is judging the perpetrator. The person who fixes themselves is judging the broken-self they are addressing. Underneath the judgment, when the judgment is hypothetically removed, is usually silence — and the silence is what the fixing has been protecting against. See [[im-judgmental-how-do-i-stop]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same fix-everything presentation goes different directions depending on what is showing up.

- If the person is **reading from notes or has prepared an intellectual presentation**: see [[reading-from-notes-or-script]]. The preparation is itself the pattern. The fixing has already started in the form of getting-this-right. Joe will ask them to set the notes down and be here, which is the first instance of the demonstration that will run through the whole session.

- If the person is **describing the fix-pattern with intellectual distance**: see [[presenting-with-an-intellectual-question]]. The person is performing curiosity about themselves rather than being present. Joe will refuse the intellectual frame and redirect to what is happening in the body right now.

- If the person is **morally framing the fixing as righteous**: Joe does not engage the moral content. He treats the fixing as the symptom and looks for what feeling the moral-framing is protecting against. The injustice the person is fighting is often a screen over the silence they cannot tolerate.

- If the person **asks "how do I stop"**: see [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]]. The question is the fixing pattern reapplied to the fixing. Joe names this and pivots to the opposite of doing — undoing. See [[undoing-not-doing]].

- If the person **goes immediately to existential despair** when the fixing is taken away ("nothing, what's the point, why are we here"): Joe does not engage the philosophy. He brings the person back to the body and the current moment. The philosophy is another version of the fixing — fixing the meaning question intellectually instead of feeling what is here.

- If the person **collapses into shame** about being a fixer: Joe refuses the moral collapse. See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]]. The fixing is human; the shame about the fixing is another layer of the same pattern.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow for this concern follows a specific shape, because the meta-nature of the concern requires a meta-response. The session itself becomes the demonstration. The fixing has to be unfixed in the room.

1. **Set down the notes.** If the person is prepared, ask them to set the preparation aside and be here. See [[set-aside-the-notes-be-here-with-me]]. This is the first place the fixing is interrupted.

2. **Refuse the broken premise.** Ask "what's wrong with you?" and treat the answer "nothing" as the operative datum. "So what's to fix?" The premise of the fixing — that there is something broken — is itself the thing being challenged. See [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]] and [[dont-presume-the-person-is-broken]].

3. **Find the judgment underneath the moral framing.** If the person frames the fixing as standing up against injustice, locate the judgment. "What do you judge them for?" Then ask: "If you couldn't judge them, what would you feel?" See [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]]. The answer is usually silence, nothingness, emptiness.

4. **Let the silence trigger the anxiety.** Once the silence arrives, the anxiety arrives next. The body has been avoiding the silence by generating fix-activity. With the activity briefly paused, the avoidance shows up as anxiety. Welcome the anxiety. Do not engineer it away. The anxiety is the threshold.

5. **Refuse the philosophical detour.** When the person reaches for existential meaning-questions ("what's the point? why are we here?"), do not engage the philosophy. The philosophy is another version of fixing. Return them to the body and the present moment.

6. **Use the session itself as the demonstration.** See [[make-the-session-itself-the-demonstration]]. Drop the conceptual conversation. Ask the person to be with you — eye contact, no fixing each other. The thing they cannot do anywhere else is doable here, with the coach.

7. **Find the driver of the fixing.** Often a chronic sense that everything I touch fails. See [[everything-i-touch-fails-drives-fixing]]. Naming this is the work; the fixing has been the strategy to avoid the feeling, and locating the feeling drops the need for the strategy.

8. **Show that fixing requires not-being-with.** Use the anthill image or the present-moment image. "What's wrong with this moment?" See [[whats-wrong-with-this-moment]]. Once the person is fully here, no fixing is available because no problem is registering.

9. **Refuse the "how do I stop" loop.** When the person asks how to stop fixing, name that the question is the fixing pattern. The work is undoing, not doing. See [[undoing-not-doing]] and [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]].

10. **Hand the mind a useful job.** Once the somatic recognition has happened, give the mind something to do that supports the undoing: deconstruct every thought with "how is that not true?" See [[mind-deconstructs-itself]] and [[apply-how-is-that-not-true-to-every-thought]].

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not engage the moral content of what the person says they need to fix. The discriminatory event, the partner's depression, the world's injustice — none of these are the work. The work is the fixing as a pattern.

- He does not let the conversation stay intellectual. Every time the person goes to philosophy or self-analysis, he brings them back to the present moment.

- He does not validate the fixer identity. "You're so caring, you're so dedicated" would reinforce the pattern. He treats the fixing as a strategy to avoid presence, not as a virtue.

- He does not give a how-to-stop instruction. Giving an instruction would convert the work into another fix-project. The work is the live experience of stopping in the room.
