# I can't stop doomscrolling

## What Someone Says

- "I can't stop scrolling on my phone."
- "I waste hours every night on my phone and I hate it."
- "I'm scrolling until 2 or 3 in the morning."
- "I scroll the second I get home from work and don't stop until I fall asleep."
- "I know it's making me miserable and I can't put it down."
- "I'm a doomscroller. I'm so bad."
- "I keep promising myself I'll stop and I never do."
- "I feel terrible after I scroll but I keep doing it."
- "I check my phone every few minutes even when there's nothing to check."

The presentation usually combines a specific time-loss complaint (hours per day, late-night scrolling, phone-first-thing-in-morning) with a self-character story ("I'm so bad at this, I'm pathetic, I should be doing something better"). The behavior is named as evidence of a personal defect rather than as the symptom it actually is.

## Detection Cues

- Specific time-loss reports ("six hours," "until 2am," "all weekend")
- The word "doomscroll" or "scroll" used self-critically
- The behavior is paired with self-attack ("I'm so pathetic," "I'm wasting my life")
- A history of attempts to stop (app blockers, screen-time limits, phone-in-another-room) that did not stick
- The behavior often co-occurs with stopping exercise, meditation, or other restorative practices — the person has dropped the activities that would compete for the dopamine
- An emotional tone of shame about the behavior that is heavier than the behavior itself warrants
- The cognitive-dissonance story is often present: "no one else does this as badly as I do" / "I'm uniquely broken in this way"
- The person describes hollow, ghost-like satisfaction — the scrolling does not produce pleasure, only the avoidance of something else
- Notifications-on, phone-in-pocket, every-question-becomes-a-lookup as the environmental signature of the pattern

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the surface complaint is the same architecture as any other shame-driven compulsive behavior. The doomscrolling is the Stage-4 escape from the Stage-3 shame thinking. See [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] for the cascade.

Specifically for scrolling, the pattern Joe hears almost always includes:

1. **Shame about the scrolling itself** — the person feels broken or pathetic for doing it
2. **A unique-to-me distortion** — "other people don't have this problem" even though the same thing is happening to hundreds of millions of others at the same moment. See [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]] and the universalization test.
3. **A cognitive-dissonance reinforcement** — the person does not see the other scrollers (because they too are alone in rooms scrolling) and the absence of visible peers gets read as evidence of unique defectiveness. See [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]].
4. **The shame is the engine, not the consequence** — the more the person beats themselves up about scrolling, the more they want to escape themselves, the more they scroll. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] and [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]].
5. **The dopamine substitution has been removed** — when the person stops exercise, meditation, sleep, social contact, the only available dopamine source is the phone, which makes the scrolling structurally inevitable.

The deepest hearing: the question "how do I stop scrolling?" is asked from the same shame structure that is producing the scrolling. Answering the question on its terms reinforces the structure. The work is on the shame that the scrolling is managing, not on the scrolling itself.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person is in heavy self-attack about the scrolling:** the shame is the engine of the loop. The move is to interrupt the self-attack ([[its-a-human-thing-redirect]]) and to name the cycle structurally ([[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]). The relief of being told "this is what humans do" often releases enough shame that the behavior loosens on its own.
- **If the person believes they are uniquely broken:** the cognitive-dissonance distortion is operating. The move is the universalization test — at this moment, hundreds of millions of other people are doing the same thing. The unique-to-me framing is the false-shame structure described in [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]].
- **If the person has stopped exercise, meditation, sleep, social contact:** the dopamine landscape has been collapsed. The phone is the only remaining source. Restoring the other sources is part of the work — not as discipline but as making room for something else to compete.
- **If the person presents the behavior as the problem:** the framing is the trap. The behavior is downstream. The work is on the shame structure or on the essential-self avoidance, not on the behavior. See [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]].
- **If the person is describing the satirical "elite doomscroller" identity ironically:** they may have heard Joe's video and are already half-aware of the structure. The move is to confirm the structure they are pointing at and to redirect toward the actual work (feel the unfelt, address the shame).

## Coaching Path

1. **Refuse the behavioral framing.** Do not give phone-management techniques as the primary work. The behavior is downstream. Giving techniques without addressing the shame reinforces the brokenness premise. See [[dont-presume-the-person-is-broken]].

2. **Name the universal pattern.** The scrolling is what humans do. The person is not uniquely broken. The cognitive-dissonance distortion that says "I'm the only one this bad" is itself the engine of the next round. See [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] and [[its-a-human-thing-redirect]].

3. **Locate the shame.** What is the person beating themselves up about? Often it is the scrolling itself in a recursion — the shame is about being someone who scrolls — but underneath is usually older material. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

4. **Apply self-forgiveness.** "I forgive myself for scrolling for six hours last night." Specific, spoken, in the body. See [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]].

5. **Restore the dopamine landscape.** Exercise, meditation, sleep, social contact — not as moral oughts but as the materials that compete for the phone's job. Without alternatives the scrolling is structurally inevitable.

6. **Address the felt-sense underneath.** What is the scrolling helping the person not feel? Sadness, anger, the felt presence of essential self? The substance (phone) loses its job when the avoided experience is welcomed. See [[joes-doomscrolling-story]] and [[joes-pot-habit]] for the parallel personal accounts.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not prescribe phone-removal as the primary intervention. Phone-removal works at Stage 4 of the cascade and addresses none of the upstream layers.
- He does not pathologize the person or the behavior. The behavior is described as the predictable output of the cascade, not as evidence of personal defect.
- He does not collude with the "I'm uniquely broken" story. He universalizes it explicitly — this is happening to hundreds of millions of people right now.
- He does not lecture about screen-time research. The frame of "you should be doing less" reinstalls the should and tightens the loop. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] on shoulds.
