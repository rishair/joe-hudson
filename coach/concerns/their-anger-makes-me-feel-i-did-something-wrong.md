# When Others Get Angry I Feel I Did Something Wrong

## What Someone Says

The specific phrasings people use when presenting this concern:

- "When my boss gets angry, I just feel like I must have done something wrong."
- "Every time my partner yells, I shut down. I feel attacked."
- "My mother gets angry and I'm immediately the bad kid again."
- "I keep apologizing even when I don't know what I did."
- "I can't be in the same room as someone who's angry."
- "I walk on eggshells around this person."
- "I scan my whole day for what I might have screwed up."
- "I know it's not logical but I feel like I'm in danger."
- "I feel like a child when she gets like that."
- "I can't think when someone is yelling at me."

Often it presents as a workplace problem ("I have a really hard time with my boss"), a relationship problem ("we can't fight because I shut down"), or a parent problem ("my mom is angry and I'm immediately five years old"). The surface concern names a specific angry person; underneath is the structural relationship to anger itself.

## Detection Cues

The AI coach should recognize this concern even when the person does not name it directly:

**Direct signals**: the person describes shutting down, freezing, or feeling attacked when someone else is angry. They describe their automatic loop of "what did I do wrong?" when another person is upset. They describe scanning their behavior for whatever might have caused the anger.

**Indirect signals**: someone repeatedly tells stories about an angry boss / parent / partner without describing their own anger about being yelled at. They apologize a lot in their descriptions. They describe the angry person's behavior in great detail and their own response barely at all (suggesting freeze). They report exhaustion from a particular relationship and the exhaustion correlates with the other person's anger episodes. They describe walking on eggshells, managing the other person's mood, performing helpfulness to avoid the anger.

**Body cues from in-session description**: the person's voice changes when they describe the angry person — gets quieter, faster, more apologetic. They use the phrase "I just" a lot ("I just froze," "I just couldn't say anything"). They describe physical responses (stomach drop, frozen, can't speak) that mark the trauma response rather than ordinary upset.

**Demographic cues that often co-occur**: a parent who was unpredictable, alcoholic, or chronically angry. A childhood where the caregiver's mood determined the safety of the room. A history of people-pleasing. High empathy. A self-concept built around being "the good one" or "the easy one."

## What Joe Hears

The concern presents as being about the other person's anger. Underneath, it is about the speaker's childhood-installed measurement system for their own wrongness. The system uses other-anger as the input. The output is self-condemnation. The system runs automatically and feels like accurate perception rather than like a learned mechanism.

Two things are happening when the other person is angry:

**First**: "I've done something wrong." Not as a logical conclusion. As a body-level certainty. The body has been trained from childhood that the caregiver's anger meant the child was bad. The competent childhood move was to assume responsibility — taking responsibility for caregiver-anger that was not yours was a smaller cost than failing to adjust when adjustment was needed. By adulthood the original conditions have passed but the system still fires. See [[dont-use-others-anger-as-the-signal-youve-done-wrong]].

**Second**: "I'm not safe." The nervous system reads the anger as physical threat even when there is no actual physical danger. The reading is preverbal and bodily. The thinking mind knows there is no danger; the body responds anyway. See [[trauma-is-present-experienced-as-past]].

These two together produce the freeze. Not the freeze of helplessness — the freeze of a child whose only adaptive move was to make themselves small while the danger passed.

The childhood logic is specific: a child cannot afford to believe that their caregiver is irrational or unsafe, because then the child has no one taking care of them. So the child concludes "I did something wrong." The conclusion preserves the relationship with the caregiver at the cost of the child's accurate self-perception. The pattern continues into adulthood because the original cost-benefit calculation that installed it has never been re-examined.

"As a kid, if your parents got angry at you, the assumption is, I've done something wrong. 'Cause the alternative is that my parents are fucking crazy and I have no one to take care of me. So as a kid, you can't think that." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

There is also a third thing happening for the highly empathic person: they are picking up the angry person's underlying fear and reading it as their own fear. The angry person is scared and out of control; the empathic person feels the fear in the room and assumes the fear belongs to them. See [[picking-up-their-fear-and-thinking-its-yours]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same concern goes different directions depending on what Joe reads:

- If [[picking-up-their-fear-and-thinking-its-yours]]: the person is empathic and is feeling the angry person's fear as if it were their own. The coaching move is to name the projection mechanism and help them distinguish.
- If the relationship to their own anger is the bottleneck: the person cannot welcome others' anger because they have not learned to welcome their own. The coaching path moves to anger-release practice. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] and [[joes-angry-executive-commute]].
- If [[self-boundary-mirrors-external-boundary]] is operating: they cannot draw a boundary with the angry person because they tolerate the same behavior from their own internal voice. The work is internal.
- If the angry person is physically dangerous: the coaching path is immediate — leave, call the cops, do whatever is needed for physical safety. The teaching about welcoming anger does not apply when there is physical risk.
- If the angry person is on the narcissistic spectrum: containment, not VIEW. See [[contain-the-narcissist]].
- If the speaker is in a freeze state describing the relationship: capacity is not present to welcome the anger. The first move is leaving, not learning to receive. See [[dont-should-yourself-to-welcome-anger]].

## Coaching Path

The typical flow from this concern. Not a script — a map with branches.

**1. Separate the physical-danger case first.** Ask whether there is any physical danger. If yes, the conversation pivots to safety. "Not okay for you to get hit. Not okay for you to think that you're physically in jeopardy. That's just absolutely not okay. So just leave. Call the cops. Do whatever you have to do to protect yourself." Most of the time the answer is no, and the conversation continues.

**2. Name the measurement system.** The first move is to make visible that the person has been treating the other's anger as the signal of their own wrongness, and that this is a learned mechanism, not direct perception. See [[dont-use-others-anger-as-the-signal-youve-done-wrong]] for the anti-pattern article and the "this is about their trauma, not about me" cognitive substitution.

**3. Move toward their own anger.** The capacity to be with another's anger is downstream of loving their own. The path: [[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[joes-angry-executive-commute]] as the canonical example, [[anger]] as the broader teaching, [[anger-is-love]] as the reframe.

**4. Introduce the boundary option as ground floor.** Before learning to welcome anger, the person needs to know they can always draw a boundary. "You don't ever have to put up with anybody's anger." The empowerment of knowing this is what allows the welcoming-or-boundary choice. See [[come-back-in-20-minutes]] for the specific boundary move that does not read as abandonment.

**5. Introduce the read that the angry person is scared.** Once the person has some access to their own anger, the read changes. The angry person stops being a perpetrator and becomes a person who is scared and out of control. See [[read-anger-and-help-as-fear]].

**6. Introduce the step-in option (only when capacity is present).** Once they can read the angry person as scared, they have the option of stepping in rather than away — naming what the angry person seems to be reaching for (engagement, being seen, the vision being taken seriously) and offering presence in the anger. See [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]] and [[the-silicon-valley-narcissist-who-stopped-yelling]] for the canonical example.

**7. Watch for the should.** If the person tries to skip ahead to welcoming before they have access to their own anger, the body goes into freeze and the practice reinforces the pattern. The capacity arrives as byproduct of doing the underlying work. See [[dont-should-yourself-to-welcome-anger]].

## What This Concern Is Not

This is not "I have an anger problem" — that concern is about the speaker's own anger. This is about the speaker's response to others' anger.

This is not "I'm being verbally abused" — that concern is about a specific pattern of control through threat of anger and warrants its own treatment. See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]]. The current concern is the receiver's pattern; the abuse concern is about whether the dynamic crosses into control.

This is not "I just don't like conflict" — the conflict-avoidance presenting concern is broader. The current concern has a specific shape: the other's anger triggers a wrongness loop in the speaker.
