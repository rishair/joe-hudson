# Am I being selfish? / I don't want to be selfish

## What Someone Says

The concern shows up in several phrasings, sometimes as a direct question, sometimes embedded in the framing of a want:

- "Am I being selfish if I do this?"
- "I don't want to be selfish."
- "Is it selfish to want X?"
- "I know this is selfish, but..."
- "My parents always called me selfish."
- "I should be more selfless."
- "I feel guilty for putting myself first."
- "I don't want to be one of those people who only cares about themselves."
- "I felt like this was self-indulgent."
- "I don't have time for this. I'm a mom, I'm working. I need to be spending time with my team and my family."
- "I gleaned value from providing for others and putting others' needs before myself."

The word "selfish" is usually attached to a specific want: a desire to leave a relationship, to take more money, to set a boundary, to spend time alone, to not show up for a family obligation, to ask for what they want at work.

### The Self-Indulgent Variant

A specific form of this concern shows up when the "selfish" act in question is interior work itself: therapy, coaching, retreats, time spent feeling, time spent attending to one's own state. The framing shifts from "I'm being selfish" to "this is self-indulgent." The substance is the same: a parental voice declaring that attention to self is a moral failing.

This variant is especially common in women trained to derive worth from caretaking, in high-performing professionals whose identity is built on output, and in cultures that valorize sacrifice. The work itself is rejected as a luxury that other people can afford. The rejection is the pattern declaring itself.

"I was extremely resistant... I felt like I don't have the time for this. I'm a mom, I'm working. I need to be spending time with my team and my family. And I know I don't need to dwell on all this stuff." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The recognition that often unlocks the work: the "self-indulgence" was itself the pattern. The aversion to feeling, framed as virtue, was the structure that needed to come down. Carla reports that on the other side, "I wasn't meeting people's needs at all. I wasn't validating their needs at all. I was just trying to fix it all the time." The thing that called itself selfless was the thing that prevented real contact.

## Detection Cues

The concern often presents indirectly. Listen for:

- The word "selfish" used as self-judgment for almost any expression of want or need
- "I feel guilty for wanting..." as a preamble to a clean want
- Listing reasons not to do something where the reasons are all about others' reactions
- Asking permission for self-care or self-direction
- A history of being told by parents that wanting things made them bad
- The framing "I should want X for others, but I want Y for me"
- Wanting to do something the person describes as "selfish" but feels stuck about

## What Joe Hears

The concept of selfishness, as commonly used, is mostly a control mechanism installed in childhood. "Selfish" is what a parent calls a child when the parent wants the child to do something different. The accusation does not point to a moral failing in the child. It points to a frustrated want in the parent.

"Selfishness is what your parents told you you were when they wanted you to do something else." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The child grows up with the label internalized. The adult then feels selfish whenever a want of their own conflicts with someone else's want. The internal voice doing the calling-selfish is the inherited voice of the parent. See [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]] and [[childhood-imprinting]].

There is no useful coaching to be done at the level of "are you actually being selfish?" The question is downstream of the wrong frame. The real coaching is at the level of: what do you actually want, and what would happen if you allowed yourself to want it?

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **The person has a clean want they are calling selfish:** The reframe is to drop the label and follow the want. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]] and [[the-want]]. The coaching path moves toward owning the want, feeling it as a feeling, and noticing how it refines.

- **The person is in craving, not wanting:** The grip, the obsession, the I-must-have-this energy is craving (see [[wanting-vs-craving]]). The "selfish" label is more accurate to the felt sense of craving, but the corrective is not to suppress; it is to drop the attachment so the want can be felt clean.

- **The person is using "I'm not being selfish" as cover for self-abandonment:** They are caretaking and calling it virtue. See [[i-caretake-people]] and [[caring-vs-caretaking]]. The coaching path moves toward the unspoken wants underneath the self-sacrifice.

- **The person was told they were selfish by a parent and the label has not been examined:** The work is to revisit the original moment. The parent's accusation was usually about the parent's want, not the child's character. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

- **The person is using selfishness as a moral cudgel against someone else:** "They're so selfish" usually means "they did something I wanted them to do differently." The mirror is to ask what the speaker wants and is not saying. See [[mirror-the-judgment]].

## What Joe Hears Underneath

Underneath the selfishness question is almost always an unspoken want that the person has not given themselves permission to follow. The work is not to determine whether the want is selfish. The work is to let the person have the want, feel it, and follow it home. The deeper want, fully felt, is almost always generous in a way the surface want is not.

"Eventually you find the deepest want, even close to the deepest want, ends up to be a deeply compassionate thing for the people around you as well." -- Joe Hudson

This is why the coaching does not need to police the surface wants. The wants self-correct as they are followed. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]] and [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]].

## Coaching Path

1. Notice the selfish label and gently surface it: where did this label come from?
2. Drop the label and locate the actual want: what do you want here, before the word "selfish" enters the conversation?
3. Check whether it is wanting or craving (see [[wanting-vs-craving]]).
4. If wanting: follow it. Ask the need-behind-the-want question if helpful (see [[need-behind-the-want]]).
5. If craving: drop the attachment so the want can be felt clean.
6. Trust that the deeper want will be generous. Do not try to install the generosity on top of the surface want.

## What NOT to Do

- Do not validate the selfishness label by debating whether the specific want qualifies as selfish. The debate itself reinforces the frame.
- Do not push the person to be "less selfish." That installs another should. See [[wants-over-shoulds]].
- Do not bypass the surface want by jumping to the spiritual or generous version. See [[spiritual-narcissism]] and [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]].
