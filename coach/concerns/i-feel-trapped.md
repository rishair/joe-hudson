# I feel trapped / They have power over me

## What Someone Says

The specific phrasings vary widely but all share a structural feature: agency is located outside the speaker.

- "I have no choice."
- "I can't stand up for myself because I'll lose my job."
- "If I tell my wife the truth, she'll leave me."
- "My boss has all the power. There's nothing I can do."
- "The system is rigged against me."
- "I'm trapped in this marriage / job / situation."
- "I can't leave because I need the money / I have kids / I'll lose insurance."
- "It's not really up to me. They decide."

In organizational settings:
- "There's nothing I can do, leadership won't change."
- "I have no power here."
- "The politics make it impossible."

## Detection Cues

The grammatical signature: "I can't" or "I have to" used to describe circumstances rather than physical impossibility. "I can't tell my boss" usually means "the consequence of telling my boss feels intolerable." That is different from "I cannot lift this building."

Also: the consistent location of agency outside the self. They, them, the system, the circumstances, the situation. The "I" appears only as the recipient of what is being done.

Often paired with [[fear-triangle]] roles, especially victim, but sometimes savior ("I have to keep everyone happy or it falls apart") or bully ("I have to control this or it falls apart").

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this is a choice that the person is not seeing as a choice. The person is choosing to avoid a feared consequence, but experiencing it as having no choice at all.

"Every time that you feel like you've been disempowered or that someone has power over you and you can't be true to yourself, then what's actually happening is that you are choosing to avoid a, a potential bad consequence. And that's a choice that you're making." -- Joe Hudson, Empower over Power

The feeling of being trapped is not the reality of being trapped. It is the felt consequence of unconsciously trading authenticity for safety. The trade is real. The trap is felt. But the person is the one making the trade.

Underneath the unconscious trade is helplessness, and underneath the helplessness is shame or blame. The person does not want to feel the helplessness, so they organize their life to avoid actions that would activate it. The avoidance becomes the cage. See [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same statement can lead in different directions depending on what Joe notices:

- **If the person is in [[fear-triangle]] as victim:** the helplessness is being used to keep others responsible for changing the situation. Joe will name the feeling of helplessness and invite the person to feel it as their own, not as evidence of being trapped.

- **If the person is in [[fear-triangle]] as savior:** the obligation is the cage. Joe will ask what would happen if they stopped saving. The fear underneath is usually that without their saving, they would not matter.

- **If the person is in [[fear-triangle]] as bully:** the control is the cage. Joe will ask what would happen if they stopped controlling. The fear underneath is usually feeling alone.

- **If the person is performing competence while saying they are trapped:** the disempowerment is unconscious. Joe will reflect back the contradiction.

- **If the person has named a specific feared consequence:** Joe can move directly to "what if you acted exactly how you want to act and saw if it happened?"

## Coaching Path

1. Locate the choice that is not being seen as a choice. What action would the person take if there were no feared consequence?

2. Name the feared consequence. What specifically does the person fear would happen?

3. Address the feeling underneath the feared consequence, not the consequence itself. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]]. The fear is the helplessness or shame that would arrive.

4. Move toward the helplessness. See [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]]. The capacity returns when the feeling moves through.

5. Test the prediction. See [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]]. 8 out of 10 times, the prediction is wrong.

6. Establish the [[empowered-vs-power]] distinction. The constraints are real. The stance inside them is the person's.

7. Where applicable, name the [[fear-triangle]] role being occupied. Each role is a strategy to obtain power over a situation by avoiding the helplessness underneath.

## What Joe Does Not Do

He does not validate the trap. He does not say "yes, you really are stuck." Even when the constraints are real (Mandela's were extreme), the location of agency is misidentified by the person. Validating the trap reinforces the misidentification.

He does not strategize about how to manipulate the external situation to avoid the consequence. The work is internal.

He does not offer false reassurance that the feared consequence will not happen. Sometimes it will. The 2 out of 10 is real. The empowerment is in being willing for it to happen, not in being assured it will not.

## The Stance Itself Is the Problem

The trapped stance is not just a misperception. It is an active organization of the person's life that produces the very dynamics they describe. See [[victim-stance-invites-abuse]]. To occupy the position of someone who has no choices is to become valuable to two specific groups (other people without choices, and people who want to take advantage), and those are the people who will show up. The trap is partly self-generated by the stance.

The mechanism is more specific than "you attract what you embody." It is perceptual. See [[oppressors-target-the-oppressible]]. People who oppress have learned to read the behavioral signature of chronic trapped-ness with high precision. The crosswalk study Joe cites demonstrates that aggressive people, given footage of pedestrians, agree on who would make a good target. The signal is detectable. The trapped person is broadcasting; the matching person finds them.

This is also why Joe's observation holds that "99.999999% of the time when I'm talking to somebody, they think they're stuck, but they're not at all stuck." The constraints are felt, not actual. The Ukrainian soldier in the trench is more constrained than the office worker behind the keyboard and feels less trapped. The trapped-feeling is the signal; the situation is rarely what makes it.

## The No-Win Childhood Origin

For people whose trapped-feeling is chronic and pre-dates any specific adult situation, the origin is almost always the no-win childhood pattern in [[freeze-became-the-best-childhood-strategy]]. The child was placed in situations where any move would have produced attack, and freezing turned out to be the lowest-cost response. The freeze became the trained response to any situation with a similar felt-shape.

"Often had a childhood where they were put in no-win situations where they couldn't get out of the situation, meaning that they couldn't be themselves and please their parents, or that they had to be a very particular way, or they suffered some sort of abuse that was no-win. And so all they could do was just freeze." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The trapped feeling in the adult is the residue. The brain has been trained to collapse the option-space to two-bad-choices, because that was the actual structure of the original situation. The adult lives in a world with many options but cannot see them, because the option-space-collapse runs faster than the conscious recognition of what is available.

## The Self-Oppression Layer

A specific layer that distinguishes chronic trapped-feeling from acute crisis: the trapped person is, by their own thoughts, doing to themselves what they are afraid of having done to them. They are removing love from themselves to avoid having love removed by others. They are silencing themselves to avoid being silenced by others. They are attacking themselves to avoid being attacked by others.

The avoidance has become the doing. See [[feeling-stuck-is-self-oppression]]. The intellectual recognition of this is part of the work; once seen, the rational basis for continuing the avoidance dissolves, because the catastrophe being avoided is already happening, being run by the self.

The exit move is [[be-yourself-despite-consequences-undoes-stuckness]]. Restore self-expression with willingness for the consequence. The doing is what changes the configuration. The trapped feeling lifts as the actual self-suppression decreases.

## When Obligation Is the Cage

A specific form of this concern: the person feels trapped inside a relationship, marriage, or role they cannot leave because of obligation. The obligation is often disguised as love ("I have to stay because I love them") but functions as the bars of the cage. This is the [[conditional-clauses-of-heart-closure]] producing the trappedness it was supposed to prevent.

The diagnostic question: if you let yourself love this person without obligation, would the trappedness change? Usually yes — the trappedness was produced by the obligation, not by the love. The path through is [[obligation-cuts-off-love]]: notice that obligation is not love and is in fact what is keeping you stuck. The exit is [[say-no-to-find-the-want]] and [[i-get-to-vs-i-have-to]].

The shape this often takes: a long marriage where both partners have organized around mutual obligation, walking on eggshells, performing care, and slowly losing the love. See [[walking-on-eggshells]] and [[the-built-up-resentment-story]]. The trap is real but it is self-constructed and self-maintained. The structure could change.
