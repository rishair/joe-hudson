# How do I stop seeking approval / I care too much what other people think of me

## What Someone Says

The concern usually arrives in self-aware form. The person knows they are doing it. They have noticed the pattern. They are asking for the way out.

- "I care too much what other people think of me."
- "I keep seeking approval from everyone and I want to stop."
- "I always want to be liked. I want everybody to like me."
- "I'm always trying to be the good one."
- "I keep performing for people."
- "I keep wondering what they think of me."
- "I'm a chronic people pleaser. How do I stop?"
- "I keep looking for someone to tell me I'm okay."
- "I keep waiting for approval before I can move."
- "Every conversation I'm tracking how I'm landing."
- "How do I stop questioning if I'm showing up just to be heard, or if I truly have something to say?"
- "I don't want to speak just to be acknowledged. Do I actually have something valuable to add?"
- "How do I know when what I have to say is worth saying?"

The concern often co-occurs with a sense of exhaustion or hollowness. The approval, when received, does not satisfy. The person reports receiving compliments and immediately needing the next one. The hunger does not fill.

## Detection Cues

**Surface signals:**
- Self-deprecating responses to compliments ("oh no, you're being too kind," "no, you're the [trait]")
- Sentences that hedge in real time ("I don't know if this is right, but...," "this is probably stupid, but...")
- A strong concern about how they came across after a conversation ends
- Frequent checking in: "did that sound okay?", "was that too much?", "did I do that right?"
- The person watches the listener's face more than they listen to themselves speak
- They describe a long list of things they did to be liked at work, in their family, in friendships

**Indirect signals:**
- A high achiever who reports no felt confidence inside the achievements (see [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]])
- Burnout from a long history of monitoring everyone's mood (see [[caring-what-others-think-is-the-burnout-engine]])
- A pattern of swapping scorecards (money to status to likes to fame) without the chase ending (see [[i-need-a-scorecard]])
- A person who is sharp and effective in one specific room (men's group, therapy, with their kids) but flat or anxious everywhere else
- The person describes feeling pushed away by people they were trying to please

**The most telling tell:** when complimented in the room, the person deflects. The deflection is the live diagnostic. The approval is being asked for and refused in the same motion.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the approval-seeking is a specific structural move: the person has stopped listening to themselves and is listening to others *for the answer to whether they exist as okay*. The approval is doing the job that self-listening would do, except it cannot do that job because the answer can always be revoked.

Two specific mechanisms are operating:

**The seeker has stopped listening.** When you are scanning the other person for approval, you cannot hear them. You are not in the conversation. You are in a meta-position, monitoring how you are being received. The listener feels the absence. They feel managed. They feel the extraction attempt. See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]].

**The approval is being used to avoid an emotion.** Underneath "I care what they think" is usually a specific feeling the person does not want to feel. Often: a sense of not-enough-ness, smallness, the felt sense of being judged. Putting the other person above or below yourself is a strategy to not have to feel that. See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]].

The concern is not a moral failing. It is a structural workaround for an avoided feeling. The person built the structure when they were young because the feeling was unbearable then. The structure stayed in place after the feeling became bearable. The work is not to suppress the approval-seeking. The work is to feel what the approval-seeking has been protecting them from.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person deflects a compliment in the session: the live read is [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]]. The deflection is the data. Joe will name it and have them try receiving in the moment. This is often the entry point into the work — the move [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] is available immediately.
- If the person describes high accomplishment with no felt confidence: the read is [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]]. The achievements were running on the engine of approval-seeking; the engine prevented confidence from arriving.
- If the person is burned out from years of monitoring: the read is [[caring-what-others-think-is-the-burnout-engine]]. The monitoring itself is the burnout fuel, not the workload.
- If the person describes swapping scorecards (money to likes to fame) and the chase continues: the read is [[i-need-a-scorecard]]. The substrate keeps changing; the function does not.
- If the person is over-explaining or rehearsing how to say things: the read is [[chasing-understanding-as-control]] or [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]]. The chase for approval is operating in the conversation itself.
- If the person says they only feel confident in one specific small context: [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]] — the confidence lives precisely where the approval-engine is off.

## Coaching Path

A typical flow when this concern surfaces:

1. **Notice the live deflection.** If the person deflects a compliment in the session, name it. "You just deflected that. Can you let it land?" The work begins in the body, not in concept. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]].

2. **Reveal the push-away.** Show the person the mechanism: seeking approval pushes people away because the seeker has stopped listening. Use the inversion question: "Think about a person who is desperate for your approval. What happens inside you?" The answer is some version of "I want to get away." See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]].

3. **Move into the receiving.** Set up the practice of letting compliments land somatically rather than deflecting them. Name the deflection as the smallness-defense it is. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] and the framing in [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]].

4. **Locate the emotion the approval is covering.** Ask: "Right now, when you notice you're seeking approval, what's the feeling underneath that you don't want to feel?" Stay with what surfaces. Often it is a small-self felt sense, or shame, or the fear of being unloved. See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]].

5. **Pivot to self-approval as the central question.** Once the emotion has been touched, introduce the inversion: "What if the question isn't 'do they approve?' but 'do I approve?'" The shift is the work. See [[chase-your-own-approval]].

6. **Name the childhood imprint, briefly.** Often the approval-engine was installed by a specific parental dynamic — rules that changed, reward channels that were inconsistent, love that was contingent on performance. Surface it as context, not as the work. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

7. **Install the daily practice.** The receiving of compliments needs reps. Suggest [[two-person-gratitude-practice]] or daily noticing of the deflection impulse. The capacity grows with use. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] for the structural rationale.

8. **Install the speak-truth practice.** This is the structural cure, not a side practice. See [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]]. The conventional framing (first stop needing approval, then speak truth) is reversed. The actual mechanism: speaking your truth is what stops the approval-seeking. The speaking is itself the self-approval, because voicing your perception is treating it as valuable. Once the perception is treated as valuable from inside, the external validator is no longer required. The operational form: [[speak-truth-twice-a-day]] — say two slightly-uncomfortable true things a day, from an open heart in a grounded body, and stay in the conversation after.

## Branching Reads

- If the concern is framed as "I'm a people pleaser" with the caretaking flavor: the move is often through [[i-caretake-people]]. The caretaking is the approval engine in service form.
- If the concern is framed as "I'm worried about offending people": the move is often through [[im-afraid-of-offending-people]]. The conflict avoidance is the approval engine in conflict-avoidance form.
- If the concern arrives via burnout: the move is often through [[im-burned-out]] and [[caring-what-others-think-is-the-burnout-engine]].
- If the concern arrives via scorecards: the move is often through [[i-need-a-scorecard]] — the chase for likes/status/recognition is the approval engine externalized.
- If the concern is framed via confidence: the move is often through [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]].
- If the concern is framed as "do I have something valuable to say": see the do-I-have-something-valuable-to-say variant below.

## The "Do I Have Something Valuable to Say" Variant

A specific framing of this concern: the person is not asking whether they should stop caring; they are asking how to know whether their contribution is "valuable enough" to make. The presenting form is the question of worth, not the question of approval. But the structure is identical: the speaker has outsourced the question of whether their voice is allowed to a metric they are trying to satisfy from outside.

Specific phrasings:
- "How do I stop questioning if I'm showing up just to have my voice be heard, or if I truly have the thing to say?"
- "Do I actually have something valuable to add, or am I just trying to be heard?"
- "I don't want to speak just to be acknowledged."

The corrective is structural: the "valuable enough" frame is unanswerable from inside. Whose input is valuable? What percentage? The person already knows the question cannot be answered ("it's perception"). The fact that they keep asking it anyway is the diagnostic — the question is functioning as a permission gate that will never open, because it is structurally incapable of opening.

The session move sequence for this variant:

1. **Refuse the moral collapse on "just to be heard."** "What's the problem with showing up just to be heard?" See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] and [[keep-asking-whats-the-problem-with-that]]. The "just" is doing the work of the prohibition. Removing the "just" exposes that wanting to be heard is not actually wrong — and that the prohibition is the unexamined material.

2. **Show the unanswerability of the worth question.** "What percentage of people's input is valuable?" The person cannot answer. Joe: "Even more of a reason. How do you know? Who are you to judge if your input is valuable?" The point is not that the person is incompetent to judge. The point is that the judgment is being made on a non-existent scale. The retrospective evidence Joe uses: "I say shit off the cuff and people come to me 3 years later and they're like, oh my God, that thing you said to me. And there's other shit that I'm like, I just laid down some profound shit and it doesn't mean shit to anybody." Reception is not predictable from inside the speaker. See [[simultaneous-reception-is-uncontrollable]].

3. **Use the cosmic/mortality frame to dissolve self-importance.** "We're all dust in the wind. None of us are gonna be remembered in 100 years. What makes it that you have to be so fucking important?" This is the targeted deployment of [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] in the approval-seeking context. The point is not nihilism. The point is the inversion: the urgency about being valuable is itself the selfishness the speaker fears. See [[the-importance-itself-is-the-selfishness]].

4. **Name reception as inherently mixed and uncontrollable.** "Right now somebody on this call is having an epiphany because you and I are having this conversation. Right now two people are fucking bored. What the fuck? This is how life is." The frame: every spoken moment lands as epiphany for some and as bored-irritation for others, simultaneously. There is no version where everyone is heightened. The wish to be only-valuable is a wish for an unavailable configuration of reality. See [[simultaneous-reception-is-uncontrollable]].

5. **Have the speaker name the controlling wish out loud, looking the coach in the eye.** "Look me right in the eye and say, 'I want to control what you think of me, Joe,' and see what it feels like." The move is the absurdity-audible move: the position becomes felt as absurd at the moment it is voiced to a real human face. See [[look-me-in-the-eye-and-name-the-controlling-wish]].

The session typically closes laughing. The laugh is the integration — the body has registered that the position the person was holding does not survive being spoken in the room. The shift is not "I no longer care what others think." The shift is "the urgency about being valuable has been seen as an absurd configuration, and the laugh dissolves it."

## The Approval-Seeking-Toward-Authority Variant

A specific intensification of this concern: when the approval-seeking is directed at an authority figure (boss, mentor, parent, spiritual teacher), the dynamic acquires the additional features described in [[authority-issues]] and Position 2 of [[the-four-authority-positions]]. The approval-seeker organizes their behavior to earn the authority's approval and over time becomes resentful — because they have been sacrificing parts of themselves to get it.

The specific move that dissolves this variant fastest:

"The best thing to do if you really want to dissolve that quickly is to say things that you think the authority figure doesn't want to hear from you that you think are really valuable and just say those, start small and then get bigger." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The structural insight: in every C-level team around a CEO, there is one person who says the hard truth — and that person is the closest to the CEO and the one most trusted. The fear of speaking the truth is almost always wrong. The truth-teller becomes the thought partner, not the fired person.

The qualifier: the truth has to be delivered from confidence, not from fear. A scared truth-teller will not be received. See [[name-the-authoritys-fear]] for the move and [[baboon-leadership-study]] for the underlying principle about why directness gets received and uncertainty does not.

## The Three Blocks Operating Underneath

When the concern is presented, three structural blocks are typically operating that prevent the simple corrective from working. See [[three-blocks-to-speaking-truth]]:

- **Doubt.** The person does not trust their own perception. They are referencing outside themselves. See [[doubt-requires-closed-heart]] for the structural account. The doubt frequently traces to the [[what-did-i-do-wrong-response-to-aggression]] pattern, particularly in people with a history of alcoholic or volatile parents.
- **Fear of consequences.** The person can see the truth clearly but is stopped by the projected outcome. The corrective: shift from single-bet evaluation to portfolio evaluation. See [[decisions-as-portfolio]] and [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]] for the long-arc shape.
- **Justifying conditioning.** The person is mistaking a conditioned reactivity for their truth. The discrimination is somatic: heart closes for conditioning, heart opens for intuition. See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] and [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the misuse.

Identifying which block is dominant for the specific person determines the corrective sequence.

## The Impossible Target

A structural fact often hidden underneath the concern: the approval-seeker is trying to make other people happy, and this is impossible. The target is moving (the same person is happy with the same action one day and unhappy the next) and depends on inputs you do not control (their state when receiving your action). The impossibility is what keeps the doubt loop running indefinitely. See [[you-cannot-make-people-happy]]. Naming the impossibility is often the move that releases the chronic doubter.

## The Foundational Installation Underneath

Beneath the approval-seeking engine there is almost always a deeper installation: the belief that value (and lovability) must be earned through doing. The person has internalized a childhood equation — do enough chores, get good enough grades, perform the right way, be a certain version of yourself, and you will be loved. The equation does not work, but the body keeps running it because it was installed before the body could choose not to.

The principle [[value-is-intrinsic-not-earned]] is the corrective. The recognition: value does not require performance. A baby is valuable. A 3-year-old is valuable. The annoying 3-year-old is as valuable as the cute one. The 3-year-old does nothing to deserve their value. Neither does the adult. The strategy of doing-to-be-valued has been operating against a structural fact about humans that the body already knows about other humans but has been refusing to apply to the self.

The related principle [[doing-things-for-love-is-prostitution]] names the structural reason the strategy cannot succeed: love produced by performance is not love but transaction. The performer remains unloved while the behavior is paid for in love. The person performing is invisible behind the performance. The receiver loves the behavior, not the person. The recognition often produces both grief (the years of wasted self-abandonment) and relief (the unwinnable game can be released).

For the coaching path: when the approval-seeker is ready to feel rather than to understand, the experiment [[turn-the-love-on-yourself-what-did-you-do]] installs the recognition in the body. The practitioner generates love for a being they love unconditionally (a kid, a dog, an infant), turns the love on themselves, and then is asked: what did you need to do to deserve that? The body answers. The recognition is the unlock.

The related installation about reputation — [[reputation-control-is-unwinnable]] — is the relational form of the same principle. The strategy of curating how others see you is structurally doomed (the signal you intend is not the signal received; the Porsche meant to signal power lands as overcompensation to half the audience). The alternative — be yourself, accept the reputation that arrives — produces a reputation that is easy to maintain because the maintenance is just being yourself. Both principles compound: the person who has dropped the doing-to-be-valued installation can also drop the reputation-management strategy, because both engines run on the same delegated-validation premise.

## The Present-Moment Cure (The Feeling You're Chasing Is Always Available Now)

A specific operational frame that often produces the fastest live shift in the approval-seeker: the recognition that the feeling they have been chasing through others' approval is identical to the feeling produced by being-good-with-self-right-now. The chase has been misdirected at an external substrate when the actual substrate is internal and always present. See [[what-you-seek-from-approval-is-self-presence]] for the structural principle and [[what-do-you-need-to-be-good-with-yourself-right-now]] for the operational question.

The move in session:

1. Ask the question with the present-moment constraint: "What do you need to do to be good with yourself right now? Right now, in this moment, what's required to just be good with yourself?" The "right now" is load-bearing — it closes the routes the mind would normally take into the past or the future.

2. Notice when the answer goes into the future: "I have to do X, then I'll be good." The future-answer is the diagnostic that the mind has left the present, which is itself the pattern. Redirect: "That's the future. What about right now?"

3. When the person touches the small felt-sense of being-good-with-self, make the follow-up move: "How much of what you're looking for from others is this? How much of what you're looking for by not getting the judgment, by not getting the criticism, is actually this feeling?" The retroactive recognition is the unlock.

4. Address the predictable objection ("but I still have to act, I have a meeting, I have to make money, I have to be a parent"): "What if you did all that from this space? What are you chasing if you have that?" The objection treats the principle as if it requires withdrawal; the answer reframes the source of action without requiring withdrawal.

The structural insight that makes this work: external approval has structural unavailability. The same person who approves today may withhold tomorrow. The substrate keeps changing. Internal good-with-self has structural availability — it requires only the relocation of the question. The recognition of this asymmetry is what dissolves the chase that willpower cannot.

This frame is the present-moment, in-this-session version of [[chase-your-own-approval]]. The two pair: chase-your-own-approval is the long-arc orientation; the present-moment cure is the in-this-moment instantiation that touches the felt-sense in the room.

## The Engage-the-Critic Move (When the Approval-Avoidance Engine Is Criticism-Avoidance)

A specific operational form of the corrective for approval-seekers whose chase is primarily driven by trying to avoid criticism: respond to criticism with questions rather than defense. See [[ask-the-criticizer-what-they-mean]].

The mechanism uses the structural observation that under most criticism is "I want to be seen." When the recipient of criticism asks genuine questions ("What do you mean by that? What am I missing? What would you have me do?"), most of the criticism dissolves — not because the recipient agreed but because the critic felt heard.

This move is paired with the present-moment cure because both require the same prerequisite: being-in-yourself. The approval-seeker who has not located the internal good-with-self cannot execute the engage-the-critic move because they are too threatened by the criticism to be in genuine wonder about it. The present-moment cure provides the ground; the engage-the-critic move is what becomes available from that ground.

For the approval-seeker, this move is particularly important because the chase has been organized around criticism-avoidance. Once criticism becomes a thing they can engage with rather than something they have to prevent, a large portion of the approval-seeking behavior loses its purpose. The engine reorganizes.

## Why Compliments Alone Won't Fix This

A structural fact that often clarifies the work for the approval-seeker: external compliments cannot do the self-esteem-building work the approval-seeker has been hoping they would. The verdict that determines whether a compliment lands is internal, and external compliments get filtered through that internal verdict. This is why decades of seeking approval do not produce the felt-confidence the approval-seeker has been chasing. See [[compliments-dont-build-self-esteem-competence-does]] for the structural principle and [[chase-your-own-approval]] for the corrective relocation of the verdict.

This matters for the coaching path because it dissolves a hidden hypothesis the approval-seeker is often still running: "if I just receive enough genuine appreciation, I'll finally feel okay." The hypothesis is structurally wrong. The receiving work (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]) does important work — it dissolves the deflective identity — but the additive route the hypothesis assumes does not exist. The substrate change is the dissolution and the relocation of the verdict, not the accumulation of validation.

## The Responsibility-Avoidance Form of Deflection

A specific form of compliment-deflection often surfaces in approval-seekers who have done the basic receiving work and still find certain compliments will not land. The deflection is preserving the current identity not against the inflation of ego but against the responsibility the corresponding identity expansion would carry. See [[the-compliment-threatens-the-identity-expansion-required]].

The diagnostic question: "If you fully accepted this compliment, what would you now be on the hook for that you currently aren't?" The answer is often specific (learning math, being the caring partner, leading the team) and reveals that the deflection has been doing protective work the standard "let it in" instruction does not address. The conversation can then move to whether the surfaced responsibility is actually unwanted or whether the unwanted-ness was itself a defense.

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not [[i-caretake-people]] — the caretaking is one strategy for getting approval, but the concern is more general. Caretaking is a behavior; approval-seeking is the underlying engine.
- It is not [[im-afraid-of-offending-people]] — the offending-people concern is approval-seeking expressed as conflict avoidance. The broader approval concern includes offending-avoidance but is not limited to it.
- It is not [[im-burned-out]] — burnout is a downstream consequence of running the approval engine for years, but the approval concern can be present without burnout being the presenting symptom.
- It is not [[i-need-a-scorecard]] — the scorecard is the approval engine made measurable. The approval concern is the underlying hunger; the scorecard is the costume.
- It is not [[how-do-i-get-people-to-like-me]] when held as a craft question. The two can present with the same words. The disambiguator is the body and the urgency. If the person is asking the craft question (no desperation, no chronic chase, no panic about specific people not liking them), the answer is the direct craft answer in [[we-like-people-who-are-interested-in-us]] — be interested in them, listen well, ask how/what questions. If the person is asking the desperation question (the engine of approval-seeking is operating underneath), the craft answer cannot land cleanly because the listening will be converted into a performance for the desired outcome. In the desperation case, the work is the deeper work outlined above, not the craft answer. Both concerns share the same surface words and different undersides; the coach must read which one is active before choosing the path.
