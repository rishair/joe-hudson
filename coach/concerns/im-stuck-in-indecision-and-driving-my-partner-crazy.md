# I'm stuck in indecision about my partner and I'm driving them crazy

## What Someone Says

- "I am completely driving my boyfriend crazy. And I drive myself crazy and I would like to stop in some way, but I really, I've been stuck in this for like 2 years."
- "He's very like, I want to move forward with you, let's go. And I'm like, I can't break up with you. Also not going forward with it. Are you the right person?"
- "I feel very frozen around it. And I know that it's really hurting him."
- "I want to be good to him, but it's hard."
- "I just want to stop doing things that upset him."
- "I would identify as like a codependent person and I go to meetings about it and stuff."
- "He is in a lot of pain a lot of the time."

The presentation is distinct from "should I stay with my ex?" The person is currently in the relationship and is not framing the question as a deliberation they want help finishing. They are framing the question as: *my chronic indecision is the symptom; it is hurting my partner; please help me stop being this way.* The decision-frame is still present, but it has metabolized into a felt sense of paralysis rather than a presenting deliberation.

## Detection Cues

The structural signature: a long-running stuck place in a current relationship, presented as a symptom (the indecision, the paralysis, the freezing) rather than as a deliberation (the choice between staying and leaving).

Specific surface markers:

- The duration is named in years, not weeks. "I've been stuck in this for 2 years." The number is part of the presentation; the chronic-ness is on the table.
- The partner's pain is named explicitly as part of the concern. "He's in a lot of pain a lot of the time." "I know it's really hurting him." The person is not separating their concern from the partner's experience of it.
- The framing centers the *partner*, not the speaker. "I'm driving my boyfriend crazy" — not "I'm in pain about this indecision." The hurt being described is the partner's hurt; the speaker's relationship to their own pain is offstage. See [[presenting-the-other-persons-pain-instead-of-your-own]].
- A want to "stop" — to stop being indecisive, to stop doing things that upset him, to stop driving him crazy. The want is to stop a behavior the speaker is identifying as the problem, not to feel something they have not yet felt.
- Self-identification with the codependence label, often with the disclaimer of going to meetings or having done work on it. The label has not produced the change; the pattern is in plain view to the speaker and still running.
- The body language of paralysis: "frozen," "stuck," "can't move." Not active deliberation, not weighing pros and cons in the moment; something more like a freeze response.
- A history that suggests the pattern predates this relationship. "With this boyfriend, 2 years" — but when asked when they first drove people crazy in general, the answer is "Oh yeah, related things for a long time."
- A pattern of acute earlier-life manifestations now turned chronic. Joe's question — "Would you drive them crazy in an acute fashion? And now it's kind of chronic." — surfaces the shift from sudden breakups with no notice to the current chronic limbo. The pattern is the same; the form has hardened.

Indirect markers:

- The person says they want to "stop" but does not name what they would feel instead.
- The relationship is described entirely through how it lands on the partner, not through what the speaker is experiencing.
- A sense of moral failing or shame about the indecision itself ("I want to stop doing things that upset him") — the speaker is treating their own response as the malfunction.

This concern is structurally connected to [[i-caretake-people]] and [[should-i-stay-with-my-ex]], but the presentation is distinct enough to warrant its own entry point. The speaker is not asking the coach to help them decide. They are asking the coach to help them stop being broken in a way that is hurting someone else.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the presented symptom is a configuration with three avoided emotions and one structural avoidance underneath them all.

The presented symptom — chronic indecision — is doing work. It is the visible form of a long-running self-abandonment pattern in which the speaker has been managing the partner's emotional state instead of inhabiting their own. The freezing is not a malfunction. It is the precise shape of a configuration in which any move toward presence would require facing a feeling the speaker has been organizing their life to avoid.

The three avoided emotions Joe surfaces in this presentation:

1. **Shame.** "I feel a lot of shame. I'm scared that he's going to be mad at me." The shame is the inward form — see [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] — of anger that has no outward channel.
2. **Anger at self.** This is the same energy as the shame, structurally; the speaker registers it as a distinct experience because it sometimes shows up as "oh, fuck, I really fucked up" rather than as the diffuse not-okay of shame proper.
3. **Anger at the partner.** "Mad at him because I'm like, can you just calm down?" The anger that wants outward direction but has no permitted target.

Underneath all three is the actual avoided thing: **the heartbreak of his pain.** The sadness of being with someone whose hurt you cannot fix, cannot manage away, cannot prevent. The grief of witnessing pain that exists whether or not you are skilled enough to cause it to be otherwise.

The configuration: the speaker has organized their entire response into one of three options — anxiously try to please, get angry at self, get angry at partner — and all three are forms of leaving. None of the three is being-with-the-pain. See [[pleasing-is-leaving-just-like-anger]]. The freezing is what happens when none of the three feels acceptable in the moment and the only remaining move is to stop moving at all.

The deeper structural pattern: the speaker is using the *partner's anger* as the signal that they have done something wrong. See [[dont-use-others-anger-as-the-signal-youve-done-wrong]]. This is a childhood-installed measurement system — likely traceable to a parent whose state was unpredictable and whose anger was the most consequential variable in the speaker's young environment. The adult version of the system still runs: the speaker reads any partner anger as data about their own wrongness, which keeps the management active, which keeps the speaker out of themselves.

The pattern is older than the current relationship. The earlier acute form — "everything's fine, then break up with no notice" — and the current chronic form are the same configuration in two costumes. The "everything's fine" was the management; the sudden break was the only available exit. The chronic indecision now is the same management without the available exit. The configuration is the work; the relationship is the venue.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same presentation can lead in different directions depending on what shows up:

- **If the person frames the request around the partner ("I'm driving him crazy") rather than around their own pain:** see [[presenting-the-other-persons-pain-instead-of-your-own]]. Joe names this directly and asks what made the person phrase it that way. The framing itself is the diagnostic. The answer ("the stuff around him feels more painful... I feel a lot of shame... I'm scared he's going to be mad at me") surfaces the management dynamic and the shame in plain view. See [[what-made-you-phrase-it-around-them]].

- **If the person describes "cycling" between two responses to the partner's anger:** Joe surfaces the third option that completes the set. "Shame is one of them. What's the other thing you cycle between?" Once both anger-at-self and anger-at-partner are named, Joe names the third option (anxiously trying to please) and the configuration becomes visible: three options, all of which are leaving. See [[pleasing-is-leaving-just-like-anger]].

- **If the person treats anxiously-trying-to-please as the obvious right answer ("it's more comfortable"):** Joe doesn't accept the comfort framing. He names the equivalence: "You're not there in either way." This is the move that takes the comfortable option off the table by revealing that it is doing the same thing the angry options are doing. The speaker stops being able to call the pleasing better.

- **If the person describes their childhood pattern of using the other person's anger as the signal they did wrong:** see [[dont-use-others-anger-as-the-signal-youve-done-wrong]]. Joe surfaces this through a live demonstration: he role-plays the partner getting angry and instructs the speaker on the specific cognitive move ("this is his trauma, this isn't about me"). See [[apply-trauma-lens-during-role-play]] and [[become-the-authority-figure]].

- **If the person says they want to "be good to him":** Joe doesn't validate the framing. "I don't see you not being good to him. I just see you avoiding the sadness that comes with his pain." The reframe takes the moral charge out of the situation and locates the actual work: the avoided sadness, not the failure of goodness.

- **If the person is identifying as codependent and going to meetings:** Joe registers this as data — the pattern is in plain view to the speaker but the label has not produced movement. He works at the level of the configuration rather than the label. The naming has done its job; the body work has not.

## Coaching Path

The path is compressed in this presentation. It can land in 9-10 minutes when the configuration is visible quickly.

1. **Surface the framing as the diagnostic.** The person framed it about the partner, not themselves. Name that and ask what made them frame it that way. See [[what-made-you-phrase-it-around-them]]. The answer surfaces the shame and the fear of the partner's anger in the first turn.

2. **Reframe the unit of work.** "Maybe we work on that instead of the indecision because it seems like that's kind of the core." The presented symptom (indecision) is moved off the table; the underlying pattern (the codependent management) is now the work. See [[reframe-the-work]].

3. **Surface the avoided emotions.** "What do you have to feel when he's pissed?" See [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-they-re-pissed]]. The question names the consequence-feeling that the management is preventing. The person typically names shame first. Joe asks for the other one ("what's the other thing you cycle between?") and surfaces anger.

4. **Name the trilemma and the underneath.** "You either have to feel anger towards self or anger towards him. Or you have to be codependent." The three options are now on the table. The codependent option is then named as also a form of leaving — see [[pleasing-is-leaving-just-like-anger]]. With all three options revealed as forms of leaving, the question becomes: what is being avoided that requires all this leaving?

5. **Surface the signal-system.** "Somehow in your mind, the way you know if you've done something wrong is if somebody else is angry." See [[dont-use-others-anger-as-the-signal-youve-done-wrong]]. The naming makes visible the childhood-installed measurement system. The speaker can see that the system is not just bad in this relationship — it is the engine of the entire configuration.

6. **Live demonstration via role-play.** Joe role-plays the partner getting angry while instructing the speaker to apply a specific cognitive lens: "you're going to literally have the thought process that is, oh, this is about his trauma. This isn't about me. That's all you're going to think." See [[apply-trauma-lens-during-role-play]] and [[become-the-authority-figure]]. The live application surfaces the underlying feeling: the heartbreak of the partner's pain that the speaker has been organizing the whole configuration to avoid.

7. **Land the actual work.** "Your job is to, when you can, be with this pain, and when you can't, leave. But everything else is just the whole codependence, all that is just an avoidance to not be there with that. That in him and that in yourself." See [[your-job-is-to-be-with-the-pain-or-leave]]. The work is named. It is not deciding whether to stay. It is increasing the capacity to be with the sadness of the partner's pain, and leaving when that capacity is not available in the moment.

## What Joe Does Not Do

He does not help the person stop being indecisive. The indecision is the symptom of the configuration; addressing the indecision directly would reinforce the framing that the indecision is the problem.

He does not adjudicate the relationship. He does not weigh in on whether the boyfriend is the right person, whether the relationship should continue, whether the two years are evidence of something fundamental. The relationship is the venue; the configuration is the work.

He does not validate the goodness of the speaker's intention to "be good to him." The goodness framing is the cover for the avoidance. Joe reframes it: "I don't see you not being good to him." The reframe removes the moral charge and locates the actual missing thing — being with the sadness.

He does not give the person more tools to manage the partner's emotional state. The whole management is the avoidance. Better management would just be more avoidance with more polish.

He does not extend the session to do the full ancestry work on the childhood imprint that installed the configuration. In the source session he names the imprint compactly — "the way you know if you've done something wrong is if somebody else is angry" — and works with it directly through the role-play. The childhood origin is implied but not excavated; the body-level work in the present is the lever.
