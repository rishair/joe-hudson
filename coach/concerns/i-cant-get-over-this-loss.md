# I can't get over this loss / I should be over this by now

## What Someone Says

The phrasings cluster around two contradictory pulls: I am still grieving (which feels like failure), and I do not want to be still grieving (which feels like the goal). Sometimes both are present in the same sentence.

- "I should be over this by now."
- "I don't know why I'm still crying about this."
- "It's been [months/years] and I'm still not okay."
- "I want to move on but I can't."
- "I keep dating people just like my ex."
- "I haven't even grieved this and it's been six months."
- "Everyone says I should be feeling better."
- "I don't know how to grieve this."
- "I feel guilty for still being sad."
- "I feel guilty for not being sad enough."

The concern often presents alongside a story about why the grief should be over: enough time has passed, the relationship was not that long, the person was not that important, other people are doing better with worse losses. The story is part of what is locking the grief in place.

## Detection Cues

The concern shows up in surface ways and in indirect ways.

**Surface:** explicit statements about a loss not having moved through, judgments about the duration or shape of the grief, requests for techniques to "get over" the loss.

**Indirect:** descriptions of repeating relationship patterns ("I always end up with the same kind of person"), unexplained low mood months or years after a loss, addiction or compulsive behavior that started or worsened around the time of the loss, descriptions of "moving on" that sound performative, anger or fear that does not seem connected to anything in the present.

Often the person does not name grief as the thing. They name the symptoms: I am drinking too much, my business is failing, I can't focus, I keep choosing the wrong people. The grief is the underlying current that has not been allowed to move.

## What Joe Hears

Most likely, the loss is undergrieved. Not because the person has not cried enough, but because the grief has been narrowed to one emotion (usually sadness), or because the identity that the loss is dismantling has not been allowed to die, or because the person is judging the shape of their grief and the judgment is shutting it down.

The "I should be over this by now" framing is itself usually one of the things locking the grief. It is a story about how grief should work (see [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]]). The stronger the story, the slower the grief.

When the concern is about a repeated pattern (the same kind of relationship, the same kind of job, the same kind of dynamic), the grief has not done its dismantling work yet. The pattern recurs because the identity that produced it has not been allowed to die. See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]].

The grief is also often more multi-emotional than the person realizes. They have been working the sadness and treating the anger, fear, relief, or helplessness as not allowed. See [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Several branches are possible depending on what is actually happening.

- **If the person has been recycling on one emotion**: usually sadness. The other emotions in the grief have been blocked, often by shame about which emotions are "appropriate." The path moves through [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]] and [[dont-shame-your-grief-process]] toward inviting the blocked emotion specifically.

- **If the person is judging their grief process**: catching themselves for crying too much, not crying enough, taking too long, being too quick. The judgment is the shutdown. The path moves through [[dont-shame-your-grief-process]] and toward making space for whichever emotion is being judged.

- **If the person is repeating a pattern**: dating the same kind of person, ending up in the same kind of job, fighting the same kind of fight. The grief has not dismantled the identity yet. The path moves through [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] toward the full grief that will dissolve the pattern.

- **If the person is "moving on" too quickly**: performing okay-ness, jumping to the next relationship, pivoting to action. The grief has been skipped. The path moves through the recognition that the skipping will produce recurrence, toward letting the grief actually arrive.

- **If the grief is non-death and getting no social support**: breakups, lost jobs, political losses, identity changes. The culture is allowing for action but not for grief. The path moves through [[helplessness-distinguishes-death-grief]] toward letting the helplessness be felt deliberately, often in private.

- **If the story is locking the grief**: "this was the worst loss," "I will never love again," "I am the one who carries this." The path moves through [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]] toward loosening the holding of the story without invalidating its content.

## Coaching Path

The flow is not a script. The branching depends on what is read.

1. Usually start by asking what the person is actually feeling underneath the "should be over this" framing. The framing is often the management layer; underneath is the grief.

2. Listen for which emotions in the grief are getting through and which are getting blocked. If sadness is the only thing showing up, suspect the others are locked. See [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]].

3. Check whether shame is shutting down some part of the grief. The diagnostic is whether the person can say what they are feeling without judging themselves for feeling it. See [[dont-shame-your-grief-process]].

4. If the grief seems to have been skipped or partialized, name what is at stake: the grief is the exit from the cycle (see [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]]). Partial grief leaves the pattern in place.

5. If the loss is non-death and the social context has been pushing toward action, name that and make space for the helplessness (see [[helplessness-distinguishes-death-grief]]).

6. If the grief is locked in a strong story, work with the story without invalidating it (see [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]]).

7. When appropriate, point toward a practice: [[embodied-stages-of-grief-ritual]], [[let-your-body-shake-it-out]], or [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] in its grief-application form.

8. The deeper question, eventually: what identity is being dismantled by this loss, and is it being allowed to die? See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]].
