# I can't get over this loss / I should be over this by now

## What Someone Says

The phrasings cluster around two contradictory pulls: I am still grieving (which feels like failure), and I do not want to be still grieving (which feels like the goal). Sometimes both are present in the same sentence.

- "I should be over this by now."
- "I don't know why I'm still crying about this."
- "It's been [months/years] and I'm still not okay."
- "I want to move on but I can't."
- "I keep dating people just like my ex."
- "I haven't even grieved this and it's been six months."
- "Everyone says I should be feeling better."
- "I don't know how to grieve this."
- "I feel guilty for still being sad."
- "I feel guilty for not being sad enough."

A late-grief variant uses a different vocabulary. The person has done significant grief work, no longer presents with acute symptoms, and frames the question as a transition problem:

- "How do I let go a little more to exit this phase that I'm in?"
- "I'm in a purgatory."
- "I'm leaving a phase of grief and stepping out into the next phase, whatever that is."
- "I feel ready, but I still think of my life as us, not just me."
- "How do I let go a little more of this mindset of my life is about us and we?"
- "Now my life is about me. I'm carrying him with me and everything, but it's just, it's hard to explain."

The late-grief variant looks different but is the same family. The block is no longer the acute emotion. The block is the framing of the transition — the assumption that "letting go" of the relationship is the prerequisite for the next phase. See the "Ready-But-Stuck Variant" section below for the full configuration.

The concern often presents alongside a story about why the grief should be over: enough time has passed, the relationship was not that long, the person was not that important, other people are doing better with worse losses. The story is part of what is locking the grief in place.

## Detection Cues

The concern shows up in surface ways and in indirect ways.

**Surface:** explicit statements about a loss not having moved through, judgments about the duration or shape of the grief, requests for techniques to "get over" the loss.

**Indirect:** descriptions of repeating relationship patterns ("I always end up with the same kind of person"), unexplained low mood months or years after a loss, addiction or compulsive behavior that started or worsened around the time of the loss, descriptions of "moving on" that sound performative, anger or fear that does not seem connected to anything in the present.

Often the person does not name grief as the thing. They name the symptoms: I am drinking too much, my business is failing, I can't focus, I keep choosing the wrong people. The grief is the underlying current that has not been allowed to move.

## What Joe Hears

Most likely, the loss is undergrieved. Not because the person has not cried enough, but because the grief has been narrowed to one emotion (usually sadness), or because the identity that the loss is dismantling has not been allowed to die, or because the person is judging the shape of their grief and the judgment is shutting it down.

The "I should be over this by now" framing is itself usually one of the things locking the grief. It is a story about how grief should work (see [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]]). The stronger the story, the slower the grief.

When the concern is about a repeated pattern (the same kind of relationship, the same kind of job, the same kind of dynamic), the grief has not done its dismantling work yet. The pattern recurs because the identity that produced it has not been allowed to die. See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]].

The grief is also often more multi-emotional than the person realizes. They have been working the sadness and treating the anger, fear, relief, or helplessness as not allowed. See [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Several branches are possible depending on what is actually happening.

- **If the person has been recycling on one emotion**: usually sadness. The other emotions in the grief have been blocked, often by shame about which emotions are "appropriate." The path moves through [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]] and [[dont-shame-your-grief-process]] toward inviting the blocked emotion specifically.

- **If the person is judging their grief process**: catching themselves for crying too much, not crying enough, taking too long, being too quick. The judgment is the shutdown. The path moves through [[dont-shame-your-grief-process]] and toward making space for whichever emotion is being judged.

- **If the person is repeating a pattern**: dating the same kind of person, ending up in the same kind of job, fighting the same kind of fight. The grief has not dismantled the identity yet. The path moves through [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] toward the full grief that will dissolve the pattern.

- **If the person is "moving on" too quickly**: performing okay-ness, jumping to the next relationship, pivoting to action. The grief has been skipped. The path moves through the recognition that the skipping will produce recurrence, toward letting the grief actually arrive.

- **If the grief is non-death and getting no social support**: breakups, lost jobs, political losses, identity changes. The culture is allowing for action but not for grief. The path moves through [[helplessness-distinguishes-death-grief]] toward letting the helplessness be felt deliberately, often in private.

- **If the story is locking the grief**: "this was the worst loss," "I will never love again," "I am the one who carries this." The path moves through [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]] toward loosening the holding of the story without invalidating its content.

- **If the relationship ended in a way that prevented expression**: a divorce delivered by letter, a breakup over text, a death that happened before the things could be said, an estrangement enforced by the other party. The anger had nowhere to go because the access to the other person was severed before the anger could be voiced. The anger has been turning inward and presenting as chronic shame about being seen ([[shame-blocks-connection-in-real-time]]) or as isolation that looks like circumstance but is actually the wall built by the inward-pointed anger. See [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]]. The path moves through letting the love for the other person surface first (often using the live room — see [[use-the-live-group-as-love-field]]), then extending the love practice to the avoided figure (which surfaces the anger that was always paired with the love), then giving the anger a clean outward channel ([[how-to-release-anger-out-loud]]). The grief that has not moved was held in place by the anger that had no exit. Once the anger moves, the grief can complete.

- **If the person caused (or believes they caused) the loss and is using self-blame as the tether**: a breakup the person attributes to their own behavior, a relationship they ended that they now think was a mistake, a falling out where they take all the responsibility. The self-blame is being held on purpose because letting go of the blame would mean letting go of the relationship. This is [[self-blame-as-tether-to-the-lost]]. The path moves through surfacing the chronicity of the self-blame as a general pattern (using [[how-is-it-not-your-fault-that-its-always-your-fault]]), offering the explicit choice between forgiving this and stopping the always-my-fault pattern ([[the-forgive-this-or-stop-blaming-everything-choice]]), asking the payoff question that surfaces the tether ("what do you get from keeping the blame alive?"), naming the trap, naming the [[addiction-to-shame]] in its self-blame variant, and moving to somatic work ([[feel-the-shame-then-feel-the-opposite]]) plus the daily practice ([[15-minutes-of-self-love-physical-therapy-for-the-heart]]). The grief that has not moved was held in place by the self-blame doing the relational work the person was unwilling to release. Once the self-blame is named as a tether, the body usually starts producing the held grief in the room. See also the specific concern [[i-cant-forgive-myself-for-hurting-them]] for this configuration.

- **If the person has done significant grief work and is now at the threshold of a new phase but inside a let-go premise**: the acute grief has moved, the person is functioning, and the question they are bringing is how to "let go a little more" so they can step into the next phase. The framing assumes the relationship to the dead must be released for the next phase to begin. The framing itself is the block — the person is asking how to do a thing that does not have to be done. The path moves through [[challenge-the-premise-in-the-framing]] applied to the let-go word ("what makes it need to be about you?"), through the recognition that the relationship continues regardless ([[death-is-not-the-end-of-the-relationship]]), through the photos-stay-up example ([[the-widow-with-his-photos-still-up]]) which dissolves the assumption that a new partner would require the dismantling of the dead person's visible presence, through the reframe of the persistent "still feeling them" as the rip in reality showing a love that's beyond you ([[death-as-rip-in-reality]]), and arrives at the body instruction to make the love non-personal — the dead person becomes the conduit through which a non-personal love extends to everything ([[the-dead-as-conduit-to-non-personal-love]]). The "try both" refinement ([[move-from-story-into-the-body]]) asks the body to hold the personal love and the non-personal love simultaneously. The arrival report is usually "painfully and wonderful at the same time" — the simultaneity is the integration. This is the late-grief variant, structurally distinct from the acute and the locked variants: the work is not to surface more grief but to reframe the relationship to the grief that has already moved.

## Coaching Path

The flow is not a script. The branching depends on what is read.

1. Usually start by asking what the person is actually feeling underneath the "should be over this" framing. The framing is often the management layer; underneath is the grief.

2. Listen for which emotions in the grief are getting through and which are getting blocked. If sadness is the only thing showing up, suspect the others are locked. See [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]].

3. Check whether shame is shutting down some part of the grief. The diagnostic is whether the person can say what they are feeling without judging themselves for feeling it. See [[dont-shame-your-grief-process]].

4. If the grief seems to have been skipped or partialized, name what is at stake: the grief is the exit from the cycle (see [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]]). Partial grief leaves the pattern in place.

5. If the loss is non-death and the social context has been pushing toward action, name that and make space for the helplessness (see [[helplessness-distinguishes-death-grief]]).

6. If the grief is locked in a strong story, work with the story without invalidating it (see [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]]).

7. When appropriate, point toward a practice: [[embodied-stages-of-grief-ritual]], [[let-your-body-shake-it-out]], or [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] in its grief-application form.

8. The deeper question, eventually: what identity is being dismantled by this loss, and is it being allowed to die? See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]].

## The Divorce-Ended-by-Letter Variant

A specific shape of this concern: the marriage or partnership ended in a way that did not allow the client to respond. A letter delivered without conversation. A breakup announced as a decision already made. A separation enforced without the client's input. The client carries the loss but also carries the specific quality of having been denied participation in the ending.

This variant produces a distinctive layering. There is grief about the loss. There is anger at the way the ending happened. There is anger at the other person for choosing the form they chose. There is often anger at adjacent figures (the friend who advised the leaving, the relationship coach who was consulted without the client's knowledge, the new partner if there was one). And underneath all of it, there is often shame — the kind that turns the unexpressed anger inward, that produces the felt sense of having been the one who was not good enough to be allowed to respond.

The work on this variant has a specific order. First, establish that the client can still feel love — often in the room, with the available people ([[use-the-live-group-as-love-field]]). Second, extend the love practice to the ex specifically, which surfaces the love-and-anger pair that has been held together. Third, give the anger a clean outward channel using the specific technique ([[how-to-release-anger-out-loud]]) — speak it, angry tone, do not stop. Fourth, after the anger has moved, name the parallel impossibility the ex was facing if the parallel is real ([[apply-the-clients-stuck-place-to-understand-the-other]]). Fifth, return to the present-tense connection in the room as the integration anchor.

The order matters. Trying to do the grief work first (before the anger has moved) leaves the grief locked because the anger underneath is still operating. Trying to do the compassion-for-the-ex work first ([[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]]) bypasses the anger and produces a thin reconciliation that the body cannot maintain. The anger has to move before either the grief or the compassion becomes available.

The closing teaching for this variant is often the principle that anchors the work: shame is anger turned inward, and the chronic shame about being seen that the client has been carrying for years since the ending is the form the unmoved anger has taken. The path out is not to dissolve the shame directly. It is to release the anger in the form Joe demonstrates — punching bag, yelling, the body moving the held charge — and let the shame dissipate as a consequence. See [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]].

## The Ready-But-Stuck Widow Variant (The Late-Grief Threshold)

A specific shape of this concern: the death happened long enough ago that the acute grief has moved. The bereaved has done significant work — therapy, EMDR, sustained allowing of the emotions, return to functioning. They are no longer in the daily crisis. They have a felt sense that the next phase is available — they can see a life in front of them, they have gifts to offer, they are not done. And they are stuck at the threshold.

The stuckness has a specific shape. It is not the inability to feel. It is the inability to figure out how to "let go" enough to step out. The framing is consistent: "I'm in a purgatory." "How do I let go a little more?" "My life was about us and now it has to be about me." The grief work has been done, and the bereaved is now asking how to do an additional thing — the "letting go" — that they assume is the prerequisite for the next phase.

The variant is structurally distinct from the acute and the locked variants. The acute variant is about the grief that is still arriving. The locked variant is about the grief that has been blocked. The ready-but-stuck variant is about the framing the person has built around the grief that has already moved. The block is no longer the emotion. The block is the conceptual structure the person is inside.

The framing usually contains three assumptions:

1. **The us-to-me assumption.** The bereaved frames the next phase as requiring a transition from "us" thinking to "me" thinking. The relationship had been "us." Now it has to become "me." The transition is the work.
2. **The let-go assumption.** The way to make the transition is to "let go" of the dead person — to release the love, the mental sharing, the carrying of them.
3. **The dismantling assumption.** A future new relationship would require the visible dismantling of the dead person's presence: the photos come down, the rings come off, the rituals end.

All three assumptions are usually wrong, or at least are not required.

The coaching path for this variant inverts each assumption:

- The us-to-me assumption gets challenged with: "What if you just continued saying this is for us? What would you be doing?" The person usually discovers that doing it for us is fully compatible with the next phase. See [[challenge-the-premise-in-the-framing]] applied to the let-go word.
- The let-go assumption gets challenged with: "There's no way for you not to have it, I would argue. ... You can't get rid of him." See [[death-is-not-the-end-of-the-relationship]].
- The dismantling assumption gets challenged with the example of the man who lost his wife and married a woman whose family was taken by a wave — the photos are probably still up. See [[the-widow-with-his-photos-still-up]].

Once the three assumptions are dissolved, the body instruction lands: make the love non-personal. The dead person becomes the conduit through which a non-personal love extends to everything. See [[the-dead-as-conduit-to-non-personal-love]]. The "try both" refinement asks the body to hold the personal love and the non-personal love simultaneously. The arrival report is usually "painfully and wonderful at the same time." See [[move-from-story-into-the-body]].

The teaching about death as a "little rip inside of reality" lands here too. The still-feeling-them that the bereaved has been pathologizing is reframed as the rip showing a love that's beyond them. The rip is the doorway, not the wound that needs to close. See [[death-as-rip-in-reality]].

The variant tends to land in a single coaching session because the grief work has already been done. What remains is the conceptual reconfiguration of what the next phase requires. Once the reconfiguration lands, the body produces the integration on its own.

A specific marker for this variant: the bereaved often mentions a pet they got after the death. The pet has been the recipient of the love that had nowhere else to go. The pet is functioning as the non-personal love's first vehicle. The teaching is then to extend what the body is already doing with the pet to everything and everyone.
