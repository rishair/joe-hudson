# How do I be authentic on stage? / How do I stop being self-conscious when I perform?

## What Someone Says

The presenting language often arrives as a question about how to do the performance better. The person has gotten advice from coaches, mentors, other performers. None of it has worked. The person asks for the next technique. Common phrasings:

- "How do I be my authentic self on stage?"
- "How do I be authentic, especially on stage?"
- "I've gotten a lot of feedback from people and..."
- "I keep hearing X is good for me, so I try to do X."
- "I get up on stage and I go into performance mode and that's not me either."
- "Stage is my biggest phobia."
- "Performing is my biggest phobia."

Often the question is presented as a question about authenticity but is actually a question about self-consciousness. The two are different. The person may not know they are different until asked. The clarification is the first move. See [[whats-the-real-question]] for the binary-clarification variant.

## Detection Cues

- The person frames the problem as a how-to question about performance.
- The person cites advice they have been given by other coaches ("a lot of coaches have told me X").
- The person has tried multiple techniques without success.
- The person is self-conscious *while presenting the question* (the pattern is firing in the room).
- The person uses the words "performance mode" or "act" to describe what happens on stage.
- The person says they have a phobia of public performance and yet has chosen to do it.
- The person reports being told contradictory things ("be authentic" and also "do deadpan").

The decisive cue: the self-consciousness is happening live in the coaching conversation. The person is anxious about being seen by Joe, by the camera, by the audience. The on-stage self-consciousness is the same self-consciousness, scaled up.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is the same structure as [[i-get-anxious-in-relationships]] in performance clothing. The biological need to be seen is intact. The shame layer that says "if you see me you'll see what's wrong with me" is intact. The person has chosen a profession that puts them on stage — which is the thing they are most afraid of — and is asking how to do it without feeling the fear that the activity itself is engineered to surface.

The deadpan advice is the structural giveaway. "Deadpan" means: do not show emotion. The advice is to suppress the very thing — emotional fluidity — that authenticity requires. The person has been trying to be authentic by following advice to suppress authenticity. The instructions she has received are an instruction to abandon herself in order to be seen. The contradiction is what is making the problem unsolvable through technique.

The deeper hearing: the person has likely chosen comedy as a way to learn how to express her emotions. The phobia of the stage is itself information — it points at the underlying desire for emotional fluidity, the thing the person is trying to develop by intentionally entering the situation that makes it hardest. The phobia is not a problem. The phobia is the signal of what is wanted. See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]].

The work is not to teach the person how to perform better. The work is to dissolve the self-consciousness by restoring connection — to self, to the audience, to whatever emotion is arising in the moment. The performance gets better as a side effect.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

This concern branches based on what is happening in the room:

- If the self-consciousness is happening live in the coaching session: bring the presenting problem into the room. See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]]. The on-stage self-consciousness is happening on the Zoom right now. Use it.

- If the person cites advice from other coaches: refuse to engage with the advice. Joe will explicitly say "I don't want to hear that. Okay, I don't care." The advice is the structural source of the problem. Continuing to discuss it would let the person stay in the technique frame.

- If the person presents the question as about authenticity when it is about self-consciousness (or vice versa): clarify which question they are actually asking. The clarification is half the work. See [[whats-the-real-question]].

- If the person is cutting off their own emotional expression in real time (laughing and then suppressing the laugh, having a feeling and then closing it down): name it the moment it happens. See [[cutting-off-your-own-authenticity-in-real-time]].

- If the person tells a story about what happens on stage: refuse the story. "I don't want to hear the story quite yet." The story is the cover; the live moment is the work.

- If the person has been told to suppress emotion (deadpan, hold it together, be cool): name the contradiction. The advice to suppress emotion is the advice that produces the self-consciousness. See [[dont-suppress-emotion-to-perform]].

## Coaching Path

The typical sequence from this concern is fast and structurally clean:

1. **Clarify the actual question.** Is this about authenticity or about self-consciousness? They are different. Give them the binary and let them choose. See [[whats-the-real-question]].

2. **Refuse the advice they came in with.** "I don't want to hear that. Okay, I don't care." The advice was the problem; reviewing it would keep them in the problem.

3. **Use the live self-consciousness.** The pattern is firing in the room. Name it. "So right now you're self-conscious?" See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]].

4. **Scale the self-consciousness.** "On a scale of 1 to 100, the most self-conscious you've ever been?" Anchor 100 to the worst version, 0 to a Zen moment with a best friend. See [[scale-the-feeling-1-to-100-with-anchors]].

5. **Ask if they can shift it one increment.** "Can you make it 39?" The smallness of the increment is what makes the practice possible. See [[ask-one-percent-less-then-how-did-you-do-that]].

6. **Ask how they did it.** When the increment lands, ask the mechanism. "How did you do that?" The person teaches themselves their own move. The teaching becomes durable practice.

7. **Catch the cut-offs in real time.** When the person has a feeling and suppresses it (laughs and stops the laugh, beats themselves up, dissociates), name it. The cut-off is the live form of the self-consciousness. See [[cutting-off-your-own-authenticity-in-real-time]].

8. **Reframe the phobia.** "There is some sort of desire to have emotional fluidity, to not feel stuck emotionally, if you're trying to get on stage to face your biggest phobia." The phobia is information about what the person is reaching for, not an obstacle to it.

9. **Cure the phobia by loving the fear.** The phobia dissolves when fear is welcomed rather than fought. "Connect with the fear. Like right now, connect to the fear like you just connected with yourself, or you like connected with me." See [[love-the-thing-you-fear-cures-the-phobia]].

The whole arc can land in five minutes if the live pattern is available to work with.

## What This Concern Is Not

- This is not stage fright that needs a technique. Techniques are what produced the problem.
- This is not a confidence issue. The person is competent enough to get on stage. The self-consciousness is in the relationship with the self, not the relationship with the skill.
- This is not [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]]. The approval-seeking concern is about wanting to be liked. This concern is about being unable to be present while being seen.
- This is not the same as [[i-get-anxious-in-relationships]] though it shares the structure. The relationship version has more time and intimacy in play; the on-stage version has more concentrated exposure with a fixed-length window and a verdict (audience response).
- This is not solved by "just being yourself." That instruction is content-less to a person whose access to themselves is exactly what is blocked by the situation.
