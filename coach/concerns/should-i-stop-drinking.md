# Should I stop drinking? / Should I drink at all?

## What Someone Says

- "I'm struggling to decide about sobriety."
- "Do I just completely stop drinking, or just drink a little bit?"
- "It's not huge drinking we're talking about. As I get older, I just feel like it's not doing me a lot of good, and yet I'm still deeply attracted to it."
- "Do I choose a path of fun and quick fix in the present moment? Or do I think about the future ups and downsides of continuing to drink?"
- "I've been kind of on and off, up and down, doing this."
- "What do I choose?"

The framing often arrives as a binary decision the person has been carrying for a while. Often there is a history of on-and-off attempts: months sober, then back to drinking, then sober again, then back.

## Detection Cues

The presentation has the shape of [[treating-it-as-a-decision-when-its-an-emotion]]. The person comes in asking for help choosing between two paths, neither of which has resolved despite repeated consideration. The deliberation has not converged, and the asking has the polish of rehearsal.

The drinking-specific signals:

- The person frames the dilemma as "fun now vs. health later" — a present-future tradeoff
- They describe their drinking as moderate ("it's not huge drinking we're talking about") — a hedge that signals the question is not really about the volume
- They describe being "deeply attracted to it" even though they know it is not serving them — the gap between intellectual knowing and somatic pull
- There is a history of stopping and starting: "the longest I've gone in the last 2 years without drinking is a month" — the cycle is the diagnostic
- The question is being held as a decision to be made rather than as a feeling to be felt

The framing is often presented with mild urgency or self-frustration, but rarely with crisis. This is not the person whose drinking is destroying their life. It is the person whose drinking is doing them quiet harm and who cannot fully stop wanting it.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this is rarely a question about alcohol. The drinking is a vehicle. The question of whether to keep using the vehicle is a surface restatement of a deeper question that has not been named.

When asked what the drinking is giving them, the answer arrives within a turn or two: "quick fix relaxation," "a change of state." Pressed further — from what to what — the underlying material surfaces. In the canonical case, the person realized within seconds: "I'm feeling into the shame and guilt piece that I'm trying to escape."

The drinking is managing shame and guilt. The question presented as "should I drink?" is structurally the question "should I keep using this vehicle to escape shame and guilt, or should I find a different vehicle?" Neither of those is the question that produces movement.

The reframe Joe reaches for: **what if the question is, "should I give up shame and guilt?"** The drinking is downstream of the avoidance. Address the avoidance and the question about the vehicle dissolves into a different one.

Underneath the question is also often [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]] in compressed form: the person uses alcohol to escape shame, the escape produces more shame, the cycle tightens. "Running away from shame and guilt, and in the running away from it, creating more shame and guilt."

And underneath the drinking itself, in the cases Joe coaches, is often a [[heart-in-a-box-want-to-drink]] mechanism: the alcohol is providing the permission to shine, to connect, to be the version of the person whose heart is not closed. The substance is the workaround for a heart that is otherwise kept boxed.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point. The same drinking question goes different directions based on what is showing up.

- **If the person treats it as a decision to make**: [[treating-it-as-a-decision-when-its-an-emotion]]. Joe will refuse the decision-frame and surface the avoided emotion. The question is not which path; it is what feeling is being avoided in both paths.
- **If shame and guilt surface quickly when asked what the drinking does**: the work is on the shame, not the drinking. Joe will reframe: "What if the question was, should I give up shame and guilt or not?" See [[reframe-the-question-to-the-blocker-emotion]].
- **If there is a hidden rebellion in the system** (the person has been telling themselves they should stop and not stopping): [[hidden-rebellion-under-the-habit]]. The "fuck you, I want what I want" is operating underneath the surface compliance. Joe will surface it and speak directly to it.
- **If the person describes the drinking as giving them access to a part of themselves they otherwise can't reach** (warmth, connection, being able to shine): [[heart-in-a-box-want-to-drink]]. The drinking is permission. Address what is keeping the permission unavailable without the substance.
- **If the person is in heavy shame-loop framing of the failed sobriety attempts**: [[shame-drives-behavior]] is operating. The shame about not being able to stop drinking is what is keeping them drinking.
- **If they want a technique or a rule for moderation**: this is [[i-cant-be-disciplined]] in alcohol form. The discipline framing is the problem.

## Coaching Path

1. **Refuse the decision-frame.** Joe does not adjudicate the should-I-or-shouldn't-I. The deliberation is the symptom. The arrival at the question repeatedly means the question is the cover.

2. **Find what the drinking is giving.** Within a turn or two, ask: what does the drinking get you? The answer points to the underlying state-shift the substance is performing. "Quick fix relaxation. A change of state. From what to what?" The from-what is the operative material.

3. **Reframe the question to the underlying emotion.** Once the underlying state is named — often shame and guilt, sometimes loneliness, sometimes constriction — restate the question. "So what if the question was, should I give up shame and guilt or not?" The reframe makes visible that the work is on the blocker emotion, not on the substance. See [[reframe-the-question-to-the-blocker-emotion]].

4. **Surface the rebellion.** Almost always, the person trying to decide whether to drink has been telling themselves they should stop. The unspoken rebellion ("fuck you, I want what I want") is doing some of the keeping. Naming it gives it air. See [[hidden-rebellion-under-the-habit]] and [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]].

5. **Find what the drinking is providing access to.** In coaching, the drinking often turns out to be permission for some quality the person is keeping boxed otherwise — heart, warmth, expression, connection. See [[heart-in-a-box-want-to-drink]]. Once the boxing is seen, the substance loses its monopoly on the access.

6. **Demonstrate the access in the present.** If the drinking is providing permission to connect, Joe shows the connection happening in the room without alcohol. "How hard is your connection with me right now?" The live demonstration breaks the assumed link between substance and access.

7. **Do not promise behavior change.** Joe does not tell the person they will stop drinking. The work is to address the structural cause. The drinking changes downstream of the work. See [[joes-pot-habit]] and [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]].

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not adjudicate the decision. He does not say "you should stop" or "moderation is fine for you." The adjudication is what the person came in to extract, and the extraction would reinforce the framing.
- He does not give a sobriety plan or a moderation rule. The rule would join the rebellion-or-submission loop ([[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]]).
- He does not pathologize the drinking. The drinking is doing something. The work is to honor what it is doing.
- He does not assume the person needs to be sober. The question of whether to drink is downstream of the work on the underlying material.
- He does not work on the alcohol directly. He works on shame, guilt, and the boxed heart underneath.
