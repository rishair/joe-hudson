# I have a hard time going all in on my business

## What Someone Says

- "I have a tough time going all in on my business."
- "The resistance to really ask and to put myself out there."
- "There's a coaching client, for instance, and I know I can help them, but to really go for the ask..."
- "I can't ask my clients to commit."
- "I have so much resistance to fully committing."
- "I know I should be going for it but I just can't."
- "My body tenses up when I try to ask."
- "I can do this but I can't fully commit to it."
- "I had this in tennis too — even when I was being aggressive in my wants, there was resistance."
- "I want to but something stops me."
- "I keep holding back and I don't know why."

The concern often arrives in a softened form. The client may not name "asking" directly. They may say "I can't fully commit," "I can't really go for it," "I have resistance," "I keep holding back." The asking is the operational form the holding-back takes — the moment where the not-going-all-in becomes most visible — but the concern is broader. It is about the inability to be fully in the want.

A common signature: the client describes the resistance as showing up in multiple domains (business, sports, dating, creative work) but presents it in the business / asking domain because that is where the cost is currently most visible.

## Detection Cues

- The client describes a clear desire (to grow the business, to help the client, to win the match, to land the deal) AND a clear blockage to acting on the desire.
- The client uses the language of "going all in" or "fully committing" or "really going for it" — terms that imply they are currently going partial.
- The client locates the resistance in the body ("heat," "tension," "tightness") when they try to take the action.
- The client identifies the asking moment specifically as the friction point — not the doing of the work, but the asking that initiates it.
- The client knows they can do the thing — they have the skill, the capacity, the readiness — but cannot make themselves do it.
- The client has a historical pattern: the resistance has been present in earlier domains (childhood asking, sports, relationships, school) and is now showing up in business.
- The client may describe parents or early caregivers who said no to asking ("it was too expensive," "it wasn't possible"). The early no's installed the pattern.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is preemptive self-rejection as a defense against feared external rejection. The client is not failing to go all in because they lack drive. The client is suppressing the going-all-in because going all in would expose them to being rejected — and the only way to prevent that exposure is to not go all in.

The structural inversion: "the only way in life to not get rejected is to make sure you reject yourself first." See [[the-only-way-to-not-be-rejected-is-to-reject-yourself-first]].

Underneath that is a specific package of self-rejection: the wants are rejected, the feelings are rejected, the resistance itself is rejected. The client has rejected their interior so thoroughly that the external rejection they fear cannot match what they are already doing to themselves.

What they are protecting against, usually: being seen with a feeling (sadness, fear, neediness, wanting) that they were trained to hide. The training often comes from childhood — parents who said no, parents who needed the child to compose themselves, parents whose own self-rejection was projected onto the child. The child learned that wanting was dangerous, and the adult is still enacting the protection.

The good news inside the concern: the part that wants to go all in is alive. It is what brought the client to the coaching call. The not-going-all-in is the suppression; the going-all-in is the part being suppressed. The work is to surface the suppression, drop a layer of it, and let the suppressed part come forward — usually some form of ambition, desire, aliveness, or wanting itself.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same surface concern goes different directions based on what Joe reads.

- If **[[heat-or-tension-in-the-body-as-resistance-data]]**: the client locates the resistance in the body, often as heat or tension when the asking is brought up. Joe will use the body location as the entry point. He will name what is happening ("what's happening to you right now?") and then [[answer-as-the-resistance]] to give the body's defense a voice.

- If the client maps the resistance back to childhood when asked ("my parents said it was too expensive / not possible"): Joe will note the early installation without spending long there. The point is not to do trauma work on the childhood scene; it is to recognize that the current pattern has roots. The work is in the present.

- If the resistance has a clear addressee ("the coaching client," "the investor," "the audience"): Joe will surface that the asking is the point where self-rejection becomes operational. The asking is what would expose the self; the self-rejection prevents the asking; the prevention is the protection.

- If the client speaks fluently and confidently about everything except the asking: read this as evidence that the asking is the specific edge. The fluency elsewhere shows the capacity. The blockage at the asking shows the defense. Don't get distracted by the fluency.

- If the client tries to intellectualize the resistance ("I think it's because of X, Y, Z"): the intellectualization is itself part of the defense. Don't engage the analysis. Move to the body. See [[answer-as-the-resistance]] for the move that bypasses intellectual analysis.

- If a feeling shows up unexpectedly during the resistance work (sadness, fear, tears): the feeling is what was being protected against. Don't move past it; let it be there. The feeling is the data about what the self-rejection has been organized around.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow from this concern:

1. **Surface the body location of the resistance.** Ask "what's happening to you right now?" when the client is in the asking-conversation. The body's response is the access point. See [[name-the-body-defense-in-real-time]].

2. **Trace the resistance pattern historically (briefly).** "Did you have this resistance in sports? In childhood?" The historical mapping confirms the pattern is structural, not situational. Don't spend long here. The point is recognition, not catharsis.

3. **Give the resistance permission to speak.** Use [[answer-as-the-resistance]]. The resistance, when given voice, names what it is resisting — usually a feeling the client has been trained to hide (sadness, fear, wanting).

4. **Trace the feeling to the feared outcome.** "What are you scared will happen if [feeling] is seen?" The feared outcome is usually rejection by the audience. Chain the surfacing: the resistance is hiding the feeling, the feeling is hiding the fear of rejection, the fear of rejection is the engine.

5. **Deliver the structural inversion.** Use [[the-only-way-to-not-be-rejected-is-to-reject-yourself-first]]. Surface that the self-rejection is the more painful rejection, and that the external rejection cannot dent it.

6. **Test the self-rejection for evidence.** Use [[find-anything-worth-rejecting]]. The empty search produces the opening for welcoming.

7. **Welcome the whole self.** Use [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]]. The welcoming addresses the package: the wants, the feelings, the resistance, the self.

8. **Name and admire the suppressed part.** Use [[coach-names-and-admires-the-suppressed-ambition]]. The part that has been pushed down — usually ambition, desire, the wanting — gets named, witnessed, and admired. The closing recognition is that being on the call itself is an act of the part.

The arc is compact when done well — the source session takes under 15 minutes. The compression is possible because the moves chain naturally: each one creates the conditions for the next.

## Variations

**Business / sales presentation.** The concern shows up as "I can't ask for the sale" or "I can't pitch" or "I can't charge what I'm worth." The work is the same; the specific addressee is the prospect / client / customer.

**Creative work presentation.** "I can't put my work out there." See [[i-cant-enter-my-creative-work]] for the adjacent concern; the asking-resistance is one layer of that broader pattern.

**Dating presentation.** "I can't ask them out" / "I can't fully commit to dating." See [[i-cant-find-love]] for the broader frame; the asking-resistance shows up specifically at the moment of expressing interest or escalating.

**Career presentation.** "I can't ask for the raise" / "I can't ask for the promotion." See [[job-interview-3d-chess]] for the canonical example of self-rejection in approval-gated career contexts.

**Athletic presentation.** "I can't fully go for it on the court" / "I tense up at the key moment." The same structural resistance, located in performance. The source session client identified this pattern in his tennis years before it showed up in business — confirming the cross-domain nature of the resistance.
