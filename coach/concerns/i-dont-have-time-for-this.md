# I don't have time for this

## What Someone Says

- "This all sounds great, but I just need to make money right now."
- "I don't have the luxury to think about this stuff."
- "Maybe once I'm successful, I can focus on personal development."
- "I'm not in a position to slow down."
- "I have a paycheck I have to make."
- "I have a particular runway and I just have to execute."
- "I'm in a hierarchy, I don't have control over how my work happens."

The concern frames personal development, ease, or artistry as competing with the urgent practical need to produce income or hit a deadline.

## Detection Cues

- The person dismisses the work as a luxury available to others but not to them.
- They cite a specific external pressure (paycheck, runway, deadline, boss) as the reason this cannot apply.
- They locate their lack of agency in the hierarchy or organization they are in.
- They speak about the work as future tense ("when I get there I can...") rather than present tense.
- They equate "slowing down to think about how I work" with "losing time."

## What Joe Hears

The dichotomy again: as if there is a separation between how you work and the work itself. There is not. The art of accomplishment is not extra. It is the *how* of the doing that is already happening. The person at the Subway counter does not have extra time to be artistic. He is artistic in the time he is already spending. See [[subway-kid]].

The concern also reveals a [[victim-stance-invites-abuse]] pattern. To say "I cannot do this because of my situation" is to own the position of someone whose life is happening to them rather than someone who is choosing their relationship to it. That position attracts the very situations that confirm the belief.

"If you think you can't do it because of your situation, then you're owning the position of a victim. It's like somebody whose life is oppressing them. And if you have that position, there's only two people who really want to spend time with you: other victims and people who abuse you." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person is genuinely in survival mode: Joe acknowledges the constraint and then questions whether the *how* of the survival can be different even if the constraint cannot. The Subway kid was in survival. The artistry came in how he was while doing the work.

- If the person is using "no time" as a defense against feeling something: Joe goes underneath. Often the busyness is a way to avoid uncomfortable feelings about what the person actually wants. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]].

- If the person is in [[victim-stance-invites-abuse]]: Joe addresses the stance directly. The position itself produces the conditions that reinforce it.

- If the person is performing busyness as identity: Joe is gentle but direct. Busy is not the same as productive. See [[sleep-deprived-soldier]].

## Coaching Path

1. Acknowledge the practical constraint without accepting the dichotomy. Yes, there is a paycheck. Yes, there is a runway. None of that prevents the work of the *how*.

2. Offer concrete examples of the work happening within constraints. [[subway-kid]] at a sandwich counter. [[mandela-empowerment]] in a prison. Neither had time or freedom. Both brought their full selves to the situation they were in.

3. Question the time math. The grinding state is often less productive than the rested state. See [[sleep-deprived-soldier]] and [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]]. The "time saved" by skipping the work of the *how* may not be saved at all.

4. Surface the victim stance if present. The "I can't because of my situation" position is itself the obstacle, not the situation. See [[victim-stance-invites-abuse]].

5. Reframe: the art of accomplishment is not an addition to the work. It is a change in the relationship to the work that is already happening. There is no extra time required.
