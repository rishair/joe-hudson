# I can't receive compliments or love from my spouse

## What Someone Says

The presenting concern often arrives via the compliments doorway. Someone notices in therapy or in life that they cannot give or receive compliments, and the thread leads into a marriage where they have been the deflecting partner for years.

- "I had marriage counseling this morning and this topic came up about how bad I am at giving and receiving compliments"
- "I'm bad at giving and receiving — both"
- "The more I thought about it, the sadder I got because I can't even say it to strangers, much less my own husband or family"
- "I didn't trust enough to give compliments and that they would be received"
- "How do I get past that? How do I be okay with giving and receiving?"
- "My husband is on the other end of the spectrum — he's the ultimate complimenter"
- "He says all these really nice things and I go 'okay, yeah, thank you, okay'"
- "How do I let it in?"
- "The problem is the people who are closest to me that haven't been there for me"
- "He's really the only one that's truly seen me"

The surface concern is compliments. The structure underneath is the spouse-side of an [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]]: the person who is being chased and cannot let the chasing land as love. The spouse has been pursuing connection for years. The person has walls up. The compliments are one specific input that cannot get through; the wall is general.

## Detection Cues

The AI coach recognizes this concern when:

- A person says they are "bad at" compliments, especially after a recent therapy session or moment of awareness
- The compliment-difficulty is mentioned with sadness or self-criticism rather than as a neutral observation
- A long-term partner is described as "the ultimate complimenter" or "always saying nice things" and the response pattern is described as flat ("I just say okay thanks")
- The person describes their partner as the pursuer over years, with themselves cast as the one with walls or the one resisting
- The duration of the dynamic is named in years or decades ("he's been chasing me for 22 years")
- The person says something like "he's the only one who's truly seen me" while reporting they cannot receive what he gives
- The phrase "the people closest to me haven't been there for me" appears alongside a present partner who actually has been there
- The person is in their 30s, 40s, 50s or older in a long marriage, working on themselves now ("I've been working over the last 2 years to become more self-aware")
- The hand-on-mouth, the "okay yeah thank you" flat response, the rapid subject-pivot — somatic signatures of a long-practiced deflection

The concern often does not present as "I have an avoidant attachment style." It presents as "I'm bad at compliments." The pattern is broader than the words. The compliments are the symptom that surfaces; the marriage-wide deflection is the structure.

## What Joe Hears

The compliments are the entry. The deeper material is the [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]] from the avoidant side: the partner who has been the recipient of years of pursuit and has not let the love land.

Three layers Joe usually finds underneath:

**The deflection-as-defense.** The compliment cannot be received because receiving it would let the love in, and the love in full quantity would [[love-causes-annihilation|annihilate]] the smallness-identity that has been organizing the person's life. The "I just say okay thanks" is the polite-deflection move (see [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]]). It looks like reception. It is structurally the wall.

**The avoidant-side of the chase configuration.** The spouse has been performing the chaser role for years. The person has been performing the wall-er role. Both are configured to keep love at the level the system can tolerate — which, for the wall-er, is "less than what is being offered." See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]] for the configuration; the standard treatment focuses on the chaser, but this concern is its mirror image.

**The locked-down sensitivity.** A person who has been a wall for decades was almost always [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift|constitutionally very sensitive]] as a child, and the rigidity is the protection that was put in around the sensitivity. The "rigid thing" their parents taught them to be was the survival adaptation. Underneath the rigidity is the receiving capacity, and underneath the receiving capacity is the original sensitivity that the lockdown was protecting.

The trifecta — deflection, avoidant role, locked-down sensitivity — usually runs together. Working any one of them works the other two.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people can present this concern and the coaching goes different directions based on what is visible.

- If [[self-criticism-kicks-in-when-invited-to-see-yourself]]: the person agrees to look at the part of themselves the partner sees, and the inner critic immediately discounts what is seen. Joe will name the criticism in real time and stay with what the partner sees, not what the critic says. See [[redirect-from-being-seen-by-them-to-seeing-yourself]].

- If [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]] shows partial reception: the compliment lands somewhat but a flash bounces. Joe will name the flash and use it as the doorway to what is in the way.

- If the person describes the partner as the only one who has ever seen them: this is the avoidant-partner's structure being made visible. Joe will [[redirect-from-being-seen-by-them-to-seeing-yourself|redirect to self-seeing]] — "we're changing that. You're seeing yourself. How about you seeing yourself? That's what matters."

- If the person can identify the [[two-forms-of-ego-good-and-bad|two forms of ego]] in themselves ("I'm bad at") and stays in self-criticism even after the teaching: the work is upstream, on [[inherent-goodness]] and on actually letting the compliment land somatically.

- If the person reports feeling overwhelmed by the expansiveness as the love starts to land: Joe names this directly. The overwhelming is correct — the person is feeling 2% of what is coming their way. The work is to keep going gently, not to back off. See [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]] for the pacing.

## Coaching Path

The session typically follows a recognizable shape, even though the specific moves vary.

1. **Demonstrate the compliment-receiving live.** Joe gives the person a genuine compliment in the session and watches what they do with it. If they deflect, he names it and re-tries. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] for the receiving move.

2. **Use the live deflection as data.** When the person deflects Joe's compliment, Joe sometimes does the reverse: deflects their compliment to him, then asks how they stayed in their open heart. See [[use-recent-deflection-to-test-receiving-live]].

3. **Pivot to the underlying marriage dynamic.** "It feels like the whole relationship with your husband has been him trying to get connection with you and you being somewhat resistant. How accurate is that?" The compliments doorway opens into the larger structure.

4. **Apology to the spouse for the distance.** "Apologize to your husband for making him chase you when all you really want is that connection. Use his name, but no shame, just like fully empowered." See [[apologize-to-partner-without-shame-for-the-distance-pattern]].

5. **Ask what they actually want.** [[what-do-you-want]] in this configuration. What do they most want from the partner in the moment of the chase. The answer is usually some version of "to keep coming at me, to save me."

6. **Save you from what?** The answer reveals what the running-away is for. Usually: "running away and not feeling the connection." The running is being run from feeling connection.

7. **Quantify the partner's showing-up.** "How many years has he been chasing you for connection?" "22." "22 years he's fucking shown up for you. Let it all the way down." See [[count-the-years-of-showing-up]].

8. **Name the expansiveness and pace it.** When the love starts to land, the body registers it as overwhelming. Joe names this as the correct response — only 2% of what's coming her way. The rigidity was protecting an extremely sensitive system. See [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]].

9. **Redirect from being-seen-by-them to seeing yourself.** When the person says "he's the only one who's truly seen me," Joe immediately pivots: "we're changing that. You're seeing yourself."

10. **Apology to self.** "You said something to your husband, you apologized. What's the apology you have for yourself?" Usually: sorry for holding you back from feeling the emotions you wanted to feel for so long. See [[apologize-to-yourself-for-holding-back-from-feeling]].
