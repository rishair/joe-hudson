# I lose myself in the fight

## What Someone Says

The most distinct version of this concern is not about content. It is about agency. The person reports being absent from their own behavior during the fight.

- "I just feel like I'm completely out of control."
- "I don't know what I'm doing. That's not who I am. That's not who I wanted to be."
- "It's like somebody animated me, like stuck their hand up me and made me a puppet."
- "Something just takes over me when we fight."
- "I look back at what I did and I don't recognize that person."
- "I act in ways I would never act with anyone else."
- "I become a person I don't want to be."

The shared signature is the use of language that distances the speaker from the actor. "Something took over." "I wasn't myself." "I don't know what came over me." The grammar of agency breaks down.

## Detection Cues

- The person describes their behavior in a fight in third person or with passive constructions ("that happened to me")
- The recurring complaint is about who they become, not about what the partner did
- The post-fight shame is specifically about the self-as-actor, not about the issue being fought about
- The fight is described as "out of nowhere" relative to the size of the trigger
- The person says they cannot recognize themselves in the recording or the recollection
- The person reports the same disorienting "puppet" experience across multiple relationships

## What Joe Hears

The "something animates me" experience is the felt sense of a [[trigger]] firing — being in your trauma rather than in yourself. The puppet feeling is not metaphor. It is accurate to the structure: the present situation has activated an old configuration, and the response is being produced by the old material, not by the current self.

"The important thing to know about that is that's trauma." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

The trauma can be capital-T (PTSD, a war zone, a discrete violent event) or small-t (a repeated childhood message that an emotion was not allowed, that a feeling had to be hidden). See [[capital-t-vs-small-t-trauma]]. The mechanism is the same: the body is being pulled back to the situation where the original wound was installed, and is reliving it in the present.

The person who loses themselves in the fight is not weak, broken, or uniquely volatile. They are doing what every traumatized nervous system does: returning to the unprocessed scene. The puppet hand is not external. It is the body trying to complete an emotional loop that was left open in the original event. See [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching points:

- If the person describes the puppet feeling with shame and self-attack: the work is first to name that this is trauma firing, not character failure. The shame is a second-order trigger. Joe would name the structure before going into content.
- If [[in-their-story]]: the person is narrating the fight from outside it rather than feeling what was firing. Joe will redirect toward the body — what is in your body right now as you describe this?
- If [[performing-growth]]: the person knows the word "trigger" and uses it with sophistication but no felt experience. Joe will look for the actual body sensation.
- If the person describes the original childhood scene easily but cannot access the emotion of it: the work is in [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]], not in further analysis.
- If the person describes the partner as "the problem": the work is to recognize the [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] structure — both are in their trauma simultaneously.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow from this concern:

1. Name what is happening structurally. "What you are describing is a trigger. The puppet feeling is the felt sense of trauma firing. You aren't doing something wrong by losing yourself; you are doing what a triggered nervous system does." See [[trigger]].

2. Distinguish what kind of trauma is firing. Capital-T or small-t. The "be strong, don't be afraid" message from a parent is real trauma even though it is not a single dramatic event. See [[capital-t-vs-small-t-trauma]].

3. Introduce the reframe of what the fight is for. The fight is not the failure of the relationship. The fight is the relationship offering up the unprocessed material so it can finally be felt. See [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]].

4. Give in-the-moment tools to regulate the nervous system enough to do the work. Breath ([[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]]). Hands on the body ([[hands-on-body-to-regulate-nervous-system]]). Naming what is happening ([[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]]).

5. Move toward the actual healing mechanism: feel what could not be felt at the time of the original wound. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. This is where the puppet feeling stops needing to recur.

6. If the person is in a relationship that is willing to do the work together, point at the medium itself. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] and [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]]. The same fight that is currently producing the puppet feeling can, with practice, become the place where the original wound finally heals.

The destination is not "you will stop losing it in fights." The destination is "the fights themselves become the precise material that releases the trauma that was making you lose it."
