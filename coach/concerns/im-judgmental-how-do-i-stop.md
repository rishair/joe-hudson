# I'm judgmental / How do I stop being judgmental?

## What Someone Says

The person is naming a pattern they have observed in themselves and want to interrupt. They are often already aware that the judgment is costing them something — energy, connection, peace — but they cannot stop the pattern through effort alone.

- "I'm so judgmental of people, I don't know how to stop."
- "I keep judging everyone and I hate that I do it."
- "I notice I'm always picking people apart in my head."
- "I'm so critical of other people. It's exhausting."
- "I think I'm better than people and I don't like that I do."
- "I'm constantly comparing myself to others and finding them wanting."
- "I want to be less judgmental but I don't know how."
- "Every time I leave an interaction I'm running judgments about the other person."

## Detection Cues

The concern appears in several surface forms:

- **Direct naming:** "I'm judgmental." The person has the word and knows the pattern.
- **Indirect via tone:** the person is describing an interaction and the description is laced with sneering, contempt, or fault-finding. The judgment is in the room before it is named.
- **Self-reproach without insight:** "I just spent the whole drive home running through everything wrong with that person." The pattern is observed but the function is not yet seen.
- **The someone-told-me variant:** a partner, friend, or coworker has named the person as judgmental and they are now wondering whether it is true.
- **The exhausted variant:** "I'm just so tired of being so hard on everyone all the time." The cost is being felt even when the function is not yet understood.
- **The spiritual variant:** "I keep judging people who haven't done the work / who are unconscious / who are limited." The judgment has wrapped itself in a self-development frame. See [[using-limiting-belief-frame-to-judge-others]] and [[dont-identify-as-awakened]].

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the surface "I judge people" is a layered structure that the coaching has to address all the way down.

**Layer 1: External judgment is downstream of internal judgment.** "If you see somebody judging you, you know that they are judging themselves 10 times harder than they're judging you." The first read on someone who reports being judgmental of others is that there is a louder inner critic running internally. The work cannot start with stopping the external judgment; it has to start with the internal one.

**Layer 2: Judgment is an emotion-management strategy.** Every judgment is protecting against a feeling the person does not want to feel. Judging someone for being late is protecting against the feeling of abandonment. Judging someone for not working hard enough is protecting against feeling alone with the responsibility. The judgment is not free-floating contempt; it is the costume the avoided emotion is wearing. See [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]] and [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]].

**Layer 3: Judgment is somatically costly.** Holding judgment requires muscular constriction. The body has to be tense to maintain the better-than position. The unhappiness of the chronically judgmental person is a structural consequence of running constriction continuously, not a moral consequence of being mean. See [[judgment-hurts-the-judger]].

**Layer 4: Judgment closes the heart.** You cannot be defended and open at the same time. Every judgment you run forecloses the love, contact, and joy you would otherwise be available for. See [[open-your-heart-while-judging-experiment]].

**Layer 5: People know you are judging them.** Facial expression, body language, and energy carry the judgment whether or not it is spoken. The interpersonal cost is paid even when no judgment is voiced. The "I'm just thinking it, not saying it" defense is not protective; the read goes through anyway.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same concern goes different directions based on what is showing up in the person.

- If the person is **performing the concern as virtue** ("I know I'm judgmental, I'm working on it") and the judgment is intact in the room, treat the meta-frame as another layer of the same pattern. The "I shouldn't be judgmental" is itself a judgment of self. Move toward what feeling that self-judgment is protecting against.

- If the person is **collapsed into shame** about being judgmental ("I'm such a bad person for thinking these things"), do not validate the moral frame. Reroute to function: what is the judgment doing for you, what is it protecting you from feeling? See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] for the move that refuses the moral collapse.

- If the person is **judging a specific person with intensity**, the intensity is the read. The intensity is the signal that something is being projected or that an emotion is being avoided. The move is to find what feeling would come up if they could not run the judgment. See [[mirror-the-judgment]] and [[judgment-vs-discernment]].

- If the person has **the spiritual or growth-frame variant** of judgment (judging people for being unconscious, limited, asleep), treat it as the same teaching but with a sharper diagnostic edge — the spiritual framing makes the judgment harder to see. See [[using-limiting-belief-frame-to-judge-others]] and [[dont-identify-as-awakened]].

- If the person is **describing a specific recurring judgment** of one person (a parent, a partner, a coworker), the projection mirror is often the cleanest opening move. See [[mirror-the-judgment]].

- If the person is **asking how to stop the judgment** as a technique question, the first move is to relocate the work from "stop judging" to "find what the judgment is protecting." The "how to stop" question is itself a should and tends to produce its own loop. See [[stick-a-fucking-shit-in-front-of-the-should]].

## Coaching Path

The typical flow for this concern follows the three-step structure from the source video, adapted to live coaching:

1. **Start with self-judgment, not external judgment.** External judgment is downstream of internal judgment. Move attention to the inner critic first. The question: "What does the voice in your head say to you all day?" The answer points at the actual work. See [[voice-in-the-head]].

2. **Find the emotion the judgment is protecting against.** Whether the judgment is of self or other, the same move applies. "If you couldn't run this judgment, what would you have to feel?" The answer is usually one of: abandonment, loneliness, the fear of being alone with responsibility, the fear of not being good enough, the fear of being like the person being judged. Feel the feeling. The judgment relaxes on its own. See [[find-the-emotion-under-self-judgment]] and [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]].

3. **Make the cost felt, not argued.** The person who knows intellectually that judgment is bad has not actually changed because the cost is intellectual rather than somatic. Run the experiments that make the cost felt: notice the constriction in the body when running a judgment; notice the heart closing while holding superiority; notice the interpersonal field changing when judgment is present. See [[open-your-heart-while-judging-experiment]] and [[judgment-hurts-the-judger]].

4. **Refuse the "I shouldn't be judgmental" should.** The instruction "stop being judgmental" is itself a judgment of self that will produce its own loop. The pivot is from "I shouldn't judge" to "I notice the pain of judging, I notice what I actually want." See [[dont-make-stop-judging-into-a-should]] (anti-pattern) and [[stick-a-fucking-shit-in-front-of-the-should]].

5. **Be clear about what the judgment is preventing.** The third move from the source: be clear about what you actually want in your life. People do not want to be around the judgmental person. Customers do not want to buy from them. Partners do not want to deepen with them. The clarity about consequences is not used as a stick to should-with; it is used as data the system already has and can return to in any moment. See [[notice-what-judgment-costs-you-and-what-you-actually-want]].

6. **Prepare for what is underneath.** When judgment relaxes, what comes up is often love — and love is the scariest emotion of all because it puts the person at risk of being hurt. The path through judgment opens onto the path through heartbreak. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] and [[letting-judgment-in]].

## The Judgment-as-Fix-Driver Variant

A specific shape: the judgment is operating as the engine of a fix-everything pattern. The person judges the wrong-doers, the unjust, the people who do not treat others the way they should be treated — and the judgment is what propels them to fix the injustice. The judgment and the fix-everything pattern ([[why-am-i-always-trying-to-fix]]) are interlocked: each justifies the other.

In this variant, the descent through judgment to silence is the same descent the fix-pattern needs to discover. "If you couldn't judge them, what would you have to feel?" produces silence, nothingness, quietness — and the fix-activity has been functioning in the same role as the judgment, both maintaining the avoidance of the silence.

When the variant is recognized, the work runs simultaneously on the judgment and on the fixing. Stopping either one releases the same underneath material. The coach does not have to pick which to work; both lead to the same place.

The canonical example: a woman who fixes everything she sees, who frames her fixing as activism against injustice, who judges those who perpetrate the injustice. Joe descends through her judgment ("if you couldn't judge them, what would you feel?" "nothingness, silence") and then names the structure ("if you can't go to judgment, then there's a vastness that makes you very anxious"). The judgment-descent is the entry into the silence the fix-pattern has been holding off. See [[fix-everything-woman-anthill-presence-session]].
