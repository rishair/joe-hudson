# I keep crashing and burning / I go all-in then it all falls apart

## What Someone Says

A recognizable shape that often arrives partially diagnosed already, framed as "a pattern":

- "I have this pattern of diving into something deeply, then it crashes."
- "I get hyperfocused on one thing and everything else falls by the wayside."
- "Every couple years I go through these big upheavals."
- "I dove into [crypto / a relationship / a sport / a project] and disappeared from everything else."
- "My attention just drains out of the rest of my life."
- "I'm a really flowy person and then I commit hard to something and then it falls apart."
- "I do this thing where I go really deep and then I crash and have to put my life back together."

The presentation usually owns the pattern as a personality trait while complaining about the consequences. The person often has language for it already ("attentional rabbit holes," "wild swings"). The intellectual map is built; the body has not yet integrated what the map describes.

## Detection Cues

- The person reports cyclical crashes: months or years of intense focus, then a crash, then rebuild.
- A list of disparate hyperfocus topics (crypto, base jumping, a relationship, a community) treated as variations of the same move.
- Pride mixed with shame: "I learn a lot from it" alongside "but the consequences are painful."
- The basic logistical tasks (taxes, registering a car, mundane life maintenance) keep getting dropped.
- The person believes other people are disappointed in them whether or not the other people have actually said so.
- They describe themselves as "not having their shit together" compared to people who seem more steady.
- They have engineered a life with enough freedom to keep doing this (entrepreneurship, contract work, geographic flexibility).
- Often paired with [[i-should-be-doing-this]] (about the dropped tasks) and [[i-need-to-improve-myself]] (about the pattern itself).

## What Joe Hears

The crashing is not the problem. The shame about the crashing is what locks the cycle. The hyperfocus itself is natural passion; somewhere down the rabbit hole the passion converts to obligation and stress, and somewhere on the other side of the crash the person gets to feel an emotion they could not feel as a kid.

Underneath the cycle: the person is [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]]. The crashes recreate the conditions for a deep heartbreak (and accompanying shame) that was never allowed to be felt. The body keeps generating the conditions because the loop is incomplete. See [[trauma-mapping]] and [[what-we-fear-we-invite]].

The original imprint is often a childhood divergence from a community the person was supposed to belong to (a religion, a family worldview, a small town). The divergence carried the implicit message: by being yourself, you are causing pain to the people who love you. That message gets recreated as adult patterns where intense personal direction (the rabbit hole) creates distance from people the person is supposed to be there for.

"As you start to relive the trauma, maybe it's happening, maybe it's not, of disappointing people around you, that creates an anxiety in you that then gets transferred probably both to them, which makes you want to not be around them, and to your work, which makes you get more hyperfocused and stressed." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person frames the pattern with intellectual confidence but flat body:** the pattern has been described many times and the description has become a way of avoiding the feeling. Joe shifts to body and present-moment ("stop talking about it, feel it"). See [[not-sometimes-right-now]].

- **If the language contains caveats and qualifiers:** the qualifier is doing work. "Disappearing attentionally from other areas" — Joe will question the word "attentionally." Why the modifier? See [[linguistic-caveat]] and [[question-the-caveat]].

- **If the person is in self-judgment about the pattern:** the shame is the lock. Joe will work to surface that the shame itself is what holds the cycle in place. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

- **If the person believes other people are disappointed without evidence:** the believed-disappointment is a projection of the original wound. Joe may ask what the third parties would actually say. See [[ask-the-witnesses]].

- **If the person reaches for "but I do learn a lot from it":** the redemptive frame is a defense. Joe does not argue with it; he separates what the person likes from what they do not like and works the not-liked part. The pride and the pain are both real.

- **If the person starts manufacturing the feeling rather than feeling it:** Joe names the manufacture and redirects. See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]].

- **If a "desire for resolution" surfaces near the end:** the desire is itself the residue of the finish-line story. Joe will use it as material. See [[desire-for-resolution]] and [[what-if-there-is-no-finish-line]].

## Coaching Path

1. **Get the concrete example.** Not the abstract pattern. "Give me an example from two years ago." The example contains the felt material; the abstraction does not.

2. **Question the qualifier words.** Words like "attentionally" or "kind of" are placeholders. They mark a place the person is softening their own experience. Push gently for what is underneath.

3. **Separate what they like from what they do not like.** The wild oscillation often produces things the person wants (learning, intensity, depth) alongside things they do not (crashed logistics, broken commitments, disconnection). Both are real. Do not collapse them into one judgment.

4. **Connect the present pattern to the original imprint.** The hyperfocus-then-disappointing-people shape often reproduces a specific childhood configuration. Name it. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

5. **Locate the unfelt feeling.** What is the cycle generating opportunities to feel? Often shame, helplessness, or heartbreak. Once located, feel it in the body now, not as abstract idea. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

6. **Surface the engineering.** The person is creating the situations that will allow them to almost-feel the avoided feeling. The shame about the avoidance is what completes the loop and keeps the cycle going. See [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]].

7. **Question the finish-line story.** The desire for resolution is the residue of a childhood arrival fantasy (often religious). Without it, what is left to do? See [[what-if-there-is-no-finish-line]] and [[no-finish-line]].

8. **Land on serenity, not resolution.** What surfaces when the story drops is not a fixed answer. It is the relaxation of seeing what is. The work is to notice each new "way of covering up" (how do I keep this? what about my taxes?) as it shows up and to see through it.

## Where People Get Stuck

- They try to manage the pattern instead of feeling what it is generating. See [[enjoy-over-manage]].
- They confuse the relief of seeing the pattern with the work of feeling what was avoided. The intellectual map gets refined while the body stays in the loop.
- They take the new clarity and turn it into a new improvement project: "now I have to stop crashing." This re-installs the cycle at a meta level. See [[tools-to-change-not-love]].
- They grip the serenity that arrives when the story drops. "How do I keep this?" The gripping is the next thing to see through. See [[quit-hope-lose-cycle]].
- They believe they have to be a "creature of habit" person to be okay. They already are a creature of habit; rabbit-holing is the habit. See [[no-finish-line]].
