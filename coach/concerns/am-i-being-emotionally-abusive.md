# Am I being emotionally abusive? / Am I in an emotionally abusive relationship?

## What Someone Says

The concern shows up in several phrasings, often hedged because the word "abuse" feels too strong for the situation being described:

- "Am I being emotionally abusive?"
- "Is what we do really abuse, or is it just fighting?"
- "I think my partner might be emotionally abusive."
- "I think I might be emotionally abusive."
- "My partner says I'm abusive but I don't think I am."
- "Is the silent treatment abuse?"
- "Is yelling abuse?"
- "Is making someone feel guilty all the time abuse?"
- "They collapse every time I bring something up. Is that abuse?"
- "I withhold love when I'm hurt. Is that abuse?"
- "We yell like our parents yelled. That's just how people fight, right?"
- "I'm not sure if this is normal fighting or something worse."

The question often comes with an implicit hope that the answer is no. The word "abuse" carries enough cultural weight that confirming it would require reorganizing the person's understanding of themselves or their relationship.

The question also sometimes comes from the receiving side, where the person is unsure whether what is happening to them counts as abuse, partly because the visible distress is on their partner's side ("they're so hurt every time I try to bring this up") and partly because the moves are quiet rather than loud.

## Detection Cues

Surface signals:
- Asking the question explicitly ("is this abuse?")
- Describing recurring fights with the same dynamic and asking whether the dynamic is normal
- Reporting that they or their partner "checked out," "went cold," or "shut down" repeatedly
- Describing a pattern of feeling guilty after raising any concern
- Describing yelling fights that "just happen" and don't go anywhere
- Reporting that the partner collapses ("can't get out of bed for days") when criticized
- Asking whether withholding love or going silent counts as abuse

Indirect signals:
- The relationship is described as one where the speaker has to manage the partner's emotions
- The speaker reports walking on eggshells
- The speaker describes the partner as fragile in a way that constrains their own behavior
- The speaker describes themselves as the bad guy in a dynamic where they cannot identify what they did
- The speaker describes a parent's fighting style and asks if their current relationship is the same
- The fighting style the speaker describes matches what they saw growing up but they have started to wonder about it

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is usually one of three situations.

**The first**: the person grew up with a fighting style that was modeled as normal and is starting to wonder whether it is. The recognition is in early stages. The default is "this is what people do." The question itself is the early move toward seeing that the default is not the only option. See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the canonical Joe-and-Tara story of this recognition.

**The second**: the person is doing emotional control without recognizing it, and the partner has started to push back. The question is being asked because the partner has named the dynamic as abusive and the speaker is trying to test whether the framing is correct. The honest answer often is yes, by the working definition, even if no physical violence is present.

**The third**: the person is on the receiving end of the quieter forms of emotional control (collapse, withdrawal, guilt-tripping) and is unsure whether to call what is happening abuse because the visible suffering is on the other side. See [[aggressive-victim-stance]] and [[withdrawal-of-love-as-passive-aggression]] for the patterns at play.

The working definition: emotional abuse is using your emotion to try to control someone. The emotion can be anger, guilt, sadness, fear, or withdrawal of love. The defining feature is the control intent, not the volume or which emotion is being deployed. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for the full elaboration.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point. The concern goes different directions depending on what the speaker is actually describing.

- If they are describing the loud version (yelling, threatening, anger as the lever): the path goes through [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] and the recognition that the modeled-as-normal fighting style is by-definition abusive. The work is not moral correction; it is developing the capacity to fight from yourself rather than from your trauma. See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] for the in-the-moment move.

- If they are describing the quiet version on themselves (withdrawal of love, going cold, "I'm just protecting myself"): the path goes through [[withdrawal-of-love-as-passive-aggression]] and the recognition that the protection framing is the cover. The work includes seeing what the closure costs the speaker, not just what it costs the partner.

- If they are describing the collapsed version on themselves or their partner (breaking down, "can't get out of bed," weeks-long shutdown when criticized): the path goes through the weak-collapsed-form section of [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] and through [[aggressive-victim-stance]]. The collapsed form is the hardest to see because the surface presentation is suffering. The function is the same as yelling.

- If they are describing being on the receiving end and questioning whether to name it: the path includes [[aggressive-victim-stance]] and the diagnostic of whether the visible distress is being deployed to constrain their behavior. Real wronged-party distress welcomes resolution. Aggressive-victim distress requires the problem to persist.

- If they are describing the guilt-tripping or fear-deployment version: the path goes through [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] and the cross-emotion principle that the control mechanism is the same regardless of which emotion is being used.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow does not start with confirming or denying the label. It starts with the working definition and then asks whether the specific behaviors fit.

1. **Surface the working definition.** Emotional abuse is using your emotion to try to control someone. The emotion can be any emotion. The control intent is what matters. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for the full version.

2. **Walk through the specific behaviors the speaker is describing.** For each behavior, the question is: is the emotion being deployed to make the other person do, stop, or change something? The honest yes/no answer to that question is the diagnostic.

3. **Distinguish abuse from non-abuse.** Strong emotion expressed cleanly (felt first, then communicated) is not abuse. Truth spoken with an open heart, even when hard, is not abuse. A boundary that includes the speaker's own actions ("if you continue yelling I will leave for 20 minutes") is not abuse. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for the specific distinction on leaving and [[boundaries]] for the general one.

4. **If the answer is "this is abuse," ground the recognition in the trauma frame rather than the moral frame.** The behaviors usually came from somewhere. They were modeled in the family of origin or developed as the only available move in early relationships. The work is not "I am a bad person." The work is "I am running an old pattern and I can develop a different one." See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]].

5. **Move to the deal-energy frame for the practical argument.** Control does not work even on its own terms. The energy required to produce the compliance is the energy required to maintain it. The system breaks eventually. See [[management-required-to-launch-predicts-management-required-forever]].

6. **Introduce the positive alternative.** See [[see-them-be-seen-find-alignment-instead-of-control]]. The four orientations that replace control: see them, understand where they are, allow yourself to be seen, find a solution that works for both.

7. **If the person is on the receiving end and the partner refuses to recognize the pattern**: the path branches toward boundaries, self-care, and sometimes the harder conversation about whether the relationship can continue in its current form. See [[see-yourself-when-they-cant-see-you]] for the inner-peace move when the other party is incapable of the recognition.

The arc is typically: definition, application to the specific behaviors, recognition (or refinement of which form is at play), trauma frame, practical argument, positive alternative.
