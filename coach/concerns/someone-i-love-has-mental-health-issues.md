# Someone I love has mental health issues / How do I be in this relationship?

## What Someone Says

The presenting language describes a relationship that used to work and no longer works in the same way, with the change attributed to the other person's mental health. The complaint is usually a mix of grief, frustration, guilt, and not-knowing-what-to-do.

- "My [relative / partner / friend] used to be someone I could really connect with, but their mental health has changed."
- "I don't know how to be in this relationship anymore."
- "How do I help them?"
- "What can I do for them, and what can I do for myself?"
- "I want to be there for them but I'm exhausted."
- "I feel guilty that I can't fix this."
- "They aren't who they used to be."

The presenting concern is usually framed as "what should I do," but underneath it are several separate threads that need to be distinguished: grief for the relationship that was, the limits of what one person can do for another, the question of self-care, the question of boundaries, and the question of when professional help is needed.

## Detection Cues

- Reports of a relationship that used to be close and has shifted.
- Naming someone else's mental health condition (depression, Alzheimer's, schizophrenia, bipolar, addiction, severe anxiety) as the reason connection has become hard.
- Exhaustion from supporting the other person.
- Resentment that has started to creep in.
- Self-blame for not being able to help more, or for needing to step back.
- An implicit assumption that good love means continuing the relationship as it was.
- An implicit assumption that taking care of self is selfish in this context.

## What Joe Hears

The relationship is going through an actual loss. The person has not yet grieved that loss because they are still trying to hold the old relationship in place while the conditions for it have changed. Until the grief lands, the new form of the relationship cannot stabilize.

Several substructures usually need to be addressed:

**The expectations have to be released.** A friend who has a physical injury and is now paralyzed is not going for walks anymore. The friendship continues, but it changes form. Mental health works the same way. Expecting the person to be rational, available, present, or coherent in the old way when their mental state has changed is asking them for something they cannot give. The expectation becomes a source of suffering for both sides.

"If I have a friend who had an injury and is paralyzed, I'm not going to go for walks with him anymore. Your friendship is going to change. And if your friend is going through mental health, you can't expect them to, depending on the mental health thing, be rational, or to be able to be connected with you in the same way, or be able to not get paranoid around you, or whatever the situation is, or be even like in this reality, so to speak." -- Joe Hudson

**The old patterns of relating to them have to be examined.** A common dynamic with Alzheimer's: the family continues to defer to the parent as if the parent's reactions and demands still represent considered authority. They do not. The continued deference is a habit of relating to the previous version of the person, not a useful response to the current one. "You shouldn't be doing what he says. Probably shouldn't have before. But you're interacting with them as if they're still coherent when they're not coherent." -- Joe Hudson

There is genuine relief available in this recognition. The pattern of relating to who they were is producing much of the friction. Updating the pattern to who they actually are now removes a category of suffering that was being mistaken for inevitable.

**Self-care is not optional.** Sacrificing yourself in service of the other person's mental health crisis does not help them and harms you. "You're not doing anybody any good by sacrificing yourself in it. And eventually, if you do sacrifice yourself in it long enough, either you're going to join them in the kind of swirl, or you're going to just resent them and lash out at some point, neither of which is going to feel good." -- Joe Hudson

The two predictable failure modes of sustained self-sacrifice: joining them in the destabilization (the helper becomes destabilized too), or resentment that eventually breaks out as lashing. Both are predictable. Both are prevented by maintaining your own care infrastructure.

**Boundaries are central, not peripheral.** Boundaries with a person in mental health crisis are not unkindness. They are the structural prerequisite for sustained presence. Without them, presence becomes resentment, presence becomes merger, presence becomes nothing.

**Professional help is part of the picture for both parties.** The other person needs professional care. You need support too -- not because you are sick, but because the load of being in close relationship with someone in a mental health crisis is genuinely heavy and merits the same kind of community support as any other major life challenge.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point. Two people with the same concern go very different directions based on what Joe notices:

- If the person is sliding into caretaking and self-sacrifice: the path goes through [[caring-vs-caretaking]] and [[losing-yourself-in-others]]. The work is restoring self-care and boundaries before continued involvement is possible.
- If the person is presenting frustration that the other person is not who they used to be: the path goes through grief. The relationship has changed. The form that loved them then has to release into the form that can love them now.
- If the person is positioning themselves as the one who has to fix this: the path goes through [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] and [[the-work-is-yours-not-mine]]. They are not the cure. They cannot be the cure. Continuing to try is part of what is exhausting them.
- If the person is reporting they have no support, no professional help, and have been carrying this alone: the path goes through the practical reality that this load is bigger than any individual relationship can hold. Professional help, support groups for people supporting others (Al-Anon, NAMI family support, dementia caregiver groups, etc.) are the answer.

## Coaching Path

1. **Distinguish the threads.** The presenting question "how do I be in this relationship" usually contains several separable concerns: grief for the relationship that was, limits of what you can do, self-care, boundaries, and professional support. Treat each one on its own terms rather than answering the conflated whole.

2. **Acknowledge the loss.** The person they used to be is gone or partially gone. The relationship that ran on that version of them is also gone or partially gone. This is a real grief and it deserves real acknowledgement. See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]]. Until this grief lands, the new form of the relationship has nowhere to settle into.

3. **Release the expectations.** Stop expecting the person to be who they used to be. Notice the specific places where the habit of relating to the previous version is still running. Update the relating pattern to who is actually in front of you now.

4. **Center self-care.** This is not optional. It is the prerequisite for sustained presence. The work routes through [[caring-vs-caretaking]] and [[stay-centered-while-holding-space]]. You cannot serve them from depletion.

5. **Set the boundaries the situation requires.** Boundaries with someone in mental health crisis are not unkindness. They are infrastructure. See [[boundaries]].

6. **Get professional help -- for them AND for you.** The professional help for them is the obvious move. The professional help for you (a therapist, a support group, a community of others in similar situations) is the less-obvious move that prevents the slide into caretaking and resentment.

7. **Allow the relationship to take its new form.** The relationship is not what it was. It can become something else. That something else is found by being present with what is, not by trying to recover what was.

## What This Concern Is Not

This concern is not "I should be able to fix this." That framing is itself the trap. The other person has their own work, their own healing, their own life. You cannot heal them. You can love them within whatever form the relationship takes now. See [[the-work-is-yours-not-mine]] and [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] -- in the version where the person's struggle is theirs to walk through and your job is not to remove it from them.

This concern is also not "I should keep showing up the way I always did until they are better." That framing collapses the relationship into a project of restoration that the other person cannot consent to and that you cannot sustain. The relationship has changed. The change is real. Loving them well means loving the version of them that is here, not refusing to update.

## Tone of the Response

The response is warm and direct, but unsentimental. The situation is hard. The teaching is not "this should be easier than you think." The teaching is "here are the parts of the hard thing that can be addressed and here is the part that has to be grieved."

There is no minimization of the loss. There is also no encouragement to sacrifice yourself in the name of being a good friend or family member. The honest reality is that sustained self-sacrifice in this context produces worse outcomes for both parties, not better.
