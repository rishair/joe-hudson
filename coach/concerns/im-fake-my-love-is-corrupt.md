# I'm fake / My love is corrupt / I want something from it

## What Someone Says

People presenting this concern are organized around a specific fear: that the love they feel is not real love because they want something from it. They have an internal scorecard that says clean love is love without an agenda, and they have caught themselves having an agenda, and therefore concluded they are fake.

- "I think it's about I'm fake, something like that"
- "It's like I don't really love, but I have some sort of hidden agenda that I'm spreading love, but I want something from it"
- "Love is dangerous"
- "When I feel love, I'm constantly mesmerized with questions like, am I corrupt? Is my motivation corrupt?"
- "I can be very cunning, and I did not love that part"
- "People who were authority for me, my mother, my husband, my father, my sisters, they would say that my intention was corrupt"
- "Somehow [my cunningness] used to be the opposite of love for me"

The concern often arrives after someone close to them named them corrupt at some point — a mother, a partner, a sibling. The person took that judgment in and now interrogates themselves with it constantly. They have an indecisiveness about it: they cannot land on whether they are 1% fake, 50% fake, or totally fake. The percentage shifts. The interrogation does not stop.

## Detection Cues

- The word "fake" or "corrupt" or "hidden agenda" applied to themselves about love
- A constant internal interrogation about whether their love is "real"
- Stagnation: love feels available and then they pull back because of the corruption question
- A history of being called corrupt or manipulative by an authority figure
- Inability to land on a specific calibration ("I can't understand how fake I am")
- The frame: wanting something from love = the love is not love
- Often paired with an inability to imagine themselves loved with their wanting included
- "Love is dangerous" or some equivalent — the body has learned love is risky

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this is not corruption at all. Underneath is [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]]: a childhood imprint in which the person learned that *wanting* makes them unlovable. The parent (often a mother) could not provide what the child wanted, felt shame about that, and shut off love rather than feel the shame. The child experienced the love withdrawal and concluded: when I want something, I am not lovable.

The adult version: any time the person feels love that contains wanting (which is most love), the old wiring fires. Wanting is detected. Love-withdrawal is anticipated. The person preempts the anticipated withdrawal by rejecting their own love as fake. The accusation "I'm corrupt" is not an honest self-assessment. It is a defense: if I reject my love first, the imagined other will not have to.

The "cunningness" the person hides is usually just the will of a child. Every five-year-old is cunning. Every three-year-old is cunning. The cunningness was never the problem. What got wired with the cunningness — the parent's shame and the resulting love-withdrawal — is the problem.

The frame "love is dangerous" is the body remembering. Love is not dangerous now. Love was dangerous then, because love came with the threat of being withdrawn the moment the child wanted something the parent could not provide.

See [[obligation-cuts-off-love]] and [[responsibility-stops-love]] for the mechanism on the parent's side: the parent traded love for the responsibility-posture about not being able to provide. See [[shame-as-disconnection]] for why the parent's shame produced the withdrawal.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If [[the-pulled-back-from-part-is-the-unloveable-part]]: when the person scans for the part of themselves that cannot be loved, the body retreats from a specific image. Joe watches for that retreat. The thing they retreat from is the part they fear cannot be loved. He goes to that.
- If [[any-amount-answer-as-total-installation]]: the person cannot calibrate ("I can't understand how fake I am"). The "any amount of wanting means I'm fake" answer signals the belief is operating at the body level, fully installed, not yet seen.
- If [[treating-felt-state-as-factual-reality]]: "I am fake" is being reported as a fact, not as a felt experience. The work is to surface that this is an interpretation, not a circumstance.
- If [[i-turned-out-fine]] or its inverse — defending the parental dynamic that installed this. Watch for the person protecting the mother or father whose shame produced the love-withdrawal.
- If [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]] or [[disassociating-in-the-coaching-room]]: when the work approaches the original moment of love-withdrawal, the body may try to leave.

## Coaching Path

The arc is short and direct when the imprint can be located.

1. **Refuse the moral frame.** When the person says "I'm fake / corrupt," Joe does not agree, dispute, or reassure. He asks [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]]: "What's wrong with that? What's wrong with wanting something from love? What's wrong with being corrupt around love?" The refusal of moral collapse is itself the first move. It introduces a frame in which the supposedly damning fact is not damning.

2. **Calibrate the totality.** Then [[calibrate-the-threshold]]: "How much? 1% fake? 2% fake?" The unanswerability of the question is the data. When the person cannot give a percentage, they are reporting that the belief is fully installed at the body level, operating before calibration is available.

3. **Have them say what they cannot believe.** Then [[have-them-say-i-want-all-of-me-loved]]: get them to speak the welcoming aloud. "I'd love to hear you just say: I want all of me loved." This is not affirmation. It is a probe — Joe is watching the body for what part cannot accept the welcoming.

4. **Scan the body for the unloveable part.** Then [[scan-body-for-what-cant-be-loved]]: "Take a second, scan your body, see what part of you can't be loved in your mind, in your heart. What is there? Any part of you?" The scan is not metaphor. The body produces a specific retreat from a specific image. Joe watches for it.

5. **Name the retreat in real time.** When the body pulls back, [[name-the-body-defense-in-real-time]]: "Look what just happened. Where did you retreat into? Where'd you go? You just pulled back." The retreat IS the diagnostic. The thing they retreated from is the part they hide.

6. **Reframe the hidden part.** Whatever the part is (cunning, manipulative, wanting-too-much, controlling), Joe normalizes it without bypassing. "Can you imagine a 5-year-old girl who is cunning? I don't know any. I don't know any teenage girl that isn't cunning. I love my girls' cunningness." The reframe is: the part you hide is not corruption, it is the will of a child.

7. **Trace to the original installation.** Once the person has touched the hidden part, Joe goes to the childhood imprint: "Of course, because your parents — every time you wanted something, your parents felt guilty they couldn't give it to you. So they didn't want to feel it." Then: "Feel your mom for a second. How did your mom feel when she couldn't give you what you wanted?" The work is to feel the parent's shame, which is the actual mechanism behind the love-withdrawal. See [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]].

8. **Name what got wired.** "She got off the love. And you heard that as, when I go for what I want, I'm not lovable." Naming the wiring is what makes the dissolution possible.

9. **Anchor the new state in the body.** When the person has touched the original imprint, the body comes back online. Joe names it: "There you are, whole body you." See [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]].

The whole arc can happen in under twenty turns when the person is available. The work is not analytical. It is somatic, located, specific.
