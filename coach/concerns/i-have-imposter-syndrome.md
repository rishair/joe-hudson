# I have imposter syndrome / I feel like an imposter

## What Someone Says

- "I have imposter syndrome"
- "I feel like a fraud"
- "I don't know what I'm doing"
- "Everyone else seems to know what they're doing"
- "I'm going to be found out"
- "I shouldn't be here"
- "I just got lucky"
- "I'm faking it until I make it but it's exhausting"
- "How do I get rid of my imposter syndrome?"
- "I keep trying to convince myself I'm not an imposter and it doesn't work"

## Detection Cues

The presenting form is usually a request for reassurance or a strategy. "I keep telling myself I'm qualified but the feeling won't go away." "I have all the credentials but I still feel like a fraud." The person has typically been trying to *convince themselves out of it* using the evidence -- and noticing that the evidence does not actually support the convincing.

Indirect signals: speaking about a role or accomplishment with hedging language ("I sort of run a company"), preemptively pointing out their gaps before anyone else can, attributing success to luck or to other people, anxiety spikes around being observed by people they perceive as the "real" experts.

A specific signature: the person is in a loop trying to argue themselves out of the feeling and is losing the argument. They are reaching for proof that they are not an imposter, and the proof is not arriving.

## What Joe Hears

The person is trying to win an argument they cannot win, because the underlying claim is true: they do not know what they are doing. Anybody doing anything real does not fully know what they are doing -- that is what makes it real. See [[everyone-doing-something-real-is-an-imposter]].

"If you think you're an imposter, it's because you are, right? None of -- nobody who's doing anything important in the world knows how to do it. They're figuring it out as they go." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

The fix is not to win the argument but to dissolve the frame. The frame says "some people know what they're doing and I am a defective version because I don't." The dissolving move is to ask who in fact does know what they're doing -- and to discover that no one does, and that the discomfort is universal.

Underneath the imposter feeling is a specific kind of fear: the fear of stepping into a bigger room. That fear is the body's signal that something new is being attempted. It is the same energy as excitement, with the breath constricted. See [[fear-vs-excitement]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person is in their head trying to win the argument with themselves: Joe goes to [[who-isnt-an-imposter]] -- the diagnostic question that dissolves the frame rather than settling the argument.
- If the person is gripping for someone-who-knows: the underlying want is for someone to tell them they are right, or for some form of perfect to exist that they can reach. Joe names that no such thing exists and lets the ground fall out. See [[perceived-certainty-not-actual-certainty]] and the connection to [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]].
- If the person is treating the feeling as a problem to make go away: the issue is the strategy of management, not the feeling. Joe moves to the felt sense: the imposter feeling is fear of a bigger room, which is the body activating, which is excitement without the breath. The practice [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] is for this.
- If the person is using imposter syndrome to avoid action: Joe may also point at what the imposter feeling is protecting them from feeling. See [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]].
- If the person is collapsed into shame about being an imposter (rather than just naming the experience): the shame is the operating problem, not the imposter feeling. Treat the shame first. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

## Coaching Path

1. Ask the dissolving question: [[who-isnt-an-imposter]]. Let the person try to name one. Watch what happens when they realize the category is empty.
2. State the principle directly: anyone doing anything of consequence does not know what they are doing. An [[expert-is-imposter-with-most-reps]]. The kindergarten frame: it does not bother you that you are a kindergartner if everyone is a kindergartner.
3. Address the desire for someone to tell them they are right. Name that there is no perfect company, no perfect book, no right way. The ground falls out. Stay with that for a beat.
4. Bring in [[only-your-own-judgment-matters]] -- the only judgment that ends up mattering is your own judgment of how you lived.
5. Move to the body: the imposter feeling is fear, and that fear is excitement without the breath. Offer [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] as the in-the-moment intervention. Offer [[list-people-you-admire-and-their-mistakes]] as the take-home experiment.
