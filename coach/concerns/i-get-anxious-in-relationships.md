# I get anxious in relationships / I don't like being seen

## What Someone Says

The presenting language is some form of "I get anxious when other people can see me," delivered with the assumption that the anxiety is mysterious and untreatable. Common phrasings:

- "When I'm in a relationship, I get super, super anxious very quickly."
- "I don't know why that happens or what to do about it."
- "I have a lot of questions. I feel like they're all kind of the same question."
- "I definitely don't like being seen. I like to hide myself."
- "I'm either putting on a mask or I'm just running away from people."
- "I do this in public all the time."
- "I freak out the moment I'm called on."

The person often arrives carrying the assumption that the anxiety is the problem to be solved and the solution is some technique they have not yet learned. The same person often presents the anxiety happening in real time the moment the conversation starts — getting called on, being put on the spot, being seen in a group.

## Detection Cues

The concern is recognizable both when named directly ("I get anxious in relationships") and when the anxiety is showing up in the room without being named:

- The person flinches at being addressed or chosen ("Oh fuck. I didn't expect that.").
- The person multiplies their questions, says they have many but they're all the same — a way of staying with a category without committing to a single question.
- The person describes the same dynamic in multiple relational contexts: dating, public situations, the current coaching conversation.
- The person uses "mask or run" language for how they manage social presence.
- The anxiety is happening live in the room and the person is describing it as a thing that happens elsewhere.

The decisive cue: the person is anxious *right now*, while presenting the problem of being anxious *in relationships*. The two are the same problem at different scales.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the surface concern is the structure of [[the-desire-to-be-seen]] running against the [[false-front]]. The biological need to be seen is intact. The shame layer that says "if you see me you'll see what's wrong with me" is also intact. The "mask or run" report is the operational form of the contradiction: present the mask, or refuse to present at all. Being actually seen — without the mask, without the running — is the configuration the person has never let happen.

The anxiety is not the problem. The anxiety is the felt presence of the contradiction. It is what shows up when the wanting-to-be-seen and the fearing-to-be-seen are both active in the same body at the same time. Joe does not treat the anxiety as something to be removed. He treats it as the entry point to the work.

What is being hidden is almost never what the person thinks. The person assumes they are hiding some specific defect or content — that there is something wrong with them, and that the seeing would expose the wrong thing. The actual content of the hiding, once it is allowed to surface, is usually emotion. The person is hiding their emotions. The mask is the no-emotion face. The running is the leaving before the emotion is visible. See [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]] for why this is structurally costly.

The shame that drives the hiding has no actual content. When the question "what's wrong with you?" is asked directly into the shame, the body almost always answers: nothing. See [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

This concern branches based on what Joe notices in how the person is showing up in the room:

- If the anxiety is happening live (the person is anxious about being called on, anxious about answering, anxious about the coaching itself): Joe brings the work into the present. He treats the current moment as the example. "So let's just talk about how it happened right now." See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]].

- If the person is using "I don't know" as a stopping point: Joe pushes through. The "I don't know" is often where the actual feeling begins. See [[give-me-a-good-attempt]].

- If the person starts [[interrupting-your-own-descent]] — sinking down into the felt territory but then surfacing with an intellectual answer: Joe names the descent and tells them to stay in it without trying to answer. The answer is the abandonment of the descent.

- If the person is [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]]: the speech is being delivered from the meta-position, monitoring how it's landing. The masking is operating in real time.

- If the person collapses into shame in the room ("Oh fuck, I'm so..."): Joe meets the shame head-on by asking what's wrong with them, but he does it warmly. See [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]].

- If the person assumes the listeners/audience are judging them: Joe tests the projection against the actual room. See [[is-the-shame-in-the-room-or-in-you]] — particularly the audience-vote variant where Joe asks listeners to disconfirm the imagined judgment.

## Coaching Path

The typical sequence from this concern is short and structurally clean:

1. **Use the live moment.** The anxiety is happening now. There is no need to recreate it. Joe pivots immediately from the abstract presentation to the in-the-room version: "How is it working for you that you're being seen right now?" See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]].

2. **Name the structure the person has already named.** The person says they don't like being seen. Joe accepts the answer without dismissing it: "So that's the reason." This is not redundant. It validates the diagnostic the person has already made and removes the search-for-a-deeper-answer that would distract from the work.

3. **Go into the shame.** Behind "I don't like being seen" is the assumption that something is wrong. Joe asks the shame to name itself: "What's wrong with you? What is it that I'm gonna see that I'm gonna think is bad?" See [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]].

4. **When the person starts going down into the felt place, instruct them to stay.** The default move is to interrupt the descent with an intellectual answer. The instruction is to keep going down without needing to find an answer. "You don't need to find an answer, just keep going there for a minute." See [[interrupting-your-own-descent]].

5. **If the person is afraid of being judged by the listeners, disconfirm in real time.** Use the actual audience to break the projected shame. The "raise your hand if you have no problem being with his tears" variant of [[is-the-shame-in-the-room-or-in-you]] does this in a group setting.

6. **Let the recognition arrive.** The person discovers that what they have been hiding is not a defective self but their own emotions. The mask is the no-emotion face. The running is the leaving before emotion is visible. The recognition reframes the anxiety: it is not "something is wrong with me," it is "I have been hiding my emotions and now they are arriving."

The whole sequence can land in five minutes when the person is already on the verge of the descent.

## Related Concerns

- [[i-want-to-be-authentic-on-stage]] — the performance version of the same structure. A stand-up comic asking how to be authentic on stage is asking the relational-anxiety question scaled up. The mask-or-run pattern there is "perform-or-freeze." The shame layer is the same. The work is structurally identical: bring the live moment into the room, name what is being hidden (almost always emotion), restore connection. The on-stage version has more concentrated exposure and a fixed-length window; the relational version has more time and intimacy.

- [[im-not-comfortable-sharing-my-story]] — the autobiographical-disclosure version of the same structure. A person who wants to share their personal story (often a recovery story, an addiction story, a survival story) but can't quite get themselves to do it comfortably. The work has the same architecture: bring the moment into the room, name what's being hidden, find that what's being hidden is emotion (often anger first, then sadness, then deeper layers). The story-sharing version often has the additional resource of a past survival skill that can be paralleled to the present work (see [[parallel-the-clients-own-survival-skill-to-the-present]]).
