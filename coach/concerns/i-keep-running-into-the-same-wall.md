# I keep running into the same wall / There's something I'm not seeing

## What Someone Says

The concern can show up as direct frustration with a repeating pattern, or as an articulate self-aware framing using the language of limiting beliefs.

Direct forms:
- "I keep doing the same thing and I don't know why."
- "I keep getting stuck in the same place."
- "I've tried everything and nothing works."
- "I see what I'm doing but I can't stop doing it."
- "The same kind of relationship/job/conflict keeps showing up."

Self-aware forms (often after some exposure to the framework):
- "I think I have a limiting belief about X."
- "There's something I'm not seeing."
- "I think I have a blind spot."
- "I know I'm doing this to myself but I can't figure out what's underneath."

The presenting feature is recursion. Whatever the surface situation, the pattern keeps recreating itself.

## Detection Cues

- The person describes the same dynamic across multiple situations.
- "Every time I try to X, Y happens."
- The person can articulate the pattern but cannot dissolve it.
- The person's effort to change the behavior has only produced more sophisticated versions of the same behavior.
- A felt constriction reliably appears in a specific context.
- The available options have collapsed to two (see [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]]).
- A specific area of life feels stuck, like a chore, or stagnated.
- The person frames their identity around the pattern: "I'm just the kind of person who..."
- Self-improvement work has not moved this particular thing.

## What Joe Hears

A limiting belief is operating. See [[limiting-belief]]. The behavior is downstream of the belief, which is why working at the behavior level has not worked. The belief itself is what needs to dissolve.

The specific shape of the limiting belief matters. There are three structural types -- false thought, misinterpretation of experience, thinking you know the whole story. The intervention shape differs across the three. See [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs]].

The wall is not the situation. The wall is a belief the person is taking as fact. The situation keeps confirming the belief because the person is reading the situation through the belief. The recursion is the structure of the trap.

"It's not so much about, it's, this isn't like a behavior control methodology. This is a realization methodology. So once you get it, if you get it in your whole body, not just your brain, if you get it in, if you grok the whole thing, then things are going to change. But you don't get to decide what changes." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same statement points different directions based on what is underneath.

- If the person already has the language of limiting beliefs and is asking how to dismantle one they have already named: check whether they have named the right one. The named belief is often the cover for a deeper one. See [[find-what-you-absolutely-believe]].

- If the person feels trapped: the limiting belief is probably a [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs|false thought]]. The trap is felt as real but is not real. See [[trappedness-feeling]].

- If the person describes life as a chore or is in "I'll be happy when X" mode: the limiting belief is probably a [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs|misinterpretation of experience]]. The present is being read through a story it does not have to have.

- If the person is stagnant or insists "that's just how it is": the limiting belief is probably a [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs|whole-story belief]]. Some angle of information is being refused.

- If the person is presenting a defining feature of themselves with absolute certainty: the move may be the direct refusal. See [[i-dont-buy-your-story]].

- If the person is using the limiting-belief frame to explain others' behavior more than their own: see [[using-limiting-belief-frame-to-judge-others]].

- If the person is in the meta-trap ("I have to see through all my limiting beliefs to be happy"): see [[limiting-belief-meta-trap]].

- If the person has had an intellectual insight and is frustrated that the behavior has not changed: see [[mind-unsees-first-body-last]]. The insight is the first layer; the body and nervous system have not yet caught up. The work continues at those layers.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow:

1. **Use the felt sense to locate the area.** Constriction, tension, stress in a specific context. The body knows where the belief lives. See [[limiting-belief-detection-signals]].

2. **Identify which kind of limiting belief is operating.** Trappedness, chore-quality, or stagnation each point at a different type. See [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs]].

3. **Find the specific belief.** Look at what the person absolutely believes to be true in this area. The unquestioned thing is the culprit. See [[find-what-you-absolutely-believe]].

4. **Begin the dismantling work.** Inquiry at the head layer. See [[inquiry-on-limiting-beliefs]]. Then move into the emotions (let the feeling underneath move). Then into the body (let the body find a different shape).

5. **Hold the expectations honestly.** The mind will release the belief first. The body will release it last. Behavior will change in ways that cannot be predicted in advance. The experiential outcome (more freedom, more joy) is what can be promised. See [[dissolution-not-behavior-control]] and [[mind-unsees-first-body-last]].

6. **Stay alert to the meta-traps.** "I should be doing this work" is itself a limiting belief. So is "I have to see through every limiting belief to be okay." See [[limiting-belief-meta-trap]].

## What Joe Does Not Do

- Does not engage the surface problem on its own terms. The behavior is not the leverage point.
- Does not promise that a specific outcome will follow.
- Does not let the person turn the framework into a tool for judging others.
- Does not let the work become another should.

## The Other Path: Pattern as Unfelt Emotion (The Matrix Framing)

A second major path the coaching takes from this concern is the [[the-matrix]] / [[trauma-mapping]] / [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] framing rather than the limiting-belief framing above. Both are operating in most cases; which one to lead with depends on the read.

**Lead with limiting belief when:** the recursion has a clearly cognitive surface (the person keeps articulating a belief about how the world is, a story that determines what they let themselves try, an "I am the kind of person who" identity claim that has fused with their behavior).

**Lead with Matrix / unfelt-emotion when:** the recursion has a clearly relational or affective surface (the person keeps ending up with the same kind of partner, the same kind of boss, the same kind of fight; the recurring problem is about how they keep feeling rather than about what they think they know; the pattern operates below the level of articulated belief).

The two framings are not in tension. The limiting belief is often the cognitive housing for the unfelt emotion; the unfelt emotion is often what makes the limiting belief unfalsifiable through ordinary reasoning. But the entry door matters. If the person is led through belief-dissolution work when the structure is emotional, the work produces understanding without movement. If the person is led through emotion-welcoming work when the structure is cognitive, the work produces emotion without insight.

A useful diagnostic question for picking the path: "Is the pattern more about what you keep believing, or about what you keep feeling?" The person's first response usually points at the right entry door. See [[noticing-the-matrix-pattern]] for the three-step diagnostic that operates on the emotional side.
