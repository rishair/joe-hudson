# I'll become lazy if I stop should-ing myself

## What Someone Says

- "If I didn't tell myself I should, I'd just sit on the couch all day."
- "I need the pressure or nothing gets done."
- "Without discipline I'd fall apart."
- "I'd just play video games and never get anything done."
- "I'd become a couch potato."
- "If I followed my wants I'd just eat cake all day."

## Detection Cues

The concern often surfaces immediately when the idea of dropping shoulds is introduced. The person assumes that the should is the only thing standing between them and total collapse. There is genuine fear in their tone.

The concern also shows up as resistance to any framing that trusts the self: "But people are lazy by nature," "You have to push yourself," "Discipline is what separates successful people."

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is a specific belief: I am not inherently good. Without the whip, I will be lazy, gluttonous, and selfish. See [[inherent-goodness]].

The belief is treated as self-evident by the person holding it, but it is bizarre when examined. The person would never apply this logic to another person they respect ("unless I tell Joe he should do a podcast, he'd never do one"). They only apply it to themselves.

"Unless I tell Joe that he should do a podcast, he's just never gonna fucking do it. So I gotta tell him he should do it. It is like, it's a nonsensical thing to really think. Like, here I am telling, doing this podcast. Nobody told me I should do it. I wanted to do it." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The concern also reveals that the person has never experienced what their wants feel like when freed from shoulds. They are predicting based on a model of themselves that the should has constructed.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the person genuinely believes they are inherently lazy:** the work is at the level of [[inherent-goodness]]. The oak tree question. The developmental argument from 0 to 7.
- **If the person is exhausted and the concern is "I'd just collapse":** Joe will validate this. If someone has been under threat for years, they will need rest when the threat lifts. That is not laziness; it is recovery. The actual couch potato pattern only happens when burnout meets new shoulds ("I should stop being on the couch").
- **If the person is afraid of their own desires:** Joe will go to [[the-want]] and [[wanting-vs-craving]]. The fear is often that the want will be ugly, but clean wants are not ugly. The fear comes from confusing want with craving.
- **If the person has children:** Joe sometimes uses the developmental argument. Children under 7 have no shoulds and learn faster than at any other time of life. They are not lazy. They follow wants.

## What Joe Says About It

"You actually may become a couch potato for a while, which sounds a little weird, but the thing is, if you have been under threat for an extended period of time, there's going to be a need to relax. There's going to be a need to recover. So if you're going cold turkey on your shoulds, yeah, it's -- you might actually just need to slow down for a bit. It's not going to be a couch potato. The couch potato thing happens when you burn out and then you tell yourself you shouldn't be burning out." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The example from schools: kids taken out of school after burnout do nothing for 5 or 6 months. Then they learn 3 or 4 times faster than they had in school. The rest is not laziness. It is the body finding its way back to wanting.

"My experience is that people who are most generative in their life are people who want to do shit. Not who feel like they should do them." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Coaching Path

1. Validate that the fear is real and widely shared.
2. Examine the underlying belief: would they apply this logic to someone they respect? Why is it only true for them?
3. Address [[inherent-goodness]] directly. The oak tree question. The baby who is not a failure for crawling.
4. Permission for the rest: if there is collapse after dropping shoulds, it is recovery, not the new permanent state.
5. Locate even one small want and let it move. The experience of following a clean want for ten minutes changes the prediction.
6. Reframe: the people who are most productive are people in their wants, not people in their shoulds.

## The Fear-Driven Motivation Variant

The same concern shows up in a parallel form not about shoulds but about fear: "I'm scared to let go of my fear because my fear is what motivates me. If I felt secure, I would do nothing." Same architecture, same fear of collapse without the whip, same prediction about a couch and a beer.

The work is the same in shape: what does the person actually want, underneath the fear? Where does action come from when it is not coming from fear? The question is often disorienting because the person has not been in a position to find out. The action has always come from the fear, so the action without the fear has never been observed and cannot be predicted.

"If we motivate ourself in the world through fear, then we're very scared to let go of the fear because then we think we'll be unmotivated. A lot of people, if they really deeply confront that kind of itch that they can never scratch, they're scared to lose it because they're scared that they'll be a couch potato drinking beer. And what's really interesting is where does action come from if it doesn't come from that fear? Where does action come from if you feel secure?" -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

The opening into this work is to treat "where does action come from when not from fear" as a real question worth investigating, not a rhetorical one. The answer, when found, is usually a quality of wanting that the fear has been mimicking and crowding out.
