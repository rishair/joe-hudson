# Should we have kids? / What if we have kids, what if we don't?

## What Someone Says

This concern arrives in the form of two trajectories the person is trying to compare. They are not asking about logistics. They are sitting in a fork that feels load-bearing for their whole life. The clock is part of the texture: usually the question has been live for years, sometimes for a couple of years, sometimes for a decade. The biological window is part of the felt pressure even when the person does not name it.

- "What if we have kids? What if we don't?"
- "I didn't ever consider that I might want kids until quite late in life."
- "I wasn't expecting to want that until I imagined it with you."
- "We've been sitting in this question for a couple of years."
- "It's super scary."
- "I didn't spend most of my life thinking that all the parenting things I was seeing applied to me in any way."
- "The clock has been running out ever since we even thought we might want to do it."
- "I'm afraid of not accomplishing the things I want to accomplish."
- "I'm afraid I won't have time for friends."
- "I'm afraid of completely changing and not being able to relate to childless people."
- "If we have kids we will be different people forever."
- "If we don't have kids we will miss something we can never get back."
- "I feel calls in both directions."

The concern usually arrives in a partnership. One or both partners are in it. The voice often switches between the two trajectories within the same sentence: I want this, I'm scared of this, also what about that. The oscillation itself is part of what the concern feels like.

## Detection Cues

- The person frames the question as two whole lives being compared, not as a single decision about a single act
- The word "trajectory" or its synonyms ("path," "direction," "completely different life") appears
- The clock or the biological window is mentioned, even in passing
- The person describes wanting kids only after meeting their partner -- the want did not pre-exist the relationship
- The person can name fears on both sides of the fork and cannot fully commit to either
- The person has been "sitting in the question" for an extended period (months or years) without resolution
- A felt sense of paralysis or oscillation -- they keep returning to the question without moving toward an answer
- The person frames it as a decision rather than as something that might arrive as a call
- Witnessing other people's parenting (good or hard) shifts the person's leaning without giving them clarity
- The person describes wanting both the love and the freedom and not being able to imagine having both

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the question is usually [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear|fear, not decision-making]]. A real choice -- one the body has settled into -- does not feel like a decision. It feels like the next obvious step. The fact that the person is "sitting in the question" for years is information: the decision frame is not the right frame. The person is trying to deliberate their way into clarity about something that does not respond to deliberation.

Joe's own experience with the call to have kids is the counter-example he offers. He had been a clear no for years. Then it shifted. Not through reasoning. Through a felt sense: "Knock, knock, knock, it's time, let me in." See [[joes-calling-to-have-kids]] for the story. The shift was not an intellectual conclusion. It was a recognition.

The teaching underneath: most major life-direction questions are not deliberated into; they are listened for. The person who is trying to decide is using the wrong instrument. The instrument is felt sense -- the body's response to imagining each path, the call (or absence of call) that arises when the question is held without an agenda.

This connects to [[enjoyment-as-surrender]]: the call is the gravity of the deeper system moving toward something. The mind's job is not to choose; the mind's job is to notice when the call has arrived and to act on it. The person stuck in the question often has not yet had the call, or has had it and not registered it because they were busy deliberating.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same presenting concern goes in different directions based on what Joe notices.

- If the person is treating this as a pros-and-cons intellectual exercise: Joe moves toward [[feel-over-figure]] and shows that the felt body knows things the mind does not. The deliberation is not the path. The body's response to each trajectory is the data.
- If the person is in a partnership where one partner is clear yes and the other is clear no: Joe does not push for resolution. He stays with the truth of the asymmetry. There is no formula. Joe describes his own experience: with his first two children, both he and Tara felt a clear call. With the third, Tara felt clear yes and Joe felt clear no. The asymmetry was real. Different decisions follow from different configurations.
- If the person presents fear of losing themselves: Joe goes to [[parenting-as-deep-tissue-massage]]. Yes, you will lose parts of who you are. That is the practice. The fear is accurate. The framing of it as loss is incomplete.
- If the person presents fear of not being financially ready: Joe offers the "babies come with loaves of bread under their arms" frame and his own experience of going from major debt to out-of-debt during Tara's pregnancy. The financial story is rarely the actual block.
- If the person has been deliberating for years: Joe checks whether the deliberation itself is the avoidance. The question becomes: what would you have to feel to be done deliberating? Often grief on one side or the other.
- If the person is witnessing other people's grief or hardship around children and finds themselves wanting kids more, not less: this is data. Joe takes it seriously. The body is responding to something the mind did not expect.

## Coaching Path

The path is not to help the person decide. The path is to help them recognize the difference between deliberation and listening.

1. Take the fear of both paths seriously. Both fears are accurate. The person who wants kids and is scared of losing themselves is correctly seeing that they will lose parts of themselves (see [[parenting-as-deep-tissue-massage]]). The person who doesn't want kids and is scared of missing something is correctly seeing that they will miss something. The accuracy of both fears is not the question. The question is what is actually being called.

2. Disrupt the decision frame. Introduce the possibility that this might arrive as a call rather than as a decision. Joe's experience of "knock, knock, knock, it's time" is offered not as a prescription but as a description of one way it can come. See [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]] and [[joes-calling-to-have-kids]].

3. Address the financial story if it is the framing they are leading with. The financial story is almost always a cover for a deeper fear. Name "babies come with loaves of bread under their arms" not as a guarantee but as a structural observation: people figure out how to take care of kids who arrive. The financial fear is rarely the actual block.

4. Honor the asymmetry if there is one in the partnership. Do not push for resolution. If one partner is yes and the other is no, that is real. The path forward might be more time, might be a different decision than each partner would make alone, might be eventually splitting. Joe is direct that he was no for a long time and Tara was yes for a long time before the call arrived for both of them.

5. Do not promise that the call will come. Some couples do not get the call and end up not having kids. Some couples have one partner who gets the call and one who doesn't. The work is not to manufacture a call; it is to be available to whatever actually arises.

6. If the person is leaning toward kids, name what they are signing up for. The deep tissue massage of identity. The unlimited heartbreak. The unrecognizability to their pre-kid self. Joe does this not to scare them off but to make sure the choice is being made with eyes open. The same teaching applies to the other path: if they are leaning toward no kids, name what they are signing up for -- the particular kind of aliveness that some people only access through raising children, the heartbreaks they will not have, the development they will not be put through.

7. Drop the assumption that this is a clean decision either way. The realities on both sides are mixed. Joe says directly that there are no regrets about having had kids. He also describes feeling like a part of him is going to be ripped away when his oldest leaves for college. Both are true at once.
