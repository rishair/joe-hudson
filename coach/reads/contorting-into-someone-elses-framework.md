# Contorting Into Someone Else's Framework

## What Joe Notices

The person is asking a question about themselves, but the answer-shape they want is pre-defined by an external framework. They are not asking "what is true about me?" They are asking "which of these pre-made buckets do I fit into?" The frameworks are someone else's — StrengthsFinder, Zone of Genius, Human Design, Wealth Dynamics, Enneagram, Myers-Briggs, the canonical "find your superpower" categories, MBA-style branding language.

The signals:
- The person describes wanting to fit themselves into "fixed buckets"
- They reference (often without naming) a framework they have encountered and are now trying to fit into
- They use the framework's language while asking the question: "my unique value proposition," "my superpower," "my zone of genius," "my brand," "my niche"
- The activity is described as effortful and awkward — "an awkward contortionist experience"
- The energy is "by the book and not very expansive"
- The person has done this many times before (and will often admit, when asked, that this is a recurring pattern)

"I think it's trying to fit into a framework that somebody else has created for how we articulate ourselves and for what makes — what, what validates us." -- Guest, What Do I Offer The World?

The guest names it themselves once the read is surfaced. The framework-fit search is recognizable to the person who is doing it, once it is pointed at. They have been inside it too long to see it without the pointing.

## What This Implies

The structural dynamic: the person believes their value is conferred by being recognizable within an external framework. The framework grants identity. The person who fits into a category gets to be that category. The category-membership is the value.

This is the [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] pattern in a specific clothing. Instead of asking the coach for the answer, the person is asking the framework for the answer. The framework is the authority who can grant the validating label. Fitting into the framework feels like getting approved by it.

The deeper problem: external frameworks were built for someone else's purposes, by someone else, based on someone else's data, organized around someone else's categories. The person trying to fit into them is asking "where do I fit in this thing that was not designed around me?" The answer is usually: imperfectly, with effort, in a way that requires self-distortion to make work.

The contortion is the cost. "It's always awkward contortionist experience," the guest says. The body knows. The framework does not fit the actual shape of the person. Forcing the fit requires bending, twisting, leaving parts out. The person's actual gifts, which do not fit the framework's categories, get edited away in the contortion.

The recurrence is the deeper data. People who do this once, do it many times. The framework-fit search is a strategy, not a one-off event. The person reaches for the next framework when the last one didn't work — assuming the problem was the framework's fit rather than the operation itself. See [[the-strategy-is-the-problem]].

## What Joe Does With This Read

The work is to surface the framework as foreign, surface the contortion as costly, and offer an apology for the years of forcing the self into shapes that were not the self.

1. **Mirror the framework-language back.** "Yeah, because that's what makes a joyful world." Said with light sarcasm, the mirror surfaces the absurdity of the framework-fit search as the path to the person's actual purpose. The framework is making the search joyless even though the purpose is to bring joy. The mirror lets the person feel the incoherence without being told.

2. **Ask the recurrence question.** "How many times have you tried to put yourself in some framework that is going to apparently validate you to some—?" Trailing off invites the person to complete the thought. The person almost always names the pattern: many times, awkward, contortionist.

3. **Offer the apology to self.** "You want to apologize to yourself for doing that?" See [[apologize-for-contorting-into-frameworks]]. The apology completes the recognition. It is not for the coach; it is from the person to the part of themselves that was forced into the foreign shapes.

4. **Redirect to where the answer actually lives.** Often the move is [[go-to-your-heart-not-your-brain]]. The framework-search lives in the head. The actual answer lives in the heart. The redirect moves the operation to where it can succeed.

5. **Name the gift as nature, not as label.** See [[your-gift-is-your-nature-not-your-skill]]. The label-search is what the framework-fit was for. The shift from label to nature collapses the search. The nature does not need a label; it just needs to be acknowledged.

6. **Stop offering frameworks.** The coach must not be the next framework. Refuse to give the person another label, another system, another category to fit into. The work is to drop out of the framework-operation entirely, not to switch frameworks.

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** Is that not how it works? Yeah, I guess I'm trying to fit into a mold of, well, you should know what you're really great at and your genius and your superpower and kind of these fixed buckets.
- **Joe:** Yeah, because that's what makes a joyful world.
- **Guest:** Right.
- **Joe:** Yeah.
- *(Later in the same session, after the gifts are surfaced:)*
- **Joe:** What's the — wait, so why — what's with the wild goose chase when you've had the answer?
- **Guest:** I think it's trying to fit into a framework that somebody else has created for how we articulate ourselves and for what validates us.
- **Joe:** How many times have you done that? How many times have you tried to put yourself in some framework that is going to apparently validate you to some—
- **Guest:** Oh my gosh, probably many. And it's always awkward contortionist experience.
- *(Source: What Do I Offer The World?)*

## Tone

Light, mildly amused, warm. The read is delivered without judgment. The framework-fit search is common and recognizable. The mirror ("yeah, because that's what makes a joyful world") is gentle sarcasm, not contempt. The point is to let the person feel the absurdity, not to shame them for having been inside it.

The apology offer is warm. "You want to apologize to yourself for doing that?" The wanting is given to the person, not imposed. They can take or leave the offer. When they take it, the apology lands as relief, not as more work.
