# Looking for What's Wrong

## What Joe Notices

The person opens the session by reporting that they have been searching for what is wrong with them. The framing is not "I have this problem" but "I have been looking for a problem." The act of looking is presented as the work.

Observable signals:

- The opening line is "I've been doing my best to find out where I'm stuck."
- The person reports they were feeling fine, but felt they needed to manufacture a stuck place because they were working with a coach.
- The framing implies that the absence of problems is itself a problem.
- A subtle should-energy underneath: "I should be working on something."
- The person speaks of being "in a good place" and then immediately pivots to needing to find a stuck place.

## What This Implies

The person has internalized a model in which growth requires problems. If there is no problem, there is no growth. The mind then produces a problem to work on. The manufactured problem may be real (everyone has plenty of patterns to work with) but the framing is doing something the person has not noticed.

The framing itself is the operative pattern. The person is showing Joe, in the first minute, the exact shape of the issue: I cannot just be okay. I have to find something to fix. The reflex to find what is wrong is the same reflex that drives the rest of their life.

This is closely related to [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] and to the principle that [[nature-improves-itself]]. The person operating under "I have to find what is wrong" is operating under a should about self-improvement.

The opening also often hides a deeper avoidance: by spending the session looking for what is wrong, the person can spend the session in their head instead of in their body. The looking is itself a form of [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].

## What Joe Does

Joe stops and asks about the framing itself. He does not accept the manufactured problem at face value. He treats the act of looking for what is wrong as the actual material.

- "What makes it not obvious? What makes you need to figure that out?"
- "What makes you not be able to say that?"
- "It's an interesting thing. Like in a weird way, what you're doing right now is assuming that there's nothing wrong, what's happening in your system. For you to get to the place that you're getting to right now."

The intervention does two things. First, it makes the framing visible so the person can see what they are doing. Second, it surfaces the question whether anything actually needs fixing, which loosens the should-energy that has been organizing the session.

Joe often returns to this read later in the session. After the substantive work has happened, he points back: "It seems to bring us back to that question, which is like, well, what made you have to think that there was something wrong?"

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** "I've been doing my best to find out where I'm stuck."
- **Joe:** "There's actually something that you said before, which is really interesting to me. You said, I'm trying to figure out how I'm stuck. What makes it not obvious? What makes you need to figure that out?"
- **Guest:** "Because I was feeling like I was doing great. Like, oh, I'm like in a good place now, a good process. Things are moving. And like, oh, I'm working with Joe. Okay, let's find a stuck place. And like, I can't just say everything is going well."
- **Joe:** "So then I'm like, hey, what makes you not be able to say that?"
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves. The opening framing becomes the entry point to the work.)*

## Tone

Curious, light, almost amused. Joe sounds like he is pointing at something interesting rather than diagnosing a defect. The curiosity is contagious; the person usually becomes curious about their own framing rather than defensive.

## What This Often Reveals

- The person believes their okayness requires justification.
- The person has equated self-work with finding problems.
- There is a should about being in process at all times.
- The actual issue is more available than the manufactured one.
