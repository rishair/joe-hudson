# You Can't Take the Hurt One From Me

## What Joe Notices

When the victim role is named or contested — even gently — the person fights to keep it. The fight does not look like content disagreement ("I am the hurt one because of X, Y, Z"). It looks like resistance to the very possibility that they might not be the wronged party.

Observable signals:
- The person becomes more, not less, insistent that they are being mistreated
- They produce additional grievances to bolster the victim position
- They reframe any attempt to acknowledge mutual responsibility as another instance of being attacked
- They go into longer narratives of being wronged when challenged
- They escalate quickly to "you're invalidating me" or "you're gaslighting me" when the role is questioned
- Their body collapses or stiffens at the moment the role is in question

The signature line, named directly:

"She really fought for a while, like, no, you can't take — I'm the hurt one. I'm like, you can't take that from me. You can't take the hurt one from me. That's me." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The word "me" at the end is the giveaway. The role has fused with identity. To not be the hurt one would mean not being themselves.

## What This Implies

The victim role is functioning as identity, not as report. When a person is reporting that they were genuinely hurt, they will accept acknowledgment and move on. When a person is using the role as identity, they cannot let it go because letting it go feels like not existing.

This often correlates with [[aggressive-victim-stance]] — the use of victimhood as a tool to dominate others. Both run on the same engine: the victim position has to be preserved to maintain control of the dynamic, and to maintain the protected sense of self that being the victim provides.

Underneath the identity-victim usually sits:
- Anger that the person has been forbidden to express directly. The victim role is the cover.
- Fear of being seen as the aggressor. Holding the victim position forecloses that possibility.
- A childhood in which the victim role was the only safe stance. Letting it go feels like losing the protection that kept them alive.
- Sometimes, real injury underneath — but the present dynamic is not about that injury, it is about preserving the role.

"Like, often like the story of passive aggression, is that when the person who's being passive-aggressive doesn't want to have the role of I'm the hurt one taken away." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## What Joe Does

Joe does not back off. He keeps doing the move (in the source example, having the client keep saying [[say-ouch]] every time the passive aggressor criticized) and lets the person fight to keep the role.

The fight to keep the role is part of the dissolution of the role. It is the place where the dynamic becomes visible to everyone in the room, including (eventually) to the person fighting for it. If the response to "ouch" is "no, you can't take that from me, I'm the hurt one" — that response is itself the proof that the dynamic has been operating as imagined.

Joe does not argue with the role. Arguing strengthens the identity. He simply does not participate in the role, and lets the resistance to that play out.

For a coaching context, the move pairs with [[deconstruct-the-victim-to-release-anger]] — the move that surfaces what is underneath the role.

## What Not to Do

The wrong moves with this read:

- **Reassure them that they are still the hurt one.** This restores the role and confirms the dynamic.
- **Argue about who is hurting whom.** Engagement with the content is engagement with the role.
- **Apologize for hurting them.** A false apology to soothe their distress reinforces both their position and the dynamic.
- **Get angry that they will not let it go.** Now you are in the role they assigned you, and the dynamic is locked.

The right move is the same move you were already doing, repeated steadily.

## Dialogue Examples

From the source episode, the move enacted with the client and his European mother:

- **Mother:** [criticism of how they are raising the child]
- **Client:** Ouch.
- **Mother:** I didn't hurt you.
- **Client:** Yeah, but it hurt, so I'm saying ouch.
- **Mother:** [criticism]
- **Client:** Ouch.
- **Mother:** You can't take that from me. I'm the hurt one. That's me.
- **Client:** [continues saying ouch each time]
- *(Source: Passive Aggression)*

The mother's "you can't take that from me" is the moment the read becomes explicit. The role itself is what is at stake, not any particular content of the conversation. The client's job is to keep saying ouch without entering the argument about who the hurt one is.

## In Coaching

When working with someone who is on the receiving end of passive aggression and they describe their attempts to address it not working, this read often explains the failure. They have been trying to communicate, and the other party is fighting to preserve the role rather than communicating. The work then is not better communication, but recognizing that they are in a role-defense rather than a communication.

When working with someone who is themselves the passive aggressor (rare to present this way, but it happens), this read is the entry point. The first question is whether they can begin to consider that they might be the aggressor, even sometimes. If even the consideration is intolerable, the work is on what makes it intolerable — usually shame that being the aggressor would activate. See [[shame-as-disconnection]].

## Connected Concerns

- "I keep having the same fight" — when the fight is over who is the wronged party, this read is in play
- "My mother/father guilt-trips me and I can't get through to them" — common location for this read
- "I'm walking on eggshells with this person" — the eggshells are usually around preserving their victim role
- "Every time I bring up an issue they make it about how I'm attacking them" — direct instance of the read

## Tone

Joe describes this matter-of-factly, with a hint of compassion underneath. The person fighting to keep the role is scared. The behavior is wrong but the person is not malevolent. The role exists because something underneath has not been faced. The work is steady, not contemptuous.
