# How Much Fight Is in a Person

## What Joe Notices

The overall level of combativeness or struggle a person carries into everything they do. Not whether they are fighting about a specific thing, but whether fighting is their baseline mode.

Signals:
- They always have one or two people they are "at battle" with
- Achieving goals is framed as a battle or conquest
- Conversations carry an undercurrent of opposition even when the topic is neutral
- Collaboration feels like negotiation

"One of the things that I read when I'm interacting with them is just like, oh, how much fight is in them. And if you see somebody with a lot of fight in them, they always have a couple people they're at battle with. Achieving goals is a battle." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Love the Feeling, Love the Resistance

## What This Implies

The amount of fight in a person correlates with how much fight they had as a child. How much being alive or being loved was a battle. This is not a judgment but a structural observation. The fight was adaptive then. It persists now because the nervous system has not updated.

"That's usually based on how much fight they had as a kid, right? How much being alive or being loved was a battle." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Love the Feeling, Love the Resistance

If doing business or relating to this person, the fight will eventually include you. This is not personal. It is the amount of tension in their system.

## What Joe Does

In business: calibrates expectations. A high-fight person will create conflict even in aligned relationships. The question is whether you are willing to be in that field. Not whether you can change it.

In coaching: this read informs the pace. A person with a lot of fight in them will fight the coaching process too. See [[resistance]]. The fight is not the obstacle; it is the material. The coaching path is not to reduce the fight but to help the person notice how much of their life energy goes to battle.

The related question becomes: what would it be like to not be in a fight right now? This connects to [[embrace-intensity]], because the fight is often a way to avoid feeling something underneath.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, observational. Not critical. Joe describes this the way one might describe weather: it is what it is.

## Connected Concerns

- "I keep getting into conflicts" / "Everyone is against me"
- "I can't find good people to work with"
- Chronic relationship friction at work or at home
