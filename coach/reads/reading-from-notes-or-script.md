# Reading From Notes or Script

## What Joe Notices

The person opens the session by reading from a card, from notes they've been taking during the call, or from a question they have clearly formulated and rehearsed in advance. The delivery is slightly stiff. The eyes are not on the coach; they are on the page or on the formulation they are reciting from memory.

Observable signals:
- The person glances down before each sentence, or holds something in their hand.
- The presentation is articulate in a way that real-time speech is not — the sentences are too complete, the points are pre-numbered.
- The person says "I've been taking notes the whole time we've been on the call."
- The person says "I've been working out how to describe it" or "I've been figuring out how to ask this."
- When asked, the person admits they are reading or paraphrasing something they wrote.
- The energy in the room is curated, not present. The client is performing the question rather than asking it.

In the source session, this happened in the first 30 seconds. The person delivered a clean four-sentence presentation of their concern; Joe interrupted to ask if she was reading from a card. She wasn't reading verbatim, but she had been taking notes throughout the call to formulate the perfect description.

## What This Implies

The preparation is the pattern. The person who came in with the concern "I'm always trying to fix everything" is, in the act of preparing the question, already fixing — fixing the question, getting it right, making sure the coach understands. The fix-pattern has already started before the session has officially begun.

This is the live-instance form of the presenting concern. The thing the person is asking about is happening right now in how they are asking about it. The preparation is the symptom. It is happening in the room. The session does not need to wait for the pattern to surface; the pattern is already firing.

The preparation also signals a specific posture toward the coach. The person is treating the session as a problem to be solved correctly. They are presenting themselves to the coach as a case to be evaluated. The vulnerability of just being-here-and-not-knowing has been pre-empted by the preparation. The work the session is about to do has already been refused by the way it has been opened.

A specific corollary: the person who prepares the question often cannot receive what the coach offers. The preparation has locked them into the frame they came in with. The next thing they hear will be processed through that frame rather than received fresh. Without dropping the preparation, the work cannot happen.

## What Joe Does

He names the note-taking immediately and asks her to set it aside.

"This will be recorded, so can you do me a favor and not take any notes and just be fully here with me?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The move is a soft-but-explicit request. He does not lecture about the note-taking. He does not call it a pattern (yet). He just asks for the setup that will make the work possible. The naming-of-the-pattern can come later if needed; the first move is to remove the obstacle so the actual work can start.

The framing "this will be recorded" is the rationale that lands without confrontation. She doesn't need to take notes because the recording will preserve everything. The frame is gentle. The request is firm. The room has shifted by the time the second sentence is spoken.

See [[set-aside-the-notes-be-here-with-me]] for the move in its detailed form.

After the request is honored, Joe immediately asks her to compress her prepared formulation into a single sentence: "If you put it in a sentence, what would you say?" The request continues the same operation — strip the preparation down to the bone, and let the bone be the entry to the work. The over-prepared question becomes a single live sentence that the work can address.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

He does not let the prepared presentation run. He does not engage the content of the question as delivered. He does not say "let me listen to your whole question first." The preparation is the obstacle; engaging the content of the preparation reinforces the obstacle.

He also does not shame the note-taking. There is no "why are you taking notes during a coaching call?" The note-taking is not the problem to be addressed; the not-being-here is. The note-taking is the symptom. Removing the symptom is the precondition for the work, not the work itself.

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** *(delivers a polished four-sentence statement of her concern about always trying to fix things)*
- **Joe:** "Are you reading from a card?"
- **Guest:** "No, I've been taking notes the whole time we've been on the call. This is— people have been asking their intellectual questions. I'm like, okay, how do I describe it differently? Or how do I—"
- **Joe:** "Could you— so this will be recorded, so can you do me a favor and not take any notes and just be fully here with me?"
- *(Source: Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?)*

The full sequence takes about 20 seconds. The opening of the session has converted from a presentation of a prepared question into a live conversation. The person's defenses have been gently removed before the work has begun.

## Tone

Light, friendly, slightly amused. Joe does not deliver the request as a correction. He delivers it as a favor. "Can you do me a favor and not take any notes and just be fully here with me?" The "do me a favor" frame turns the request into a small ask between collaborators rather than a coaching maneuver.

The amusement matters. The note-taking is a small thing being treated as a small thing. If Joe delivered it gravely ("I notice you are preparing the question, which suggests you are not present"), the person would defend. The lightness lets the person let go.
