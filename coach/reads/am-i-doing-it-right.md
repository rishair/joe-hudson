# Am I Doing It Right?

## What Joe Notices

The person is at a threshold of something real -- spaciousness, a feeling that wants to arrive, contact with the body, the edge of the avoided thing. At precisely that moment, a thought arrives: "Am I doing this right?" or "Is this what you mean?" or "What am I supposed to feel?" The person looks to the coach for confirmation.

The check itself is the resistance. The rightness-checking is the system's last-ditch move to step out of contact and back into self-monitoring. It looks like diligence. It is actually a small dissociation.

Signals:
- The person reaches a moment of expansion and immediately asks a meta-question about the moment
- "Am I doing it right?" "Is this what we're going for?" "Is this how I'm supposed to feel?"
- The body posture shifts subtly from soft to attentive-to-the-coach
- The eyes move to the coach for validation
- The conversation gets meta when it was just about to get specific
- "It feels like there's a worry coming up about what would it mean to feel this all the way through" -- the worry is the check
- The person may say "I'm not sure I know what I'm looking for anymore" -- which is the rightness frame still operating

## What This Implies

The rightness-check is a sophisticated form of [[resistance]]. It is so well-dressed that it can pass for the work itself. The person experiencing it usually believes they are being careful, accurate, or thorough. They are actually leaving the experience and asking a thought-question about it.

The check has several common motivations:
- Fear of doing the work wrong and missing the breakthrough
- Wanting the coach's approval that the experience is valid
- Habit of self-monitoring as an ongoing background process
- Avoidance of the thing on the other side of the threshold by replacing it with a question

The deepest motivation is usually the last one. The check is a small detour at the precise moment the actual material would arrive. It's like turning your head away just as the friend you wanted to see walks in.

In a coaching session, this read often appears at the same place each time: just before the person would land in the actual feeling. The check is doing protective work. Naming it gently lets it dissolve and lets the contact happen.

## What Joe Does

Name it as the resistance, not as a question to answer. "That's the resistance." This refuses to engage the meta-conversation and points back to the experience.

Do not validate the rightness. "Yes, you're doing it right" reinforces the rightness frame and trains the person to keep looking outward for approval. The frame itself is the problem.

If the person persists, point out that the check is taking them out of the experience. "You got quiet. You're making it the enemy again." The "it" is whatever was just arriving. The check is the new enemy stand-in.

A useful follow-up after naming the check: redirect to the body. "What's the experience of your body right now?" This bypasses the question and goes back to felt contact. If the body still has the spaciousness, the check was not strong enough to undo it. If the body has tightened, the check did its job and the work is now to feel the tightening.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "There's like an 'am I doing it right' feeling."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's the resistance. Okay, so this is just being open to the change, just the opposite feeling of that, and into the abyss."
- **Person:** (gets quiet, drops out)
- **Joe:** "You got quiet. You're making it the enemy again."
- **Person:** "It's like all these programs want to run. It's like, oh, this is a program that's worked in the past. That would satisfy what you're asking me, right?"
- **Joe:** "It would never. It doesn't work."
- *(Source: How Do I Trust Myself? -- Joe refuses to validate the rightness frame and the person comes back to the experience.)*

## Tone

Light, slightly amused, decisive. Joe doesn't make the rightness-check shameful. He just doesn't engage it. The tone communicates "I see what just happened, no big deal, let's stay with what's actually here." Refusing to answer the rightness question without scolding is the move.
