# Pride in the Elaborate Cage

## What Joe Notices

The client recognizes the structure of their own stuckness — how the patterns interlock, how the tools they have been using are part of the cage, how cleverly the system has been arranged to keep them in place. And rather than collapsing into shame about it, the client shows pride. Appreciation. A kind of awe at the wileyness of the construction.

"Look at how elaborate this cage." -- Brett Kistler, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The signature is the absence of shame paired with the presence of recognition. The client is seeing the pattern fully, holding nothing back about how it operates, and the response is "wow" rather than "fuck, I'm broken." There is even something that sounds like ownership: "yeah, I did that, look at this thing I built."

"It is very sweet. There isn't a shame around it. There is a, there's like somewhat of a pride in it, which is really quite lovely." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

## What This Implies

The client has the capacity for the work. Shame about the pattern would mean the client is still using the pattern's content as evidence against themselves. Pride about the pattern means the client has moved to a different relationship — they can see the strategy as a strategy, appreciate its function, honor what it did, and consider releasing it without making the release into a judgment about who they have been.

This is one of the strongest in-session signals that real change is available. The capacity to see the pattern as a creation rather than as a verdict is what makes the pattern releasable. As long as the pattern is a verdict ("this is who I am, this is what's wrong with me"), it has to be defended against. Once the pattern is a creation ("look at this thing I built to survive that"), it can be set down because the survival no longer requires it.

The pride also signals that the relational ground in the session is solid. The client trusts the coach enough to celebrate something that, in a different context, might be presented as evidence of brokenness. The celebration requires safety; the safety is what allows the recognition to be held without shame.

## Why Pride and Not Shame

The shame response treats the survival strategy as a personal defect. "I'm so stuck. There's something wrong with me. I should have figured this out by now." The shame frame collapses the pattern and the self into one. To release the pattern, the self has to be repaired, which is impossible because the self was never broken — only the pattern was operating.

The pride response treats the survival strategy as a thing the self produced. "Look at this thing I built. It was very effective at keeping me safe. It is also keeping me stuck." The pride frame separates the pattern from the self. The pattern can be released without the self having to be repaired. The self was the one who built the pattern; the self can also be the one who lets it go.

This is structurally the same shift as [[the-pattern-is-not-personal]] — the pattern as installed-by-circumstance rather than as defining-of-self — but with an additional element: ownership. The pride-in-the-cage version includes "I made this, and I'm impressed with what I made." The pattern-is-not-personal version focuses on the installation; the pride version focuses on the artistry of the response.

Both are valid. Both can co-occur. The pride version is the warmer of the two, and it is often what arrives later in a session when the work has freed enough space for the client to feel affection for the version of themselves that built the cage.

## The Sweetness

Joe specifically names the pride as "sweet." The sweetness is not condescension. It is recognition that the self-who-built-the-cage was being protective. The cage looks elaborate from the outside. From the inside, every piece of the cage was load-bearing in its own moment — each piece was solving for a real survival need. The client built what they needed to build. The fact that the cage is now keeping them stuck does not diminish that it was, at the time of construction, an act of self-preservation.

The sweetness of the pride is also that it includes affection for the version of self who built the cage. "Five-year-old me figured out how to be the best so that I would be safe; that was actually really smart of her." The affection extends backward to the parts of self that made the survival possible. The release of the pattern can then happen without abandoning those parts of self — they get to be honored for what they did, even as the strategy they installed is no longer needed.

## What Joe Does

**Confirm the pride out loud.** When the client shows pride in the cage, naming it can strengthen the recognition: "yeah, it's quite sweet, there's a pride in it." The naming gives the client permission to keep holding the pride without judging themselves for the holding.

**Use the pride as confirmation of capacity.** The presence of pride rather than shame tells the coach the client can do the work. Subsequent moves can be more direct, more challenging, more in service of the change because the client has demonstrated they are not going to collapse into shame.

**Let the pride and the work coexist.** The pride is not the end of the work; it is a sign the work can continue at a deeper level. The coach can move toward the next layer (the suppressed emotion, the underlying need, the take-home practice) with the pride present as the ground.

**Watch for the pride to shift into the next emotion.** Pride is often a midpoint. The next emotion after pride is often grief — for the years the cage was needed, for the energy spent maintaining it, for what was foregone while it was in operation. Holding space for the shift is part of the work.

## The Mistake the Read Helps the Coach Avoid

Without this read, a coach might interpret the client's apparent delight at their own stuckness as resistance or as a deflection. The thought: "she's making light of this, she's not taking it seriously, she's avoiding the work." The interpretation is wrong. The pride is not avoidance; it is the body's accurate response to recognizing something it can now release.

The read tells the coach to honor the pride rather than to confront it. Confronting the pride ("this is no laughing matter, you've been stuck for years") would push the client back into shame, which would re-load the pattern with the very weight that pride had just removed. The work would stall at exactly the moment it was opening up.

## The Mirror Read: Joy at the Worst Fear

The pride read often co-occurs with [[joy-relief-when-told-the-worst-fear]]. The two readings have related but distinct mechanisms:

**Joy-relief at the worst fear:** the body releases the load of pretending. The recognition that the feared thing is true ends the pretense's job.

**Pride in the elaborate cage:** the self recognizes its own creative achievement in building the cage. The appreciation includes the cage and the maker of the cage as worthy of regard.

Both are signs of capacity. Both signal that the work has reached the level where the client can witness their pattern without collapsing into shame about it. When both are present, the coaching can move with significant freedom because the client is no longer fragile around the material.

## What This Read Is Not

It is not denial or making light of the situation. Denial would skip over the recognition. The pride includes full recognition; it just refuses the shame.

It is not narcissistic self-celebration. The pride is about the cleverness of a survival strategy that worked, not about the client being inherently superior. The pride includes the awareness that the strategy is now in the way and needs to be released.

It is not the same as the [[absence-of-resistance-to-the-suck]] read. That read is about acceptance without bracing. This read is about active appreciation of the construction. Both involve absence of suffering, but the affective content is different — acceptance vs. honoring.

It is not the same as the [[performing-growth]] anti-read where the client says the right things without actually feeling them. The pride here is felt — visible in the body, sustained, accompanied by softening rather than performance.

## Dialogue Example

The source session contains a clear instance:

- **Joe (in original session):** "So we're going to go back to, you're not going to be the best. Period."
- **Client:** "Yeah. I'm not the best." (with sadness arriving)
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So you have two survival techniques that are working here."
- **Client:** "Yes. And I'm just fucking tired of that. I am frustrated."
- **Joe:** "So what I'm seeing now is the sadness actually comes out when you're fucking angry. So you avoid anger with sadness and you avoid sadness with somatic presencing. What the fuck?"
- **Client:** "I really worked this." (laughing, with appreciation)
- *(Source: Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence)*

The "I really worked this" is the pride. The client is recognizing the elaboration of her own strategy — multiple defenses stacked, each catching what the other lets through. The response is appreciation, not shame.

Brett names what he sees in the breakdown:

"It seems like there's a part of her that feels kind of proud for the wileyness and the intelligence and the way that she's stayed stuck. Like there's almost a way that her system is kind of honoring, like, hey, well, you know what? Maybe I've been stuck. Maybe I've felt stuck, but like, look at how elaborate this cage." -- Brett Kistler, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

Joe confirms: "Yeah, exactly. There is that. It is very sweet."

## Tone

Affectionate and slightly amused. The pride is genuinely lovely to witness, and the coach's response matches the lightness. There is no need to be solemn about the moment; the client's body is doing the right thing. The coach's tone affirms that the pride is welcome.
