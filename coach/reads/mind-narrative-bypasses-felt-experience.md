# Mind Narrative Bypasses Felt Experience

## What Joe Notices

The person is feeling something — visibly, observably, in their body. They are shaking. They have tears coming. They are clearly in contact with sensation. And in the same breath they are telling the coach they cannot feel, they do not know how to feel, they are trying to feel and it is not working. The claim that they cannot feel is operating *while the feeling is happening*.

The mechanism is one of speed. The mind is moving so fast it arrives at the narrative ("I can't feel," "I don't know how to do this," "emotions are bad") before the felt-data has had time to register in awareness. The narrative becomes the report on the experience even though the experience is, second-by-second, contradicting the narrative.

The person is not lying. They genuinely believe they cannot feel. The belief is being produced by the speed of the narrative, which is intercepting the felt-data before the felt-data can be consciously held. The mind is a faster channel than the body, and when the mind is operating at full velocity, it can completely override the body's report by reporting first.

Signals:

- The person describes being shaken or tearful while saying they cannot access feeling
- The person reports doing mindfulness or feeling exercises and says they do not work, while their body is visibly responsive
- The person interrupts their own felt-experience with multiple thoughts in rapid succession, never landing in any of them
- The person finishes one sentence and is already in the next thought before the first sentence's content has had time to register
- Asked to feel something specific, the person produces a flurry of explanations about why they cannot, while the feeling is plainly happening
- The person uses the word "trying" repeatedly when describing emotional contact — "I'm trying to feel," "I'm trying to do everything you said," "I'm trying to listen to my body"
- The voice carries a quality of speed itself — slightly too quick, slightly too articulate, the cadence of someone trying to outrun something
- The person can describe the feeling intellectually (where it is located, what it is like) while denying that they can feel it

The decisive marker: the person produces felt-data and contradicts it within the same speech. Not a sequential contradiction (felt-data, then narrative override later) but a simultaneous one (felt-data and "I can't feel" in the same breath).

## What This Implies

The mind-arriving-faster pattern is doing protective work. The felt-data is real and the felt-data is overwhelming. The mind's job, in this configuration, is to keep the person in the report-zone (where the feeling is being described rather than inhabited) because the actual inhabiting would be too much. The narrative is a relief from the felt-experience. The person is using their own intellect as a buffer against their own body.

This is structurally the same mechanism Joe describes elsewhere as the head-as-relief-from-overwhelm, but operating at a much smaller timescale. The bypass is not happening over hours or days; it is happening in the seconds between the felt-data and the report on the felt-data. The mind is constructing the narrative continuously, frame by frame, faster than the experience itself.

The bypass is especially common in people running a [[self-reliance-emotional-signature|self-reliance configuration]] from early childhood. The original environment did not provide a holder for overwhelming feelings; the child's mind became the holder by becoming faster than the feelings. The mind became the way the child stayed functional. The configuration continued into adulthood. The person is now functioning beautifully and bypassing their own life moment-by-moment.

"What's happening is that his mind is coming in with narrative so quickly, so hard that it bypasses, like it tricks him into thinking that he's not feeling." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The trick framing matters. The person is not deliberately avoiding. The mind is *tricking* them. The trick is the speed itself. Slowing down is what dissolves the trick because the slowing allows the felt-data to register before the narrative arrives.

The implication for what is actually present: the feeling is happening. The capacity to feel is already there. The reports of "I can't feel" or "I don't know how to feel" are not accurate descriptions of the person's capacity; they are descriptions of the speed at which the narrative is overriding the felt-data. Working on "how to feel" is working on the wrong problem. The work is to slow down enough that what is already being felt can be noticed as such.

## What Joe Does

**Slow the person down deliberately.** This is the first move. The narrative speed is the engine of the bypass; reducing the speed is the only intervention that reaches the actual mechanism. Joe will literally tell the person to slow down, will pause them mid-sentence, will insert silence between turns, will instruct them not to narrate ("you don't have to narrate anything until I ask, because it'll slow it down a bit").

**Hold the felt-data they have produced and refuse the contradictory narrative.** When the person says "I can't feel" while shaking, Joe does not argue. He just holds the shaking as data and refuses the not-feeling claim by asking the next question from inside the felt-data. "You just told me you don't feel good — how do you know that?" The question forces the person to acknowledge the felt-data they have been overriding. The acknowledgment is the first crack in the bypass.

**Name the mechanism without making it the problem.** Joe will sometimes name what is happening directly so the person can see it: "your mind is coming in with narrative so quickly that it bypasses the feeling." The naming is not a correction. It is a description that gives the head a structural picture so the body can come back. See [[name-the-avoidance-cascade-from-essence-to-addiction]] for the broader family of this move. The naming gives the mind a job (understanding the bypass) so that the body can have the space to feel.

**Use the body location as the anchor.** Once the felt-data has been acknowledged, the next move is to land in a specific location in the body. "What's the part of your body that feels the worst right now?" The specific location is too concrete for the narrative to override quickly. The mind can construct a narrative about "feeling everything" but it has more trouble running away from "this specific spot." See [[love-the-worst-feeling-part-like-your-parents-should-have]].

**Refuse the how-question.** When the person asks "how do I do this?" — the how-question is the narrative reasserting itself. Joe declines to answer. "I'm not going to buy that you don't know how to do it. You did it." See [[how-question-as-defense-against-having]]. Engaging the how-question would restart the bypass.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

He does not engage with the narrative-content as if the narrative were accurate. The person's claim that they cannot feel is not treated as a true report on their capacity; it is treated as the bypass operating. Joe does not try to teach them how to feel, because teaching how to feel would reinforce the premise that they cannot feel, which is the bypass's premise.

He does not slow down by explaining slowness or by giving meditation instructions. He slows them down by instructing them not to narrate. The instruction is operationally simple and immediately effective. "Don't narrate anything until I ask."

He does not validate the not-feeling claim. The validation would reinforce the bypass. Instead, he holds the felt-data the person has produced (shaking, tears, sensations) as the data that overrides the not-feeling claim. The person is being treated as already feeling, because they are.

## Dialogue Example

The canonical demonstration from the source session — Joe's commentary on his own coaching of an 18-year-old who is shaking and reporting that he cannot feel:

- **Person (visibly shaking):** "Even I'm shaking right now. It feels so distressing and overwhelming. I've been using mindfulness, but I'm just so curious. I, I, emotions are just so bad."
- **Joe (slows him down by removing the Notion document he's reading from, asks him to throw it on the bed behind him)**
- **Person (laughs, becomes more present):** *(begins reporting felt-experience more directly)*
- **Joe's commentary later in the breakdown:** "He moves really quickly from one thing to another. That's usually a sign of intelligence and some ADHD potentially. But he's moving. He can't even particularly complete a thought before telling me another thought. And so this is something that's going to make this learning experience or this kind of learning more challenging for him. So the mindfulness is great that he's doing, but slowing him down so he can really feel the thing. So he's clearly feeling — even though he says he's having a hard time feeling, he's literally telling me while his body is shaking that his body is shaking. Somebody who can't feel wouldn't be able to do that. But what's happening is that his mind is coming in with narrative so quickly, so hard that it bypasses, like it tricks him into thinking that he's not feeling."
- *(Source: "This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown)*

The structural point: Joe is reading the speed itself as the diagnostic, and naming the speed as the obstacle to feeling rather than as a separate issue. The person's "I can't feel" report is being treated as the byproduct of the speed, not as accurate data about the person's capacity.

Later in the same session, when the person sits with a specific body location and is loving it, Joe explicitly instructs the person to slow down: "You don't have to narrate anything until I ask, because it'll slow it down a bit." The instruction is the operational form of working with this read.

## Tone

Patient, slightly slower than the person's pace. Joe's voice deliberately lands later in the conversation than the person expects. The slowness is itself an intervention. If Joe matched the person's speed, the bypass would continue uninterrupted. By being slower, Joe creates the gap in which the felt-data can register.

The commentary delivery is curious and warm, not pathologizing. The mind-speed is treated as a feature of intelligent people who have been alone too long with overwhelming material. The bypass is honored as the protective work it has been doing. The naming is structural ("his mind is coming in with narrative so quickly") rather than judgmental.
