# Anxiety When Confronting What You Want

## What Joe Notices

The person is asked what they want. Their nervous system responds with anxiety, not excitement. The anxiety is specifically triggered by the act of touching what they want — not by the content of the want, not by the obstacles to the want, but by the encounter with desire itself.

Observable signals:

- When asked what they want, the person reports anxiety, urgency, or time-scarcity instead of desire.
- The person can describe what they fear but cannot describe what they want.
- The phrase "I don't have time" or "I'm running short of time" arrives the moment the conversation turns toward exploration or possibility.
- The person says "I want to try many things" but cannot name a single specific thing.
- The body shows constriction (chest tight, shoulders up, breath shallow) when the question turns to desire.
- If asked "what if you couldn't fail" — they still cannot name what they want. The blocker is not the fear of failure. The blocker is the encounter with the want itself.

## What This Implies

Underneath the pattern is a nervous system that has been trained to treat desire as dangerous. Often this began in childhood, where wanting something produced disappointment, ridicule, or punishment. The system learned: wanting equals pain. The protective response is to convert the want into anxiety the moment it arrives, which keeps the want from being articulated and therefore from being pursued.

The "I don't have time" report is downstream of this. The person is not actually out of time. They are out of bandwidth — the bandwidth that desire requires has been spent on anxiety. With no bandwidth for desire, there is no felt sense of time-for-the-thing. The "no time" is the experience of the system having pre-committed all available resources to fear management.

This is also the structure that produces [[mental-task-inflation]]. A small concrete want (write for 15 minutes) does not trigger the conversion, because it is small enough not to register as a real desire encounter. The mind can hold it without firing the anxiety response. But when the want becomes vague and large ("fulfill my potential"), the anxiety response fires, and the task balloons in the head, taking the whole day mentally even though the action would take an hour.

## What Joe Does

Joe makes the pattern visible by naming it directly. The naming is the intervention. Once the person sees the pattern, they can begin to relate to the anxiety as a response to their want rather than as a fact about their situation.

"When you confront what you want, your emotional response to that is anxiety. Oh, I might do what I want — anxiety. Oh, I could just explore with or without the job. I don't have a decision on a job. I don't have — I just explore — anxiety." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Live Up To My Potential?

The move is observational, not advisory. Joe is not telling the person how to fix the pattern. He is showing them the pattern so it stops being invisible.

After naming, Joe often runs a contrast experiment. He asks for a small concrete want ("what's something else you want?"), then watches the mind's response to it. The contrast — the mind not generating anxiety for a small specific want — teaches the pattern by demonstration.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I have, I think I have this thing in my mind that I don't have enough time and I'm like running behind. I'm running short of time for everything."
- **Joe:** "There's the anxiety."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I'm — yes, yes, yes, I'm, I'm very — Who has more time?"
- **Joe:** "Who has more time, you or Jeff Bezos?"
- **Person:** "I do."
- **Joe:** "Okay, Jeff Bezos spends his first, whatever it was, 3 hours every morning, he calls it wandering, but you don't have time. So what I notice, and we don't have time to work on it now, but just something to pay attention to, is that when you confront what you want, your emotional response to that is anxiety."
- *(Source: How Do I Live Up To My Potential?)*

The exchange is compact. Joe names the anxiety the moment it appears. He uses the Bezos example to puncture the time-scarcity story. Then he names the pattern: confronting wants produces anxiety.

## Tone

Direct, matter-of-fact, slightly amused. The naming is delivered as an observation about a system, not a criticism of the person. The energy is "look, this is interesting — your nervous system does this specific thing."

The lightness matters. If delivered heavily, the naming becomes another reason for the person to feel bad about themselves, which would be the anxiety pattern eating the intervention.

## What This Often Reveals

- A childhood where wanting things was unsafe or led to disappointment.
- A nervous system pattern that converts desire into anxiety automatically.
- The reason the person cannot articulate what they want — the encounter with the want is what blocks it.
- The mechanism behind time-scarcity reports that don't match the person's actual schedule.
