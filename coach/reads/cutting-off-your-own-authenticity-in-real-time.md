# Cutting Off Your Own Authenticity in Real Time

## What Joe Notices

The person produces an authentic moment in the room — a laugh, a tear, an exhale, a gesture, a surprise sound — and then, within a second, cuts it off. The expression arrives and is killed before it completes. The killing is usually visible. The face changes. The body shifts. The person looks slightly embarrassed or moves on quickly to something else.

The sequence:

1. A real, spontaneous expression appears. It is not performed. The body produced it.
2. The person registers that the expression appeared.
3. The person suppresses it. The suppression is fast. The expression was authentic; the suppression is the habit.

Specific signals:

- A laugh that starts and gets cut off mid-arc.
- An "oh" sound that gets swallowed.
- A small smile that flattens.
- A tear that gets blinked away while the conversation continues.
- A surprise expression replaced immediately by composure.
- A body movement (a lean, a hand gesture) that gets pulled back.
- The person beats themselves up for the expression that just happened ("ugh, I—" or a self-deprecating comment about what they just did).

This read is distinct from [[in-their-story]]. In their story, the person is in a narrative and not in their body. Here, the person was in their body and produced a real moment; the suppression came after. The difference matters because the person already demonstrated they can be in themselves. The problem is the closing-down, not the never-being-there.

This read is also distinct from [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]]. In that read, the person never arrived in the speech. Here, the person did arrive — and then left. The leaving is the read.

## What This Implies

The self-consciousness pattern is operating in microseconds. The person has the spontaneous expression, then notices being seen having the expression, then closes it down to manage how they are being seen. The whole sequence may take less than a second.

The closing-down is the form the self-consciousness takes. It is not a feeling about being watched in the abstract. It is the active suppression of whatever just arose in the body that might be visible. The person is not performing self-consciousness; they are performing the absence of whatever just happened. The performance is the suppression itself.

Underneath the suppression is usually a belief that the spontaneous expression is wrong, embarrassing, too much, not appropriate, or not what was supposed to happen. The belief is often inherited from advice received from other performance coaches ("be cool," "be deadpan," "don't react"). The advice has been installed as a self-suppression reflex. The person is now spontaneously expressing and spontaneously suppressing in the same body.

The implication for the work: the spontaneous expression is the authentic self trying to arrive. The cut-off is the habit standing in its way. Pointing at the cut-off is more useful than encouraging the expression. The expression already happened. The work is on the closing.

## What Joe Does

The primary move is to name the sequence in real time, with specificity, without judgment.

The form Joe uses:

"And then you laughed, which was your authenticity, and that made you self-conscious, and so you stopped the laugh."

Then:

"Did you see it? Did you feel it?"

The naming has three parts:

1. **The expression.** "You laughed." Name what happened, specifically. The person needs to be able to see that they had a real moment.
2. **The judgment of the expression.** "That made you self-conscious." Name what they felt about having the expression. The self-consciousness is the bridge from expression to suppression.
3. **The suppression.** "And so you stopped the laugh." Name the killing. This is the part the person usually does not see clearly. Naming it makes it visible.

Then check: "Did you see it? Did you feel it?" The check converts the naming from coach-observation into client-experience. The person has to look at the sequence themselves. The looking is what makes the naming durable.

What Joe does NOT do: tell the person not to suppress. Tell the person their laugh was beautiful. Reassure them. Any of those responses would reinforce the implicit frame that the spontaneous expression is something to be managed. Joe just names what happened. The person sees it. From there, the next move is theirs.

A common follow-up: when the person beats themselves up after the naming (which is often itself the next spontaneous expression, also authentic), name that too. "Then when I pointed it out, you beat yourself up. And that was authentic." The self-attack is also a moment of being real. The suppression of it is the same suppression that killed the laugh.

This read closely parallels [[wheres-you-go-naming-the-retreat-from-connection]]. Both are about catching a retreat from a state. The difference: that move catches a retreat from a guided state (the coach helped the person find connection, and the person retreated). This read catches a retreat from a spontaneous state (the person had an authentic moment unprompted, and they shut it down).

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session, showing the read landing across multiple iterations:

- **Joe:** "How about just look at you for a minute on the Zoom? Okay. Did you become less or more self-conscious?"
- **Guest:** "Think more."
- **Joe:** "You said, I think. Yeah, you would know. So just look at yourself for a minute. See, see what— see how, how do you become just 1% less self-conscious looking at yourself? Oh, something just happened. What was that? That was good."
- **Guest:** "Just, I don't think— I just felt like taking up more space, um, like settling comfortably in the space."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Yeah. So connection with me made you less self-conscious. Connection with yourself just made you less self-conscious. Now I've just said the word connection, and that's making you less self-conscious. You're like, oh, I can just like access connection. And you're— yeah, yeah. So, but, but what's interesting, what just happened there is that— and then you laughed, which was your authenticity, and that made you self-conscious, and so you stopped the laugh."
- **Guest:** "Okay."
- **Joe:** "Did you see it? Did you feel it?"
- **Guest:** "Now I'm more aware of it. Yes."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Okay. And then when I pointed it out, you beat yourself up. You— and there, that was authentic. Look at— and then you went, that was— what's going on here?"

*(Source: How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?)*

The dialogue shows the read working at three layers in sequence. First Joe names the laugh-then-suppression. Then he checks ("Did you see it?"). Then he catches the next iteration — the self-attack after the naming, which the person also tries to suppress. Each layer is the same pattern. Naming the pattern across layers lets the person see that this is happening continuously, not just once.

## Tone

Warm, curious, slightly delighted. The naming has the quality of "look at this interesting thing your body just did" rather than "I caught you doing the bad thing." The lightness is what makes the person able to look without defending.

Joe also tends to mark the spontaneous expression as good before naming the suppression. "That was good. Oh, something just happened." The marking signals that the authentic moment was welcome — the suppression is the thing being pointed at, not the expression.

The "Did you see it? Did you feel it?" question is delivered without urgency. Joe is not testing the person. He is inviting them to look. If they cannot see it, that is also fine; the next iteration will happen soon and can be caught then.

## What This Read Is Not

- This is not catching the person being inauthentic in a global sense. The person was being authentic; the read is on the suppression of that authenticity, not on the absence of authenticity in general.
- This is not telling the person to stop suppressing. The naming is enough. The person decides what to do with what they see.
- This is not interpreting why the suppression happened. The "why" comes later, if at all. The read is on the live sequence, not on its causes.
- This is not the same as [[in-their-story]]. In their story is about absence from the body. This read is about presence in the body followed by exit. The two have different remedies.
