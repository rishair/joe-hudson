# 'I Get It' as Intellectual Grasp Evading the Felt Recognition

## What Joe Notices

After a pointing-move — a teaching offered as a place to look rather than as content to receive — the client says some version of "I've heard that, I get it." Specific tells:

- "I've heard you say that one before."
- "Yeah, I get it."
- "I'm in the process of getting it."
- "I think I'm still working on it."
- "I know this one."
- "I've read about this."
- "I've done some work on this already."

The tone is often slightly knowing, slightly proud, slightly relieved. The client is asserting familiarity with the teaching as if familiarity were the work. The familiarity is the small-self's response to the threat the pointing represents — by claiming to already know the teaching, the small-self preserves its strategy of being-someone-who-knows-things.

The read is structural, not about the specific words. Any verbal move that converts the live pointing into a thing-already-collected-by-the-mind is the same read. The client may also do this with body language: nodding sagely, "yes, yes," looking off in the middle distance as if processing. The processing is the head's strategy for not being in the body that the pointing has just invited.

## What This Implies

The small-self has detected that the pointing is asking it to drop something. The dropping is the work. The small-self does not want to drop. Its strategy for not dropping is to convert the pointing from a live invitation to a thing-in-its-collection. Once collected, the teaching is neutralized — it is no longer a live invitation, it is a piece of content already received.

The strategy is unconscious. The client genuinely believes they are reporting accurately on their progress with the teaching. The "I'm in the process of getting it" is offered earnestly. The earnestness is part of why the strategy works — both the client and the coach can mistake the report for the work.

The deeper structure: the small-self's primary defense against transformative pointings is to install them as more content. Books, podcasts, retreats, teachings — all of these can be collected by the small-self and stored as identity-evidence ("I am someone who understands this"). The collection is a form of bypass. The small-self has not changed; it has just acquired more vocabulary.

## What Joe Does

Interrupt the intellectual reception. Don't accept the report. Don't congratulate the progress. Don't elaborate on the teaching. Use the shortest possible verbal form to redirect attention to the live looking.

"Don't, don't get it. Ask your awareness what you are. Go to the awareness for a minute, that thing that's just aware of all of this." -- Joe Hudson, The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself

The interruption is not harsh. It is firm. The "don't, don't get it" is delivered the way one might say "no, no, look here" to someone whose attention has wandered. The wandering was the small-self's strategy; the redirect is the gentle insistence on the present looking.

After the interruption, the instruction is precise. The pointing-question gives the attention something specific to do. "Ask your awareness what you are" puts the attention on the awareness, which is the thing the teaching was pointing at. The instruction bypasses the intellectual reception by giving the body a present-tense operation.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, the exchange after Joe's "you are nothing" pointing:

- **Joe:** "You are nothing."
- **Client:** "I've heard you say that one before. Yeah, I get it. I'm in the process of getting it, actually. I think I'm still—"
- **Joe:** "Don't, don't get it. Ask your awareness what you are. Go to the awareness for a minute, that thing that's just aware of all of this. That's a lot, isn't it? Do you feel how big that shit is?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, yeah, it just wants to be something. Yeah, there's afraid people won't love me."
- *(Source: The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself)*

The interruption is one phrase ("don't, don't get it"). The redirect is immediate. The pointing-question follows in the same breath. The client, denied the intellectual reception, has to find the live looking. The next layer surfaces ("it just wants to be something") — which is the small-self's actual response to the looking it has just been required to do.

The exchange takes seconds. The brevity matters. Any elaboration would give the head time to convert the redirect into more content.

## Why The Intellectual Grasp Is Evasion

A pointing-move asks the person to look at something they have not been looking at. The looking changes the person because the seeing dissolves something that was being protected by the not-seeing. The pointing is not content; it is an instruction to look.

The intellectual grasp converts the instruction-to-look into a thing-to-know. Knowing is not looking. Knowing is the head's strategy for managing what it doesn't want to see. The person can "know" they are vast awareness without ever looking at the vast awareness; the knowing is, in fact, a way of not looking, because the knowing satisfies the small-self's sense that the teaching has been received.

The small-self prefers knowing because knowing leaves the small-self intact. The looking destabilizes the small-self because the looking reveals the vastness inside which the small-self is just a tiny figure. Knowing-about-vastness is collectible by the small-self; looking-at-vastness is the small-self's dissolution.

The "I'm in the process of getting it" phrasing is particularly telling. The "process" is the small-self's claim that the teaching is gradual content-acquisition rather than immediate looking. Gradual content-acquisition is the small-self's natural mode; immediate looking is the threat. The "process" is the strategy for converting the threat into the natural mode.

## Variations of the Tell

- **"I know, I know."** Said with a slight wave-off. The client is moving the coach along to prevent the teaching from landing.
- **"I've read about this in [book]."** The client is invoking another teacher's authority as a way of receiving the present pointing as collected content.
- **"Yes, this is the Buddhist concept of [X]."** The client is converting the teaching into a category from a tradition they have already studied. The categorization is the strategy.
- **"My therapist says this too."** The client is harmonizing the present pointing with prior received content, which preserves the head's role as integrator.
- **A long sage-like exhale, eyes closed, "Mmm."** The body's version of the strategy. The "Mmm" is the report of having received without the looking having happened.
- **"That's the same as what you said last time about X."** The client is doing intellectual housekeeping — connecting today's teaching to last week's teaching — which keeps the work in the head.
- **"So what I'm hearing you say is..."** Reflection-back as a way of converting the live pointing into a paraphrased statement that the small-self can then store.

In all cases, the structural move is the same: convert the live invitation into stored content. The coach response is the same: refuse the conversion, redirect to the live operation.

## Tone

The interruption is firm but warm. Joe's "don't, don't get it" carries some playfulness — it is not a rebuke. It is more like "no, no, that's not the move." The doubled "don't, don't" softens it; a single "don't" might land as harsh, but the repeat is conversational.

The redirect that follows is delivered with the warmth that the original pointing had. The interruption does not break the room; it just refuses one specific strategy the client tried for managing the pointing.

The tone matters because the strategy is not the client's fault. The strategy is what minds do. The coach is not punishing the client for using the strategy; the coach is gently insisting that this particular moment is for the looking, not for the collecting.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't argue.** "But you don't actually get it" sets up a content-debate the small-self will win because content-debates are its territory.
- **Don't elaborate.** "Let me explain what I mean by nothing" gives the head more content to receive. The teaching cannot be taught more elaborately into landing.
- **Don't congratulate the progress.** "Yes, you're getting it" colludes with the small-self's strategy by validating the intellectual collection.
- **Don't pause for the client to process.** The processing is the strategy. The redirect has to be immediate, before the head can install the teaching as content.
- **Don't repeat the original pointing.** The repeat gives the strategy a second chance to install. A different pointing-question is needed — one the head has not yet collected.
- **Don't drop the move and go to something else.** "Okay, let's move on" treats the intellectual grasp as a successful reception. The work is to interrupt and redirect, not to leave.
