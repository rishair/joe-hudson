# Interrupting Your Own Descent

## What Joe Notices

The person is asked a question that points at the felt territory — usually "what's wrong with you?" or "what are you hiding?" or some other invitation into a body-level inquiry. Instead of dismissing the question, the person starts to actually go there. They begin descending. The body softens or sinks. The eyes drop. The pace slows. The "I don't know" that comes out is not a refusal; it is the beginning of the work — the person is in the not-knowing, opening to what is there.

Then, almost immediately, the person surfaces. They give an intellectual answer to the question. The descent stops. The work that was just beginning is replaced by content.

The observable signature:

- The person says "I don't know" with a tone that is not dismissive — it is genuine, soft, possibly accompanied by a small smile or a sinking inward.
- The body starts to drop. Eyes may close, head may lower, breath may deepen.
- A pause begins. The pause feels different from a thinking pause — there is felt activity, not cognitive activity.
- Then the person speaks. The speech contains an intellectual answer ("I guess there's also this aspect of like shame," or some other framed content).
- The descent stops. The body returns to its prior state. The work that was just beginning is over.

The contrast with [[intellectual-process-with-no-somatic-component]]: that read catches a person who never went somatic at all. This read catches a person who went somatic for a moment and then left. The difference matters because the leaving is the diagnostic. The person *can* go down. They have the capacity. The pattern is to interrupt the capacity by jumping to answer.

## What This Implies

The descent is the work. The answer is the avoidance of the work. The person has a learned response to questions about the felt territory: give the answer the question seems to be asking for. The response was probably installed early — answer the adult, perform competence, do not stay in the not-knowing. The mechanism keeps the person out of the felt place by reflexively filling the space with cognitive content.

The structural problem: the question that points at the felt territory is not actually asking for an answer. The question is asking for the descent. The "answer" is the head's attempt to satisfy the question on terms the head can handle. The head is operating as the gatekeeper. It will offer content as a substitute for the felt experience the question was trying to invite.

This is also the live form of [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]] in reverse. There, the question pulls the person out of themselves. Here, the descent pulls the person into themselves, and the head answers to pull them back out. Both have the same structure: a felt process gets interrupted by a cognitive move that looks reasonable on the surface.

The "I don't know" is the doorway. Most people treat "I don't know" as a dead end. In this configuration, the "I don't know" is actually the work — it is the person finding themselves in the not-knowing, which is the only place from which the felt answer can arrive. The interrupting-answer is the move that closes the doorway.

## What Joe Does

Joe names what just happened with precision. He does not let the answer stand. He describes the descent that the person was in, names that the person interrupted it, and instructs them to go back without trying to answer this time.

The form: "You started going down, like I don't know how else to describe this, but you started going down and then you answered my question. Don't — just keep going down for a minute."

The key instruction is the second sentence: keep going without needing to find an answer. The person's reflex is to descend in order to retrieve an answer. The instruction removes the answer-requirement. The descent is the point. The not-knowing is the place to stay.

The variant for the same read: "You don't need to find an answer, just keep going there for a minute."

Joe also distinguishes the kind of "going down" — it is felt, not visual. When the person clarifies "you mean like looking down?" Joe corrects: "I don't know what it was. It was like you started sinking down into yourself when you looked for what was wrong with you, and you said, I don't know. Like, so you kind of like went into this, I don't know, and then you answered, but you didn't keep exploring the I don't know."

The precision matters. The person is being asked to stay with the felt descent, not to perform a body motion. The distinction prevents the instruction from becoming another cognitive task.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "So what's wrong with you? What is it that I'm gonna see that I'm gonna think is — that's bad?"
- **Guest:** *(sinks down into himself, pause)* "I don't know, right?"
- **Joe:** "Isn't that fucking weird?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "So you started — you started going down, like, I don't know how else to describe this, but you started going down and then you answered my question. Don't — just keep going down for a minute."
- **Guest:** "You mean like looking down, or—"
- **Joe:** "I don't know what it was. It was like you started sinking down into yourself when you looked for what was wrong with you, and you said, I don't know. Like, so you kind of like went into this, I don't know, and then you answered, but you didn't keep exploring the I don't know. So what's wrong with you? What is it that we're going to find out when we see you that's shameful? You don't need to find an answer, just keep going there for a minute."
- *(Source: Social Anxiety Breakthrough)*

The instruction is layered: stay with the descent, do not require an answer, the not-knowing is the work. The repetition of the question ("what's wrong with you?") with the explicit no-answer-required is the move that lets the person actually stay with the question instead of bouncing off it.

## Tone

Slow, warm, observational. Joe is describing what he saw with precision but without urgency. The tone of the instruction is permission-giving — "you don't need to find an answer" is a release, not a demand. The slowness matters. If the instruction is delivered quickly, the person reads it as another task and re-enters the answering mode.

There is also a quality of curiosity. "Isn't that fucking weird?" is Joe sharing his own delight at the strangeness of the not-knowing. The sharing makes the not-knowing into something interesting rather than something deficient. The person can stay there because their staying is being affirmed.
