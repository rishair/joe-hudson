# Rehearsing How to Say It

## What Joe Notices

The person is working out how to deliver something before they say it. They are running multiple versions through their head, looking for the wording that will produce the desired reaction in the listener. They may explicitly describe this rehearsal, or it may show up in the careful, almost prepared quality of their speech.

Specific signals:
- "I've been trying to figure out how to say this to her."
- "Let me think about the best way to put this."
- The person workshops the same statement with multiple framings in conversation with the coach.
- They process the issue with a safe third party before approaching the actual person.
- They have a "quick paragraph" they intend to deliver and are looking for confirmation it is the right one.
- Their actual speech with the relevant person is noticeably more polished than their speech elsewhere.

"If you're worried about how you're gonna say it so that the person across from you can hear it and accept it, and you've run over multiple solutions, multiple ways of talking to them in your head, with the hopes that you're going to get it so that they'll have a different reaction than they normally do, then you're definitely in it. Then you're definitely caretaker." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

## What This Implies

The rehearsal is designed to control the listener's reaction. The hope is that the right wording will produce a different response than the listener normally has. This is the definition of [[caring-vs-caretaking]] in action: managing the other person's experience.

The rehearsal also serves the speaker. By delivering the prepared paragraph, the speaker does not have to feel their way through the moment. They have something to hide behind: a polished line that is technically vulnerable but emotionally protected.

Brett names this dynamic in the source: "Now I've gotten to my core wants. I've gotten to my authenticity. I know what I want. Now what's the way that I can say it in a quick paragraph that I can hide behind? Yeah, that's exactly right. And that will exactly get this point across without me messing it up or having to hold my ground in a sense or truly feel into myself in the moment."

The rehearsed paragraph is a substitute for being present. The person has done the emotional work in a safe context and now wants to deliver the result without re-entering the feeling state. The listener feels the absence even when the words are correct.

## What Joe Does

Joe names the rehearsal as a diagnostic of caretaking. The naming alone often produces immediate recognition.

He invites the alternative: have the conversation without the script. Bring the constriction that arises into the conversation itself, rather than pre-processing it away.

The specific move Joe offers: open the conversation by naming the rehearsal and the fear. "I'm nervous that in the middle of this conversation, I am going to constrict and I'm not going to bring my full true self here, and I don't want that. What I want is to love you with my full self. And if you see me constricting, if you see me not being myself, please point it out to me and just give me a couple minutes or seconds or whatever I need to get back to being able to bring my full truth to you."

This reframes the conversation as a co-created event in which the speaker's struggle is allowed to be visible. The vulnerability is the act of letting the unscripted self show up, not the act of delivering the prepared truth.

He also reframes the moment of constriction as the practice opportunity, not the failure to avoid. "How do we open up and let it permeate through us? That's like wonderful news. It means it gives you practice. It's like the best batting cage in the world." See [[embrace-intensity]].

## Why the Rehearsed Version Often Fails

Even when the prepared paragraph is delivered cleanly, the listener typically receives it differently than the speaker expected. Two reasons:

First, the listener feels the absence of presence. A prepared statement, no matter how thoughtful, lacks the in-the-moment quality of authentic contact. The listener may not be able to articulate what is missing, but they sense it. The conversation does not land as connection.

Second, the rehearsal often produces an over-justified, over-explained delivery. The speaker has anticipated objections and pre-answered them. The listener feels managed.

The clean alternative is not unrehearsed in the sense of unprepared. It is unrehearsed in the sense of unscripted. The speaker knows what is true. They do not know exactly how they will say it, because they have not pre-decided what reaction is acceptable.

## Connection to the Subtle Layer

Beyond the obvious rehearsal of difficult conversations, this read extends to the smallest scales of speech. Joe's instruction in the source:

"Listen very carefully to every sentence that you're speaking to them. Notice how many of them are subtle excuses or subtle hedges, not completely owning yourself, not completely owning your experience, hedging it, shaving it, so that they will think differently of you than what you're scared they're gonna think of you." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

Every modification of speech to manage the listener's impression is a micro-rehearsal. The mature version of this practice is to notice the rehearsals at sentence level, not just at conversation level.

## Tone

Curious and clarifying. The rehearsal is so habitual most people do not notice they are doing it. The naming is invitational, not accusatory. "Notice the rehearsal" produces more shift than "stop rehearsing."

When the person resists ("but I just want to say it well"), Joe holds the line that the wanting-to-say-it-well is the diagnostic, not the well-saying.
