# Said Want Without Felt Want

## What Joe Notices

The person states a want. The body produces the opposite. The mouth says "I want this to be easy" while the body in the room is in tension, the conversation is becoming complicated, and the produced output is hardness. The said-want and the felt-want diverge, and the divergence is visible.

Joe reads this by tracking the field, not just the words. If a person genuinely wanted ease, the room would have ease in it. If a person genuinely wanted fun, the conversation would be fun. The presence of the want in the room is the evidence the want is real. The absence of the want from the room — while the words for the want are being said — is the read.

Signals:

- The person says "I want X" and the moment of saying it produces non-X in the body or the room.
- The stated want has a quality of recitation. The words are correct; the felt body underneath them is unchanged.
- The conversation continues to produce the opposite of the stated want even after the want has been stated.
- The person reaches for words that sound like wanting ("I want," "I'd like," "I love") without the felt backing the words would have if the want were live.
- The body shows no movement when the want is stated. A real want, said aloud, usually produces a small somatic shift — a softening, a quickening, a settling. The said-want-without-felt-want produces nothing in the body.
- The same want has been stated many times before and never produced action.

## What This Implies

The want is not real in the way the person thinks it is. They have the concept of wanting X. They do not have the embodied wanting of X. The concept is generating the words. The body is doing something else. Sometimes the body is producing the opposite of the stated want, which suggests there is an actual want that contradicts the stated one. Sometimes the body is producing nothing, which suggests the want is structurally absent — the person has been operating from a should about what they should want rather than from a want.

This is closely related to [[the-want]] as a distinction from a should. The said want without a felt want is usually a should pretending to be a want. The person has been told (or has told themselves) that they should want X, and now they say they want X, and the body has not been consulted.

In creative work, the read is particularly important. A person who reports wanting to create but never creates is not in a discipline problem. They are in a want problem. The said-want has been carrying the burden of explaining why they think they should be creating, while the actual want — if there is one — has not been located. See [[i-cant-enter-my-creative-work]].

In coaching conversations, the read often appears alongside [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]]. The person is trying to want what they say they want, and the trying is the live demonstration that the wanting is not actually happening on its own.

## What Joe Does

Name the gap directly. Use the precise diagnostic phrasing.

> "I hear that you say it, but I don't hear you actually wanting it, because if you wanted it, like, it would be easy and fun and beautiful right now, which it is for me anyways. For you, it's hard." -- Joe Hudson, 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled

Two moves in one sentence. First, the gap is named ("I hear you say it, but I don't hear you actually wanting it"). Second, the evidence is offered ("if you wanted it, it would be easy and fun and beautiful right now"). The evidence is what makes the naming actionable. The person can check: is the easy-and-fun-and-beautiful present right now? If not, the want is not present right now.

The reference to "it is for me" is critical. Joe names that the want is producing the experience in his side of the room. The room contains the contrast: one person wanting the ease that is in the room, another person saying they want the ease while producing hardness. The contrast makes the diagnostic landable.

After the gap is named:

- Refuse to argue with the stated want. The person will often defend the want ("but I really do want this"). Joe does not engage with the defense. He stays at the structural level: what is being produced is the data; the stated want without the produced version is not the data.
- Make the produced output the operational material. If the person is producing hardness while wanting ease, the hardness is what's actually here. The work is with the hardness, not with the abstract want for ease.
- Use the read as the bridge to [[the-elevator-descent-to-source]] or [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]]. The body that is producing hardness needs to land somewhere new before the want for ease can be re-felt.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Client:** "Yeah, I don't want it to be hard. I want it to be fun and happy and beautiful."
- **Joe:** "I hear that you say it, but I don't hear you actually wanting it, because if you wanted it, like, it would be easy and fun and beautiful right now, which it is for me anyways. For you, it's hard."
- **Client:** "I know why your tag is F you, Joe Hudson. Yes, I got that the first video. I cried for 3 days."
- *(Source: 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled)*

The client's response — humor about Joe's reputation — is the marker that the read landed. She heard the gap. The crying-for-three-days reference is the previous time the gap was named for her, which produced movement. The current naming is producing the same kind of seeing.

## Tone

Direct, slightly blunt, with warmth underneath. The naming is not delivered as accusation. It is delivered as observation, with the observation made factual by the evidence ("which it is for me anyways"). The warmth comes through in the implicit message: I am telling you this because the gap is real and you can do something about it once you see it.

The bluntness is necessary. The said want without felt want has been protected by social politeness; people generally do not name the discrepancy because pointing out that someone does not actually want what they say they want feels rude. The protection is what allows the gap to persist. The naming breaks the protection.

## What This Read Is Not

- Not "the person is lying." The person believes they want the thing. The believing is sincere. What is missing is the embodiment.
- Not "the person should want it more." Wanting more is the same operation that produces the said-want without the felt-want. The work is not to increase the wanting; it is to locate where the actual wanting is.
- Not always indicative of a should. Sometimes the body has not yet caught up with a new want. The diagnostic is not "you don't really want this." It is "the wanting isn't present in the room right now."
- Not a moral failing of the person. The gap is structural and common. Most people walk around stating wants that are concepts rather than felt pulls. The work is to notice and to relocate.
