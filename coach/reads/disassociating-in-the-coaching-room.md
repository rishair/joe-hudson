# Disassociating in the Coaching Room

## What Joe Notices

The person is attempting to feel something difficult and a subtle disconnection happens. Their eyes drop or move away. Their face goes slightly slack. The energy in the body thins. They report being "back" a moment later, often noting that something was harder than expected.

Signals:
- Eye contact breaks at the moment of feeling
- The person says "I'm bringing myself back in" or "there's some mild dissociation"
- The body posture softens or collapses slightly
- The voice flattens
- The person looks past Joe rather than at him
- A subtle but distinct "leaving" while still in the chair

The decisive signal is the timing. The disconnection happens at the moment the person is closest to feeling what they came to feel. The disassociation is not random. It is a precise response to the threshold.

## What This Implies

The person is encountering the edge of their capacity to be present with the feeling. The body has learned that this feeling, in this context (often with an authority-coded figure), is dangerous. Dissociation is the body's protective response: thin the connection so the feeling cannot land fully.

In the coaching room, the coach is sometimes occupying the authority slot. The dissociation is then a real-time replay of the childhood pattern: the person leaves their body when the powerful person is close. The room becomes the living version of the workplace.

"I feel like I lose connection with myself when I look at you. I don't lose connection with myself. It's like a thinner path or something. It's a lot more difficult." -- (person speaking in Father Figures and Authority)

The dissociation is not failure. It is the pattern showing up where it can finally be worked with. The childhood version had no skilled witness. The current version has one.

## What Joe Does

Name it without making it a problem. "I notice you stop looking at me when you're trying to feel this." The naming itself often interrupts the pattern enough to let the person come back.

Stay present. Joe does not chase the person or push them to come back faster. He lets them notice the leaving and the returning. The capacity grows through the small loop: leaving, noticing, returning.

Use it as material. The pattern that runs at work is the same pattern running in the room. Once both are visible, the pattern is the work.

Ask what the feeling would be without the dissociation. "What would this feeling be if you weren't resisting it? If you weren't disassociating, if you weren't scared?" This bridges the person from the protective move to the underlying experience.

## The Intellectualizing Variant

The eye-drop variant is the most visible form of dissociation in the room. There is a subtler form: the person, on the edge of feeling something, leaves the body by going into thought. They start analyzing what just happened. They make a smart observation. They ask an interesting question. The intellectual move feels engaged -- it doesn't look like leaving -- but it is the same protective motion as the eye-drop, achieved through the head instead of through the body.

Signals of the intellectualizing variant:
- The person reaches the edge of a feeling and starts thinking about the feeling
- "Now I'm intellectualizing. I'm in my head now. Dissociating from it."
- An interesting analytical observation appears precisely at the moment something would otherwise be felt
- A meta-question ("am I doing this right?") that pulls the person out of contact -- see [[am-i-doing-it-right]]
- A pivot to a teaching question or a thought experiment
- The person notes that the conversation "got interesting" right as they were about to drop in

The intellectualizing variant is harder to name because it doesn't look like leaving. It looks like good participation. But the timing tells the truth -- the head-trip happens precisely at the moment of contact, the same way the eye-drop does.

What Joe does is the same as with the eye-drop. He names it without making it bad. "What just happened? You were there behind the eyes. Then I asked you an interesting question and..." This lets the person notice the leaving in real time. Often they name it themselves: "I'm intellectualizing now."

A specific subtle move Joe makes: when the person dissociates into a flattering analytical observation ("you ask such interesting questions"), he doesn't accept the compliment. He names that the compliment is the dissociation. "Then I asked you an interesting question. And then you..." The compliment was a pivot away from what was being felt. Naming the pivot brings the person back.

See also [[intellectual-question-with-emotional-body]] for the version of this read that happens at the start of a session rather than mid-session.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "I notice you stop looking at me when you're trying to feel this."
- **Person:** "Yeah. I feel like I lose connection with myself when I look at you."
- **Joe:** "So what would this feeling be if you weren't resisting it? If you weren't disassociating, if you weren't scared?"
- **Person:** "It feels really alive. It also feels almost like so alive that also feels scary."
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. Joe uses the live dissociation to find what is on the other side of it.)*

## Tone

Quiet, observational, slow. Joe does not match the dissociation with intensity. He matches it with steady presence. The pacing widens to allow the person to come back at their own rate.

## The Live-Pattern Variant: "You're Doing It Right Now"

A specific deployment of this read when the person has explicitly named the dissociation pattern as something they do generally and is doing it in the room. The standard signals are present (eye-drop, posture softening, etc.) but the person is also reporting the pattern verbally as historical or general ("I live a lot of my life in my head," "I've been isolated"). The descriptive frame is itself maintaining the distance from the live event.

Joe's compressed move for this variant: "You're isolated right now, bro. You're isolating the sadness that's happening right now, like in this moment."

The naming is short. It refuses the descriptive frame and points at the present-tense fact. The person cannot keep the pattern at a historical distance when it is happening in their body as they describe it. See [[point-out-theyre-already-doing-it]] for the inverse-variant of the move that runs on this read, and [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]] for the related read where the person's "I'm trying" is itself the dissociation in real time.
