# The Fight-or-Roll-Over Binary

## What Joe Notices

When the person describes how they engage with authority (a boss, a parent, a powerful figure), only two options are on the table: fight or roll over. Sometimes the person names the binary explicitly. More often it is implicit in the language.

Signals:
- "I either want to fight myself or I just want to roll over and stay safe."
- "I either confront them or I shut down."
- The person describes their workplace pattern as oscillating between rebellion and compliance with no third stance
- When asked what they would do, the person can only describe variations of the two poles
- Joe asks for a third option and the person finds only flavors of fight or flavors of submission

The decisive moment is when Joe asks "how would it be to do neither?" and the person cannot generate a felt sense of what neither would be. The body has only two stances rehearsed.

## What This Implies

The fight-or-roll-over binary is the cognitive shape of a power-over frame. The world is divided into who controls and who is controlled. Both options leave the authority in the control seat. To fight is to react against. To roll over is to react beneath. Both are reactions. Neither is empowerment.

"To rebel against somebody or to do what they tell you, both of them put them in the control seat." -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

This is also an instance of [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]]. The narrow option space (two) is what unfelt fear looks like in cognition. The fear underneath is usually fear of feeling the helplessness that arrived in childhood when fighting the authority was punished and rolling over was the only available adaptation.

The two poles often alternate. Rebellion bleeds into collapse and collapse bleeds back into rebellion. "It's just like softer. Maybe if I fight a little quieter. Because then I'll also feel the collapse the other side, and I'm like, wait, yeah, okay, stand back up." Both moves are still inside the binary.

## What Joe Does

First, name the binary. Make it visible. "There's the fight, there's the roll over. What's the third option?" Often the person cannot answer immediately.

Then ask for the felt sense of the opposite. "What would the opposite of fighting feel like?" Watch for whether the person can drop into the body to find it, or whether the mind keeps generating variations of the original two.

Then introduce the third stance directly. Joe uses several names: [[upright-undefended-love]], "balance," "non-fight, non-rolling over." The somatic version: not falling back (defense), not falling forward (submission), but upright.

If the person finds the third stance, Joe role-plays the authority figure to let the person practice it live. See [[become-the-authority-figure]].

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "How would it be to say to me, Joe, I want to surrender to you or I want to fight you? I don't want to be with you. I just want to surrender to you or fight you."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I want to fight you. Or I want to roll over and not get hurt."
- **Joe:** "How much distance did that just create between us?"
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. Joe uses the live relational moment to make the binary observable.)*

- **Joe:** "What's the opposite of fighting? It feels like you're still fighting a little bit."
- **Person:** "I am. I'm totally still fighting. Just a little. It's just like softer. Maybe if I fight a little quieter."
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. The "softer fight" is still inside the binary.)*

## Tone

Direct but not confrontational. Joe names the binary as a structure to examine, not as a flaw to fix. He uses the live coaching relationship as the demonstration field rather than only talking about workplace examples.
