# Body Cues of Shame About Being Seen

## What Joe Notices

In the first seconds of the conversation, before anyone has said anything substantial, the body is already telling the story. The cues cluster around being-seen and wanting-to-be-seen.

The specific cues that surface in the source-session commentary:

- **Looking down and to the right.** Repeatedly, in the first three or four exchanges. Not occasional eye-flicks but a recurring direction the eyes go when meeting the coach's face becomes hard. "He's having a hard time seeing me. He's having a hard time seeing himself."
- **Head tilt straight down.** Distinct from the side-tilt. When asked "what would getting that question answered get you?" — what the person hopes would happen if their concern were addressed — the head goes straight down rather than to the side. The straight-down tilt signals the deeper layer of shame: shame about *wanting* to be seen, not just shame about not being good enough to be seen.
- **Hedging clusters.** "My immediate reaction is like some way of loving myself maybe." Multiple hedges in one short answer ("like," "some way of," "maybe") signal the layer of "it's not okay to love myself, to want to be seen, like all these things that are 100% completely human of us that we all want."
- **Decrease in cluster density after being seen.** After the person feels received without attack, the cues decrease — not to zero, but the three maybes become one, the four look-downs become one. The decrease itself is a usable signal of the work landing.

The decisive frame: these are not nervous tics or personality features. They are the body executing the not-letting-myself-be-seen pattern in real time. Each cue is the [[the-desire-to-be-seen]] see-me/don't-see-me push-pull leaking into the body.

## What This Implies

Two stages of shame are at play simultaneously. The shallower layer is "I'm not good enough to be seen." The deeper layer is "I want to be seen."

"So oftentimes you're working in both of those two sections with somebody. It's not just the being seen, it's the, is it okay to want to be seen? I want to be seen. I want you to see me. Like, there's a lot of shame in that for some folks." — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

See [[two-layers-of-shame-about-being-seen]] for the underlying principle.

The hedging is the verbal form of the same pattern. The "maybe" before "loving myself" is the body's way of pre-disclaiming the want — saying "I want this but please don't take it that I'm really claiming I want this." The qualifier protects against the imagined punishment for wanting.

The reason this read is so useful early in a session: it gives the coach the shape of the work before the person has fully named what they came for. By the time the person says "I want to recognize my accomplishments" or "how do I love myself?" the body has already revealed that the obstacle is shame about both *being seen* and *wanting to be seen*. The coaching can be aimed at the right layer immediately.

## What Joe Does

The move is not to point out the cues to the person. The cues are diagnostic, not material to be discussed. The move is to use the cues to inform the coaching response.

- **Reflect the wanting back as okay.** When the person hedges the want ("maybe self-love"), receive the want without attack and without making them justify it. Often: a small confirmation, a continued presence, a sense that the want is welcome. The hedge will reduce in the next exchange.
- **Question the question.** "How is the answer to that question going to help you? What's the strategy of getting that question answered gets you?" This pulls the person off the surface concern and into what they actually want from getting it answered — which is usually the felt experience the shame is blocking access to.
- **Move quickly to the in-the-room felt experience.** Once the want is named (even hedged), pivot to the demonstration of the want being met. See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]].
- **Watch for the cluster density to drop.** As the work lands, the cues should decrease. If they don't, the person isn't receiving what the work is offering. Adjust.
- **Don't catalog the cues out loud.** Naming "you keep looking down" risks adding shame to the body's natural protective movement. The cues are for the coach to read, not for the person to be told about. The exception is when the cue itself has become the obstacle to the work continuing — see [[name-the-look-down]].

## Dialogue Example

The commentary-overlay structure makes this dialogue unusual: it's Joe and Brett narrating what was happening in the body during the original session.

- **Person:** "Why do I tend to dismiss my accomplishments and also to even more than ignore my needs?"
- **Joe (in session):** "How is the answer to that question going to help you?"
- **Joe (commentary):** "So there again, stop, stop again. Now it went for a straight head tilt. It wasn't even a side, it was just a straight down head tilt when I asked him, what would it get you?"
- *(Source: How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown)*

The straight-down head tilt is what tells Joe the second layer (shame about wanting) is active, not just the first layer (shame about being inadequate).

- **Person:** "My immediate reaction is like some way of loving myself maybe."
- **Joe (commentary):** "So he said like, maybe, there's so much hedging there, which is just more of the — it's not okay to love myself, to want to be seen, like all these things that are 100% completely human of us that we all want."
- *(Source: How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown)*

The hedging-cluster read is what tells Joe the wanting-to-be-seen is the territory that needs to be welcomed.

After the person feels received without attack on the want:

- **Brett (commentary):** "Pausing here because you can already see how he's doing less of the physical hedging. In having been seen for wanting self-love and that not being attacked or shamed, there's a way that he's more present with you."
- **Joe (commentary):** "Absolutely. And it's not no hedging, it's less hedging, right? There's one maybe instead of three maybes. There's one look down instead of four look downs."
- *(Source: How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown)*

The cluster-density drop is the diagnostic that the welcoming has landed.

## Tone

The reading happens silently. There is no "I notice you keep looking down." The lightness of the coach's response — the small confirmations, the absence of evaluation — is the welcome the body is responding to.

When Joe narrates the cues in commentary, the tone is matter-of-fact and curious, not clinical. "He's looking down and to the right, so he's having a hard time seeing me." The cues are described as the body trying to manage an impossible situation, not as evidence that the person is broken.

## What This Read Is Not

- It is not a personality assessment. The person may not always look down. The cues are specifically active in the moments where being-seen is in question.
- It is not evidence that the person should be told to stop hedging. The hedging is doing a job. See [[welcome-the-trying]] for the analogous move on effortful patterns. Telling the person to stop hedging would add shame to the protective behavior.
- It is not a substitute for asking. The cues inform the coach's hypothesis. The work confirms or revises it. A person can look down for many reasons; the cluster of cues plus the verbal pattern is what makes the read.
