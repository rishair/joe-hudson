# Letting It In vs. Deflecting the Welcome

## What Joe Notices

When one person offers a welcome -- a "I love you in your emotions," "I want all of you," "you don't have to be the strong one" -- the receiver's body shows whether the welcome landed or bounced. Joe watches the reception, not just the offering.

The signals of letting it in:
- Body softens and opens rather than holding still
- Eyes maintain contact through the receiving
- Breath drops or deepens slightly
- A small visible relaxation in the shoulders, jaw, or face
- The receiver does not immediately respond with words (the body is processing before the language)

The signals of deflecting:
- A small flinch or contraction at one specific moment
- Eyes drop briefly
- A barely visible head shake
- An internal "yes but" that produces a tightening around the mouth
- A polite verbal acknowledgment that lands flat
- The receiver moves quickly to respond rather than absorbing

"You let that in. Usually when this happens, the person doesn't let it in, but I saw you fully let that in. You had like one little flash maybe where you didn't believe something. Is that, is that accurate, or did you -- is it -- was it all in?" -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Hold It All Anymore

The read is granular. It is not "did they let it in or not." It is "they let it in mostly, except for one flash where something did not land." Identifying the specific flash makes the unfelt resistance addressable.

## What This Implies

When someone has been operating under a belief that organizes years of behavior ("I have to be the strong one," "they can't handle me"), an offered welcome runs into that architecture. The body cannot fully receive what contradicts the structure it has been built around. The flash of not-letting-in is the architecture detecting the contradiction and partially deflecting.

The partial deflection is not bad faith. It is the body's habit. The same body that has been bracing for years cannot instantly stop bracing because someone said the right words. What can happen in the moment is partial reception, with the bouncing-off-parts visible to a coach who is watching.

The bounce reveals where the next work is. If the welcome had landed completely, the work would be over. The fact that something flashed means there is more structure to unwind.

## What Joe Does

Joe names what he saw. He does so without judgment and without making it the next problem.

"You had like one little flash maybe where you didn't believe something."

Then he checks the perception against the receiver's experience: "Is that accurate?" The receiver almost always knows. They felt the flash. They did not have language for it. The coach naming it gives them the chance to look at it.

After confirming, Joe often uses this as the bridge to the next layer of work. In the source session, the bridge was: "Is that enough for you to be able to just not believe the voice in your head anymore, at least with Christopher?" The "no" that came back was the data that the constraint is not held only by the partner's welcome; it is also held by an internal voice. The work then moves to the voice. See [[voice-in-the-head]].

## What Joe Does Not Do

He does not push the receiver to "let it in more." Pressure to receive backfires. The body does not soften under pressure.

He does not interpret the flash. He does not say "you didn't believe him because of your childhood." He names what he saw and lets the receiver name what it was.

He does not skip the check. Even when the body is clearly receiving, asking "is that accurate?" gives the receiver agency over the read. The read is collaborative, not imposed.

## Dialogue Example

After Christopher said directly: "Baby, I never want you to deal with my emotions for me. I just want you to be with me when you feel like you can... I love all of you, every part."

- **Joe:** "You let that in. Usually when this happens, the person doesn't let it in, but I saw you fully let that in. You had like one little flash maybe where you didn't believe something. Is that accurate, or did you -- is it -- was it all in?"
- **Sadie:** "No, maybe, but no, I, I think I took it in. Yeah, it was nice."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Okay. But still, somehow or another, that sounds like -- is that how -- how is that enough? As a closed question, but is that enough for you to be able to just not believe the voice in your head anymore, at least with Christopher?"
- **Sadie:** "No."

*(Source: I Can't Hold It All Anymore)*

The exchange: noticing what was let in, naming the small flash, checking against the receiver's experience, then using the partial reception as the bridge to the deeper work that is still needed. The whole sequence takes about thirty seconds.

## Tone

Joe delivers the read warmly. The noticing is presented as good news ("you let that in -- usually people don't"), then refined by the precise observation. The receiver is not being caught at something. They are being shown what they did well and where the remaining work is.

The tone matters. The same observation delivered as a catch ("I saw you not let it in") produces shame. The same observation delivered as appreciation with refinement ("you let it in -- there was a small flash") produces curiosity about the flash.
