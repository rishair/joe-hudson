# Treating a Felt State as Factual Reality

## What Joe Notices

The person describes a felt state using language that asserts it as factual. "I am stuck." "I have no choice." "I can't move." The grammar is declarative. The content is feeling.

The signature is a small flatness in how the report is delivered. The person is not exploring whether the statement is true. They are reporting it as the condition of their life. The feeling-state has been promoted to a fact-status without anyone noticing it.

A specific tell: when Joe gently challenges the report (asking what their life looked like a year ago, or pointing out that they are right now in the action of doing the session), the person can immediately produce counter-evidence. "Very different." "I've taken a lot of risk." The counter-evidence is right there, available. It was the language that had been functioning to keep the felt state operating as if it were the actual state.

The person is not lying. They are reporting accurately the feeling. The misidentification is in treating the feeling as the condition rather than as a sensation in the body that can be worked with.

## What This Implies

Underneath this read is a coaching opportunity that is immediate and structural. The work cannot proceed if the felt state is treated as the fact. Trying to work on "you are stuck" goes nowhere because the person is not stuck. They feel stuck. Until the distinction is surfaced, the conversation will keep trying to solve the wrong problem.

The collapse usually has a function. As long as "I feel stuck" stays smuggled in as "I am stuck," the felt sense doesn't have to be approached as a feeling. It gets handled at the wrong layer — as a circumstance to fix, a situation to escape, a problem to solve. The handling never lands because the layer is wrong.

The structural opportunity: once the distinction is named, the conversation can move from circumstance-talk to feeling-work. From "what do I do about my stuckness?" to "how does the stuckness feel?" The first question is unanswerable from inside the felt state. The second is the door into the felt material.

This is the same mechanism as [[problem-vs-something-happening]] at the level of language. The collapse of feeling into fact is what converts a felt experience into a "problem." Surfacing the collapse converts it back into something that can be felt through.

## What Joe Does

The move is precise and quick. Joe asks a question that surfaces evidence the person already has, then names the distinction with directness.

In the source transcript:

"What did your life look like a year ago?" — Person: "Very different." — Joe: "There, there we go. That would be not stuck."

Then, when the person tries to reassert "but for the last year it's felt stuck":

"Wait, I'm with you. I'm not gonna — but, but you said you — so you feel stuck. You just said it felt stuck. Okay, if your life looked a lot different, then it's not actually as stuck. I'm just saying, are we working on the fact that you're stuck, or are we working on the fact that you feel stuck?"

The person answers: "I think feeling stuck. Yeah, yeah, yeah."

The conversation has now moved to the right layer. Joe immediately follows with: "Great. And how does stuck feel?" — which is the entry into the felt material.

The structural form of the move:
1. Surface counter-evidence the person already has. Ask about another time, another situation, another piece of their own life that contradicts the totalizing report.
2. Get the person to agree that the counter-evidence is real.
3. Name the distinction directly. Are we working on the fact that you're X, or the fact that you feel X?
4. Once the person picks the feeling, ask how it feels in the body. The work has moved to the layer where movement is possible.

A related move when the counter-evidence is harder to produce: [[point-at-the-present-evidence]]. The person is right now, in this conversation, doing the action of someone who is not stuck. The session itself is evidence. "You are — you just took an action of going into the unknown and proving that you're not stuck."

## Tone

Light, almost playful. The point is not to catch the person in a contradiction. The point is to surface what they already know so the work can land on the right layer.

The "I'm not gonna — but" is characteristic. Joe is not arguing. He is gently insisting on the distinction long enough that the person sees it themselves. The cheerfulness keeps it from feeling like a takedown.

If the person gets defensive ("how is that?" or "I don't think you're hearing me"), Joe stays patient and re-states the distinction. The defensiveness is itself information — it is the system trying to preserve the felt-as-actual collapse because the collapse has been doing work.

## What This Read Is Not

This read is not a dismissal of the felt state. The person is feeling stuck. The feeling is real. The point of the read is precisely to validate the feeling by putting it on the layer where it can be worked with, rather than leaving it on the layer where it functions as a permanent self-description.

It is also not the same as the [[looking-for-whats-wrong]] read. That read is about a person who is manufacturing a problem to bring. This read is about a person who has a real felt experience but has misidentified it as a circumstance. The two are different.

It is also not the savior or victim reads from the [[fear-triangle]]. The person here is not necessarily in any of those roles. They are simply confusing a feeling for a fact. The triangle reads can be present too, but the felt-as-actual read is more basic and often comes first.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I'm not happy with the current state of things."
- **Joe:** "No."
- **Person:** "Yet I feel, I feel scared to take the actions that I want to take, and I feel stuck because the known is more familiar."
- **Joe:** "How long, what percentage of your life from 20 years old till today would that sentence be true? I am not happy with the state of things, and I am scared to take the risk of going into the unknown."
- **Person:** "Maybe 30 to 50. So I've taken a lot of risk, but I still keep finding myself in this space."
- **Joe:** "This what space of being unsatisfied with the way things are?"
- **Person:** "Yeah, feeling stuck, but feeling so terrified of making the next move that it's more familiar and safer to be in the stuck and unhappiness."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, I just want to point out that you just took an action of going into the unknown and proving that you're not stuck."
- *(Source: I Am Scared Of The Unknown — Joe surfaces both the historical counter-evidence ("30 to 50 percent of your life means you've also been unstuck") and the present-tense counter-evidence (the action of being in the session) in quick succession.)*
