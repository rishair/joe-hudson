# In the Story "I'm Not Being Seen"

## What Joe Notices

The person comes in with a complaint that someone (boss, partner, parent, friend) does not see them. The complaint is delivered with conviction. The evidence is specific. The frustration is real.

Signals this is a story rather than a clean grievance:
- The complaint locates the entire problem in the other person's failure to see
- The person has not asked directly for what they want, but expects to be seen
- The person is not letting themselves be seen in the current moment (eyes down, performance posture, careful language)
- The same complaint shows up with multiple people in the same role configuration
- The complaint is doing work: it justifies withdrawal, rebellion, or non-performance

The decisive signal is when Joe asks "how much are you letting yourself be seen right now?" and the answer is low. The complaint about not being seen is being held by someone who is actively not being seen.

## What This Implies

The "not being seen" frame is partially true and entirely useful as a defense. It accomplishes several things at once: it justifies the current emotional state, it places responsibility on the other, it protects against the risk of actually showing up, and it converts the person's own discomfort with being seen into an external obstacle.

The deeper layer: the person is wrapped up in their own wanting (to be seen, to be acknowledged, to be received in a specific way) in the same way they accuse the other of being wrapped up in themselves. The judgment of the authority figure is a mirror.

"How is that not being wrapped up in yourself? It's wrapped up in my wanting." -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

This read also includes a third feature: the percentage trick. Asked "how clearly are you seeing them?" the person admits they are not seeing clearly either. Nobody sees anybody 100%. The "they don't see me" complaint requires a threshold ("if it's below 50% that's not good enough"), but the person rarely applies the same threshold to their own seeing.

## What Joe Does

Mirror the judgment back. The move: take whatever quality the person is accusing the other of and ask "how is that not true about you?" The question is delivered without judgment. It is a wonder question, not an attack.

"What makes you judge your father? ... How is that not true about you? That's a scary question for me to ask, but how is that not true about you?" -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

Then ask about the underlying components. Joe maps "I'm not being seen" onto three things the person is not living: the want (they have not asked for what they want), the being seen (they are not letting themselves be seen), and the empowerment (they are responding to the dynamic rather than standing in their own ground).

After the mirror, the question becomes: how would it be to stop right now and let yourself be seen? This pulls the dynamic out of the past and into the room.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "They don't see me. It's very clear they don't know how I tick."
- **Joe:** "What's the evidence?"
- **Person:** [describes the evidence]
- **Joe:** "What if they did see you and you can't recognize it? I'm not saying that that's true by any stretch, but if it were true, what would it mean?"
- **Person:** "Well, it would mean I'm judging the shit out of them."
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority)*

- **Person:** "I'm specifically triggered if it's a man who reminds me of my dad."
- **Joe:** "How am I feeling about you right now in your estimation?" [Joe uses the live coaching relationship to test the story.]
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority)*

## Tone

Curious, not accusatory. Joe explicitly flags when he is asking a hard question: "That's a scary question for me to ask." He does not deliver the mirror as a gotcha. He delivers it as a wondering.
