# Trying to Be Comfortable Instead of Being Uncomfortable

## What Joe Notices

The person reports a specific body state — lightheaded, out-of-body, floaty, dissociated — while attempting something that they intellectually want to do. The natural assumption is that the dissociation is the symptom of the discomfort. It isn't. It's the symptom of the trying-to-not-be-uncomfortable. The body is dissociating because the person is fighting their own felt state. The fight is what produces the floatiness, not the felt state itself.

The signature:

- The person names the discomfort and then describes the body symptom.
- The body symptom is dissociative: lightheaded, out-of-body, floaty, head-not-attached, watching-from-outside.
- The discomfort itself is not the problem the person reports. The dissociation is.
- The person, when asked, distinguishes that the dissociation is what happens when they're "trying to be comfortable," not when they're "actually uncomfortable."
- Once the person sits inside the discomfort rather than fighting it, the dissociation lifts immediately — sometimes within a single sentence.

The clearest version of this read shows up when Joe asks directly: "Is that what happens when you're uncomfortable, or when you're trying to be comfortable?" The person answers without hesitation: "When I'm trying to be comfortable." The body knows. The body's report is precise. The dissociation maps to the trying, not to the underlying state.

## What This Implies

The person has been operating on a frame in which their job is to overcome the discomfort in order to do the thing. The frame has produced a chronic management-mode in which the discomfort is treated as a problem to be solved before the action can happen. The body has adapted to this frame by producing dissociation: a way of partially leaving the situation so that the discomfort is at a distance from the speaking apparatus.

The cost: the dissociation prevents the actual action from being delivered with any presence. The person is half-out-of-body while speaking. The speaking is correspondingly thin. The dissociation, which was supposed to make the discomfort manageable, has made the action itself unavailable as a full version. The person now feels both uncomfortable AND dissociated AND incompetent at the action. The trying produces the very inadequacy it was trying to prevent.

The structural problem is the comfort-as-prerequisite frame. The person believes they have to become comfortable before they can do the thing. The frame is exactly backwards. Comfort is not the precondition. Discomfort is the conduit. The fight against the discomfort is the only thing producing the symptom they're trying to remove.

This read is the live cousin of [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] and [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]. In abandoning-self-while-speaking, the speaker has left their body to manage the listener. Here, the speaker has left their body to manage their own felt state. The structural shape is the same — the body has been vacated as a strategy — but the target of the management is different. The signal is also different: abandoning-self-while-speaking produces forward-leaning, monitoring, pressured speech. This read produces floaty, dissociative, half-present speech.

The deeper implication: the person has likely learned to manage their felt states from very early. The lightheaded-and-out-of-body response is not the original response. The original response was the felt state itself. Somewhere along the way the system learned that the felt state was unacceptable and developed the management-and-dissociation strategy to keep the felt state at bay. The adult is now running the strategy automatically in any situation that produces the felt state. The strategy produces the dissociation, the dissociation gets read as the problem, and the actual felt state never gets felt.

## What Joe Does

The primary move is to surface the three-mode diagnostic: are you trying to overcome the discomfort, are you fully in the discomfort, or is the discomfort not happening at all? See [[whats-happening-in-your-system-three-modes]]. The diagnostic surfaces which mode is running and gives the person a vocabulary for the difference.

- **Name the three modes.** "What's happening in your system right now? Are you trying to overcome the discomfort? Are you fully in the discomfort? Are you comfortable and discomfort's not happening? What of those three things is going on?" The naming gives the person language for distinguishing the modes that have all felt like "discomfort" from the inside.

- **Confirm which mode is producing the dissociation.** "Is that what happens when you're uncomfortable or when you're trying to be comfortable?" The question is precise. The person can usually answer immediately. The body knows.

- **Instruct them to switch modes.** "See what it's like to sit in that discomfort, walk right into the middle of it, and then speak from there." See [[walk-into-the-discomfort-and-speak-from-there]]. The instruction is not to feel more, not to overcome, not to push through. The instruction is to occupy the discomfort as the place from which the speaking happens.

- **Challenge the comfort-as-prerequisite premise.** "What makes you need to be comfortable? What if being uncomfortable is the best way to share your story?" See [[challenge-the-premise-in-the-framing]]. The frame that comfort is the precondition has to be dissolved. As long as it's running, the trying will run, and the dissociation will run.

- **Watch for the immediate lift.** When the person stops trying to be comfortable and instead is in the discomfort, the dissociation usually lifts inside one or two sentences. The voice gets fuller. The body settles. The eyes come present. The shift is observable and Joe will name it: "all of a sudden something just shifted." See [[name-the-shift-everyone-felt]].

What Joe does NOT do: address the dissociation as a thing to be reduced through breath work, grounding exercises, or somatic practices. The dissociation is a symptom of the wrong operation. Fix the operation and the dissociation lifts on its own. Adding a grounding exercise on top of the wrong operation is more management — and the management is what's producing the dissociation in the first place.

## Dialogue Examples

The compressed exchange from the source session:

- **Joe:** "What's happening in your system right now? Are you trying to overcome the discomfort? Are you fully in the discomfort? Are you comfortable and discomfort's not happening? What of those three things is going on?"
- **Person:** "I feel kind of like lightheaded, like sort of out-of-body experience."
- **Joe:** "And is that what happens when you're uncomfortable or when you're trying to be comfortable?"
- **Person:** "I think when I'm trying to be comfortable."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so try to be — yeah, see what it's like to like instead there's this discomfort feeling in you. I want you to like sit in that discomfort, walk right into the middle of it, and then speak from there. Like try to speak from — not even try, speak from the discomfort rather than from the desire to overcome it."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The diagnostic question produces an instant answer. The body knows which mode is running. The instruction that follows is to switch modes — not to add a practice, not to fix the discomfort, just to relocate the speaking from "from the trying" to "from the discomfort."

## Tone

Curious, observational. Joe is not alarmed by the dissociation, not in a rush to fix it. The three-mode question is asked with genuine interest, as if he's checking which gear is engaged. The naming of "trying to be comfortable" is matter-of-fact — this is a thing the body does, it's identifiable, here's how to spot it.

The instruction that follows ("walk right into the middle of it") is slower and gentler. The walking-into is the work. Rushing the instruction undoes it. Joe gives the person time to actually relocate themselves before the speaking continues.

When the shift happens — when the person speaks from the discomfort and the dissociation lifts — Joe's tone changes immediately. The recognition is acknowledged with weight ("all of a sudden something just shifted"). The acknowledgment is the wiring; the body is learning that being-in-the-discomfort is welcome and works.
