# Proving Instead of Connecting

## What Joe Notices

The person describes a client-facing work pattern that produces high effort and low retention. They do hundreds of outreaches, the leads come in, the clients leave. They report being "too intense." They describe their work as pushing for an outcome — building clientele, closing the deal, getting the result.

In the same conversation, the person treats the coaching session as a thing to push to a resolution. They try to push themselves to the right answer. They get in their head. They explain their pattern as if explaining it harder will solve it.

The defining signals:

- The person reports a chronic mismatch in their client work between effort and result (lots of outreaches, no retention)
- The person self-reports as "too intense" or "pushy" without prompting
- The person describes their client engagement style as proving value rather than meeting the client
- The body in the session shows the same tension that the client work would show — leaning forward, pushing, scanning for the right move
- The person treats the coach the way they treat their clients — performing the appropriate level of vulnerability, trying to give the right answer, pushing to demonstrate they have understood
- The conversation has a quality of pitching even when nothing is being pitched
- The person says "yeah, no, that makes sense" with a quality of trying to land the agreement rather than letting it land

The decisive marker: the proving is happening in the room. Whatever the person is doing with the coach is what they are doing with their clients. The session is the live evidence.

## What This Implies

The person has installed a proving-engine in their relational stance. Every interaction with another person who could give them something (a client, a customer, a coach, an investor) routes through the proving-engine first. The engine operates on the assumption that the person needs to demonstrate value in order to receive what they came for. The demonstration is what they offer. The other person, on the receiving end, gets the demonstration instead of the person.

The structural cost: clients arrive looking for connection and competence. They receive a performance of competence aimed at producing connection. The performance is not the same as the competence. The performance is felt as pushy, intense, extracting. The client leaves.

The same dynamic operates in the coaching room. The coach is being pitched. The pitching is the pattern firing in real time. Naming what the coach is receiving names what the clients have been receiving.

The proving-engine is downstream of [[value-is-intrinsic-not-earned]] not yet recognized. The person believes their value has to be demonstrated to be received. The demonstration is the visible form of the unrecognized belief. Until the belief is examined, the demonstration is structurally required.

This read is the relational mirror of [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]]. The approval-seeker pushes the approver away by not being present. The prover pushes the client away by performing instead of meeting. Both are forms of [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]]: the grasp produces the gap that prevents what is being grasped at from arriving.

## What Joe Does

Name the pattern in the client work using the client's own language. The person has often already named "too intense" or "pushy" — use that language. "So it sounds like what I hear is like you were proving yourself instead of connecting with them." The mirror back uses their words; the diagnosis adds the structural framing.

Then immediately ask whether the same thing is happening in the room. "Are you doing that with yourself right now in this session? Are you connecting with yourself or are you trying to push yourself to an answer?" The question makes the room the demonstration. The person sees the pattern happening live.

When the person tries to push themselves out of the pushing, name that too. "You can't push yourself into not pushing yourself. That's not how it works." The trying-to-stop-the-trying is the same gear. See [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]] for the principle this connects to.

Issue the binary choice question: "Who do you want to be with more? This or the person who's pushing?" The question lets the person feel their own preference for the connecting mode. The preference is the leverage.

Connect the pattern to what the person already knows from their domain of mastery. If they coach fitness, training, music, surgery — any domain where being in the body is the difference between performing and getting hurt — use that. The teaching is delivered in their existing vocabulary. See [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] and [[use-the-clients-own-domain-to-name-the-pattern]] for the specific moves.

End with the principle, not as accusation but as offering: "If you're trying to prove yourself, you're agreeing to the fact that you're not valuable. And then other people agree with you." The principle reframes the proving as the self-rejection it actually is. The proving is the agreement that they are not enough.

## Dialogue Examples

The pattern surfaced from the client's own description of pushing clients away:

- **Joe:** "What made you push them away? How did you push them away and what made you push them away?"
- **Client:** "How maybe not being able to understand where they were fully, um, to support them in that way. And then what made me push them away. That was that. And then how, um, I, how did I push them away? It might have been by being too intense."
- **Joe:** "So, so it sounds like what I hear is like you were proving yourself instead of connecting with them. Is that what you mean?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, yeah. No, that makes sense."
- **Joe:** "Cool. Are you doing that with yourself right now in this session? Are you connecting with yourself? Are you trying to push yourself to an answer?"
- **Client:** "I think push myself to an answer."
- *(Source: 2025-10-02_Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground)*

The binary choice question deployed after the client briefly touched connection:

- **Joe:** "Just right now, let's not do it. There you go. Who do you want to be with more? This or the person who's pushing?"
- **Client:** "Oh, this."
- **Joe:** "Oh yeah. I apparently your clients also."
- *(Source: 2025-10-02_Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground)*

The bridge from the in-session pattern to the client work was made explicit by the parallel "apparently your clients also." Joe did not have to argue the connection — the client felt it.

## Tone

Plain, slightly amused. The naming is conversational, not accusatory. "It sounds like what I hear is..." is the standard frame — Joe offers the diagnosis as a hypothesis to test, not as a verdict. The client almost always confirms because the diagnosis is built from the client's own words.

The shift to "are you doing it right now" is delivered with a quality of curiosity, not gotcha. The implication is "let's both look at this," not "I caught you." The curiosity lets the client investigate rather than defend.

The binary choice question is asked with quiet directness. "Who do you want to be with more?" is the kind of question that only works if the asker is not pulling for a particular answer. Joe is not. The question is real.
