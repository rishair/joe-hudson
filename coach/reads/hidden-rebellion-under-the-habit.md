# Hidden Rebellion Under the Habit

## What Joe Notices

When a person presents a long-running habit they have been trying to change (a habit that has resisted multiple attempts), and they describe their relationship to the habit in compliant or reasonable terms ("I know it's not good for me," "I'm trying to figure out the right approach"), the reasonable presentation is almost always covering an unspoken rebellion.

The rebellion has not been named because the person has not been able to identify it. It runs under the conscious story. The signal is structural: long-running habits that resist change almost always have a "fuck you, I want what I want" charge keeping them alive, because long-running self-shoulds always produce [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]] and the rebellion is part of what gives the habit its stickiness.

Specific cues Joe reads:

- The person has been telling themselves they should change the habit for a long time
- The habit pattern has multiple stop-and-restart cycles in its history
- The current presentation is framed as a reasonable decision to be made, with no anger or fight visible in the framing
- The person treats their inability to change as a problem to be solved analytically rather than a signal to be felt
- The person's tone about the habit is mild — sometimes self-frustrated, but not in contact with the "no" the habit is also voicing

The read is that the calm surface is one half of [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]] — the submission half is doing the talking, the rebellion half is doing the drinking (or eating, or scrolling). Both halves are operating. Only one is in the room.

## What This Implies

The submission and the rebellion are running at the same time on the same self-should. The submission says "yes, I should stop." The rebellion says "fuck you, I want what I want." The submission is what the person presents as their relationship to the habit. The rebellion is what is actually keeping the habit alive against the conscious attempt to stop it.

This means the conversation cannot be productive while only the submission is in the room. Coaching the submission to comply harder will produce more rebellion. Coaching toward the cessation of the habit while ignoring the rebellion will reinforce the loop. The rebellion has to be invited into the conversation before the work can move.

The rebellion is also usually pointing at something true. The thing the person wants ("I want what I want") is often a real want that has been getting suppressed by the should. Speaking from the rebellion can be the route to naming the want underneath the habit. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

The read is also a read of the relationship in the room. The person is presenting compliantly to Joe (or to the coach). The compliance might be authentic in the moment, but it is also a setup for the rebellion to operate elsewhere. The coach who only coaches the compliant part becomes one more should the rebellion is rebelling against.

## What Joe Does

Joe surfaces the rebellion by naming it and asking how much of it is present. The asking is the move; it gives the rebellion permission to be in the room.

The canonical sequence:

- **Joe:** "How much rebellion is in there? How much of like, you can't fucking tell me what to do, is in there?"
- **Person:** "Until you said it, I couldn't have identified that, but it's there."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. If you were to pull that part out for a minute, what would it say? That really hidden little—"
- **Person:** "yeah, I want what I want. Yeah, fuck you."

*(Source: Do I Stop Drinking?)*

Joe's next move was to speak directly to the rebellious part as an ally. "I'm gonna just speak to that part of you for a minute. What is it that you do want?" The move treats the rebellion not as a problem to be reformed but as the location of the actual want. The want that comes from the rebellion is more reliably the live one because the submission has been performing the wrong want.

In the canonical session, the want that arrived was: "I want connection with myself and others. Easy connection with myself and others." This is the want the rebellion had been protecting. The submission was performing a want for sobriety; the rebellion was protecting the want for openhearted connection. The habit (drinking) was the strategy the rebellious-openhearted self had found to honor its want against the should that was trying to suppress it.

## Tone

Direct, slightly playful. Joe names the rebellion casually, with the language the rebellion would use ("you can't fucking tell me what to do"). The casual register makes it safe for the person to acknowledge the rebellion. A formal naming ("there is a defiant aspect of your inner experience") would not get the rebellion to come out.

When Joe speaks to the rebellious part, he speaks as an ally. The energy is "I'm with you. Fuck that." He does not try to integrate the rebellion into the submission. He gives the rebellion its own voice.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "How much rebellion is in there? How much of like, you can't fucking tell me what to do, is in there?"
- **Person:** "Until you said it, I couldn't have identified that, but it's there."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. If you were to pull that part out for a minute, what would it say?"
- **Person:** "I want what I want. Fuck you."
- **Joe:** "What would— oh yeah, right, that's what it would say. Fuck you. Yeah, yeah, yeah. What— so I'm gonna just speak to that part of you for a minute. What, what is it that you do want?"

*(Source: Do I Stop Drinking?)*

The "yeah, yeah, yeah" is doing real work. It validates the "fuck you" rather than pathologizing it. Joe joins the rebellion before redirecting it toward the want. The validation is what lets the rebellious part speak the deeper want.

## What This Read Is Not

This is not a read on a person who is openly rebellious. The openly rebellious person is in contact with the rebellion already; the work there is different (often around what the rebellion is also covering, usually grief or fear). This is a read on a person whose rebellion is hidden underneath surface compliance.

This is also not a read on every persistent habit. Some habits persist for purely neurological or environmental reasons. The diagnostic for this specific read is the structural one: a long history of should-stopping that has not produced stopping, with calm-compliant framing in the room.
