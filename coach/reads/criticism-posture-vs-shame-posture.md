# Criticism Posture vs. Shame Posture

## What Joe Notices

Two distinct body signatures, both indicating different forms of emotional repression. Esme articulates the distinction, attributing the framing to Joe:

- **Criticism posture: the shoulders.** When someone has been getting criticized a lot, the body holds something in the shoulders. The shoulders tell the story.
- **Shame posture: the tucked butt.** When someone is carrying shame, the body holds something in the hips — a tucked-under quality, what they call "the shame butt."

"The shame, like the sh— there's the body language that says shame and then there's the body language that says, I get criticized, you know, and one, right, the criticism comes from the shoulders and the shame comes kind of from the butt where like the hips are kind of in." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

"Tucked butt." -- Joe, confirming.

"Yeah, exactly. A shame butt. Shame butt." -- Esme.

These are observational reads. The body holds the affective history in specific locations, and a trained eye can read which kind of held-ness is present.

## What This Implies

The reads are useful because criticism and shame produce different internal experiences and require different responses.

**Criticism (shoulders):** Held in the shoulders, this is the body's response to having received critical input from outside repeatedly — typically a parent, sometimes a teacher, coach, or peer. The shoulders carry the bracing against the criticism. The person carrying this is often hyper-vigilant about their own performance, expecting evaluation, ready to defend or correct.

**Shame (tucked butt):** Held in the hips/butt area, this is the body's response to carrying the felt sense of being unworthy, wrong, or bad. It often produces an internal critical voice that runs constantly. The person carrying shame may not be currently being criticized by anyone; the criticism has been installed and now runs from inside.

The distinction matters because the underlying dynamic is different. Criticism is the body responding to ongoing external pressure. Shame is the body responding to internalized self-pressure that may have started externally but is now self-sustaining.

## The Substance-Use Pattern Esme Observes

Esme connects these two body signatures to the substances kids in her school gravitate toward, observing a pattern:

**The vape kids tend to show the criticism posture.** "I noticed that a lot of the kids who vape a lot have the criticism — the posture of someone who gets criticized a lot."

**The weed kids tend to show more shame than criticism.** "There's more shame than there is criticism with, um, that I've observed with, um, with weed for some reason. And I don't exactly know why that is, but I think my guess would be that shame kind of leads to an internal critic, uh, critical voice. And smoking weed probably puts that voice on pause temporarily."

The observation is that different substances are addressing different held states. Weed quiets the internal critical voice — which is exactly what someone carrying shame is suffering from. Vaping is doing something different — possibly related to the activation/discharge needs of someone carrying constant external pressure.

Joe extends the observation about weed specifically, noting that for him in his 20s, smoking weed correlated with repressed anger: all the anger was being turned inward as shame, and the weed quieted the resulting internal voice. When he started moving the anger directly, his desire for weed went away.

This is the parenting application: a teen smoking a lot of weed is often a teen carrying shame and/or repressed anger. Treating the surface (the substance) without addressing the held state (the shame, the anger, the internal critical voice) does not resolve the issue.

## What Joe Does With These Reads

The reads inform what the coaching addresses:

- **For a kid (or adult) carrying the criticism posture:** the work often involves looking at the source of the criticism. Who is criticizing them? What is the relationship to that source? Can they hold their own ground against the criticism without bracing against it? The shoulders soften when the criticism either stops being received as personal or when the person learns to draw boundaries around it.
- **For a kid (or adult) carrying shame:** the work involves the internal critical voice and the felt sense of unworthiness underneath it. See [[shame-as-disconnection]] and [[shame-stagnates-emotion]]. The body opens up when the shame stops being held and starts being welcomed.

The reads also inform what the parent (or coach) should not do:
- Adding more criticism to someone in the criticism posture intensifies the held-ness rather than producing change.
- Adding more shame to someone in the shame posture — even subtly, through disappointed looks or sighs — also intensifies the held-ness.

This is why the principles around not-shaming-kids and not-installing-the-not-okay-signal matter so much developmentally. See [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]] and [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]]. The body holds what gets transmitted, and what is held shapes the affective baseline of the person's adult life.

## Where These Reads Connect

The reads complement [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]] — chin up and jaw clamped as somatic signatures of emotional repression. Together, these three body reads start to map how the body holds different forms of repressed material:

- **Chin up / jaw clamped:** stopping the emotion from rising
- **Shoulders held:** bracing against external criticism
- **Tucked butt / hips in:** holding installed shame

A skilled body read combines these. Someone with chin up, shoulders held, and tucked butt is carrying all three: actively repressing emotion, bracing against expected criticism, and holding installed shame. The specific combination tells the coach what the person's nervous system is currently doing.

## Tone

The reads are offered conversationally and with humor (the "shame butt" naming makes both Joe and Esme laugh). Hold the humor in the application — these are observational tools, not solemn diagnoses. The somatic teaching can be light and still land.
