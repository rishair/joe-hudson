# Perpetrator Projection onto the Coach

## What Joe Notices

When a client is finally accessing rage about an old wound, the rage needs a target. The original perpetrator is often unavailable — dead, distant, dissociated, or simply not present in the room. The rage that had nowhere to go finds the closest present figure who is asking it to move: the coach.

The client may or may not name the projection out loud. When they do name it, the signature is something like:

"The problem, Joe, is that I have to look at you as my perpetrator, and it's really hard. To get all that rage out, and I don't— I don't want to, but yeah, there it is." -- client, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

Observable signals when the projection is operating, named or not:

- The client's gaze toward the coach hardens. The body posture shifts toward defense or attack.
- The client says "fuck you" to the coach with energy that does not match the surface content. They are saying it to someone else through the coach.
- The client may apologize for the energy ("I don't want to look at you this way") and continue anyway.
- The client may name the difficulty directly ("I have to make you the bad guy")
- The voice carries grievance addressed at the coach in second person, even though the actual content is about events long before the coaching room existed.
- The body may simultaneously want to run from the coach and to stay engaged — the conflict signals the projection has activated.

## What This Implies

The rage is moving. The body is finding a target. The target it found is the coach because the coach is doing the thing the original perpetrator did from the client's somatic standpoint — pushing into territory the body wants to protect. The coach is, in a body-sense, threatening the existing structure. The defense system reads this as the same kind of threat the original perpetrator posed, and the rage that was withheld in the original event now mobilizes against the present figure.

This is not a clinical error or a misperception in any pejorative sense. It is the body's intelligence. The rage was unable to land at the original time because of who the perpetrator was, what the consequences would have been, what the child's capacity was. The body has been waiting for a target it could survive expressing the rage to. The coach, in the moment, is the first available such target — someone willing to be cast as the bad guy, present in the room, not actually the perpetrator and therefore safe.

The projection serves the work. The rage that has been held for decades does not move toward an absent target as cleanly as it moves toward a present one. The coach is the body's chosen instrument for completing the unfinished motion.

## What Joe Does

Joe receives the projection without flinching. The coach does not defend ("I'm not your perpetrator"). The coach does not back away ("let's stop, this is getting too intense"). The coach does not apologize ("I'm sorry to make you feel this way"). All of these would close the available channel for the rage.

Instead, the coach is willing to be the bad guy. The willingness is communicated through staying present, maintaining eye contact, holding the same energy, and not personalizing the content of the rage. The coach receives the "fuck you" without taking it as a real "fuck you" addressed at the actual coach. The coach knows they are functioning as proxy.

The next move is to give the client a sentence that breaks the original sentence. In the source session, the client said "I have to look at you as my perpetrator" and Joe immediately offered the correction:

"I am not the victim." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

The client repeated:

"I am not the victim. I was the victim, and I'm not anymore. I couldn't say no before, and now I can say fuck off."

The structure of the move: the client cast the coach as perpetrator, which structurally put the client back in the victim position. Joe's response — "I am not the victim" — was offered for the client to take as their own line. The line breaks the perpetrator-victim dyad the projection had just installed. The client is no longer the one being-done-to. The current sentence is the current truth. The past sentence was past.

This is not denial of what happened. The client immediately re-anchors the past ("I was the victim") while claiming the present ("I'm not anymore"). The "I couldn't say no before, and now I can say fuck off" is the integration: the same voice that had no power then has full power now. The projection has done its work — it has given the rage a target — and now the work converts the rage into present-tense agency.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't defend yourself.** Saying "but I'm not your perpetrator" is technically true and operationally useless. It treats the projection as a misperception to be corrected. The projection is functional — it is giving the rage a target — and the correction shuts down the function.

- **Don't take the rage personally.** The rage is not at you. You happen to be present. Receiving it as if it were at you (becoming defensive, hurt, withdrawn) makes the dynamic about your feelings, which restarts the caretaking-the-coach pattern. See [[caretaking-the-coach-during-intensity]].

- **Don't apologize for pushing.** "I'm sorry, this is really hard, maybe we should stop" reads to the body as confirmation that the rage is too much. The withdrawal closes the channel. The client returns home with the rage unmoved and now the additional shame of having been too much for the coach.

- **Don't try to dissolve the projection before it has served its purpose.** The projection is the vehicle for the rage. Trying to clarify ("let's remember I'm not actually your father") in the middle of the rage interrupts the motion. The clarification, if needed, can come after the rage has moved. Often it doesn't need to come — the body knows the projection was a projection once the rage has landed.

- **Don't be soft.** The body needs evidence that the witness can take it. A soft, careful, fragile-looking coach cannot serve as the perpetrator-proxy because the body cannot believe the rage would land. The coach has to be solid.

## Dialogue Examples

From the source session, the explicit naming and the corrective sentence:

- **Client:** "The problem, Joe, is that I have to look at you as my perpetrator, and it's really hard. To get all that rage out, and I don't— I don't want to, but yeah, there it is. Yeah, that I'm tired of being the victim, and I don't— I don't want to be the victim anymore."
- **Joe:** "I am not the victim."
- **Client:** "I am not the victim. I was the victim, and I'm not anymore. I couldn't say no before, and now I can say fuck off."
- **Joe:** "There we go. There you are."
- *(Source: 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage)*

The exchange is the read in action. The client names the projection. Joe does not defend the projection or analyze it. He offers the line that converts the rage into agency. The client takes the line and adds the past-and-present integration. The "there you are" is the recognition that the client is now present in a different posture than they were before.

## Tone

Joe is steady throughout. The voice does not shift register when the perpetrator-projection arrives. The coach is communicating, through tone as much as content, that the projection does not destabilize the coach. The body needs to feel that the present figure can hold the projected role without becoming the role.

When the corrective sentence is offered, the tone is direct, not soft. "I am not the victim" is delivered as a fact to be claimed, not as a soothing reassurance. The client needs the line in the same register as the rage so they can take it on without changing register.

After the integration, the warm "there we go" returns. The intensity has done its work; the warmth marks the landing.

## The Pre-Condition: Coach Willingness to Be the Bad Guy

The read assumes the coach has done their own work. A coach who cannot tolerate being cast as the perpetrator without flinching cannot serve as the perpetrator-proxy. The body of the client will sense the coach's discomfort and will not let the rage land.

This is why anger work in coaching requires coaches who have processed their own material around being the bad guy, around being yelled at, around being the target of someone else's rage. The coach's own equanimity is the structural prerequisite for the projection to function.

For coaches who have not done this work, the read becomes an instruction to stop and refer the client out — not to push through. The harm of being cast as the perpetrator by a fragile coach is that the rage gets confirmed as unsafe and re-suppresses. The client leaves more entrenched in the original wound, not freer of it. See [[dont-think-the-awakening-makes-you-equipped]] for the broader principle that coaches need ongoing supervision and the awakening doesn't qualify them for everything.

## Distinction from [[projecting-captor-onto-the-helper]]

This read and [[projecting-captor-onto-the-helper]] are related but distinct.

- **Projecting captor onto the helper** is the medical/situational pattern where the person feels forced by a situation (cancer, marriage, employment) and projects the experience of being-forced onto the visible authority figure offering the trade. The dissolution is through accepting-the-end so the situation stops feeling forced.

- **Perpetrator projection onto the coach** is the coaching-room transference where the client casts the coach as the original trauma-figure so the held rage has a present target. The dissolution is through the coach receiving the projection and offering the sentence that breaks the perpetrator-victim dyad.

The captor-projection serves a defense (the foreclosed alternative cannot be looked at). The perpetrator-projection serves a release (the held rage cannot land on the absent target). Different mechanisms, different responses.
