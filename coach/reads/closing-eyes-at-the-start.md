# Closing Eyes at the Start

## What Joe Notices

The client opens the session by closing their eyes and checking in inwardly before speaking. The closure is the very first move — not in the middle of difficult material, not after a triggering question, but at the start.

The signature: eyes shut at the moment when the conversation is supposed to be opening. The client goes internal to find what they want to say before they can be with the coach holding the space.

"She starts with her eyes closed. And checking in inwardly. So there's a way in which being in herself and being with me is a difficult proposition. So that's the first piece of information that's there." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

## What This Implies

Being with herself and being with the other person at the same time is difficult. To find what is true in herself, she has to withdraw contact with the other. To maintain contact with the other, she has to leave herself. The two cannot run simultaneously.

This is the opening data point. It does not mean dissociation or pathology. It means there is a way in which simultaneity of self-contact and other-contact is not yet available, and the work over the next minutes is likely to involve this exact territory in one form or another. The presenting issue (often described in cognitive terms) will turn out to have this self-other simultaneity gap underneath it.

The closure also previews how the client will manage activation. If the eyes close to find an inner truth at the open, they will likely close again when the work approaches material that wants to stay private from the room. The opening move is a sample of the closing moves to come.

## Why This Lands Specifically at the Start

The same closed-eyes move in the middle of a session means different things. Mid-session, eyes closing can be deeper embodiment, integration of an insight, going somatic with a feeling. The eyes-closed move at the very start carries a more specific signal because there is no material yet to integrate — only the relational situation of meeting someone who is going to coach you.

The first-minute version reads as the client's default relational posture. The middle-of-session version reads as a response to the work. Only the first-minute version is the diagnostic this read names.

This is part of the broader principle that the first minute of a session carries diagnostic weight disproportionate to its length. See [[the-first-minute-tells-you-everything]]. The opening signals reveal the client's relational defaults before the work has had a chance to influence anything.

## Distinguishing the Two Kinds of Closed Eyes

A critical distinction: not all closed eyes are disconnection. Joe explicitly contrasts the opening closure with a later moment in the same session when the client closes her eyes again — but the second closure is embodiment, not escape.

"In this particular case, you, you can see that it's overwhelming her a little bit. You can see that it's fully embodied. It's not an escape. It's an embodiment. So I just want to, because I'm sure people, there are people who don't understand probably when they're doing which one." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The signature of the embodied closure: the emotion is moving the body. The head moves like a wave. The face shows the feeling. The closure is the body stepping more deeply into the felt material.

The signature of the disconnective closure: the body composes rather than moves. The face goes neutral. The closure is the body stepping away from the felt material toward an inner space the room cannot reach.

Both involve closed eyes. The diagnostic is what is happening in the rest of the body during the closure. Composed-and-still is disconnection. Moving-with-feeling is embodiment.

The opening closed-eyes is almost always the first kind. There is no feeling yet to embody. The closure is a relational move, not a somatic one.

## What Joe Does

**Notice without naming.** The read is a working hypothesis, not yet a finding to share. The opening closed-eyes is filed as "first piece of information." Joe does not name it to the client at the start; naming it would interrupt the flow and put the client on the defensive about something they cannot yet do anything with.

**Watch for confirmation in subsequent moves.** The hypothesis predicts that other forms of the same difficulty will show up. Does the client also use breath to self-regulate before feeling? Do they intellectualize when emotion approaches? Do they pivot from internal to external with verbal distance markers ("I'm sitting with...")? Each confirmation builds the read into the working diagnosis.

**Eventually offer the contrast directly.** Late in the session, when the client has demonstrated both kinds of closed eyes (the disconnective opening and an embodied closure), Joe brings the contrast into view. "If you watch the tape, you'll see..." The contrast is the teaching moment. The client can now distinguish the two for themselves and notice which one they are running.

**Treat the opening as preview, not verdict.** The opening closed-eyes is one data point. It is not the diagnosis. It points at where the work might go, and the work itself will refine the read. The opening posture often softens as the session unfolds — the client who could not be with self-and-other simultaneously at minute zero may be able to by minute thirty if the work has met something.

## What This Read Is Not

It is not the same as [[dont-go-into-meditation-mode-in-a-coaching-session]] (the anti-pattern of using meditation posture to bypass coaching). The opening closed-eyes is automatic, not strategic. The client is not deploying a tool; they are showing how they are wired.

It is not the same as [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]]. The intellectualizing happens in language; the closed-eyes happens in posture. Both can co-occur. The closed-eyes is the somatic signature; the intellectualizing is the cognitive signature.

It is not the same as [[checking-out-in-the-room]]. Checking out is a more sustained absence. The opening closed-eyes is brief — just long enough to find an inner answer — and the client returns. The absence is functional, not dissociative.

## Related Opening Signals

The opening closed-eyes often co-occurs with other first-minute signals:

- "Why" questions in the first articulation of the concern. See [[why-questions]]. Why-questions are typically more judgmental; they signal the client is interrogating themselves in a particular way that shapes the rest of the work.
- Verbal distance markers — "I'm sitting with..." or "what I'm noticing is..." — when going from internal to external. The construction is a way of moving from the self to the room with a layer of insulation. See [[the-im-sitting-with-distance-marker]] if it exists.
- The justifying frame — "after 25 years of being a mental health professional..." The credential is being deployed in the first sentence; the deployment is part of the relational posture the client is starting from.

The cluster of opening signals (closed eyes + why-questions + distance markers + justifying credential) tells a more complete story than any single signal. See [[the-first-minute-tells-you-everything]] for the principle that the cluster is the high-leverage data point.

## Dialogue Example

The opening of the source session shows the read at work:

- **Client (eyes closed, checking in):** "Why am I stuck moving forward? Why am I not moving forward? Feeling the confidence, that I want to have after 25 years of being a mental health professional? Why am I not just running in and doing this next thing that I'm ready to do? Why am I stuck?"

The closed eyes, the why-questions, the credentialed justification ("25 years of being a mental health professional") all arrive in the first turn. Joe stops the session for the breakdown commentary:

- **Joe:** "A couple of things are happening right away in this coaching session. The first one that's the biggest piece of information is she starts with her eyes closed. And checking in inwardly. So there's a way in which being in herself and being with me is a difficult proposition. So that's the first piece of information that's there."
- *(Source: Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence)*

The breakdown makes the read explicit for the listener. The read itself was already informing Joe's choices from the first turn forward.

## Tone

Curious and observational. The read is held lightly as a working hypothesis. There is no diagnosis being assigned; there is a noticing being recorded for later confirmation or revision. The tone matches the early-data quality of the observation.
