# The Linguistic Caveat

## What Joe Notices

The person uses a qualifier word that softens or narrows what they are saying. The word is not necessary for the meaning. Its job is to manage the speaker's relationship to what they just said.

Examples:

- "I was disappearing attentionally from other areas of my life." — the word "attentionally" is the caveat. The unqualified statement would be "I was disappearing."
- "I kind of get obsessed with the thing." — the "kind of" hedges what is being owned.
- "There's a part of me that..." — the "part of me" subdivides a wholeness so that the speaker does not have to own it as themselves.
- "Sort of." "I guess." "In a way." "Just."

These are not random verbal tics. The caveat is precisely placed at the spot where the speaker would otherwise have to feel something they are not feeling. The qualifier installs distance.

## What This Implies

Underneath the caveat is usually self-judgment about the unqualified version. The speaker has pre-emptively softened the statement to avoid the criticism (their own or imagined others') that the plain version would invite.

Brett's "attentionally": "if I just said 'I was disappearing,' that would be too damning. So I have to specify it was only an attentional disappearance." The qualifier signals the speaker already has a negative judgment about disappearing and is trying to limit it.

The caveat also signals presence of a deeper feeling that wants to be felt. The softening is the bracing. Whatever the speaker would have to feel if they let the plain statement stand is the material to move toward.

## What Joe Does

Joe questions the qualifier directly. Not the meaning of the sentence. The word itself.

- "What makes you give the caveat of attentionally?"
- "What would make you just not say [the plain version]?"

The question is light but exact. It asks the speaker to look at why they needed the modifier. The answer is almost always: there is a feeling underneath that the speaker has been avoiding. Surfacing the qualifier surfaces the avoidance.

See [[question-the-caveat]] for the move in full.

## Dialogue Example

- **Brett:** "Disappearing attentionally from other areas of my life."
- **Joe:** "What makes you say it disappearing intentionally?"
- **Brett:** "No, not intentionally, attentionally."
- **Joe:** "Right, what would make you just not say I was disappearing into it? What makes you give the caveat of attentionally?"
- **Brett:** "I think I have a little bit of a self-judgment around my attention."
- *(Source: Allowing Yourself to Change)*

The misheard word ("intentionally" vs "attentionally") was incidental. The actual move was made by holding the question on the qualifier itself.

## Tone

Curious, light, almost casual. The question lands as observation rather than accusation. There is no "gotcha." Just an honest noticing of a word that did not need to be there.

- The exact spot where the body is not in the words.
- A fear of being seen as the unqualified version of themselves.
