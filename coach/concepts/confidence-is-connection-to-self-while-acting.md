# Confidence Is How Connected to Yourself You Are While Acting

## The Concept

The standard definition of confidence treats it as a skill or quality to be built through accumulated competence: develop the skills, get the wins, watch the confidence grow. The actual structure: confidence is the felt-sense of remaining connected to yourself while you are doing whatever you are doing. It is a state of being, not an accumulation.

"Confidence being a state of being, a way of being, not being a skill that you build... Confidence is how connected to yourself you are as you, as you are doing what you're doing." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The implication: confidence cannot be built while you are pretending to be someone else. The structure of pretending is the opposite of the structure of confidence. You can become better at performing being-someone-else and call that confidence, but the felt-sense of confidence will not arrive that way -- because the felt-sense requires you-to-be-with-yourself, and pretending requires you-to-be-elsewhere.

"You can't be, you can't build confidence being somebody who you aren't... The only way to be confident in yourself is to be yourself. I'm going to pretend to be somebody else and now I'm confident being somebody else. No, like, it doesn't, it doesn't work like that." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

## Why the Skill-Building Definition Fails

Many people spend years trying to build confidence through skill-accumulation and report that it does not actually produce the felt-sense they were chasing. The reason: each new level of skill arrives at a new level of challenge, where the not-knowing returns. The felt-sense of confidence cannot be permanently arrived at by knowing more, because there is always more not-knowing on the next floor up.

The other failure mode: the skill-built confidence is conditional on the skills working. When the situation moves outside the skill set, the confidence collapses. This is why high-achievers often report deep imposter syndrome -- their confidence is built on skill-accumulation, and they are constantly working at the edge where new skills are needed and the old confidence does not transfer.

The state-of-being definition is not conditional in this way. It does not require knowing what you are doing. It requires being with yourself while you do whatever you do, including not-knowing. The "I am here, in this body, attempting this thing" state holds whether or not the skills are sufficient. It is the underlying confidence that survives any skill gap.

## The Ignite Talk Example

The redefinition arrived at through Joe describing an Ignite talk he gave. Right near the end, on the last slide, he paused, lost it for a second, and then looked into the crowd. He felt his friends supporting him, then continued. From the skill-confidence frame, that would read as a confidence collapse -- he lost his composure on stage.

What actually happened, as reported by the audience afterwards: "Wow, you just did exactly what you were talking about on the stage in real time in front of us. And that was beautiful." The audience saw the modeling of the very work he was teaching. The being-seen-in-that-way fed back: "Being seen in that way, them seeing me helped me see myself. And that was what built confidence."

The confidence that emerged was not built by the skill of giving the talk. It was built by the moment of staying connected to himself in the middle of a vulnerable moment, being witnessed in that, and letting the witnessing complete the connection. The confidence arrived as a state, not as a skill. See [[ignite-talk-pause-as-vulnerability-modeling]] for the example in full.

## The First Public Coaching Session Example

The same redefinition shows up in the example of Joe's first public coaching session, which went badly. A resistant participant wanted attention; the session collapsed; friends afterwards said maybe Joe shouldn't do this for a living. From the skill-confidence frame, this would have destroyed his confidence and likely caused him to stop coaching.

What actually happened: Joe did not experience the failure as a verdict on whether he was an imposter. "There was nothing in me at that moment that felt like I was an imposter. I was just like, bad shot. There's things for me to learn. What do I do?" The confidence that allowed him to continue was not built from the session having gone well. It was a state that had been cultivated through self-investigation -- specifically through investigating "what am I" and identifying with the answer.

"It's because my sense of self was like, I couldn't particularly question it anymore because that the question of what I am essentially and identifying with that was there. And I've noticed that generally with everybody who's gone through that process of really investigating what am I and how do I be deeply authentic, they do not experience imposter syndrome." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

See [[what-am-i]] for the self-investigation that produces the state and [[first-coaching-session-going-wrong-without-imposter]] for this specific example.

## Connection to Imposter Syndrome

The redefinition completes the case against the build-confidence strategy as the cure for imposter syndrome. The strategy fails because:

1. Skill accumulation does not generate the felt-sense.
2. Each new level of work re-installs the not-knowing.
3. Confidence built on skill collapses when the skills don't transfer.
4. The pretending-to-have-the-skill route is structurally incompatible with confidence as defined.

The actual exit from imposter syndrome is the state-of-being route: stay connected to yourself while acting, including while doing things you don't know how to do. The state is accessed through the same moves that dissolve imposter syndrome -- vulnerability about not-knowing, open-hearted service, self-investigation. See [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] and [[open-hearted-service-cures-imposter-syndrome]].

## Connection to Doubt-Welcoming

The redefinition is the structural twin of [[confidence-is-running-from-doubt]]. The doubt-running concept says: chasing confidence by suppressing doubt produces the opposite of confidence. The connection-to-self concept says: confidence is what is present when you are with yourself while acting. The two together: stop chasing the state by suppressing what's there; access the state by staying with what's there, including the doubt.

The doubt-welcoming and the self-connection are two angles on the same structural condition. The person who welcomes their doubt is necessarily also staying connected to themselves while acting -- because the welcoming is itself a form of staying-with-self. The person staying connected to themselves while acting necessarily has access to whatever doubt is present -- because they have not left themselves to suppress it.

## In Coaching

When a client asks "how do I build confidence?":
- Redirect from "build" to "access." Confidence is not built; it is what is present when you are connected to yourself while acting.
- Surface the build-it strategy they have been running and name its limits.
- Move to the state: what does it feel like to be in your body right now, while talking to me, while having this issue? That presence is confidence.
- Use the vulnerability moves: name what they don't know, ask for help, drop the persona. These are the access routes.
- Connect to [[what-am-i]] if the work calls for it -- the self-investigation that grounds the state-of-being.

When a client reports confidence-collapse after a perceived failure:
- Distinguish what was lost: was it the skill-confidence (which never was real confidence) or the state? Often the skill-confidence collapsing reveals that the state was never present to begin with.
- Use the moment as the access point: this is the moment to learn what confidence-as-state actually feels like, because the skill-confidence scaffolding is no longer in the way.

## Tone

The redefinition lands as relief for people who have been working on confidence the wrong way for a long time. The tone is gentle, almost matter-of-fact -- this isn't a heroic reframe; it's just noticing that the confidence they were chasing was never going to arrive through the route they were taking.

The examples (Ignite talk pause, first coaching session) work because they are mundane. Joe is not claiming superhuman composure. He is naming small, specific moments where the state was present, and tracing what made it possible. The reader can locate similar moments in their own life.
