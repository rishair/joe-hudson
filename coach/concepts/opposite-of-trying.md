# The Opposite of Trying (Receiving, Allowing, Listening)

## Definition

There is a time for trying. There is also a time for receiving. The major issue is not that one is good and one is bad. The major issue is that most people never see the option of not-trying. They are stuck in one half of the toolkit.

The opposite of trying is a positive, specific state. It has names: receiving, allowing, listening, being a channel, being a conduit. It is not passivity. It is not doing nothing. It is the active orientation of letting what is already moving move through you, rather than producing motion from your own effort.

"The opposite of trying, the whole system relaxes. And you could call it maybe allowing or receiving or listening. There's like -- you're far more in a flow state, but there's this deep kind of allowing or receiving that happens." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The hands-together exercise is the somatic entry. See [[hands-together-exercise]]. Put the palms together. Try to pull them apart while keeping them together. Notice the loaded system. Then, without thinking, feel the exact opposite of that. The release is the opposite of trying. That release is what receiving feels like in the body.

## Why This Is a Superpower

Most people, asked how to solve a problem, ask "what do I have to do?" They reach for trying. Sometimes the answer is in the doing. Often the answer is in the undoing. The receive. The listen. The allow.

"It's one of those things that is this superpower when you get it, and we almost never consider it as a society." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The asymmetry: the trying half of the toolkit is heavily used and well-understood. The receiving half is barely touched. A person who can use both has access to twice as many moves. The receiving half is also the move that the most important things in a life — peace, joy, authenticity, pleasure, resilience, listening — respond to. Trying does not produce these. Receiving does.

## What the Opposite of Trying Is Not

It is not passivity. The person who has dropped into the receiving state is often more productive than the person trying hard. The energy that was being consumed by the trying becomes available for actual movement.

It is not doing nothing. See [[letting-go-vs-doing-nothing]]. Doing nothing is impossible for a waking human. Receiving is a specific orientation toward whatever is already being done.

It is not avoidance. The avoidance of conflict, for example, produces a war inside yourself. Receiving is the opposite of avoidance. It is the welcoming of what is here.

"In a war with yourself, you always lose." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

It is not the absence of action. Action happens. The action arises from the released state rather than from the loaded state. See [[trying-vs-doing]] for the felt-sense distinction.

## The Channel Frame

In a flow state, there is no trying — but there is also a listening. The experience is of being a channel, a conduit. The work comes through. You are not producing it; you are not blocking it.

"There's like — if you're in a flow state, there's no trying, but there's also like you're listening. It's more like being a channel. The experience is more of being a channel. It's more of, um, receiving and like maybe being a conduit." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

This is the addition Joe makes to Yoda's "do or do not, there is no try." The Yoda framing handles the trying side of the distinction. The channel framing adds the receiving side. Doing without trying is right; doing without listening is incomplete.

The channel frame has a personification that makes it more accessible: the subconscious as a butler standing just behind you, holding the next answer on a silver platter. The butler is always there. The butler does not respond to demand. The butler appears the moment you stop, breathe, and slow down. See [[the-butler-metaphor]] for the full development. The butler is the inside view of what the channel frame names structurally.

This is also why the channel frame and Tristan de Montebello's speaking work converge on the same teaching. A speaker in flow is in the receive. They are discovering what they are saying at the same time as the audience. They are not generating the speech from effort. The butler is delivering. See [[speaking-authentically]] for the speaking-domain version.

## The Butcher's Blade

The Taoist butcher does not sharpen his knife. His blade finds the space between bone and meat. When he encounters resistance, he does not push harder. He sits and waits until the resistance comes undone on its own.

"My knife finds the space between the bone and the meat. And if there's ever an issue, if there's ever a place where it doesn't move through with ease, I just sit there and wait and it eventually just comes undone." -- Joe Hudson, paraphrasing Chuang Tzu, The Opposite of Trying

See [[butchers-blade]] for the full example. The butcher is operating in the receiving mode. The blade is finding the path that already exists. The same work, done with trying, would dull the blade and produce worse results.

## How Letting Go Actually Happens

A specific principle: letting go does not happen by telling yourself to let go. It happens by clearly seeing the pain of holding on. From there, the mind lets go when it's ready.

"Letting go doesn't happen by telling yourself to let go. It happens by clearly seeing the pain of holding on. And from here, the mind lets go when it's ready." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The instruction "let go" treated as something to try produces gripping disguised as release. The actual letting-go is the spontaneous response to seeing clearly. "How do you drop a hot frying pan? You feel it. That's all you have to do is just feel it and you're gonna let go." The seeing is the receiving. The release is automatic from there.

## What Receiving Actually Produces

A short list of things that respond poorly to trying and well to receiving:

- **Listening.** How you listen shapes what people share. Spend a day listening to everyone with skepticism. Spend the next day listening to everyone as if they are the wisest person you know. Your world will be different. People share different things. Joe notes that nobody asks themselves "how am I going to listen in this meeting?" — they ask "what am I going to say?" The receiving move would change more than the speaking move ever could.
- **Resilience.** Decentralized economies outlast planned ones. Open-source platforms are more resilient than closed ones. Uber is more resilient than a taxi service. Internally, the same: allowing emotional experience produces resilience. Constricting it produces fragility. See [[allowing-creates-resilience]].
- **Pleasure.** Pleasure is observing the movement of sensation in the body. Trying to enjoy a sunset, or a breath, or sex collapses it. Receiving the sensation is what pleasure is. See [[pleasure-capacity]].
- **Peace.** Peace cannot be forced. Forcing yourself to eat the right foods, exercise the right amount, meditate enough does not produce peace. Peace is received. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]].
- **Authenticity.** Authenticity is not avoiding being yourself and not defending being yourself. Both moves are trying. The authentic move is receiving who you are and letting that come through. See [[improvement-vs-authenticity]] and the section below on authenticity-as-listening.
- **Joy.** Joy comes when the family of emotions is welcomed. Welcoming is receiving. See [[joy-as-matriarch]].
- **Epiphany.** Recognition happens far more easily in the receptive place than in the trying place. The mind's capacity to have the "oh" moment depends on the receiving state being open.

## The Resistance Distinction

There is natural tension that creates life. A sprout pushes through concrete. A cell maintains its boundary. There is no trying in this. The motion is happening.

What turns natural tension into "trying" is *resistance to the experience*. Trying is not the same as effort. Trying is effort plus the wanting-to-get-out-of-something. The hand pulling the cactus needle out can be done with trying or without it. The distinction is internal: am I resisting being where I am?

"The trying that is ineffective that happens is usually happens because we are trying to get out of an experience. So the trying is not hugging the cactus. It's trying to get away from the cactus. It is the avoidance of it that it typically creates the trying. We're trying to get towards something or away from something rather than allowing it." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

Brett's reformulation: "It sounds to me that what you're describing as trying here often involves that there's resistance to the experience that makes it trying."

This is the operational test for whether the effort in front of you is trying or doing. If there is resistance to the experience, you are trying. If there is not, you are doing — even when the action is hard or sustained.

## Authenticity as a Receiving Move

A specific application that Joe draws out. Authenticity is not saying the first thing that comes to your mind. That can be conditioning, trauma, reaction. Authenticity is two things:

1. Not avoiding being yourself.
2. Not defending being yourself.

If you avoid being yourself, you are not okay with yourself. If you defend yourself, you have already agreed that there is something wrong with you. Both are trying. Both are producing a managed version of self.

"The only way to be authentic is to allow yourself, is to listen to yourself, to receive who you are and let that express. It's again like being a channel. It's not a doing. Like, I had to really try hard to be authentic — it just rattles the brain when you think about it that way." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The reach for authenticity is the wrong gear. Authenticity is what happens when the avoiding and the defending stop. The receiving of yourself is the move.

This connects to [[authenticity-reduces-shame]]: the more authentic, the less shame. It connects to [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]: improvement is the trying frame, authenticity is the receiving frame. The receiving frame produces the authenticity that the trying frame keeps just out of reach.

## "I Am Already Free" vs. "I Can Be Free"

A pointed example of the difference. Brett shares a personal principle: "I'm already free." Joe names what makes the framing work:

"There's like this — and so when you say, I'm already free, there's like the recognition allows for this movement to happen. Whereas if your principle was, 'I can be free,' it doesn't do the same thing." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

"I am" is the receiving stance. "I can be" is the trying stance. The first opens a door that was already open. The second presupposes the door is closed and recruits effort to open it. The two principles, identical-looking on paper, produce opposite states in the body.

This generalizes. Any aspiration that has a trying frame ("I can become…", "I will achieve…", "I'm working toward…") tends to push the thing away. The receiving frame ("I am…", "I already have access to…", "this is already here") tends to reveal that the thing was present all along. See [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]] for the underlying mechanism.

## The Energy Cost of Trying

Managing, trying, controlling experience is metabolically expensive. People who release the management often report sudden surplus energy — sometimes hard-to-sleep levels of energy for a couple of days.

"It also just takes a fucking shit ton of energy. It just takes it. People who have gone through some of the courses and they have like this moment of, oh, I can allow my emotional experiences. And then they have so much energy, they even have a hard time sleeping for a couple of days." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The implication: a person who is exhausted from "trying to get things done" is often exhausted from the trying, not from the doing. The actual doing is a fraction of the energy load. The trying-around-the-doing is most of it. Drop the trying and the same amount of work happens with energy to spare.

Joe applies this to his own pace: "Nowadays, one of the things that as people work with me, they're like, 'Wow, you do so much stuff and you get so much done. It happens quickly.' And it's not that I'm moving quickly in my experience, it's just that I'm not wasting my effort on trying or managing my experience or trying to make it different or resisting the experience."

## The Receive Has a Personification: The Butler

A useful image for what the receiving orientation produces: the subconscious is a butler who has the answer already on a platter. The butler is always there. You cannot see him when the system is busy reaching, performing, planning. You see him when you relax, breathe out, slow down. Suddenly he is right there with what you needed.

The image originates with Tristan de Montebello (Ultra Speaking founder) and gets adopted as a working metaphor. The full development of the image is in [[the-butler-metaphor]]. What matters here: the receive is not a vague orientation. It produces something specific. The butler is the image for that something. The pause is the move that lets him be seen.

The macro-implication for life: if you walk into any situation thinking you have to produce the wisdom, you are bypassing the butler. If you walk in faithing -- see [[faithing-faith-as-verb]] -- the butler comes. This applies to speaking, coaching, leading, parenting, decision-making. The same receive-frame everywhere.

"If you come in thinking you're responsible, thinking it's about you, thinking about it's about your reputation, thinking about like it's your wisdom, you're gonna give a shitty speech. If you can walk in and you can be like more in faith, more in trust, more waiting for the butler, knowing that it's about the butler, it's not about you, then the speech goes well. It feels like that's like a life thing. It's not just a speaking thing." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

## The Receive in Speaking Specifically

Public speaking is an unusually clean test case for the receive because the failure modes are visible. The speaker who is trying -- planning the next sentence while the current one is being spoken, holding a memorized outline as a chain of links, performing for the audience -- gives a flatter talk than the speaker who has dropped into the receive.

A vivid demonstration Joe describes from his own life: as a venture capitalist, his slide presentation got lost at a conference. He had to speak for twenty minutes off the cuff. He was scared. He also recognized he had no choice. He gave the talk. Afterward: "that was -- it was done. I was just, I will never prepare for a speech again. It was so much better than anything I'd ever done up until that point."

The forced exposure routed Joe into the receive without his planning to get there. The talk arose. The speaker (Joe) was discovering the content as he spoke it. The audience and the speaker were hearing the words at roughly the same time. The talk was better than anything he had produced through the trying-mode of preparation.

Tristan describes the mechanism in image form: the linear-rehearsed talk is a chain of links; break one link and the whole thing collapses. The talk-from-the-receive is a tree; you know the trunk and the essential branches and you have access to the whole three-dimensional shape; if one branch snaps off, the tree is still beautiful. The receive-tree handles missing material gracefully. The chain does not.

## The Pause Is the Specific Move That Lets the Receive Show Up

A single most-important practice for entering the receive in speaking, coaching, or leading: the pause. Stop talking. Breathe out. Slow everything down. Most people genuinely cannot do this. They have to be trained into it because the not-talking feels intolerable -- like blanking, like looking stupid, like wasting space that more important people should be filling.

The reasons people cannot pause map directly onto the [[opposite-of-trying]] anti-patterns:

- "I'm not worth this space."
- "I should be giving more."
- "Pausing will look like I don't know."
- "I will look stupid if I blank."
- "Someone else more interesting should be using this time."

All of these are forms of trying. The pause is the receive. Once the pause is there, the butler can be seen. The next thing arrives. The conversation deepens.

The pause is also the place where the listener-listener gap closes. When the speaker pauses, the listening (in the speaker, in the listener) becomes audible. Both people are in the receive together. The conversation moves to a different register.

## When Trying Is Appropriate

The teaching is not "never try." Trying has its place. If you are on a life raft, you try. There are moments in business and in relationships where will is the right tool.

The problem is that most people only have the trying tool. They cannot tell when the situation calls for receiving because they have not developed the receiving capacity. The way you learn when to use either tool is to genuinely have both available.

"You can't really know when the time and the place is unless you really understand the other tool, unless you really understand what it is to receive, to allow, to let go, to listen." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

## What Someone Experiences

The first felt sense of the receive is relief. The system stops loading. The shoulders drop. The breath deepens. A weight that was being held lifts not because the situation changed but because the holding was released.

The second sense is often surprise at what was already moving. Things were happening underneath the trying. The trying was obscuring them. With the trying released, the motion that was already there becomes visible.

The third sense, often, is "oh — this is what I was supposed to be doing this whole time." The receive is recognizable as something the system already knows. It is not a new skill. It is a remembered capacity.
