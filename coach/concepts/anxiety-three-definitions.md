# Anxiety Defined Through Three Brains

## Definition

Anxiety can be defined three different ways depending on which of the three intelligences -- head, heart, or gut -- is doing the looking. Each definition is true. Each points at a different feature of the same phenomenon. Together they give a more complete picture than any single definition can.

The three are not in competition. They are three angles on the same signal. Treating any one as the whole produces a partial response that misses what the other two are reporting.

See [[head-heart-gut]] for the broader framework of the three intelligences.

## The Head Definition: Constantly Preparing for Attack

From the perspective of the prefrontal cortex, anxiety is the mind's constant scanning for what could go wrong, and the constant modulation of behavior and environment to prevent the bad thing.

"For me, anxiety for the head is just a constantly preparing for attack. It's trying to figure out what's gonna go wrong and modulate your performance and your environment to prevent anything from going wrong, whatever that is." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The catch built into this definition: what the mind labels as "wrong" frequently turns out to be a gift. The vigilance is honest. The threat assessment is not.

This is the layer most people are aware of when they say they are anxious. It is the most legible to the intellect because it is the layer the intellect is running.

## The Heart Definition: Unmet Needs

From the mammalian emotional perspective, anxiety is what an animal feels when its needs are not being met. Hunger, cold, separation from the pack, threat to offspring -- the mammal becomes anxious when something is missing.

Humans have far more complex needs than other mammals because the human "self" has been extended beyond the body. See [[identity-extension-expands-fear]]. A human can have unmet needs around reputation, status, relationship, meaning, autonomy, recognition. Each unmet need produces its own anxiety signal.

"From the heart perspective, from the emotional mammalian perspective, I would say it's unmet needs, right? If you just think about a mammal, they're anxious when their needs aren't being met. Our needs, because we have a sense of self, are far more complicated probably than many mammals." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

This is the most actionable definition. If anxiety equals unmet need, the response is to find and meet the need. See [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]] for the practice that operationalizes this view.

## The Gut Definition: Constriction of Life Force

From the nervous system, anxiety is the constriction of life force. Energy is moving through the body. Anxiety is what that energy looks like when it has been clamped down on. Loosen the clamp, and what remains is just energy -- vitality, aliveness, life force.

"From a nervous system, it's just that constriction of life force, the anxiety. And I think that's when I was saying the more I paid attention to it, the more it's life force. And what's happening there for me in that experiment was that the more I paid attention to it, the less the constriction was necessary." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

See [[anxiety-as-constricted-life-force]] for the full treatment.

This is the deepest definition. It points to anxiety as not actually a separate phenomenon at all. It is life force with a contraction around it. The contraction can release.

## Why Three Definitions Matter

Most attempts to address anxiety pick one definition and try to fix that layer. Cognitive approaches target the head. Therapy that focuses on needs and relationships targets the heart. Somatic and breathwork practices target the gut.

Each approach helps to the extent that it addresses its layer. Each is incomplete to the extent that it ignores the other two.

A more complete response works all three layers:
- **Head:** Question the catastrophe scenarios. Notice what is being prepared against.
- **Heart:** Find the unmet need. Articulate it. Ask for it or grieve it.
- **Gut:** Bring attention to the constriction in the body. Let the life force underneath become visible.

When all three layers are addressed, the anxiety has nowhere left to hide. The signal has been received at every level it is being sent.

## The Immediate-Action Advantage of the Heart View

The heart definition (unmet needs) is highlighted as particularly important because it is the most immediately actionable. Asking "what need is this signaling?" gives the person something specific to do. The head definition (vigilance) tends to amplify itself if engaged head-on. The gut definition (life force constriction) requires somatic attention that takes practice.

"That generalized anxiety that they have is perpetuated because they're not getting their needs met and they're not seeing the signal of their anxiety as, 'oh, I have needs here that aren't being met that I can ask for.'" -- Joe Hudson, Welcoming Fear (reflecting the same teaching)

For someone new to the work, leading with the heart definition often produces the fastest relief. The other two definitions can be brought in later as the person develops the capacity to work with them.

## In Coaching

When a person presents anxiety, ask which layer they have access to first. Some people can feel the constriction immediately. Some can articulate the unmet need without needing to feel anything. Some are entirely in the head, listing every possible bad outcome.

Meet them where they have access. Then expand. The person stuck in the head needs gentle redirection to the body and to the need. The person stuck in the body needs help articulating what need the body is signaling. The person stuck on needs may need to feel the somatic layer to notice that even after the need is named, the body is still holding.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The three definitions are offered as tools, not as a hierarchy. Each is true. The choice of which to lead with is pragmatic.
