# Momentum and Margin Allow Dysfunction to Persist for Years

## Definition

The functionality of a team is not the only component of a company's success. Product, margin, talent, market timing, and momentum also matter. When the other components are strong, a company can sustain serious team dysfunction for years before the dysfunction shows up in results. This is what allows so many dysfunctional organizations to look successful from the outside and to stop interrogating their own dynamics.

A company in a low-margin, high-competition, shrinking market dies fast if it is dysfunctional. A company in blue ocean with high margins and a growing total addressable market can be dysfunctional and still grow. The level of dysfunction a company can tolerate is set by the strength of its non-team components, not by the importance of team functionality itself.

## In Practice

"A functional team is one component of making a successful company. You know, a great product is another component. How much — what your margins are is another component. Level of the talent in the room is another component. There's so many things that are components of a successful company. And then there's also momentum, meaning oftentimes when companies get dysfunctional, like a big company, a Fortune 500 company, it can take years for them to recognize it in business results. And even when you have those business results, they can last for years before any actions. Poor business results can be there for years before an activist shareholder comes in or before you get crushed by your competition." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The lag is the point. The signal that team dysfunction is hurting the company arrives in business results that are themselves lagging by years. By the time the dysfunction is visible in the numbers, it has been operating in the team for a long time, and the cost of unwinding it has compounded.

## The Conditions That Mask Dysfunction

- **High margin businesses** -- profitable enough that inefficiency does not threaten survival
- **Blue ocean markets** -- no competitor pressing on weakness
- **Growing total addressable market** -- demand grows faster than dysfunction can erode capability
- **Patent or trade-secret moats** -- competitors structurally prevented from exploiting weakness
- **Distribution monopolies** -- customers cannot leave
- **Strong product momentum** -- past quality keeps customers paying current bills

"If you have, you know, a way to keep your competitors at bay, whether that be with patents, which are less useful these days, or trade secrets, or distribution monopolies, whatever that is, then you can be more dysfunctional and still survive." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Conditions That Force Functionality

The complementary list:

- **Innovation-dependent businesses** -- innovation requires functionality; dysfunction kills it fast
- **Low-margin businesses** -- no slack to absorb inefficiency
- **High-competition markets** -- every weakness gets exploited
- **Shrinking total addressable market** -- decline accelerates dysfunction

"Some companies don't need to be creative to stay in business, and some companies really need to be creative to stay in business if your business is innovation. And dysfunction will kill innovation really, really quickly. It's why very few big businesses innovate effectively, and if they are, they're doing it through acquisition." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## Why This Matters for the Leader

The leader who relies on results-based feedback to know whether their team is functional is using a signal that lags by years. By the time the team's dysfunction is visible in numbers, the cost of repair is enormous and the window for graceful intervention has closed.

The leader who uses team-level diagnostics -- [[invigorated-vs-depleted-as-functionality-measure]], the patterns in [[signs-of-a-dysfunctional-team]] and [[signs-of-a-dysfunctional-leader]] -- has access to leading indicators that the results-based view does not provide.

The principle that does the work: team functionality is always available as a lever, even when nothing else is in trouble. "Teamwork is one of the best levers to pull because it affects everything else."

## The Soil Metaphor

"If you're really looking to optimize your company, teamwork is one of the best levers to pull because it affects everything else. It's like the soil in which everything grows in is people's relationships and their dynamics. And if you have bad soil, it's hard for anything to grow." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The soil metaphor inverts the typical priority. Team functionality is not one of many components on a balance sheet. It is the medium in which everything else operates. A company can grow on bad soil if the seeds and weather are exceptional, but it cannot grow indefinitely.

## Implications for Diagnostics

A company that is succeeding is not necessarily a functional company. A company that is failing is not necessarily a dysfunctional company. The relationship between team functionality and business results is heavily filtered through market conditions and momentum. The diagnostic for functionality has to be applied directly to the team, not inferred from results.

This is the same logic as Joe's earlier point about measuring [[functional-team]]: output-based measures of functionality fail when the field is uniformly dysfunctional or when tailwinds are strong. "In strong enough winds even turkeys can fly."

## When Someone Experiences This

- A CEO sees revenue growing and assumes the team must be working
- A team member can see the dysfunction but cannot get the leadership to take it seriously because the numbers are fine
- A consultant comes in after years of dysfunction has finally shown up in results and finds that intervention is now ten times harder than it would have been at the start
- A company that succeeded by riding a wave hits the end of the wave and discovers it does not have the team to navigate the new conditions
