# Functional Team

## Definition

A functional team is context-dependent in its specifics but uniform in its core: a team where the people who consistently belong to it want to be part of it. The Navy SEAL team, the cryptocurrency hedge fund, and the canned tomato processing house are all "functional" in different ways. The shared underlying quality across all of them is trust.

"What is a functional team is not an answerable question. I don't think there is such a thing. And the reason I say that is because a functional team that is a cryptocurrency hedge fund is going to look different than a functional team that is an elite Navy SEAL group, which would be different than a functional team of a canned tomato processing house. So functional is context-dependent on some level. And at some level, it is the case that all teams that are functional have some shared quality. Mostly that quality is trust." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## Two Ways to Measure

**Output-based:** Are you meeting your goals? Are you achieving better results than other teams in your field? This measure has two failure modes. First, if everyone in your field is dysfunctional, you can be dysfunctional and not know it because you are the best of a bad lot. Second, in strong tailwinds, mediocre teams look great. "In strong enough winds even turkeys can fly." Bitcoin investors who got in early are not necessarily great investors.

**Desirability-based:** Do the people who stay on the team want to be on it? Do they enjoy performing as part of it? This is the better measure.

"The best way I know to measure is that people want to be a part of it. People like it. And I don't mean everybody, but the people who are staying, who are consistently a part of that team, enjoy being a part of the team. They want to be a part of the team. They like performing as a part of that team. That's functionality." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Crucial Qualifier: "Not For Everybody"

The desirability measure is not "everyone likes it here." A functional team knows how to get rid of the wrong people quickly, or move the right person into the right position when they are in the wrong one. The team's ability to release misfit members is part of its functionality, not a violation of it.

"A functional team knows how to get rid of the wrong people quickly, or get the wrong people into the right position, so to speak, or get the right person in the right position even if they're in the wrong position. But it basically means that getting rid of people and attracting the right people is part of what makes a great team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

This is what differentiates "functional" from "comfortable for everyone." A team that retains people who do not belong is not functional; it has prioritized the appearance of inclusion over the reality of fit.

## Context Determines Specifics

What functional looks like depends entirely on what the team is doing.

- An engineering team has to agree on the context of what they are discussing. Without that, they fight over examples instead of the underlying points.
- A soccer team does not need this; the context (the game) is shared and visible.
- Engineers do not need physical prowess; soccer players do.

"A functional team that is a cryptocurrency hedge fund is going to look different than a functional team that is an elite Navy SEAL group." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The implication: copying a functional team from one domain into another fails. The Navy SEAL team's practices do not produce a functional family. The Silicon Valley engineering team's norms do not produce a functional sports team. The function must be designed for the context.

## Teams Are Everywhere

A team is any group with shared purpose. Inside your company, yes. Also: relationships with clients and their teams, partners on a movie, investors, your family, your community.

"What's amazing often is that you see executives who can run really super functional teams at work, but they don't apply some of those learnings to their own family, right? Where they're very interested in hearing the wisdom of all the people on the team at work, but they're not interested in hearing all the wisdom of the family team, the husband or the teenage kids." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The reverse failure is also common: running your family the way you run your military unit. The principles for building functionality (trust, the conditions that allow trust) apply across domains. The forms that functionality takes do not.

See [[family-as-team]] for the cross-domain application.

## What Builds Functionality

The mechanisms that produce functional teams in any context:

- **Trust** in its multiple dimensions. See [[trust-as-multifaceted]].
- **Conflict that is engaged, not avoided.** See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].
- **Clear performance standards** so people know what makes them stay or go. Politics arise where standards are unclear. See [[politics-from-unclear-standards]].
- **Owned wants** rather than hidden ones. See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] and [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]].
- **Iterative design** of metrics and principles, not static rules. See [[team-as-scientific-method]].
- **Structure that empowers** rather than constrains. See [[structure-and-empowerment]].

## Functionality Is a Spectrum, Not a Binary

The first reframe for a leader trying to grow team functionality: stop asking "is my team functional?" and start asking "how much more functional could this team be?" Leaders who treat revenue as an infinite game default to treating team functionality as a binary, and that frame caps the team at whatever level it has already reached. See [[dysfunction-as-spectrum]].

## Team As Soil

Team functionality is not one component among others on a balance sheet. It is the medium in which all the other components operate. Bad soil produces bad outcomes everywhere; good soil compounds the effect of every seed.

"It's like the soil in which everything grows in is people's relationships and their dynamics. And if you have bad soil, it's hard for anything to grow." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

This is also why a company in strong tailwinds (high margins, blue ocean, growing market) can be dysfunctional and look successful for years. The other components carry the weight while the soil quietly degrades. See [[momentum-allows-dysfunction-to-persist]].

## The Direct Diagnostic

The cleanest measure of where a team sits on the functionality spectrum: how do people feel at the end of meetings? Invigorated or depleted? The body integrates the totality of the dynamic in a way the agenda cannot. See [[invigorated-vs-depleted-as-functionality-measure]].

A practice that operationalizes this measure: [[five-star-meeting-commitment]].

## When Someone Experiences This

- You walk into a team and the energy is high; people look at each other, talk to each other, and seem engaged in the work
- The people who stay genuinely like being there; the people who leave do so cleanly
- The team's wisdom flows into decisions rather than getting stuck in politics
- Difficult conversations happen as part of the work rather than as crises
- The team can change goals and stay functional, because the functionality is in the relationships, not the goals
