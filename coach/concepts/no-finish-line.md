# No Finish Line

## Definition

The finish-line story is the unspoken assumption that there is an arrival point at which the work will be complete, the self repaired, and the person finally able to live. The arrival is always located in the future. The current life is implicitly a rehearsal for the arrived life.

There is no such arrival. There is no done state. The story of arrival is a leftover, often imported from a childhood religion that promised heaven, but it persists in secular form: enlightenment, success, healed-ness, freedom. The structure is identical. The destination changes.

The work is to see the finish-line story for what it is and let it dissolve. What is left when it dissolves is the present. The present turns out to contain what the future was supposed to provide.

## In Practice

The most direct framing in Joe's words: "What if there is no complete, no done, no heaven?"

The finish-line story functions as a permission structure. The person tells themselves: once I arrive, I will be allowed to enjoy / relax / connect / feel free. The implicit corollary is that until then, they are not allowed to. The story rations the very thing the person is trying to reach.

Brett's example shows the religious origin clearly. As a Christian child he thought: "I could just kill myself right now and go straight to heaven. Why not do that?" The logic of arrival was already running. Leaving the religion did not remove the logic. The arrival point shifted from heaven to whatever the adult version became — alignment, joy, accomplishment, connection. The structure ("when I get there, I'll be okay") survived the loss of the content.

"What if this is just the rest of the letting go of the story? Like, the finish line part of the story is now gone." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

## The Residual Religion

Many secular self-improvement frames import the structure of religious arrival without naming it. "When I am enlightened." "When I am healed." "When I have done the work." Each of these specifies an arrival point that, on examination, has no clear edge. The person can never quite check the box. The not-quite-checking is itself the trap.

The trap is not specific to people from religious backgrounds. The shape of the human psyche, given enough time, will manufacture an arrival point if one is not provided. Achievement culture provides one (the next milestone). Spiritual seeker culture provides one (the next state). Therapy culture sometimes provides one (the fully integrated self). The cultures differ. The shape is identical.

## The Diagnostic Question

If you can name a state in which you would be "done," you have a finish-line story. The state may sound modest ("when I finally feel okay with my body") or grand ("when I am free of suffering"). The grandness does not matter. The structure matters.

The deeper diagnostic: does the present moment feel like preparation or like life? If preparation, the finish-line story is active. The future has eaten the present.

## What Dissolves When the Story Drops

A specific bodily relaxation. The sprint-energy that was directed toward the finish line drops out. The person often notices their body for the first time in a while. "I just feel a lot more relaxed in my body now. There's a way that doing work and taking on challenges doesn't feel like the last final sprint before the finish."

Also dropped: the rationing. The connection, joy, ease that were supposed to arrive at the finish are immediately available because they are not, in fact, future-located. They are properties of being alive, not rewards for completing the work.

This is also where the question [[how-is-that-not-here-right-now]] tends to bite. Whatever the finish line was supposed to deliver is usually already in the room.

## What Tries to Replace It

The first thing that tries to replace the dropped finish-line story is a new finish line: "okay, so the goal is to live without a finish line." The arrival fantasy installs itself one level up. This needs to be noticed and let go too.

"It's the fear that the serenity could go away. Like, that's a possibility. Like, all we did was see what was. We didn't, we didn't do anything. Like, you didn't become a different person. All you had to do was just see what was actually happening and the serenity came in." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

The work is not to achieve a permanent state of no-finish-line-thinking. It is to keep seeing through the finish lines as they arise. Each iteration is its own undoing. See [[undoing-not-doing]].

## The Doing vs. Undoing Difference

A serenity that comes from a doing must be kept. A serenity that comes from an undoing does not need to be kept; it was what was already there once the obstruction was removed.

"The difference is that there's not a doing, there's an undoing. Right, so it's like, I did something and I need to keep it, is very different than I undid something and saw the truth. Then what do you have to do to keep it?" -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

This is why the question "how do I keep this?" is the diagnostic for the finish-line story re-installing. The question presupposes that the current state is a doing that could lapse. Seeing that it is an undoing — that there is nothing to keep — dissolves the question. See [[quit-hope-lose-cycle]].

## The Connection Version: "I'll Be There When..."

The finish-line story has a specific form when the value at stake is [[connection]]. The person holds their own connection -- with themselves, with the present, with the people around them -- hostage to some future condition. The condition is always pending.

"I'll be there when I quit smoking. I'll be there when I have the money. I'll be there when so-and-so loves me. I'll be there when ... Yeah, you're there right now." -- Joe Hudson, Connection: A State Beyond States

The carrot-on-a-stick structure is exact. The condition is always one step ahead of the person's current state. The person walks toward it for decades and never arrives. The connection that was supposed to be at the end is in fact unreachable from the structure that was set up to reach it.

The mechanism by which this loses connection is straightforward: connection lives only in the present. Future-orientation pulls attention away from the present. The thing the person is chasing is locked in the room they keep leaving.

"You can't be connected with yourself if it's dependent on something that is not actually present with you right now already." -- Brett Kistler, paraphrasing Joe

The dissolution of the I'll-be-there-when story is therefore also the entry into connection. There is no different "future you" that gets to be connected. There is only the present you, who already is. The recognition does not solve the conditions the person wants; it stops conditioning aliveness on those conditions being met.

This applies to people who have achieved everything they were chasing and still feel disconnected. Joe sees this constantly in his coaching: people who got the money, the partner, the recognition, the body, and report being more miserable than before. The finish line was the wrong device. There was no "there" to be in. The connection was always where they were standing.

"You see people do this all the time. I'll be there when I quit smoking. I'll be there when I have the money. I'll be there when so-and-so loves me." -- Joe Hudson

The instruction is not to give up on the future conditions. It is to stop using them as the price of admission to being alive now.

## The Freedom Version: "I'll Be Free When..."

The same finish-line structure operates around the word "freedom." The person says: I'll be free when I have the money. I'll be free when I'm meditating cleanly. I'll be free when my partner loves me right. I'll be free when I've healed the thing. Each statement places freedom on the other side of a condition.

The condition is the structure of the trap, not the path through it. See [[nothing-is-required-for-freedom]] for the full treatment. The freedom that is being chased is structurally available now. The chase is what holds it at a distance.

A specific feature of the freedom version: the conditions can be acquisitive ("when I have X") or renunciatory ("when I no longer need X"). Both are conditions. "I'll be free when I have the partner" and "I'll be free when I stop caring whether I have the partner" are the same trap with opposite content. The structure is the placing of freedom on the other side of any condition.

"Once I meditate long enough, I will be free is like, is a big trap. Once I have enough money, I'll be free is a big trap. Once I get it, once I listen to the guru long enough and I get it, then I'll be free... Or I have to stop caring whether people love me to be free. Like, all of it's BS." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

The disproof of the freedom-conditions is structural: the conditions, even when met, never produce the freedom they were supposed to produce. There is the brief window of the joy when a condition is met (see [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]] for the mechanism), then a new condition spins up, then the chase resumes. The freedom is what was there before the conditions installed themselves and what would be there if they dissolved.

The freedom version of the finish line is also where [[external-freedom-vs-internal-freedom]] becomes operational. The conditions that feel necessary are typically conditions on the external-resources kind of freedom. The freedom that is actually being sought is the felt-state kind, which has no conditions. The category error -- chasing the second through the conditions for the first -- is what keeps the structure self-sealing.

## Cover-Ups Keep Showing Up

Once the finish-line story has been seen, automatic ways of covering up the present continue to show up. Each one is a small instance of the same arrival logic. "How do I keep this?" "What about my taxes?" "What's the next step?" Each is the mind trying to put the present moment back into preparation-for-future mode.

The instruction is to watch them come and see through them. Not to defeat them. Not to make them stop coming. Just to recognize each one as another small version of the same story and let it pass.

"Notice how much stuff is just — it's just kind of automatic ways in which we cover ourselves. And they're just showing up. And that's the really cool part about this process is that you can just start to see, and especially in these moments of clarity, you can start to see all the ways that you wanna cover yourself up." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

## When Someone Experiences This

- A perpetual sense of sprinting toward something that never arrives.
- A felt life as "preparation" rather than "this is it."
- The next milestone always being the one that will finally do it.
- A specific religious or arrival imprint from childhood that has been disavowed but never fully metabolized.
- The serenity of insight repeatedly converting into a project to maintain the serenity.

## Tone

Spacious, quiet. Joe does not deliver this as a teaching but as an inquiry. "What happens to your sense of self if you deeply accept that there is no finish line?" The question is asked, not stated. The person's body answers, not their words.
