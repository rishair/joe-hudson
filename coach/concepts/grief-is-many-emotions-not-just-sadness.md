# Grief Is Many Emotions, Not Just Sadness

## Definition

Grief is an emotional experience, but it is not a single emotion. Sadness is part of it, but sadness is closer to the wrapper than the content. Inside grief: anger, fear, helplessness, denial, sometimes relief, sometimes joy. The grief process is the movement of all of these. Treating grief as "the sad emotion" misnames what is actually happening and predicts which parts will get suppressed.

"It's an emotional feeling, but it's not like sadness. Sadness is a part of it, almost like sadness is the wrapper of it, but there's anger and fear and helplessness. There's a whole bunch of denial and there's a whole bunch of emotional experiences that come along with grief. It's not just sadness." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

## How Grief Actually Moves

When grief is allowed to flow, the movement is not linear. It is not sad-then-angry-then-accepting. It flips between emotional states in a pattern that looks chaotic from the outside but is the system metabolizing the loss from multiple angles.

"When grief moves fluidly, it can go from anger to sadness to anger to sadness to anger to sadness to fear to anger to sadness. Different waves come through at different times. It's not as linear or logical as we would like it to be, or some of us would like it to be." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

This connects to [[logic-vs-emotional-movement]]: the mind expects emotional process to follow a logical sequence, and grief in particular does not. The expectation of linearity is part of why the grief process gets interrupted -- the person assumes that returning to anger after they "already did" anger means something has gone wrong.

## The Stages-of-Grief Frame, Used Correctly

The Kübler-Ross stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) and their popular extensions (shock, guilt, gratitude) are a useful map of what emotions tend to show up in grief. They were never meant to be a linear sequence.

"The stages of grief themselves, they don't entirely resonate with me, but they do and they don't, meaning I think that you do experience all of those, or there's a good chance to experience all those. I've noticed that people don't always experience them in that order. And also sometimes some of them don't happen for some people's grief process and other ones do." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

Joe describes the map as "a little bit like an Escher map. You need to turn it the right way to see the perspective that's helpful to you." The map is reliable as a list of what tends to appear, unreliable as an order.

The useful application is the other direction: take the emotional menu of grief and use it as a checklist for what might be present and unfelt. If sadness is moving but everything else is locked, see what happens when fear is invited. See [[embodied-stages-of-grief-ritual]] for the explicit practice that came out of this idea.

## What Gets Suppressed When Grief Is Treated as Sadness

When the cultural expectation is that grief equals sadness, the other emotions in grief get pathologized.

Anger at the dead person feels wrong, so it does not get felt. Fear about what comes next feels selfish, so it does not get acknowledged. Relief that a hard situation has ended feels shameful, so it gets shut down. Helplessness feels like weakness, so it gets defended against.

Joe describes a woman who lost her husband after fifty-three years. She was sitting with the grief, and she said: "I don't want to admit it, but I feel relief." The moment she let it through, she started to tear up -- and the moment she started to tear up, she shut it down. Both the relief and the sadness got blocked because the relief was not allowed.

"What's interesting is we tell ourselves that one of the emotional states that we feel is not okay in the process, but what that does is it shuts down all the other ones too. Or it makes them spin. It makes it like, oh, and I'm still scared and I'm still scared, or I'm still sad and I'm still sad when we tell ourselves that we can't have one of the emotions." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

This is the mechanism: shutting one door shuts all the doors. Whichever emotion is the "not allowed" one in this person's grief becomes the bottleneck. The rest pile up behind it or recycle in place. See [[joy-as-matriarch]] for the broader principle of which this is one application.

## Anger in Grief Specifically

Anger is the emotion in grief that gets shut down most often. There is no logical reason to be angry at someone who died, especially if they did not choose to die, so the mind refuses to allow the anger. The body has it anyway.

Joe describes leading a circle of friends after the death of his mentor Case. The first half of the room was sadness, sadness, sadness. He was halfway through and very angry, with no story to justify it. He let the anger out anyway.

"I just fucking got so angry. I was so pissed. There was no reason for it, of course. And you could just see the whole room like went to another level. It was sitting there in the whole room and nobody would express it. The expression of that anger allowed for so much more depth and movement to happen in the room and a variety of emotional experiences." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The anger did not have to be justified to be useful. Its appearance was the unlocking. The whole room's grief moved more freely once the anger had been let through.

See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] for the related mechanism in the other direction (anger as cover for sadness), and [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] for the practice when anger is locked.

## The Diagnostic Use

If grief is not moving, run the menu. Ask: which emotion in this grief is not being allowed? Sadness, anger, fear, helplessness, relief, denial, even gratitude. One of them is being held back. The one that is being held is the one to invite next.

The diagnostic is not "what should I be feeling," it is "what am I telling myself I cannot feel here." That is usually the next door.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. Joe is not making a moral claim that all emotions in grief are good; he is describing the mechanism. The casual swearing in the Case story ("I just fucking got so angry") is part of the tone — anger in grief gets to be the disreputable cousin, and welcoming it is the unlock.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is grieving and sadness is the only emotion getting through.
- When someone reports a grief process that feels stuck.
- When a person is judging themselves for feeling anger or relief or fear in their grief.
- When the cultural expectation has narrowed someone's permission to grieve.
- When a grief is recycling on one emotion (often sadness) for an unusually long time.
