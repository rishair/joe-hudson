# Verbal Abuse Is Trying to Control Someone With the Threat of Anger

## Definition

Verbal abuse is the use of anger -- in the form of yelling, threatening, or coercion -- to control another person. The defining feature is not the volume or the language. It is the intent: anger is being used as the tool of control.

"The way I would take it now is that we were trying to control each other with our anger. So anytime that you're trying to control somebody with the threat of something, I would call that abusive." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The definition is offered as a working frame for couples doing fight work. It is not a clinical or legal definition. It is a practical one: any move that uses anger or its threat to make the other person do, stop, or feel something is functioning as abuse, regardless of how culturally normal the move is.

## Joe's Origin Story for This Definition

Joe and Tara, early in their relationship, took their fights to a therapist. The therapist named the fights as verbal abuse. Joe and Tara initially rejected this. "That's not verbal abuse, that's just what people do. We saw our parents do it." When they looked into it, they recognized the therapist was correct.

"And she was like, no, that's verbal abuse. And we looked into it. We're like, oh fuck, we're verbally abusing each other." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The story is offered as the canonical example of how invisible this is when it has been modeled in childhood. The behavior felt like "what people do" because it was what their parents did. The recognition required an outside reading.

The pre-marriage moment that surfaces in the 2025 telling: visiting Tara's parents' house before the wedding and walking into "a crazy-ass yell fest. It was like out of control." Joe's recognition at the time: "oh, she's like, apparently her family's like mine, it was just yelling a lot." The pattern was visible across both families of origin and was the unmarked default the couple inherited.

The specific image Joe uses to mark how bad it got at the beginning: a Miata and a pitchfork.

"Tara and I used to live partially in the city and partially on these 200 acres. There was this moment where she's sitting in her Miata, revving her engine, threatening to run me over, and I am sitting above it with a pitchfork, which was the closest thing, threatening to hit the hood of her car. That's how bad our fights got at the beginning. Oh, it was brutal. I never physically hit her. And she never physically hit me, but it was like, those fights were brutal." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

The image is offered as the canonical reference point for how far inside the abusive-fighting zone the couple started. The carve-out matters: no actual physical contact happened. But the threats of physical violence were real and were delivered, and the threats themselves are what the body codes (see [[nervous-system-codes-threats-as-real]]). The story functions as an upper bound on "where can a relationship start from and still arrive at fights that heal" — very far inside the danger zone, as long as the work to interrupt the pattern actually happens.

The expanded definition that came from the therapist:

"Her definition, which has stuck with me forever, is: emotional abuse is when you are using your emotion to try to control somebody." -- Joe Hudson, How To Know If You Are In An Emotionally Abusive Relationship

This definition is broader than the verbal-abuse-via-anger frame. It is the same structural definition, applied to all emotions (anger, guilt, sadness, fear, withdrawal of love). The control intent is what makes the move abusive, regardless of which emotion is being deployed. The full extension to all emotions sits in [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]]. This article is the anger-specific application of the same definition.

Initial reaction at the time: "I was like, no, we're not. We're just yelling. Abuse is something like when you're whacking somebody on the head with like a cast iron skillet or something." The recognition that yelling-to-control is also abuse, not only physical violence, required the therapist's intervention and the readings she assigned.

## What the Behaviors Look Like

The behaviors Joe lists explicitly:

- **Yelling at each other without permission.** Anger directed at the partner, not as release, but as discharge into them.
- **Getting angry at each other without permission.** The same in a quieter register: the partner becomes the target of the anger work.
- **Threatening divorce.** Using the existence of the relationship as a lever to produce a change in behavior.
- **Threatening something in the future.** The if-you-don't-X-then-I-will-Y structure.
- **Any kind of physical violence.** The obvious version.

The unifying structure across all of these: the anger is being deployed to make the other person do something, stop something, or change something. The control intent is what marks the behavior as abusive.

## The "Takes a Long Time to Heal" List

Joe organizes the same set of behaviors from a different angle: not "what counts as abuse" but "what kinds of moves leave a long-term residue in the body of the person on the receiving end." The list is largely the same; the framing emphasizes the asymmetric damage timeline.

"There's certain things in fighting that take a long time to heal. If you do in a fight, it's going to take a long time to heal. The threat of any kind of physical violence is going to take a long time to heal, any kind of physical violence... Yelling, insults is another -- like insulting somebody, that can have the same kind of big emotional hit. And that erodes trust over a long period of time. Generally threatening somebody with future stuff -- I'm going to divorce you, I'm going to cheat on you -- any of those big threats are going to be incredibly damaging and it's going to take a long time to heal out of those." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

The list as Joe organizes it from this angle:
- The threat of any physical violence
- Any actual physical violence
- Yelling
- Insulting
- Threatening anything in the future (divorce, cheating, leaving)

The same list shows up under the abuse definition and under the long-time-to-heal frame. Both framings describe the same set of moves. The reason both framings converge on the same set is the underlying mechanism: see [[nervous-system-codes-threats-as-real]]. The body codes these specific moves as real threats and brakes around the angry partner thereafter. The control intent and the long-time-to-heal damage are two faces of the same phenomenon — the body has registered the threat as real, regardless of the speaker's later regret or the conscious mind's later discount.

This converges with [[disagreement-that-heals-vs-retraumatizes]]: any of these moves, in a fight, puts the fight on the retraumatizing side of the line rather than the healing side. The fight that crosses any of these lines installs damage that subsequent fights then have to work around.

## Why the Permission Carve-Out Matters

Joe is explicit that the same behaviors can be non-abusive if both partners have consented in advance to a frame where anger can be expressed directly. This is the carve-out from [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]]: the permission frame, with explicit consent, can convert what would otherwise be a discharge-at-partner move into a piece of relational practice.

The default assumption is that no such permission exists. Without an actual asked-and-granted permission for anger to be present in a specific way, the behaviors function as abusive regardless of how comfortable the relationship has been with them. Cultural normalization (the parents did it, the friend group does it) does not constitute permission.

## The Lost-Yourself Test

A second diagnostic works alongside the control-intent definition. If you cannot look back on the fight with pride two or three days later, you lost yourself in it. Losing yourself in a fight is closely related to the abuse pattern, because the lost-self state is the state in which the trauma controls the fighting rather than the fighter controlling themselves.

"If you've lost yourself in the fight, if at the end of the fight you're like, what happened? Or that's not how I want it to be. Or, and I don't mean right after the fight, I mean 2 days, 3 days after the fight. If you can't look back on that fight with pride, then you're cycling and you're retraumatizing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The retraumatization frame matters because it links the abuse pattern to the trauma pattern. Two people in their trauma, both retraumatizing each other, both lost in fights that recreate the original wounds -- this is the structure that abuse-without-permission produces. It is not a moral failing; it is two nervous systems running their oldest patterns at each other.

## The Trauma Frame

Joe consistently places the abuse pattern in the context of trauma rather than character. Two people fighting abusively are not bad people; they are two people in their trauma fighting against the present partner as if the present partner were the original wound. See [[i-cant-trust-my-thoughts-right-now]] for the felt sense of being in trauma during a fight.

"If a human being fought in Fallujah and they're back in Ohio and a car backfires, they think they're in Fallujah again. If they have PTSD, if they're in their trauma. We think that we're kids again. We think that we're in old relationships again. We're not in that moment. And so we are wildly reactive to one another's statements." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The implication: the move toward not-abusive fighting is not a moral correction. It is the work of recognizing that you are in your trauma, naming it as trauma, and developing the capacity to fight from yourself rather than from the trauma. See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] for the in-the-moment move.

## The Two-Marriages Frame: There Are Four People in the Room

A specific elaboration of the trauma frame. In many couples, the fight is not actually happening between the two adults present. It is happening between the two adults and the four parental figures the two adults are projecting onto each other.

"I was just talking to a person and they were talking about how they have two marriages. And I think a lot of people would relate to this. One marriage is between them, and one relationship is between their parents. Except for— so let's say them is John and Nancy. So one relationship is John and Nancy, and one relationship is the father of Nancy and the mother of John. And they've, they're literally seeing each other's mother. They're seeing their own mother in their wife, and seeing their own father in the husband. They're just in their trauma. They're not even in the reality that they live in, and they're reacting that way." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The structural image: in a marriage between John and Nancy, when both partners are in their trauma, the fight is actually happening between four people. Nancy is fighting John-as-her-father. John is fighting Nancy-as-his-mother. The two adult selves who would, if present, be able to have an adult conversation, have been temporarily replaced by the child selves who are still trying to win the fight with the original parents.

This is why the fight is so disproportionate to its content. The intensity is not about what happened between John and Nancy this afternoon. The intensity is about what John never got to say to his mother and what Nancy never got to say to her father. The content of the fight is the surface; the depth is the unfinished business of the original wounds.

The implication for the abuse frame: the abusive moves often are not aimed at the partner. They are aimed at the parent the trauma has converted the partner into. This does not change the impact (the partner still receives the abuse) but it changes the work. The work is not to convince the partner to stop being abusive. The work is to see that the abuse is aimed at someone who is not in the room, and to develop the capacity to stop fighting the absent parent through the present partner.

The naming move that interrupts the two-marriages dynamic: [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]]. The naming is the move by which the partner says "I have just left the room and started fighting my mother again -- I am back" so the actual two-person marriage can resume.

## What Is Not Abusive

The distinction matters because some couples, after learning this frame, swing into the opposite error and start treating any expression of anger or strong emotion as abusive. This is a misreading.

Anger that is moved before the conversation, then communicated cleanly, is not abusive. See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] for the full move.

Truth spoken with an open heart, even when it produces a difficult conversation, is not abusive. Speaking truth that is hard for the partner to hear is one of the central activities of fighting well.

A boundary that is clear and includes the partner -- "if you continue yelling I will leave the room for twenty minutes" -- is not abusive. See [[boundaries]] and [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]].

The defining feature stays consistent: is the move being used to control the other person via the threat of anger? If yes, it is functioning as abuse. If no -- if it is communication, expression, truth-telling, or boundary-drawing -- it is the work of fighting well.

## The Connection to the Aggressive Victim Stance

A specific variant: when the abuse is delivered from a victim stance ("look what you made me do"), it is harder to see as abuse because the surface presentation is suffering rather than aggression. See [[aggressive-victim-stance]]. The structure is the same: the anger or distress is being deployed to control the partner's behavior. The victim wrapping obscures the control intent but does not change it.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Recognizing, after a fight, that you were trying to make the partner change rather than communicate something
- Noticing that the threats you made were not boundaries but levers
- Hearing a therapist or friend name your normal fighting style as abusive and being shocked
- Realizing that the way you fight is exactly how your parents fought
- Finding that you cannot look back on recent fights with pride
- Noticing the partner is afraid of you in the conversation -- a slow-developing awareness

- When someone is afraid of their own anger because they have used it to control before

## Tone

Plain, structural, not moralizing. Joe describes his own pattern with humor and frankness. The recognition is offered as data rather than indictment. The point is not that abusers are bad people; the point is that the behaviors are abusive and the work is to find different mechanisms for the energy that was previously discharged as control.
