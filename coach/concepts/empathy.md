# Empathy

## Definition

Empathy is being with somebody's experience without losing yourself in it. Not watching their experience from a distance. Not wanting to change it. Being with them in it while remaining in your own body.

"It's not watching somebody's experience, and it's not wanting to change somebody's experience. It's being with them in the experience without losing yourself in it." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

A sharper one-line formulation Joe uses: empathy is being **with** somebody **without being in** somebody. The preposition is the entire teaching. With keeps you beside, anchored, able to see them. In dissolves the boundary and you lose access to what is actually happening because their state has become your state. See [[being-with-vs-being-in]] for the full distinction and the operational mechanism (10-20% body attention) that holds the with-position in place.

"Empathy is like being with somebody without being in somebody. But oftentimes we find ourselves in somebody." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself

Empathy is a felt sense, not an intellectual understanding. It cannot be fully described by logic any more than the color green can be described to someone who has never seen it. Proprioception (knowing where your left foot is without looking) is a felt sense. Knowing when you are done going to the bathroom is a felt sense. Empathy operates at the same level: below thought, in the body.

"Empathy is so much of a feeling more than it is an intellectual understanding." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

## What Empathy Is Not

- **Not fixing.** Trying to make someone feel better is not empathy. It is a response to your own discomfort with their emotion. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]].
- **Not merging.** Feeling whatever the other person feels until you lose your own emotional state is not empathy. It is codependence. See [[losing-yourself-in-others]].
- **Not agreeing with the story.** Saying "you're right, your boss is terrible" is validating a narrative, not being with a person's emotion. If you buy into their story, you are in them, not with them.
- **Not absorbing shame.** When someone is ashamed, empathy is not joining them in shame. That is losing yourself.
- **Not analyzing.** Watching someone's emotions like observing an ant colony, recognizing patterns but not being in it, is observation, not empathy. There is a difference between being with a friend and being with a shrink who is psychoanalyzing them. The other person can feel which one is happening, even without naming it. When the analyzing mode shows up, the other person disconnects -- and the listener, with body attention, can notice the disconnection and trace it back to their own slip into analysis.
- **Not sympathy.** See [[empathy-vs-sympathy]].

## In Practice

Empathy is the E in [[view]]. It requires maintaining your own center while being fully present to another person's emotional experience. The person feels accompanied, not managed.

Empathy combined with [[impartiality]] creates safety. The person senses that you are with them AND that you have no agenda to change them. This allows them to relax, be honest, and move at their own pace.

### The Body Is the Key

The primary practice for empathy is [[staying-in-your-body]]. Put some attention in your own physical body while you are with another person. Feel the bottom of your feet. Notice how sound lands in your inner ear. This prevents losing yourself in the other person while staying fully present.

"To be empathetic in a successful way is to maintain a certain amount of your awareness in your own body." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

When the body is rigid, feeling stops. Jaw locked, belly tight, shoulders hunched: these are signs of resistance to feeling. Keeping the body loose allows empathy to flow. Feelings live in muscles. The way a person walks into a room, the way they hold their lips, how they hunch their shoulders: all of this reveals what emotions they are willing and unwilling to feel. See [[emotions-live-in-muscles]].

### Fear Shuts Down Empathy

From an evolutionary standpoint, when in fear, the brain takes empathy offline. The fear response (fight, flight, freeze) is incompatible with being present to another's experience. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

- **Fight:** The emotion you start to feel makes you angry. You try to stop it. Like the angry person on the plane when a baby cries.
- **Flight:** You look at the environment and try to manage the actors in it. You lose yourself in the other person's world as a way to control it.
- **Freeze:** Dissociation. The eyes haze over. Checked out.

The path through fear is to feel it, welcome it, invite it in. Not to manage it or project it onto someone else. "How do you feel the emotion? Feel it. I don't mean be taken away by it." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

### Empathy as Data

When you are empathetic, people become more open because they feel you are with them. You get more data, more truth, more ideas for solutions. When people sense they are being managed, they back off. The solutions they give you are solutions to get you to stop managing them, not their real solutions.

"When you have empathy with someone, they're more likely to be open because they feel that you're with them. And you can't do anything to show to them, you're just empathetic and it just occurs." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

Empathy also catches your own partiality. When you feel someone's reaction to you, you notice: "Oh, I was being partial there." The other person's response becomes a mirror for your own state.

### Empathy in Business

Focus groups fail when they ask emotional questions through the intellect. People say they want a product and then do not buy it. "Henry Ford said, if I gave my customers what they wanted, I would have given them a faster horse." The intellect answers the question it can see. Empathy reads the emotional truth underneath.

A team meeting where everyone agrees but no one is excited is a failure of empathy. The intellectual agreement happened but the emotional resistance was not addressed. People make decisions based on emotions. That is why they can all agree to something in a meeting and then not do it. Feeling into the resistance, the excitement, the rigidity in the room changes everything.

A fundraising meeting where the investor gets distant: naming it ("I noticed you're getting distant, what happened?") opens a real conversation. Empathy reveals what cannot be spoken intellectually.

## When Someone Experiences This

The absence of empathy is instantly felt. When someone shares something emotional and meets a blank wall or a quick redirect, trust erodes. When someone shares and is met with genuine presence without agenda, something relaxes in their system. This is often the moment people become willing to go deeper.

Whatever you have a hard time empathizing with in others is something you have a hard time feeling in yourself. This is the diagnostic value of empathy: it reveals your own emotional limits.

"Our capacity to love the parts of ourselves is directly correlated to our capacity to love the parts of other people." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

## What Deepening Empathy Produces

- Decisions become clearer because emotions are no longer feared. See [[decisions-are-emotional]].
- [[caring-vs-caretaking]]: support replaces fixing, and that support is returned.
- Standing in truth becomes easier as fear of emotional states drops.
- Empowerment grows. A CEO who shifted from caretaking to being with people started asking "How do you want to fix it?" instead of telling them how. Everything changed.
- Capacity to love expands.

## Turbulence

As emotions begin to be felt and resistance works through, there can be turbulence. Unexpected tears. Moments of intensity. The discomfort is not in the emotions. It is in the resistance to them. Nobody who goes through this process ends up controlled by emotions. The fear that allowing emotions means they will take over is a projection: because you have been controlling them, you assume they will control you.

"Take it slow. If the emotional tube is kinked, be gentle with the unkinking." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

## The Developmental Arc

Empathy follows the larger pattern of [[simplicity-on-the-other-side-of-complexity]]. A two-year-old has simple emotional life: feels anger, yells. Then a long middle phase of identifying emotions, naming them in the body, learning to feel them without harming others, discovering they can be loved. Eventually the system arrives back at simplicity, but now integrated: emotions move fluidly, the person is not run by them, not hurting others with them.

The middle complexity is necessary. Skipping it produces the first simplicity dressed up as the second. The person who claims they "just have emotions" without doing the middle work is operating on the unworked starting point, which collapses under any real intensity. The integrated empathic capacity is what is available on the far side of the complexity, not before it.

## Curiosity About How Far Down It Goes

Emotions are often stacked. Feeling the anger reveals the hurt underneath. Feeling the hurt reveals fear. Feeling fear reveals helplessness. If only the top layer gets felt, the next one starts driving in its place. The dynamic looks like "I dealt with the anger and now I'm acting from a completely different unconscious place." See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]].

The orientation: be curious about how far down it goes. Bring [[vulnerability]] with yourself about your emotional state. Bring [[impartiality]] to how you feel. The investigation is endless and that is fine -- "to think that there's an end is no good. It's not going to be servicing your journey at all."

The combination of gentleness and curiosity is what makes the descent sustainable. Gentleness without curiosity stays surface. Curiosity without gentleness becomes a forced excavation that re-traumatizes. Both at once: the system can drop another layer without bracing.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person is emotional and the temptation is to fix, redirect, or analyze.
- When someone has shared something and the conversation feels like it wants to speed past the feeling.
- When the coach notices an impulse to make things better rather than be present.
- When someone describes being unable to be with a particular emotion in others.
- When "meaningless" conversations feel frustrating, the frustration reveals an emotional limit in the frustrated person, not a deficiency in the conversation.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- The person feels safe enough to access what is underneath the surface emotion.
- Creates the conditions for [[vulnerability]] in the other person.
- Combined with [[wonder]], opens the space for genuine exploration rather than performance.
- Reveals where the coach's own emotional capacity has limits.
- In business: more data, better decisions, deeper customer understanding, team alignment that holds.

## Anti-Patterns

- [[losing-yourself-in-others]]: Merging with another person's emotions instead of being with them.
- [[view-as-technique]]: Faking empathy with techniques (mirroring body language, repeating words) without the felt sense. It comes off as false and creepy.

## Distinctions

- [[empathy-vs-sympathy]]: Sympathy puts you above the other person. Empathy is beside them.
- [[caring-vs-caretaking]]: Being in support vs trying to make them feel better.
