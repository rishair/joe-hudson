# Impartiality

## Definition

Impartiality is not trying to achieve an outcome for yourself or others. It is the difference between driving to work on a fixed route at a fixed time and taking a Sunday drive with no destination. Both involve driving. One is controlled by agenda; the other is open to what arises.

Explained to an 11-year-old: "I trust you. You know what's best. I'm going to follow your lead. I think you know what you need better than anybody else could know what you need, and I want to explore that with you." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

The opposite is partiality: I know what you need. Let me give you advice. I want you to be better, healed, different. I want you to feel like I'm valuable in this conversation.

When someone else is resisting, the partial response is to want them to stop resisting. "In other words, change to make me happy. Change. I want you to change right now to make me happy." The impartial response is to allow them their path. See [[resistance]]. "No matter how wise you get, you don't get to have more knowledge than the person you're talking to about them, or more wisdom." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Love the Feeling, Love the Resistance

Complete impartiality is asymptotic. Every sentence carries some degree of agenda. The practice is to move steadily toward less partiality, not to achieve zero. Every sentence has a want underneath it. That is as human as breathing or having emotions.

## Why Impartiality Matters

The subtle message behind partiality is that you think you are smarter than the other person. Giving unsolicited advice tells them: I know what's best for you. This confirms the essential myth most people carry: that they are not good enough. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

When you speak to the wise part of someone, that is the part that shows up. When you speak to the helpless part, that is what you get. Impartiality is addressing the wise person, not the broken one.

"If you're telling somebody that you know what's best for them, you're also making the myth that they're not good enough true for them." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

## In Practice

Impartiality is the I in [[view]] and the component most people find hardest to grasp, especially in business.

The common objection: without partiality, nothing gets done. The reframe: clear intention and clear goals are compatible with impartiality. The partiality that kills effectiveness is attachment to *how* the goal is reached. A basketball team has a clear goal (put the ball in the hoop) but cannot plan every pass in advance. The player who is open to what emerges makes the right pass.

"If I can be impartial in these ways, not give up my goal, but be impartial in how this thing happens and be impartial with people, I actually discover what is." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

In business, impartiality reveals a natural current: where customers want to go, where employees want to go, what the market needs. Reading and following this current takes less effort than fighting it. Going against the flow is like building a canal instead of following the river.

The boat metaphor makes this concrete: "You're on a boat going down a river. It's important to row the boat, but it is more important to read the river. And if you are partial in reading the river, you're not reading the river." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection. Goals and determination are compatible with impartiality. An oak tree grows to 40 feet tall. That is determination. It also grows impartially, in the flow of things.

In directing actors, demanding a specific emotional result produces a stiff performance. Giving a clear objective and trusting the actor's process produces excellence. "Recognizing something that's right and going with it rather than trying to get it to be your version of right is the practice of impartiality in business." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

### In Hiring and Sales

Impartial hiring: rather than convincing talented people to join, state the reality of the role. "Do you want in? Is this something that inspires you?" Then you know you have someone who chose in, not someone you convinced.

An artist struggling to sell was told to go get 50 rejections. After 10 calls, he was in 3 galleries. Dropping the partiality of trying to convince let him be himself, which is what the galleries wanted.

"The best salesmen will challenge, ask difficult questions, will even look at a pipeline and try to get to a no as quickly as possible." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

The same principle scales to whole categories. Most bad-debt collection works by intimidation: "I will intimidate you until you pay me." A company like Detli inverts this by approaching debtors as collaborators: "I want to work with you about this." Most people want their debt relieved, and the impartial approach gets a much better result. Aggressive phone-bank models can work short-term when it is a pure numbers game (100 calls to get 3 or 4), but they degrade. The aggressive culture turns the team against the leader because the team does not want to feel what they are making others feel. The person who shifts to impartiality usually leaves and finds work that is life-affirming.

### In Management

Managers often think their job is to push people toward the right outcome. The reframe: if employees do not want to do the right thing for the company, the problem is hiring, not management. If you assume the people working for you want to do the right thing, then the question is only whether they can see it or are capable of it. Education does not require partiality.

"My way is never the best way. It is a part of the best solution." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

Great leaders know they are missing something. They want people in the room smarter than them. Impartiality is the only way to discover what you are missing.

### In Parenting

If an adult child does not want to be around you, there is almost certainly an agenda present. "If they don't want to be around you, I guarantee you have an agenda for them. You want them to be safe. You want them to be a doctor or whatever. They might be around you, but they don't want to be, and you feel it, and you feel unloved because of it." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

### Impartiality With Self

The VIEW framework focuses impartiality on conversations with others. But impartiality with oneself is a separate, complementary practice. Most meditation, for example, is managing the experience rather than being with whatever arises. That is not meditation; it is torture. When there is no management of the internal experience, what remains is bliss.

"I trust my inherent goodness, I trust the impulse of life that moves through me." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

The voice in the head that criticizes and pushes is the inner partiality. Releasing it means releasing expectations of how you should be.

## Manipulation vs. Impartiality

Subtle manipulation (nudging without owning the intent) is a common substitute for impartiality. It looks like letting go of agenda, but the agenda still operates underneath. The difference is felt in the body. Manipulation feels uncomfortable to do and uncomfortable to receive. Impartiality feels spacious.

"If you're subtly nudging or trying to, and you're not owning your partiality, if you're not owning your wants in that situation, then you're not being straightforward with them. It's a subtle form of lying." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3

Most people can feel when someone is manipulating, even if they tolerate it by social contract. People who are straightforward about morally questionable actions can sustain them for a long time. People who are duplicitous about their intentions almost always blow up within five years, whether at the scale of relationships or economies.

The body knows impartiality faster than the mind does. To calibrate: recall a moment of being subtly manipulative and feel how it sits in the body. Then recall a moment of being straightforward with a want, even when it was scary. The difference is the guide.

## Fear of Outcomes and Impartiality

Attachment to outcomes is what makes impartiality hard. The fear is never about the event. It is about what will have to be felt if the event happens. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]]. Three paths to release the grip:
1. Visualize the feared outcome and feel everything in advance. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].
2. Grieve the loss in advance.
3. Own the partiality out loud. See [[owning-your-partiality]].

What we fear, we often subconsciously invite. The things done to avoid the feared outcome are often the things that bring it about. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]].

There is also a useful distinction between [[short-term-vs-long-term-partiality]]: short-term partiality is fear-driven, long-term partiality is principles-driven.

## Relationship to Wants and Boundaries

Impartiality does not mean suppressing wants. It means owning them openly. See [[wants-vs-partiality]]. Owning a want is vulnerable. Being partial about it is hidden. Partiality is the want you will not name.

Practicing impartiality often requires drawing *more* boundaries, not fewer. It is easier to be partial (trying to fix someone, hinting, managing) than to say directly: "I don't want to hear this story anymore" or "If this isn't happening, I have to assume you don't want to work here."

"One of the reasons people are so partial is because they're not drawing the boundaries that they want." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

## What Impartiality Produces

- Deeper connection. An older, successful entrepreneur at a workshop had his first impartial conversation and wept: "I don't think I've ever had an impartial conversation in my whole life. I had no idea the level of connection that I was missing." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality
- Freedom. When Joe stopped needing his wife to stop being angry, it was not a fix for the fight. It was freedom for him: "I can love her just as she is, no matter what's happening." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality
- Better ideas. Time spent managing people becomes time spent learning.
- Less repetitive conflict. Having the same conversation 10 times with the same person is a sign of partiality. If you stop wanting them to be different, the loop breaks.
- Less victimhood in your orbit. People stuck in victim narratives gravitate away from those who will not participate in fixing them.

## The Do Not List

These are the common forms of partiality in VIEW conversations. See also [[fixing-people]].

- Don't try to fix people.
- Don't try to get them to a conclusion.
- Don't try to be valuable or have them see you as valuable.
- Don't want them to be different.
- Don't look to convince.
- Don't try to lead them to a solution.
- Don't want them to see things your way.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is trying to control an outcome and creating resistance.
- When a conversation feels like a power struggle.
- When someone notices they are "selling" rather than exploring.
- When a parent's adult child avoids them.
- When the same argument recurs in a relationship.
- When a manager micromanages because they do not trust the team to arrive at the right answer.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Opens access to information that agenda-driven conversation blocks.
- Allows the best solution to emerge rather than forcing a predetermined one.
- Combined with [[empathy]], creates the safety others need to speak honestly.
- Requires [[vulnerability]] to own wants and draw boundaries directly.
- When partiality is noticed mid-conversation, naming it ("I notice I'm trying to fix you, and I apologize for that") is itself a VIEW move that builds trust.

## Distinctions

- [[wants-vs-partiality]]: Wants are owned openly. Partiality is the hidden version of wanting.
- [[impartial-apathy]]: "I don't care" is not impartiality. It means "I care but I don't want to feel it."

## Boundary Conditions

Impartiality does not mean denying wants. Denying wants to appear impartial leads to dissociation and joylessness. "If you try to pretend that you don't have wants, you start disassociating. All is bliss and that kind of thing, which is true, it is all bliss. But if you're denying your own wants and your own humanity, it'll sap all the joy out of your life." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

If you want them to see you as impartial, you are being partial.

Short-term partiality is fear-based: I need this outcome right now. Long-term partiality is principles-based: what kind of world do I want to live in? The principled version is less about controlling specific outcomes and more about watching for how the world wants to provide what you value, then moving with it.
