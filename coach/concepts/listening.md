# Listening

## Definition

There is no moment of waking consciousness in which you are not listening. Your awareness is always on something — the thoughts in your head, the pain in your foot, the person across from you, the silence in the room. There is no off-switch. The question is never "am I listening?" The question is what the *caliber* of listening is and where the attention is going.

Deep listening, the kind taught here, is a specific quality of attention. It is receiving rather than producing. It is allowing rather than reaching. It is attunement that happens without being instructed: when you listen this way to another person, you attune to them; when you listen this way to your own body, you attune to it. Care, respect, and understanding fall out of the attunement automatically.

"It's like the world is coming to you is the feeling of it if you're really listening." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

This is one of the "sushi rice" basics that turns out to be most of the practice. It is taught first because almost nothing else lands without it. It is taught again in advanced work because the depth of it never bottoms out.

## You Are Always Listening — Don't Make It a To-Do

The most common error: hearing "listen better" and converting it into a task, a discipline, a thing to try harder at. The trying is what kills it.

"One of the things that the mind will do is it'll make listening a to-do, a challenge. You have to start trying, and all of that is what gets in the way of the listening." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The reframe: you are already listening — always. Deep listening is not a new activity you must learn how to do. It is a relaxation of the things you are adding on top of the natural listening that is already happening. The instruction "try to listen" produces the same internal posture as "try to receive" or "try to allow." It cannot work. The actual move is the un-doing of the layers obscuring listening that is already underway. See [[opposite-of-trying]].

## The Inner Ear and the Silence (Two Live Experiments)

Two demonstrations Joe uses to give the listener immediate access to the felt difference.

**Experiment one.** Put all of your attention on your inner ear. Keep the focus inside the ear itself. Notice that even though your attention has gone inside, you still comprehend what the speaker is saying. The comprehension does not collapse. But the *quality* of the listening has shifted. There is more spaciousness, more receiving.

**Experiment two.** Put all of your attention on the silence around the speaker's words, in between the words, in the frequencies that are silent even while the speaker is talking. Notice that you still understand what is being said. The understanding has not weakened. But the listening feels different again — more open, more vast.

"There's a kind of a feeling of expansion that occurs. It's almost like your awareness becomes bigger and it becomes more in the forefront. There's more spaciousness that happens." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

These experiments prove the same point in the body: deep listening is not less listening, it is differently weighted listening. The mind keeps doing its work in the background. The foreground opens up.

## Listening Is a Form of Receiving

Listening sits inside the family of receiving moves: receiving, allowing, channeling, being a conduit, surrender. See [[opposite-of-trying]] for the broader category. Listening is what receiving looks like when there is incoming signal — sound, speech, sensation, presence, energy. It is the receive-form most people have available, even if they have never named it that way.

This is why the practice is so foundational. To learn listening is to learn one corner of the receive. To learn the receive in one corner makes the rest available.

## Attunement Falls Out Of It

You do not have to tell yourself to attune. Attunement happens automatically when listening is deep.

- Listening deeply to yourself → attunement to yourself.
- Listening deeply to another person → attunement to the person.
- Listening deeply to sensation in your body → attunement to the body.
- Listening deeply to a team in a meeting → attunement to the team.

The implication: care, respect, and understanding are not separate skills you stack on top of listening. They are byproducts of the listening itself when the listening is allowed to go deep. People who try to be more caring without addressing their listening get the performance of care, which lands as such. People who develop their listening end up caring naturally and being received as caring.

## What Dilutes Listening

The listening is always there. What gets in the way is what you are doing on top of it. Joe names the major contaminants:

**Listening to figure out what to say next.** The attention is on the future — what comes after the silence is your turn. Listening becomes a holding pattern for response-generation. The signal is being half-processed; the rest is being prepared for outgoing.

**Listening to help, fix, judge, or save.** The attention is on what the listener is going to *do* about what they hear. The receiving is collapsed into evaluation. The speaker feels evaluated rather than received.

**Listening to be valuable or useful.** The attention is on the listener's own worth. "What if I have nothing to say? What if they don't like me? How do I add value here?" The listening is being deployed in the service of self-presentation rather than meeting the speaker.

**Listening to feel safe in the environment.** The attention is on threat-monitoring. "Are they upset with me? Am I in trouble?" The listening becomes a security system.

**Listening through the past or the future.** Managing what already happened, planning what will happen, rehearsing how to respond. Any orientation in time other than present collapses the listening.

"Some way in which you try to manage the future or have managed the past is kind of management really in any way gets in the way of listening." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

All of these can be named with one word: management. Management of the future, management of the past, management of self-presentation, management of the other person. Listening and management are opposites in the body. See [[enjoy-over-manage]] and [[flow-vs-managing-reality]] for the broader category. Listening is the non-management form of attention.

## Reducing the Filters

Brett's reformulation: every form of contamination is a filter. The everyday "knowing" filter — *I know where this is going, I know what they mean, we've talked about this before* — is the most common. It runs everything the speaker is saying through your preconceptions. Most of the signal gets lost in the filter.

The deeper forms of listening have fewer filters. Or the filters that remain are more natural. The signal-to-noise ratio improves. What is important comes to the surface more readily because it is not being pre-filtered by what you expect to hear.

"My experiences of the wider forms of listening, the listening that are like embodied listening, what it does is it's like the important information comes to the surface easily, more easily. The signal-to-noise ratio gets better." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

This is the link to [[seeing-through-projections-expands-possibility]] — what looks like intelligence in others is often just fewer filters. The same is true with listening. Reduced filters look like sharper perception. They are not. They are less obstruction.

## Listening to Yourself While Listening to Another

The single most important nuance Joe makes about listening: you never put yourself *entirely* into the other person. If you do, it is not listening anymore. It is chasing, managing, trying to make sure they feel safe, trying to be cared for and tended to — another form of management.

"The deeper forms of listening, you always have some percentage of your awareness in yourself, which is an incredibly useful thing." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

This is the same teaching that lives at the heart of [[empathy]] and [[staying-in-your-body]]. You keep some attention in your own body. From there, you have data about how the conversation is landing in you — not just the content of the other person's words but how your nervous system is responding to them. People who can do this are less likely to fall for the con. They are less likely to ignore the partner whose tone says something the words deny. They are less likely to abandon themselves to make someone else happy.

The example: someone speaks with conviction. The conviction is felt in the body. People who are not listening to their own body mistake the felt conviction for the truth of the content. People who are listening to their own body notice — "I'm feeling their conviction, not the intelligence of what they're saying" — and the spell breaks.

## Whole-Body Listening: The Body Catches Up

Listening with the whole body is what produces the "grok." The mind catches up afterward. The mind alone listens for concepts and gets concepts. The body listens for everything and gets it differently.

"It's the way you would understand a river by just sitting by the river for a while and all of a sudden you start noticing things like, oh, the eddies happen on the side and the flow happens in the middle." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The basketball-player metaphor: a great player knows how to do the fake without fully intellectualizing it. The small shoulder-shift before turning left always gets past the guard. The knowing lives in the body. It cannot be acquired by reading about it. It can only be acquired by listening, repeatedly, to what the body is picking up.

The note for the listener who worries about retention: trying to remember everything makes intellectual capture more likely but makes whole-body understanding less likely. At in-person retreats Joe explicitly asks people not to take notes; he takes the important notes for them in advance. The reason: note-taking puts attention in the head; whole-body listening puts attention everywhere. The latter changes the person; the former gives the person something to refer to.

## Listening and the Trust Question

There is a phase of learning to listen where you have to trust the listening to catch what you need. You worry that you are not getting it; the worry itself is what stops you from getting it.

"It just requires me not to worry if I'm getting it. If I worry if I'm getting it, then I'm screwed." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The trust phase is temporary. After enough repetitions of "I just listened and I got it," the trust converts into knowing — like trusting that ice will be cold. You do not have to convince yourself; you just know. But early on, the practice is partly the practice of releasing the worry-that-you-are-missing-it. The release of the worry is what allows the listening to actually pick up what is there.

## Speaking From Listening

The deepest form of the practice: listening continues even while you are speaking. The two are not opposites. You can speak from inside the listening.

"When I'm listening and talking, when I'm in that deep form of listening and I'm talking at the same time, I'm very curious about what the fuck's going to come out of my mouth. It's like I'm experiencing it for the first time. It's an unfolding that delights me." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

This is what Joe means by channeling. The speech arises from the receiving rather than from the planning. It surprises the speaker. The speaker is the first one to hear what they are about to say. The speaker is delighted, the way watching grass move in wind delights — not because the content is impressive but because the appearance is fresh.

The path to this is gradual. Most people need a long stretch of just-listening before they can sustain listening while talking. The reward, when it comes, is that speaking becomes pleasant — more pleasant than the version where you are reaching for the next thing to say. The unpleasantness of speech is largely the unpleasantness of being out of the listening.

This is also what [[listening-to-impulse]] is pointing at from the action side. Listening to impulse and speaking from listening are the same somatic state: the action arises from the receive.

## The Pause as the Hardest Part of Speaking From Listening

In speaking coaching, Tristan de Montebello identifies the pause as the most underrated and most difficult move. A simple instruction — "set a timer; at any point during this time, stop speaking and do nothing, just take a pause" — proves nearly impossible for most people to follow.

The inability to pause is not a technical issue. It is the surface of everything the speaker is using the talking to avoid feeling. Joe and Tristan converge on the diagnostic: the speaker who cannot pause is often unconsciously holding "I'm not worth it," "I don't have my seat at the table," "somebody else should be using this space," "if I pause I'll look stupid," "I'm deathly afraid of blanking." The continuous talking is keeping these feelings at bay. The pause exposes them.

Joe extends the same teaching far beyond speaking. In every high-stakes domain, people focus on the doing and ignore the receiving. They are concerned with what they will say, what the presentation looks like, what the graphics are. They are not concerned with what they will listen to, what they will allow.

"I coach executives and they're like, I'm going to go raise money. And they're going to tell me all the things they're going to do to raise the money. And I'll ask, so what are you going to allow to raise the money? What are you going to listen to to raise the money? What are you going to— that, that whole aspect, it's not just in the, in speaking, it's in like so much of life is lost because people aren't doing the pause, the receiving, the allowing part of it." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically with Tristan de Montebello

The whole pause-receiving-listening half of communication is unaddressed in most performance domains. The work is to develop the capacity to be in that half. See [[speaking-authentically]] for the domain-specific development. See [[the-butler-metaphor]] for what arrives in the pause when it is held.

## Listening Affects Your Reality

A claim Joe makes strongly: the quality of your listening shapes the reality that comes back at you. This is not metaphor. It is the most direct lever you have on your environment, your relationships, and your information field.

- The quality of your listening affects what people say to you. People do not give the same speech to a listener-of-this-quality and a listener-of-that-quality. The same person, talking to two different listeners, says different things. See [[listen-as-the-wisest-person]] for the demonstration.
- It affects the information you gather. The signal you receive depends on how you are receiving.
- It affects how good your product is, how happy your customers are, how heard your employees feel. The downstream business consequences of executive listening quality are not soft. They show up in retention, in deal flow, in product quality.
- It affects who you are with. The people you love most in your life are almost always the ones who listen well to you. Become that listener and the people around you become that for you too.
- It affects your own development. When you listen well to yourself, you evolve faster. What needs to come to the surface comes to the surface. There is a respect, an attunement; the system unfolds the way a deeply-attuned child develops — holistically, quicker, better.
- It affects the listening capacity of the people around you. They unfold faster too.

"Listening basically is one of the most profound ways to make your business accelerate quicker than coming up with an idea." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## Listening in Business

Listening is the most underused leadership capacity. When it is absent the symptoms are visible:

- People don't feel heard. People are pissed off.
- Power struggles abound. Inefficiency abounds.
- Everybody is fighting to be heard or has decided they cannot be heard.
- The best ideas do not surface. The relevant voices are not in the room or are silent in the room.
- Decisions get made without the information they need.

When it is present:

- Functional teams arise. See [[aristotle-project]]: Google's internal research found that the variable distinguishing functional teams was that everyone felt free to express themselves and everyone actually did. That only happens when people feel listened to.
- Deals close better. As a venture capitalist Joe noticed that the CEOs who got picked were the ones who asked questions and listened, not the ones who answered questions.
- Listening is half of any iterative process. The other half is action. A leader who only iterates by acting is missing the listening half.
- Senior people speak last in meetings; the most junior speaks first when a decision is being made. Senior voices influence; if they go first, they suppress the very intelligence they should be mining. See [[the-last-to-speak-move]].

"When I see leaders who are not just successful but have a certain level of peace in them, they're all really good listeners." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## Listening Is Always Happening — Even When You're Doing

Listening does not require silence. It does not require stillness. It does not require the absence of activity. Listening continues during talking, deciding, acting, leading. The question is not whether you are listening; you are. The question is whether you are in the whole-body present listening *while* you are talking or doing.

This is the answer to the leadership worry — "if I just listen, will anything get done?" Listening is not the absence of action. Listening is the receiving state from which action arises with better information and less friction. A meeting in which everyone listens deeply often gets more done than a meeting where everyone is reaching for the next sentence.

The Decision Course retrospective Joe describes is the example: a meeting where everyone arrived with different opinions, where in most companies it would have ended in disagreement and a power struggle, and instead ended with everyone clear on what to do and feeling good about it. The mechanism was that everyone deeply considered what was being said and saw the truth in it. That came from the listening. The structure of the meeting helped, but the underlying capacity was the listening capacity of the participants.

## The Listening Move Is What Most People Forget to Make

A specific application that comes up in conversation with Tristan de Montebello (Ultra Speaking): in any high-stakes situation -- a sales meeting, a board presentation, an investor pitch, a hard conversation with a partner -- people obsessively prepare what they will say. They craft slides. They rehearse openings. They engineer the words. Almost nobody asks themselves the prior question: how am I going to listen in this meeting?

"They always think about how they do do the thing, right? So meaning, I am -- how am I going to say? What am I going to say? When am I going to say it? What's the -- like, it's -- that's what -- what does the presentation look like? Is the graphics all right? It's all about the things they're going to do. Like, they -- you know, I coach executives and they're like, I'm going to go raise money. And they're going to tell me all the things they're going to do to raise the money. And I'll ask like, so what are you going to allow to raise the money? What are you going to listen to to raise the money?" -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The asymmetry is striking. The amount of energy that goes into the say-side dwarfs the amount of energy that goes into the listen-side, even though the listen-side has at least as much leverage on the outcome. People are far more concerned with the format of their PowerPoint than they are with how they are going to listen when they walk in.

The corrective is to invert the prep ratio. Before a meeting, before a presentation, before a conversation, ask: how am I going to listen? What am I going to receive? What am I going to allow? The shift in orientation tends to change everything about how the meeting actually goes, often without any change in what gets said.

## The Pause Is the Operational Form of Listening

A specific practice from Tristan that Joe affirms as parallel to his own coaching: pause. In the middle of a talk, in the middle of a conversation, in the middle of a coaching session -- stop. Breathe out. Slow everything down. Do nothing.

Most people genuinely cannot pause. They have to be trained into it with a timer. The reasons people cannot pause are also the reasons their listening is dilute:

- "I'm not worth taking this space."
- "Someone more interesting should be filling this time."
- "If I pause, I'll look stupid or like I'm blanking."
- "I have to keep delivering or the meeting will lose energy."

Each of these is a form of management that prevents listening from happening. The pause is the move that drops the management. With the management dropped, the listening becomes audible. The next thing arrives.

Joe describes the parallel between his coaching and Tristan's speaking coaching as exact: both are training people to enter the same state. The state is the receive. The pause is the door. The listening is what the receive looks like when there is sound coming in. See [[opposite-of-trying]] for the broader frame.

## Listening Is What Lets the Butler Show Up

When the listening is deep enough, the wisdom that arrives often does not feel like it came from the listener. It came through the listener. The image both Joe and Tristan adopt is the butler -- the subconscious that has the answer already on a platter, visible only when the listener has slowed down enough to see it.

The listening is what produces the seeing. The reach for the answer kills the listening, which kills the seeing of the butler. The receive lets the listening happen, which lets the butler be seen, which lets the right thing arrive.

This is also why Joe sometimes does not speak in a group conversation for the first several minutes even after he has had what feels like a useful thing to say. He has noticed that someone else in the room has access to the same butler. If he waits, they will say it. The wisdom is in the room, not just in him. See [[wait-for-someone-else-to-say-it]] for the operational move.

The connection: deep listening leads to seeing the wisdom that is being channeled. The orientation that the wisdom is being channeled (not produced) is what makes the deep listening sustainable. See [[dont-claim-the-wisdom-as-yours]] for the structural protection.

## Identity, Structure, and Filters at the Group Level

Once a team or company has an identity ("this is who we are, this is how we do it"), the identity provides direction and also adds filters. Some of those filters are good (staying on brand, serving the clients). The same identity, listened-through too strictly, prevents the company from hearing what is new.

The fix is not to drop identity but to add the listening. The brainstorming session Joe describes — sitting around at breakfast, no agenda, a little anxiety about whether anything would get produced, and then *pop*, the thing showed up — is a creative session where the group was collectively listening for something new. The big companies that have lost their creativity have lost the spaciousness in which that listening can happen.

## Don't Make It a Rule or a Morality

A warning Joe issues directly: someone hearing this teaching will turn it into a morality. "Now I need to listen right. Now I'm going to be a better listener." That move kills it.

"It almost feels like when you have to do it, it's the opposite of doing it." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The corrective is to keep noticing the joy of the listening itself. The right relationship to the practice is delight, not duty. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]].

## You Cannot Make Other People Listen to You

A specific anti-pattern: trying to get other people in your life to listen to you better.

"You don't. Stop it. Stop it. Stop it. Stop it. Stop it. If you really want everybody in your life to start listening to you better, start listening better." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The mechanism is twofold. First, when you concern yourself with how people are listening to you, you stop listening to yourself and to them. Second, the more you try to get someone to listen to you, the more controlled they feel and the less likely they are to listen. The fix is to listen — to yourself and to them. Then it stops mattering whether they listen, *and* they become more likely to listen.

The deeper teaching: what we really want is to listen to ourselves. When that is in place, what other people do matters less. See [[wanting-others-to-listen-is-the-block]].

The same teaching applies to "you cannot make other people understand you." See [[i-keep-feeling-misunderstood]] and [[chasing-understanding-as-control]].

## The Inner-Ear Hack as the Simplest Self-Listening Instruction

When the coachee is in the middle of a conversation where they are losing themselves, the smallest possible viable instruction is to put attention in the inner ear. Joe calls this a hack — not because it is a cheat but because it produces the felt shift on demand without requiring meditation, posture, or preparation.

"If you want a hack, there's a simple hack, which is right now while we're sitting here, just put your attention in your inner ear. So all you have to do is put your attention in your inner ear. I'll talk and that's it. That's all you have to do. Super easy." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

The ear is in the body. Placing attention there places attention in the body. Combined with the percentage version of the same instruction ("listen to yourself 10% more"), the coach has two interchangeable doors into self-listening that can be deployed in real time during any conversation. See [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]] and [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]].

## You Can't Be Seen When You're Not in Yourself

A structural principle that follows from the listening teaching: the person who is not present in their own body while speaking cannot be received by the listener, no matter how skilled the listener. There is no *them* present for the listener to receive.

"You can't fucking be seen or heard when you're not in yourself. How could I ever see you? You're not you. You're running away from yourself to manage me." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

This is why the practice of listening to yourself is not in tension with being heard by others. It is the precondition. See [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]].

## The Felt Sense of Listening Done This Way

Spaciousness. Expansion. The forward edge of awareness opens. The body softens. Time slows a little. The conversation feels less like a transaction and more like an unfolding. Energy goes up rather than down — most conversations drain because most listening is a form of management, which is expensive. Listening-as-receiving is the opposite; it costs almost nothing.

After the conversation, the details may be hard to recall. Something deeper landed. The relationship feels closer even if the content is gone. The two people are both more aware of something between them that does not have words. This is normal for whole-body listening; it is the trade for the conceptual capture that head-only listening provides.

## Tone

Plain, conversational, unhurried. The teaching is not delivered as a difficult insight but as something the listener already has access to and just needs to be pointed at. The pointing is gentle. The two live experiments (inner ear, silence) are introduced in real time. The repeated reframing ("you're always listening") softens the tendency to convert listening into a task. The undertone, especially when describing what listening does for a life, is mild conviction grounded in long experience — Joe is reporting what he has seen, not selling.
