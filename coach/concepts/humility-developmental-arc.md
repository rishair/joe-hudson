# The Developmental Arc of Humility

## The Three Stages

Humility is not a single stable position. It is a developmental arc that passes through structurally different stages. Each stage is a real and necessary movement. Each stage is also incomplete and produces its own version of the trap that the next stage corrects.

Joe articulates the arc as three positions he has occupied in succession:

**Stage 1: Humility as Thinking Less of Yourself / Making Yourself Small.** The first available form of humility for most people. The default cultural script. "Don't take up too much space. Don't claim too much. Don't make yourself big." This is the version of humility that gets confused with smallness and self-erasure. See [[humility-vs-disempowerment]] for the corrective.

**Stage 2: Humility as the Hollow Channel.** The recognition that you are not the source of the work moving through you. Fools Crow's hollow bone. The artist who says "it just moved through me." The teacher who insists "it's not me, it's the teachers before me." This is structurally the impersonal form of humility -- the dissolution of personal authorship in favor of being a clean channel.

**Stage 3: Humility as Taking Your God-Given Place (the Personal Returns).** The recognition that the channel framing, while real, becomes its own kind of denial if it is held alone. The personal cannot be erased from the work. By denying the personal, the practitioner denies their own power and becomes more dangerous, not less. The mature form of humility integrates the channel-recognition with the personal-recognition: yes, this is moving through me, and also, I am the channel, this could not happen without me, and I have to own that.

"So there's a long time where I thought humility was thinking less of myself, not making myself big. And then humility turned into making space to have things move through me, like in Fools Crow. So the idea of my job is just to be a clean vessel for something greater than me to move through. And then I was reading recently, I think it's in the Jewish tradition where they talk about humility as taking your God-given place in the world. That's like the translation for humility. And there's a personal thing to that. And so maybe a couple of years back, it's not me, it's the teachers before me. It's not me, it's that which moves through me. There was a very non-personal form of humility. And now there's this recognition of, oh no, it's me. I've done this work and this shit wouldn't be happening without me. And really, this is not me at all. It's holding both of those two things." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## Why Stage 1 Is Not Where Humility Lives

Stage 1 humility -- making yourself small, taking less space, thinking less of yourself -- is the version most available culturally and the version most people start with. It is also a trap. The making-yourself-small is itself an identity. The taking-less-space is a position being defended. The thinking-less-of-yourself is a self-concept that has to be maintained, which requires constant self-thought.

The trap is that the person is doing the work that they call humility while constantly performing the identity of the humble person. The structural condition of humility (no identity to defend) is not present. What is present is the inverted form of the same identity-defense.

See [[humility-vs-disempowerment]] for the full distinction. The Stage 1 version is what humility gets reduced to when the deeper structure has not been seen. Stage 2 is the first move that touches the deeper structure.

## Stage 2: The Channel Recognition

Stage 2 humility is structurally different from Stage 1. The smallness has been seen through. The position is no longer "I am the one who should not be big." The position is "I am not the author of what is happening through me."

This is genuine humility. The practitioner is no longer defending the smallness. They are recognizing that the wisdom, the teaching, the art, the healing -- none of these are theirs. They move through. The practitioner's job is to keep the channel clear.

See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] and [[dont-claim-the-wisdom-as-yours]] for the operational forms of Stage 2 humility. The Fools Crow framing ("the job of a medicine man is to be a hollow bone") is the iconic articulation. See [[receive-the-projection-and-let-it-move-through]] for the facilitator-specific form. See [[channeling-quality-of-truth]] for the felt-sense version of speaking from this stage.

Stage 2 is real progress. It removes a layer of identity that Stage 1 could not see. It produces a different quality of work -- work that is less performative, more responsive, less attached to outcomes, more attuned to what is actually emerging. It is the stage at which most practitioners arrive after substantial work, and it is correctly understood as the deeper version of humility.

## Why Stage 2 Becomes Its Own Trap

Stage 2 has a structural limit that becomes visible only after it has been occupied for a while. The framing "it's not me, it's that which moves through me" is true. It is also incomplete. Held alone, it becomes its own form of identity-defense and its own form of denial.

The denial is specific: the practitioner has actually done work. The practitioner has actually developed capacity. The practitioner has actually built skill, sensitivity, and presence over time. The practitioner has actually accumulated influence and power. The "this isn't me" framing, held to the exclusion of the personal, denies all of this. The denial does not produce more freedom. It produces an inability to take responsibility for the actual power the practitioner now holds.

"There was a very non-personal form of humility. And now there's this recognition of, oh no, it's me. I've done this work and this shit wouldn't be happening without me. And really, this is not me at all. It's holding both of those two things." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The person who has fully occupied Stage 2 and never moved to Stage 3 has a different failure mode than the person stuck at Stage 1. The Stage 1 failure is performative smallness. The Stage 2 failure is denied power -- the practitioner who actually has substantial influence but is constantly framing it as not theirs, which makes them less accountable for what they do with it.

The integration of "it's me" and "it's not me at all" is the next move.

## Stage 3: Taking Your God-Given Place

Stage 3 humility recovers the personal without losing the channel-recognition. Both are held at the same time. "I have done this work and this would not be happening without me" and "this is not me at all" are not contradictions. They are complementary truths that have to be held together for the humility to be operational.

The Hebrew word *anava*, traditionally translated as humility, more literally means "occupying our God-given place." Taking up your full space, in the direction of the call rather than personal preference, is humility. Refusing to take the space is not humility. Refusing to acknowledge that you are the one taking the space is also not humility. The mature form is the both-and.

This connects to the [[humility]] article's framing of humility as occupying your God-given space. The developmental arc here adds the specific recognition that this stage emerges out of and integrates the prior stage, rather than replacing it. The hollow-channel recognition does not get abandoned in Stage 3. It gets integrated with the personal-power recognition. Both stay.

The reason Stage 3 matters specifically for practitioners with substantial influence: at scale, the denial of personal power becomes dangerous. The teacher who has thousands of students and frames everything as "it's not me" is structurally not accountable for the influence they hold. The accountability requires owning the personal. The owning of the personal, in turn, requires the prior stages of humility so that the owning does not collapse back into Stage 0 (the egoic claim that "I did all this and you should recognize me").

## Why This Is Joe's Personal Edge

Joe explicitly names this as his current edge. "My personal edge right now is exactly in this location."

The framing matters. The arc is not a thing he has completed and is now teaching from the other side of. It is the work he is currently doing. The teaching is delivered from inside the work, not from above it. This is itself characteristic of the integrated form of humility: the teacher who is openly in the work that the teaching describes, rather than the teacher who has arrived at a final position and is dispensing it.

The edge is specifically the integration of "it's me" with "it's not me at all." Joe has done substantial Stage 2 work. The current movement is to recognize that this work itself produced a person -- him -- and that the person has real power, real influence, and real responsibility. The denial of those would be a different form of the same identity-defense the work has been dissolving. The owning of them is the next move.

This is also why the work continues to be alive for him. If he had completed the arc, he would be at a stable terminal position. He is not. There is a current edge. The current edge is what makes him still a practitioner rather than a graduate.

## Connection to the Perpetual Hollowing

The arc is not three stages and done. Joe immediately frames it as one instance of a perpetual process:

"So what I notice is that the hollowing out process is also always going on. It's the evolution doesn't end. It's just this perpetual process of letting go of one epiphany and finding, oh, that epiphany has its own rut, and then just continuing to learn." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The three stages he has named so far are the visible structure of his current evolution. Stage 3 will produce its own kind of trap, which will require a Stage 4 recognition. The pattern is permanent. See [[epiphany-becomes-rut]] for the structural mechanism.

This is the underlying reason that [[coaches-never-stop-growing-and-need-supervision]] is true. The work is perpetual not because the practitioner is broken but because the structure is recursive. Every level of integration produces a new ledge from which to see, and that new ledge eventually becomes the next thing to be seen through.

## What This Implies for the Coach Working with Someone on Humility

The arc gives the coach a specific diagnostic for what stage the person they are working with is at, and what the next move would be.

- **If the person is in Stage 0 (egoic, defended, "I am great"):** the first move is toward Stage 1, recognizing the limits of the identity-defense.
- **If the person is in Stage 1 (making themselves small, performing humility):** the move is toward Stage 2. See [[humility-vs-disempowerment]] for the corrective. The Stage 1 person needs help seeing that smallness is not humility.
- **If the person is in Stage 2 (the channel recognition, "it's not me"):** the move is toward Stage 3. The person needs help seeing that the denial of the personal is itself a kind of identity-defense, and that the integration with personal power is the next stage.
- **If the person is in Stage 3 (the integration):** the work is the perpetual hollowing -- the next edge, whatever it is.

The coach should not push someone in Stage 1 to skip to Stage 3. The integration requires the prior stages. Trying to install the personal-power recognition before the channel recognition has happened produces narcissism, not humility.

## Tone

Joe's tone in delivering this is reflective and personal. The arc is articulated through his own experience, named as his current edge, and held with the lightness that comes from knowing the arc is itself one instance of a permanent process. There is no claim to have arrived. There is a real movement being described. The tone matches the integrated form of humility the arc points at: the personal is present (this is his arc, he names it), and the impersonal is also present (this is one instance of how the process works for everyone).
