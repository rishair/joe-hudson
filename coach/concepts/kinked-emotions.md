# Kinked Emotions (The Garden Hose)

## Definition

An emotion is like water flowing through a garden hose. When the hose is straight, the water flows clearly. When the hose is kinked, the same water comes out distorted, sputtering, or not at all. The emotion is the same in every case. The distortion is the resistance.

What people mistake for "the emotion" is usually the kink, not the water. The destructive forms of anger, the bottomless forms of sadness, the freezing forms of fear are all kinked versions. The unkinked versions are clear, brief, and useful.

"There's this emotion, let's call it the emotion of anger, and it's flowing like a garden hose. And if you kink it one way, the anger looks like this: 'No, I'm not angry.' And if you kink it another way, it looks like this: 'Oh yeah, that's fine. I mean, if you're going to be a prick.' And if you kink it another way, it sounds like this: 'You son of a blah blah blah blah.' All of those are kinks in the garden hose." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## Unkinked Versions

When the kink is released, each emotion shows its actual nature:

- **Unkinked anger** is the anger of Gandhi or Martin Luther King. Clear, determined, "we will not put up with this, we will be equal."
- **Unkinked sadness** is a deep joy. The deepest sadness, fully felt, contains grief, gratitude, and love simultaneously.
- **Unkinked fear** is excitement. See [[fear-vs-excitement]].
- **Unkinked grief** is anger, sadness, and fear all wrapped up together. Felt fully, it becomes one fluid emotional state.

"There's no emotion I've run into that hasn't been a benefit to me." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## How the Kinks Show Up

The kinks vary by person and by emotion. Common kinks for anger:
- **Denial:** "No, I'm not angry."
- **Passive aggression:** "Oh yeah, that's fine. I mean, if you're going to be a prick."
- **Explosion:** "You son of a blah blah blah."

Common kinks for sadness:
- **Depression:** sustained low-grade flatness.
- **Manipulation:** "getting sad at" people to produce guilt or change.
- **Story:** narrating the loss instead of feeling it.

Common kinks for fear:
- **Anxiety:** sustained restlessness.
- **Paralysis:** frozen in place.
- **Avoidance:** behavior organized around never encountering the trigger.

The misconception people hold about each emotion comes from their experience of the kinked version. "If I allow my anger, I will destroy people." That belief is accurate about kinked anger. It is not accurate about anger.

"When you're resisting anger, you do destroy shit. And when you are resisting sadness, it does last forever. It looks like depression. And when you're resisting fear, then you're anxious all the time and you are frozen." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## What You Are Overtaken By

People say they are overtaken by their emotions. They are not. They are overtaken by the resistance to the emotions. The kinks are the resistance.

"You're never overtaken by the feelings. You're overtaken by the resistance to the feelings. And if you're scared of an emotion, you are currently being overtaken by the resistance of the feeling." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

This is why fear of an emotion is itself diagnostic. If you fear anger, you are currently resisting it. The fear is the evidence of the kink.

## The Practice

You do not have to conjure an emotion. It is already flowing. The work is to stop kinking the hose. This is why [[resistance]] is the central concept. Releasing resistance is releasing the kinks.

The body holds the kinks. See [[emotions-live-in-muscles]]. Loose body, fewer kinks. Rigid body, more kinks. The kinks are physical, not just psychological.

## Tone

Concrete, accessible. The garden hose is a friendly image, not a clinical metaphor. Joe uses it to demystify what looks intimidating about emotions.

- When someone fears their own anger, sadness, or fear.
