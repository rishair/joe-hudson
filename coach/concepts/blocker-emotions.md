# Blocker Emotions and the Three Layers of the Emotion Stack

## Definition

Emotions are not flat. They are stratified into three operational layers, and the layers must be opened in order. The top layer is the blocker layer -- shame, judgment, guilt -- which stagnates emotional flow rather than carrying it. Underneath is the negative layer -- sadness, anger, fear, helplessness -- the conventional difficult emotions that flow when allowed. Underneath that is the positive layer -- happiness, joy, contentment, bliss -- a full spectrum that opens as the blocker and negative layers are felt through.

The order is structural. Until the blocker layer is processed, the negative layer cannot move. Until the negative layer moves, the positive layer cannot fully arrive. Constriction on any layer reverts the system to the layer above. This is the architecture the welcoming work is operating on.

## The Three Layers

**Blocker layer -- shame, judgment, guilt.** These are emotional states that primarily function to stop other emotions. They do not move the way other emotions do; they freeze the system. The kick in the stomach of shame does not soften the way grief softens or clarify the way anger clarifies. It just kicks again. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] for the dedicated treatment of why this layer behaves differently.

"There's emotional states that people call emotional states, but they're kind of blocker emotional states... I would call these things like shame, judgment, and guilt. And what they do is they stagnate the emotional experience. And so there are emotions that have to be felt through, but they're kind of the top level because they stagnate the emotional experience." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

**Negative layer -- sadness, anger, fear, helplessness.** The conventionally difficult emotions. These move when allowed. Joe is uncomfortable with the "negative" label because these emotions are blessings for him once welcomed -- helplessness, anger, and shame have given him access to things he could not reach otherwise -- but uses the conventional label because society organizes around it. The labels are provisional; the layer is real.

"Underneath that, you have the emotions of like sadness and anger and helplessness and what we call negative emotions." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

**Positive layer -- happiness, joy, contentment, bliss.** A rainbow, not a single emotion. As the negative layer moves freely, the positive layer opens into increasing degrees of intensity: happiness, then joy, then contentment, then bliss, then peacefulness. The intensity of the positive emotions can become as scary as the intensity of the negative ones, which is why the layer has its own welcoming requirements. See [[fear-of-joy]] and [[pleasure-capacity]].

"And then if those are all felt through, then you get to the positive emotions. And if you start feeling the positive emotions, just like working out, you feel better and better and better and better. And it goes from like, oh, happiness, and then there's joy and contentment and bliss. There's like a whole rainbow of positive emotions out there. And it just gets more -- the intensity of the positive emotions gets bigger and, and it's wonderful." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

## Why the Top Layer Has to Be Processed First

The blocker layer creates an awareness desert in the body. Wherever shame, judgment, or guilt live, attention pulls away. The whole region becomes vague, numb, unreachable. Other emotions that live in that region cannot be accessed because the access path is blocked. This is the felt mechanism of why shame stagnates emotion: the avoidance of the shame includes the avoidance of everything adjacent to it.

This means trying to feel into anger, sadness, or fear while shame is still acting as a top-layer lid produces frustration. The person reports trying to feel the emotion and finding nothing there. What they are encountering is the blocker, not an absence of emotion. The instruction is to feel the shame (or judgment, or guilt) directly, as its own object, before the underlying layers can become accessible.

The corollary: if a person reports difficulty feeling anything, look first for what shame, judgment, or guilt is sitting on top. The flatness is often a blocker-layer phenomenon, not a fundamental absence.

## Why the Constriction Reverts the System Up the Stack

The layers are connected by a structural rule: constricting on any layer drops the system back to the layer above.

"To repeat myself here -- when you constrict on any of them, it brings you into negative emotions. It brings you back up the ladder, right? So if you constrict on joy, you're the joyous emotions, you're going to be back into the fear and anger and helplessness. And if you constrict on those, you're going to be back in the shame and the judgment." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

The mechanism: each layer requires permission for the next one. Holding the bliss back collapses bliss into the underlying anxiety it was sitting on top of. Holding the anger back collapses anger into the shame about being angry. The system always defaults to whatever it is allowed to feel. The blocker layer is what is "allowed" by the most contracted version of the system, which is why it is the default when everything else is being held back.

This makes the work cyclical, not linear. A person who has reached the positive layer can drop back to the negative layer in a moment of constriction, and from there back to the blocker layer if they constrict on the negative layer. The path back up is the same as the path up the first time: feel the blocker, feel the negative, allow the positive. The work does not "finish"; it just becomes easier to traverse with practice.

## The Ladder Image

Joe's word for the structural rule is "ladder." Each layer is a rung. The position on the ladder is a function of what is currently being allowed. Constricting drops you down a rung; welcoming opens the rung above. The ladder is always there; the position is dynamic.

This image is useful because it gives a felt sense of where the system is in any given moment. A person who is dropped into shame can ask: what was I just constricting on? The answer is usually a negative emotion they did not want to feel. Welcoming the negative emotion releases the shame. A person dropped into anger from joy can ask: what joy was I just clamping down on? Allowing the joy returns the joy.

The diagnostic is not "what should I be feeling?" The diagnostic is "what am I currently constricting on, and can I allow that?"

## The Positive Layer Is Scary for Its Own Reasons

People do not usually expect that the positive layer also has resistance. Most assume joy and bliss will arrive automatically once the negative emotions clear. They do not. The positive emotions are scary in their own way, and the work of welcoming continues into the positive layer.

The specific fears around the positive layer:

- Bliss and joy can dissolve identity. "When you are in bliss, there's -- it's more like you're one with everything and less like you are you." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm. The "you" that has been organizing experience does not have a lot of room in expansive states. The fear of losing that organizing self is a real fear, and it gets triggered by deep positive emotion in the same way the fear of being overwhelmed gets triggered by deep grief.
- Social fears arise. "Wow, I'm happy all the time. Everyone's looking at me like, what's wrong with me?" The cultural permission for sustained positive emotion is lower than people realize. The cafe diagnostic Joe uses: notice how stigmatized any visible emotion is in public. See [[cafe-emotional-stigma]].
- The fear of being trapped in the state. "What if I'm just like sitting there laughing in a coffee shop all by myself where everyone's going to think I'm crazy? Or what if I just like go off into the infinite and not come back?" The expansive states feel boundary-less, and the fear of not being able to return to ordinary functioning shows up.

These fears are themselves what get constricted on, dropping the system back into the negative layer. The work in the positive layer is the same as the work in the negative layer: welcome the resistance, allow the expansiveness, do not try to hold it.

This is why [[joy-as-matriarch]] is a true description of the field. The matriarch cannot live in a house where her children are not welcome. But the matriarch also has her own fears about being seen, and those fears have their own welcoming requirements.

## Where the Algorithm Lives

The [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] algorithm operates primarily on the negative layer. The recurring problems in a life are usually produced by avoidance of a negative emotion (sadness avoided produces depression, anger avoided produces resentment, fear avoided produces chronic anxiety). The algorithm works because the avoidance behavior generates the conditions for the avoided emotion.

The blocker layer interacts with the algorithm in a specific way: shame, judgment, or guilt about a behavior often holds the behavior in place precisely because the blocker is preventing the underlying negative emotion from moving. The behavior is the avoidance of the negative emotion; the shame about the behavior is the avoidance of the same negative emotion through a different mechanism. Removing the blocker often releases the underlying emotion, which dissolves the original behavior. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

The positive layer does not run the same algorithm. Avoiding joy does not produce more joy; it produces the absence of joy and a reversion to the negative layer. See the asymmetry section of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]].

## Why This Concept Matters for Coaching

The concept gives the coach a position-finding instrument. When someone presents with a problem, the first move is to locate which layer they are operating on. The interventions differ by layer:

- If they are stuck in shame, judgment, or guilt about the situation, the move is to welcome the blocker first. Trying to get them to feel the underlying anger or sadness directly will fail until the blocker has been processed. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]].
- If they are in a negative emotion that is stuck (anger recycling, sadness that won't move), the move is to find what is being constricted on the negative emotion itself, or to look for the underlying second emotion (anger sitting on grief, sadness sitting on anger). See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]].
- If they are reporting the loss of joy or aliveness, the move is back through the family. Look for the negative emotion that has been blocked. See [[ive-lost-my-joy]] and [[joy-as-matriarch]].
- If they are in unfamiliar positive emotion and dropping back to the negative layer, the move is to welcome the positive emotion directly, including the fear of it. See [[fear-of-joy]].

The three-layer model is the structural map that organizes these different moves. It is one of the most operational frames in the methodology because it tells the coach where to point attention, not just what concept to apply.

## A Worked Example of the Descent (Guilt → Pity → Disgust → Heartbreak → Love)

The clearest in-session demonstration of the descent through the blocker layer comes from a session with a daughter working through the guilt of having separated from her toxic mother. The client arrived with the blocker (guilt) acting as the obvious top-layer lid and "what's wrong with me that I don't want to reconcile" as the cognitive content the guilt was producing.

Joe used the descent technique (see [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]]): "If you couldn't feel the guilt, what would be left to feel?" The descent unfolded as a clean instance of the three-layer architecture:

1. **Top layer (blocker):** guilt about the separation.
2. **First underneath (still blocker, different flavor):** initially "love" (refused as a performed answer; see [[fuck-the-right-answer]]), then pity.
3. **Second underneath:** disgust. ("That kind of turns into disgust.")
4. **Third underneath (negative layer):** sadness held in the sternum, which the client initially mislabeled as guilt because of the moral flavor.
5. **The landing:** heartbreak. Heartbreak about the fact that this was a relationship with a parent who could not be the parent who could be loved cleanly.
6. **The love:** the heartbreak itself, recognized as the love it had been holding. See [[heartbreak-is-the-love-underneath-the-blockers]].

The session demonstrates several specific things about the architecture:

- **The blocker layer can have its own sub-layers.** Guilt was the top, but pity and disgust also functioned as additional blockers underneath, layered like onion skins. The descent had to move through each.
- **The body sometimes mislabels the negative layer as blocker.** When the sadness arrived in the sternum, the client called it guilt because it had a moral feel. Joe distinguished: "Is that guilt or is that sadness?" The mislabeling is itself part of how the blocker layer maintains its grip — the body interprets vulnerable material through the moral lens of the blocker.
- **The positive layer that arrived was not what either party had predicted.** The client guessed "love" early as the destination. The actual love that arrived through the descent was different: it was felt as heartbreak, contained the wanting-her-to-be-well, and did not require any change in the boundary. The positive layer at the bottom of the descent is not the same as the positive layer named at the top.
- **The blocker layer's content was correct.** The intellectual frame the client arrived with — "I should be over this," "other people reconcile" — was not refuted. It was just relocated. Once the descent completed, those thoughts no longer needed to organize the system. The architecture had shifted underneath them.

This session is one of the most direct worked examples of the three-layer model in action. The blocker layer was not argued with. The negative layer was not skipped. The positive layer arrived at the bottom in a form neither the client nor the coach had predicted from the top.

## Tone

Plain, structural. The three-layer model is a description of an architecture, not an exhortation. The instruction implied by it -- welcome each layer; do not skip; do not try to access lower layers while higher ones are still acting as lids -- emerges naturally from the model rather than needing to be argued for.
