# Upright Undefended Love

## Definition

A stance that is loving and empowered at the same time. Not submission (falling forward into the other, losing yourself). Not fight (defending against the other, walling off). Not distance (managing the relationship from far enough away that it cannot land). Upright means weight evenly distributed, neither falling back nor falling forward. Undefended means no wall. Love means the heart is open to the person who is there.

"That empowered upright non-falling-forward love, non-submissive love." -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

This is the third option to the [[fight-or-roll-over-binary]]. People raised to either fight or submit to authority figures have rehearsed those two stances and not this one. Locating it takes felt practice.

## In Practice

The stance has a specific somatic signature. The body is upright, neither falling forward (which the person often discovers is what love feels like to them, but is actually a form of submission) nor falling back (which is defense, distance, fight prep). The heart is open. The eyes are present. The breath is moving.

"It feels really alive. It also feels almost like so alive that also feels scary." -- (person in Father Figures and Authority describing the stance once located)

The aliveness is the diagnostic. Submission feels like collapse. Fight feels like tightness. Distance feels like withdrawal. Upright undefended love feels alive, present, often scary in its intensity.

## What People Confuse It With

**Falling forward.** Many people experience love as a leaning toward the other, a giving, a moving into. This is often submission in love's costume. The body collapses into the other. The self shrinks to accommodate.

"Love feels a lot like falling forward." -- (person in Father Figures and Authority, naming the confusion)

If love requires you to give up your ground, it is not upright. It is submission. The work is to find love that does not require ground-giving.

**Balanced distance.** Some people, having learned that falling forward costs them, settle for balance through distance. They keep the other at a distance and call it boundary. The distance is still fight, just quieter.

"I'm going to keep you at a distance. I will be over here and you're gonna be over there." -- (person in Father Figures and Authority, recognizing the distance as still being fight)

If the love requires distance to remain safe, the love has not arrived. The defense is still operating.

**Performed openness.** Some people learn the words and posture of openness without the felt state. They say loving things, hold open body language, and remain internally walled. The other person feels the absence.

## How to Find It

The stance is usually located by feeling the opposite of fight and the opposite of submission and discovering what remains. Joe's instruction:

"How would it be to try it again and feel the feeling of fight or rolling over and then letting your body feel the exact opposite of those emotions?" -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

Not "feel love." Not "be empowered." Feel both directions of the binary, then find the body's answer to the negation of both. What emerges is usually felt as aliveness, sometimes as scary aliveness.

For people whose love defaults to falling forward, finding the upright version requires literally noticing the somatic forward-lean and choosing to stand upright while keeping the heart open. The combination is often unfamiliar enough to feel like new territory.

## Connection to Empowerment

Upright undefended love is the somatic version of [[love-requires-empowerment]]. The principle says love requires empowerment. The somatic instruction is to be upright (empowered) while remaining undefended (loving). One without the other is incomplete.

Empowered without undefended is hard, brittle, controlling. Undefended without empowered is submissive, collapsing, dissolved. The combination is the stance Joe is pointing at.

## In Coaching the Authority Dynamic

When working with someone who freezes around authority figures, upright undefended love is often the third option that breaks the [[fight-or-roll-over-binary]]. The person has rehearsed fight (rebellion at work, distance from the boss) and roll-over (compliance, performance, suppression). The new move is to be upright with the boss, undefended toward the boss, loving toward the boss.

This is often tested with [[become-the-authority-figure]]: Joe steps into the role and the person practices the new stance live. The role-play is the rehearsal.

## What This Does Not Mean

It does not mean welcoming abuse. It does not mean having no boundaries. It does not mean smiling through painful interactions. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]: boundaries can be drawn from the upright undefended state. The state and the boundary are compatible.

It also does not mean liking the authority figure. The person in the source session did not have to like her boss to be upright and undefended with her boss. She had to be willing to let her actual response (including hurt, including disagreement) move through her without collapsing or fighting.

"And it did hurt. Yeah. And it is absolutely fine that you don't see the way it hurt. It's okay." -- (person practicing the stance in Father Figures and Authority)

This is the upright undefended response. The hurt is felt. The other's failure to see the hurt is welcomed. The person remains upright.
