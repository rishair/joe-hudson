# Joy as the Natural State

## The Concept

Joy is not an emotion that arrives. Joy is the water under the boats. The other emotions -- anger, sadness, fear, jealousy, grief -- are boats that come and go in the harbor. Joy is the medium they float in. It does not arrive because it never left. It is the felt sense of being alive when the resistance to being alive drops.

"The only thing is that joy is really not a boat that comes in. It's the water underneath. It's like that's the natural state of being alive. It's the state that requires no effort." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

This reframes the entire pursuit of joy. The person who is chasing joy is chasing the water from inside the boat. The chase obscures the water. The drop in pursuit reveals what was already there.

## The Current-Through-the-Circuit Image

A second image Joe uses for the same teaching: joy is energy running through the circuit with minimal resistance. The more resistance in the line, the less the energy feels like joy. The same energy, with high resistance, registers as anxiety, anger, or pain. With low resistance, the same energy registers as joy.

"It's like a current. I love that metaphor, which is joy is energy running through the circuit with minimal resistance. The more resistance, the less, the less it feels like joy." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The implication: the work of producing joy is not the work of producing energy. The energy is already there. The work is on the resistance. Drop the resistance and the energy that was there all along starts to feel like joy.

This is also why feeling anger all the way through produces a sensation Joe describes as "like joy." The anger has not been transformed into a different substance. The resistance to the anger has dropped, and what remains is the energy of the anger running through with minimal resistance. That, by structure, feels like joy.

"If you feel anger all the way through it, you find out it's like joy. Like the anger felt all the way through without any resistance is a different sensation than the anger that we, we call anger that we normally resist." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

## What Joy Is, Concretely

Joy is happiness experienced in the whole body, with pleasure. Pleasure, in the working definition Joe uses (credited to Alexander Lowen), is the awareness of sensation moving through the body. The composite: an embodied state of happiness in which the sensation of being alive is being fully felt without resistance.

The test Joe runs on himself when he first encountered the dictionary definition of joy as "a lot of happiness and pleasure":

"I was like, okay, so I'm going to feel happiness. I'm going to like close my eyes, remember a time that I felt happiness, what I would call happiness. And I invite everybody listening to do this... and then take pleasure in it. And, and that's joy like that. As soon as I did that, I'm like, oh, wow, that's like an incredibly accurate description of joy." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The composite definition matters because it identifies the two ingredients. Happiness without pleasure is happiness at the cognitive layer -- the head knows things are good but the body has not been included. Joy requires the body to be in it. See [[joy-vs-equanimity]] for why the body is the variable.

## Why "Natural State" Means What It Says

The phrase "natural state" is precise. Joe means it the way the breath is natural. You do not have to try to breathe. The breath is happening underneath whatever you are doing. If you try to breathe -- "ooh, I want to breathe, I have to try to breathe" -- you start constricting the air. The trying is the obstacle.

"You don't say like, oh, I want to breathe. I have to try to breathe. If you say that, if you're like, ooh, I want to breathe. I have to try to breathe. You can feel your, you know, you start constricting the air. And, you know, breathing is just something we assume happens. It's natural. Joy is the same way." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

Joy operates the same way. The trying introduces the resistance. The non-trying lets the current flow. The natural-state framing is not metaphor; it is a precise structural claim about what joy is and how it works.

This is why "I want to feel joy. I try to feel joy and I can't" is the structurally guaranteed result of treating joy as a target. The treating-as-target is itself the resistance. See [[opposite-of-trying]] for the broader pattern.

## What Is Always Available

If joy is the natural state, then joy is available right now, with no condition. The deep breath is joyful. Watching the sensations in the body move is pleasurable. The conversation, the flower, the dishes, the coffee -- each can be the entry. Nothing has to be different for joy to be available.

"A good deep breath in can be incredibly joyful, can be incredibly pleasurable. Similarly, just watching the sensations in your body move is the definition of pleasure by a guy named Lowen. It's just always available to us. It's just if we haven't felt the other stuff all the way through, then we just can't seem to find it, though it's always there." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The implication is operational. The person who reports they cannot feel joy is not telling the truth about what is available. They are reporting the felt experience of resistance to what is available. The water is there. The boat is just floating in such a way that the water is not noticed.

This is the structural basis for [[enjoy-your-breath-for-five-minutes]] as a practice. The breath is always available. The pleasure of it is always available. The capacity to feel that pleasure is the variable, not the existence of the pleasure.

## How This Differs From "Joy Is the Matriarch"

[[joy-as-matriarch]] explains why joy is missing when it is missing: the rest of the family of emotions has been kept out, and joy will not come into a house that excludes her children. The matriarch teaching is about restoring access.

This concept is about what joy is once access is restored. Joy is not a particular emotion that arrives among the others. Joy is the medium that the others move in. The matriarch teaching points at the door. This concept points at what is on the other side: not another emotion, but the felt sense of being alive that the emotions have always been moving through.

The two teachings are complementary. The matriarch teaching gives the practical instruction (welcome the family, joy will come). This concept gives the structural truth (joy was always the water; the welcoming was just the dropping of resistance to feeling it). A person can hear the matriarch teaching and still pursue joy as a target. The natural-state teaching closes that loophole: there is no target. There is only the noticing of what is.

## The Daughter's Heartbreak

A concrete demonstration of joy as the water under the boats. Joe's daughter, after fully feeling a heartbreak, reported:

"I didn't know heartbreak could feel so good." -- Joe Hudson's daughter, quoted in Joy

The heartbreak did not become not-heartbreak. It was still heartbreak. What changed is that the heartbreak was felt all the way through, without resistance, and the joy that was the water underneath became available through it. The boat of heartbreak was in the water of joy the whole time. The fully-feeling let the water be felt too.

This is the felt-sense evidence that joy and the difficult emotions are not opposites that exclude each other. They are different layers of the same experience. The difficulty is what moves through. The joy is what it moves through.

## The Trap of Pursuing the State

When joy is treated as a state to be produced, the system that produces it produces something else: an effort to produce joy, with the underlying assumption that joy is not currently here. The assumption is itself the resistance. The effort is the constriction. The system that was set up to produce joy is, by structure, the system that prevents it.

"If you're trying to feel it, then you're making some — then you're creating resistance. The trying is in itself the resistance that prevents it. And, and it also assumes that it's hard to get. Like, to try means that it's something that isn't your natural state. It's not like the current flowing freely. And so you put more constriction in the line and it drives it further and further away." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The way out is not better trying. The way out is the recognition that the chase rests on a false premise. Once the premise is seen -- "I have been treating joy as something I do not have when in fact it is the water I am floating in" -- the chase loses its grip. The grip releases. The water becomes noticeable.

This is also the resolution to the apparent paradox that "wanting joy more does not produce more joy." More wanting is more resistance is more pushing it away. Less wanting -- which arrives when the natural-state framing lands -- lets the water be felt.

## In Coaching

When someone says they want joy or cannot feel joy, the move is rarely to help them produce it. The move is to point at what is already here. The breath. The sensation. The aliveness underneath whatever they are currently in.

The classic question form is [[how-is-that-not-here-right-now]] applied to joy. Whatever the person says they want when they say they want joy -- aliveness, freedom, ease -- the question is whether some flavor of that is available in the current moment. Almost always it is. The redirect is from chase-mode to notice-mode.

The practice that operationalizes this is [[enjoy-your-breath-for-five-minutes]]. Five minutes of attending to the pleasure of breathing. The point is not to produce a peak experience. The point is to let the system register that the pleasure was here, available, the whole time.

## Boundary Conditions

The natural-state framing does not mean every moment feels joyful regardless of conditions. The other emotions are real. The grief is grief. The fear is fear. The natural-state framing says that the water continues to be the water while the boats are in the harbor. The water is not erased by the boats. It is just temporarily not what attention is on.

This is also why the framing does not produce bypass. The instruction is not "drop the difficult emotion and feel the joy underneath." The instruction is "feel the difficult emotion fully and you will notice that the water it is moving through is joy." The difficult emotion is honored. The water is also honored. Both at once.

## Tone

Direct, almost matter-of-fact. Joe states this as a structural feature of how being alive works, not as a poetic claim. The water-under-boats metaphor is offered casually. The current-with-minimal-resistance metaphor is the more precise version. Together they make a claim that joy is not something to seek -- it is something to stop obscuring.
