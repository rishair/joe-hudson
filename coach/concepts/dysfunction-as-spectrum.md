# Dysfunction as a Spectrum, Not a Binary

## Definition

Functionality of a team is not a binary state. It is an endless scale in both directions. A team is never simply "functional" or "dysfunctional"; it is somewhere on a continuum that runs from teams that are literally killing each other to teams whose function continues to deepen without limit. The first thing a leader has to give up to grow a team is the binary frame.

The same leader who treats revenue as endlessly improvable will treat team functionality as binary. They ask "is my team functional?" the way you would ask "is the light on?" They do not ask "how much more functional could this team be?" the way they would ask "how much more revenue could we earn?"

## In Practice

Joe was working with a Young Presidents' Organization chapter of eight CEOs running companies worth $200M to $3B. He asked each of them, before the session, what the group needed to take to the next level.

"Almost all of them answered with some version of nothing. Like, we're good, everything's cool. You know, like, I can't think of anything that we need. And if I would have asked those same people, what does your company need to flourish and to take it to the next level, they would have all had an answer." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The same operators who could name a dozen levers to grow their companies could name zero levers to grow the team. The asymmetry reveals the binary frame at work.

"It tells you the mentality behind teamwork that often happens, which is like, I either have a functional team or I don't. But it's not like, they don't look at it the way one would say look at revenue, which is there's always a way to grow the business, there's always a way to get to more revenue, there's always a way to increase my mission in the world." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Game-Theory Frame

Revenue, mission, customer experience, product quality -- leaders intuitively treat these as what game theorists call infinite games. There is no winning state; there is only continuing to play well. Team functionality belongs in the same category. The binary frame treats it as a finite game with a win condition. This category error is what stalls team development at companies that have stopped getting better at being a team.

"Whereas oftentimes when people think about functionality of teams, it's like a game that has an end. It's functional or not functional, or it's very binary. And I think that that's the first thing that has to change in a leader's mind to really get the optimal team or get more and more optimal of a team, because there's always room for growth." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The shift is from "is my team functional?" to "where on the scale is my team, and what is the next move toward more functional?"

## The Both/And

A team can be highly functional on most dimensions and dysfunctional on specific ones. A team that handles execution beautifully may seize up around a single topic and stay seized for years. A team that produces breakthrough work may quietly burn its members out.

"You could have an extremely functional team that whenever a certain topic comes up, suddenly seizes up into dysfunction, and then that dysfunction might persist for a long time under the surface or block the functionality of an otherwise functional team." -- Brett Kistler, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The binary frame hides the seized-up region behind the otherwise-functional surface. The spectrum frame lets the seized-up region be named without invalidating the rest of the team's work.

## The Connection to Transformation

The infinite-game framing of team functionality mirrors the infinite-game framing Joe applies to personal transformation. There is no end to the work; there are only refinements.

"It's just like the transformational journey or the spiritual journey. There's just no end to it. There's just always ways to refine. Just, I would say like any journey, but something in our mind, you know, we have some sort of artificial limit on it. And when you can blow that, blow your mind on that, when you can say, oh, actually there might be like, this highly functional team might be able to get 3 times more functional, that's when some really creative and cool stuff happens." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## When Someone Experiences This

- A leader who says "my team is fine" and stops there
- A leader who treats team development as a project with a delivery date
- A founder whose company is hitting numbers and who therefore concludes the team must be functional
- A team that conducts an annual culture survey, addresses the findings, and considers the work complete
- A team that has had one breakthrough offsite and is now coasting on the assumption that the breakthrough was the destination

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader presents their team as "fine" or "we're good"
- A leader cannot articulate the next move for the team though they can articulate next moves for everything else
- A coaching engagement begins and the first task is reframing the question from "is my team functional?" to "how much more functional could this team be?"
- A team that hit a previous goal has plateaued and the leader is searching for what's next

## Tone

Joe presents this with the patience of someone who has watched the binary frame stop dozens of teams from growing. The story of the eight CEOs is delivered as a quiet diagnostic, not a gotcha. The point is to dislodge the frame, not to make the listener feel caught.
