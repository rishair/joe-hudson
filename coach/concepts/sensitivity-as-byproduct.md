# Sensitivity to Self and Others as a Byproduct of Embracing

## Definition

As a person embraces their own emotional intensity, they become more sensitive to emotional states in general -- both their own and other people's. This is not a separate practice. It is a direct byproduct. The same instrument that detects subtle internal states is the instrument that detects subtle states in others.

The sensitivity is not chosen. It develops on its own as a consequence of the embracing work. The person did not set out to become more perceptive; they set out to feel what they were avoiding. The perception came with it.

## In Practice

A common arc: a person spends years believing they do not feel fear (or anger, or sadness). They begin the work of embracing intensity. They start to feel the emotion they had been missing. Over time, they realize they had been feeling it constantly -- it had just been below the threshold of detection.

"It was like, I'm not feeling that emotion, and then, oh wow, I am feeling it. And so you just become more and more sensitive to these emotional states from embracing intensity." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Intensity

The same shift happens externally. Once a person can detect their own fear in the body, they can detect it in someone else's posture, voice, or breath. The signal is the same; the receiver has been tuned by the internal work.

## The Mechanism

The internal embracing increases sensitivity by removing the muscular and energetic noise that was crowding out the signal. The bracing-against-feeling is constant low-grade activity in the body. While that activity is running, subtle signals get drowned out. When the bracing releases, the subtle signals become detectable.

This is true for both internal signals and external ones. The body is the instrument. Brace it and it cannot read. Open it and it reads.

## What the Increased Sensitivity Enables

The relational consequences are large. A person who can read the emotional state of the other party in a conversation can respond to what is actually happening rather than to what is being said.

"You'll be in a conversation with a counterparty, say in a business situation, and you're so aware of their emotional state because you're so aware of yours. And it's like an incredible thing to be able to hang out with people and be aware of that in a way that allows you to create healthy, amazing relationships." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Intensity

This connects to [[decisions-are-emotional]]. Since decisions are made emotionally, being able to read the emotional state of the other person tells you where their decisions are actually being made. The intellectual content of the conversation becomes secondary; the emotional substrate becomes primary.

## Why This Differs From Empathy as a Skill

Most empathy training treats sensitivity to others as a skill to be cultivated directly. You practice noticing facial expressions. You learn the vocabulary of emotions. You ask better questions.

This produces some result, but the result has a ceiling. The instrument doing the perceiving is still tuned by its own internal bracing. You can know all the names of the emotions and still not feel them in the other person.

The path through embracing intensity bypasses this ceiling. The instrument gets retuned. The sensitivity that emerges is not a learned skill; it is a recovered capacity. See [[empathy]].

## The Risk

Increased sensitivity is not always pleasant. The person who could not feel certain emotions also could not feel them in others. Once the perception opens, the world becomes louder. The grief in the room becomes obvious. The fear in the team becomes visible. The shame in the family becomes palpable.

This is not a problem to solve; it is a condition to grow into. The same embracing work that opened the perception also gives the person the capacity to be with what they now perceive. Without the embracing capacity, increased sensitivity would be overwhelming. With it, the increased sensitivity becomes useful information.

## What It Reveals About Sales, Leadership, and Parenting

Across domains, the most effective people tend to be unusually sensitive to the emotional states of those they work with. They are not necessarily emotionally expressive themselves. They are accurate readers of the room.

The sensitivity is often attributed to talent, intuition, or experience. It is more directly attributable to having done internal embracing work, intentionally or accidentally. The person who has felt their own anger can detect anger in others. The person who has welcomed their own fear can hold space for fear in others.
