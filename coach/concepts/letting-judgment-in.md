# Letting Judgment In

## Definition

The common image of strength is letting judgment "roll off your back." This is protection, not vulnerability. The vulnerable alternative is letting judgment in and letting it break your heart.

This does not mean groveling or agreeing with the judgment. It means feeling the pain of being judged rather than deflecting it. The distinction is between genuine imperviousness (where judgment genuinely does not land, which is rare and cannot be forced) and performed imperviousness (where you pretend not to care while suppressing the sting).

"If you're trying to be patient, you're not patient." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## In Practice

When someone judges you, there is discomfort underneath. The reflex is to manage that discomfort: dismiss the person, look down on them, puff up, counterattack. The practice is instead to feel the discomfort fully.

"It's letting it break your heart. It doesn't mean that you have to grovel at the floor for the person, which immediately is what people's brains go to. Because one of the greatest strengths that most people don't have is the ability to actually just feel their emotions. They're just constantly trying to manage them because they're really scared shitless of their emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

Joe describes a personal practice of about six weeks where he deliberately let judgment break his heart instead of defending against it. He cried in unexpected places. After moving through this, most judgment stopped registering at all. The heartbreak provided freedom, not weakness.

"Now when people judge me, there's just like no, very seldom a reaction. And that's what the heartbreak provides. It provides you the courage to love more deeply." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## What Judgment Reveals

People who judge others are avoiding their own emotions. If you feel in your body anytime you are judging someone, you can notice that the judgment is holding a feeling at bay. There is an exercise where people state their judgment of someone, then identify the feelings underneath. When someone judges you and you let it in, you can often see that their judgment is about themselves, not about you.

"What you notice is that what people are judging you for is really not about you, it's about what they judge themselves for." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## Connection to Confession and Healing

Letting judgment in is the same mechanism that makes confession (in Catholic tradition) and making amends (in AA) effective. Vulnerably admitting what you have done and seeing that you can still be loved inside of it dissolves shame. If those things stay hidden, shame stays in the closet.

"If we don't share those things, there's no way that the shame can come out of the closet and be seen and be loved." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is defended against feedback or criticism.
- When someone's habitual response to judgment is counterattack or dismissal.
- When someone asks how to "not care what others think." The path is through, not around.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Over time, the fear of judgment loses its charge. Not through avoidance but through completion.
- Connects to [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: the pain of judgment is often a childhood wound (being seen as wrong) that was never fully felt.
- Connects to [[vulnerability]]: letting judgment in is one of the most advanced forms of vulnerability practice.
