# The Want

## Definition

The want is the impulse that animates action. It is what moves a crawler to toddle, a toddler to walk, a walker to run. It is the same impulse that pulls an adult toward deeper intimacy, expanded consciousness, more love. If allowed, it leads all the way to freedom.

"The want is just that very simple impulse that's moving us, that moves us to have a closer relationship with our loved ones. It is a constant pull that leads us all the way down the developmental line. If we allow it, it'll take us all the way to freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Want as Impulse, Want as Emotion

The want has two faces. Both are real. Recognizing them as one thing in two forms is what allows the want to be worked with.

**Want as impulse:** the felt sense of "I want to move now, I want to say this, I want to make this." If the impulse meets no friction, it just becomes action. Walking to the bathroom does not produce an experience of wanting. The impulse is there but is not registered as a feeling.

**Want as emotion:** the moment the impulse meets friction (internal or external), the want becomes a feeling. It is then an emotion like anger or sadness: something to feel through the body, not something to solve.

"There is the impulse side of the wanting, and then there is also the emotional side of the wanting. I'm just gonna walk to the bathroom right now, and there's no experience of wanting in that process because there's no friction met. But as soon as that impulse meets any level of friction, then there's this experience of wanting." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## The Felt Texture of Wanting

When the want is allowed as a feeling, without trying to figure out how to get the wanted thing, it is one of the most pleasant emotional states available. Close to love.

"To allow a desire deeply inside of you, I think it's why so many of the Sufi poets, they talk about desire in this way that they just love desire. So this longing, because that longing is so close to love, it's so close to that expansive acceptance of everything." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

This is why mystic traditions across cultures speak of longing as a spiritual path. The Sufis and the tantric traditions are pointing at the same thing: when wanting is allowed as a pure feeling, it opens into love.

## Wants Are Somatically Expansive

A clean want feels expansive in the body. The shoulders soften, the chest opens, the breath deepens. A craving (see [[wanting-vs-craving]]) does the opposite: it contracts, tightens, grips.

This is the diagnostic. Two people can describe wanting the same thing -- a piece of cake, a relationship, a million dollars -- but the body shows whether the want is clean or whether craving has overlaid it.

## The Want Is Always There

The want is so woven into human nature that almost every sentence a person speaks contains one. The experiment: after each sentence, name what you wanted to get out of saying it. There is always a want underneath.

"Right now I want to have you guys understand what I'm saying. Right now I want you to taste the deep pleasure of wanting. So there's always this subconscious want behind almost every sense we have. It is such a part of our human nature, we cannot get away from it. All we can do is own it, or we can sublimate it. And should is just a way of sublimating it." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

There is no person without wants. There are only people who have been taught to hide them, deny them, or convert them into shoulds.

## Why Wanting Is Scary

Wants are intimate. They sit at the core. When a want is rejected -- when the person someone asked out said no, when the dream the family laughed at -- the rejection lands at the core. Most people learn early to hide their wants to avoid that specific pain.

"There's a pain that we feel from being rejected in our wants. It's like a deep level of rejection. So we're all kind of school kids that got deeply rejected when we asked someone out on a date. And so we're like hesitant to do it again because our wants are this deeply intimate thing." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The fear is rarely about not getting the thing. Most people have many wants that did not get met and survived just fine. The fear is about the exposure of having shown the want and being rejected for it.

## How to Work With It

- **As impulse:** notice it, follow it before the should intercepts. See [[listening-to-impulse]].
- **As emotion:** feel it fully, without rushing to satisfy or to suppress. See [[feeling-the-want-as-emotion]].
- **As a sequence:** follow the want home; it deepens. The first want often opens onto a more accurate one underneath. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]] and [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]].
- **As a need:** trace the surface want to the universal need it serves. See [[need-behind-the-want]].

## The Want at the Organizational Level

The same principle that operates inside a person operates inside a group of people. Wants that are not spoken do not disappear. They route through whatever channels are available. In organizations, the available channels are political: gossip, factional alignment, passive resistance, side-conversations after meetings.

A team's wants are the union of every individual team member's wants. When most of those wants are unspoken, the team has nowhere to put them except the political channel. See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] for the full mechanism.

This means the work of owning wants is not only personal. It is organizational. A leader who hides their wants installs a particular kind of dysfunction in their team. A team that hides its wants from the leader installs a different kind. The remedy is the same in both directions: the wants get spoken.

The leadership form of the principle: when the leader says exactly what they want (even uncomfortable wants like "I want to make this call myself"), the team relaxes. The clarity is a gift. See [[tell-them-like-an-asshole]] for the specific move that makes this visible.
