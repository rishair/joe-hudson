# Obligation as a Form of Management (Pushes Things Away)

## Definition

Obligation is, in its essence, a form of management. When you act from obligation toward a thing — a person, a state of mind, money, your own behavior — you are attempting to manage the thing into a particular shape. The management is not perceived from the inside as management; it is perceived as duty, love, or responsibility. But functionally it is a control move.

What things do under management is what humans do under management: resist, retreat, decline.

"Obligation is in its essence a form of management. So there's not only a not letting it in, there's a pushing away of it, right? Things don't want to be managed." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Counter-Example That Reveals the Mechanism

The mechanism becomes obvious by inversion. Joe's example: nobody has ever said "I really love my wife because she manages me." The phrase is absurd on its face. Yet people describe behaviors toward partners, money, or work that are structurally the same as management, and call it love or responsibility.

A good manager is someone who gives autonomy and appreciation. That is the entire definition of a manager that people actually like. The moment management adds a controlling shape — "do this so that this outcome happens" — it falls into the obligation register. The same is true in any relationship.

"You might say, oh, they're a good manager. But what that means is that they give you autonomy and appreciation. That's what a good manager — if I talk to them and they're like, I really love my manager, I'm like, oh, tell me about the autonomy. Tell me about the appreciation, because that's usually, not always, but usually what's there. But nobody really wants to be managed." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## How It Manifests in Relationships

The obligation-as-management pattern in relationships is rarely identified as management by the person doing it. The forms it takes are familiar:

- Buying someone something so that they love you (managing their feelings toward you)
- Taking care of someone so they are not in a bad mood when you get home (managing their state)
- Reassuring a partner's anxiety to make it stop (managing their anxiety)
- Walking on eggshells to prevent a trigger (managing their reactivity)
- Saying the right thing at the right time to be the good partner (managing their perception of you)

Each of these is performed under the felt label of love, care, or being a good partner. Each is structurally a management move. Each produces the predictable result: the thing being managed (love, peace, perception) gets pushed further away.

"I feel obligated to buy you something so that you love me. So I'm trying to manage your love for me, or I feel obligated to take care of you and make you happy so that you're not in a bad mood when I get home. Or I feel obligated... where it's like, it's going to be good. We're all okay. We're going to get like managing the person's anxiety out of a sense of obligation. Because they didn't want to have to sit with the person's anxiety. But all that does is increase the anxiety." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## How It Manifests Toward Money

Money is the cleanest case for showing the push-away. A person who feels obligated to make money does not love money. Without love for money, no real movement happens toward it. The person may say they want money, but they cannot pursue what they cannot love.

"If somebody feels like they have an obligation to make money, it doesn't allow you to love money... since you can't love the money, then like, why do you want it in your life? Like, if money is this oppressive obligation, like, that doesn't really motivate you to make it. Like, you might say, oh my God, I want money, I want money. But you're not going to actually make it if it's an oppressive obligation thing that cuts off your love. Like, what? Like, we're not wired to feel obligated and cut off from love. That's not like, that's not our wiring. So, yeah. So it's inadvertently pushing money away." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

The push-away here is psychological, not magical. It operates through the everyday choices that follow from felt obligation: the avoidance, the procrastination, the half-effort, the resentment that makes you reject opportunities that come with strings. The aggregate effect is the thing not arriving. See [[expensive-to-be-poor]].

## How It Manifests Toward Work

Joe's clearest field observation: the highest-performing entrepreneurs do not relate to their business as an obligation. The middle and lower band do.

"And jobs, one of the places that I see it the most is with managers or owners, and they just feel like the sense of obligation, like, oh, I have to do this for this person. I have to do this for the customers. I have my boss that I have to do this for. I have the board that I have to do this for. And it just takes their love of the business away. And when you look at the entrepreneurs that are just wildly successful, one of the things I notice about it is they don't have that sense of obligation. They have a sense of like, we're going to get this done and we're doing this, but they don't feel — there's a feeling of I get to, I want to, I want to build this business. There's not this feeling of I have to keep the customers happy." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

This is also why obligation-driven work produces less resilience. When the inevitable rough patch hits, the obligated worker quits because there was no love sustaining them. The wanting worker stays because the love is the fuel.

## The Symmetry: Not Letting In + Pushing Away

The principle has two simultaneous motions that look like one:

1. **Not letting it in.** The closed body cannot receive what is being offered. Love offered to an obligated body bounces off.
2. **Pushing it away.** The management itself signals to the thing being managed that it should resist. People sense being managed and retreat.

These look like the same move but they are mechanically distinct. The closed body is a receiving failure; the management is an active output failure. Together they produce a feedback loop in which the obligated person is convinced they are doing everything right while the thing they want keeps slipping away.

"Yeah, it oppresses you... There's not only a not letting it in, there's a pushing away of it." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Connection to [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]

Obligation-as-management is one of the cleanest mechanisms underneath the broader principle that what we fear, we invite. The person who fears losing love manages for love, and the management pushes love away. The person who fears poverty manages money obsessively, and the management makes money flee. The fear produces the management produces the loss.

See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the master pattern. Obligation-as-management is the specific everyday operation of the pattern in caregiving, money, and work.

## What to Do With It

The reframe is the same as for [[obligation-cuts-off-love]]: not "stop being obligated" but "notice what the obligation is producing." Once a person sees that managing the partner's mood pushes the mood the other way, they often stop without being told. The seeing is the move.

The replacement is not the opposite of management (passivity, neglect). The replacement is the felt state from which clean action arises: wanting, loving, being interested in. A person who genuinely wants their partner to feel good will offer things that feel good without managing the partner's response. A person who genuinely loves their work will work hard without managing the outcomes. The action is similar; the felt source is different; the result is opposite.

See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[i-get-to-vs-i-have-to]] for the replacement orientation.

## The Body Pays the Tax

The management does not only push the thing away. It also constricts the body that is doing the managing. The body has to hold itself rigid to maintain the management posture. Rigid bodies cannot respond to their environment well. The performance of the very thing being managed suffers because the body that is performing it has lost its responsiveness. See [[potato-chipping-the-rigid-body-under-obligation]] for the wingsuit-flying example that makes this somatically vivid.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The exhausting work of managing a partner's emotional state and the sense that it never quite works
- The post-effort resentment when you have done everything right and they are still upset
- The mystery of why money seems to slip through your fingers despite your discipline
- The slow drying-up of a business or art form that you used to love
- The sensation, when on the receiving end, of someone being weirdly nice to you in a way that makes you want to retreat
- The recognition that you have organized your life around managing things, and the things have responded by moving away

## Tone

Plain and observational. The phrase "things don't want to be managed" is delivered as if pointing out a fact about how reality works, not as moral teaching. Joe lets the absurdity of "I love my wife because she manages me" do the work of revealing what people are actually doing in the name of love.
