# Compassion as Truth From an Open Heart

## Definition

Compassion has two requirements, not one. It is open-heartedness AND speaking the truth. Either one alone is not it.

"The truth is that being compassionate is when we are open-hearted and speaking our truth. Because then people can see a reality that they might not be able to see." -- Joe Hudson, The Boy Who Taught Me Truth

Open-hearted without truth is niceness. Truth without an open heart is criticism or attack. Compassion is the specific combination: truth delivered while the heart is open.

The effect of real compassion: the other person gets to see a reality they could not previously see. The function of compassion is the same as the function of vulnerability — to make the unseen visible. See [[vulnerability]].

## What Compassion Is Not

The most common misuse of the word "compassion" is to make it synonymous with "being nice."

"Oftentimes what we think of compassion is, like, being nice. We think of being loving as being nice. When I'm hanging out, say, like on Twitter, or if I'm interacting with people coaching, oftentimes people confuse this idea of, oh, I don't want to say anything that upsets them, with that's being nice, that's being loving, that's being compassionate." -- Joe Hudson, The Boy Who Taught Me Truth

Three words are collapsed into one another by this mistake: compassion, love, nice. They are not the same thing. The collapse is what produces conflict-avoidance dressed up as virtue.

The diagnostic for whether something is compassion or niceness: is something true being said, and is the heart open while saying it? If the heart is open but no truth is being delivered, that is just kindness — pleasant but not compassion. If truth is being delivered but the heart is closed, that is just criticism — possibly accurate, not compassion.

See [[love-vs-nice]] for the parallel distinction about love specifically. See [[nice-vs-kind-from-open-heart]] for the social-register version of the same confusion.

## Why People Make the Mistake

The collapse happens because the niceness move is easier and looks similar from the outside. Withholding a hard truth in the name of "not wanting to hurt them" feels virtuous. It feels compassionate.

What is actually happening is usually the protection of the speaker, not the listener. The speaker does not want to risk the rupture, the backlash, the social cost. The withholding is fear-based, not love-based. Dressing it up as compassion lets the speaker feel like a good person while protecting themselves.

This is why Joe singles out Twitter and coaching as the contexts where he watches the mistake happen most. Both are environments where people are visibly trying to be good, visibly trying to be loving, and the easiest way to look loving without risking anything is to refuse to say anything that might upset anyone.

## What Real Compassion Costs

The reference case for what real compassion looks like is [[alex-bell-told-joe-he-lied]]. Alex was a peer at boarding school. He sat Joe down and said: "Hey, Joe, like we would all probably like you if you didn't lie so much." Joe was a compulsive liar at the time. The lying stopped almost immediately.

Three features made it compassion rather than criticism:

1. **It was true.** Joe was, in fact, lying compulsively.
2. **The heart was open.** Joe felt seen, not attacked.
3. **It was costly for Alex.** No one else in the social group was willing to say it.

"It was the most compassionate act that anybody had ever done for me up until that time in my life." -- Joe Hudson, The Boy Who Taught Me Truth

Niceness would have been to let the lying continue. The nice peers were the ones who said nothing. They were doing what felt safe and calling it kindness. Alex did the harder thing — he took the risk of being rejected, attacked, or denied — and that risk is what made the act compassionate.

The implication: real compassion costs the giver something. Withholding the truth costs nothing.

## The Mechanism

The reason compassion-as-truth-from-an-open-heart works: it lets the receiver see a reality they could not previously see. Defense mechanisms keep people from seeing themselves. When someone with an open heart names what is obviously true but unspoken, the defense has less to push against. The truth is not coming as attack, so it cannot be repelled as attack. The truth is just there, with care around it.

Joe's lying stopped the instant Alex spoke because the function of the lying (to be seen, to be liked) collapsed when Alex said "we would like you more if you didn't lie." The lying had been an attempt to get the very thing Alex was now offering for free, conditional on the lying stopping. The compassion was not just the truth-telling. It was the truth-telling that also showed Joe a way out.

See [[love-as-deep-welcoming]] for the underlying state. Compassion is one of the operational forms welcoming can take when truth is required.

## When Someone Experiences This

When you find yourself thinking "I don't want to say anything that upsets them, I want to be compassionate" — check whether what you are calling compassion is actually fear of conflict. If the truth would help them and you are not saying it, you are being nice, not compassionate.

When someone has said a hard truth to you and you felt seen rather than attacked — that was compassion. Notice what made the difference: their heart was open. You can use the experience as a reference for what to give others.

When you watch online discourse and see people praised for "compassion" because they refused to engage with difficult content — pause. The compassion test is whether truth got spoken from an open heart. The refusal to engage is sometimes wise. It is not, by itself, compassion.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone says "I want to be compassionate" but means "I want to avoid saying anything difficult."
- When a person is debating whether to tell a friend, family member, or colleague a hard truth and is worried that doing so would be uncompassionate.
- When a person has been on the receiving end of a hard truth and is trying to figure out whether it was love or attack.
- When the conversation is about online discourse, social media, or coaching cultures and what counts as virtue.
- When a person is collapsing love, compassion, and niceness into one undifferentiated category.

## Anti-Patterns

- Using "compassion" as cover for conflict-avoidance. The withholding is fear, not compassion.
- Speaking the hard truth from a closed heart and calling it compassion. The closed-heart version is criticism, even when the content is accurate.
- Performing compassion publicly to be seen as a good person. Compassion is the act, not the appearance of the act.
- Deciding the receiver "can't handle" the truth and calling the withholding compassion. See [[you-think-they-cant-handle-it]].

## Boundary Conditions

This does not mean every hard truth should be spoken at every moment. Discernment about timing, capacity, and consent still applies. The test is not "did I say the hard thing." The test is "if I am withholding, am I withholding because love requires the wait, or because I am afraid?"

It also does not mean cruelty in honest clothing is compassion. The two-part test is strict: open heart AND truth. Drop either one and the act is not compassion.
