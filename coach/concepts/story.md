# Story

## Definition

Any idea you have. The idea structure you put around a situation. Not the situation itself, but the framing of it.

"Story meaning like any idea that you have. The idea that you put around a situation. So I'm outside and I'm in nature and I'm like, 'Oh, this is enjoyable.' That's a story that it's enjoyable. Or, 'Nature is beautiful.' That's a story. Or, 'Wow, I wish they hadn't cut down all these trees.' That's a story. Or, 'Mankind is destroying the environment.' That's a story. It's not that they're true or not true. It's not about that. It's just, it's literally the idea structure that you put around whatever it is that you're experiencing." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The word covers every thought a person has, not just the dramatic ones. "This is enjoyable" is a story. "I am angry" is a story. "I am a good person" is a story. "I should call my mother" is a story. Every sentence the mind produces is a story in this sense.

## Every Thought, Not Just Limiting Ones

A [[limiting-belief]] is a specific kind of story: one that limits the person's capacity to be themselves, get what they want, or be happy. Story is the broader category. Most stories are not limiting. They are just the ordinary ideational layer the mind continuously generates around experience.

"Unlike a limiting belief, which is like a story that's actually limiting you. Every single thought you have is a story. There's none that isn't." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

This matters because the work of seeing through limiting beliefs is sometimes mistaken for the work of seeing through all stories. They are not the same. Seeing through a limiting belief frees you from a specific constraint. Seeing through all stories produces the spacious quality of [[self-realization]], which is a different kind of work and a different developmental stage.

## Story Can Separate or Connect

A common framing of story is that it separates you from direct experience. This is partly true. But it is not the whole picture. A thought can also bring you more deeply into connection with the experience. The same thought can do both at different times.

"There's ways in which a thought can actually bring you more deeply into connection with yourself as well. The thought can do either. The thought can make you either more or less connected." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The variable that determines which direction the story goes is how much the person believes the story to be true. The more belief, the more separation. The less belief, the more the story can serve as a pointer rather than a wall.

## No Thought Is Fully True or Fully False

"I can't find any thought that I have that's 100% true. And I can't find any thought that I have that's 100% false." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

Thoughts by their nature are limited. They cannot describe reality completely. A single intellectual function cannot capture what requires multiple intelligences (head, heart, gut). Painting reality with only the intellect is like painting a portrait with one color. See [[head-heart-gut]].

This is the philosophical floor for the "untruth in every thought" practice. See [[untruth-in-every-thought]]. The voice's claims are not refuted by finding a counter-claim that is more true. They are softened by recognizing that no thought, including the counter-thought, can carry the full weight the voice is putting on it.

## Story Is Tied to Emotion

Every story has an emotional component. Some are nearly invisible -- a story about a tree has a faint emotional charge. Some are dense -- a story like "my father hates me" or "I am worthless" carries deep emotional weight. The stories about identity have the heaviest emotional load.

"All of them will have an emotional component to them. And some of them are, you know, somewhat benign emotional components. And some of them are not. Like, my dad loves me, my dad hates me, my dad's critical of me, often say would have a much deeper emotional component." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The story-emotion link runs both ways. Stories produce emotions. Emotions also produce stories -- the mind tries to construct a story to explain a feeling. See [[emotions-and-stories-dance]].

## Stories Are Necessary

The work is not to eliminate stories. Stories are what make people human. The point is to hold them lightly enough that they do not lock the system.

"I love them. I love seeing them. It's so important. Stories are so important." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

A developmental stage that erases stories entirely is not the goal. Joe describes a period in his own life when he could not tell stories at all. He then realized how important they were. The mature stage is being able to tell stories with full engagement while not believing them as truth claims.

"There's nothing in me that buys into it. And for a while, I was like that. Like, I just couldn't tell stories. Like, there was a good year and a half where I was just like, I don't even know what — and now I realize how important they are. Like, they're what make us humans." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

## Stories Change With Development

Stories are not static. A useful story at one stage can be a limiting story at the next. Joe gives a recurring example: someone who initially says "my parents were perfect" often needs to come to "my parents had issues" before they can move. Later they may need to release the story that their parents were broken to keep growing. Each story is necessary at its stage. Holding on past the stage is what creates the problem.

"All the stories are very necessary and they definitely interact deeply with the emotional experience and to some degree, almost the same thing in a way." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

See [[stories-are-developmental-scaffolding]] for the principle.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Telling themselves the same thing about a person or situation for years
- Catching the moment when a felt sense gets immediately wrapped in an interpretation
- Noticing that what was certain yesterday feels less solid today
- Watching themselves construct a narrative to explain a body sensation

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person presents a strongly-held interpretation of a situation as fact.
- When the conversation needs to widen from "this is what happened" to "this is how I am framing what happened."
- When introducing [[limiting-belief]] work and establishing what category of thing a belief is.
- When a person asks how to deal with their thoughts and needs a frame for what thoughts are.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[limiting-belief]] when the story is one that limits.
- Toward [[emotions-and-stories-dance]] for the relationship between story and emotion.
- Toward [[feeling-changes-story]] when the work is to release emotion to clarify the story.
- Toward [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]] when the emotion is stuck and the story is rigid.
- Toward [[untruth-in-every-thought]] for the inquiry practice on a specific story.
- Toward [[stories-are-developmental-scaffolding]] when the question is whether to keep or release a particular story.
- Toward [[self-realization]] when the inquiry is into what is true when no story is held.

## Boundary Conditions

The teaching that "every thought is a story" can be misused. It can become a way to dismiss any thought that is inconvenient. "Oh, that's just a story" becomes the new defense against feeling, against feedback, against responsibility. See [[dont-use-story-frame-to-bypass]].

The teaching is meant to loosen the grip of belief, not to disqualify all thinking. The intellect remains a critical tool. The point is that it operates downstream of emotion and inside the larger field of experience -- not as the final arbiter of what is real.
