# Purpose Is an Unfolding, Not a Destination

## Definition

Purpose is not a fixed answer the person eventually arrives at. It is an unfolding shape that reveals itself over time, deepens with each season of living, and continues to refine across the lifespan. The whole journey is the purpose. The arrival is not a moment; it is the continuous arriving.

"Instead of, 'I'm gonna go find my purpose and then I'm gonna have it,' it's: 'I am on a journey and the whole journey reveals my purpose.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Find Your Purpose

Brett added: "Is infused with purpose."

Joe: "Is infused. And 2 years in the journey, you look back and the idea of purpose has totally changed. 20 years, you look back and the idea of purpose has totally changed. Yeah, there's a maturation in it. And so trying to jump it is, you know, it reminds me of my daughters when they were younger wanting to be older. You know, it's like, what? This is the process."

The maturation cannot be skipped. The desire to skip it — to find the final answer now, to arrive once and be done — is the same shape as a child wanting to be older. The process is not in the way of the purpose. The process is the purpose.

## How Joe Explains It

Joe describes his own arc. Songs and comics first — that was his purpose. Then business. Then the human-development work he does now. Each phase, in its time, was the purpose. Looking back, the through-line is consistent: the constant interest in who we are, how humans work, how we see ourselves more clearly. The forms varied. The shape underneath was stable.

But the recognition of the shape itself has refined over time. Joe is direct about this:

"It's unfolding. I still don't have complete clarity over it. I don't know where it's gonna lead." -- Joe Hudson, How To Find Your Purpose

Decades into the work, Joe describes himself as still without complete clarity. The unfolding is ongoing. The clarity is more than it was; it is not all there is to be had. The frame is not "find the answer and have it" but "live the unfolding and let it reveal itself further."

The five-stage structure of how the unfolding develops, which Joe names in this conversation:

1. **Discovery and fighting against it** — the early phase, where the person is sensing the call but resisting it.
2. **Acknowledging it and doing the scary thing** — the phase where the person says yes to the call and acts despite the fear.
3. **A feeling of choicelessness** — the phase where the deliberation drops and the action is no longer questioned.
4. **Refinement of the form** — over years and decades, the form the purpose takes evolves; the through-line stays.
5. **Continued evolution** — the unfolding does not stop. The 60-year-old's purpose is more refined than the 40-year-old's, which was more refined than the 20-year-old's.

"Living your purpose is a process. It's an evolution, it grows. And part of the time of living your purpose is discovery and fighting against it. And part of it is acknowledging it and doing the scary thing. And part of it is a feeling of choicelessness. And you don't get to shortcut that stuff. There's a way in which it develops." -- Joe Hudson, How To Find Your Purpose

## When Someone Experiences This

This concept matters most for two groups:

**People who think they should already have the answer.** They are 25 or 35 or 45 and still feel they have not "figured out" their purpose. The frame they are using is the destination-frame; they are evaluating themselves against an arrival that the destination-frame promises but never delivers. The unfolding-frame relieves the pressure: the work is to keep living the unfolding, not to arrive at the answer.

**People who had a clear sense of purpose at one age and now do not.** Often this happens at major life transitions — children growing up, a career chapter ending, a relationship changing. The person feels they have lost their purpose. The unfolding-frame names what is actually happening: the form is changing, and the new form has not yet stabilized. The through-line is still there; the vehicle is shifting. The work is to attend to the new vehicle, not to chase the old form.

In both cases, the destination-frame is producing the suffering. The unfolding-frame is the structural correction.

## The Daughter-Wanting-To-Be-Older Analogy

The analogy Joe offers — children wanting to be older — captures the structure of trying to skip the unfolding. The child cannot become 18 by wanting to be 18. The 18-year-old has lived the years that produced the 18-year-old. Skipping is not available. The maturation is the route, not an obstacle on the route.

Applied to purpose: trying to find the final answer at 25 is structurally the same move as the child wanting to be 18. The 65-year-old's relationship to their purpose is the product of having lived the years. The 25-year-old cannot have that relationship now. The 25-year-old can only live the 25-year-old's relationship to their purpose, which will be partial, which will refine, which will be unrecognizable from the 65-year-old's vantage looking back.

The relief in the analogy: the child does not have to do anything to become 18. The years deliver the 18-year-old. The person living the unfolding does not have to do anything to deliver the refinement. The living delivers it.

## The Difference From Drift

The unfolding is not the same as drift. The unfolding has direction; drift does not.

- **Unfolding:** the person is in some form of living the call, refining the alignment, doing the scary steps as they arise. The forms may change; the engagement is consistent.
- **Drift:** the person is not engaged with the call. They are moving through time without participation in their own unfolding. The forms may change; the engagement is absent.

The difference is felt in the system. The unfolding produces aliveness across the changes. The drift produces a low-grade flatness across the changes. The aliveness is the marker of engagement with the unfolding; the flatness is the marker of disengagement.

A coach can ask: "Across the changes in your life, is there a thread of engagement, or has there been a flatness?" The answer reveals which is happening.

## What This Concept Replaces

The unfolding concept replaces several frames that produce suffering when used as the operative model for purpose:

- **The find-it-and-have-it frame.** Treats purpose as an object to be located. Produces the search-for-purpose, which is itself the obstruction. See [[searching-for-purpose-pushes-it-away]].
- **The one-perfect-thing frame.** Treats purpose as a single activity that is the right one. Produces analysis paralysis and the dismissal of activities that are partial expressions of the through-line.
- **The arrival frame.** Treats purpose as something the person eventually gets to and then stays in. Produces despair when the form changes ("I had my purpose and now I've lost it") and rigidity when the person clings to a form that has stopped fitting.
- **The skip-the-process frame.** Treats the years of unfolding as wasted time before the real answer arrives. Produces the unwillingness to engage with the partial forms that are the only forms available now.

The unfolding concept dissolves all of these. The work is to live the unfolding. The forms will change. The through-line will refine. The journey is the purpose.

## Implication for the Recognition Moment

The recognition of purpose — when it arrives, whether through a stranger at a hot springs (see [[the-hot-springs-massage-message]]) or through a quieter inner moment — is not the end of the work. It is the beginning of the conscious unfolding.

Before the recognition, the person was unfolding without knowing they were. After the recognition, the unfolding becomes conscious. The forms continue to change. The through-line continues to refine. The recognition only opened the door; the living is what walks through it.

This is important because some people, after a moment of recognition, expect to have arrived. They have not. They have started. The disappointment that comes from misreading the recognition as the arrival is itself part of the unfolding — but the disappointment can be skipped if the unfolding-frame is held from the start.

## What This Means for the AI Coach

When someone presents the purpose question, the unfolding-frame should be made available early. The destination-frame is the default; without active replacement, the person will keep operating in it.

The replacement is operational, not just conceptual. Specific moves:

- Reframe questions about "finding" purpose into questions about "living" the unfolding. "Where are you in the unfolding right now?" is a better question than "what is your purpose?"
- Validate the partial. The person is somewhere in the maturation. The somewhere is not the final form. That is fine.
- Name when the person is trying to skip the process. "It sounds like you want to be at the 20-years-later vantage now. That's not available. What is available is the unfolding you're in right now."
- After a recognition moment, frame what comes next as the conscious unfolding, not as the arrival. The recognition is the door; the living is the work.

When someone reports they had a sense of purpose and lost it: do not collude with the loss-frame. The form changed. The through-line is still there. The work is to attend to the new form, not to mourn the old one as if it were the only one.

## Tone

Joe delivers this concept with warmth and a small amount of self-deprecation. He places himself inside the unfolding — "I still don't have complete clarity over it." The teaching is not from someone who has arrived. It is from someone who is in it.

The daughter-wanting-to-be-older analogy is delivered with affection. The framing is not "you are wrong for wanting the answer now"; it is "of course you want the answer now, and the answer is not available now in the way you want it; what is available is the living of the unfolding."
