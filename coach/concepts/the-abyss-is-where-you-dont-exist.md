# The Abyss Is Where You Don't Exist

## The Definition

When Joe tells a coachee to "go into the abyss," he is not using a metaphor. He is naming a specific structural feature of the territory the coachee is avoiding: it is the place where the self that has been navigating does not exist, or where what the self has been taking itself to be changes dramatically.

The instruction, in his own words:

"When you hear me talking about going into the abyss, when someone's like scared of something and I say, oh, go into the abyss, I'm not pointing at anything significantly different than: it's going into the place you're scared of, and the place you're scared of is where you don't exist. Or what you think you are changes dramatically." -- Joe Hudson, How Death Brings Life Closer

The clarification matters because "the abyss" can sound mystical or large-scale -- the void, the dark night, the existential nothing. Joe is naming something more operational. The abyss in any given moment is wherever the coachee's avoidance is. The reason the avoidance is operating is that going there would dissolve, or rearrange, the self that is doing the avoiding. The two things -- the avoided territory and the territory-where-the-self-isn't -- are the same territory.

## Why This Clarification Matters

The word "abyss" carries weight that can become a distraction. A coachee can hear the word and produce a picture of a vast existential void, then become convinced that what they are avoiding is not that big, and that the abyss instruction therefore does not apply to them.

The operational definition removes this escape. Every avoidance qualifies. The shape of the avoidance is the shape of the abyss in that case. The coachee who is avoiding feeling sad has an abyss the size of feeling-sad. The coachee who is avoiding looking at how they have been showing up in their marriage has an abyss the size of that recognition. The coachee who is avoiding the possibility of madness has an abyss that includes that possibility.

In each case, the structural feature is the same: the avoidance is in service of preserving the current configuration of self. Stepping into the avoided thing requires the current configuration to release. The release is the abyss. The release is what the system is treating as catastrophic.

## What Makes It Feel Abyssal

The felt-experience of approaching the abyss is dominated by the system's prediction that going further will produce annihilation. The prediction is technically accurate, in a specific sense: the configuration of self that has been navigating will not survive the entry. Something will change. The change cannot be predicted from inside the current configuration, because predictive capacity is itself part of what is being asked to release.

The system reads this unpredictability as life-threatening. The fear that arises at the threshold of the abyss is the fear of dying. Whether the actual stakes are biological or symbolic, the bodily-felt threat is the same. This is why even minor avoidances can produce major fear when approached directly. The avoidance is doing protective work that the system cannot distinguish from biological survival.

See [[every-moment-is-an-exit-point]] for the structural account of why this same fear arrives in ordinary moments. The exit-point fear and the abyss fear are the same activation in different contexts.

## What Is Actually There

The pattern, across many coaching sessions: what is actually there, when the coachee enters the avoided territory without bracing, is not annihilation. It is one of a few specific things.

**Peace.** This is the most common surprise. The thing the coachee was avoiding turns out to be peace. The fear was the resistance to the peace, because the peace dissolves the self-maintenance project. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]].

**Love.** A close second. The avoided territory turns out to contain the love that has been kept at bay. The fear of the love -- of being touched by it, of receiving it, of becoming responsible for what it requires -- has been masquerading as the fear of something more abstract. See [[what-were-avoiding-is-usually-love]].

**Sensation.** When neither peace nor love is the specific content, what is often there is plain bodily sensation. Breath. Heartbeat. The pressure of the chair against the body. The fear had been protecting the system from the directness of bodily contact with what is here.

**The actual feeling.** Sadness arrives. Anger arrives. A wail comes out. The feeling that was being protected against turns out to be the feeling, and the feeling, when felt, moves through.

In none of these cases does the predicted annihilation happen. The self that entered the territory does change, sometimes substantially, but the change is not destruction. It is reconfiguration. The new configuration is, by the coachee's report, more spacious than the one that entered.

## The Coaching Move That Operationalizes This

The concept is the structural complement to the move [[lets-go-there]]. The move is the invitation. The concept is what is being invited into. When a coachee names the avoided thing, Joe says "let's go there." The concept clarifies what the going-there is: stepping into the place where the current configuration of self does not exist, or where what the self has been taking itself to be is going to change.

Knowing the concept makes the move land differently. Without the concept, the coachee can hear "let's go there" as an invitation to deepen into difficult feeling. With the concept, the coachee can hear "let's go there" as an invitation to step into the place that has been protected against because the self that has been navigating cannot fully survive the entry. The bigger frame supports the willingness to actually go.

## The Boundary: This Is Not Always the Right Frame

The concept is operationally useful for coachees who are in active avoidance of something that is, in fact, ready to be entered. It is not useful as a generic injunction to "go into the abyss" regardless of context.

A coachee in acute crisis is not in need of the abyss frame; they need stabilization. A coachee with insufficient containment for the dissolution is not served by the dissolution being accelerated. The frame applies when the coachee has the capacity to be in the territory and the avoidance is what is keeping them out. This is the standard discernment of when [[lets-go-there]] is the right move.

The frame also does not mean every uncomfortable territory is the abyss. Some uncomfortable territories are practical problems that benefit from practical engagement. The frame is for the territories whose discomfort is in service of preserving a configuration of self that is asking to release. The discrimination requires reading [[how-much-fight]] is in the person, where the energy of the avoidance is going, and whether what is being avoided is structural or circumstantial.

## What This Replaces

The default framing of the avoided territory is that it is dangerous and the avoidance is rational. This frame keeps the avoidance in place. The coachee continues to organize life around not encountering the avoided thing, and the cost of the organization grows over time.

The replacement frame: the avoided territory is the place where the current configuration of self does not exist. The avoidance is what is keeping the configuration in place. The configuration is what is producing the suffering. Entering the territory dissolves both the suffering and the configuration that was its source.

This is structurally what [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]] articulates as a principle. The concept here is the operational definition of the territory that the principle says is peace.

## The Compression With the Death-Acknowledgement Work

Joe is offering this definition in a conversation about literal death. The pivot is direct: the abyss the coachee is avoiding in any given session is structurally the same as the larger abyss the dying person is being made to face. The literal mortality and the moment-to-moment exit-point are the same structure at different scales.

This is why the death-acknowledgement work in [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] generalizes. Once the person is in contact with the felt-reality of literal death, the felt-reality of the moment-to-moment dying becomes more available, and the abyss of any specific avoidance becomes easier to step into. The bigger frame supports the smaller move.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- When a coachee uses the word "abyss" themselves and the question is what it points at
- When [[lets-go-there]] has been offered and the coachee asks what they are being invited into
- When the avoidance the coachee has named is being framed as too small to count as a real avoidance
- When a coachee is in a long arc of avoidance of something they cannot name precisely
- When the coaching has touched [[every-moment-is-an-exit-point]] and the question becomes what the exit-point actually is
- When the conversation about [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] needs to be made operational at the scale of one specific avoidance

## Tone

Joe offers the clarification in the middle of a conversation about literal death, almost in passing. The tone is matter-of-fact, almost technical, in the way that someone who has run a tool many times can describe the tool plainly. There is no drama in the way he names what the abyss is. The matter-of-factness is part of what makes the concept usable -- if the word "abyss" were always delivered with weight, the concept could not be deployed lightly, and many of the moments when it applies would go un-met.
