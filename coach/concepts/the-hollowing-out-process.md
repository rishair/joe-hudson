# The Hollowing Out Process

## Definition

The hollowing out process is the ongoing developmental arc by which a person who teaches, coaches, or facilitates becomes structurally able to hold the role without distortion. It is not a one-time event. It is a continuous process of letting go of each provisional epiphany about humility once that epiphany has become its own form of identity, and finding the next layer of integration. The image at the center is Fools Crow's hollow bone: the practitioner's job is to be the channel through which something larger moves, and the work is to keep that channel clean.

The process does not end. Each new layer of integration reveals the next layer that needs attention. The phases described below are not stages that one completes; they are positions that one inhabits, often cycling through them as the work continues.

"The hollowing out process is also always going on. The evolution doesn't end. It seems to be just this perpetual process of letting go of one epiphany and finding, oh, that epiphany has its own rut. And then just continuing to learn." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The concept matters specifically for the role of coach, facilitator, or teacher because these roles structurally pressure the practitioner toward identity calcification. The hollowing out is the continuous structural protection against the calcification.

## The Three Phases (As Joe Lives Them)

Joe describes his own arc through humility in three phases. The phases are not exclusive; they continue to be present in different proportions over time.

**Phase 1: Humility as thinking less of yourself / making yourself small.**

The first available model of humility, often inherited from culture or family. The person tries not to take up space, not to claim what they have done, not to be seen as proud. The structure is still about the self -- the self is being made small as the strategy for being humble. This is not yet humility; it is a defended identity (the small self) doing the work of looking like humility. See [[humility]] for the full critique of this version.

Joe spent time here: "There's a long time where I thought humility was thinking less of myself, not making myself big."

**Phase 2: Humility as making space for something to move through (the channel phase).**

The person recognizes the small-self version was still about the self. The next move is to step out of the personal frame entirely. The work that moves through them is not theirs. They are the channel; something larger is the source. The Fools Crow hollow bone image lives here. "It's not me, it's the teachers before me. It's not me, it's that which moves through me." The work becomes possible to hold because it has been depersonalized.

This is a real and necessary phase. It produces a non-defensive relationship to praise and to criticism. The praise is not for the person; the criticism is not of the person. The work moves through, the person stays out of the way. The depth of work that becomes possible in this phase exceeds anything available in phase 1.

"Then humility turned into making space to have things move through me, like in Fools Crow. So the idea of my job is just to be a clean vessel for something greater than me to move through." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

**Phase 3: Humility as taking your God-given place (the personal-and-impersonal both-and).**

The third phase is the harder one because it appears to contradict phase 2. After living in the channel-phase long enough, the person notices that the "it's not me at all" frame is also becoming a defense. It protects them from owning what is actually personal about the work. The denial of their own power becomes its own distortion. They start to see: there is something deeply personal here. I have done this work. This would not be happening without me. I do have power. I do have influence.

The both-and: it is both completely personal and completely not. The person is not abandoning the channel-phase; they are adding back the personal that the channel-phase had displaced. The new posture holds both truths at once. The work moves through them AND they are the one through whom it is moving. The power is real AND the power is not theirs to claim.

Joe's specific naming, drawing on a Hebrew framing of humility:

"Now there's this recognition of, oh no, it's me. I've done this work and this shit wouldn't be happening without me. And really, this is not me at all. But it's holding both of those two things." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

"I was reading recently, I think it's in the Jewish tradition where they talk about humility as being given, taking your God-given place in the world. That's like the translation for humility. And there's a personal thing to that." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

## The Specific Risk of Each Phase

Each phase is a real piece of the work. Each phase also has a specific failure mode.

- **Phase 1 failure mode:** the small self as identity. The person becomes attached to being small. Smallness becomes the defense. This is the "humility" of the person who refuses to acknowledge what they have done, who deflects every compliment, who organizes their self-image around their unworthiness. See [[humility-vs-disempowerment]].

- **Phase 2 failure mode:** denial of the personal makes the practitioner more dangerous, not less. The "it's not me, it's the work moving through" frame can become cover for unowned authority. The person has real power; pretending it is not theirs makes the power operate unconsciously, which is more dangerous than the power operating consciously. They become unable to take responsibility for the impact they have because in their frame, they are not the one having the impact.

"By denying that, I'm actually denying my power and I become more dangerous." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

This is the failure mode Joe is specifically working with at the current edge. The channel-frame, held alone, becomes its own form of distortion because it allows the practitioner to disclaim authorship of effects they are in fact producing. The personal has to come back in for the practitioner to be fully accountable to what is moving through them.

- **Phase 3 failure mode:** the personal swings back too far and becomes self-aggrandizement again. The practitioner who has been holding "it's not me" for years can re-discover the personal and then over-correct into "it's all me." The both-and is hard precisely because it asks the practitioner to hold two felt-states that the mind keeps trying to collapse into one or the other.

The continuous form of the process is moving among these phases as the situation requires, with the field of the both-and as the deepest container. The mistake at any phase is to lock into the phase as if it were the answer.

## "I Have a Tremendous Amount of Power" as the Recovered Personal

The specific recovery that Joe is currently working with: the recognition that the work he does has real influence, real power, real impact, and that owning this is itself the next move in the hollowing out -- not a regression from it.

"To actually own the personal nature of it and to say, oh wow, I actually, I do have a tremendous amount of power. I actually have a tremendous amount of influence." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

This is structurally important because it interrupts the "I'm just a humble channel" pattern that lets teachers off the hook for the actual influence they have. A coach or teacher who has been working in phase 2 for a long time can use the channel-frame as defense against feedback ("I'm just allowing the work to come through; if it landed badly that's not me"). The recovery in phase 3 is the move that makes them accountable again.

The owning is not bravado. It is the acknowledgment that real things are happening, that the person is real, that the work is being shaped by who they are, and that this reality has to be held alongside the not-me frame, not in place of it.

## The Permeability Frame for Phase 3 (Self Becoming Larger, Not Smaller)

In a later articulation of his own work, Joe gives the phase-3 move a more specific name: permeability of self. The frame shifts from "less me through dissolution" to "more space through permeability." The two are not in conflict — phase 3 holds both — but the permeability frame names a specific operational quality of how the personal comes back in.

"Up until now, the way that the journey has been is it's become less me through dissolution, meaning the recognition that it — the teaching isn't mine, it's coming through me, that it's not personal, that it's um, that it's like a gift. And that's all — it's always kind of been how I've been able to see more clearly through the process and be a better conduit for what wants to move. ... What I'm getting to now is what I'm realizing is that actually the personal has to be reincorporated back into it for, for me to be able to be healthy if this thing continues to grow." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

"So now instead of the freedom coming from like a dissolution of self, it's almost more like a permeability of self. It's like it's becoming so large that there's more space." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The permeability frame matters because it specifies what re-incorporating the personal looks like operationally. The practitioner is not contracting back into a defended ego. The practitioner is allowing themselves to be porous — to let the impact of the work, the lives changed by the work, the people the work touches, actually land in the body. The personal recovery in phase 3 is specifically the recovery of permeability, not the recovery of a closed self-structure.

This matters most for practitioners whose work has reach. The dissolution frame, held alone, can become the structural mechanism by which the practitioner avoids feeling the impact of their work landing in people. Phase 3, through permeability, requires the impact to be felt. See [[dissolution-of-self-vs-permeability-of-self]] for the developed distinction and [[let-it-break-your-heart-on-a-consistent-basis]] for the daily practice that lives the move.

The structural risk this addresses: the well-documented tendency for power and influence to erode empathy ([[power-erodes-empathy]]). The dissolution-only practitioner can accumulate influence while gradually losing contact with the people the influence affects. Permeability is the structural counter. The practitioner becomes more, not less, able to be moved by the people their work touches.

## Why This Is Different From the Phases Reversing

A reasonable concern: doesn't phase 3 just reverse phase 2 and put us back in the ego-phase? No. The phases are not on a line where phase 3 reverses phase 2. They are layered. Each phase remains operative; the next phase is added on top.

In phase 3, the channel-frame is still fully held. The work is still not the person's in the sense phase 2 named. AND the person is also real, also the source of the specific shape of how the work comes through, also accountable for the impact. The both-and is structurally different from either pole alone.

The image: phase 2 is the hollow bone. Phase 3 is the recognition that someone has to hold the bone in the right position, has to keep it clean, has to be the specific person whose specific bone this is. The wind moves through. The bone is still a specific bone shaped by a specific person's life. The wind would not produce the same music through a different bone.

## The Continuous Form

The phases keep cycling. A coach can pass through all three in a single conversation. A coach can spend years in one before the next becomes available. A coach who has integrated all three can still find themselves needing to do the phase-1 work in a new domain where the smallness is the issue, or the phase-2 work in a new domain where the personal has been claimed too tightly.

The point is the orientation, not the linear progression. The orientation: the work of becoming a clean vessel is continuous. Every position that gets adopted will reveal its own distortion eventually. The willingness to keep finding the next distortion and to keep softening it is what the hollowing out actually is.

"It seems to be just this perpetual process of letting go of one epiphany and finding, oh, that epiphany has its own rut. And then just continuing to learn." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

## In Practice

For a coach asking how to live this concept:

- Notice which phase you have currently been operating from. If you cannot tell, ask the people closest to you what they observe.
- If you have been in phase 1 (small self) -- the work is the channel-phase. Stop apologizing for the work you do. Let it move through cleanly. The hollow-bone instruction is your next move.
- If you have been in phase 2 (the channel) for a long time -- the work might be back at the personal. Are you using "it's not me" as a defense against feedback? Are you disclaiming authorship of effects you are actually producing? The personal recovery is your next move.
- If you have been holding the both-and -- watch where you collapse it into one pole or the other. The collapse is the next surface of the work.

The continuous question: where am I right now using a previous integration as a defense against the next layer? That question, asked honestly, is the hollowing out as a daily practice.

## The Relationship to the Three-Phase Arc of Coach Development

Joe's three phases of humility map roughly onto the development a coach goes through over years of practice. The new coach often starts in phase 1 (deferring to teachers, making themselves small, not claiming authority). The mid-career coach often lives in phase 2 (the channel, the work moving through, no personal authorship). The senior coach who has been doing the work long enough often confronts phase 3 (the both-and, the recovery of the personal that does not abandon the channel).

This is not a curriculum. It is an empirical observation about what tends to surface. The coach who skips phase 1 and tries to be the channel immediately produces the false-channel that is actually their unowned ego. The coach who stays in phase 2 forever produces the impersonal-coach who cannot be accountable. The coach who tries to live in phase 3 without having lived in phase 2 produces the personal-coach who has not yet been hollowed enough to hold the work as larger than themselves.

The path through is to be where you are, to do the work of that phase honestly, and to let the next phase emerge when the current one shows its own distortion.

## Tone

Reflective, slow, exploratory. Joe describes this concept while actively working out his current edge in it. The tone is not the certainty of someone teaching a settled framework; it is the openness of someone naming the next layer they are finding in themselves. This is fitting for a concept about continuous work -- the description of the concept models the concept.

He moves from one phase-description to the next with the small acknowledgments that mark live thinking ("and then there was this form of, oh, this isn't me..."). The teaching transmits as discovery rather than as delivery.
