# Money as Surrogate

## Definition

Money rarely stays as money in the mind. The mind treats it as a stand-in for something else. The most common surrogates: a parent's love, security, community, power, empowerment, experience itself. The person believes they are pursuing money. They are actually pursuing what money has come to symbolize. This is the subconscious misinterpretation that creates most of the confusion people have around money.

"The thing that people subconsciously think it is, is money is love or money is power or money is their father's love or their mother's love. And I think that is the subconscious misinterpretation of money that creates a lot of confusion for people." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

## The Mechanism

The surrogate gets installed early. A person's relationship with money usually follows the shape of their early relationship with love, with authority, or with some other formative dynamic. If a parent's love was hard to get, money becomes the thing that is hard to get. If a parent's love was withheld and conditional, money becomes a withheld and conditional substitute for that love. The person is not chasing money. They are chasing the original thing, using money as the proxy.

This is a form of [[trauma-mapping]] applied to the money system. The body knows the shape of the original relationship and seeks to recreate it. Money is the most available material in modern society for that recreation.

"My relationship with money, and then I discovered most other people I meet, their relationship with money often follows a pattern of their relationship with the love of their parents, or the relationship of love with authority, or their relationship of something important in their early childhood, and that money has become a surrogate for whatever relationship that is." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

## The Common Surrogates

- Parent's love (most common)
- Authority's approval
- Security
- Community
- Power
- Empowerment
- Experience itself

Each produces a different shape of pursuit. Money-as-security is anxious accumulation. Money-as-love is the unending hunger. Money-as-power is the comparative grind. Money-as-experience is the spending that never satisfies. The behavior pattern reveals which surrogate is running.

## Why It Cannot Be Satisfied

Money can buy what money buys. It cannot buy what it has been recruited to stand in for. A surrogate father's love is not a father's love. A surrogate community is not community. The mind keeps insisting the next amount will fix it because the mind has not yet seen that the wrong thing is being purchased.

This is why no amount is ever enough. The pursuit is structurally incapable of completing because the surrogate cannot satisfy the original want.

## Locating the Surrogate

The somatic trace is the technique. When the money feeling hits (the kick in the stomach reading about a competitor's exit, the panic about a bill, the hunger looking at a number) stop. Close the eyes. Trace the feeling back to the first time it was felt. The trace usually lands in childhood, in the original relationship that money is now standing in for.

See [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] for the full version of this move. The WhatsApp news, the hammock in El Salvador, the kick in the stomach traced back to the unreachable father.

## Once the Surrogate Is Seen

The next move is to separate what is actually wanted from money as one possible avenue toward it. The want underneath the money pursuit is rarely "more money." It is the parent's love, or security, or community, or power. Once named, the want can be pursued directly. Money may or may not still be part of how it gets met. But it stops being the entire architecture.

"It's really important to be able to separate what you want from money, and money being only one avenue to get there." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

This is the money-domain version of [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

## The Reverse Form

The same mechanism produces the inverted pursuit: money is bad, money is evil, money must be avoided. This is also a surrogate. Often it is rebellion against the parent or authority that money got tied to. Joe's first iteration with money was that money was evil, during the period when he was most angry at his father. Same surrogate, opposite stance.

The fix is the same either way: locate what money is actually standing in for. The relationship with money does not heal at the level of money.

See [[money-is-bad]] for the inverse form as it presents in coaching.

## The Projection Mechanism

The mind installs the surrogate through a projection that is structurally identical to how children handle whatever is most powerful in their lives. From age 0 to about 7, children are in theta brain state, the same state associated with hypnosis. Emotional patterns get installed during this period. As the child grows, those patterns need something to attach to. Mom and Dad used to be the thing that said what could and could not be done. After leaving home, money becomes the thing that says what can and cannot be done. The pattern transfers from the parent to the next equivalent power.

"As kids, we get into very specific emotional patterns. And then as they grow up, they're going to place those patterns on something, probably of equivalent importance in their life. So when they leave mom and dad, then that pattern needs to hit something else. And money's a great place for it to hit." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

Money is uniquely available for this projection because anything can be projected onto it and the evidence will be there. Money built the most prosperous society. Money caused the most disenfranchisement. Money is freedom. Money is prison. Money is power. Money is love. Whatever a person needs to project, money will support the projection.

"You can just project anything you want onto money. You can project it's great. You can project it's bad. You can project it's neutral. And there's evidence to show all of it." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

The same projection mechanism applies to bosses ("they treat their boss like their dad"), authorities, institutions. Money is just the most universal target because of its centrality in modern life.

## What Humans Put Their Faith In

A deeper layer of the projection: humans put their faith in things that have short supply, that can be held, that have immediate power. This is biologically sensible for a hunter holding a spear. The spear is in short supply (effort to make), can be held, has immediate power. Faith makes sense.

Money inherits the same template. It is treated as scarce (must be by design, or no one believes in it), can be held or owned, and has immediate power. The faith follows automatically.

The question Joe raises: what would happen if humans put their faith in something that was in utter abundance, had no scarcity, and did not have short-term power? Would the problems associated with the current faith dissolve? He does not claim to know. He names it as the question worth asking.

"If every human being on the planet put their faith in something that was in utter abundance, and there was clear that we have enough for everybody, and that I can give freely, I can have faith in long-term investment, like the way we do with money -- what would happen to the problems of our world?" -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

This is the structural framing of the surrogate problem at the social level. The relationship with money does not heal at the level of money, individually or collectively. It heals when faith finds a different object.

## The Scorecard-Swap Pattern

A specific form the surrogate takes: a person notices that one scorecard (money) isn't delivering and switches to another (likes, attention, notoriety, recognition). The new scorecard runs the same operation in the same place. The substrate changes; the function does not. This is the surrogate analysis at the level of measurement: it is not just that money is standing in for love; it is that any external metric will stand in for love as long as the substitution is happening.

The K session shows the pattern explicitly. K spent his early career chasing money. He "kind of achieved some of those and then kind of very quietly said, well, I don't care about those things, but instead I want likes and attention and notoriety and people to talk about me." The pivot felt like personal growth. The structure was identical: an external metric was being used to answer the question "am I okay?"

What makes this version of the surrogate especially insidious: the person feels they have done growth work because they have noticed one scorecard was empty. The new scorecard is dressed in the language of meaning (likes feel more "human" than money) and so feels like an upgrade. But the same childhood imprint, the same need for an external verdict on okayness, is operating. The substrate switch is the symptom; the structure is the disease.

The coaching implication: when a person reports "I used to care about money but now I care about X," check what X is doing. If X is delivering oscillating self-worth in the same way money used to, the surrogate has not changed; it has only shifted substrate. The work is at the substrate level. See [[scorecard-keeping-as-identity-structure]] for the diagnostic read and [[whats-the-scorecard-that-cant-be-taken-away]] for the move that invites an internal substrate.

## Status, Fame, and Power as the Same Surrogate

The surrogate mechanism does not isolate to money. It extends to anything in the same conceptual family. For Joe, the linkage was direct: his father's pursuit of money was experienced as a pursuit of status, so anything that read as status (fame, notoriety, cachet, power) became loaded with the same charge.

"There's a linkage here with fame too -- power, fame, any kind of cachet, notoriety, status, anything around status. Because to me, my father's quest for money was all about status. So any version of that... money was a representation of anything I projected onto it." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

The implication for coaching: when a person presents a charged relationship with money, the same charge is likely showing up in their relationship with fame, status, public recognition, or power, in whatever shape those things take in their life. Working only on the money surrogate without checking for the parallel surrogates leaves the structure mostly intact.

The example Joe gives: as a young man avoiding money, he would also avoid spiritual teachers whose books had author photos on the back, judging them as seeking notoriety and therefore untrustworthy. The same surrogate that made him reject money made him reject the visible markers of teacher-as-public-figure. The teachers were not the problem. The projection was the same projection in a different costume.

## The Spirituality Parallel: Awakening as the Same Surrogate

A deeper layer of the same mechanism: the surrogate that runs in the money domain often also runs in the spirituality domain, with awakening (or enlightenment, or "being a good spiritual person") substituting for the same parental love that money was substituting for.

The structure: if I am good enough, if I meditate enough, if I have a calm enough mind, if I eat the right thing, if I am pure enough -- then I can have it. "It" is whatever the original want was. The hurdles are the same hurdles a person sets up in the money domain. The premise is the same premise: I have to earn the love by being worthy of it.

"I had this idea that like it took competence to be rich... It's the same. It's like this stuff isn't earned. Money is not always earned... Awakening is not always earned either. Like it can also be a gift. And it doesn't work the way that we're told that it works." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

"I had set up hurdle after hurdle after hurdle after hurdle that was required, some level of competence, some perfection, something that I had to do so that I could get what I wanted. And those were all my projections. And when that fell apart, it became really clear. It's like, oh, all those hurdles I've made for myself so that I can become wealthy, awakened, happy -- it's all bullshit." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

The recognition that both surrogates are running the same script is itself often what produces the dissolution of both. As long as one surrogate is in operation (chasing awakening) while the other is being avoided (rejecting money), the structure is intact. When both are seen as the same projection looking for the same parental love, the structure becomes available for release.

See [[state-seeking]] for the related anti-pattern in the spiritual domain. The surrogate mechanism is what makes state-seeking persistent: the chase is for the same thing that money is chasing.

## Community as the Other Faith Object

The closest concrete example Joe offers of what humans put their faith in besides money: community. In his early twenties, he went directly from teaching Head Start in the Hayes Valley projects in San Francisco to working in international stock lending. The contrast was visible. One population relied on money for survival and happiness. The other population relied on community for survival and happiness.

"One has money, one has community. One relies on money for survival, and one relies on community for survival. One generally relies on money for happiness. One generally relies on community for happiness." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

The observation underneath: each side wanted what the other had. The community-rich wanted the money. The money-rich did not always want the community (often community felt heavy to them, because of how they were taught). The fact that both populations carried [[poverty-mentality]] in one form or another is the precise content of the surrogate analysis: the resource that is scarce in one's life becomes the projection screen, regardless of which resource the rest of one's life is rich in.

"Both of them had poverty in one way or another." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

This pairs with the broader question about faith objects: community satisfies the criteria of abundance (anyone can be in community), no scarcity, no short-term power. Faith placed in community produces different outcomes than faith placed in money. Neither population in Joe's observation had pure faith in community; the people without money were actively wanting money, indicating that money still held some of the faith. But the relative weight was shifted, and the lived experience differed accordingly.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The reaction to news about other people's money is visceral rather than measured
- "Enough" keeps moving (each milestone reveals a new gap)
- Decisions about money carry weight far in excess of what the actual stakes warrant
- The person describes money in unusually charged terms (good, evil, freedom, prison, savior, trap)
- A windfall does not produce the predicted relief

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports that no amount of money would be enough
- When money decisions produce disproportionate emotional intensity
- When the pursuit of money has a "should" quality that feels older than the current circumstances. See [[i-should-be-doing-this]].
- When a person's parents wielded love conditionally and that person now wields money against themselves the same way

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward the somatic trace: close the eyes, follow the feeling back to its origin
