# Enjoying the Roller Coaster From the Ground

## The Image

Life produces a continuous stream of ups and downs. The co-founder gets cancer. The client rejects you. The funding dries up. Then the deal closes. Then the kid struggles. Then the kid thrives. The events arrive without permission, in any order, at any pace.

The conventional response is to ride the roller coaster. You strap yourself into the seat and feel every climb and drop as a literal change in your altitude. Your sense of self-worth and your sense of safety rise and fall with the events.

Jerry's reframe: the point is not to get better at riding the roller coaster. The point is to learn to enjoy the roller coaster from the ground.

"The point of riding the roller coaster is not to get better at riding roller coasters. It's to learn to not board the roller coaster in the first place. And so if I were to say, what does freedom and acceptance mean to me? It means being able to enjoy the roller coaster from the ground, to not attach my sense of self-worth or my sense of safety, my own essence, to some sort of outcome. And that feels like freedom." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

Joe affirms the reframe. The image becomes one of the cleanest available pictures of what freedom from outcome-attachment actually looks like.

## What This Replaces

The default growth narrative says: develop your tolerance for ups and downs. Get more resilient. Be able to handle bigger swings without falling apart. Stay positive through the lows. Stay humble through the highs. The implicit picture is of someone who has learned to ride the roller coaster expertly. They still board the ride. They are just less wrecked by it.

This is real progress and worth doing. It is also not where the work ends.

The roller-coaster-from-the-ground image points at a structurally different place. The freedom is not in better riding. The freedom is in not being on the ride. The ups and downs still happen. They are still observable. They are still interesting. They are no longer happening to a "you" whose essence rises and falls with them.

## What the Ground Is

The ground is the layer of you that does not change with outcomes. The deal closes; that layer does not. The deal collapses; that layer does not. The kid succeeds; that layer does not. The kid struggles; that layer does not.

This is not detachment in the cold or distant sense. It is the recognition that what is actually you is not what is on the roller coaster. The roller coaster carries outcomes, identities, projects, roles, results. You are something else. The work is to know this directly, not as a concept.

When this is known directly, the outcomes can still be tracked, the events can still be felt, the family can still matter, the work can still be done. None of that has to be sacrificed. What gets released is the attachment of self-worth and safety to any of it. The ride continues. The rider has gotten off.

## The Difference Between Not Caring and Enjoying From the Ground

A common misreading: freedom from outcome-attachment means not caring. The successful business outcome and the failed business outcome become equally meaningless. The healthy child and the sick child become equally unaffecting.

This is not what the image points at. Jerry is explicit: he is still on the ride about his children. He still rides the roller coaster of his daughter's wedding day, of his kid going through a rough patch at college, of the future grandchildren. He is not pretending those rides are not happening. The Velcro is real.

"I am not free of that Velcro. It still gets stuck. And it gets stuck for me. You know, I'm the old fart in the room, right? My kids are 34, 32, and 28. And so where I go is, will they have the life that I want them to have? And will their children have the life. When I see people that I love struggling, you have a kid who goes to college and they're having a rough experience. Oh my God, my heart breaks. That's where I'm still riding the roller coaster." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The freedom is not the absence of caring. The freedom is the recognition that the rides are rides, that the essence is not on the ride, and that even the rides that are still active (because the Velcro is still active) are happening to a layer that is not the same as the ground.

The rides that remain are honest. Jerry does not pretend his daughter's wedding day was just another day. He says it was real. The father-of-the-bride thing was a real feeling. The tears at "Daddy, thank you for giving me the most beautiful day of my life" were real tears. And underneath those real tears, there is still a ground that is not what the tears are on. Both layers are true at the same time. The integration is what the image points at.

See [[velcro-the-attachments-that-remain]] for the related concept about the honest acknowledgment of what still has us.

## The Image and the Conventional Spiritual Bypass

The roller-coaster-from-the-ground image is also a corrective to the spiritual bypass that often gets confused with freedom. The bypass version: I have transcended outcome-attachment. I am unaffected. Nothing touches me. I am free.

This sounds like the image. It is the opposite of the image. The bypass version is a new identity (the unaffected one) being defended as fiercely as any other identity. The defending is the tell. The person who has actually arrived at the ground is not defending the ground; the ground does not require defense because it is not a position. The person in the bypass is defending the position because the position is being held against the continuing pull of outcome-attachment that has not actually released.

The roller-coaster-from-the-ground image admits the pull. Jerry admits the pull. Joe admits the pull (the storytelling about his daughters that follows immediately). The admitted pull is part of what makes the image structurally honest. Freedom does not mean the gravitational field has been turned off. Freedom means knowing what is essence and what is in the gravitational field, and not confusing them.

## How the Shift Happens

The shift from riding to watching from the ground is not produced by an act of will. The person cannot decide to stop boarding the roller coaster. The boarding is what the system is configured to do.

The shift comes through the same path the rest of the work goes through: feeling what has been unfelt, welcoming what has been resisted, letting the identities that were riding the roller coaster dissolve when they are met with full presence. As each identity releases its grip, the layer underneath it becomes available. Eventually the layer underneath all of them becomes available. That layer is the ground.

This is not a one-time event. It is a continuous practice. People who have done substantial work still find themselves on the roller coaster about specific domains (Jerry's children, Joe's children). The remaining rides are the remaining work. The ground is what is left when even those rides settle. The settling continues as long as the person continues the work.

See [[every-identity-has-limits]] for the structural reason this is a continuous process, and [[purpose-is-an-unfolding-not-a-destination]] for the related principle about the work never being done.

## When This Image Is Useful

- When someone is asking what freedom from outcome-attachment looks like and the conventional descriptions are not landing
- When someone is exhausted from the up-and-down of their work or life and is asking how to get steadier
- When someone has been working on equanimity and has produced something that looks more like flatness or numbness than freedom
- When the question is whether genuine freedom requires not caring
- When teaching about the difference between equanimity that includes care and equanimity that bypasses care
- When someone has had a recent run of outcomes and is being thrown around emotionally by them
- When a leader is asking how to be in a chaotic period without being destroyed by it
- When teaching about [[purpose-is-how-not-what]] -- the roller coaster is what; the ground is where the how comes from

## Tone

Jerry delivers the image with the directness of someone who has lived it. There is no piety. The roller coaster is real, the ride still happens for him about his kids, the essence is still distinguishable from the ride. The tone holds all three at once. Joe's affirmation is silent in the transcript -- he listens, then immediately follows with his own admission of how much he wants to tell stories about his daughters now. The follow-on is the modeling of the integration. The teaching is not "transcend the love"; the teaching is "love fully, and also know that the loving is happening on a layer that is not the only layer."
