# The Migratory Path (You Know How You're Supposed to Develop)

## Definition

There is a knowing inside the person about how they are supposed to evolve. The knowing is not conscious. It is the same kind of knowing a bird has about its migratory path home. A kid knows they are meant to walk at a certain age. The knowing arrives by mimicry partly, but it also arrives as an internal pull that says: this is what is next for me.

The migratory path is the developmental sequence that is already encoded. The person does not have to design it. They have to listen for it.

"We have, like, a migratory path in us. We know how we're supposed to evolve. We know how we're supposed to develop. We don't know it consciously, but we know it. Kids know that they're meant to walk when they're, when, when they get to a certain age and they, we do it through mimicry to some degree, but we just know that certain things are supposed to happen the way a bird knows its migratory, migratory path home." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## What This Implies for Experiments

The reason experiments are powerful is that they let the person follow the migratory path instead of following a teacher or a book. A book gives one or two epiphanies on first reading because those are the epiphanies the person is currently on the path for. The other content of the book does not land because it is not where the person is in the sequence.

"I could read a book and I might be able to get one or two big epiphanies out of it. And I read it again 6 months later and I get a different 2 or 3 epiphanies. I couldn't get the other ones 'cause it wasn't where I was on my path. But if you're following that, that, that migratory path, your experiments will be right on your path." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

The experiments designed from the migratory path are always the right experiments. There is no efficiency cost from doing the "wrong" experiment first. The pull is itself the curriculum-designer. Trust the pull, and the next experiment is the one the person needed to be doing.

## How to Listen for It

The migratory path is the pull, not the should. The person has to learn to distinguish the two. The should sounds like obligation: "I should be doing this, I should learn this." The pull sounds like curiosity, wonder, or pain that wants attention. Both can present as urges. They feel different in the body.

"It's not, how do I overcome this thing? It's, what do I want to wonder about? What do I explore? What do I not know about the pain? And then you're just on that migratory path." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

The discipline of listening to the pull rather than the should takes time to develop. It is one of the major learning effects of running experiments over time -- the person gets better at telling the pull from the should because experiments require the person to act on the pull and watch what happens. See [[should-vs-want-body-check]] for the body-felt diagnostic.

## What Counts as Listening

Three signals point to the migratory path being active:

**Wonder.** The thing the person genuinely wonders about. Not the thing they think they should be curious about. The thing where the question is alive in them.

**Pain.** The place where something is painful and the person does not yet know what to do about it. Pain that has not been resolved is one of the migratory path's main pointers. See [[where-you-are-is-the-lesson]] for the broader frame.

**Curiosity.** Specifically the small "what the fuck was that" moment that arrives when something unexpected happens. Joe's first experiment came from that moment: he traced a thought back to its origin once because someone had suggested it, the experience surprised him, and the next experiment built on the surprise.

## The Trust Component

The migratory path requires trust. The person has to trust that the next thing to learn is already known to them on some level. Without that trust, the person turns to books, teachers, and external authorities to tell them what to do next. With the trust, the person follows the internal sequence and the experiments build on each other in the right order.

"There's that. Category as well, that, that through line." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

The trust is not blind. It is developed by running experiments and watching them work. The first experiments are small. Each one teaches the person that the next pull was real. After many experiments, the trust in the migratory path becomes the operating mode. The person no longer needs the external authority because the internal authority has been validated by experience.

This is structurally what [[dont-take-my-word-for-it]] is pointing at. The reason to run an experiment rather than take Joe's word is not just so the person knows for themselves; it is so the person can develop trust in their own migratory path. The teacher's role is not to tell the student what is next. It is to help the student start listening.

## The Surrender Stage

Over years of running experiments, the relationship to the migratory path matures. The path becomes obvious. The experiment shows up in awareness and the person feels: that is the next thing. The act of running the experiment becomes a surrender into what the path is asking for.

"And now it's very much like a surrender. The experiment shows up and it's like, oh, that's obviously something I have to do. And I surrender into the experiment almost like it's a, you know, it's a traditional guru. Like my experiments are my gurus on some level." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

This is the third stage of the evolution-of-experimenter arc (rebellion -> internalization -> surrender). The migratory path was always operating; the person learns, over time, how to follow it more cleanly. See [[evolution-of-the-experimenter]].

## The Coach Also Follows the Client's Migratory Path

A second application of the concept that Joe surfaces in the Charisma conversation: the coach who is working with a client is, in the best case, following the client's migratory path rather than imposing their own. The client knows where they need to go next better than the coach does. The coach's job is to help the client listen to their own pull, not to design the path for them.

"My assumption is this is their birthright. That there's work to be done. But let's start with the assumption that this is your birthright. This isn't something that you have to achieve... I'm definitely — pattern recognition is happening. But what the other thing that's happening is I'm really letting them lead because somehow or another what I've noticed is, and it might not be the most efficient path, but it is a more efficient path than the other ones I've run across, which is to — that person knows where they need to go next better than I do." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The coaching application extends the metaphor: Joe adds the salmon and the tree alongside the bird.

"We have this innate migratory path in us, meaning that the way that a salmon knows how to get back to — we have this thing that pulls us into our own development. The way a tree knows how to extend towards the sun, we have our way of knowing how to extend towards the sun. And so generally what I'm trying to do is I'm trying to — I'm following that in the person." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

Three metaphors for the same structural feature: the salmon knows the route home; the bird knows the migration; the tree knows how to extend toward the sun. Each is an example of an organism with an internal navigator that does not need external instruction to find what it needs. The human migratory path is the same kind of navigator. The coach's work, when working with someone else, is to attend to *their* navigator, not to provide one.

This is also the structural reason for [[coach-as-sonar-ping-and-listen-for-where-theyre-ready]]. The pinging and listening is the operational form of following someone else's migratory path. The bounces from the pings tell the coach where the client is currently being pulled. The coach designs the next ping in service to that pull, not in service to a destination the coach has chosen.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a coachee is asking what they should do next and Joe wants to redirect them to their own pull
- When a coachee is over-relying on books, teachers, or external authorities
- When a coachee is afraid that without external guidance they will go in the wrong direction
- When a coachee is following a should rather than a want and the experiment they are designing is not theirs
- When the question is "how do I know what to work on next?" and the answer is the migratory path is already telling them
- When training other coaches who are tempted to design destinations for their clients rather than attending to where the clients are being pulled

## Tone

Joe describes this concept with quiet conviction. The metaphor (a bird's migratory path) is delivered without mysticism -- it is named as a structural fact about how humans work, not as a special spiritual claim. The certainty is grounded in his own experience of running experiments for decades and watching the pull lead him to the right thing each time.
