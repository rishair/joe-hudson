# The Idea of Self vs. The Actual Self

## Definition

People do not typically fall in love with themselves. They fall in love with an *idea* of themselves -- a constructed self-image, often impressive, often coherent, often well-defended. The trouble is not the idea. The trouble is when the idea is held as if it were the actual self, and the actual self (which is messier, more contradictory, often less impressive in the ways the idea was) cannot be met.

The most useful framing comes from a phrase Aaron Taylor offered and Joe responded to immediately:

"Narcissists don't fall in love with themselves. They fall in love with the idea of themselves." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

"Love that one." -- Joe Hudson, Feel Your Way to Freedom

"That's good." -- Joe Hudson, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The phrase makes a distinction that the colloquial understanding of narcissism misses. The narcissist's problem is not too much self-love. It is the substitution of an idea of self for the actual self, with all the love going to the idea.

## The Mechanism

The idea of self has a life of its own. It accumulates accomplishments, identities, roles, stories about who the person is and what they have done. It is the version of the person that gets presented in conversation, on a resume, in a wedding toast. It is constructed from feedback over decades and is generally well-maintained.

The actual self is whatever is actually happening. It includes the contradictions, the parts that do not fit the idea, the moments that the idea has to edit out to remain coherent. The actual self is necessarily more multifaceted than any idea of it can be.

The trouble arises when the two diverge significantly and the person continues operating from the idea. The actual self leaks out anyway -- in the body, in the way relationships actually function, in the felt quality of the person's life. The discrepancy produces a particular kind of suffering. The person works to maintain an idea that has less and less to do with what is actually true, and the cost of maintenance rises.

## The Blind Date

Aaron's image for the moment when the discrepancy comes into view:

"The idea of myself has been meeting my actual self on a profound level over the last couple weeks, and it's not going so well. It's like a blind date from hell in certain respects." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The image is precise. A blind date involves meeting someone you have only had ideas about. The disappointment is structural: the idea was never accurate, because it was an idea, not a person. When the actual person shows up, the gap is felt.

The same thing happens internally. The idea of self has been the operating frame for years or decades. The actual self has been continuing to exist underneath, occasionally surfacing, mostly suppressed. When the two meet in a sustained way -- often through some combination of crisis, awakening, sustained work, or grace -- the meeting can be hard. The idea has to die some, or be revised, or be relinquished. The actual self has to be met without the protection of the idea.

The meeting is hard in a specific way: the actual self is often grievable. The person has lost time being the idea. The relationships that were calibrated to the idea have to readjust. The accomplishments that were earned by the idea may register differently when the actual self looks at them.

## Why Joe Lands on This

Joe's immediate response ("Love that one. That's good") signals that the phrase captured something he had been articulating in other ways. The body of his teaching has many adjacent framings:

- [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]: improvement strengthens the idea, authenticity meets the actual.
- [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]]: the fears that need feeling are fears of losing the idea; what survives is the actual.
- [[self-realization]]: the question "what am I" addresses the actual, not the idea.
- [[inherent-goodness]]: the idea is often built to compensate for the felt absence of inherent goodness; the actual self does not need the compensation.

The "idea of self" framing organizes these around a specific distinction that is operationally useful: the work is not to improve the self. It is to release the *idea of the self* and let the actual self be met.

## The Narcissism Inversion

The colloquial reading of narcissism is that the narcissist loves themselves too much. The Aaron/Joe reading inverts this. The narcissist cannot love themselves at all, because they have never met themselves. All their love is going to an idea. The actual self, never met, never loved.

This explains the brittleness of the narcissistic structure. The idea must be defended at all costs because there is nothing else. If the idea fails, there is no actual self that has been related to. The defense produces the behaviors that the colloquial reading of narcissism identifies: the rage at small slights (any threat to the idea is existential), the inability to apologize (apology would acknowledge the gap), the grandiosity (the idea must be inflated to stay coherent).

The healing is not to love themselves less. It is to begin meeting the actual self, which has been waiting underneath the idea for whoever shows up.

## The Universal Application

The Aaron/Joe framing is sharp enough to apply to clinical narcissism, but the concept generalizes. Most people have some version of this. The "idea of self" is the work-in-progress that gets shown to others. The actual self is what continues to exist underneath. The degree to which someone is healthy is roughly the degree to which the gap is small and the contact is frequent.

Most coaching work, at some level, is an introduction between the idea of self and the actual self. The framework, the practices, the questions are all in service of that meeting. The meeting goes well when the idea has been held lightly and the actual self has been allowed to surface periodically. It goes hard when the idea has been gripped tightly for decades.

## What This Concept Produces in Coaching

A coachee who is performing the idea of themselves can sometimes be invited into the actual self with this distinction. Naming the two ("there is an idea of you, and there is an actual you, and right now I'm meeting the idea") opens a different kind of contact. The coachee can either defend the idea harder (which is information) or begin to drop into the actual self in front of the coach (which is the doorway).

The coach's job is not to break the idea. The idea is doing work. The coach's job is to make space for the actual self to surface, and then to be in contact with what is actually there. The idea will release its grip on its own once the actual self has been welcomed enough times.

## The Idea Often Operates Subconsciously

A sharpening of the concept that comes from the identity teaching: the idea of self does not have to be conscious to be operating. Most of the idea is built below the level of self-report. The person can describe their conscious self-image with confidence and still be governed by a deeper idea they have never named.

Joe gives the studied example: kids told "you are smart" become unwilling to try after failing, because trying and failing would prove the identity wrong. The "smart" identity is operating, but the kids may not be able to articulate it. They just stop trying.

"Maybe those kids could say, 'I have an identity of being smart.' Maybe those kids just think they're smart. Maybe those kids aren't even conscious of that subconscious identity that's occurring." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

His own example: a recurring emotional sequence -- chasing something he could not get, then feeling deprived and resentful -- had been operating as identity across money, bosses, his father, women's love. Each domain was the same script with a different cast. It was "how he knew himself to be," but it was not in his conscious self-description.

"That chain of emotional experiences had become a part of my identity, but it was subconscious. I had no idea that until that moment, I had no idea that that was part of the identity and that it had played out with money. It had played out with bosses. It had played out with my dad. It played out with women's love." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

The implication for the idea-of-self frame: the idea is rarely fully visible. The conscious self-description is often the smaller, more curated layer. The deeper layer is the recurring emotional and behavioral patterns that the person experiences as "just how I am." The blind date with the actual self requires meeting this deeper, often-subconscious idea, not only the polished one.

## What Someone Experiences

The internal experience of the gap, when it comes into view:

- A sense that "I have been living for someone who is not actually me."
- A grief about lost time spent being an impressive version of oneself rather than an actual person.
- A felt distance from one's own accomplishments, which were earned by the idea and don't quite belong to the actual self.
- A sense that the people who love the idea may not know the actual self.
- A fear of what it would mean to be met as the actual self, including by oneself.

These experiences are not problems to be solved. They are the felt sense of the meeting beginning to happen. The work is to stay with them rather than to retreat into a new improved idea of self.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A coachee describes a high-functioning life that does not feel like theirs.
- A coachee reports that the people who love them do not really know them.
- A coachee uses phrases like "I feel like a fraud" or "I'm pretending" inside contexts where they are objectively succeeding.
- A coachee is in a transition where the idea that worked is no longer working.
- The narcissism word comes up, either in self-description or in description of someone else, and the colloquial frame is producing more shame than insight.
