# The Small Untruth in Every Thought

## Definition

Every statement the recurring negative [[voice-in-the-head]] makes contains a small untruth. Not a wholesale lie. A slight misalignment, an unexamined assumption, a logical gap. The work is to find the untruth, not to argue with the whole statement.

This is one of the most reliable ways to dismantle the voice's authority. The voice presents its statements as truth. Locate the small false thing inside them and the truth-claim collapses. What remains is the part that is actually accurate, which is usually a fraction of what the voice was insisting on.

"To really look and find out that there's a little bit of untruth in everything the voice in the head says, everything the voice in the head says, and to find that, it gives you a lot of freedom and perspective from the voice in the head itself." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

## The Voice Contradicts Itself

The clearest evidence the voice is not a truth-teller: it asserts contradictory things in the same week, often the same day.

"You were too cocky there. You were too humble there. You spoke too much. You didn't listen enough. You listened too much. You didn't speak enough." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

If both contradictory statements were true, both would have to be wrong. The voice is not delivering accurate feedback. It is delivering whatever criticism is most available, regardless of internal consistency. Once this is seen, the next statement the voice makes is much harder to swallow whole.

"If you really start looking at how the voice in the head operates, it doesn't give you actually a place to succeed often. It's like there's no way out. Everything's — there's a problem with everything." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

## What the Untruth Usually Is

Common locations of the small untruth:

**The unexamined "should."** "I should lose weight." According to whom? By what standard? The voice rarely has an answer. "Should" in this usage means "what is," but the speaker is not the weight they "are." The grammar collapses on inspection. See [[should-as-threat]].

**The hidden binary.** "Either I apologize or I look weak." Almost never is the situation actually binary. The voice presents two options and pretends those are the only ones. See [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]].

**The catastrophic consequence.** "If I say this, they'll leave." The predicted disaster is usually a fraction as likely as the voice claims. See [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]].

**The unfounded authority.** "Everyone will think you're an idiot." Everyone? Who specifically? The voice generalizes wildly.

**The assumed permanence.** "You'll always be this way." Always? Based on what? Patterns change all the time.

**The smuggled assumption.** "You should have known better." Should you have? With what information? The voice often retroactively assigns knowledge the speaker did not possess.

## The Lawyer Move

The practice of locating the untruth is best expressed as the lawyer move. Become an argumentative lawyer to the voice — not an adversary, but a lawyer doing their job.

Voice: "I should lose weight."
Lawyer:
- Should according to whom?
- What do you mean by should? Isn't "should" supposed to mean "what is"?
- Shouldn't you be the weight you are?
- You should lose weight because what — you'll die early?
- What makes dying early bad?
- You've been saying this for a decade and it hasn't worked. So what makes you keep saying it?
- Maybe you should say "I want to lose weight."
- What's the response to "I should lose weight"? Usually it's rebellion. So the voice isn't even helping with what it claims to want.

See [[lawyer-the-voice]] for the full practice.

## What This Produces

When the small untruth is located, three things happen:

1. **The voice loses some authority.** It is harder to take it as a truth-teller after it has been caught misleading. The next time it asserts something, there is a built-in pause.

2. **The actual truth, if any, becomes available.** Often there is something real underneath the voice's framing. "I'd like to feel better in my body" is closer to the truth than "I should lose weight." The lawyer process surfaces what was actually wanted.

3. **The voice gets quieter.** Repeatedly locating its untruths reduces its grip. Not by attacking it, but by exposing its weak claims to ordinary inquiry. See [[awareness-as-change-agent]].

## The Truth Inside the Untruth

Joe explicitly addresses the obvious objection: what about the times the voice is right? What about when you actually did mess up?

The answer separates the recurring negative voice from accurate, useful self-correction. If a person notices "I did that wrong" once, takes action to correct it, and moves on, that is functional feedback. That is not the recurring negative voice. That is integrity in action.

"If you're in your head saying, wow, you screwed it up with the client, and that, say, happens once, then it's not a recurring negative thing, and you immediately take action on it, then whatever's happening is it's an effective, efficient cycle. But if you're saying it multiple times and doing nothing about it, or you're saying it 20 times and then doing something about it. That is not an efficient cycle. That's just self-abuse." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The diagnostic: how many times has it been said, and has any action followed? The voice that says it once and prompts action is signal. The voice that says it twenty times without action is the abusive recurring voice.

The truth inside the untruth is often available, but rarely in the form the voice is delivering it. The voice's framing distorts even the accurate observations.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The voice has been saying the same critical thing for years and the criticism has not produced change.
- The person notices the voice contradicting itself.
- The person is starting to doubt that the voice is delivering accurate information.
- The person wants to push back on the voice but does not want to fight it.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[voice-in-the-head]] has been named and the person is ready to investigate its content.
- When [[believing-the-convenient-lie]] is showing up at the self-talk level.
- When the person is locked in rumination and needs a way out other than suppression.

## Tone

Investigative, playful, slightly mischievous. Joe approaches the voice's claims the way a curious lawyer approaches a witness's story — assuming there are holes and looking for them with interest, not malice. The energy is not "I'm going to destroy you" but "let's see what you actually have here."

## Boundary Conditions

This practice fails if it becomes another form of self-attack. "Now I'll prove the voice is lying so I can shame myself for believing it" is the same dynamic in new clothes. The practice works when it carries the lightness of inquiry, not the heaviness of trying to win.

It also fails if the untruth is used to dismiss genuinely useful feedback. If the voice is pointing at something real that needs attention, the move is not to lawyer it away — it is to take action and let the voice subside.
