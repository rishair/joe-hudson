# Two Levels of Hurt in Being Misunderstood

## Definition

When someone reports the pain of being misunderstood, two distinct hurts are usually stacked on top of each other. The first is the obvious one: I did not feel understood. The second is rarely named but is often the deeper wound: the goodness I was bringing was not recognized. The first is about content. The second is about the giver.

"There's almost two levels of hurt when someone doesn't understand you. The first level of hurt is that you don't feel understood. The second level of hurt is like I would say that you don't feel like the goodness in which you bring is being recognized." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

The two hurts look like one hurt from the outside. The person reports the surface complaint: "they don't understand me." But the visceral pain is more often on the second layer. The speaker is offering something — an intention, a care, a gift — and the failure to be received feels like rejection of the offering, not just of the content.

## In Practice

The diagnostic is in the body. The first-layer hurt is frustrating but bearable. The second-layer hurt produces a specific quality of grief or shame, as if the person themselves were being seen as not worth receiving. When the coach names the second layer accurately, the coachee often responds with surprise and recognition. Joe's coaching session with Savannah gave a vivid example:

- **Joe:** "What I notice is there's almost two levels of hurt when someone doesn't understand you. The first level of hurt is that you don't feel understood. The second level of hurt is like I would say that you don't feel like the goodness in which you bring is being recognized."
- **Savannah:** "Bro, how can you see me like that? That's exactly right. Yeah, like, I'm coming into this with truly good intentions, with seeking understanding and, like, you know, communication and connection, really. Oh my God, that's what it is. I'm seeking connection, and when people are choosing to misunderstand me, it's like they're saying No, we're not."
- *(Source: 2024-04-12 How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?)*

The naming opened the door to the actual wound. Until then, the coachee was working on a surface problem ("how do I get them to understand me"). Once the second-layer hurt was visible, the conversation could move to what was actually being defended.

## When Someone Experiences This

Common experience: the person can name many specific instances of being misunderstood. They can recite the words, the moments, the failures of reception. But the felt pain is disproportionate to what those moments would warrant if the issue were only content. The disproportion is the second layer leaking through.

Phrases that signal the second layer is operating:

- "I'm coming into this with truly good intentions."
- "I just want to connect."
- "I'm trying to do the right thing here."
- "They're choosing to misunderstand me."
- "It feels personal."
- "I'm a careful, kind person — why am I being received this way?"

The second-layer hurt also explains why technique-based communication advice often fails. The person is not actually trying to solve a content problem. They are trying to repair a felt sense that what they are offering is being received as nothing. Better phrasing does not fix that. Being recognized as a giver of good things does.

## What the Coach Does With This

Name it when you see it. The naming itself is often the intervention. The coachee usually has not articulated the second layer to themselves; hearing it from the coach gives them language for what they have been carrying.

Once it is named, the path forward is not to chase external validation of the goodness. That is the same chasing pattern in a different costume. The path is to bring the recognition inside. The coachee learns to recognize their own goodness — to register it themselves — rather than requiring an external listener to confirm it. From that place, external recognition becomes a welcome bonus rather than a desperate need.

A useful next move: Joe pointed out that he himself had been understanding her without issue, and that her own response — "bro, how can you see me like that?" — was evidence that being seen was possible and was happening in the room. The contact had landed. The coachee just needed to register the landing. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] for the receiving practice.

## What This Is Not

This is not the claim that all misunderstanding involves a second-layer hurt. Sometimes communication just fails on content. The diagnostic for the second layer is the intensity and quality of the pain — disproportionate to the content failure, with a personal sting that does not resolve when the content is clarified.

This is also not a claim that the second layer is the deepest layer. Often there is a third layer underneath: the childhood imprint that taught the person their happiness was dependent on someone else's reaction. See [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]] for the path from the second layer to the underlying imprint.

## Tone

Joe names the second layer with quiet conviction. The naming is not delivered as an insight to be admired; it is delivered as a observation that the coachee can verify in their own body. When Savannah confirms it ("bro, how can you see me like that?"), Joe does not seize the moment for emphasis. He lets the recognition land and then moves the work forward.
