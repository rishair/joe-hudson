# Velcro: The Attachments That Remain Even After the Work

## The Image

After substantial work, some attachments release. The deal-closing version of you stops being thrown around by every outcome. The needing-to-be-right version of you softens. The needing-to-be-admired version of you fades. The conditions for self-worth and safety that used to require constant external maintenance start to be available from inside.

And then there are the places where the attachment does not release. The kids. The grandkids. The ones you love who are struggling. The pull is still there. The Velcro still sticks.

Jerry's framing: name the Velcro honestly. Do not pretend the work has produced more freedom than it has. The Velcro that remains is what remains. Naming it is part of the freedom, not a failure of it.

"I am not free of that Velcro. It still gets stuck. And it gets stuck for me. You know, I'm the old fart in the room, right? My kids are 34, 32, and 28. And so where I go is, will they have the life that I want them to have? And will their children have the life. When I see people that I love struggling, you have a kid who goes to college and they're having a rough experience. Oh my God, my heart breaks. That's where I'm still riding the roller coaster. And I don't know that I'll ever be free of that one. And I don't know that I want to be free of that one." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## Why Naming the Velcro Matters

The work of releasing attachments tends to attract a counterfeit version: the person who claims to have released more than they have. The counterfeit is durable because it is socially rewarded. The person who claims full freedom from outcome-attachment gets read as advanced. The person who admits to still being pulled gets read as not yet there.

Naming the Velcro inverts this social pressure. The honest admission is the move. The unsaid Velcro becomes the defended position. The said Velcro becomes integrated material that does not have to be defended.

Jerry's admission is the modeling. He has done substantial work. He has been a CEO, a VC, a coach, an author. He has been doing the inner work for decades. And he is the one who says, on the record, that he is not free of the Velcro about his children. The honesty is what makes his work credible. The honesty is also what keeps the work true.

The counter-version: the person who has been doing the work for a decade and reports that they are now free of attachment to their children's wellbeing. The report is suspect. Either the person is not paying attention, or the report is a defended position, or both. The Velcro about children is structurally hard to release because it is built into the parental wiring at a level that the inner work cannot fully reach. The honest admission is structurally aligned with what is actually true about the system.

## And I Don't Know That I Want to Be Free of That One

Jerry adds a second clause that completes the teaching: "And I don't know that I want to be free of that one."

This matters because it inverts the implicit assumption that all attachment-release is desirable. Some Velcro is the thing that makes the love possible. Releasing every attachment would mean releasing the conditions that allow caring about anyone. The freedom-from-attachment teaching is not the freedom-from-loving teaching. The work releases the suffering that attachment produces when it is unconscious. It does not release the conditions for caring.

Jerry is naming a specific class of Velcro that he would not release even if he could. This is structurally different from "I haven't gotten to that one yet." It is "this one is part of the design." His daughter's wedding day is supposed to be a real feeling. The "Daddy, thank you for giving me the most beautiful day of my life" is supposed to land. If those landings stopped, something would be lost that should not be lost.

The teaching is therefore not "release all Velcro." The teaching is "release the unconscious Velcro that produces suffering, and let the conscious Velcro that produces love continue to operate." The first reduces the suffering. The second preserves the loving.

## How This Connects to Riding the Roller Coaster From the Ground

The [[riding-the-roller-coaster-from-the-ground]] image points at the goal: enjoy the ride from the ground, do not attach essence to outcome. The Velcro concept points at the honest current state: there are still places where the boarding happens. Both are true at the same time. The image is the direction; the Velcro is the position.

The integration is in holding both. Without the image, the Velcro can be experienced as proof that no freedom is possible. Without the Velcro, the image can be performed as a position rather than lived as a direction. With both, the work is honest: here is the direction, here is where I currently am, here is what is still pulling, here is what I am okay with continuing to be pulled by.

## The Diagnostic Question: Where Is Your Velcro?

The concept becomes operational through the question. When working with someone who has done substantial inner work, the question is not "are you free." The question is "where is your Velcro?"

The honest answer is the data. It tells you:

- Where the remaining work is, if the person wants to do it
- Which attachments the person would not release even if they could (these are the love-attachments)
- Whether the person is in honest contact with their actual current state or is performing a position
- What domain the most current emotional weight is concentrated in (often children, often parents, often a specific creative project, often a specific relationship)

The person who cannot name any Velcro is either uniquely far along (rare) or has lost contact with the layer where the Velcro lives (more common). The person who can name the Velcro specifically is generally in honest contact with their work.

## Joe's Velcro

Joe's response to Jerry's velcro admission is to immediately want to tell stories about his daughters. The transition is not commentary on the principle. It is the live demonstration of his own version of the same Velcro. The teaching is not delivered as separate from the teacher. The teacher is showing where his own Velcro is, in real time, in the same conversation that named the concept.

"All I want to do now is tell stories about my daughters." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

This is what the integrated form of the teaching looks like. The principle has been named. The teachers are openly admitting their own current Velcro. The Velcro is not a problem to be solved; it is acknowledged material that continues to operate while the work continues. Nothing is being hidden. Nothing is being claimed that is not present.

## The Velcro That You Want to Stay Stuck Is the Velcro That Produces Love

A specific operational extension. Some Velcro is the channel through which love flows. Cutting it would not produce more freedom; it would produce less love. Jerry's Velcro about his children is in this category. Joe's is too. The wedding day, the daughter calling "Daddy," the grandchildren-to-come -- these are not problems. They are the channels.

The teaching gives the person permission to distinguish:

- Velcro that produces suffering and would be useful to release (the unconscious, automatic attachments to outcomes, recognition, identity)
- Velcro that produces love and is supposed to stay (the conscious, chosen attachments to people, work, and life)

The work releases the first and protects the second. Confusing the two -- trying to release the love-Velcro because it looks like attachment -- is a failure mode. The Velcro that you would not release even if you could is structurally different from the Velcro that has you against your will.

## Tone

Jerry's tone in delivering this is direct, slightly self-deprecating ("I'm the old fart in the room"), warm. There is no shame in the admission of the Velcro. There is no pride either. The admission is the modeling. The Velcro is data, not character. The tone matches the position: the Velcro is real and acknowledged, and that is what makes the rest of the conversation honest.

Joe's tone in immediately demonstrating his own version of the Velcro is matching. The teachers are not above the principle. They are inside it. The intimacy of the demonstration is what makes the teaching land.
