# Forgiveness as "Stop Making Them a Monster"

## Definition

Forgiveness in the deep healing sense is not absolving the harm, agreeing the harm was okay, or returning to relationship with the harm-doer. It is the specific move of stopping the construction of the other person as a monster. It requires opening the heart to them while continuing to hold that what they did was not okay.

"In your healing process, there's some way that you're going to have to give up on the fact that the person who raped you was wrong. When I see people heal from deep trauma, deep pain, they have to go through a process of not holding the other [as wrong]." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The structural shape: "yes, what happened was not okay" can coexist with "and they are a human being, not a monster, and my heart is open to them." Until both are true, the healing has not completed.

## What This Is Not

**Not minimization.** "It was totally not okay for that to happen to me. It's totally not okay for that person to do it for me." The harm is named clearly. The not-okayness is preserved.

**Not reconciliation.** Forgiveness in this sense does not require returning to relationship, allowing access, or being present to the harm-doer in any way. The internal work of opening the heart is separable from the external action of being in proximity. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] for how the two can be split.

**Not a moral obligation.** This is not "you should forgive." Joe is describing the structural mechanics of healing — what he has observed across many cases. The work is hard and slow. The recommendation to do it is functional (it produces healing) rather than moral (you should).

**Not religious forgiveness.** The concept overlaps with religious teachings on forgiveness but does not derive from them. The framing is psychological-structural. The mechanism is the same one that operates in [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]] — the heart cannot open while the head is enforcing the wrongness.

**Not erasing memory.** The harm continues to be remembered. The body continues to know what happened. What changes is the framing of the harm-doer's humanity.

## Why "Wrong" Has to Be Released

The structural argument: holding someone as wrong requires the heart to close to them. The heart that is closed cannot heal. Healing is the heart's work, and the heart works when it is open.

"You can say, yeah, it was totally not okay for that to happen to me. It's totally not okay for that person to do it for me. But you can't see them as wrong and still heal." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

This is the same mechanism as in [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]]: the thought-frame "they are wrong" enables the body-state "closed heart." The body-state is the obstacle to healing. So the thought-frame has to release, or the healing cannot complete.

This is non-obvious because it appears unfair. The harm-doer did wrong; why should the survivor have to give up the wrongness-judgment? The answer is structural, not moral. The survivor is the one healing. The wrongness-judgment is what is preventing the healing. The release is for the survivor, not for the harm-doer.

## The Direction of the Work

The work runs from "they are a monster" to "they are a human being who did this thing." The transition takes time, often years. The intermediate states are valid.

What helps:
- Slowness. The work cannot be rushed. Pushing oneself to forgive before the heart is ready is a form of self-violence.
- Feeling the anger and hurt first. The wrongness-frame is often holding the anger and hurt at a distance. Letting the feeling come through is what eventually loosens the frame.
- Recognition that they were also shaped by what shaped them. This is not an excuse for what they did. It is a recognition of their full humanity. (Brett's framing in the same conversation: "oh yeah, my parents were scared. And that's like their — their fear came out in a certain way.")
- The body-felt sense of one's own closed heart as a cost. When the heart-closure begins to be felt as the survivor's own suffering rather than as appropriate moral response, the motivation for the work becomes available.

What does not help:
- "Forgive and forget."
- Being told that holding the wrongness is itself wrong (the meta-judgment recreates the problem).
- Trying to feel compassion for the harm-doer before the anger and hurt have moved.
- Religious or cultural pressure to forgive on a timeline.

## What Forgiveness Produces

"Then seeing through and just seeing the entire situation for what it was both relieves us of any self-judgment we have, relieves us of judgment of the other, and also gives us a deeper perspective to avoid those outcomes again because we can see them coming from a mile away." -- Brett Kistler, Question the Assumption (compressed framing Joe affirms)

Three things:

1. **Self-judgment releases.** The wrongness-frame often turns on the self too: "I should have stopped it," "I should be over this by now." When the frame releases from the other, it also releases from the self.
2. **Judgment of the other releases.** The structural and felt closure dissolves. The heart opens. The survivor is no longer carrying the closed-heart cost.
3. **Pattern-recognition becomes available.** With the wrongness-frame no longer organizing the perception, the actual mechanics of what happened become visible. The survivor can see the patterns from a distance and recognize them in advance the next time they appear.

The third is the unexpected gift. The wrongness-frame functions as a kind of fog. It feels protective ("I will never let this happen again, because I will recognize this monster") but it actually obscures the actual signs and dynamics. The released frame produces clearer pattern-recognition than the held frame ever did.

## Healing of Childhood Trauma

The same pattern applies in childhood-trauma work. The child's framing was often "my parent was wrong/bad/scary." The healing requires moving to "my parent was scared/limited/shaped by their own wound." The truth of what happened is preserved. The framing of the parent shifts.

This is consistent with [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] and the surrounding family-work concerns. The internal forgiveness move can happen even when the external relationship is appropriately cut off. They are separate.

The body of the child often resists this move because the wrongness-frame felt protective. Letting the parent be human in the framing can feel like losing the protection. The work is to discover that the wrongness-frame was never actually protective — it was just costly — and that the protection comes from the released perception, not the held grievance.

## The Connection to Right-and-Wrong Falling Away

This is a specific case of [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]]. The general principle (right and wrong loses grip at thought-level, becomes commanding at action-level) appears here as: the survivor cannot heal while holding the harm-doer as wrong at the thought-level, and the only available action that produces healing is the opening of the heart.

It is also a specific application of [[dehumanization-begins-with-right-and-wrong]]. Holding the harm-doer as a "monster" is dehumanization, with the same structure as larger-scale dehumanization. The cost is the same: heart-closure, the structural impossibility of the move (healing) that requires open-heartedness.

## The Sibling Framing: Forgiveness as Self-Care

[[forgiveness-as-self-care]] is the AOA-Tara Howley framing of the same work from a different angle. Where this article focuses on what releases (the wrongness-frame, the monster-construction), the self-care framing focuses on why the work is being done (to care for your own heart, to restore the body to openness).

Both arrive at the same place — the open heart. The mechanism is the same. The framings are complementary tools for the coach:

- Use this article's framing when the obstacle is the structural wrongness-judgment that the person cannot release.
- Use the self-care framing when the obstacle is corruption of the word forgiveness by religious or cultural coercion. The reframe gives the work back to the person.

The two articles cross-reference each other and the further articles in the cluster: [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]] for the actual process, [[forgiveness-invites-different-reality]] for the relational side-effect, [[forgiveness-changes-identity]] for the deeper soul-work dimension.

## The Critical Caveat

The fightability of the situation is preserved. Forgiveness as "stop making them a monster" does not mean accepting the harm or ceasing to act to prevent more of it.

"That doesn't mean what is happening is totally not okay. And I'm even to the point of, I'm going to fight to stop that. Great. Not saying not to do that. I'm just saying you can do it with an open heart and you can do it without making the other an animal or taking steps to make them less than you." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

A survivor can pursue legal action, advocate for systemic change, refuse all contact, and forgive in this sense — all at once. The forgiveness is internal. The external actions can be whatever serves safety and justice.

## When This Is Premature

The move cannot be done from the wrong stage. A person still in the immediate aftermath of harm, still in active danger, still in the anger-and-hurt stage, is not ready. Pushing the move at this stage is harm in itself.

The move becomes available when:
- The harm is no longer immediate.
- Safety has been established.
- The anger and hurt have been felt enough to begin to move.
- The cost of the closed heart has begun to be noticed.
- The person is asking, in their own way, how to be done with this.

The coach's job is to recognize the readiness rather than impose it.

## Tone

Joe holds this carefully. He is explicit about what it does not mean before he says what it does mean, because the misreadings are easy and damaging. The tone is grave for the harm and gentle for the survivor. There is no pressure in the framing. The work is the survivor's, on the survivor's timeline.
