# The Fear of Being Destroyed by Your Own Anger

## Definition

The most cited fear about anger is that anger will destroy something external -- a relationship, a person, a friendship. Underneath this stated fear is a less-spoken one: that the person will be destroyed by their own anger. That if they let it out, they will lose themselves in it.

"It's not really about this fear of destruction that's happening. It's really about the fear that I will be destroyed by my own anger. If I let anger out, I might lose myself in it." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

This deeper fear is the actual driver of the resistance. The stated fear (I will destroy them) is a more socially acceptable version of the underlying fear (I will lose me).

## How the Fear Reveals Itself

The fear is visible in what the mind does not consider. When a person is angry at a friend, the available options usually collapse to two: get angry at them, or repress the anger.

"What makes that happen? If you really think about that for a second, it's like, so I'm mad at a friend, and then I tell myself, well, I can't get angry at them because I might lose my friendship with them, so I'll just go get angry somewhere else. Nobody thinks that." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

Going to the beach to yell. Writing something angry and burning it. Driving and yelling. These options exist and are obvious in retrospect, but they do not arise in real time. The blindness to the third option is the diagnostic. If the fear were really about the friendship, the mind would search for alternatives. The mind does not search because the actual fear is about losing self-coherence in the anger itself, and any expression of the anger triggers that fear.

## Why the Fear Has Weight

The fear is not irrational. If anger has been suppressed for years or decades, the held charge is large. The first expression of it can feel overwhelming because the volume of held material is overwhelming. The body's expectation, calibrated to the held charge, predicts loss of control.

Two things to know about this prediction:

**First, the loss-of-control prediction is about kinked anger, not anger.** See [[kinked-emotions]]. The version of anger people fear is what anger becomes when it has been resisted for years. Unkinked anger -- anger that has been allowed to flow daily -- does not feel like loss of control. It feels like clarity.

**Second, the felt charge is movable.** Joe's daily emotional yoga practice exists precisely because the held charge can be released in private, deliberately, over time. Each release lowers the charge of the next one. After enough release, anger arises and moves through in five minutes without drama. See [[daily-emotional-yoga]].

## The Self-Fulfilling Loop

The fear of being destroyed by anger keeps the person from releasing it. The unreleased anger builds. The built-up anger, when it finally comes out, does in fact feel destructive (because it is the kinked, accumulated version). The destructive expression confirms the original fear. The loop closes.

This is one instance of the larger pattern in [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The fear of anger's destructive power produces the conditions that make anger destructive. Releasing the fear (by allowing daily release) defuses both the charge and the destruction.

## What the Fear Is Right About

There is a subtler truth inside the fear. Allowing anger fully does destroy something -- specifically, the hard-edged sense of separate self. As anger becomes fluid and stops being personal, the person becomes less the distinguished bounded individual. Something does dissolve.

"If you allow anger to fully move through you, then there is a way it destroys everything, particularly yourself. Meaning, being angry at people when it's repressed is a way that you create separation... And when anger is fluid and you see that it's not about them or you, and there's no stories, and it's not personal, it's just something that moves through you, then life becomes impersonal. And therefore you are less that distinguished, hard-edged human." -- Joe Hudson, Stages of Emotional Development

The fear is sensing this real thing -- a softening of the rigid self -- and describing it as destruction. The work is not to dismiss the fear but to see what it is pointing at and discover that what is on the other side of the feared dissolution is not catastrophe but fluidity.

## In Coaching

When a person says "I can't get angry because I'll lose control," the coaching path:

1. Acknowledge the fear is reasonable given the held charge.
2. Distinguish kinked anger (what they fear) from anger (the actual emotion). See [[kinked-emotions]].
3. Make the "third option" visible. They can go yell at the beach. They can drive. They can write and burn. The choice is not just at-the-person-or-not.
4. Begin with a small, contained release in private. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]].
5. Track the change in baseline charge over weeks. The fear adjusts as the held charge drops.

When a person says "I never get angry," check whether the absence is real or a defense against this fear. The check: ask what they are determined about, what they care about that has been wronged. If there is no energy in the answer, the fear is likely doing the work of keeping anger out of awareness.

## Tone

Joe holds this gently. The fear is real, the person is not weak for having it. The work is patient and incremental. The fear is not the enemy; it is what the system has been doing to protect itself.
