# Projections

## Definition

A projection is a learned assumption laid over present reality. The word covers four distinct phenomena that all share one structure: the mind imposes a pattern from the past onto what is actually happening now, and then experiences the pattern as reality.

There are four kinds, distinguished by what is being projected and onto what:

1. **Psychological projection.** The parts of yourself you cannot own — good or bad — get projected onto other people. Triggering, idolizing, and accusing are the signals.
2. **Projection onto the world.** What you learned in the first 8 or 9 years about how money, love, authority, or any other category works gets recreated in your adult life. You attract, manipulate, and prove the pattern.
3. **Projection of self.** You do not see the world; you see yourself in the world. A thief sees a world of thieves. Someone full of self-love sees a world trying to love itself.
4. **Projection of I.** The most foundational: the assumption that there is a separate "you" at all. The identity that feels solid is itself a projection over awareness.

The four are not separate problems. They are nested levels of the same mechanism. The deeper you see through one, the more available the others become.

## The Four Types in Joe's Words

**Psychological projection:**
"The parts of ourselves that we can't own, the parts of ourselves that are either good or bad, but that we can't have full ownership over, we project onto other people. And this is something that happens when you're deeply triggered." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

The accusation is the giveaway: "as if you can presume to know what their reality is. So that's projecting the unknown parts of ourselves." Admiration works the same way: "if you see somebody as like super brilliant, there's no person I've met that doesn't have their own level of brilliance in some capacity. And so if you see that, admire that, put that up on a pedestal, it's a strong indicator that you can't see it in yourself."

See [[mirror-the-judgment]] and [[how-is-that-not-true-about-you]] for the coaching move that works directly with this layer.

**Projection onto the world:**
"How we lived our first 8 or 9 years of life when we're in theta brainwaves and where we're basically learning what life is. And we might learn that love is associated with shame, or money is associated with lack, or authority is associated with anger. And then we go and recreate those projections in our life because we're like, okay, that's what we learned it is." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

This is the same machinery as [[childhood-imprinting]] and [[trauma-mapping]] viewed from the perception side. The childhood lesson becomes the perceptual lens, and the lens organizes reality to confirm itself. You attract the people. You manipulate the conditions. You see the confirming evidence and miss the disconfirming evidence.

**Projection of self:**
"We don't really see reality, we see ourselves in reality. So that would be like if somebody is a thief, they see the world as a world of thieves. If somebody has a deep relationship of self-love, then they see the world as love. And even when they see the atrocities of the world, they see it as people trying to love themselves and they're not capable." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

Simpler version: "If it's weak for you to cry, then it's weak for others to cry." The way you relate to yourself is the way you relate to the world. The diagnostic does not require any introspection on the other; the diagnostic is your relationship to yourself.

**Projection of I:**
"There is a you that is separate. So we have this identity. And at the very core of that sense of identity is the idea that there is an I that exists as separate." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

This is the territory of [[self-realization]] and [[what-am-i]]. The body, the emotional state, the 45-year-old identity — each candidate for "what I am" can be examined and found insufficient. "If I cut you in half, if you think you're your body and I cut you in half, which half is you?"

## How Projections Build Reality, Not Just Filter It

The naive frame: projections distort what you see of reality. The accurate frame: projections build reality.

"It's not that we only see the world, we create the world. When we're living in and through a projection, it's not just that we see the evidence that it's true. But you also attract the same experiences, and you also manipulate events to create the same experience." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

Three operations at once:
- **Perception:** seeing the evidence that confirms, missing the evidence that disconfirms
- **Attraction:** unconsciously drawing in the people and situations that fit
- **Manipulation:** acting in ways that elicit the responses you expect

This is the same architecture as [[trauma-mapping]] and [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]]. Projection is the perceptual face of that architecture.

## The Emotional Mechanism

The reason a person keeps recreating the projected pattern is that the body holds an incomplete emotional experience. The projection is the body's strategy for getting another chance.

"That emotion that wasn't allowed to be felt all the way through, that wasn't allowed to move all the way through you, is looking to recreate circumstances so that it can move all the way through you, and then the circumstances stop getting recreated." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

This connects to [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. The way out of a projection is not better seeing or better logic. It is feeling the unfelt emotion that the projection is built to keep generating opportunities to feel.

## Useful vs. Unuseful, Not Good vs. Bad

Projections are not inherently bad. "I project onto snakes that they're deadly. And now obviously all snakes aren't deadly, and I might find a snake and think it's deadly and jump away, but it's not deadly." Some projections are productive (avoid the dangerous-looking thing). Some are destructive (recreate the abandoning relationship).

"They're just kind of useful or more useful or less useful." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

The question is not "how do I have no projections" — that is not on offer. The question is "which projections create peace, joy, productivity, love, purpose, and which create the opposite?"

See [[choosing-the-projection-that-serves]].

## The Confrontation: Everything Is a Projection

The intellectual difficulty most people hit when they start seeing through projections is the realization that there is no thought, no feeling, no body sensation that can be fully trusted. The world becomes a kaleidoscope.

"There's no thought that you can fully trust. There's no emotional experience that you can fully trust. There's no body sensation that you can fully trust. It doesn't mean you can't trust it, but it is literally like the world becomes a kaleidoscope." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

This is "some scary shit" if interpreted as "I am out of control." It is huge freedom if held with the recognition that operations still happen, decisions still get made, purpose still moves you. "You start choosing the projection that serves you best, the projection that allows for more freedom, that allows for more love, that allows for more joy."

The fear that "if I see all of this is a projection, I won't be able to act" is unfounded. Animals act. Dragonflies act. Action does not require the certainty that you are not in a projection.

## Direct Practice with a Tree

A simple exercise: look at a tree as a tree. Then see it not as a tree. See it as the thing in front of you with no label, no category, no projection. This is what "deep presence" points at. Not a permanent state, but a recognition that the projection is always running and can be set down momentarily.

"This is what, when people talk about deep presence, this is what they're talking about, is to have a moment or two without like the projection operating at full speed." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

## Applied: The Cult Projection

A specific application of projection-of-self that arose when Joe was working with the cult question. The external cult is the projection of the internal cult. The mind has its own internal structure of unexamined belief, faithful following, and guru-voice. That structure gets projected outward as the recognition (or rejection) of cult dynamics in groups.

"So that, that's just -- it's so much joy in me right now hearing that. Like that, I love that, how you've turned, shown that the cult is a projection of the internal cult and how our internal structure is very cult-like. We have a guru that's the voice in the head that says things, and we believe it without questioning whatsoever." -- Joe Hudson, Group Cohesion vs. Cult Dynamics

The diagnostic: a person who rejects external cults with strong charge is often projecting the disowned recognition of their own internal cult dynamics. The recognition of [[cult-of-one]] is what releases the projection. Once the internal cult is seen and accepted, the external cult does not need to carry the disowned material, and the relationship to external groups becomes more nuanced. The same applies in the positive form: idealizing a particular community or teacher is the projection of the unowned wisdom or belonging the person already carries inside.

## Working with Each Layer

Different layers, different entry points:

- **Psychological projection:** "Every time that you're triggered is a great example of how you are projecting a disowned part of yourself, or any way in which you idolize somebody, you're projecting a disowned part of yourself." The trigger and the idolization are the entry points.

- **Projection onto the world:** List 10 things that are important to you (money, love, authority, decision-making). For each, ask: what did I learn about this in childhood? Then notice how you are recreating it, manipulating to confirm it, and proving it.

- **Projection of I:** "Just say, 'What am I?' and really sit in the question rather than to look for an answer." Also: notice the part of yourself that has always been there. Put attention onto attention. See [[what-am-i]] and [[whats-looking-out-behind-my-eyes]].

## Why Seeing Through I Is Not Enough

There is a particular trap with the projection of I: a person sees through the identity but the emotional projections continue running underneath, harder to access than before.

"You can have a lot of cognitive freedom, you can have a lot of intellectual freedom when you see through the personal eye, when you have that kind of awakening, but it doesn't change the emotional experience of stuff. In fact, it can make the emotional experience harder to access. Because it starts operating at a more dissociated way." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

The diagnostic: "you see people who have that peace, but without the joy, when they have that like calmness, but they don't have like the exuberance of life. Like if they don't laugh easily — that's a pretty good indicator that the identity has shifted, but the emotional experience has been suppressed." See [[zen-sickness]] for the full failure mode.

## The Three Integration Questions

Joe offers three closing questions for sitting with this material:

1. Write down four of the things that trigger you most in the world. In what ways are you judging or disowning that part of yourself? (Psychological projection.)
2. Look at who you admire, who you put on a pedestal. What are the parts of them that you admire, and how do you not own that aspect of yourself? (Psychological projection, positive form.)
3. What's looking out behind your eyes? (Projection of I — and "I encourage you not to answer that question, just be in it.")

See [[whats-looking-out-behind-my-eyes]] for the third as a standalone practice.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Catching yourself accusing someone of exactly what you are doing
- Realizing every romantic partner has the same complaint about you, or you about them
- Reading every silence as criticism
- Seeing the world as broken because you feel broken
- Suspecting that the version of yourself you defend most fiercely is not quite real

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports the same pattern repeating across different relationships, jobs, or eras
- When someone delivers a strong judgment of another person with heat
- When someone idolizes another person in a way that flattens themselves
- When a person says "the world is X" with conviction (and X is what they are running)
- When [[trauma-mapping]] has been named and a wider framing is helpful
- When [[i-freeze-around-authority]] turns out to involve someone occupying a parental slot

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[mirror-the-judgment]] when the projection is psychological
- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] when the projection onto the world is identified
- Toward [[what-am-i]] when the projection of I is the layer in play
- Toward [[falling-in-love-with-projections]] when the person starts fighting their projections
- Toward [[choosing-the-projection-that-serves]] when "everything is a projection" creates fear of not being able to act

## Tone

Joe lays out the four-part taxonomy carefully and analytically — this is one of the more structured frames he offers. He does not get mystical about it. He returns to concrete examples (the snake, the thief, the cut-in-half body) to keep the framework grounded. The tone shifts toward warmth when describing the "huge freedom" available, and toward steadiness when describing the kaleidoscope confrontation that scares people.

## Boundary Conditions

This framework can be misused as an intellectual escape from feeling. "Everything is a projection" can become a stance that bypasses the emotional work the projections were built to keep generating. The recognition that the world is a projection does not resolve the emotional patterns. Only [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] does that.

The framework is also not a license to dismiss the experiences of others as "their projection." Used that way, it is itself a projection — and a particularly defensive one.
