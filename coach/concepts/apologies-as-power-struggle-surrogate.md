# Apologies as Power-Struggle Surrogate for Being Seen

## Definition

Many apologies function not as repair but as moves in a power struggle. The person apologizing is the loser; the person being apologized to is the winner. This dynamic is a weak surrogate for what the people actually want, which is to be seen, acknowledged, and respected by someone who also respects themselves.

"A lot of people use apologies as part of a power struggle, and the person who apologizes is the loser, and the person who's apologized to is the winner. It's a really weak surrogate for being seen. It's a really weak surrogate for being acknowledged and being respected and being respected by somebody who respects themselves." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## The Sugar-High Mechanism

The win-the-apology version of apology gives a small hit of feeling on top. "I won that. I'm in control for a moment." The hit is real but thin. It does not last and it does not satisfy the underlying need. The need is to be seen. The surrogate is to be apologized to. The surrogate doesn't actually feed the need, which is why the same fight recurs.

"It makes you feel good for a little while. It's like a sugar high. Okay, I feel like I won that or I'm in control for a moment. But it feels a shit ton better to be with somebody that they respect themselves and they respect you. They see themselves and they see you, and it just feels great." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## Why Couples Fight About Apologies

Most relationship fights are about not feeling seen. If both partners felt seen, respected, and acknowledged, the fight would not happen. The apology becomes the proxy battle for the actual need. Each fight is two people trying to get the other to acknowledge them by extracting the surrogate currency of apology.

"You have husbands and wives and they get into fights. Those fights are all about power struggle. If both people felt like, 'Oh, I'm being seen and I'm being respected and I'm being acknowledged,' there's no fight. The fight is when somebody doesn't feel seen, doesn't feel acknowledged, doesn't feel respected. So the apology is this surrogate for what's really wanted." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

This connects to [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]]: both people experience the fight as defending themselves from attack. Both also experience it as fighting for acknowledgment that the other is withholding.

## What the Apology Becomes When It Leaves the Power Struggle

Once the apology stops being a move in the power struggle, it stops being personal in a different way. It is no longer about who won or who is owed something. It becomes about the freedom both parties get from the acknowledgment having happened.

"The more and more the apology happens, it's not about me or them. The apology is about freedom for both of us. It just creates freedom if you can do it empowered. It just creates far more freedom for everybody. It's about freedom. It's not personal anymore, which seems weird, right? It's an apology. It feels like one of the most personal things." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The apology continues to feel intimate. The intimacy is no longer because it is a personal exchange of loss and gain. The intimacy is because freedom for both parties is being produced in the room. See [[upright-apology]] for the form this matured apology takes.

## Why the Surrogate Persists

The cowering apology is "not as good of a feeling, but it's better than feeling like a loser. So people go for it." The asymmetric appeal is the trap. When the alternative is feeling like the loser in the power struggle, the cowering apology from the other person feels like a step up. But it is not actually feeding the want. It is keeping the fight alive in a new form.

The way out is not to win more apologies. The way out is to step out of the power struggle and into a relationship where both people see themselves and each other. See [[upright-apology]] for the kind of apology that does feed the want, and [[being-heard]] for the underlying need.

## When Someone Experiences This

- After "winning" an apology from a partner, the relief is short. Within hours or days, the irritation returns. Nothing actually changed.
- Recurring fights with the same structure: who is going to apologize for what. The content varies; the structure repeats.
- A growing pile of unspoken "you owe me an apology" items in the relationship.
- A felt sense of being undefended even after the apology you wanted: the apology came, but you still don't feel seen.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- The user describes wanting an apology from someone and not getting it. The diagnostic question is: would the apology actually satisfy you, or are you in the power struggle?
- The user describes giving in and apologizing to end a fight. The diagnostic question is: did you give an upright apology, or did you cower?
- The user has the same fight repeatedly. The structural read is: this fight is the surrogate. What is actually wanted underneath?

## Coaching Path

1. Surface the power-struggle structure. Name that the fight may not be about its content.
2. Ask what the user is actually wanting from the apology. Often it is to be seen, acknowledged, respected. Not the apology itself.
3. Move toward asking for what is actually wanted. See [[state-the-want-not-the-action]]. The want is "to be seen." The action (apology) is the surrogate.
4. Address the apology side too. If the user is cowering when they apologize, work toward [[upright-apology]]. If the user is withholding apologies as a power move, work toward the willingness to apologize uprightly.
5. Move toward [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]] for the underlying frame of the fight.

## Tone

Steady, clarifying. Not condemning either party. Both people in the dynamic are organized around an unmet need. The naming is offered as a way out, not as a judgment of either side.
