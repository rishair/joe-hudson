# Conditional Clauses of Heart Closure

## Definition

A specific mechanism by which the imprinted definition of love produces moment-by-moment heart closure. The person carries unspoken if-then clauses that link opening the heart to losing something else they cannot afford to lose. The clauses operate subconsciously. They fire automatically. The result is a heart that closes at specific predictable junctures, and the closure produces the very trappedness it was attempting to prevent.

Typical clauses:

- "If I open my heart, then I have to stay with them."
- "If I open my heart, then I won't hold my boundaries."
- "If I open my heart, then I'll have to put up with the abuse."
- "If I open my heart, then I won't be able to leave."
- "If I open my heart, then I won't stand for the thing that's important to me."
- "If I open my heart, then I won't be able to get my sexual desires fulfilled."

Each clause has the same structure. Opening the heart is paired with the loss of agency, boundary, or self. The pairing is the imprint speaking. The actual operation of love does not require the pairing. But the body believes it does.

"Anything that you think you have to close your heart for, like, if I open my heart, then I have to stay with them. If I open my heart, then I have to put up with the abuse. If I open my heart, then I won't hold my boundaries. If I open my heart, then I won't stand for the thing that's important to me." -- Joe Hudson, How Love Gets Confused

## Why the Clauses Operate

The clauses are residue from the childhood definition of love. See [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]]. If love in the household required staying, then opening the heart to anyone in adulthood activates the implicit rule that opening = staying. If love in the household required tolerating abuse, then opening the heart activates the implicit rule that opening = tolerating. The body learned that opening and the cost are the same move.

The intellectual mind in adulthood usually knows this is wrong. The person can articulate that loving someone does not require staying with them. But the somatic system runs on the old rule. When the heart starts to open, the body senses the cost arriving and closes the heart to prevent it.

The closure happens fast and subconsciously. The person experiences it as "I just don't feel love for them right now," "I'm cold today," "something is off." The actual mechanism is the if-then firing.

## The Trappedness Created by the Closure

The closure does not prevent the feared outcome. It produces it.

A person who closes their heart to avoid staying in a bad relationship ends up trapped in a relationship that has no warmth — which is the trappedness they were trying to avoid. A person who closes their heart to maintain boundaries ends up with cold boundaries that the partner cannot meet — which makes the boundaries less effective, not more. A person who closes their heart to keep the option of leaving creates a relationship dead enough that leaving becomes the only option, but they had no actual freedom in the choice because the closure produced the conditions.

"It creates a feeling of trappedness too. If you don't feel like you can leave and be loving and love yourself because it would be bad for you not to be loving this person in the way that you've been conditioned to believe love is, then all that's left is to feel trapped. And that's a fear state. And so now you're bringing a whole bunch of fear into where, where love could be." -- Joe Hudson and Brett Kistler, How Love Gets Confused

The clauses are protective in intent. They are destructive in effect. The very thing the closure was supposed to protect against is the thing the closure produces.

## The Example: Loving in the Leaving

Joe's reference case: a client questioning his marriage who was not being loving. The diagnostic surfaced the clause: he thought he could not leave and love them at the same time, because leaving while loving would be disloyal, would be leading them on. The conclusion: to be honest, he had to close his heart.

The reframe that broke the clause: "I can love this person even if I leave. I can love this person in the leaving. I can love this person in the staying."

What happened next demonstrates the mechanism in reverse. The moment he saw that loving did not require staying, the heart could open. With the heart open, the wife felt seen and heard for the first time, started relaxing and unfolding and being vulnerable, and the whole relationship — which months earlier had been on the verge of ending — became workable.

"As soon as that person realized, 'oh, I can love this person even if I leave, I can love this person in the leaving, I can love this person in the staying, I can love this person. That's actually where my freedom is, is to love them unconditionally.' And loving unconditionally, even that thought process, somebody who's listening to this and, 'wait, you can't leave somebody and love them unconditionally.' Right? And so as soon as that person realized it, they were able to love their wife in the way that was actually really appealing. It fixed the whole relationship." -- Joe Hudson, How Love Gets Confused

The example clarifies that the loving does not commit you to the staying. The clause that said it did was the imprint. Once the clause is seen, the heart can be open through any outcome. The actual decision about staying or leaving becomes downstream of the heart being open, not upstream.

## The Surface Diagnostic

The way the clauses show up in conversation is often as a frustration that does not seem to have a target. The person reports: "I don't feel like I love them anymore," or "I feel cold," or "I want to leave but I feel bad about wanting to leave." The closed-off feeling is the symptom. The clause running underneath is the cause.

The diagnostic questions that surface the clause:

- What would happen if you let yourself love them?
- What does opening your heart make you afraid you would have to do?
- What does opening your heart make you afraid you couldn't do?
- If you loved them fully, what would you have to stay in / put up with / lose?

The answers contain the clause. "If I let myself love them, I'd have to stay" is the clause. "If I let myself love them, I couldn't hold the boundary about X" is the clause. The clause is not the literal contents of the relationship. The clause is what the body imported from childhood about what love costs.

## Why Naming the Clause Often Breaks It

The clauses live by being unconscious. Once the clause is explicitly named ("I think loving them means I have to stay"), the intellectual mind can usually see immediately that the linkage is not actually true. Loving someone does not mean staying. Loving someone does not mean putting up with abuse. Loving someone does not mean losing the boundary.

The naming alone is often not sufficient to dissolve the clause at the somatic level. But it is usually sufficient to introduce daylight between the loving and the cost. The daylight allows the heart to test opening without immediately closing in response to the assumed cost. Repeated opening, with the cost not materializing, slowly retrains the body.

The reframe to provide once the clause is named: love is the welcoming, and the welcoming is independent of any particular action. You can welcome someone you are leaving. You can welcome someone you are holding a hard boundary with. You can welcome someone whose behavior you are about to call out. The welcoming and the action are separable. The clause that said they were the same was the imprint. See [[love-as-deep-welcoming]].

## Connection to Love Requires Empowerment

The clauses are the operational expression of [[love-requires-empowerment]] from the opposite direction. The principle states that love cannot exist without empowerment. The conditional clauses are the specific way disempowerment manifests as heart closure. The person who believes opening the heart will cost them their boundary, their freedom, or their self is unable to love freely. The clauses contain the cost the person is too disempowered to pay.

Working with the clauses is one path to the empowerment. Each time a clause is seen and the heart opens anyway, the person discovers that the cost was not actually mandatory. Each such discovery is empowering. Empowerment grows. The clauses lose their grip. Love becomes available without the closure.

## When Someone Experiences This

When a person reports feeling cold in a relationship they used to feel warm in — the clause is firing somewhere. Find which one.

When a person says "I want to leave but I also love them" as if those are in conflict — the conflict is the clause. The loving and the leaving are not in conflict. The clause that says they are is the issue.

When a person is "trying to be loving" and producing rigidity — they are bumping into a clause they have not seen. The trying is overriding the closure for a moment but the closure returns.

When a person says they "shouldn't feel love for X because they did Y" — the clause is "if I love them after this I'll have to forgive Y." The loving and the forgiving are separable. The clause has fused them.

When a couple has lost sexual attraction despite still claiming to love each other — multiple clauses are likely operating on both sides. The mutual management has produced the loss.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The clauses are nearly universal and predictable. Joe does not present them as personal failings; they are the imprint at work. The naming is delivered with curiosity rather than judgment. Once named, the absurdity of the clause is usually visible to the person, which produces relief, sometimes laughter.
