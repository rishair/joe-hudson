# Grief as Gateway to Self-Love

## Definition

A watershed moment in healing a pattern: the person grieves the fact that they have been doing this to themselves for years or decades. They see how much they have missed. This grief is not a setback. It is the precursor to loving the part of themselves that carried the pattern.

## In Practice

"There's a moment where they grieve the fact that they've been doing this to themselves or they've been agreeing to this for the last 20 or 30 years and that they could have -- and that moment is often the precursor to loving themselves or loving that aspect of themselves. That grief." -- Joe Hudson, A Discussion with Dr. Bessel van der Kolk & Joe Hudson

The grief is not about the original wound. It is about the time spent in the pattern after the original wound. The person sees clearly: "I have been recreating this for decades. I did not have to." That recognition, fully felt, opens the door to self-compassion.

This connects to shame: the part of the pattern that is hardest to face is not what was done to the person but what the person did in response. The ways they complied, adapted, repeated. Grieving that, rather than defending it, is the turn.

## The Expansion to the Full Pattern

The grief tends to expand as it moves. The first wave is for the immediate recognition. The next waves are for everything that supported the pattern -- the relationships organized around it, the choices made in service of it, the years arranged to keep it intact. The full grief is for the whole architecture of the pattern, not just for its recognition.

Joe's example from a different transcript: a close friend started grieving a breakup and ended up grieving everything that had put him in that relationship -- his way of seeking love, his relationships with his parents, the lifestyle the pattern had required. He started with the surface loss and ended with the full structure. See [[joes-friend-yuma-grief-drives]] for the story.

This expansion is what makes the grief actually dissolve the pattern (see [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]]). Grieving only the surface loss leaves most of the structure intact. The grief has to be allowed to follow the layers down.

## Why This Works as Identity Dismantling

The pattern is held in place by an identity (see [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]]). The pattern continues because the identity that produced it has not died. Grieving the years in the pattern is grieving the identity that produced the pattern. Once the identity has been allowed to die, the pattern loses its organizing principle.

The self-love that follows is not love for the suffering version of the self. It is love that becomes available because the identity that required the suffering has dissolved. There is now a self present that does not need to recreate the pattern. That self can be loved.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- After [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] when the person sees the pattern clearly for the first time
- After [[trauma-mapping]] patterns have been named and the person realizes how long they have been operating
- When [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] has been established and the grief is starting to do its dismantling work
- When a person is in the middle of grieving a loss and the grief is starting to expand to the layers beneath

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[loving-all-parts]], particularly the parts that carried the pattern
- Toward resolution rather than management (see [[managing-vs-healing-trauma]])
- Toward [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] not happening anymore: the pattern stops recreating because the identity is gone
