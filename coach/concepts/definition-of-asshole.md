# Joe's Definition of Asshole (And Why Seeking Out Difficult People Isn't the Path)

## Definition

An asshole is "somebody who continually takes their emotions out on me without apology, without recognition, without making amends, and it happens all the time."

"My definition is somebody who continually takes their emotions out on me without apology, without recognition, without making amends, and it happens all the time. So that's the way I would define that." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The definition has four operative pieces, all of which have to be present:

1. **They take their emotions out on you.** Not just "they have hard emotions." They discharge them onto you (see [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] for the general anti-pattern of doing emotion at people).
2. **Without apology.** No [[upright-apology]] for the impact afterward.
3. **Without recognition or making amends.** They do not acknowledge what happened or attempt to repair.
4. **It happens all the time.** Not a moment, not a phase, not a rough patch. A continuous pattern.

The four-piece definition matters because most people who get called assholes are missing one or more of the pieces. A person who discharges at you once and then apologizes is not an asshole. A person who has a hard month is not an asshole. A person who discharges, refuses apology, refuses repair, and does it continuously, is.

## Why the Definition Belongs to You, Not to Joe

Joe begins his answer by deflecting the question itself: it does not really matter what his definition is. What matters is your definition, and what you want to be around.

"The first one is it doesn't really matter what my definition of an asshole is. What matters is your definition of an asshole and what you want to be around and what you don't want to be around, or what triggers you and what doesn't trigger you." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

This is consistent with [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]]. The definition is a personal calibration, not a doctrine. Joe shares his to demonstrate what a workable definition looks like, not to import it into someone else's life.

## Don't Seek Out People Who Trigger You

The companion teaching: it is true that being around people who trigger you can reveal patterns. It is not true that the path to freedom is finding the most triggering people you can.

"Is it helpful to be around people who trigger us? Yes, it's helpful to be around people who trigger us, but in no way do I suggest going out and finding the people who trigger you most and then being a part of their friend group and hanging out with them... I'm not suggesting that you go and find the worst people in the world, say, okay, I'm gonna go commit murder so I can be in prison with a whole bunch of people who trigger me. I don't think that's any path to freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The reason: triggering people are already around you. The people you love, the people you live with, the family members you cannot fully avoid. The triggers that are calling to be worked with are already present. There is no need to manufacture more.

"You'll be triggered enough by the people that you love, and that's the place to start. That attraction is the way to know the best place to find those folks." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

This is structurally similar to [[dont-confuse-embracing-fear-with-inviting-danger]] and [[dont-specialize-in-the-scariest-fear]]. The pattern that wants to be worked is the one that arrives in your life on its own. Engineering harder situations to practice on is the opposite of the work.

## The 500-Pound Lift Argument

Even for someone who has done significant work and is rarely triggered, hanging around assholes is not the practice.

"If somebody can lift 500 pounds, it doesn't mean that they go and lift 500 pounds all the time. Or if someone can walk on glass, it doesn't mean they go walk on glass all the time. That's -- there's no point in that. There's an enjoyableness to life which is really critical." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The capacity to handle difficulty does not impose an obligation to seek it. A life organized around the discomfort one can survive is not the same as a life organized around what one wants.

## The Harder Trigger Most People Avoid

If you really want to work on a difficult trigger, work on enjoying peace.

"One of the hardest things for us to accept is that our life can be enjoyable and peaceful. That's actually the harder thing. So if you really want to work on a trigger, work on that one. Work on how being in joy and peace for an extended period of time activates a shit ton of fear in you. That's a much better way to spend your time than finding a whole bunch of ways to be at dis-ease." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

See [[fear-of-joy]] and [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]]. The pattern most people avoid by manufacturing difficulty is the pattern of allowing ease. The trigger that needs the most attention is usually the trigger that contentment activates.

## The Shortcut

The framing reframes the entire spiritual-development premise of "go expose yourself to triggers so you can be free of them."

"The whole idea, to some degree, of the spiritual journey -- to go hang out with a whole bunch of people who will trigger you -- that whole idea is so that you can have the life that you want to have. Shortcut: just have the life that you want to have, and then when triggers happen, deal with them." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The path through triggers is real. The path around triggers via aversion is also real. But the shortcut is to construct the life you actually want and then meet whatever triggers arise within it.

## Brett's Reframe: Asshole as Pointer

Brett adds a perception piece: the word "asshole" often arises when you perceive someone has power over you. The label is sometimes a signal of where you have not yet drawn a boundary or done your work, not a property of the other person.

"There's the perception that they have power over you... if I actually take care of myself and get to the root of what is actually not okay for me in that situation, I draw the boundary, then suddenly they're not an asshole anymore in my world. What they are is somebody who is exhibiting certain behaviors and characteristics that can come from trauma that I've never seen, that I just don't know. There's just then the wonder opens up." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #2

This is not to dissolve the category entirely. Some people meet Joe's four-part definition and remain assholes regardless of how much work you do. But the felt-sense of "this person is an asshole" can also be a pointer to where you are buying into a power dynamic rather than drawing a boundary from a centered place.

The two readings are not in conflict. Both can be true. The work is to do both: draw the boundary from a centered place, and notice when "asshole" is the label for "someone I'm letting have power over me."

## When Someone Experiences This

- You find yourself wondering whether someone is "really" an asshole or whether you are being too sensitive
- You feel like you should be able to handle this person but cannot
- You are around someone who keeps discharging at you and you keep absorbing it
- You are tempted to lean in to a difficult relationship because "it's good for my growth"
- You are reading spiritual literature that suggests seeking out difficult people as a path

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone asks "should I cut this person off?" or "am I being too sensitive?"
- When someone is trying to decide whether to leave a relationship or workplace because of a specific person
- When someone has heard "triggers are gifts" and is leaning into the most triggering situations
- When someone is judging themselves for being unable to handle a particular person
- When [[principles-free-you]] is being introduced and "I don't work with assholes" is the example principle in play

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[triggers-reduce-when-you-know-you-can-leave]]: the mechanism by which triggers diminish has more to do with knowing you can leave than with exposure
- Toward [[boundaries]]: the boundary is the operational answer to the asshole problem
- Toward [[fear-of-joy]] and [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]]: the trigger that actually wants attention is usually peace, not difficulty
- Toward [[enjoy-over-manage]]: the shortcut is to construct the life you want rather than manage your way through trigger-inducing situations
- Toward [[principles-free-you]]: the principled stance ("I don't work with assholes") removes the decision-by-decision negotiation

## Tone

Direct, light. The teaching is delivered with humor (the "commit murder so I can be in prison with people who trigger me" line). The seriousness is held lightly. The point is not to enforce a category but to free the person from a misreading of the work.
