# Withdrawal of Love as Passive Aggression

## Definition

When you withdraw your love from someone — close your heart, go cold, stop offering warmth or presence — you are usually doing it for one of three reasons: to punish them, to leverage their behavior change, or out of pure vindictiveness. In all three cases, it is aggression. It is also, less obviously, an act of harm against yourself.

This pattern serves as a metaphor for the whole shape of passive aggression. The passive aggressor feels they are protecting themselves. The other person and the relationship pay the cost. So does the passive aggressor, even when they cannot feel it yet.

"What's amazing to me is that like if we withdraw love, it hurts us. Like it's a way in which we are cutting off love from ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Why the Withdrawal Is Aggression

The function of withdrawing love is to produce a state in the other. The state is usually some version of: regret, shame, anxiety about losing you, or compliance. Whether or not the person doing it is fully aware of this, the function is the same.

The most common framing for the move from the inside is "I am just protecting myself." This is the cover. The cover is necessary because if the person doing it acknowledged that they were trying to produce a specific painful state in the other, the move would lose its plausible deniability — and would have to be acknowledged as aggression.

"Sometimes that passive aggression is consciously known and sometimes it is not. It is, no, I'm just protecting myself. So that's just an interesting thing about it." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The other person typically registers the withdrawal correctly. They may not name it. They may feel guilty in response. They may feel like they have done something wrong (and the withdrawal is designed to produce exactly that feeling).

"The other person might feel it. They might. And even if they don't say it, they might feel guilty, but they felt that withdrawal. They felt like they've done something wrong." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Why It Hurts You

This is the part most people doing it cannot see in the moment. The withdrawal of love from another is also the withdrawal of love from yourself. Love does not function as a stream you can direct at one person while keeping it flowing toward yourself. When the heart closes, it closes.

The mechanism: love moves through you when you let it flow toward others. When you actively shut it off — close your heart, withhold your warmth, go cold — the same closure cuts off the inflow. You are no longer in the state where love is moving through you at all.

"When we are open to love, if we're loving somebody else, not caretaking, but loving somebody, actually loving somebody else, the love equally comes to us and through us and to them. Cutting it off, you're cutting off that love. It's really quite a painful experience once you really realize what it's like to live with an open heart. To close it is just sucks." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The cost is invisible to most people because they have never sustained the open-heart state long enough to know what they are cutting off. The withdrawal feels like "protecting myself" because the closed-heart state has been so habitual it registers as the default. Only after enough experience of the open state does the cost of the closure become viscerally clear.

## Withdrawal of Love as One of the Five Forms of Emotional Control

A specific framing that lands the withdrawal pattern inside the broader emotional-abuse taxonomy: withdrawal of love is the fifth form of emotion-as-control, alongside controlling-with-anger, controlling-with-guilt, controlling-with-sadness, and controlling-with-fear. The unifying definition (using emotion to make the other person do, stop, or change something) holds across all five.

"You see relationships where somebody dominates through control. Those aren't happy relationships, whether it's controlling through big emotion sadness, or big emotion anger, or by withholding love. All of that, none of it leads to a happy relationship and just never works." -- Joe Hudson, How To Know If You Are In An Emotionally Abusive Relationship

The implication: withdrawal of love is often catalogued separately from emotional abuse because it is quieter and has the appearance of self-protection rather than aggression. The unifying frame collapses that separation. The function is the same as yelling: produce a behavior change in the other through the deployment (or withholding) of an emotion. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for the full cross-emotion principle.

## Why It Is the Metaphor for All Passive Aggression

The withdrawal of love stands in for the whole pattern because it has every feature of passive aggression:

- **Aggression with plausible deniability.** "I am just protecting myself" is the cover. The other person feels the impact but the doer can disclaim intent.
- **Control through fragility.** The other person has to manage their behavior to keep the love flowing. The withholder retains power without ever directly demanding anything.
- **The doer pays a cost they cannot see.** The closure is self-amputating. The damage to the self is real and slow.
- **Often goes unrecognized as aggression.** Most people withdrawing love do not see themselves as aggressive. They see themselves as wounded, protecting, taking space.

The pattern is general. Other passive-aggressive moves work the same way: they exact a cost from the other while exacting a cost from the doer that the doer cannot yet feel.

## When Withdrawal Is Not Passive Aggression

Not every act of stepping back is passive aggression. The distinction sits in [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] and in [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

Withdrawal that is not passive aggression:
- "I need 20 minutes, I'll be back." (Taking space with a timeline.)
- "I am not able to be present right now, I am going for a walk." (Stating your own state honestly.)
- "I don't want to talk about this right now, can we revisit in an hour?" (Naming a boundary, offering re-engagement.)
- The heart stays open even while the body takes distance. The intention is to come back able to be present, not to punish.

Withdrawal that is passive aggression:
- Going cold without naming what you are doing
- Withholding warmth or presence to produce a behavioral change
- Stonewalling — the silent treatment as discipline
- The "I am protecting myself" closure that is timed to maximally hurt
- The closure that has no timeline and no intent to re-engage

The diagnostic: is the heart open with distance, or is the heart closed? Is there an honest invitation to return, or is the withdrawal designed to produce a reaction?

## In Coaching

When someone reports they have "checked out" of a relationship or "gone cold," check whether this is conscious passive aggression. Often the person describes it as self-protection. The first move is not to confront the framing but to ask what they actually want, and whether their current behavior is producing it.

When someone reports their partner has gone cold, check whether the partner's stated reason ("I'm just taking space") matches the felt sense. The felt sense of "they are punishing me" is often correct, even when the partner cannot name it as such.

When someone reports they are exhausted by the open-hearted work and want to close back down, the teaching about the cost-to-self of closure is the gentle counter. Closing the heart is not rest. It is a different kind of work, with a slow-accruing cost.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They have just gone cold with a partner and are framing it as self-protection
- They are aware they "withhold love when hurt" and have begun to suspect this is not the pure self-care they thought
- Their partner has accused them of giving the silent treatment and they reject the label
- They have been on the receiving end of withdrawal and are debating whether to confront it
- They have begun to notice that the times they go cold are also the times they feel worst inside

## Tone

Joe describes this with care. There is no condemnation of people who withdraw love. The pattern usually came from somewhere — typically from a childhood where direct expression of need or anger was unsafe and withdrawal was the only available move. The teaching is observational: this is what the move does, including to you.
