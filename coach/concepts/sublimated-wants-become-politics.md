# Sublimated Wants Become Politics and Gossip

## Definition

When individuals in an organization do not name their wants, the wants do not disappear. They come out sideways: as politics, as gossip, as factional alignment, as drama. The organizational dysfunction is the leak of all the unspoken individual wants.

This is the group-level version of [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]]. Unspoken wants are emotions that have nowhere to go. They route through whatever channels are available, which in organizations are usually political ones.

"Whatever the want is that you have, if you sublimate it, it comes out in different ways. So what you start seeing is where there's a whole bunch of wants that have been subjugated in an organization, it becomes very political." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## The Mechanism

A person in a meeting has a want: this meeting is boring, end it. They do not say it because it would be "selfish" or "rude" or "rocking the boat." The want goes underground. It surfaces later as:

- Side conversations after the meeting complaining about the meeting
- A pattern of low engagement that the person cannot explain
- Resentment toward whoever called the meeting
- Gossip about the meeting's pointlessness
- Positioning against the meeting-caller in unrelated decisions

Multiply by every meeting and every withheld want across the organization. The cumulative leak is the organization's political culture.

## Why It Becomes Gossip Specifically

Gossip is the unauthorized channel for the wants that the authorized channels do not accept. If "I want this meeting to end" is not sayable to the meeting, it gets said about the meeting to a peer in the hallway. The form changes (request becomes complaint) but the underlying want is the same. The hallway becomes the venue for everything the room cannot hold.

The same dynamic produces:

- "Who would be a good replacement for X?" gossip when "I disagree with X's approach" is not sayable
- "Did you hear what Y said?" gossip when "I am hurt by Y" is not sayable
- "Leadership has no idea what they're doing" gossip when "I want to be consulted on decisions" is not sayable

In every case, the gossip is the unspoken want surfacing in a form that requires no one to own it directly.

## The CEO Case That Makes It Visible

A CEO who is 80% collaborative but 20% wants-it-done-her-way, who will not own the 20%, creates massive organizational dysfunction. The team cannot tell which mode is active. The CEO appears to invite collaboration but then overrides decisions in ways that feel arbitrary. The team learns to read between the lines, to predict, to position. The hidden 20% generates more politics than the explicit 100% of either pure mode would.

"Let's say 80%, they want collaboration, but 20%, they just want it done their way. But they won't say, I just want it done this way on this one for me. And they're always trying to be collaborative, that creates a tremendous amount of guck in the system too, all sorts of politics." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The "guck" is the political residue of the unowned want. The team is working out what the CEO actually wants because she will not say it. The work the team should be doing on the actual task gets redirected into psychological speculation about leadership intent.

This is true of any leader who hides any want. Each hidden want produces its own ration of guck.

## The Counterintuitive Implication

A leader who clearly states a want most people would consider "tyrannical" (I want everyone to do exactly what I say) often produces less political dysfunction than a leader who pretends to want collaboration while actually wanting compliance. The first is at least navigable. People know what they signed up for and can stay or leave. The second is unnavigable because the rules keep shifting.

"There are definitely CEOs who run their companies that way. There's definitely people who want to be in companies like that. Wow, it's efficient. One guy tells me what to do. Boom, I know how to succeed." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The principle is not that command-and-control is good. It is that owned wants produce less guck than hidden wants, even when the owned want is "I want to be in charge."

## The Withheld Want Is Often Shared

When one person in a meeting finally says the want everyone is feeling ("I'm bored, I want this meeting to end"), the room often relaxes visibly. Other people smile. The meeting either shifts into something useful or ends.

"You'll see somebody go, wow, okay, this meeting's boring. I really want to be more interested in this meeting. And all of a sudden, like 10 people smile. They're like, yeah." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

This is the signal that the want was distributed across the room and the suppression was distributed too. The first person to say it does not introduce a new problem. They name the problem everyone was already working around.

## The Connection to Caretaking at Scale

The organizational version of [[i-caretake-people]] is the organization in which everyone is caretaking everyone else's hypothetical feelings. The meeting drags on because no one wants to be the one to suggest ending it. The bad strategy continues because no one wants to be the one to challenge it. The toxic colleague stays because no one wants to be the one to name the toxicity.

The collective caretaking produces collective dysfunction. The cost is paid by everyone, but no one is responsible because no one acted; they only failed to act.

See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for the individual mechanism. The organizational dynamic is the same pattern playing out across many people simultaneously.

## What Resolves It

- Leaders model owning their wants, including unflattering ones. See [[tell-them-like-an-asshole]].
- Individuals are coached toward [[wants-vs-partiality]]: name what you want without weaponizing it.
- Meeting structures invite the dissent that politics would otherwise route through gossip channels.
- Resentment is treated as the diagnostic it is. See [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]].
- The team is helped to see that asking for what you want is more functional, not less, than performing the absence of wants.
- Leaders adopt the daily practice of [[state-the-want-not-the-action]]: replace "here's the action and the owner" with "I want and need X." The want is named; the action and the owner are left to the team to discover. This makes the leader's wants the primary visible signal rather than the leader's pre-formed solution.

## The Parallel: Unclear Standards Also Produce Politics

This concept names one source of organizational politics: individuals not naming their wants. The parallel source is structural: the organization has not made its performance standards clear. See [[politics-from-unclear-standards]].

Both produce the same surface: people directing their anxiety relationally rather than at the work. The first source is individual (each person's hidden want leaks); the second is structural (the system has not provided clear criteria for success). Most political organizations have both problems simultaneously.

The resolution requires addressing both. Naming individual wants improves directness but does not solve the structural ambiguity. Clarifying performance standards improves direction but does not address the leaks of hidden wants. The functional team has both: clear standards and named wants.

## The Moral Inversion That Makes the Resolution Stick

The leaders who hide wants do so under the belief that hiding them is service. The reframe that makes the resolution sustainable is [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]]: the leader's wants are not personal preferences to subordinate; they are organizational data the team needs. Naming them is a duty, not an indulgence. Hiding them deprives the team.

Without this moral inversion, the leader who tries to state wants more openly will revert. The old morality (suppression is responsible) overrides the new behavior. The behavior holds when the morality flips.

- When the gap between what is said in meetings and said about meetings is large
