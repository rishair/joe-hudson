# Spin (How Much You're Pedaling vs. How Much the Bike Is Moving)

## Definition

Spin is the gap between effort expended and forward motion produced. It is the felt-and-measurable signal that you are working hard without getting anywhere. Spin is what happens when the wheels are turning but the bike is not moving, or when you are rowing the boat but rowing against the current.

It is a productivity diagnostic that is more honest than most productivity diagnostics because it ignores throughput and asks the harder question: did anything actually move? See [[pace-and-spin]] for the pairing with pace.

"It's very hard to know if our productivity is useful or if it's just a dopamine fix. But it's very easy to know if you feel like you're spinning, which means you're putting a lot of effort into something and you don't see the thing you want moving forward." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

## Why Spin Is the Cleaner Read

The deeper measure (was the productivity useful?) is hard to answer in the middle of working. The body has been trained to confuse the dopamine of completion with the satisfaction of production. The mind cannot reliably distinguish them in real time.

Spin is easier to feel. You know when you are putting effort in and not seeing the thing you wanted move. That sensation is reliable in a way that the verbal question "was this productive?" is not. The body can be lied to about productivity. It is much harder to lie to the body about spin.

This is why Joe uses spin as the team check-in. It bypasses the mind's stories about how much was accomplished and goes straight to the felt sense of whether the wheels were spinning.

## The Bike-Pedaling Image

"How much do I feel like I'm spinning? How much of the effort that I'm putting into pedaling is actually moving the bike forward?" -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The bike image is more useful than abstract definitions because the diagnostic is bodily. A person who has pedaled hard on a bike that is not moving knows exactly what spin feels like. The legs are working. The chain is engaged. The bike is not advancing. Something is wrong with the system, not with the effort.

The same diagnostic at the work scale: the days are full, the meetings are run, the emails are answered, and something visible has not moved. The error is not in the effort. The error is in the application of the effort.

## Spin Across Time Horizons

Spin is not one question. It is a check across multiple horizons:

- **Week spin:** how much of this week's effort actually moved what I wanted to move?
- **Month spin:** did the four weeks of effort add up to a month of motion in the right direction?
- **Year spin:** did the months add up to a year of progress on what I actually care about?

A week can be spin-free and the month be deep spin -- each week was efficient at the wrong project. A month can be spin-free and the year be deep spin -- each month was deep in the wrong mountain. The check has to run at all horizons because each horizon catches what the shorter ones cannot see.

"There's a viewpoint of what spin is for the week. There's also a viewpoint of what spin is for the month. There's also a viewpoint what spin is for the year. And those different ways of thinking about spin are really important because it tells us, hey, how much of what we're doing is actually productive." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

## What Produces Spin

Common sources:

- **Confusing responding with doing.** The email/Slack/text loop produces high dopamine throughput and low forward motion. See [[doing-doesnt-equal-producing]].
- **Working on the wrong layer.** Patching symptoms instead of fixing the cause. The MG-versus-Lexus pattern. See [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] for the root-cause lens.
- **Not knowing the forest.** When the purpose of the productivity is not seen, the productivity will not aim at it. See [[productivity-is-the-trees-purpose-is-the-forest]].
- **Manufactured urgency.** Driving from adrenaline tends to produce spin because the urgency directs effort toward the loudest thing, not the most important. See [[urgency-as-motivation]].
- **Avoiding the uncomfortable task.** The one task the person doesn't know how to do is usually the highest-leverage one. Avoiding it produces high activity around lower-leverage work. See [[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]].

## What Reduces Spin

- **Ask what would make most of this unnecessary.** Three things on the list that would render the rest moot. See [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]].
- **Reflect at the hour scale.** "What did I actually move in that hour?" with "responded to people" disallowed as an answer. See [[reflection-after-an-hour]].
- **Get clear on the forest.** What is all this productivity actually for? Until that is named, spin is the default. See [[what-is-the-productivity-for]].
- **Do the hardest thing first.** The most uncomfortable task is usually the most leveraged. See [[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]].
- **Notice the body's signal.** The frenetic, anxious productivity state usually produces spin. The relaxed, present state usually produces leverage. See [[productivity-as-fear-based-engine]] and [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]].

## When Someone Experiences This

- The end-of-week sense of "I worked hard and I'm not sure what changed."
- Crossing dozens of items off a list and the underlying project is in the same place it was Monday.
- The realization at the end of a year that the year looked busy and produced nothing the person actually wanted.
- The team meeting where everyone reports activity and the company is flat.

## Tone

Direct, almost relieved. Spin is a name for something most people have felt and not had words for. Naming it tends to produce immediate recognition. Joe delivers it without drama; the drama is in the recognition itself.
