# The Heartbreak Underneath the Blockers Is the Love

## Definition

When a relational concern presents with layered emotions on top — guilt, disgust, pity, judgment, shame — the felt material at the bottom of the descent is almost always heartbreak. And the heartbreak is not separate from the love. The heartbreak IS what the love looks like in a situation where the other person could not be loved cleanly.

This is the core insight for working with concerns like [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]]: the love is not absent and needs to be manufactured. The love is buried under the blockers and needs to be uncovered. Once uncovered, the love does not require reconciliation, contact, or repair with the other person. It only requires that the heartbreak be allowed.

## The Source Teaching

The teaching comes through in a session about a daughter who had separated from her toxic mother. The client said she did not know how to love her mother, did not even know what love toward her mother would be. Joe took her through the descent: guilt to sadness to pity to disgust. Underneath the disgust, when the client stayed with it, she landed on a stuck place in the sternum that she initially called guilt and then recognized as sadness.

Joe named what was there:

"There is heartbreak in the fact that this is a relationship with your mom." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Love My Mom

Then the central reframe:

"It's a scary-ass thing to feel. It's why you have all those layers on top of it. Because in the heartbreak and that sadness, it's like, that's the love. That's the love you have. Of course you want your mom to be well. Of course you want a good relationship with her. Of course you want her to fucking take care of herself and to respect the fact that you take care of yourself. Of course you want your mom to call up and say, oh sweetheart, I get why you fucking had to separate from me, and I'm so sorry. And how do we — how do I show up in a way that you can feel supported? Fucking of course you want that." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Love My Mom

The "of course" is doing the load-bearing work. The "of course" is what the heartbreak is. The wanting-her-to-be-well is the love. The fact that she could not be that, and likely never will be, is what makes the love land as heartbreak rather than as joy.

## The Structure

The blocker emotions (see [[blocker-emotions]]) sit on top of the more vulnerable material for a reason. They prevent the heartbreak from arriving. The heartbreak is dangerous to the system not because it is painful but because the heartbreak contains the love. The love, once acknowledged, breaks the convenient story that the person being separated from never mattered.

A clean story would say: "She's toxic. I cut her off. I feel nothing for her." That story is what the blockers protect. The story is operationally useful — it keeps the boundary easy to maintain — but it is not true. The actual felt material is more complicated. The person did matter. The person still matters. The wanting-them-to-be-well is alive. The fact that they cannot be the person who could be loved is the wound.

The blockers — guilt, disgust, judgment — are doing the work of preserving the clean story. They keep the love invisible by occupying the foreground with feelings that have nothing to do with love and that justify the distance.

The descent through the blockers (see [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]]) makes the love visible. The visibility is the heartbreak. The heartbreak is the love.

## Why the Heartbreak Does Not Require Reconciliation

A common confusion: if the love is there, doesn't that mean the relationship should be repaired?

No. The love does not require contact. The love does not require the other person to change. The love can exist in full, alongside a permanent and necessary boundary, without the boundary being a betrayal of the love.

"What can happen is that you can look at your mom one day and you can just feel nothing but love and compassion. And that doesn't mean she's in your life. That doesn't mean you talk to each other. But when she crosses your mind, she crosses your heart. You're not defended, you're not protecting." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Love My Mom

The end state is not reconciliation. The end state is: the love is felt cleanly, the heart is not defended, and the boundary remains. Three things at once. None contradicts the others.

This is the difference between [[care-vs-caretaking]] applied to estrangement. Caring about the person does not mean being responsible for them. Loving them does not mean letting them back in.

## Why This Concept Matters for an AI Coach

Without this concept, an AI coach working a concern like [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] will get stuck. The available framings — "you set a good boundary," "the guilt is your inner critic," "you don't owe them anything" — are all true and all miss the point. The point is not the boundary's correctness. The point is the love that is underneath the guilt, which has been waiting to be felt.

The coach who knows about heartbreak-as-the-love can do something different. Instead of validating the boundary and arguing with the guilt, the coach can hold the descent through the blocker stack and arrive at the place where the love becomes visible. The arrival is what produces the shift the client came for, even though the client did not know that was what they were after.

## The Connection to Heartbreak as Capacity Expansion

This concept is one specific application of the broader teaching in [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]. Heartbreak in general expands the heart's capacity to love. The version of heartbreak that arrives at the bottom of a blocker stack is a particular instance: the heartbreak about the relationship that could not be what it should have been.

The expansion mechanism is the same. The defended heart — the one that was being protected by the guilt-disgust-pity layers — is what gets broken open. What remains is more capable of welcoming. The welcoming first lands on the family member, who can now be loved across the necessary distance. Over time it extends elsewhere: to other relationships, to the self.

## The Compassion-of-the-Guts Pointer

After the heartbreak lands and the love becomes visible, Joe sometimes offers a practice for taking the recognition further over time. The Greek word for compassion comes from a root meaning "of the guts." The instruction: find something that brings love into your heart easily (in the source session, the client's dog). Get in touch with that heart-feeling. Then let the feeling drop down into the gut — the place where the difficult material has been stored.

The practice is offered as a pointer for later, not as the immediate work. It is one way to keep building the muscle of letting love coexist with stored difficulty. See [[compassion-as-of-the-guts]].

## What This Concept Is Not

- **Not a sentimentalization of toxic relationships.** The love is real and the boundary is real. Acknowledging the love does not soften the necessity of the separation.
- **Not the same as forgiveness.** Forgiveness, in the moral sense, may or may not be relevant. The love is felt regardless. A person can love a parent fully and still not forgive specific acts. The categories are different.
- **Not a one-time achievement.** The blockers will reassemble. The descent has to be made again. The love that is recovered today will need to be re-recovered tomorrow. Each recovery deepens the capacity.
- **Not exclusive to family.** The same structure shows up in any relational concern where layered blockers obscure underlying love: estranged friends, ended partnerships, former colleagues, former teachers. The family case is the most common because the blockers are usually heaviest with family.

## When Someone Experiences This

When you are in a situation that requires distance from someone you also love and the love keeps showing up as guilt or disgust or pity instead of as love: the blockers are doing their work. The love is underneath. Allowing the heartbreak is how the love becomes visible again.

When you are sure you feel nothing for a person and the certainty has a defensive quality to it: the certainty is the blocker. The nothing is not nothing.

When the obvious story ("they were terrible, I cut them off, good riddance") seems insufficient even though it is correct: the insufficiency is the love asking to be felt.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person presents with guilt or disgust about a family member they have set boundaries with.
- When the descent through emotion layers (see [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]]) is approaching the bottom and the next layer needs naming.
- When [[concerns/i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] or any concern about a difficult relational separation is being worked.
- When someone is convinced they feel nothing for a person they spent years entangled with.
- When a relationship has ended (death, breakup, estrangement, divorce) and the person is reporting only the surface emotions, not the underlying love.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward letting the heartbreak fully arrive. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] for what the heartbreak then does over time.
- Toward holding the boundary and the love simultaneously. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] and [[care-vs-caretaking]].
- Toward [[compassion-as-of-the-guts]] as a practice for keeping the recovered love alive across time.
- Toward [[love-as-deep-welcoming]]: the operational definition of love that includes the welcoming of what hurts.
- Toward [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] applied to the self: not denying the person their pain about the family member, and not denying them the gold of the love that the pain contains.

## Tone

Warm. Slightly sad. The teaching lands as a relief, not as an instruction. The client usually softens when this lands; the heartbreak is recognized as something familiar that has been waiting to be named.

Joe delivers the "of course you want her to call up and say I'm sorry" line with the same energy a friend would use sitting next to someone in grief. The "fucking of course you want that" is not aggressive. It is permission. The expletive carries the heat that turns the abstract teaching into a felt one: of course you do.
