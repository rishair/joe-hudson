# The Three Blocks to Speaking Your Truth

## Definition

A three-part taxonomy of what prevents people from speaking their truth. Any specific instance of withholding truth maps to one (or more) of these three:

1. **Doubt** — not trusting your own perception. Lack of self-referencing. The "what did I do wrong?" loop running in the background.
2. **Fear of the consequences** — the result-oriented worry that saying the truth will produce a bad outcome.
3. **Justifying conditioning** — treating a bad pattern as truth. Calling a reactivity an intuition.

The three blocks are distinct in mechanism but often run together in any specific instance. The taxonomy is useful because it identifies which corrective move applies to which block.

"The 3 main reasons that people are not following their truth is 1, doubt, 2, fear of the consequences, and 3, because they're justifying conditioning. They're justifying a bad pattern and calling it truth, right? Those are the things that seem to happen." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

## Block 1: Doubt

The person does not trust their own perception. They are running the internal question "did I see that right? Did I feel that right? Am I sure?" The doubt prevents the speaking because the speaking requires treating the perception as valuable enough to voice, and the doubt is denying its value.

The structural origin: the person is referencing outside themselves rather than inside. They are looking for confirmation from external sources before they will trust their own perception. The looking is the mechanism that prevents the trust. See [[doubt-requires-closed-heart]] for the full account.

Joe's specific framing: "in the doubt side of it, it's a lack of self-referencing." The person has lost the capacity to be their own reference point. Restoring the capacity is the work that dissolves the block.

The corrective: speaking the truth despite the doubt. The act of voicing is the act of self-referencing. The doubt cannot survive being repeatedly bypassed by the speaking. See [[speak-truth-twice-a-day]].

## Block 2: Fear of the Consequences

The person can see the truth clearly. They know what to say. They are stopped by the projected outcome — what will happen if they say it. The job loss, the relationship loss, the disapproval, the conflict. The fear of outcome is the block.

The structural feature: the person is treating the decision as a single bet. The whole world is on this one moment. Joe contrasts this with the portfolio frame: if I make this kind of decision a hundred times, what does my track record look like? See [[decisions-as-portfolio]] and [[good-decision-is-how-you-make-it-not-the-outcome]].

"In the result-oriented, oh, I'm scared of the results. That one's interesting because most people are thinking about a decision as a single decision. They're thinking about it as a single bet, and it's the whole world is on that bet instead of seeing it as a portfolio of decisions." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

The corrective: shift from outcome-tracking to process-tracking. Trust that the portfolio will reflect the truth-speaking over time, even when individual instances produce bad outcomes. The truth-telling daughter had some specific losses; the portfolio was overwhelmingly positive. See [[track-process-metrics-not-outcomes]] and [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]].

## Block 3: Justifying Conditioning

The person believes they are speaking their truth when they are actually expressing a conditioned reaction. The reactivity has been mistaken for intuition. The pattern has been dressed up as authenticity. The person is not blocked from speaking — they are speaking — but what they are speaking is not the truth.

This is the most dangerous of the three because the person experiences themselves as doing the right thing. They are convinced they are being honest. They may even be using the language of self-possession to defend the reactivity. The discrimination required is the somatic one: does the heart open or close when this impulse arises? See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]].

The corrective: develop the somatic discrimination. Pay attention to the body on every immediate decision. Notice which impulses arrive with the heart closed (conditioning) and which arrive with the heart open (intuition). Speak from the latter. Let the former be felt and discharged before speaking. See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] for the operational test and [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the related misuse pattern.

## How the Three Blocks Interact

In practice, the three rarely arrive in isolation. Common combinations:

**Doubt + Fear of Consequences.** The person isn't sure if they're seeing it right (doubt), and even if they are, what would happen if they said it (fear). The combined block is paralytic. The doubt prevents the speaking from feeling justified; the fear prevents the speaking even when the doubt is overridden. Both must be addressed.

**Fear of Consequences + Justifying Conditioning.** The person has a reactivity that wants out. The reactivity feels like truth. The fear of consequences slows them, but the conditioning-disguised-as-truth keeps insisting. When the speaking finally happens, it lands as a reactive discharge rather than a clean truth.

**All three together.** The most common case for chronic withholders. The doubt prevents trust in the perception; the fear prevents speaking even when trust is granted; the conditioning slips in when speech does happen and converts the truth into a discharge. The full structure requires work on all three.

## Which Block Is Which

A diagnostic for the coach (or the self-coaching person):

**Doubt presents as:** "I'm not sure if I'm right." "What if I'm misreading this?" "Maybe I'm being paranoid." "I should think about this more."

**Fear of consequences presents as:** "But if I say this, then X will happen." "I can't risk that outcome." "I have to keep this job." "I can't lose this relationship."

**Justifying conditioning presents as:** "This is just who I am." "I'm being honest." "I'm just speaking my truth here." "I shouldn't have to censor what I really feel." Said in the context of a clearly conditioned reactivity that the body shows is operating from a closed heart.

The diagnostic matters because the corrective is different in each case. Telling the doubter "just trust yourself" reinforces the doubt by giving it more to question. Telling the consequence-fearer "say it anyway" without the portfolio frame produces panic. Telling the conditioning-justifier "speak your truth" feeds the very pattern that's blocking the truth.

## The Sequencing

Joe's implied order in the source teaching:

1. Address doubt first (build the self-referencing capacity, install the heart-open default)
2. Address fear of consequences (introduce the portfolio frame, the process-focus)
3. Address justifying conditioning (develop the somatic discrimination)

The order is not arbitrary. Doubt prevents the speaking from happening at all. Once speaking is possible, fear of consequences shapes whether the speaking can be sustained. Once it is sustained, the conditioning question becomes "what am I actually speaking?" — the question of whether what comes out is the truth or a reactivity in truth's clothing.

In practice the sequence is not strictly linear. The blocks interact. Work on one often surfaces the next. But the order is a reasonable default for the early work.

## When This Framework Is Needed

- A person presents the question "why can't I speak my truth?" and needs a diagnostic
- A person has tried "speaking their truth" and the attempts have backfired
- A coach is working with a client who is stuck somewhere in the truth-telling sequence and needs to identify which block is operating
- A person has multiple competing blocks running and needs to disentangle them
- A teacher is explaining what gets in the way of self-possession

## Tone

Joe lists the three with the directness of a teacher mapping a problem space. The "those are the things that seem to happen" closer is characteristic — a slight hedge that acknowledges the taxonomy is not exhaustive, while still committing to the three as the operative ones. The tone is workmanlike, useful, applied.
