# Defense

## Definition

Defense is what closes the heart and creates separation. It has two layers:

**Mental defense:** Any thought pattern that creates separation between you and another. They don't understand. I'm better than them. This course moves too slow for me. They come from an inferior race. They come from a better race. Any way you mark the other as not-you is a form of mental defense.

**Somatic defense:** A literal wall in the body. Typically in front of the body, running from roughly the perineum up to the top of the head. Different people experience it more strongly at different points along the wall. Underneath the wall, in the gut, there is subtle fear.

"On the mind side, defense is any way that you've decided that there's separation. Somatically, it's literally like a wall. And on a gut level, it's a subtle fear." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## What Defense Is Protecting

Defense protects against an emotion you do not want to feel. Drop the defense and the emotion arrives. That arrival is what defense is preventing.

"There's a feeling when we lower our defenses that we're -- what we're actually doing is allowing a whole bunch of emotions we don't want to feel to be felt. And those emotions purify us." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

This is why defense is felt as protection. It is protecting the constructed self from what would dissolve it. The dissolution is felt in advance as annihilation.

"Every time we lower our defense, there's kind of this little thing inside of us is like, oh, we're gonna be fucking destroyed. We're gonna be destroyed. Don't do that." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The intuition is correct. Something does get destroyed. The defended sense of self gets destroyed. What remains afterward is not destroyed but is also not the same as what was defended.

## Defense vs. Boundary

Common confusion: if I drop my defense, I will have no boundaries.

This is not how it works. Defense and boundary are different operations.

**Defense** closes the heart and creates separation. It is reactive, somatic, mental. It is about protecting against feeling.

**Boundary** is a clear statement of what you will do. It does not require closing the heart. It can be drawn from a fully open state. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]].

"Just because I have to draw a boundary or I want to draw a boundary doesn't mean that I can't love you." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

You can drop every defense and still draw clean boundaries. You can be in a literal fight with someone and still have an open heart toward them. The Bhagavad Gita's instruction is to fight the war and to do so without closing the heart.

## Defense in Conflict

Most people, when entering a conflict, drop the other's humanness. They make the other an other, make them wrong, make them an obstacle.

"You can think of any circumstance where I feel like I have a conflict with somebody, it's so easy to drop their humanness. To make them an other, to make them wrong, to make them an obstacle. And that never helps the conflict." -- Brett Kistler, Love over Defense

The defended fighter is less effective, not more. Fighters know this. A common instruction in martial arts: try to get your opponent angry. The angry opponent is the defended opponent. The defended opponent is the less effective opponent.

The frightening corollary: what happens if the person you are fighting has an open heart for you and is still determined to win? They are more effective than you. See [[examples/joes-board-member]] for an extended illustration.

## How to Lower Defense

Dropping defense is not done by trying harder. It is done by feeling what the defense was protecting against.

The instruction set:
1. Notice the wall. Locate it somatically. Notice where it is strongest.
2. Notice the fear underneath. Usually in the gut, often subtle.
3. Allow the emotion the defense was holding back to arrive. This is the intensity.
4. Surrender. Not to the situation. To not defending.

"You're not surrendering to the circumstances. You're surrendering to not defending yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

This is a specific use of the word "surrender." The surrender is to your own welcoming, not to the other's will.

The Pema Chödrön line that Joe uses to point at this: "Open yourself up for annihilation, because that way you can find out what part of yourself can't be annihilated."

## The Practice With Repeated Defense

Defense is rarely dropped once and then gone. It is dropped over and over in repeated situations. Each drop allows more of the underlying emotion to be felt. Each cycle increases capacity.

Joe's six-week practice of letting judgment break his heart (see [[letting-judgment-in]]) is one form of this work. Practice deliberately not defending in situations where defense was automatic. Cry in unexpected places. Stay in the discomfort. After many cycles, the judgment stops registering as it once did.

The same principle applies in interpersonal conflict, in board meetings, in awkward conversations. Each cycle of choosing welcoming over defense in a hard moment expands the capacity to do so.

## When Someone Experiences This

When someone reports feeling closed, walled off, in their head, separate, above the situation, contemptuous, dismissive: defense is operating.

When someone describes a conflict in terms of how the other person is wrong: defense is operating. The wrongness frame is one of the most reliable defense markers.

When someone wants to feel love or vulnerability and cannot get there: the defense is in the way. The path is not to push harder toward love. The path is to feel what the defense is protecting against.

- When the body is held tight in conversation. Shoulders up, jaw clenched, eyes hard.
- When a person says they want to be more loving but cannot feel love.
