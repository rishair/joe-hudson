# Passive Aggression Toward Yourself

## Definition

Passive aggression is not just something people do to each other. It happens inside the head, between parts of the self. Two of its most common internal forms hide especially well because both wear the costume of help: the chipper "you can do it!" coach, and the quiet voice that says "yeah, this isn't going to work."

Both are anger at the self that has been kinked into a deniable form. The body knows; the mind protests that it is being supportive.

## The Cheery Internal Coach as Passive Aggression

The "turn that frown upside down" voice. "You can do it!" "Everything is great!" "Don't worry about it, just be positive!" The voice presents as a friend. Underneath, it is telling the part of you that is suffering that it is not allowed to feel what it is feeling.

That is aggression. Aggression in the form of help.

"One of my favorites is the, like, the like super helpful coach self-talk. It's like, 'you can do it, everything's great,' like yeah, like, and you're like, you know, one part of you is like, oh man, this day's crap. Turn that frown upside down says that like, it's like you're basically saying you're not allowed to feel this way, I need you to feel this way. Like that's a form of aggression." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The form is identical to the external version. The aggressor presents as the helper or victim ("I am just trying to make you feel better"). The aggression is the demand that the other part be different than it is. The deniability is the friendliness of the wrapper.

## Why It Looks Like Friendship

The cheerful coach voice is praised as the antidote to the inner critic. Self-help culture installs it as a virtue. The person who runs it feels they are being kind to themselves. They are often surprised to learn that the voice is functioning as aggression.

The signature that betrays the form: the part of the self that is suffering does not soften. It either goes underground (and emerges later as a much bigger version of the same feeling) or it gets louder against the cheering. The cheery voice and the suffering part end up locked in the same dynamic that an external bully and external victim get locked in.

There is a reality this points to: the long-term consequences of running this voice match the long-term consequences of running external passive aggression. The relationship is between you and you, and the relationship slowly stagnates.

"We've all seen the dozens of movies about somebody who's like really trying to live like that for 20 years and then goes into this deep, dark hole. And, and those movies are made because there's a reality to that. So the passive-aggression usually ends up just like in a relationship into some fucking explosion. You know, I don't know why we seemed happy and then he just left after 20 years, or she just left. Like, there's a lot of passive-aggression going on in those relationships." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The internal version produces the same shape. The person seems fine for years, then collapses.

## The Undermining Voice as Passive Aggression

The mirror form. After a moment of insight, a glimpse of joy, a felt sense of capacity — the voice arrives that says:

- "Yeah, I'm gonna get right to that."
- "Oh yeah, no, that's never gonna work."
- "I'm never gonna change. This is never gonna happen."
- "How do I make this stay?" (with the implied "I can't.")

This voice does not look like aggression because it sounds like resignation or realism. It is aggression. It undermines the part of the self that just saw something true, by quietly attacking the possibility of acting on it.

"The part of the internal dialogue that goes, 'Oh, I just saw that thing. How do I make it stay?' That's like passive aggression. That's, that's like, it's an undermining. It's a saying that like, you can't, it's not going to work. It's like that little, 'Yeah, this is never going to work,' that part of your voice in your head also can be incredibly passive-aggressive." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The function is the same as external passive aggression: produce a state in the other (here, the other part of yourself) while preserving plausible deniability about being the cause. The voice can claim it was just being realistic.

## The Pattern: Critical Voice Triggers Internal Passive Aggression

There is a third internal form that runs alongside the cheery coach and the undermining voice: the passive-aggressive response from the listener-self when the critical voice issues commands. The critical voice says "you should, you should, you should." A part of the self responds with "yeah, fuck off, I'm not doing that" — or worse, "yeah, you're right, I should — but really, fuck off, I'm not doing that."

This is the everyday operation of the same principle that produces external passive aggression. Nobody likes being told what to do, including by oneself. The internal response to the internal command takes the same shape as the external response to external commands. See [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]] for the broader pattern this fits inside.

"We know this because, like the voice in the head. If you have a critical voice in the head and the critical voice in the head is like, 'you should do up, up, up, up, up,' and it gets really shitty at you, notice the reaction is usually some form of passive-aggressive because you haven't done everything the voice in the head has told you to do. You're like, 'yeah, man, fuck off. I'm not doing that.' Or 'yeah, you're right. I should do that. But really, fuck off. I'm not doing that.'" -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression: How To Identify & Respond

The diagnostic value of this framing: the long-running bad habits the voice has been demanding change on for years are still in place because the internal listener has been passive-aggressing them away. The compliance is on the surface; the noncompliance is the actual behavior. This is why the bully-voice approach fails as a change strategy — it produces the same passive-aggressive response in the self that bullying produces in others.

The corollary is that softening the critical voice (see [[apply-view-to-the-voice]]) often produces behavior change where bullying did not. The part being addressed is no longer in the position of having to be passive-aggressive about the demand.

## The Pair: Bully and Undermining

These two voices typically run together. The bully voice says "you gotta lose weight, you gotta work harder, you're 40 and you don't own a house yet." The undermining voice replies "yeah, this is never going to work."

"There's often a bully, which is like, 'you gotta lose weight, you gotta work harder, you gotta make more friends, or you're 40 years old and you don't own a house yet,' whatever the thing the bully is saying. And then there's the other side of it, which is like, 'Yeah, yeah, I'm gonna get right to that,' like, or, 'Oh yeah, no, that's never gonna work. I'm never gonna change. This is never gonna happen.'" -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The bully is direct aggression at the self. The undermining is passive aggression at the self. Together they form the same locked dynamic that a bully and a victim form externally. See [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]] Form 1 (inward) for the broader treatment.

## Why This Matters for the AI Coach

A person presenting with the cheery coach voice is often not labeled as having an inner-critic problem. They are praised for being positive. They may not know there is a problem until the explosion arrives.

A person presenting with the undermining "it's not going to work" voice is often labeled as just being negative or pessimistic. They may have been told to be more positive — which only installs the cheery-coach version of the same aggression on top.

The diagnostic move: ask whether the suffering part of the self has actually softened in the presence of the cheery voice, or whether it has gone underground. Ask whether the undermining voice can be heard as protective of something — usually it is guarding against the disappointment of failed change.

The intervention is not to fight either voice. See [[dont-fight-the-voice]]. The intervention is to recognize the voice as aggression that has been kinked into a form the system can deny, then to apply the same moves used for external passive aggression: name it, draw a boundary on it, refuse to accept it as kindness or realism.

## The Bidirectional Mechanism

The internal and external versions reinforce each other. When you accept passive aggression from yourself, you train your nervous system to expect it from others. When you tolerate it from others, you reinforce its acceptability inside.

"When we allow ourselves to be aggressive to ourselves, then we allow almost in kind other people to be that aggressive with us. We expect it." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

See [[self-aggression-permits-others-aggression]] for the principle.

The corollary: drawing boundaries on passive aggression from others is itself a message to the internal aggressor. Refusing to accept guilt trips from your mother sends a signal to the part of your voice that uses guilt internally that this is no longer acceptable. The work proceeds on both sides at once.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The person reports they "try to stay positive" and is exhausted by the effort
- The person describes years of seeming-fine followed by an unexpected collapse or rupture
- The person describes a quiet voice that talks down every insight or change attempt
- The person reports they have done a lot of inner work and "things still don't really shift"
- The person says they are not really self-critical but also cannot identify any positive movement
- The person uses "should" language about their own feelings ("I shouldn't be sad about this")

## How Joe Responds

The move is to name the cheery voice or the undermining voice as a form of aggression, not as the friend it presents as. This often lands as a shock because the person has been crediting the voice as helpful or realistic.

The next move is to attend to the part of the self that the voice has been aggressive toward. What is it actually feeling? What has it been told not to feel? The work is similar to the work with external passive aggression: the underlying anger needs a path, and the part being suppressed needs permission to actually be what it is.

## Tone

Joe acknowledges that this teaching will bother people. The cheery coach voice is widely praised, so naming it as aggression cuts against the cultural consensus. Joe's delivery is direct but not contemptuous of the people who run the voice. The voice came from somewhere — usually a childhood in which direct anger was not allowed and the person learned to suppress feelings under the cover of being positive.

"This is going to bother a lot of people, but one of my favorites is the, like, the like super helpful coach self-talk." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The "this is going to bother people" framing acknowledges that the teaching disturbs an identity people have built around being positive. The teaching is still delivered.
