# Shame as Disconnection

## Definition

Shame is the felt sense of disconnection. Not a verdict on your character, not evidence that something is wrong with you. A signal. The body's report that connection has been broken, either with another person or with yourself.

"With humans, shame is basically the feeling you get with disconnection often." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

Reframed this way, shame becomes a feature, not a bug. It is a built-in reminder that connection is broken so reconnection can happen.

"If you see shame that way, as just a signal that you're disconnected, then it is like, it's like having a constant reminder whenever you feel disconnected. And so it allows you to reconnect. It's an amazing feature." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

## The Auntie Metaphor

Shame is socially installed and socially extinguished. The same act produces shame or not depending on the response of the people around the child.

"A little kid with a whole bunch of aunties and the little kid farts and the auntie's, 'Oh my God, I can't believe that you farted.' That kid's gonna feel shame because that disconnection. But if all the aunties laugh and be like, 'Oh, you little tooter,' then they'll probably fart again. They don't feel shame." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The act is the same. The shame is the disconnection, not the fart. This is why shame is not a property of the behavior but of the relational field.

## Shame as Tribe-Training (The Civic Frame)

Above the relational mechanism, there is an evolutionary one. Shame trains people to be functional members of a tribe. The disconnection signal is not arbitrary; it evolved because tribes that produced this signal in their members were more cohesive, and cohesive tribes survived. Shame is nature's way of teaching the child what is and is not acceptable inside the group.

"It is nature's way of training us to be good citizens, right? It's not a perfect methodology, or good tribe members, or good family members. And the way it works, for the most part, is that when you are ostracized from the group, you feel shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

"It keeps us inside the fence, right? That's the thing is it keeps us inside of that line, which is what we need evolutionarily to exist as tribe, right? Like as a small group, as a village, as a nation to some degree." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

This is also why shame varies so wildly across cultures and even across families. The fence is drawn by the specific tribe. Your aunties' fence and your neighbor's aunties' fence enclose different territories. "Your neighbor could have a completely different set of shames than you have, which is just fascinating." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

The implication for the modern person: most contemporary humans live among multiple tribes simultaneously (family, professional, friend group, online community, religious community), each of which installed a different fence. The shames that result are often contradictory. You can address shame by asking which tribe installed it and whether you actually want to live by that tribe's norms.

## The Electric Fence Image

Brett offers the electric dog fence as a metaphor for installed shame. The dog is shocked at the property line until it learns not to approach. After the training, even with the fence off, the dog does not cross. Most humans live the same way around their installed shames: the shock has not arrived in years, but the avoidance still organizes the life. See [[electric-fence-with-dog]] for the full extension, and [[addiction-to-shame]] for the pattern of dogs that learn to run through the fence quickly because the freedom on the other side is worth the brief shock.

The metaphor also illuminates what the shame is for. The fence was put up to keep the dog inside the yard for its own safety. The shame was installed to keep the child inside the tribe for the child's safety. Both can outlive their usefulness while the body keeps enforcing the original training.

## The Two Sources of Shame

Disconnection comes from two directions:

1. Someone disconnects from you. They get upset, withdraw, get angry, or stop seeing you. You feel the shame.
2. You disconnect from yourself. You betray your truth, hide who you are, or perform someone else's expectations. You also feel the shame.

Both produce the same felt sense. This is the trap of conflict avoidance: trying to avoid the first source (offending people) creates the second source (abandoning yourself). See [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]].

"If you get mad at me, then I feel disconnected. And if I don't be myself, I feel disconnected." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

It is a lose-lose unless the shame itself is allowed to be felt rather than avoided.

## What People Experience

Shame in the body shows up as:

- A pulling-away sensation
- Emptiness or sadness underneath
- A wall, a bubble, or pressure
- The impulse to apologize for existing
- Freezing or flushing
- The urge to fix or change yourself
- A "kick in the stomach" that arrives at the same intensity every time the topic comes up, year after year

These are all the body's report of broken connection. The person reaches for the wrong solution: fix the behavior, fix the self, fix the other person. The actual signal is: reconnect.

## Shame Stagnates the Emotional Field Around It

A second feature of shame, distinct from its function as a connection signal: shame stops emotional movement. Other emotions move when felt -- anger clarifies, grief softens. Shame just kicks again. The body region where shame lives becomes a place awareness will not go, which makes the shame durable in a way other emotions are not. People carry the same shame for decades because the avoidance reflex is what is keeping it stuck.

This stagnation property is the mechanism underneath [[shame-drives-behavior]] and [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] for the full treatment. The two features (disconnection signal and emotional stagnation) work together: the disconnection arrives, the stagnation prevents it from processing, the unprocessed signal stays available to be triggered indefinitely.

## In Practice

When someone presents with conflict avoidance, fear of offending, or compulsive people-pleasing, the operative emotion is almost always shame. The behavior pattern is organized around not feeling shame.

The coaching move is to reframe shame as a signal rather than a verdict. The person who hears "shame is disconnection" can begin to relate to the feeling differently. The same body sensation that used to mean "something is wrong with me" begins to mean "the connection is broken, what would reconnect it?"

This is why [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] applies to shame as much as to anger or grief. The shame must be felt, not fixed. Felt, it moves. Avoided, it grows.

## Questions Joe Asks

- "What's the feeling that comes in you when that person gets offended?"
- "What's the internal feeling you're trying to avoid?"
- "What's so uncomfortable about shame? What's wrong with it?"
- "How else are you going to heal your shame?"

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, almost cheerful. Joe talks about shame the way someone might talk about a useful tool, not a wound. The reframe itself is part of the medicine: hearing shame described as "an amazing feature" loosens the gravity of the word.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not treat shame as evidence of being broken. The shame is information about the connection, not about your worth.
- Do not try to get rid of shame. Trying to remove shame creates more of it. See [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]].
- Do not name shame as a way to avoid feeling it. See [[dont-name-emotion-to-avoid-feeling]].
