# Overwhelm

## Definition

Overwhelm has two definitions that point at the same phenomenon from two angles.

**The flow definition:** Overwhelm is the moment you think you have to leave flow to manage reality. You were focused, relaxed, moving toward something. Then something happened, and you decided you now have to push the river instead of going with it. That moment is the beginning of overwhelm.

**The emotional definition:** Overwhelm is the felt sense of carrying more emotion than the system is currently processing. The conscious story is that there is too much to do, too many demands, too little time. The deeper truth is that there are unprocessed emotions stacking up, and the system reads the accumulation as "too much."

"It's the moment where you think you have to get out of flow to take care of yourself. There's this moment where you're kind of in flow and then something happens and you think you have to manage it. You think you have to manage reality." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

"We think we're overwhelmed because of X, Y, and Z. I have a call, and then I also have to get to this place, and then, but I have a big job thing... but what's actually happening on an emotional level is that there's all these big emotions and you're not feeling them, and so you're not allowing them to pass all the way through you, and so your system is overwhelmed with the amount of emotions that you have." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The two definitions converge. Leaving flow to manage reality is what produces the inner pressure. The inner pressure is what feels like overwhelm. The external pressure (the to-do list) is real, but the to-do list is not what is being experienced as overwhelm. What is being experienced is the accumulated unprocessed emotion that the management posture is holding in place.

## Overwhelm Is a Form of Fear

At its base, overwhelm is anxiety. The nervous system is activated. Fight, flight, or freeze is engaged. Whatever feels like "I have to manage this," underneath it, the body is afraid. Often the fear is of the emotions that would surface if the management stopped. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]].

This makes overwhelm part of the larger family of fear-related states. See [[fear-vs-anxiety]] and [[anxiety-three-definitions]] for the broader map.

## External and Internal Always Combine

There is no overwhelm that is purely external. There is no overwhelm that is purely internal. Both are always present.

A war zone produces a lot of external pressure. The internal piece is still operative: what is being processed and what is not. A person in a war zone who is processing the grief and fear in real time is in a different state than a person in a war zone who is not. Both are in the war zone. Only one is overwhelmed.

"It doesn't matter if you're feeling overwhelmed. There's an opportunity to allow yourself to feel something and process it. Anything that you can't process in real time typically means there's some more resistance to it that can still be let go." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The external is not in your control. The internal is. The teaching is not "your overwhelm is your fault" -- see [[dont-add-the-overwhelm-of-creating-the-overwhelm]]. The teaching is that the internal layer is where the work is, regardless of the size of the external.

## The Backlog Mechanism

When an emotion is not processed in the moment it arises, it stays in the system. Multiple unprocessed emotions accumulate. The accumulation produces a baseline state of "too much," which the conscious mind explains by pointing at the external situation.

Joe's example: when his daughter was in the hospital, he handled the doctors, the food, the logistics. His partner Tara was processing the emotional experience in something closer to real time. Around day 7 or 10, Joe hit a wall and needed to spend time being emotional. Tara had not hit the same wall because she had been moving the emotions through as they arose.

"There's always a chance to look inside and say, okay, what needs to be processed? What is it that I'm dealing with." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

This is the operational implication. When overwhelm shows up, the question to bring inside is: what emotion has been stacking up that I have not let move?

## The Flow That Was Broken

Flow here means: focused, relaxed, moving toward something, going with the river. It is not laziness. It is not wandering. It is purposeful motion without strain.

The moment flow breaks is the moment something happens (a request, a fear, an external event) and the response shifts from going with to pushing against. From responding to managing. The shift is small and almost always unconscious. By the time the overwhelm is felt, the shift has already happened.

The work, then, is partly retrospective. By the time you notice you are overwhelmed, you are already out of flow. Returning to flow requires noticing where you stopped flowing and what you started managing.

## Nervous System State

When the body is in overwhelm, fight/flight/freeze is engaged. This has predictable consequences. The capacity to think drops. The capacity to learn drops. Accuracy drops. Speed of completion drops. Memory drops. People are more likely to leave the keys, more likely to make small errors, more likely to forget what was said in a meeting.

The cruel irony: the body in overwhelm feels like it is being productive. The sense of urgency and motion masquerades as accomplishment. See [[overwhelm-reduces-productivity]] for the data on this illusion.

## What "Processing the Emotion" Looks Like in Overwhelm

The instruction is not to feel the overwhelm itself. The overwhelm is the symptom. The work is to find what the overwhelm is carrying.

The reliable approach: ask "what is the thing I do not want to have happen?" Almost always there is a false end. "If I do not get this done, my boss will be mad." That is the false end. Then: visualize the boss being mad. Feel everything that would come up. Grieve it. Move through it. The boss's anger will turn out to be a stand-in for an earlier anger that taught you that being productive was the condition for love. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]] and [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].

The intellectual understanding comes after the emotional work, not before. Trying to figure out why you are overwhelmed before feeling the underlying emotion typically just adds another layer of overwhelm. The thing you cannot figure out becomes another item on the list.

## In Coaching

When someone presents overwhelm, do not start with the to-do list. The to-do list is the visible surface. Start by asking what emotion is not being felt. If they cannot answer that directly, ask what they are scared will happen. The fear is more accessible than the unfelt emotion, and the unfelt emotion is sitting inside the feared outcome.

If the person is acute (in a meeting, at a deadline, in a hospital), teach [[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]] as the immediate regulator. But warn against using the breath to repress: that turns the breath into another piece of management and adds to the overwhelm.

If the person says they have always been like this, look at the early imprint. Where did they learn that being in management mode was the condition for safety? Usually a parent who modeled it, a household that required it, or a survival situation that rewarded it.

## Tone

Direct, matter-of-fact, slightly amused at the cultural assumption that overwhelm equals productivity. Joe takes overwhelm seriously without dramatizing it. The phrasing is precise: he resists "you are creating your overwhelm" early in someone's journey because that framing tends to compound the problem.
