# Three Keepers of Stuck Beliefs (I'm Bad, It's Hard, I'll Be Unsafe)

## Definition

There are three categories of belief that keep other limiting beliefs stuck. When a person has intellectually seen through a limiting belief but the body and the behavior have not caught up, one of these three is almost always doing the holding.

1. **I'm bad.**
2. **It's hard.**
3. **I'll be unsafe.**

These are not themselves the surface limiting beliefs people work on. They are the meta-beliefs that prevent the surface beliefs from dissolving. They are the keepers.

"There's 3 main categories of these beliefs. The first one is I'm bad. The second one is it's hard. And the third is I'll be unsafe. Those are the 3 categories of beliefs that even works with like letting go of limiting beliefs. I can't let go of limiting beliefs because I'm bad. I can't let go of limiting beliefs because it's hard. I can't let go of limiting beliefs because I won't be safe." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## Distinguished From the Seven Limiting Beliefs

[[seven-limiting-beliefs]] catalogs the content of common limiting beliefs (perfection over connection, improvement over authenticity, shoulds before wants, defense before love, "I'll be happy when X"). The three keepers operate at a different level. They are not the beliefs you work on — they are the reasons your work does not stick.

The seven are what people believe. The three are why beliefs won't dissolve even after being seen through.

A person can work on "I should be more productive" (a surface limiting belief) for years. The work does not stick because underneath is "if I'm not productive, I'm bad" (a keeper). The keeper has to be addressed for the surface belief to release.

## I'm Bad

The first keeper. The belief that something is essentially wrong with you, that you are flawed by nature, that change cannot happen because you are broken.

"Bad would be like, you should be ashamed, you're responsible, you tried and you can't, I'm broken, there's something essentially wrong with me. It's any way in which you tell yourself that something can't change because you're flawed by nature." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The keeper operates by converting every failure to change into evidence of badness, which then justifies the failure: "I knew I couldn't change. I'm bad. Therefore nothing I do will work." The loop is closed and self-reinforcing.

### Where I'm Bad Comes From

Two main sources.

**Childhood transmission.** Shame as a tool of control. Being called naughty, bad, wrong many times before the age of seven. The shame gets installed as identity rather than as response to specific behavior. See [[childhood-imprinting]] and [[shame-as-disconnection]].

"Most of us were raised with shame as a tool of control. So we've been guilted or shamed, you know, you're naughty. I've seen parents tell their kids they're naughty 5 times in an afternoon. It's really hard if you get told that for your whole life to come out of it and think, oh, I'm not naughty, I'm good, I'm inherently good." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

**Evidence-gathering after the fact.** Once installed, the belief recruits external events as proof. Someone attacks you — instead of "they're having a bad day," your mind says "I'm bad and they noticed." Past actions you regret get filed as proof that you are bad rather than as evidence that you were operating from ignorance. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

"You start looking for evidence that you're bad because other people attack you. So instead of thinking, oh, that's because they're having a bad day, they're projecting, they're having an issue, you think, oh, I've done something wrong, I'm bad." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

### How to See Through I'm Bad

Three moves:

1. **Look for the contrary evidence.** If you have been trying to change something for twenty years, that shows persistence, care for yourself, strength, self-awareness — the components of what a "good person" is. The evidence you have been ignoring is the same evidence reframed.

2. **Try to articulate what specifically makes you bad.** "Okay, what exactly is it that makes you bad? I'm overweight. Okay, well, there are a lot of overweight people. Are they all bad? No. Then what exactly is it that makes you bad?" If you keep asking, there is no answer. There is no essence-thing. The badness collapses under specification.

3. **The three-month inherent goodness experiment.** Tell yourself you are inherently good for three months. See what happens. We tend to become what we tell ourselves we are. See [[three-month-inherent-goodness-experiment]].

The deeper move is [[inherent-goodness]] itself — the orientation that nothing about you needs to be earned in the first place.

## It's Hard

The second keeper. The belief that change will require enormous effort, suffering, endurance, that the path cannot be enjoyed, that it has to be a chore.

"It generally means that there has to be a lot of effort, it's gonna suck, they're gonna have to endure it, that there's going to need to be a lot of trying, that it can't be simple, or that they can't enjoy the process." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The keeper operates by inflating the cost of change above the cost of staying stuck. Even though staying stuck is also exhausting, the imagined hardness of change feels worse, so the person stays.

### The Strange Math

"To endure shit is hard. And to tell yourself you have to endure stuff is hard." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The person believes change is hard while doing the exhausting work of staying the same. The math is wrong but it does not feel wrong from inside, because the current exhaustion is familiar and the imagined exhaustion of change is unknown.

### Why It's Hard Persists

Two reasons.

**Ignorance of the path.** You have not done it before, so you do not know it can be easier. The drywall analogy: a person who has never hung drywall thinks it's hard. The person who has hung drywall for ten years finds it easy. Most "this is hard" beliefs are about a skill that has not been acquired, not about an inherent difficulty.

"As soon as I go to the person who knows how to hang drywall and he's like, here it is, it's easy, do it like this, then it's immediately going to be easier for me. So like anything, a lot of the hardness is about ignorance, and we've been trying the same thing in the same way over and over and over again." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

**Unfelt emotions holding the behavior in place.** The reason behavior change feels hard is not that the behavior is hard to change. It is that the emotion underneath has not been fully felt. Once the emotion moves, the behavior changes easily. The hardness was about emotional avoidance, not behavioral difficulty.

### How to See Through It's Hard

Look at the changes you have already made effortlessly. Your beliefs about God, money, sex, education, work, love have all changed thousands of times across your life without effort. You did not notice they were changing because the brain prefers continuous-self framing. But the changes happened. The hardness is a story the mind tells about a future change; the past is full of evidence that changes happen easily.

"You don't even know who the hell did it. You didn't even notice it was done. It's super easy. And when you fully let that in, then you can see that it's not that hard." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The other move is finding the comparable. There are people who enjoy starting businesses, enjoy going to therapy, enjoy the change. The existence of those people disproves the "hard" frame. See [[find-the-comparable]].

## I'll Be Unsafe

The third keeper. The nervous-system level belief that change will lead to being abandoned, unloved, exposed, attacked, or destroyed.

"Unsafe means that I'm going to be abandoned, I'm not going to be loved, something bad will happen, the other foot is going to fall, anything like that. Anything that it's like you're not going to be okay. It's a more nervous system kind of way of looking at it." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The keeper operates by activating the threat response whenever change is contemplated. The body refuses to release the belief because release feels like danger.

### Where I'll Be Unsafe Comes From

**Childhood experience.** Being punished, abandoned, unloved, hit when you were small. The body learned that safety was contingent and could be removed. The belief is not cognitive; it is encoded in the nervous system.

**Bad-things-happen reasoning.** "I was happy and then this bad thing happened, so I can't be happy because then the next foot's gonna always fall. The nature of things that are good and smooth and peaceful is that they do not last forever." The body interprets the inevitable arrival of difficulty as confirmation that safety is impossible.

### The Reframe

You are not actually safe in any ultimate sense. You will die. The world is in flux. The only safety available is internal. See [[knowing-yourself-is-the-only-safety]].

"You're not safe, right? You're definitely going to die. And so ultimately, in that way, there is no safety. The only safety that you can have, the only security you can have is the security of knowing yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

But on a practical level, the unsafety beliefs are wildly exaggerated. Everyone listening has been waiting for impending doom that has not occurred. Your career has gone roughly up and to the right. You have fed yourself. You have had friends. The doom story is rarely accurate.

The intervention is the same: find comparables. People have started companies, lost jobs, gone through divorce, gone to prison, lost everything — and built a life on the other side. The fact of their continuance is evidence the unsafety story exaggerates.

### The Precipice Reframe

For people deep in the work, the moments that feel unsafe ("I'm going to lose all my friends," "I won't be able to operate in society") become recognizable as growth signals rather than threats.

"Anytime I have that thought now, I'm like, oh cool, I'm on the precipice of a very cool change. That fear is kind of like a road sign of that." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The unsafety feeling, once met enough times and shown to be wrong each time, becomes a marker of imminent breakthrough rather than imminent doom.

## How the Three Interact

The three keepers often appear together. A single stuck belief can be held in place by all three simultaneously: "I can't change this because I'm broken, it's too hard, and trying will make me unsafe." Each keeper has its own intervention, but the work on one usually loosens the others.

The most foundational is "I'm bad." Inherent goodness is the bedrock. When that is shaken, the other two often loosen because they were partly held in place by the underlying shame. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

## At the Bottom of the Latticework

In [[latticework-of-limiting-beliefs]], these three are typically what is found at the bottom of a chain. You unhook surface beliefs and intermediate beliefs and eventually arrive at one of the three. From here the work shifts from unhooking pieces of the structure to working with the foundation the structure rests on.

## In Coaching

A coach working with someone whose limiting-belief work is not landing should listen for which keeper is operating. Is the client saying "I should be able to change this but I can't" (often I'm bad)? Is the client saying "this is going to take so much work" (often it's hard)? Is the client backing away when transformation feels close (often I'll be unsafe)?

The intervention then targets the keeper, not the surface belief. The surface belief will dissolve once the keeper is addressed.

## Tone

Joe presents these as common, observable, and dissolvable. He is not cataloging tragedy; he is naming a structure that can be worked. The matter-of-factness makes the three keepers approachable rather than overwhelming.
