# Uncertainty

## Definition

Uncertainty is what is happening now. It is not a special condition that arrives during difficult periods. It is the actual state of being alive at every moment.

"Uncertainty is what's happening now. Like right now, we are not certain about how this podcast is going to go. We are not certain about if it's going to be a good podcast. We're not certain really ever of anything." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The brain runs a prediction routine. The routine is good enough at modeling daily patterns -- wake up, breakfast, work -- that the predicted accuracy registers in the system as certainty. The certainty is not a property of the world. It is a property of the prediction's reliability over short horizons in stable conditions.

The world has not changed when the prediction routine stops working. The prediction routine has just hit conditions it cannot model. The felt-experience of "the world has become uncertain" is the prediction routine failing, not the world losing a property called certainty that it previously had.

"You might wake up and your house might fall on you. You might wake up and your kid's sick that day. You might wake up and you know, we've entered World War III. There are times that are more predictable and less predictable, but there's no really such thing as a certain time. Nobody sat down in 19— in 2010 and said, 'I'm certain that 2020 would look the way 2020 looked.' Nobody. And if they did, they were wrong." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

## What "Uncertain Times" Are, More Accurately

The framing "we are living in uncertain times" is subtly wrong because it implies times-without-uncertainty exist. The more accurate framing:

"All it's doing is saying this time is only bringing a mirror to the reality of our situation, which is, yeah, transformation. You are not the same person you were a decade ago, a year ago. Nobody who's listening to this podcast is. They might want to convince themselves that they are. So we're all changing. It's all changing all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

Periods called "uncertain" are periods where the standard illusion of certainty is harder to maintain. The reality they reveal is the reality that was always there.

## Cycles

Uncertainty is not random noise. It moves in cycles, and the cycles are observable across scales.

- Redwood forests have big fires every 50 years, small fires every 5.
- Predator-prey populations cycle up and down.
- Human institutions redefine themselves on approximately 80-year cycles.

Inside these cycles, the moments of rapid transformation are the moments when the most change becomes possible. See [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]]. The same cycle that is felt as chaos and threat is the cycle that contains the largest opportunity for reorganization.

This generalizes to personal life. The divorce, the lost job, the sick child, the moment of "holy shit, everything is changing" — these are the moments where most personal transformation becomes possible. The work is to take advantage of those moments rather than to wait them out.

## The Short-Term vs. Long-Term Neurology

Uncertainty produces different states in the nervous system depending on duration.

**Short-term uncertainty: dopamine.** The unexpected twist in the song, the unpredictable moment in the movie, the adventure that starts when "I'm going to travel through Southeast Asia." Short-term uncertainty is what gives life its texture. We seek it. We are bored without it. Teenagers who grow up in too-stable conditions manufacture drama because the system requires some uncertainty to feel alive.

"There's some level of uncertainty that makes us feel alive and we want it." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

**Long-term sustained uncertainty: cortisol.** When uncertainty runs past the window of tolerance and stays past it, the system shifts. Cortisol replaces dopamine. Negative thinking takes over. Doomsdaying begins. Problem-solving capacity goes offline. The person becomes less effective, not more.

The shift between these states is not about the amount of uncertainty. It is about the duration of unprocessed uncertainty in a person who has not taken ownership of how they want to be inside it. The same external situation produces dopamine in one person and cortisol in another based entirely on whether the situation is being met or being braced against.

## The Window of Tolerance and the Transformation Trade-Off

Most people are looking for a perfect window of tolerance — enough uncertainty to feel alive, not so much that they tip into cortisol. This is a reasonable target for daily life. It is also the window inside which massive transformation almost never happens.

"There's part of us that likes uncertainty. We know we thrive off of uncertainty on some level. We want the adventure, we want the movie, we want the song to be a little different than every other pop song. We want that uncertain twist. And then there's some window of tolerance where we're either living with uncertainty for so long that it starts making us neurologically have more cortisol, and therefore we start thinking more negatively, we start doomsdaying, or we go out of that window of tolerance and we start trying to exact control and we get rigid, or we get to the point where we're just not as effective. And so oftentimes what humans are looking for is to be in this perfect window of tolerance. Yeah, but massive transformation doesn't happen in that window of tolerance very often. It's very rare that massive transformation happens in that window of tolerance." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The implication for coaching: a person who is consistently uncomfortable about the level of uncertainty in their life is also a person who is consistently being offered the opportunity for transformation. The discomfort is not the problem. The bracing against the discomfort is.

## Why We Want Some Uncertainty

If certainty were actually available and chosen, the result would be unlivable. The thought experiment Joe offers:

> Could you imagine you're like, I am going to marry this person and I'm told from on high with no uncertainty that this is the way the marriage is going to be. This is how you're going to feel for the entire marriage.

That marriage would have no transformation in it. The same applies to a retreat where every step is known in advance, a career where every promotion is scripted, a friendship where every conversation is predictable. The certainty is what we say we want; the having-of-it is what we would refuse if it were actually offered.

This is one of the reasons the search for certainty during chaotic times is misdirected even on its own terms. The thing being searched for is not actually wanted.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The market crashes and the person spends three months in a fear-fog.
- A new technology arrives and the person feels paralyzed about their career.
- An election produces an outcome the person did not expect and they cannot think clearly for weeks.
- A pandemic begins and the person oscillates between catastrophizing and forced positivity.
- A diagnosis arrives in the family and the person becomes unable to function in the rest of their life.
- A person in chronic uncertainty discovers they have stopped registering their own anxiety as anxiety — it has just become how they feel.

The common shape: the person is acting as if certainty existed and they have temporarily lost access to it. The actual situation: certainty was always an illusion and the person is being asked to live in the real conditions of being alive.

## Tone

Joe is not solemn about uncertainty. He treats it as factual and almost casual. The reframe — "uncertainty is what's happening now" — is delivered in the tone of someone naming what is in front of everyone. The implicit invitation is to relax the bracing, not to take on a new project.
