# Authority Issues

## Definition

An authority issue is any pattern in which a person treats an authority figure as something other than a human being. The specific costume varies — the authority is feared, pleased, rebelled against, looked to for validation, idealized, demonized — but the structural feature is the same: a projection is being laid over the person who occupies the authority slot, and that projection is doing the relating instead of the actual relationship.

"Authority issues basically means that you are treating authorities not like human beings... that you either think that the authority is trying to oppress you, or the authority is somebody that you're trying to please, or the authority somehow can validate you, or you're just not treating them like a normal human being the way you would treat a friend." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

Almost everyone has them. The childhood relationship with parents — the first authorities — installs the template, and the template transfers onto whatever inherits the parental slot: teacher, boss, mentor, spiritual teacher, CEO, president, institution. The shape stays; the target rotates. This is the [[projections]] mechanism running in the authority domain.

The diagnostic is not the strength of the feeling toward the authority. It is whether the authority is being met as a person. Strong feelings can be present in human-to-human meeting. The authority issue is the part that is reacting to something other than the person in front of you.

## The Two Costs

**Personal cost: distorted access.** If the authority issue is rebellion, mentorship becomes inaccessible. The person cannot learn from teachers because all teachers fail the projection test. They reinvent the wheel. They get kicked out of college for being right. They do not get good advice through their life. Joe describes his own version:

"That relationship was not functional at all. So I went through life not trusting any authority. The idea of having mentorship, I couldn't even conceive of it until I was well into my 30s that somebody would mentor me. So I lost that. Like with teachers, I just didn't trust any teacher. I could read the book, but I wouldn't actually go and hang out with any kind of spiritual teacher because I couldn't trust them." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

If the authority issue is approval-seeking, the person does not get to think for themselves, does not get to know their own wants, leads their life from assumed authority preferences rather than from their truth. See [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] for the presenting concern and its full coaching path.

**Relational cost: distorted authority.** The authority figure does not get treated as a human, which makes it harder for the authority to act as one. Nobody gives them real feedback. Nobody gives them bad news. They get either kissed-up-to or attacked-for-no-reason — sometimes both within the same hour. This produces the documented loss of empathy in long-term power holders, the selfishness, the conviction that ordinary people need to be told what to do, and the deep loneliness that runs through people at the top of large organizations. See [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] for the lonely-CEO concern and the underlying mechanism.

The two costs interact. The follower's projection distorts the authority. The distorted authority behaves in ways that justify the original projection. The system stabilizes around the dysfunction. This is why Joe describes the relationship as a dance rather than a one-way structure:

"It really creates some crazy dynamics for the person who is apparently in an authority as well... the culture affects the CEO as much as the CEO affects the culture. It is an actual dance. There's the authority and the culture, they have a dance together... the authority figure is channeling, like, the way the group treats them." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## The Four Positions

A person with an authority issue typically occupies one of four positions in relation to authority. Each is a distinct pattern with a distinct origin, distinct cost, and distinct coaching path. See [[the-four-authority-positions]] for the structural map of all four with the specific moves for each.

Briefly:

1. **The Rebel.** "Fuck authority. I don't trust you." Often originates in a childhood where it was not safe to have wants, so the rebellion preserved a self that could.
2. **The Approval-Seeker.** "Tell me I did well." Often originates in a childhood where being good produced love, so the seeking preserved access to it.
3. **The Reluctant Authority.** "I don't want to be the authority because I hated being controlled by one." Often the rebel who became the leader and now under-asserts, leaving the team in chaos. See [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]].
4. **The Alone-At-The-Top Authority.** "I'm carrying it all and nobody really sees me." Often the leader who has lost contact with the team because the team's projections and the leader's own walls have closed off the human channel. See [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]] and [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]].

A single person often runs more than one position. The rebel-turned-CEO often runs Position 1 with their board and Position 3 with their team and Position 4 with themselves at the end of the day.

## The Mechanism: Handing Power Over

What unifies all four positions is a single structural move: the person hands their power over to the authority instead of treating the relationship as one between two empowered humans.

"It's, on a personal level, we're handing our power over one way or another, whether I'm handing the — like I handed my power over by rebelling against, or somebody's handing power over by trying to get approval. Either way, we're not paying attention to our choices. And if we really deal with it and treat them like a human, then we're empowered. But we have to take responsibility for the relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

Rebellion looks like a refusal to hand over power. Structurally, it is the opposite. To rebel is to organize one's behavior around opposing what the authority wants. The authority is still the reference point. The behavior is still determined by what the authority is doing. This is why [[empowered-vs-power]] explicitly names rebellion as a disempowered stance: both compliance and rebellion put the authority in the control seat.

Empowerment in the authority domain looks like neither rebellion nor compliance: it looks like being you, having your wants, speaking the truth, regardless of what the authority is doing. The relationship becomes two humans, each with a role, working together. See the [[fight-or-roll-over-binary]] for the trap and [[upright-undefended-love]] for the third option.

## The Same Pattern Internally

The voice in the head often acts as an authority figure. The same costume that runs externally runs internally:

"The critical voice in the head often acts like an authority figure and as you don't buy into it, your reflection in the world is that you don't buy into the authority outside either. You're like, 'oh, you've got some good things to say, you've got — maybe I have to translate you. No, I'm not going to listen to that.' That kind of freedom that you have with your own internal authority figure often shows exactly how you're going to treat authorities in your own life." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

This is why the work on authority issues is not primarily about managing the external boss or the actual father. It is about the internal authority — the voice that says you should, the voice that says you have not earned it, the voice that says you are not allowed. The relationship to the internal authority is the substrate. The external relationships are downstream.

## The Inverse: When the Authority Has Authority Issues

The authority figure has their own authority issue. The lonely-CEO who hates being treated as the boss is running an authority issue with themselves — they took the role but cannot inhabit it because they associate the role with what their own original authority did to them. The CEO who handles everything except the moment when control is impossible is also running one. The angry leader who attacks people when scared is running one. See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] for the CEO-patterns version and [[no-doctrine-leadership]] for the under-asserting version.

## The Same Pattern in Couples

Romantic relationships frequently run the same dynamic. One partner under-asserts their authority ("I don't want to make the decisions, I don't want to be the parent here"), and the other partner gets either left alone in it or drifts into the parental slot. The dynamic Joe names for organizations applies to couples without modification:

"That dynamic that you're talking about wouldn't necessarily work on the leader if they were able to stay really in themselves, in their vulnerability and their curiosity... I don't have anything to say about it. I just — yes, you are absolutely fucking right. That's a beautiful scene." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues (responding to the couples observation)

The same four positions appear in marriages. The same handing-over of power. The same dance. The same coaching paths work in modified form. See [[romantic-relationships]] for the broader domain.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They lose access to themselves around a specific category of person (often parental in shape)
- They cannot accept mentorship even when it is being offered
- They consistently dismiss teachers as not good enough
- They organize their life around earning their boss's approval and feel hollow when it arrives
- They have become the leader they swore they would not become
- They feel alone at the top while surrounded by people
- They notice that they cannot listen to themselves because the authority voice (external or internal) is too loud

## The Diagnostic for an AI Coach

The presence of an authority issue is recognizable by features that do not fit the present-tense relationship:

- The intensity of reaction is disproportionate to the person
- The reaction shows up with one category of person and not others
- The person uses parental language to describe a non-parent ("my boss is just like my dad")
- The person describes the authority in stark terms (all good, all bad)
- The person reports being unable to think clearly in the authority's presence
- The person idealizes a teacher to the point of self-flattening
- The person dismisses a teacher with heat
- The leader-coachee describes the team in terms of what they fail to do, never what they bring
- The leader-coachee describes themselves as alone in a position that is full of people

When two or more of these features appear, the authority issue frame is worth introducing. The introduction is rarely the first move — usually the work proceeds through the felt experience (the freeze, the seeking, the loneliness) and surfaces the authority frame as the pattern becomes visible.

## Tone

Joe holds this topic with a particular warmth, partly because it is his own territory. He describes his "well-seasoned bouquet" of authority issues with humor. He names what they cost him — the inability to receive mentorship, getting kicked out of college, reinventing the wheel — without melodrama. He also names what they gave him: the experimental freedom that produced this work. The framing is generative rather than pathological: authority issues are not failures to be fixed but patterns to be seen, with both costs and benefits visible.

## Boundary Conditions

The "see them as human" instruction has a real limit: some authorities are running their own severe pathology, and "seeing them as human" can be misread as tolerating abuse. See [[how-do-i-deal-with-narcissistic-parent]] for the narcissist case. The principle is not "everyone deserves your openness." The principle is "your projection is preventing you from accurately reading the person in front of you." Sometimes the accurate reading is that the person is dangerous. The projection-clearing makes that reading possible; it does not predetermine its content.

The framework also is not a license to dismiss legitimate criticism of bad leadership as the critic's authority issue. The presence of authority issues in the speaker does not invalidate the content of the criticism. The two questions — "is this true?" and "is my projection coloring how I'm receiving this?" — are separate questions. Both deserve honest answers.
