# Power Dynamics: Two People Who Both Think They're Defending

## Definition

Every power dynamic between two people has the same structure: both people think they are defending, and both people think the other is attacking. Neither sees themselves as the aggressor. Neither hears the other as defending. The conflict runs because the asymmetry is invisible from inside either head.

This is the simplest available frame for what is happening in any non-playful power struggle: a boss and a peer fighting, a wife and a husband fighting, two friends, two political factions. Play power dynamics (agreed-upon, sexual, gamed) are excluded. The frame is for unconsented conflict where each side is trying to get their way.

"All power dynamics are is two people who are scared. And the most simple way to look at it, even more simple than the drama triangle, is to see that it's two people who think they're defending themselves and think the other person is attacking them. So one person feels like they're defending and the other person feels like they're being attacked. And both people walk around thinking what they just said was a defense and the other person is hearing that as an attack." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

## Why This Frame Dissolves the Dynamic

The instant a person sees the structure, the apparent attack stops being an attack. The other person becomes legibly scared rather than maliciously aggressive. The internal stance of "I am being attacked and must defend" loses its grip because the input has been reclassified.

"If you can see all of these conflicts where someone's trying to have power over the other person, it immediately, the whole thing dissolves. You're just like, oh, wait a second. Okay, that person's not trying to attack me. That person thinks they're defending themselves right now." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

The frame is not a strategy to win the conflict. It is a reframe that exits the conflict's logic. The frame does not require the other person to agree, change, or even know it has been applied. It works through one person's perception shift.

## The Underlying Fears

Both people are scared of losing something they love. They may be scared of different things. They are both scared.

The fears are not symmetrical in content, but they are symmetrical in structure. One may be scared of losing control. The other may be scared of losing dignity. One may be scared of being abandoned. The other may be scared of being engulfed. The specific fears differ. The fact of fear underneath the defense is the same.

This is why winning the argument almost never satisfies. The fear was not addressed by the win. The next conflict will be the same fear, restaged with new content.

## Fear Shows Up as Fight, Flight, or Freeze

The fear produces one of three responses, which become the three roles of the [[fear-triangle]]:
- **Fight** -> bully behavior
- **Freeze** -> victim behavior
- **Flight** -> savior behavior (leaving yourself to go fix the other)

Each role is fear in a costume. None of the costumes look like fear from the inside. The bully feels alone and self-reliant. The victim feels trapped and stuck. The savior feels obligated and responsible. None of them feels "scared" the way scared is normally pictured, and that is precisely why the fear is hard to see and the dynamic is hard to exit.

## Avoidance Creates the Thing

The deepest fasteners of the dynamic: the behavior each role uses to avoid its core feeling produces more of that feeling.

- The victim's passive-aggression produces agitation in others. The agitated reaction makes the victim feel more stuck.
- The bully's "fuck you all" stance pushes people away. The distance makes the bully feel more alone.
- The savior's caretaking signals to the other that they are incapable. The other resents being managed. The savior feels more obligated to repair the damage.

"It's why when you're in those kind of power dynamics, everybody feels so helpless and frustrated. It's because it's like, fuck, I don't know how to get out of this because we're driving the thing that we don't want to us through our actions that we use to try to avoid it." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

This is one specific case of the broader principle [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The mechanism in power dynamics is mediated through the other person's reaction. The other person becomes the delivery vehicle for the thing the role exists to avoid.

## Asymmetry Is Visible to Others

The frame describes why outside observers can see the dynamic clearly while the participants cannot. From the outside, two scared people defending against each other are visible as two scared people defending against each other. From inside either head, the only visible feature is the other person's apparent aggression.

This is why a third party can often dissolve a fight that the two participants cannot resolve. The third party is not in either defensive frame and can name the symmetry. Naming the symmetry is often enough.

## The Same Structure in Every Relationship Fight

The frame holds with particular force in long-term relationships. Joe is unequivocal: every fight in a relationship runs on this structure.

"All these fights that we get into in our relationships, they're all -- you can break them down to pretty much the same basic thing. It's like one person feels unseen and -- or so both people feel unseen in that moment or feel unheard, unseen, not grokked, not respected, some version of that. And there's a desire to change the other person. And that is the quintessential part of all fights and that somebody's closed or both people have closed down their heart." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

"I've never seen a fight that isn't basically about that in a relationship." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The diagnostic for a relationship fight has three components and all three are usually present:

1. Both people feel unseen / unheard / disrespected
2. Both people want the other to change
3. Both people have closed their hearts

The exit move is correspondingly clear: one or both people drops the demand-for-change, opens their heart, and gives real attention to seeing the other person. The fight does not require both people to do this. One person can flip the switch and the fight dissolves -- though it may take time.

"Every fight can be resolved with one and/or both people saying, 'Oh, I'm going to love you unconditionally even in this situation. Here, let me take the time to see you. Let me make sure that I'm seeing you correctly. Let me make sure that I understand what you need to be respected. And I'm not going to try to change you anymore.' If you flip that switch in a fight, the fight goes away. And it might take a while to go away." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The reason most people do not flip the switch is the conditional clause: "I'm not gonna open my heart until they open their heart. I'm not gonna see them until I'm seen. I'm always the one who sees them. I need a partner that does the work." Each of these is the same hostage move -- making your own freedom contingent on the partner's behavior, which means you never get it.

"It's like saying, hey, I'm not going to be free until they're free. Like, what the hell? It makes no sense from this perspective." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The reframe: my freedom is mine. Opening my heart is mine. Whether the partner does the same is theirs. Doing it first, regardless of whether they reciprocate, is the move that ends the fight. See [[connection-doesnt-require-the-other]] and [[upright-undefended-love]] for the underlying principles.

## What This Frame Is Not

It is not "both sides are equally responsible." Fault is a separate question. The structure (mutual defense, mutual fear) is symmetric. The harm may not be. Naming the structure is not a moral equivalence claim; it is a perceptual one.

It is not a denial that real attack exists. Some attacks are attacks. The frame applies when both people would each report that they are defending and the other is attacking. If only one party feels attacked and the other is openly aggressive, the structure is different.

It is not a tool to use during the fight to win the fight. Applied internally as a reframe ("oh, they think they are defending"), it usually dissolves the fight. Applied externally as a weapon ("you only think you're attacking; really you're defending"), it adds another layer of attack and intensifies the dynamic.

## The Internal Version

Inside one head, the same dynamic plays. The voice in the head sets up an argument with itself in which both sides feel attacked. The bully voice criticizes. The victim voice protests being criticized. The savior voice tries to fix both. All three feel defended. All three feel attacked by the others.

The internal version is the same structure as the external one. See [[fear-triangle]] for the three roles as internal voices.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A relationship where every conversation feels like an attack and every response feels like reasonable defense
- A workplace dynamic where you and a colleague both feel the other is being unreasonable
- A family pattern where everyone is sure they are the one being misunderstood
- A political conversation where both sides claim self-defense against the other's aggression
- An internal loop where one part of you is criticizing another part, and both feel justified

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents a conflict and the entire narrative is "they did this to me, so I had to respond"
- When two participants in a session each describe a recent fight in terms only of the other's attacks
- When a person is in [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]] about a relationship: "either I let them walk over me or I have to fight them"
- When [[anxiety-as-constricted-life-force]] is active and the person is in a high-conflict period

## Tone

Plain, observational, almost biological. Joe does not deliver this as a moral teaching. It is closer to weather reporting. Two scared people, both defending, both feeling attacked. The plainness is what makes the frame land. There is no blame in it.
