# Trust Is Multifaceted

## Definition

Trust is not a single quality. It is a cluster of distinct trusts, each addressing a different dimension of working together. Two people can have high trust in one dimension and zero trust in another. Listing out the dimensions makes the actual relationship visible.

"Trust is multifaceted. If I'm working with you, we work together. So I have trust for you. I have trust that we will talk and we will listen to each other and we will be true to ourselves. I have trust financially that you're not gonna screw me and that I am not going to screw you. I have trust in you that we can have conflict and we can resolve that conflict and be stronger on the other side. I have trust that you're gonna do the things that are important to making the podcast successful and you're gonna do the things you say you're gonna do. I have trust in your capacity to perform on the podcast. Trust is multifaceted." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Dimensions

The dimensions Joe enumerates in this articulation:

- **Communicative trust:** We will talk and we will listen to each other and we will be true to ourselves.
- **Financial trust:** You are not going to screw me; I am not going to screw you.
- **Conflict trust:** We can have conflict and we can resolve it and be stronger on the other side.
- **Reliability trust:** You are going to do the things you say you are going to do.
- **Importance trust:** You are going to do the things that are important to the work succeeding.
- **Performance trust:** You can do the job; you have the capacity to perform.

The list is not exhaustive. Each relationship may surface its own dimensions. The point is that any blanket statement like "I trust this person" or "I don't trust this person" hides the actual structure underneath.

## Why This Matters

When trust is treated as one thing, breakdowns are mysterious. "I don't trust them" is non-actionable. When trust is treated as multifaceted, the specific dimension that is broken becomes addressable.

A common pattern: a team has high reliability trust and high financial trust, but no conflict trust. The team gets the work done but cannot handle disagreement. The blanket statement "we have a great team" hides the fact that one dimension is missing and is producing all the dysfunction. Naming the missing dimension makes the work possible.

Another pattern: a CEO has high performance trust in a direct report but low communicative trust. The CEO keeps the person and keeps avoiding the difficult conversations because the person performs. The performance covers for the communication failure. The hidden cost is the team's morale, which is being eroded by the unaddressed communication problem.

## Each Dimension Has Its Own Repair

Different breakdowns require different repairs. Reliability trust gets rebuilt by following through. Financial trust gets rebuilt by transparent accounting. Conflict trust gets rebuilt by having a hard conversation and surviving it. Communicative trust gets rebuilt by saying the thing that has been left unsaid.

Trying to repair the wrong dimension produces wasted effort. "Let's spend more time together" does not fix financial trust. "Let's be more transparent about money" does not fix conflict trust.

## Trust Is Hard to Manipulate

A defining property of trust compared to its weaker relative [[trust-vs-safety|safety]]: trust is hard to weaponize. You cannot demand trust the way you can demand safety. You can demand specific behaviors that would make you more trustworthy ("stop stealing from me"), but the trust itself has to be earned through the behaviors.

"It's very hard to control somebody out of trust. You need to be more trustworthy by X, Y, and Z usually is a pretty solid like, yeah, you've stolen from me and I can't trust you, so this is going to end the relationship, or you need to figure out how to convince me to trust you again. So it's harder to manipulate other people with trust." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The asymmetry: "I don't feel safe" can be used to demand others change their behavior even when the demand is arbitrary. "I don't trust you" can be addressed only by becoming more trustworthy, which is the actual work. The harder-to-manipulate quality of trust is why it is the better organizing concept for team functionality.

## How Trust Gets Built

Across all dimensions, trust is built through similar mechanisms:

- **Having the difficult conversations** rather than avoiding them. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]] and [[trust-requires-conflict]].
- **Doing what you say you will do.** The reliability dimension is the simplest to articulate and the most often violated.
- **Dealing with the dirty laundry.** Whether between people, about a failing product, or about a leader's wandering attention, the unaddressed thing erodes trust. Naming it builds trust.
- **Sharing what is happening for you.** A leader who hides their own state cannot be trusted because the team is always trying to read what is actually going on. See [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]] and the principle below.

"Building trust is critical and conflict avoidance is one of the ways to destroy that. Not doing what you say you're gonna do, allowing people to be in a non-accountable situation, like not dealing with the dirty laundry, whether that's dirty laundry between two people, whether that's the fact that this product isn't selling, whether that's the fact that, you know, you want to have sex with people outside of your marriage. Not dealing with that, not talking about it, not bringing it up, that is a way to destroy trust. Bringing it up is a way to create trust." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Withholding Erodes Trust

A specific mechanism: if you cannot tell the people you work with what is happening with you that might affect the relationship, there is no trust. The withholding itself is the betrayal of trust, regardless of how legitimate the reason for withholding feels.

"If I can't tell you, if I can't tell you what's going on with me that might affect our relationship, there's no trust in that." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

This is especially common with leaders. The CEO who hides their fear from the team, believing they are protecting the team, is destroying the team's ability to trust them. The team learns to brace for the bad mood, to predict the reaction, to operate around the unspoken state. The protection becomes the corrosion.

"You see that all the time, particularly with executives, particularly with CEOs, is that they don't share with their team how they're feeling because they have this idea that they need to take care of everybody, that they're responsible for everybody. So they have to handle it all on their own. And so they're not saying to their team, hey, I'm really scared about X, Y, and Z, or the way you said that doesn't work for me. Because they think that they have to fix it. And therefore their team can't trust them." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The leader is also failing to trust the team in the reverse direction. The withholding signals "I don't trust you to handle this." The team feels the distrust as the absence of being included. See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]].

## The False-Trust Trap

A common pattern: people lead with feigned trust they do not actually have because they want to be in a team that trusts each other. They then either get burned, or they defend themselves subtly while continuing to perform trust.

"Sometimes we can fall into the belief that we wanna be in a team that trusts one another, so we just lead by feigning trust that we don't actually have. And then we get either burned or we subtly defend ourselves in ways that we don't own without just being clear about where..."

Joe completes the thought: "There's no trust in that."

The repair is not to perform more trust. The repair is to name the actual state of trust honestly. "I notice I am not trusting you here, and I don't want to be in a team where I am pretending to trust you." Naming the dimension where trust is missing is the first move toward building it.

## Self-Trust Is Also Multifaceted

The same structural insight applies to trust of self. "I don't trust myself" is as non-actionable as "I don't trust them." The actual structure has dimensions:

- **Trust in your action capacity:** that you will do what you say you will do for yourself
- **Trust in your perception:** that what you notice is real, that your read of a situation is reliable
- **Trust in your intuition:** that the impulse from your body has information worth following
- **Trust in your discernment:** that you can tell fear from intuition, projection from perception
- **Trust in your aliveness:** that what you want is worth following, that your fire is not dangerous
- **Trust in your worth:** that you are okay if the outcome doesn't go the way you wanted

When a person says "I can't trust myself," they often mean one or two of these dimensions specifically. They are trusting themselves in many of the others continuously without noticing. The blanket statement obscures what is actually missing.

See [[how-do-i-trust-myself]] for the full coaching path on self-trust collapse and [[how-do-you-not-trust-it]] for the inversion question that surfaces which dimensions are actually working.

A common pattern: the person has high action-capacity trust and high perception trust but low discernment trust about a specific decision domain (career, relationship, money). They report "I don't trust myself" globally. The actual issue is the one domain. Once named, the issue is workable -- it stops being a self-trust collapse and becomes a specific domain question.

A deeper pattern: the trust collapse is actually fear-of-outcome dressed as a trust question. See [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]]. The person is reading the felt-decision quality of their current situation as evidence of self-trust failure. The fear is what's producing the decision-feel and the decision-feel is what's producing the trust frame. Underneath the trust question is an outcome attachment that has not been felt yet.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You realize you have been treating "I trust them" or "I don't trust them" as final, when the actual structure is more granular
- You notice that one specific dimension of trust is broken in a key relationship, but the rest are intact
- You see that your team's dysfunction is in one specific dimension, and other dimensions are working
- You catch yourself performing trust you do not have, or denying trust that is actually present in some dimensions
- A leader recognizes they have high performance trust in someone but low communicative trust, and that they have been letting the performance cover for the communication failure

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A team conversation about "we need more trust" without specifying what kind
- A leader trying to repair a relationship and not knowing where the actual breakdown is
- A team member experiencing the diffuse sense of not being able to rely on a peer and trying to name what specifically is off
- A coaching session where blanket statements about trust are blocking the diagnosis
