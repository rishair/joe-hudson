# Humility vs. Disempowerment (Making Yourself Small)

## The Distinction

The two get confused constantly. They look similar in social terms: the person speaks softly, defers, does not take up space, claims not to know better than the room. Structurally they are opposites.

**Humility** is the dissolution of the defending self. There is no identity left to push, so there is no push. The person is empowered, capable of conflict, capable of self-promotion, capable of saying hard things -- but not running on the identity that would make those acts about themselves.

**Disempowerment** (often called humility, especially by the disempowered person) is making yourself small as a strategy. It runs on identity: "I am small," "I do not deserve to take up space," "I should not have wants." It is conflict avoidance, codependence, or caretaking dressed in virtue language. The "smallness" is the identity being protected.

The distinguishing question: what would happen if you actually took up your full space? If the answer involves discomfort, fear of being attacked, or fear of losing approval, you are not looking at humility. You are looking at the strategy that has been called humility.

## Why They Get Confused

The social training is the cause. From childhood, many people are taught that humility means smallness: don't brag, don't ask for things, defer to authority, put others first. The shape of that training is the shape of conflict avoidance. The child who learns to be "humble" in this sense learns to suppress wants, suppress conflict, suppress self.

The adult version: a person who never asks for a raise, never takes credit for their work, never disagrees with the boss, calls this humility. It is not. It is suppression with a virtue label.

"Disempowerment gets confused with humility because people are actually just being conflict avoidant, or they're being codependent, which means that they're trying to make somebody else happy so that they don't get attacked or they don't get in trouble." -- Joe Hudson, Humility

The distinction matters because the strategy installs the very problems it tries to avoid. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]] and [[walking-on-eggshells]].

## What Each Looks Like Under Pressure

The clearest test comes when conflict arrives.

**The disempowered person** retreats. Goes quiet. Tries to make themselves smaller so the threat passes. May resentfully comply. May passive-aggressively express what cannot be said directly. May leak through guilt induction. The retreat is the protection of the identity ("I am the one who avoids trouble").

**The humble person** stays present. May get hit. May get attacked. Does not defend. Does not attack back. Is not made smaller by the attack because there is no self there to be made smaller. The non-defendedness is itself a kind of force.

"Humility often means that you're walking into an attack. It means that you're walking into a situation that you know you're going to be attacked and you're doing it with an open heart instead of doing it with a defensive shield. The humble person doesn't avoid the conflict, but comes in with an open heart, comes in undefended. And that is incredibly empowering. When you actually experience someone coming at you undefended, it's like in Taoism, they talk about it: if someone's coming at you with a sword, be water. If you ever see a man with a sword attack a lake, that's what it feels like. And you're like, 'oh shit, I'm the lake.'" -- Joe Hudson, Humility

The Martin Luther King example: walking into attacks repeatedly, undefended, nonviolent, not attacking back, but not making himself small either. That required humility. It was not avoidance. It was the opposite.

## What Each Looks Like With Recognition

A live diagnostic: the person wins an award.

**The disempowered person** deflects. "Oh no, no, it was nothing. The team did all the work. I didn't really do anything." The recognition does not land. The people offering the recognition feel like they wasted their effort.

**The humble person** receives. Lets the recognition move through. Does not make it about themselves but does not block it either. The recognition does its work. The room feels honored.

"It doesn't get in for you, and the people who are giving it feel like, 'okay, well, that was dumb. Why'd we do this?'" -- Brett Kistler, on the deflection version

The deflection is not humility. It is the protection of the identity "I am small." See [[humility]] for the receiving-as-hollow-bone move.

## What Each Looks Like With Self-Promotion

A common live question: how do I self-promote without losing humility? The framing assumes self-promotion and humility are at odds. They are not. They are at odds with disempowerment.

The humble version: if the call requires self-promotion, you self-promote. You do it with the discomfort that comes with doing things in the way of your identity. You stay awake to the dangers of self-promotion (it can pull you into ego, it can attract attack). You listen to the wisdom in the resistance. But you do the thing because the call says to do it.

The disempowered version: you refuse to self-promote because "that's not who I am." The refusal is the identity protecting itself.

"Even if that call is self-promotion, which makes you feel uncomfortable -- I do things in our business all the time that make me feel uncomfortable. But that's the calling. That requires a certain amount of humility because every one of those uncomfortable things sands away at my ego. They're uncomfortable specifically because they are in the way of my identity." -- Joe Hudson, Humility

For the underlying mechanic of following the call see [[enjoyment-as-surrender]].

## What Each Looks Like With Self-Care

The clearest structural difference: humility includes self-care. Disempowerment does not.

The humble person takes care of themselves because the channel cannot stay clear otherwise. The disempowered person does not, because the identity "I am the one who serves" prohibits it.

"Isn't that fascinating? Humility actually requires self-care. And in fact, that is exactly what it requires. You'll notice people who actually have a deep sense of humility are very good at taking care of themselves generally." -- Joe Hudson, Humility

If a person calls themselves humble and is also chronically depleted, self-abandoning, or burning out from service, the diagnosis is not humility. It is the disempowered version. See [[what-is-truly-compassionate]] and [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

## How to Tell Which One Is Operating in Yourself

Ask: what would happen if I took up my full space right now?

- If the answer is fear of attack: disempowerment
- If the answer is fear of being seen as arrogant: disempowerment (the identity "humble" is being protected)
- If the answer is fear of losing approval: disempowerment
- If the answer is fear of having to feel uncomfortable as identity gets sanded away: probably humility moving in
- If the answer is "nothing -- I'd just take up the space": humility already present

The 12-year-old-with-an-abusive-father test: imagine a child raised in an unsafe environment asking "how do I be humble here?" Telling them to make themselves small is not humility. The answer is whatever takes care of them. That principle scales. Humility for an adult includes taking care of themselves. Anything that does not is the disempowered version.

"What is the humble behavior from the son? Is it to make themselves small and back away? Is it to stand up and fight? What's the humble behavior? My answer would be whatever is required to take care of themselves." -- exchange between Joe Hudson and Brett Kistler, Humility

## How to Tell Which One Is Operating in Someone Else

Listen for what gets defended. Disempowered "humility" defends. It defends the smallness. Suggest the person is more capable than they claim and watch the defense come out. Suggest they should ask for more and watch the resistance arrive in the language of "that's not who I am."

The humble person does not defend the humility. They will laugh at the suggestion that they are not humble. They will let the suggestion in. They will receive it the same way they receive a compliment: through and through.

If someone calls themselves humble and gets defensive when challenged, the humility is the identity. See [[the-arrogantly-humble-paradox]].

## The Risk in Confusing Them

When disempowerment is taught as humility, people install the strategy as virtue. The conflict avoidance gets locked in by the moral framing. The codependence gets locked in. The self-suppression gets locked in. The person now has to defend not just the behavior but its righteousness.

This is part of why so much spiritual teaching produces people who are passive, self-erasing, and resentful underneath. The teaching pointed at humility. The student installed disempowerment. The two looked similar enough that the substitution was not caught.

See [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the related dynamic where ego dresses up in spiritual language. See [[weaponized-not-caretaking]] for the other failure mode where someone uses "not caretaking" as a wall.

## The Empowered Side of Humility

The most important consequence of the distinction: humility is not the opposite of empowerment. It includes empowerment. It is what allows empowerment to exist without collapsing into arrogance.

"Most people wouldn't think that feeling is associated with humility. Like if you actually feel, 'oh, I'm totally empowered to create the life that I want and to create the world that I want' -- most people are going to think, 'oh, that feeling is really a sister or brother of arrogance.' But it ends up not being that at all because that feeling can't actually really happen unless there's a lot of love and not very much fear happening. There's this deep well of emptiness behind that empowerment, which is the humility." -- Joe Hudson, Humility

The visualization that makes this directly available in the body is [[feel-love-and-empowerment-as-the-same]]. The underlying principle is [[love-requires-empowerment]].
