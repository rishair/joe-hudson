# Grounded Excitement vs. Ungrounded Excitement

## Definition

Excitement is not a single signal. There is grounded excitement -- the body genuinely amped and metabolically prepared for what is about to happen, with any anxieties present integrated into the readiness -- and there is ungrounded excitement -- excitement that is covering an anxiety the person is not letting move through.

Both feel like excitement on the surface. The difference is whether the underlying anxiety has been integrated or is being managed out of awareness. The first is a green light. The second looks like a green light and is actually the suppression that produces the failure.

This is a finer cut than [[fear-vs-excitement]]. Fear vs. excitement asks whether the energy is constricted or open. Grounded vs. ungrounded asks, within the open-feeling energy, whether it is integrated or compensatory.

## Grounded Excitement

The body is amped in a way that fits the situation. There is anticipation. There is sharpness. There is a sense of metabolic readiness -- the system is prepared for what is about to happen. If anxieties are present, they have been allowed to move through the body and they are now part of the readiness rather than something being held off.

Brett Kistler describes it from BASE jumping:

"If I'm feeling a grounded level of excitement and anticipation, and I feel like my body is amped in such a way that is like metabolically prepared for what I'm about to do. And I feel that I'm in flow, and I feel like flow is accessible to me. And I'm also continuing -- I'm not just reaching that state because I'm closing, putting on the blinders. I'm reaching that state because I'm welcoming any red flags that there might be. And yeah, just, you're not to be found. Everything feels right." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The somatic signature: amped but not bouncy, sharp but not jittery, alert without seeking. The person is in flow or has flow accessible to them. Red flags, if they arise, get welcomed and addressed -- they do not interrupt the state because the state is built from welcoming, not avoidance.

## Ungrounded Excitement

The excitement is real but it is sitting on top of an unfelt anxiety. The body is bouncy rather than amped. The excitement has a quality of clinging or grasping. The person is reaching for the opportunity, the jump, the deal, partly because the underlying anxiety has not been let through.

"If you're really excited but there's like that excitement is sort of covering up a little bit of anxiety and you're not letting that anxiety move through and integrate into your excitement, then you're going to find yourself a little bit bouncy." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

Other forms ungrounded excitement can take:
- Straight anxiety dressed as excitement -- the person reports feeling pumped but the body is actually braced
- Anxiety funneling into anger -- the person becomes irritable at the exit point, finds someone else to manage, triple-checks the gear of a less experienced jumper
- Reaching for the opportunity in a moment of fear about something else (the numbers, the market, the relationship) -- the excitement is real but it is being recruited as escape

"Like, oh yeah, the numbers are scary, the economy just tanked, everything's a mess, and look at all this opportunity out there. And that's a -- looking at the opportunity is a wonderful thing to do, and clinging to the opportunity, grasping for it, there's a, there's a difference, there's a different feeling in that." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The grasp is the tell. Excitement that wants is different from excitement that needs. The grounded version has nothing to compensate for; the ungrounded version is trying to use the opportunity to manage the fear.

## The Felt Difference

Grounded excitement: amped, sharp, integrated, in flow. The body fits the situation. Anxieties, if present, have been welcomed.

Ungrounded excitement: bouncy, grasping, slightly off-tempo. The body is reaching. Anxieties have been put aside rather than felt through.

The two states can look identical from outside. The person who has trained to feel the difference inside their own body can read it in seconds. The person who has not will mistake one for the other regularly.

## Why It Matters

In high-stakes domains, the mistake of acting on ungrounded excitement is expensive. The BASE jumper who is excited because they don't want to climb down the mountain with their friends makes a worse jump than the one who is excited because everything has been checked and the body is ready. The CEO who is excited about the deal because they need a win makes a worse decision than the one who is excited because the deal is actually good.

The mechanism: ungrounded excitement has dissociated the person from a real signal. The signal will still be there. It will still affect the outcome. The person just won't have access to it during the decision.

Brett's example of a jump where ungrounded excitement would form:

"Let's say that I really want to feel safe and make this jump because I'm with a group of people and I don't want them to have to climb down this mountain with me that we just climbed up, and I don't want them to have to wait by the car while I climb down. So I'm more afraid of disappointing people and not being seen than I am of the jump. Then I might just dissociate myself from some of the risks of the jump so that I reach this point where I'm like, aha, okay, I'm safe." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The "aha, okay, I'm safe" is the ungrounded green light. The body has dissociated. The mind has constructed safety. The fear of disappointing people has been routed under awareness. The jump still happens. The dissociation goes with it.

## The Diagnostic

Two questions to discriminate:

1. **Is there any anxiety I'm not letting myself feel?** If yes, the excitement is at least partly compensatory. The anxiety needs to move through before the excitement can be trusted.

2. **What am I not talking about?** If there is a topic the person is steering around -- the wind conditions, the late night before, the unresolved issue with the partner, the part of the deal that doesn't sit right -- that avoidance is the ungroundedness. The thing not being addressed is what the body is bouncing around.

"What makes me not wanna talk about the wind conditions over that ridge over there? Like, what's making me focus on that it's a nice bright sunny day and not on that I'm really tired and we were out late partying last night and I'm, you know, not really feeling on it right now." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The thing being avoided is the location of the ungrounded layer. Talking about it -- letting the anxiety it generates move through the body -- either resolves the ungroundedness (and the excitement integrates) or surfaces a real "don't do this" signal.

## In Business Contexts

The same distinction applies in a boardroom. A team that is excited about a strategy because the underlying risks have been faced and integrated is in grounded excitement. A team that is excited because no one is willing to feel the consequence of the alternative is in ungrounded excitement.

Joe makes the parallel explicit:

"This is such a metaphor for business, right? It's like the things that we don't talk about, which usually get us in trouble, though they're kind of in the side of our mind, like something's not happening right, right there, but let's not pay attention to it." -- Joe Hudson, But... Is It Safe?

Brett extends:

"You imagine in a boardroom, if there's a risk and you're assessing the risk, and nobody in the room is willing to actually feel through the consequence of taking a wrong action in that risk, then you might -- or the consequence of like the action you may have to take may be more difficult. You may not want to lay people off, you may not want to lay off somebody you have a personal attachment or relationship to. And since you're not willing to look at that, you're far more likely to be like, oh, here's a story about how it's safe... rather than really seeing the deeper reality." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The mechanism is identical to the cliff. The story about why this is safe is built to avoid feeling the consequence of the action that fear would surface. The excitement about the deal floats on top of the unfelt fear.

## The Connection to Defensiveness

When ungrounded excitement is present, defensiveness in the system is one of the signals it leaves. The body knows the excitement is covering something. When the topic that is being avoided gets approached, the system braces.

Joe's diagnostic phrase from the conversation:

"Grounded excitement also sounds like if you notice any defensiveness in your system, that's a red flag." -- Joe Hudson, But... Is It Safe?

If you cannot talk about the wind conditions without defensiveness arising, the excitement about the jump is not grounded. If you cannot talk about the part of the deal you don't want to look at without your body bracing, the excitement about the deal is not grounded. The defensiveness is the trace of what the excitement is trying not to be.

## What This Replaces

The default frame: excitement is good, fear is bad. The presence of excitement means proceed.

The replacement frame: excitement comes in two forms. One is a true green light. The other is the suppression of a fear that is about to express itself through the outcome of the action you are about to take. Discriminate before proceeding.

This connects to [[dont-step-on-the-signal]]: ungrounded excitement is one of the ways fear gets stepped on. It does not look like overriding the fear because the felt state is excitement, not constriction. The signal still got stepped on; it just got stepped on through a more pleasant-feeling mechanism.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person is about to make a high-stakes decision and feels strongly positive about it
- When a team is unanimously excited about a strategy and no one is voicing concerns
- When the excitement has a grasping quality -- the person is reaching for the opportunity rather than meeting it
- When the situation has external pressure (peers, timing, momentum) that could be driving the excitement
- When the person reports feeling "amped" but the body looks bouncy from outside
