# Intuition vs. Conditioning

## The Distinction

Both intuition and conditioning produce immediate responses. You feel a strong pull in one direction with no analysis between input and output. Because both arrive with the same quality of immediacy, the mind cannot reliably tell them apart by speed or by certainty. The mind is the wrong tool. The discrimination is somatic.

The test: notice what happens in the heart at the moment of the immediate response. Intuition arrives with the heart open or opening. Negative conditioning arrives with the heart closing down. The closure is the diagnostic. If you can feel your heart shut as the impulse fires, you are in a conditioned reaction. If the heart stays open or expands, you are in intuition.

"There's the question of, am I having an immediate reaction because I'm following my truth and that's intuition, or am I having an immediate reaction and that's conditioning and it's going to throw me into that shithole pattern that I've thrown myself into a dozen times. And what I notice is that it's a somatic experience to find that out. It's not an intellectual one. And so my recommendation to most people is just every time you make an immediate decision, pay attention to your body and see what the difference is between the two. For me, my experience is that my heart closes down if I'm in conditioning, if I'm in unhealthy conditioning." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

## Why the Mind Cannot Make This Call

The mind is itself one of the surfaces conditioning runs on. The conditioned reaction has typically been rehearsed so many times that the mind has built a complete rationalization for it. By the time the impulse arrives, the mind is already producing the justification. "I should be angry here," "this is how I always handle this," "the situation calls for X" — these are not deliberations. They are post-hoc translations of a body pattern that has already fired.

The same is true in reverse. The mind that has been afraid of intuition (because intuition has cost the person something in the past) will rationalize against the intuitive impulse: "I shouldn't trust this, I'm being impulsive, I should think this through." Both the rationalization-for and the rationalization-against arrive with the same mental quality of confidence. The mind is not the discriminator.

The body is. The heart specifically. The heart is one organ that does not lie about which mode is running, because the closure or opening is the mechanical signature of the mode itself. Conditioning that is negative requires the heart to close — that closure is what allowed the original pattern to install (often around an aggression, a betrayal, a hurt). The closure stays as part of the pattern's signature. Intuition does not require the closure. It often produces a softening, a settling, a felt sense of recognition that operates with the heart open.

## Not All Conditioning Is Negative

A specific qualifier: the test is for negative conditioning, not for all conditioning. Most of life runs on conditioning, and most of that conditioning is helpful. The habit of testing whether your coffee is too hot before you drink it is conditioning. Driving on the right side of the road. Not putting your hand on the stove. These do not produce heart-closure because they are not protective patterns wired around an unbearable feeling. They are practical learnings.

"Some conditioning is great, by the way, right? I, I want to be conditioned not to drink hot coffee that's too damn hot, and that doesn't make me close down my heart when I test to see that my coffee is hot or not hot or whatever. But when I'm in a pattern of, say, rebelling against authority when I was 28 years old, man, my heart had to close down. If I'm in a pattern of reacting to somebody the way I've reacted to people when I felt abandoned, my heart has to close down." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

The distinction inside conditioning: useful conditioning (no heart closure required) vs. negative conditioning (heart closure required, because the pattern was installed around an unbearable feeling and the closure was what made it survivable). It is only the second category that the somatic test discriminates from intuition.

## Examples of the Diagnostic in Use

**Rebelling against authority.** A person at 28 has an immediate impulse to push back against a boss. Is this intuition (the boss is genuinely wrong and the pushback is the right move) or conditioning (a wired pattern from childhood that fires at any authority figure)? The body answer: if the heart closes as the impulse arises, it is the conditioning. The closure is the wiring that was installed around the original (probably parental) aggression.

**Reacting to perceived abandonment.** A partner is late to dinner. The immediate impulse is to be cold, to punish, to withdraw. Is this intuition (something is actually wrong) or conditioning (the abandonment wiring firing)? The body answer: the heart closes for the conditioning. The intuition (if the situation actually warrants concern) would arrive with the heart open and a clear sense of what to say.

**Saying yes to a request.** Someone asks for help. The immediate impulse is yes. Is this intuition (you actually want to help) or conditioning (the approval-seeking pattern saying yes to avoid disappointing them)? The body answer: if the heart closes around the yes (because saying yes requires shutting down what you actually feel), it is the conditioning. If the heart stays open with the yes, it is real availability.

**The pucker before truth.** See [[pucker-before-truth]]. The body signature of truth approaching is a small pucker (a slight bracing). This is distinct from the heart-closure of conditioning. The pucker is the body acknowledging risk; the heart is still open. The conditioned reaction is heavier — the whole chest tightens, the heart shuts down.

## How to Train the Discrimination

Joe's recommendation: every time you have an immediate response, pay attention to the body. Just notice. Over weeks, the signatures become unmistakable.

"My recommendation to most people is just every time you make an immediate decision, pay attention to your body and see what the difference is between the two." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

The training is not about overriding the responses in real time. That comes later. The first job is just to see the difference, repeatedly, so that the difference becomes obvious. Once the difference is obvious, the question "is this intuition or conditioning?" answers itself in the body the moment the impulse arrives.

The training compounds with [[staying-in-your-body]]. The 10% body-attention practice (see [[10-percent-body-attention-practice]]) is the structural prerequisite. You cannot notice the heart's closure or opening if you are not in your body. The body-attention practice makes the heart-check available.

## The Bewilderment Marker for Intuition

A related qualitative marker, from Brett: when you are channeling something true, there is often a vulnerability and openness to it, sometimes even bewilderment — "did I just say that? Is that what I really feel?" This is different from the conditioned reaction, which arrives with the felt quality of "of course, this is what I always do, this is what is right" — the spear and shield in front of a phalanx of warriors behind you.

The bewilderment-feeling-marker for intuition pairs with the open-heart-test. Both point at the same underlying mode. Intuition is unrehearsed. Conditioning is over-rehearsed. The mind cannot tell the rehearsed from the unrehearsed because both feel automatic. The body can. The heart specifically.

## What This Distinction Resolves

When this distinction is in operation, the question "how do I trust myself?" becomes answerable in real time. The question often arrived in the abstract because the person was trying to develop a general policy for what to trust. The distinction is not a policy. It is a moment-by-moment somatic check. You do not need a general policy. You need to be able to feel your heart.

The distinction is also the resolution of [[how-do-i-trust-myself]]'s second layer — "how do I tell intuition from fear or projection?" The fear and projection are usually the conditioning. The intuition is the intuition. The heart tells you which is which.

## Boundary Conditions

The test depends on the person being in their body. A person who is dissociated, intellectualizing, or numbed cannot run the test reliably. The first work for them is to establish body access (see [[staying-in-your-body]] and [[attention-is-feeling]]).

The test also depends on the heart being available. A person whose heart has been closed as a default operating mode (chronic guardedness, post-trauma armoring) may not feel either opening or closure clearly because the baseline is closed. For them, the work is to thaw the baseline first; the discrimination comes later.

For people in active acute fear, the heart closes for protective reasons that are not "conditioning" in the negative-pattern sense. The test does not apply during acute danger. It applies during normal-life immediate responses.
