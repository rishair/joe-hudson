# Liking Your Kid vs. Loving Your Kid

## The Distinction

Love is a felt orientation toward someone — wanting their wellbeing, the bond, the commitment. Like is a felt enjoyment of who they actually are, transmitted in the day-to-day way you treat them. Most parents love their kids deeply. Far fewer kids feel that their parents like them. The gap is not in the love. The gap is in what gets transmitted as the daily field around the kid.

Esme names the distinction in a single phrase: "Cause love and like, you're such a different thing." Joe affirms it as a real measure of how a kid is being parented.

## The Question Joe Asks About Other Kids

"How many, how many kids actually feel like their parents like them? Like, I don't mean, yeah, like, no, that the kids feel like they're liked." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The question is asked sideways through the daughter — "of your friends, how many kids do you look at them and you're like, oh, they feel like their parents like them?" — and Esme's answer is "not many." She also says "I think most of them feel loved by their parents." The two are decoupled. Loved, yes. Liked, no.

## What "Liking" Communicates That "Loving" Does Not

Loving the kid says: I am committed to you, you matter to me, I want your wellbeing. It is a structural orientation.

Liking the kid says: I enjoy who you are right now. You as you actually are, today, in your specific weirdness and feistiness and interests, are a person I am glad is here. It is a specific, daily, felt transmission.

A parent who loves but does not communicate liking sends a different message than they think they are sending. The kid registers the love (rules, presence, sacrifices made) and also registers the constant correction or adjustment, which subtracts from the felt sense of being liked. The kid concludes: I am loved because that is what parents do, but I am not someone they would have picked as a friend if I were not their kid.

"Even if a parent does like their kid, what I notice is it's like, you shouldn't be angry. You shouldn't be this. You shouldn't be that. You should — like, you're too feisty. Why aren't you getting the sport? It's like, they're, they're not getting communicated like, hey, I like you. They're getting communicated like a lot of like, this is the way you should be, which means you're not naturally that way, which means like you need to be controlled, you need to be managed. We like, we don't trust you that you're going to like show up." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The structural message inside constant correction is: I do not like the way you naturally are. It does not matter that the parent loves the kid. The transmission is independent of the love.

## The Two Sides

**Loving (without communicating like):**
- Pursues the kid's wellbeing, including correction the parent thinks is needed
- Holds the parent's vision of who the kid should be
- Sacrifices for the kid
- Tells the kid the kid is loved
- Often performs the same set of behaviors as a parent who also likes the kid, but with a different underlying message
- Produces a kid who feels loved but managed — and who registers that some part of them is not okay

**Liking (which contains loving inside it):**
- Enjoys who the kid actually is right now
- Is curious about their world (see [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]])
- Treats the kid the way you would treat an adult you respect (see [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]])
- Does not project the wished-for version of the kid into how you treat the actual kid
- Transmits the message: you, as you are, are someone I am glad to spend time with
- Produces a kid who feels both loved and liked — who feels welcomed as themselves

## Why the Distinction Matters

A kid who feels loved-but-not-liked carries forward the structural belief that their actual self is not lovable. They may know intellectually that their parents love them. They may even feel loved by their parents. But the felt sense of "who I am is welcome here" is not installed.

This is the same dynamic that produces the cold teenage years many parents brace for. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]] for the full mechanism. The kid who has spent years feeling not-liked, while being told they are loved, eventually has the autonomy to leave and does. The leaving registers to the parent as inexplicable rejection. From the kid's side, it is self-protection.

## How "Like" Gets Communicated

The communication of liking is not a one-time declaration. It is a thousand small things that accumulate into a field.

- The parent's eyes when the kid walks in: lit up, or evaluative?
- The parent's questions: curious about the kid's actual world, or steering toward the parent's preferred topics?
- The parent's response to the kid's quirks: enjoyment, or low-grade attempts to adjust them?
- The parent's response to the kid bringing them something exciting: matching the kid's excitement, or assessing whether the kid should be excited about that?
- The parent's posture when sitting with the kid: present and at ease, or distracted and waiting for the interaction to conclude?
- The parent's tone when there is no agenda: warm and interested, or transactional?

The field these add up to is what the kid registers as "liked" or "not liked." No single moment matters. The accumulation matters.

## Why Parents Default to Loving-Without-Liking

The default is usually not a failure of love. It is two structural problems.

**Problem 1: The parent does not like themselves.** A parent who is in constant self-correction radiates the same energy onto the child. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]] — "Mostly because the parent is sending themselves the signal all the time that they need to be adjusted." The parent who likes themselves can like their kid. The parent who is hard on themselves transmits that hardness regardless of intent.

**Problem 2: The parent is operating from a vision of who the kid should be rather than meeting the kid who is here.** The parent has projected the kid they expected, or wanted, or were trained to want. The actual kid is being compared against that projection in real time. The kid feels the comparison even if it is never named. The "liking" of the actual kid cannot be communicated because the actual kid is not the one the parent is attending to.

The fix at the structural level: the parent has to actually meet the kid as the kid is. See [[meet-each-kid-where-they-are]]. The fix at the felt level: the parent has to do enough of their own work that they can drop the constant adjustment and just enjoy the kid's presence. See [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]].

## The Diagnostic Question

The cleanest diagnostic Joe applies: does your kid feel like you like them? Not whether they feel loved. Whether they feel liked. This is a different question and most parents have not asked it.

If the answer is no, or unclear, the work is not to declare more love. The work is to look at what the parent is actually communicating in the day-to-day, and to address the structural conditions (self-criticism, projected vision of the kid, ongoing adjustment) that are blocking the transmission of like.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent realizes they cannot recall when they last felt unmixed enjoyment of their kid's presence
- A parent registers that the kid does not seem comfortable being themselves around them
- A teenager has gone cold and the parent is bewildered because the parent loves them deeply
- An adult is working through their childhood and recognizes that they always felt loved but never liked
- A parent of a young child is trying to break a generational pattern of loved-not-liked
- A coachee describes a kid (or themselves at that age) who felt managed

## Tone

Joe asks the question with a slight pause — "that's sad" — when Esme answers "not many." The tone is not accusatory. It is observational. The distinction is offered as a useful question, not as judgment of parents who have not been asking it. Hold that tone in the article: this is a frame most parents have not been given, and naming it gives them new traction without shaming them for what they were not seeing.
