# Empathy vs. Sympathy

## Definition

The difference between empathy and sympathy is positioning. Sympathy puts you above the other person. Empathy puts you beside them.

Sympathy: "Oh, you poor thing. Let me help you." This requires the helper to be less broken than the helped. It assumes not feeling the emotion is the better state. It subtly says: I am above your situation, looking down.

Empathy: "I am with you in this." No hierarchy. No fixing. No implication that the emotion should be different. Just presence.

## The Two Sides

**Sympathy (above):**
- Wanting to fix them implies you need to be less broken.
- Wanting them not to feel it implies not feeling is better.
- Buying into their story: "Yeah, that guy is terrible." Now you are lost in the narrative, not present to the person.
- Often feels like being managed. The sympathizer has an agenda: make the pain go away.

**Empathy (beside):**
- Being with them, not saving them.
- Supporting without needing things to change.
- Not buying the story but feeling the person behind it.
- "If you're here to help me, no thank you, but if you're here to work together on our mutual freedom, let's get to work." -- Aboriginal saying, cited by Joe Hudson, Empathy

## Why The Distinction Matters

Sympathy triggers the very dynamic it tries to fix. If someone is in pain and you position yourself above them, the implicit message is: you should not be where you are. This adds shame to their pain. Empathy communicates: where you are is okay. I can be here with you. This is what allows the person to move through the emotion rather than performing recovery.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone's impulse to comfort is really an impulse to stop another's pain because it is uncomfortable to be near.
- When "helping" a friend or colleague consistently leaves them feeling smaller, not seen.
- When the helper feels drained after every emotional conversation.
