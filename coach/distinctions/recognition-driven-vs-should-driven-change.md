# Recognition-Driven vs Should-Driven Change

## The Distinction

Behavior change is driven by one of two engines, and the engine determines whether the change holds. The engines look similar from the outside and produce nearly identical opening statements. Underneath they are structurally opposite.

**Recognition-driven change:** "Oh, I see it. That's not how I want to be." The change wants to happen from inside the person. The pattern has been seen clearly enough that continuing it would feel absurd. The energy is release.

**Should-driven change:** "I shouldn't be like this. I need to change." The change is being imposed from a value or morality that hasn't been earned by direct experience. The pattern is judged as bad. The energy is force.

The same procedural action (announcing, apologizing, taking new actions) produces very different results depending on which engine is running. Recognition-driven change tends to hold and to feel like coming home. Should-driven change tends to revert under stress and to feel like a chore.

## Why The Distinction Matters

It determines whether the [[four-as-of-behavior-change|4 A's]] will work. Most other behavior-change frameworks too. The frameworks are tools for converting recognition into action. They don't manufacture recognition. Applied to a should, they produce compliance theater, not change.

"It works really well when you have a recognition... If it is something that you think, some way you think you should be, when you haven't had the recognition of, oh, that's not the way I want to be, you're still in the should of it, then it's much less likely to work, and it's going to be messy towards you and towards everybody else." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The distinction also reveals what to work on. If the client is in a should, the work isn't applying the framework — it's producing the recognition. The change can't happen until the seeing has happened.

## The Two Engines Side By Side

**Recognition:**
- Has a locatable moment of seeing.
- Has the *of course* quality — the body softens slightly when the truth lands.
- Is owned from inside ("I see it") rather than borrowed from outside ("they said I do this").
- Looks at the pattern with open-hearted curiosity, not self-rejection.
- Carries its own forward motion. The next behavior feels obvious, not required.
- Often arrives with relief or grief rather than determination.

**Should:**
- Has no specific recognition moment. Or the "recognition" is actually someone else's diagnosis adopted as self-judgment.
- Has the *I'm bad* quality — the body braces when the change is named.
- Is borrowed from outside (a teacher, a partner, a cultural value, a moral framework).
- Looks at the pattern with judgment and self-attack.
- Carries no forward motion of its own — requires willpower, accountability, enforcement.
- Often arrives with determination, performance, or grim resolve.

Joe's framing in the contrast example:
- Should: "I should be more committed. I shouldn't be commitment-phobic." That's adopting a value, not seeing a pattern.
- Recognition: "Oh, I'm not committing to stuff because I'm scared of getting rejected. Oh, and I'm scared of getting rejected because I've been rejecting myself." That's the actual seeing — and only at that point does the change have an engine that produces movement.

"That's not a realization, that's a should. There's not, oh, oh wow, I'm not committing to stuff because I'm scared of getting rejected. Oh, and I'm scared of getting rejected because I've been rejecting myself. Okay. That's the moment to do it." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## Why Recognition Holds and Should Doesn't

Recognition holds because it's working with the system, not against it. The seeing has already produced the *not wanting to do this anymore*. The change is just the natural expression of what was seen. There's no internal opposition because the part of the person that wanted the old pattern has updated.

Should doesn't hold because it's working against the system. The part of the person that wanted the old pattern still wants it — it just hasn't been seen. The change is being enforced over it rather than emerging from its update. Under stress, the unseen part reasserts itself and the change reverts.

This is why "knowing better" doesn't change behavior. Knowing better is a should. The behavior keeps happening because the system hasn't updated; only the conscious mind has acquired a new opinion. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] for the related mechanism: knowing-better that converts into shame entrenches the behavior rather than dissolving it.

## The Diagnostic Question

Before any behavior-change work: "did you have a recognition, or did you decide you should?"

If recognition: proceed with the framework. The recognition will carry the work.

If should: the work isn't the framework. The work is producing the recognition. That can involve [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] underneath the pattern, inquiry that gets to the *of course* moment, or simply sitting with the pattern open-heartedly until it's actually seen rather than judged. See [[recognition-feeling-balance-then-call-emerges]] for the order of operations.

The honest answer to this question is the most important pre-flight check for any behavior-change protocol. The framework can't manufacture what it requires as input.

## The Body Test

A felt-sense test that complements the cognitive question: when you name the change you want to make, does your body soften slightly (recognition) or brace slightly (should)?

The softening is the felt signature that you're moving with what you've seen. The bracing is the felt signature that you're forcing something the system doesn't yet agree with.

The test isn't infallible — some recognitions arrive with grief, which can feel like bracing — but in combination with the question above it usually clarifies which engine is operating.

## When Recognition Hasn't Arrived Yet

When a client is in a should, the move isn't to push them into the framework anyway. The move is to do the work that produces recognition. This usually involves:

1. Feeling the pattern from inside. Not from the distance of judgment but from the felt sense of what the pattern is doing.
2. Inquiring into what the pattern is protecting. The pattern is always avoiding an emotional experience.
3. Letting the *of course* arrive. When the protection is seen, the pattern often looks self-evidently unnecessary.

The recognition is downstream of this work. It can't be willed. It can be supported by creating the conditions in which seeing becomes possible.

## Why This Matters for Coaching

Coaches who don't make this distinction often impose behavior change on clients who haven't had the recognition yet. The result is a client who tries the framework, fails, and concludes "I can't change" — when really the framework was being applied outside its operating range.

The skill is in knowing when to deploy the framework and when to do the upstream work first. The diagnostic question and the body test give the coach the information.
