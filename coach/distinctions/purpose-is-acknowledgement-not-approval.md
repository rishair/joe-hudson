# Purpose Is Acknowledgement, Not Approval

## The Distinction

**Approval** is an external thing. It comes from other people. It is given by them, can be withheld by them, requires them to deliver it. Approval-seeking moves the seeker outward — into the other person, into the audience, into the public reception.

**Acknowledgement** of purpose is an internal thing. It happens inside the person. The thing being acknowledged is already true; the acknowledging is admitting that it is true. Acknowledgement does not require anyone else to deliver anything. It only requires the person to stop pretending the truth is not the truth.

These two movements look almost identical on the surface. Both involve recognition. Both feel like the thing the person is reaching for. They go opposite directions. Approval moves outward; acknowledgement moves inward. Approval depletes; acknowledgement settles. Approval cannot resolve; acknowledgement resolves immediately.

The confusion of the two is one of the most common structural errors in the purpose-question.

"Oftentimes people confuse this, 'Oh, I have a sense of purpose,' but they're really asking is what can I be acknowledged for?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Find Your Purpose

Brett caught it: "Where's the approval gonna come from?"

Joe confirmed: "Where's the approval gonna come from? Which is interesting because it's just the opposite. They're looking to be told, what it is on some level. And the interesting point there is if the purpose isn't something that has to be found, it's something that has to be acknowledged."

## How the Confusion Works

The person asking "what is my purpose?" often means, without knowing they mean it, "where can I get recognized?" The two questions feel similar because both are about finding something that will make the person feel okay. They are structurally opposite.

- "What is my purpose?" framed as approval-seeking: "Where in the world will I be acknowledged for what I do? What activity will produce the most praise? What role will let me feel important?"
- "What is my purpose?" framed as acknowledgement: "What is already true about who I am that I have not let myself admit? What is the shape my nature has that I have been avoiding seeing?"

The first version sends the person looking for external sources of validation. They scan jobs, projects, public reception. They evaluate options by the size of the audience or the prestige of the position. They cannot find an answer because no answer satisfies — the seeking itself is the wrong shape for what they actually need.

The second version turns the person inward. They feel what is already operating. They notice what is being avoided. They acknowledge what is already true. The answer arrives because the question is well-formed.

## The Diagnostic

You can diagnose which frame someone is in by listening for what they say when asked "what would change if you had it?"

- **Approval-frame:** "I would finally be respected. People would take me seriously. I'd know I was doing something important. I'd feel valuable."
- **Acknowledgement-frame:** "I would be able to stop hiding from what I already know. I would do the thing I've been avoiding. I would let myself be in alignment with what I actually am."

The first set of answers is all external — about other people's responses. The second set is all internal — about the person's relationship to their own truth.

Another diagnostic: who is the audience in the imagined success?

- **Approval-frame:** the audience is other people. The success is being seen, recognized, validated by them.
- **Acknowledgement-frame:** there is no audience. The success is the doing of the thing itself, regardless of whether anyone sees.

When the audience moves from external to absent, the frame has shifted from approval to acknowledgement.

## Why Approval Cannot Resolve the Purpose Question

The approval-frame sends the person looking for something that, even if found, will not resolve the question. Approval is impermanent. It has to be re-earned constantly. The next moment requires the next dose. The person is never settled.

Even when the approval is delivered, it does not produce the experience of being in one's purpose. It produces a brief hit of validation, followed by the return of the original question. "I got recognized for this — am I now in my purpose?" The answer is always uncertain because purpose was never about the recognition.

This is why people in high-approval roles often report that they are not in their purpose. They have all the external validation they could want. They still cannot feel the thing they were looking for. The thing was never out there. See [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]] for the related pattern.

## Why Acknowledgement Resolves Immediately

The acknowledgement-frame works because the work it asks for is achievable. The person can, in any moment, choose to stop denying what is already true. The denial was a posture; dropping the posture is something the person has control over.

Once the acknowledgement happens, the question stops re-presenting itself. The person has admitted the truth. The truth does not need to be acknowledged again every five minutes; once it is acknowledged, it is acknowledged.

What comes after the acknowledgement is action — the work of moving toward what has been acknowledged. The action can be scary. See [[purpose-asks-you-to-be-scary-then-effortless]]. But the question of what to acknowledge has been settled.

## The Internal vs External Direction

The deeper structure of the distinction is about direction:

- Approval has the person looking outward, asking others for something.
- Acknowledgement has the person looking inward, admitting something to themselves.

The directions are opposite. Living in the approval-frame depletes the person — they are continuously leaking attention and energy outward, seeking the reception that will not arrive. Living in the acknowledgement-frame settles the person — they are at home with what they actually are, no longer fighting it.

"Rather than an outside thing, it's an inside thing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Find Your Purpose

This is also the distinction between purpose-as-trap and purpose-as-freedom. Purpose pursued through approval is a trap; it cannot resolve. Purpose received through acknowledgement is freedom; it resolves the moment the acknowledgement happens.

## The Specific Confusion: "Recognition" Has Two Meanings

The word "recognition" itself carries the confusion. It can mean:

- **External recognition** (being acknowledged by others) — this is approval-shaped.
- **Internal recognition** (acknowledging what is already true within oneself) — this is acknowledgement-shaped.

Both meanings of the word are in play when someone says "I want recognition for my work." Often the person does not know which one they mean. The work of clarifying which is which is part of the coaching.

When someone says "I want to be recognized for this," the question is: "Recognized by whom?" If the answer is "by other people," the work is around approval. If the answer is "by myself," the work is around acknowledgement. The two pieces of work are different, and confusing them produces dysfunction.

## The Books-You're-Reading Clue

Joe offered a specific operational move for surfacing the acknowledgement:

"You see some purpose-driven books about how to find your purpose, and they'll say something like, notice all the books that you're reading. What is it about? That's gonna tell you your purpose. And it's less of a finding and it's more of an acknowledgement." -- Joe Hudson, How To Find Your Purpose

The books-you-read move works because it surfaces what is already true. The person is already drawn to certain topics. The drawing is data about the shape of their nature. The acknowledgement is to stop pretending the drawing is random and to admit it points at something.

The move is specifically acknowledgement-shaped because it asks the person to look inward at their actual existing patterns, not outward at potential opportunities for approval. The data was always there; the move surfaces it.

This generalizes. The activities the person keeps returning to, the conversations they keep wanting to have, the questions that keep showing up in their attention — all of these are clues. The acknowledgement is to stop dismissing the clues and to admit they form a pattern.

## What This Means for the AI Coach

When a person presents the purpose question, the first move is to diagnose which frame they are in.

If the person is in the approval-frame:
- They will describe purpose in terms of external recognition, status, importance.
- They will evaluate options by audience size or prestige.
- They will not have a felt-sense of what they are drawn to internally — only of what they want to be seen for.
- The coaching move is to first surface the approval-frame as the wrong frame. See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]] and [[chase-your-own-approval]]. Until the frame shifts, the purpose question cannot be answered.

If the person is in the acknowledgement-frame:
- They will already be reporting some felt-sense of what they are drawn to.
- They will be uncertain about the form, not about the through-line.
- The coaching move is to support the acknowledgement — name what is already operating, ask what is being avoided, point at the books-they-read or the conversations they keep wanting to have.

Many people start in the approval-frame without knowing it. The shift to the acknowledgement-frame is part of the work. The shift cannot be skipped. The purpose question asked from the approval-frame produces no useful answer; the purpose question asked from the acknowledgement-frame answers itself.

## Tone

Joe delivers this distinction with a note of mild surprise, as if naming something that should be obvious but is consistently missed. The "Where's the approval gonna come from? Which is interesting because it's just the opposite" carries a small amused recognition — the structure of the confusion is almost neat enough to be funny. The tone is not condescending; the confusion is one Joe himself has been in. The teaching is delivered from inside, not from above.

When working with someone in the approval-frame, the tone is gentler. Naming the approval-seeking can land as criticism if delivered without care. The frame is what is being addressed, not the person. The person is doing the best they can with the frame they have. The work is to make a different frame available.
