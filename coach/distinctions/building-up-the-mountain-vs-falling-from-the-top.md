# Building Up the Mountain vs. Falling From the Top

## The Distinction

There are two shapes long relationships can take, and the shape that produces sustainability is opposite to the shape that the culture celebrates.

**Falling from the top.** Two people meet, fall in love, experience the relationship as immediately right. The match feels perfect. There is little friction. The early period has no work in it because no work seems required. The relationship starts at the top of the mountain — the peak of intensity, fit, ease. From the top, the only direction available is down.

**Building up the mountain.** Two people meet, do not start as a perfect fit, encounter friction early, and build the relationship by working together to climb. The strength is in the climbing. The structure is in the building. The intimacy is the byproduct of the work, not the starting condition. The trajectory is upward over time.

The two shapes look opposite from the outside. The falling-from-the-top relationship looks ideal and the building-up-the-mountain relationship looks difficult, especially in the first years. The actual trajectories run in opposite directions. The ideal-looking relationship is usually losing altitude. The difficult-looking relationship is usually gaining it.

## Sobonfu Somé's Source

The distinction comes from Sobonfu Somé, the Dagara teacher Tara cites:

"There's a teacher, beloved teacher, a teacher I loved, Sobonfu Somé, who came from a part of Africa where they had arranged marriages. And not that I'm encouraging arranged marriages, but she talked about how when you fall in love with your perfect partner, you stop at the top, you start at the top of the mountain and the only place to go is down, like falling down a mountain. Whereas with an arranged marriage, with putting together with someone, you have to build and muscle strength and strengthen the marriage to get up the mountain. So it's a building, building, building. And creating structure to get to the top of the mountain. And so I think that marriage is about building and creating structures and attachments." -- Tara Howley, Can Marriage Make You a Better Person?

The arranged-marriage context is illustrative, not prescriptive. The point is not that arranged marriages are better. The point is that arranged marriages start in the position the building-up-the-mountain relationship starts in regardless of whether arrangement was involved: with the recognition that work is required, that the fit is not yet there, that the structure must be built.

## The Romantic-Love Trap

The "starting at the top" position is the default outcome of the romantic-love frame. The early oxytocin period (see [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]]) produces the felt sense that the relationship is already at peak. The peak is what the couple is trying to preserve. Any decline from the peak gets read as the relationship failing.

The trap: the peak was the starting condition, not the achievement. The relationship has not yet been built. The structure that holds long relationships does not exist yet. The early couple is in love with the peak experience, not with each other in the structural sense. When the peak fades — which it always does — there is nothing underneath it that has been built.

This is the structural reason why Joe observes that the "great fit, no issues" relationships often look the best in years one through five and the worst in year twenty-five:

"I notice a dynamic that typically people who are like, it's just a great fit and here we are and we don't have a lot of issues, usually have a pretty, in 25 years, most of them seem to have a pretty dead marriage or one of them looks up one day and says, you know, you're Snickers and I've just decided I like Mars. So like, I see a lot of, a lot of relationships end that way that seemed like, oh, look, we have a great relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Can Marriage Make You a Better Person?

The decline from the top is gradual, often invisible to the couple, and then suddenly visible as "we have nothing left" or "we are not the same people anymore." The structure that would have made the long marriage work was never built because the early conditions did not require building it.

## The Building-Up Pattern

The building-up-the-mountain pattern looks like the opposite of what the culture celebrates. Three years of hell at the beginning. Friends telling them to break up. Reenacting parents' trauma. Toxic communication. Pitchfork fights.

The structural feature underneath the surface mess: every fight, every conflict, every difficult passage is producing the construction of the structure that did not exist at the start. Every reach for a tool — therapy, retreats, books, couples coaching — is mortar between bricks. Every choice to stay through a hard period is one more stone added to the foundation.

The pattern across a long building-up relationship is not "we have less conflict now." It is "the structure that holds our relationship is strong enough that conflict no longer threatens it." The conflict often does not disappear. What changes is that the structure can hold conflict without breaking.

This is the structural reading of Joe and Tara's "many marriages together" — each conflict-period built the next floor of the structure. Each "death" of the previous configuration was the foundation for the next configuration that the structure could now hold. See [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] for the move that operates inside this pattern.

## What This Reframes

The distinction reframes several things people commonly assume about relationships:

- **"We don't have any problems" is not a good sign in a long relationship.** It usually means the relationship is still living on the early peak and the structure has not been required yet. The first real test will be the test the relationship has not prepared for.
- **"We fight constantly" is not necessarily a bad sign.** If the fighting is productive (see [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]]) it is the medium of construction. The amount of fighting is a poor predictor of the relationship's future. The quality of what the fighting produces is the predictor.
- **The early "this is so easy" is not the destination.** It is the absence of the work that the destination requires. Easy at the start is structurally consistent with empty at year twenty.
- **The friends-saying-divorce-them assessment is operating on the wrong metric.** The friends are reading the surface (the conflict, the toxicity) and missing the structural variable (whether the couple is using the difficulty as material). Outside observers can read the surface and miss the structure. See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] for the way this misread shaped early reactions to Joe and Tara.

## What This Does Not Mean

This is not the claim that all good relationships should start with difficulty. Some couples meet, click, and do the work together from the start without an extreme early period. The distinction is structural, not about how rough the early period is. The structural variable is whether construction is happening, not whether the early period is hard.

This is also not a romanticization of suffering in relationships. Couples in abusive or chronically harmful patterns are not "building up the mountain." See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the boundary. The distinction applies to relationships where both partners are doing their own work (see [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]]); without that, the difficulty is just damage.

This is also not a prescription for arranged marriage. Sobonfu's frame is illustrative of a structural pattern. The pattern is available to people in any kind of relationship — the question is whether the construction work is happening, not how the relationship started.

## The Diagnostic Question for Coaching

When a person is presenting their relationship for assessment, the diagnostic question is not "is this hard?" or "is this easy?" but "is structure being built?"

Structure-being-built shows up as:
- Repeated conflict in which something gets learned and integrated
- Both partners doing their own work (see [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]])
- Tools being acquired and used (therapy, frameworks, agreements)
- Each conflict cycle producing the next layer of intimacy or capacity
- The partners noticing they are different people than they were three years ago

Structure-not-being-built shows up as:
- Avoidance of the same conflicts repeatedly
- Either no fights or the same fight with no movement
- The early-peak frame still operating as the standard ("we used to be so good")
- The partners are essentially the same people they were three years ago

The first pattern is building up the mountain. The second pattern is either still at the top falling slowly, or never had the peak and is plateaued. Both produce predictable outcomes in long relationships.

## Why This Matters Operationally

For a coach working with someone in the early-to-mid years of a relationship, the distinction does several things:

- **It reframes the assessment.** A person presenting "we're having a really hard time" is not necessarily presenting a failing relationship. The question is what the difficulty is building.
- **It dissolves the friends' judgment as the operating metric.** "All my friends say I should leave" is information about how the surface reads, not about whether the structure is being built.
- **It points the work at construction.** The move is not "find a relationship without difficulty." The move is "use the difficulty to build the structure."
- **It catches the dead-fit relationship before it looks like one.** The 25-year-dead-fit marriage was diagnosable at year three by the absence of construction. The coaching can name this before the slow decline produces its outcome.
