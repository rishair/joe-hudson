# Principle vs. Value

## The Distinction

A **value** describes what you stand for in the abstract. It rarely produces a specific action because it covers behavior you would already do (or already not do). "We value honesty" does not change a decision because dishonesty is not a live option anyway.

A **principle** is designed for choice. It tells you what to do in the moment something is at stake. Principles are evaluated by whether they actually change what you do when you act.

"The difference is, however, that principles for me is how do you make a decision by it... A principle is all designed on choice. It's all designed on how do you make choices automatically because you live by a set of principles. And so they should very much be designed to create action and to clarify decision-making so you don't have to stop and think about big decisions." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

## How Values Became Generic

The corporate values movement started as something closer to principles. It was Covey, the Franklin Planner author, and then every company in the 1990s and 2000s ran the same exercise. Two problems crept in.

**Generic abstractions.** "We value honesty. We value integrity. We value excellence." These are nearly universal, so they do not differentiate behavior. Everyone in business already claims to value honesty. The values exercise produces a poster, not a decision-changer.

**Top-down imposition.** Values were generated by a small group, then announced to the company. They were not lived through, not iterated on, not tested. They became wall decorations.

The original Covey conception, when applied at the family level (mission statements and values in a Mormon home, for example), was closer to principles in spirit. Somewhere on the way to corporate adoption, the action-orientation was lost.

## The Test of a Principle

If a principle does not change what you do under pressure, it is a value or a preference, not a principle. The test is operational:

- Does this guide a choice when two options are available?
- Does following this require you to do something you would not otherwise do?
- When the stakes are real, does this still hold?

"My value, I value honesty, for instance. So unless I am choosing to be dishonest, which is not a choice that I would make almost ever, then there's no choice that I'm going to make based on that value. Whereas a principle is all designed on choice." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

## Morality vs. Action

Values tend toward morality: what not to do. Principles tend toward action: what to do.

"It's kind of more of a morality. It's kind of like the value situation seems to be more of a morality, and it's about what not to do. And principles are more about what you do." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

A value tells you what kind of person you are claiming to be. A principle tells you how to act when you do not know what to do.

## Examples of Each

**Value:** "We value transparency."
**Principle:** "Transparent markets are efficient markets, so we videotape all our meetings and any employee can watch any tape with their name in it." See [[ray-dalio-transparency]].

**Value:** "I value connection."
**Principle:** "Connection, connection, connection. When in doubt, I connect. Before deciding, I connect. After difficulty, I connect." See [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]].

**Value:** "I value growth."
**Principle:** "I embrace intensity. When I do not want to look at something, I look at it. When I want to avoid a conversation, I have it." See [[embrace-intensity]].

The pattern: the value names the territory. The principle states the move.

## The Practical Consequence

Companies and individuals routinely do values exercises and report no change in behavior six months later. The exercise generated agreement, not action.

The same time spent on principles produces something different: a set of moves that get used in real decisions. The wall poster is replaced by an internal compass. See [[principles-free-you]] for what living by principles produces.

## When This Distinction Matters in Coaching

A client who says "I value family" is usually saying "I am the kind of person who would say I value family." This is not a coaching error -- it just does not yet contain a decision. The coaching move is to convert the value into a principle: "When the work demand and the family demand both arrive at 6pm, what is the principle that decides?"

The conversion is uncomfortable because principles, unlike values, require trade-offs to be explicit. The value can stay vague. The principle has to do work.
