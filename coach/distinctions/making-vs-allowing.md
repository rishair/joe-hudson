# Making vs. Allowing (First Half vs. Second Half of Life)

## The Distinction

There are two fundamentally different orientations toward how a person engages with their life. The first orients around making things happen: striving, becoming, accumulating, achieving, building, getting, having. The second orients around allowing what is already there to come forward: removing what is in the way, surrendering to the pull, letting the existing capacities reveal themselves.

These often correspond to two life phases, though the correspondence is not strict. The "first half of life" typically requires making. The ego, the career, the family, the body of work -- all are built. The "second half of life" often reorients toward allowing. The structures are largely in place. The work becomes one of letting what was already inside come forward.

Aaron Taylor's framing, naming the shift from the perspective of mid-life:

"The first part of my life was about being and doing and having and becoming and getting and amassing, and is around second base, or approach the tee box if you will, on the back nine, my life is really becoming about what it is I can allow. Like, and it -- that assumption is that everything I need for these last 9 holes is already there. The key is for me to get out of the way. I don't have to make it, I just have to allow it, to get out of the way and allow what's already there to come up." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

## What "Making" Looks Like

The making orientation operates from the assumption that something needs to be added. The person needs to become more, achieve more, fix more, build more. The internal stance is forward-leaning, gripping, effortful. The metric is whether something new appeared as a result of the effort.

This is not bad. The making orientation builds careers, raises families, develops capacities, creates structures. Most of the genuine accomplishment in a life happens through making in this sense. The ego that does the making is the same ego that allows the person to function in the world.

The trouble is when making is the only mode. The person who has built the career, raised the family, accomplished the goals continues running the making engine. There is nothing left to build. The engine starts producing friction instead of momentum. The friction is the signal that the orientation needs to shift.

## What "Allowing" Looks Like

The allowing orientation operates from the assumption that what is needed is already present. The work is not to add but to remove what is in the way. The internal stance is more receptive, more spacious, less effortful in the gripping sense. The metric is not whether something new appeared but whether what was already there became more available.

This is not passivity. Aaron is explicit that this orientation does not mean losing ambition. Joe checks this directly:

- **Joe:** Is that not true for you though? Because I don't see an unambitious, undriven man.
- **Aaron:** No.
- **Joe:** When I heard your approach on the back nine, I didn't hear that you lost your ambition.
- **Aaron:** It's diff -- it's, you know, you got to let go to take control. You got to, you know, hit your knees to finally stand up. It's like this oxymoronical piece that we're talking about.

The ambition is still there. What has changed is the relationship to it. The ambition is no longer being driven from the engine that grips. It is being followed from the engine that allows.

## Why the Shift Matters

The first half / second half framing is widespread (Richard Rohr's "Falling Upward" is one source; the framing predates that book by many traditions). The reason the framing recurs is structural: the making engine has a natural lifespan within a given identity-structure. Once the identity has been built, continued making produces diminishing returns and rising friction. The system needs a different operating mode for the next phase.

The trouble is that the making engine often does not know how to stop. The person who built their identity around achievement keeps trying to achieve the next thing -- including achieving "the second half of life." This produces a perverse loop where the person is trying to *make themselves allow*. The making engine has eaten the allowing project.

The escape from this loop is not more making. It is letting the making engine quiet by itself, which often happens through some combination of exhaustion, loss, awakening, or grace. The person discovers that the things that mattered most -- the felt qualities of their life, the depth of relationship, the sense of being themselves -- never came from making in the first place. They came from the moments when the making stopped.

## The Aaron-Joe Exchange

The exchange in the transcript captures something specific about how this distinction operates in practice. Joe's read on Aaron's framing of his children is sharp:

- **Joe:** My guess is if I was to meet your sons they're already far more towards the way you're looking at life on the back nine than you were even like 6, 7 years ago. [...] To some degree, your kids are already allowing it to come to them. They're doing the work, but their attitude, their perspective isn't like, I have to prove myself as much as it is, Oh yeah, I'm letting this come to me.

This move is doing two things at once. It tests whether the children's "drivenness" that Aaron is worried about is actually the same drivenness Aaron had at their age, or whether it is already a more allowing version. And it surfaces the possibility that Aaron's project of teaching his children the back-nine wisdom may be misplaced -- they may already have it.

## The Felt Sense

The distinction can be felt in the body, not just understood conceptually. Making, when it is the dominant mode, has a forward-leaning quality. The shoulders, the jaw, the breath all carry the effort. The body is in motion toward something not-yet-here.

Allowing has a different quality. The body is more upright, more settled. There is space around the next action. The breath is deeper. The effort that remains is the effort of the actual task, not the effort of gripping the outcome.

Aaron names this as "you got to let go to take control. You got to, you know, hit your knees to finally stand up." The paradox is real. The control that comes from allowing is more reliable than the control that comes from making, because it does not require continuous gripping to maintain.

## When This Distinction Is Useful

- A coachee has hit a wall in mid-life. The strategies that built the career are no longer producing satisfaction.
- A coachee is exhausted from continuous striving and is asking what they are supposed to do next.
- A coachee has accomplished what they set out to accomplish and reports feeling flat.
- A coachee is in a transformation process and is trying to *make themselves transform*. The distinction surfaces the loop.
- A coachee is parenting and worrying that their children lack the drive they had. The distinction questions whether the drive is what is needed.

The distinction does not fit when the coachee genuinely is in a build phase. A 27-year-old at the start of a career does not need to be invited into allowing as the primary mode. The making engine is what the life-stage actually requires. The distinction is most useful at the transition points and at the recognition that the engine has reached its end.

## Boundary Conditions

The allowing orientation is not the same as drift, passivity, or giving up. The person in allowing mode is often more productive than the person in making mode -- they are simply producing through a different mechanism. The work is happening through them rather than being driven by them.

The distinction is also not a moral hierarchy. Making is not less spiritual than allowing. The early-career drive that builds the foundation is part of the structure that later allowing rests on. A life is not better for being mostly allowing. A life is better for using the orientation that fits the moment.
