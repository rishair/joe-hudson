# Transparent Identity vs. Rigid Identity

## The Two Sides

**Rigid identity:** The identity is taken personally. Defended. Filtered for. Any challenge to it produces constriction, defense, fear. The person is "in" the identity rather than holding it. There is no flexibility around it. Setting it down feels impossible or like death.

**Transparent identity:** The identity is held lightly. The person can see through it to what is actually here. The identity is still operating -- there is still "someone" speaking, still self-recognition -- but it is not gripping. The person can set it down when it stops serving and pick it back up when it does. The same identity becomes more fluid, more gentle, more universal, more resilient.

"As you learn to love all the aspects of yourself, the identity becomes far more fluid. It becomes far more transparent. It starts to identify with oneness or nothingness or love or peace or inherent goodness. The identification becomes softer, it becomes more universal, it becomes more gentle, it becomes less rigid, it becomes more fluid." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## Why the Distinction Matters

The common misunderstanding: the goal of identity work is to have no identity. This is impossible (see [[identity]] and [[dont-fight-identity]]). The goal is not absence but transparency. The same identity can become a cage or a comfortable structure depending on how it is held.

This is the structural reason why two people with similar self-descriptions can have radically different lives. "I am a CEO" held rigidly produces a person who cannot make decisions that threaten the title, cannot leave when leaving is right, cannot fail in front of the team. "I am a CEO" held transparently produces a person who does the job well and can also drop the role when the moment requires.

The content of the identity is largely the same. The grip is different.

## The Word "Tenderize"

Joe uses a specific word for the process: tenderize. The same identity becomes tender, soft, pliable. Not destroyed. Not erased. Tenderized.

"The practice of VIEW is not just a communication tool. It's also -- in the long term, it tenderizes our identity. It allows our identity to be more transparent." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

Brett: "I like that term, tenderizes."

The word matters because it captures what does not happen in identity work. The identity does not get killed, replaced, transcended. It gets tender. The texture changes. It stops being a wall and starts being a membrane.

## The Diagnostic for Rigidity

Several signals indicate an identity is being held rigidly:

- Defensiveness when the identity is touched
- Constriction in the body around topics related to the identity
- Fear when asked "who would you be without this?"
- Filtering of reality to confirm the identity (see [[every-identity-has-limits]])
- Inability to see evidence against the identity (positive or negative)
- A felt sense of "this is me" that cannot be questioned

"How much, what kind of constriction, what kind of defense, what kind of 'oh my gosh, what would I be without that' creates fear. There's lots of physical responses that tell people it's [identity]." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## The Diagnostic for Transparency

Conversely, the signals of a transparent identity:

- The person can be in VIEW around the topic -- vulnerable, impartial, empathetic, in wonder
- The identity can be discussed without the body constricting
- Feedback that challenges the identity can be received without immediate defense
- The person can imagine being different and not feel threatened
- The identity is held with something close to humor or curiosity

"If you're in VIEW around a topic about yourself, there's less of a chance that you're in identity. If it's easy for you to be vulnerable around it, if you're impartial about it, if you have empathy for others around it, if you are in wonder about it, it's far less likely to be a strong part of your identity." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

See [[view-around-self-as-identity-diagnostic]] for the practice that uses this as a diagnostic.

## What Transparency Allows

A transparent identity does not prevent functioning. It enables it more fully. The CEO who holds the role transparently can:

- Kill projects that should be killed (no hoarder identity to defend)
- Be vulnerable with the team (no "if I'm vulnerable I'll be hurt" identity to maintain)
- Fail in front of others (no "I must appear competent" identity to protect)
- Leave the role when it stops serving (no "I am a CEO" to grip)

The same applies in any domain. The transparent parent can let the child surprise them. The transparent partner can let the relationship change shape. The transparent teacher can not know an answer.

"It's to be able to see all the aspects of yourself, all the aspects of the diamond, so to speak. It allows for far more light to shine through. It allows for the beauty to be there, and it allows for a far more, both sustainable and flexible, resilient kind of sense of self." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## The Direction of Tenderization

As identity tenderizes over a lifetime, the identification tends to move toward progressively more universal categories.

- Early: identification with specific roles and stories ("I am a programmer," "I am the responsible one")
- Middle: identification with broader categories ("I am loving," "I am a parent")
- Later: identification with universal categories ("I am awareness," "I am love," "I am peace")
- Latest: even the universal identifications tenderize. "I am everything and I am nothing" can be held without defense.

This is not a hierarchy in the sense that earlier identifications are bad. The same person can be a programmer and love and awareness without contradiction once the identifications are held lightly. The tenderization is what allows the layering rather than the substitution.

See [[expanding-sense-of-self]] for the developmental trajectory.

## The Ocean and the Rock

A specific image for the two sides of the distinction. The rigid identity is a rock: definite shape, defended edges, takes damage from what hits it. The transparent identity is an ocean: no fixed shape, no edges to defend, absorbs what hits it without harm.

"That sense of self becomes, instead of like this very hard rock, it moves from this very hard rock into the sense of an ocean. And the sword attacks the ocean and the ocean doesn't care. You get to do whatever you want to do. It's this really amazing place to hang out. And if you're limiting in your belief, if you say, no, I am dumb, I am smart, I am successful, then all of a sudden you can't do everything that an ocean can do. You're just more of a rock." -- Joe Hudson, How To Break Free From Beliefs That Keep You Small

The image is precise about the operational difference. The rock-self can be cut. The ocean-self cannot be cut because there are no edges to cut. A sword swung at the rock either chips it or breaks against it; a sword swung at the ocean passes through and leaves the ocean unchanged. This is not a metaphor for invulnerability through hardening. It is the opposite: the apparent invulnerability comes from the absence of the hardness that the sword was looking for.

The pairing with "you can't do everything that an ocean can do" if you stay as a rock is also operationally specific. The rigid identity restricts the range of action available because every action has to be consistent with the defended self-definitions. The transparent identity has access to the full range because there are no definitions that the action might contradict.

## The Both-True Demonstration

What this looks like in a conversation: external assessments, including opposite ones, can be received and held as both information without either having to be defended or denied.

"Somebody can come up to me and say, hey Joe, you're an asshole. And I can, yeah, absolutely, I guarantee it. ... And somebody can say, hey Joe, like, I see how much you love and care and that you put your whole life into being a benefit to people. And I go, yeah, that's totally true too. And all of a sudden there's nothing that's really going to trigger me, no judgment that is going to be thrown at me that I'm going to defend. There's nothing to defend anymore." -- Joe Hudson, How To Break Free From Beliefs That Keep You Small

The structural feature: both assessments are received as true. Not "the truth is somewhere in the middle." Both fully true. The system that can hold both does not require either to be the totality of self, so there is no defense required. The non-defensive posture is not a discipline; it is what happens when both can be held.

This is the live demonstration of the transparent-identity side of the distinction. The rigid version of the same self would have had to argue with the asshole assessment or downplay the loving one (or vice versa). The transparent version lets both stand. See [[holding-contrary-beliefs-as-development]] for the cognitive capacity that makes the both-true posture possible.

## The Coaching Goal

The goal of identity work is not to deliver a new, better identity to the coachee. It is to invite the coachee into a more transparent relationship with whatever identity they are currently holding.

The question is not "who should I be?" The question is "can I hold who I am loosely enough that life can move through me?"

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a coachee is gripping an identity they suspect is causing problems
- When a coachee has tried to replace an identity and found the replacement is also rigid
- When the goal of "no identity" or "no ego" has produced frustration or self-attack
- When the coachee is ready to consider that flexibility, not content, is the work
