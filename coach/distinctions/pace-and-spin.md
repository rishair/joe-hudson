# Pace and Spin

## The Distinction

Pace and spin are two separate productivity diagnostics. Either one alone misses what is happening in the work. Both together give the full read.

**Pace** is the question of sustainability over time. Are you running this work at a tempo you can come back to next year, or are you sprinting toward collapse? Pace is the marathon question. The business is a marathon. The right pace is the one you can come back to tomorrow, next month, and the next year without burning out, without losing the want to do it, without arriving at the finish line so depleted that the win is unenjoyable.

**Spin** is the question of effort-to-output ratio in the present. Of the effort you are putting in, how much is actually moving the thing forward? How much is rowing against the current? How much is pedaling a bike that is barely moving? Spin is the diagnostic for whether the activity is productive or just feels productive.

A person can have a perfect spin and a wrong pace (everything they do moves the ball forward, but they are sprinting and will burn out in six months). A person can have a perfect pace and bad spin (they are sustainable, but most of their effort is not actually producing anything). The diagnostic value comes from checking both.

## The Origin in Joe's Team Practice

The distinction is one Joe uses as a team check-in.

"In our team meetings, when we're doing team meetings, one of the things that we do is check in on what's the pace and what's the spin. Because I noticed that it's very hard to know if our productivity is useful or if it's just a dopamine fix, but it's very easy to know if you feel like you're spinning, which means you're putting a lot of effort into something and you don't see the thing you want moving forward." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The reason the check is needed: real productivity is hard to see. Dopamine-driven activity feels productive, looks productive, and is not. See [[productivity-as-dopamine-fix]]. The team check-in catches what individual self-assessment misses, because the person in the dopamine loop is the least able to see they are in it.

## Why Pace Matters

"If you're running a marathon, which a business is, you want to make sure your pace is right. Are you doing it at a sustainable pace that's enjoyable so that you want to come back to work, so that you can feel fulfilled, so that you want to come back the next year, not going to burn out? If you really care about what you're doing, that's important." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The pace question only matters if the work is something you care about. For someone just trying to flip a company and retire to a Hawaiian island, pace is irrelevant -- you can sprint, sell, leave, and (typically) discover you feel irrelevant and start beating yourself up for not making a second business. The pace question matters for the work you intend to keep doing.

Pace is also the answer to the "I'll rest when I get there" trap. There is no there. The business is a marathon. The pace you run today is the pace you have agreed to run for the duration. If that pace is sprint-pace, you will collapse. If that pace is sustainable, you will arrive.

This is the operational complement to [[energy-management-over-time-management]]. Energy management is the principle. Pace is the question that operationalizes it. "Am I at a sustainable pace?" is the daily-level test of whether energy is being managed well.

## Why Spin Matters

"Spin is when you can look back on a week and say, how much of it do I think was productive, was actually getting us to the right place that we wanted to go in an efficient way, in a way that didn't create a lot of rowing against the current. And there's a viewpoint of what spin is for the week. There's also a viewpoint of what spin is for the month. There's also a viewpoint of what spin is for the year. And those different ways of thinking about spin are really important because it tells us, hey, how much of what we're doing is actually productive." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

Spin operates at three time horizons:

- **Week-level spin:** Look back at the last week. How much of the work moved the ball forward? How much was answering emails, putting out fires, doing things that felt productive in the moment but produced no visible change?
- **Month-level spin:** Look back at the last month. What is different in the business that wasn't different 30 days ago? If nothing is different, the month was high-spin.
- **Year-level spin:** Look back at the year. What changed? What got produced? What got removed? If the company looks roughly the same as a year ago, the year was high-spin regardless of how busy it felt.

The three horizons matter because spin is invisible at short time scales. An hour of email-answering feels productive. A week of email-answering still mostly feels productive. But pull back to month or year, and the absence of forward motion becomes visible. The longer time horizons are the ones that catch what the shorter ones miss.

## The Spin Check Is Easier Than the Pace Check

Joe notes that the spin diagnostic is more accessible than the pace one. The reason: spin can be felt directly. The body knows when it is rowing against the current. The mind knows when it has put in effort and the thing has not moved. The felt sense of "I worked really hard and nothing changed" is unmistakable once attended to.

Pace is harder to assess from inside because the sprint feels normal once it has been the pace for a while. The body adapts. The mind normalizes. The question "is this sustainable?" requires stepping outside the current state, which the current state often prevents.

For this reason, the practical entry point for most people is the spin check. Start there. The pace check follows naturally once the spin check has surfaced what is actually moving and what is not.

## What the Check Sounds Like in a Meeting

The team check-in is short. It is not a strategic review. It is a moment of attention.

- "What's the pace right now?"
- "What's the spin?"
- "Of the work this week, how much moved the ball forward?"
- "Of the effort we're spending, how much is rowing upriver?"

The check is meant to interrupt the default assumption that being busy equals being productive. It is meant to expose what the default assumption hides.

The follow-up move depends on the answer. If the spin is high, the question becomes: what would let us spend less effort on what is not moving the ball? See [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]]. If the pace is wrong, the question becomes: what do we need to drop, restructure, or change so that we can sustain this for the next year?

## The Underlying Mechanism

Both pace and spin are signals that catch what raw output measures miss. A team measuring only hours worked, tasks completed, emails answered, or meetings attended will look productive even when nothing is happening. The classical metrics report the activity; they do not report the movement.

Pace and spin report the movement. They are the diagnostics that catch the gap between activity and accomplishment.

In Joe's framing, this is the diagnostic equivalent of the [[hungry-lion-principle]] (see [[overwhelm-reduces-productivity]]). The lion that hasn't eaten is hyper-active and getting nothing. The relaxed lion is the better hunter. The pace-and-spin check is the question a lion would ask if it could: am I actually hunting, or am I just running around?

## When Someone Experiences This

- They feel like they worked really hard this week but cannot point to anything that changed in the business.
- They are exhausted but their team is also exhausted and the product hasn't shipped.
- They keep saying "I just need to get through this week" and the next week is the same.
- They feel busy and unimportant at the same time -- the busy not producing the felt-sense of meaningful work.
- They look at the calendar and feel a low-grade dread about the upcoming meetings without being able to say why.

## In Practice

The minimum viable practice: a weekly check. Either alone or with a team. Two questions. Answer them honestly.

- What was the pace this week? Sustainable, or sprint?
- What was the spin? Of the effort spent, what fraction moved the ball forward?

For teams, doing this in a meeting normalizes the questions and the honest answers. Once the team is fluent in pace and spin, the questions become part of normal decision-making. "Is this meeting going to add to our spin? Is this initiative going to break our pace?" become live considerations rather than after-the-fact regrets.

## Tone

Operational. Direct. The two terms function as instruments. Joe introduces them the way a coach introduces a stopwatch -- as something practical you use to see what is actually happening. The terms themselves do not need to be defended philosophically. They earn their keep by surfacing what the team did not see before.
