# Boundary With vs. Boundary Against

## The Distinction

A boundary against a person is a wall. A boundary with a person is a connection.

The mechanics of stating the boundary can look identical. The internal experience is opposite. The difference is felt by the receiver, and the difference determines whether the boundary opens or closes the relationship.

"That's the subtle thing about boundaries. People think boundaries are against, right? Because we value this idea of freedom so greatly in ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## The Two Sides

**Boundary against:**
- Felt as separation, protection, defense.
- Heart closes when stating it.
- The intention is to keep the person out.
- The person is positioned as a threat.
- Often delivered with edge, righteousness, or distance.
- The receiver feels rejected.

**Boundary with:**
- Felt as a gift, both to self and other.
- Heart opens when stating it.
- The intention is for the relationship to work, for both people.
- The person is welcomed; the behavior is what is being addressed.
- Delivered with care.
- The receiver feels seen, not pushed away.

## The Test

The internal test for whether a boundary is "with" or "against" is whether stating it opens or closes the heart toward the other person.

"I know that I'm drawing a great boundary when, when it opens up my heart for the person that I'm drawing the boundary with." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

If stating the boundary makes you feel more love for the person, the boundary is "with." If stating the boundary makes you feel more separate, more righteous, more closed, the boundary is "against."

The body knows. Notice what happens internally as the boundary is spoken or rehearsed. The signature of "against" is bracing. The signature of "with" is softening.

The other person's reaction is irrelevant to the test. The test is about the internal state of the speaker, before delivery.

"Just the saying it, no matter what their reaction is. Maybe their reaction is fuck you. And maybe their reaction is, oh, thank God you said that. It doesn't matter. Whatever it is, when you say it, your capacity to love them has increased." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

See [[does-saying-it-increase-your-love]] for the question that operationalizes this test in live coaching.

## You Are Not Asking Them to Change

The critical move in drawing a boundary with someone: you are saying what you will do, not what they must do.

"If I'm drawing a boundary with a friend and that person has a tendency to get angry, I would say, my boundary with you is when you get angry, I'm going to walk away, happy to re-engage with you whenever you're not yelling at me. I'm not asking them to stop yelling at me. I'm saying what I'm going to do." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

This is what makes the boundary fully empowered. It takes responsibility for the self. It places no demand on the other. It leaves the other free.

Demands disguised as boundaries are not boundaries. "My boundary is that you stop drinking" is a demand, not a boundary. "My boundary is I'm not in the room when you're drinking" is a boundary.

## Boundary as Safety, Not Control

A clean boundary creates safety in the relationship, not control over it. The other person can do what they want. The boundary makes clear what will follow, and the relationship can hold either outcome.

"It's like creating a background of safety and connection regardless of how they act so that they don't have to be a certain way." -- Brett Kistler, Love over Defense

The other person, knowing the boundary, can choose freely. The freedom is what allows the relationship to keep functioning even when behaviors conflict.

## Boundaries With Self

The same distinction applies internally.

A boundary against yourself is a war with a part of yourself. "I won't let myself do that." "I have to stop being that way." It creates the same kind of internal separation that defense creates externally. The part being walled off doubles down or rebels.

A boundary with yourself is the gift framing. "If I'm noticing myself getting angry, I am going to separate myself from other people so that I don't get angry at them." This is a boundary that protects multiple parts of the self at once. The anger, the people who would receive it, the part of you that would have to clean up afterward.

"In that boundary, I stop making myself wrong. And when I'm drawing it, the trick is when I'm literally thinking about drawing it, it doesn't feel like an oppression, it feels like a gift." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The check is the same: does setting this boundary open your heart to yourself, or close it?

## The Paradox

A boundary appears to be an act of separation, and a deep welcoming appears to be the absence of separation. How can a boundary be a deep welcoming?

The resolution: separation can be loving. Separation that protects both parties, that does not demand change, that is given as a gift, is connection in the form of structure. The structure makes the love operable.

"There is nothing that you can do that's truly compassionate for you, that's also not compassionate for those around you in that circumstance. It's the same with a boundary." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

Anything actually compassionate for you turns out to be compassionate for everyone in the situation. If it is compassionate for you but not for them, look again; the apparent compassion may be self-justification.

## Boundaries as Love's Infrastructure

The "with" framing is not a softening of the boundary. It is the recognition that boundaries are required for love to survive.

"There's no iconic figure of love, there's no paragon of love that isn't really strongly boundaried, whether that's like a great mom or Martin Luther King or Mother Teresa. All of these people were incredibly boundaried. So boundaries are part of what makes a loving relationship. It creates trust." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The reason love requires boundaries: love cannot survive domination. A relationship in which one party can dominate another, intentionally or not, cannot sustain love. The boundary refuses the domination, which preserves the conditions in which love can live.

"It's really hard to love something that can dominate you." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

This is the structural answer to people who believe boundaries are unloving. The opposite is true: the absence of boundaries makes sustained love impossible, because the dominated party cannot keep loving the dominator.

## Connection to VIEW

A boundary drawn with [[vulnerability]] (here is what is true for me), [[impartiality]] (I am not telling you what to do), [[empathy]] (I see you and care about you), and [[wonder]] (I don't know how you will respond and that is fine) is necessarily a boundary "with." VIEW makes the with-vs-against distinction unnecessary because VIEW only produces "with" boundaries.
