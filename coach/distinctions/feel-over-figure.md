# Feel Over Figure

## Definition

Figuring is the intellect strategizing toward a goal: this is the outcome I want, how do I get there. Feeling is allowing the emotional reality of the moment to move. The two are not opposed. The intellect is essential. But when emotion is being avoided, the intellect cannot produce clarity. Clarity comes from being okay with whatever emotional state arises, not from logical analysis.

"Clarity doesn't come from being logical because it doesn't work. You can't do it in your decision-making. It comes from being okay with whatever emotional state arises." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## What Each Is Good For

The intellect is excellent at strategizing the path from A to B once A and B are known. It can solve puzzles, build bridges, and structure conversations. It cannot generate the postulates from which it operates. Those postulates are emotional. This is why two intelligent people with the same facts can logically justify opposite conclusions. They have different emotional postulates underneath.

"Figuring it out at its most essential is just your intellect. It's to strategize, it's to try to solve the puzzle. It's like, this is the outcome that I want, how do I get there. That is what the intellect is really good at. But that's what it means when you're figuring, when you're intellectualizing." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

Feeling is what sets the goal in the first place. It also sets the risk profile. If you cannot bear to feel the loss of a girlfriend, your risk tolerance collapses and you cannot be yourself in the relationship. If you can bear it, you act from what you want.

## Why Feeling Comes First

When emotion is blocked, decisions cannot be clear. The brain runs on emotional data. Without it, decision-making collapses (see [[decisions-are-emotional]] for the neuroscience). Trying to think your way past an unfelt emotion produces stuck loops, binary thinking, judgment, and indecision.

Feel first. Then figure. Not as a rule but as a sequence. The emotional movement clears the postulates. Once the postulates are clear, the intellect has something honest to work with.

"It's when your emotions are blocked or when you're trying to deeply avoid an emotion, then your decision-making can't be clear. It's just as simple as that." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## Gödel's Incompleteness as the Mathematical Mirror

All sets of logic are either incomplete or rest on a postulate. Logic cannot be entirely logical. In a coaching context, the postulates underneath logic are emotions. This is why people can logically defend opposite positions: their rationale is downstream of their feelings, not the other way around.

"It's not because their rationale is good or bad, it's because they have different postulates behind their rationale." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## The Practical Sequence

Joe describes the sequence in many specific situations:
- Go release your anger by yourself. Then talk to the person.
- Go mourn the marriage all the way through. Then have the conversation about what you need.
- Get really, really scared. Then talk to your boss about the raise.

The action comes from what you want, not from avoiding what you do not want to feel. The emotional experience clears the path so the action is no longer contaminated by avoidance. See [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]].

## The Trap of Naming Without Feeling

A common failure mode in this work: the person learns to identify their emotions ("I'm feeling angry right now") and uses the naming as a substitute for the feeling. Joe describes this happening to himself after a decade of emotional work. Someone told him: "You're naming an emotion so you don't have to feel it."

Naming is a real stage. It is also a trap once it becomes the new constraint. See [[stages-of-emotional-development]] and [[epiphany-becomes-rut]].

## Tone

Direct, plain. Not anti-intellect. Joe loves watching great minds at work. The distinction is about sequence and sufficiency, not about choosing sides.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is making lists of pros and cons and still cannot decide.
- When someone is intellectualizing a situation that obviously has emotional weight.
- When someone shows up clear in their reasoning but flat in their body.
