# A Problem vs. Something That Is Happening

## The Distinction

Two different things are usually called by the same word. There is a situation that is happening in a life and needs to be addressed. And there is "a problem" -- a situation that consumes the person, guides their attention, pushes them around, and is rebelled against and resisted. The first is just a situation. The second is the situation plus a particular relationship to it.

"I'm making a distinction between something that's happening in our life that needs to be addressed and a problem. A problem being something that consumes us and guides us and pushes us and we rebel against and resist." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

The distinction matters because the same situation can be either, depending on the relationship to it. A car accident can be a problem (consuming, all-absorbing, sustained crisis) or it can be something that happened (acknowledged, grieved, addressed, integrated). The event is the same. The internal state differs. The internal state is what determines whether the word "problem" applies.

## What Makes Something a Problem

A situation becomes "a problem" when one or more of these are true:

- **It is consuming.** It takes up disproportionate attention, often crowding out other parts of life that should also be present.
- **It is guiding behavior in a stuck way.** Choices are being made to avoid it or manage it rather than to live well.
- **It is being rebelled against and resisted.** There is an internal "no" being pushed against the situation, even when no useful action is available.
- **It has emotional charge that is not being felt.** The unfelt emotion underneath the situation is what gives it the gravitational pull that makes it consume attention. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

The opposite stance, where the situation is "something happening" rather than "a problem": the situation is being addressed, the emotions are being felt, attention can move elsewhere when appropriate, and there is no internal war with what is. The situation may still be painful. The pain is just the emotion moving through; it is not being made into something more than it is.

## The Time-Bound Quality of Problems

Joe makes a subtle point about the temporal character of "a problem." Most situations become problems, then stop being problems, then potentially become problems again, depending on the relationship to them.

"We have all had something stolen from us and there's a time when it's a problem and there's a time when it's not a problem. And it maybe never feels good, but there's a time when it wraps us up and I'll call that a problem. And there's a time when we've let go of it." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

The phasing is important. The thing being stolen is the event. The state of being "wrapped up" by it is the problem. The state of having "let go of it" is the same event becoming something that happened. The event has not changed across the transition; the relationship has.

This means "problem" is not a property of an event. It is a property of the relationship between a person and an event. The same event is a problem for the person who is wrapped up in it and is not a problem for the person who has felt through it.

## How Avoidance Sustains the Problem State

The mechanism that keeps situations stuck in "problem" status is emotional avoidance. The unfelt emotions about the situation generate the gravitational pull that makes attention return to it. The mind keeps coming back because the body has not completed what it needed to complete. The "problem" is the body's signal that an emotional loop is still open.

"Trying to avoid that is what makes that a longer problem, a more consistent problem, a problem that repeats itself." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

This connects directly to [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. For the recurring problems in a life, the avoidance is producing the recurrence. For one-time events that become sustained problems, the avoidance is keeping the event in problem-status longer than it would otherwise be. The shared mechanism: unfelt emotion produces stuck attention.

The corollary: feeling the emotions associated with the situation often moves the situation out of "problem" status, even when nothing about the situation has materially changed. The event is still what it is. The relationship to it has become fluid rather than stuck. The "problem" dissolves into "something that happened that I am addressing as it needs to be addressed."

## The Distinction from Suffering vs. Pain

This distinction is the practical, situational form of the deeper [[suffering-vs-pain]] distinction. Pain is the emotion arising from a situation. Suffering is the resistance to the pain. In the same way, "something happening" is the event itself. "A problem" is the event plus the resistance, plus the unfelt emotions, plus the wrapping-up of attention.

The Buddhist frame: pain is unavoidable, suffering is optional. The Joe frame: situations are unavoidable, problems are optional. The two distinctions are pointing at the same architecture from different angles.

## Why This Distinction Matters for Coaching

When someone presents what they call "a problem," the first move is often to test which kind it is. Is this a situation that needs to be addressed and the person has the resources to address it? Or is this a situation that has become a problem because of unfelt emotions and avoided feelings underneath?

The diagnostic questions:

- Is the situation consuming a disproportionate amount of your attention?
- What emotion are you not wanting to feel about it?
- If you imagine that you fully grieved it / fully felt the anger / fully welcomed the helplessness, would it still be a problem in the way it currently is?
- What would addressing this look like from a state where it is something happening rather than a problem?

The distinction lets the coaching work happen at the right layer. If the situation is just something happening, the coaching is practical: what is the next move, what is needed, what is in the way of taking that move. If the situation has become a problem, the coaching is emotional first: what is being avoided, how is the avoidance keeping the situation in problem-status, what would feeling the avoided emotion release.

## The "Recurring Problems" Application

The distinction is the foundation for [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]'s focus on recurring problems specifically. Joe is careful that the algorithm is not meant to apply to every event that occurs ("a tree fell on my car is not your emotional avoidance"). The algorithm is meant to apply to the recurring problems in a life. The recurring quality is itself the signal that the emotional layer is operative -- the recurrence is the evidence that the body is still generating opportunities to feel the unfelt emotion.

A one-time event that becomes a sustained problem and a situation that keeps recurring as a pattern are the same mechanism viewed at different time scales. Both are produced by unfelt emotion holding the situation in problem-status, either by extending its duration (one event) or by recreating it (pattern). The distinction between "problem" and "something happening" gives the coach a way to talk about both without conflating them with random material events.

## Tone

Careful. Joe draws this distinction with precision because the conflation of "problem" with "event" is what makes people miss where the actual work is. The careful language is itself part of the medicine: introducing the distinction lets the person see their own situation differently, often releasing the wrapped-up state without further intervention.
