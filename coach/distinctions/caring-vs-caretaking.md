# Caring vs. Caretaking

## Definition

Caring is being in support of someone. Caretaking is trying to manage their experience so you do not have to feel your own.

The operational definition Joe uses: caretaking is when you are trying to manage another person's emotions through what looks like care so that you do not have to experience something you do not want to experience. Caring has no agenda for the person's emotional state. Caretaking has the agenda buried under the appearance of help.

"That's actually the way that I would define the difference between caring for somebody and caretaking somebody is the idea that you're trying to manage their experience. And particularly, this gets particularly dangerous when you're managing their experience for your experience." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

## The Two Sides

**Caring (empathic support):**
- Being with the person in whatever they are feeling.
- Not needing their emotional state to change.
- Empowering them to find their own path.
- Asking "How do you want to fix it?" instead of "Here's how we fix it."
- The person on the receiving end feels seen and empowered.
- Joy in the act itself. The other's reaction is unimportant.

"Caring for is when you're moving not out of any desire to change the person. Your desire is just simply to relish and enjoy the feeling of caring for somebody. It is an unbelievable joy to care for somebody. And it's just a great experience to have, but you're not trying to change them in any way. Their reaction to your care is completely unimportant." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

**Caretaking (emotional management):**
- Trying to make the person feel better so you can feel better.
- Managing their emotional state because your peace depends on it.
- "If you feel happy, I'll be happy. If you're not angry, I'll be happy."
- Breeds victim mentality in the people around you. If you take responsibility for their emotions, they stop taking responsibility for themselves.
- The person on the receiving end feels managed, not seen.
- The wife managing the husband's anger so she does not have to feel her own fear. The friend managing the other's sadness so they do not have to feel their own.

## Why the Distinction Matters

Caretaking creates dependency. A caretaking CEO produces a company full of victims because people learn that their leader will solve emotional problems for them. When the CEO shifts to caring, to being with people and asking "How are you going to fix it?", everything changes. People become empowered to solve their own problems.

Caretaking also does not work. You cannot change someone else's emotional state. The attempt is exhausting and futile. Caring requires less effort and produces more connection.

"As you stop fearing all these emotional states, then you just stand in your truth more and more. There's just a deeper level of empowerment that happens for you and for the people around you." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy

## The Diagnostics

There are several reliable signals that distinguish caring from caretaking. See dedicated articles for each:

- [[resentment-as-caretaking-diagnostic]]: caretaking eventually breeds resentment in both directions. Caring does not.
- [[softening-as-caring-diagnostic]]: people who really care for others over time become softer, less rigid, with bigger hearts. Caretakers get more neurotic, more isolated, more spun up.
- [[you-think-they-cant-handle-it]]: thinking the other person is too weak to hear your truth is one of the clearest tells that you are in caretaking.
- [[rehearsing-how-to-say-it]]: running multiple ways of saying something in your head to get a different reaction is caretaking, even at the most subtle level.

## The Self-Recognition: "I Was Managing Everyone, Not Serving Them"

A specific pattern of recognition that often arrives once a caretaker drops the role: the realization that the caretaking was not for the people being cared for. It was for the caretaker's own sense of control. The frame of "I was giving to everyone" turns inside out into "I was managing everyone." The word that lands for many people on the other side of this recognition is *manipulation*.

"One of the other big changes in me was -- I saw myself as always giving to others, but in reality what I was doing is managing everybody and managing everybody's emotions and trying to keep everybody happy. And letting go of that has been massive because what I didn't realize is that that wasn't fun or enjoyable for the people that I love. And, you know, I thought I was serving them all this time, but in reality, I was -- it doesn't feel good to admit, but it was a form of manipulation." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The word is not used to shame. It is used to describe accurately what the dynamic actually was. The caretaking optimized for the caretaker's preferred emotional state in the room. The other people were inputs to that optimization. They felt managed, not loved -- which is why they responded to the dropping of the role with relief and increased affection.

A specific evidence the dropping of the role produces: people start expressing love to the former caretaker more freely. Carla describes a colleague telling her "I love you" in a way that "just doesn't happen" in her professional world. The reading: when the management was operating, the others sensed the agenda and could not move toward her. When the management dropped, the opening became visible and they did.

## Resentment as the Tell

Caretaking eventually generates resentment in both people. The caretaker resents the sacrifice. The cared-for resents being treated as incapable.

"Caretaking eventually will lead to resentment in you. You will start resenting the person and they will start resenting you because they have no choice but to resent you because you're treating them like they're kids." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The caretaker's internal sequence: I am sacrificing myself every day for you, I am not speaking my truth, I am positioning my truth so you can hear it. And you are angry at me? Then forget you.

The cared-for's internal sequence: I am a full adult. Why does this person think I need my emotions managed? Screw off, I can take care of myself. What makes you think you are better than me?

The resentment is not the malfunction. It is the system telling both people the dynamic is corrupt. The exit is to stop caretaking, not to manage the resentment.

## What the Person With No Caretaking Becomes

People who can care without abandonment of themselves are recognizable. They have a specific somatic signature.

"There's a way in which they have used this experience of caring for somebody to dissolve themselves, to sandpaper their edges. And they're just soft people. I don't mean soft like not strong. They're far stronger than most people. I mean soft as in that they're not rigid." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

This soft-but-strong state is the long-term marker. Caretakers in the same external role (caring for an addict, a sick parent, a child) become harder, more isolated, more spun up. Carers become softer.

A specific marker: they often feel the emotions the cared-for person is not feeling. They weep at sadness the other person is avoiding. They take in what is not being processed.

"The people I see who really care for, who are really able to care for others without any kind of abandonment of themselves, they often feel people's emotions when the person isn't feeling their emotions. You'll see them weep at or take in the sadness that the other person is avoiding." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

This is not the same as [[losing-yourself-in-others]] (the empathic merging that loses the self). The carer remains themselves while feeling what is unprocessed in the other. The marker is the open-hearted contact without loss of ground.

## Caretaking Is the Savior in the Fear Triangle

Caretaking is the workplace and relational form of the savior role in the [[fear-triangle]]. The savior runs away from their own experience by going into the other person to fix them. The fix is for the savior's anxiety, not for the other person's actual benefit.

"If I can make it so you don't get drunk, I'll feel safe. If I can make it that you are happy, then I'll feel safe. And so I'm running away from myself, going into you to try to fix my issues." -- Joe Hudson, Empower over Power

The signature feeling of the savior is obligation. When obligation is the engine of helping, caretaking is happening, not caring. The exit is to feel the helplessness underneath the obligation rather than to perform yet more saving.

Joe's example: philanthropy from guilt. Canadians bringing clothes to a Nicaraguan village out of guilt feel good about themselves and disempower the recipients, who now depend on the giver. Philanthropy that works is exchange, not gift. "Good philanthropy is an exchange. It's not a gift. It's a recognition that you're getting as much from it as you're giving."

## Caretaking Is the Shadow of the Care Face of Love

Care is one of the [[four-faces-of-love]]. Every face has a shadow form, and the shadow of care is caretaking. When care is pursued without the anchor of deep welcoming, it collapses into caretaking. The caretaker is trying to deliver care without the welcoming that makes care clean.

"The care can be codependency." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The corrective is not to do less caring. It is to add welcoming back. Welcoming the person as they are, including welcoming their suffering as theirs to walk through, makes the care clean. The clean care has no need to fix.

Loving someone is not caretaking them. The two operations are different and often opposed.

"Don't mistake love for caretaking. Loving yourself, loving somebody else isn't caretaking them. It's not saying yes, no, even if you want to say no. It's not going against your truth. It's not trying to make them happier. It's just having a deep welcoming for who they are." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## Caretaking Reinforces the Behavior It Is Trying To Stop

A central counterintuitive finding: caretaking strengthens the very dynamic the caretaker is trying to neutralize. The wife managing her husband's anger teaches him that getting angry produces compliance. The family member rescuing the addict from consequences removes the bottom that recovery requires. The boss protecting underperformers from accountability produces a culture of underperformance.

"If I get angry, you will stop being yourself and you will try to manage my experience, which gives me all the power in the relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The evidence Joe cites about addiction: the hardest cases to recover are independently wealthy alcoholics with no one depending on them. No bottom available. The caretaking is the bottom that was supposed to arrive.

"Most of the time, the codependence is just making it so that the person doesn't hit their bottom. And so you're actually hurting them." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The reframe for someone insisting they have to caretake: "What makes you think that they're going to not die of a drug overdose or not go homeless if you're taking care of them? Do you think that every single person who has a drug overdose is because their brother stopped caring for them?"

## When the Person Cannot Care for Themselves

The most common objection: "But my situation is different. They actually need me." This applies to children, the elderly, the sick, the disabled. Joe's distinction: these situations require care. The question is whether the care is clean care or caretaking.

"With children, you actually do have like quite a responsibility to take care of kids. And yet a lot of people caretake kids. 'Please don't cry, Billy.' It's a perfect example of it." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The same external action (changing the diaper, feeding the parent, paying for the addict's housing) can be care or caretaking. The diagnostic is internal: the resentment, the felt obligation, the management of their experience to control your own.

The objection itself is often diagnostic. "There are people that I really have to take care of" is frequently a cover for unwillingness to feel what would arise if the caretaking stopped. The fear of losing control is the same fear the abuser has when contemplating stopping the abuse. "Everybody's got their control in different ways."

## The Pre-Feel Move

For someone insisting they have no choice, Joe offers a specific instruction before making any decision: feel the feared loss in advance.

"If you're being honest with yourself, feel the loss. Feel the loss before you even make your decision. I'm not saying stop or start, just feel the loss. Like, oh yeah, if I stop this, they're gonna die. Feel your person's death, mourn it, go all the way through it so that you're making decisions that are based on what's best for them, not because you are trying to avoid the grief." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The function: most caretaking is grief avoidance. Pre-feel the grief and the decision is no longer driven by it. The decision can be made from clarity about what is actually best, not from fear of what would have to be felt. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].

## At Work and In Groups

The same pattern operates in groups. Optimizing for harmony, fitting in, not making waves. People not speaking their truth to each other. In tech and creative work, this is fatal: the inventiveness requires being able to be wrong out loud.

"There is a real possibility that you will be ousted from the group. There's also an equally real possibility that you will force the healing of the group." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The leadership implication: a healthy culture is one where everyone is acting like a leader from time to time. Caretaking the group, muting yourself to fit in, kills leadership. Leadership is the tip of the spear, going into new territory. The new territory cannot be reached by a group optimizing for current harmony.

The accountability question: how do you hold someone accountable in this framing? The very framing reveals the caretaking. Holding someone accountable with love is not a problem. The reason it feels like a problem is the caretaking impulse to protect the underperformer from consequences.

"The reason that you wouldn't hold them accountable is because you're being caretaking, and the reason that you wouldn't fire them is because you're being caretaking, and then they will become resentful of you." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

See [[just-business-vs-impersonal]] and [[three-ways-to-fire-someone]] for the related distinctions on how to deliver hard accountability cleanly.

## Subtle Caretaking

Beyond the obvious cases, caretaking operates at vanishingly small scales. The instruction: listen to every sentence you speak to someone you love. Notice how many are subtle excuses, subtle hedges, not completely owning your experience.

"If you're scared about what somebody's gonna think about you and you're modifying your behavior in any way, that is a form of caretaking. And so it just gets more and more subtle. It's subtle till you see it." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The subtlety is not a sign that the work is finished. It is a sign that the work has gone deeper. See [[development-as-corkscrew]]: each new turn reveals the same pattern at a finer grain.

## Stages People Go Through

The transition out of caretaking is not clean. There are predictable stages:

1. **Unaware:** the caretaking is invisible. It is just called caring.
2. **Aware and judging self:** "I'm doing this. That's bad. I can't be doing that." The person starts a war with their own caretaker.
3. **Judging others:** the judgment of self spills outward. Other people doing caretaking become irritating. See [[weaponized-not-caretaking]].
4. **Pedestal:** "I'm done with caretaking. Everyone else needs to do their work." A new form of distance dressed as evolution.
5. **Cutting off:** "I'm not responsible for your feelings, it's not my job to make you feel better" delivered as a wall, not as truth.
6. **Caring without abandonment:** the caring becomes clean. Softness emerges. The person can be open-hearted without being lost.
7. **Forgetting boundaries:** once caring feels good, the impulse arises to remove all boundaries. This produces the next round of trouble. Care without boundaries is not sustainable. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]].

"The biggest pitfall is people will get through one layer of how they're caretaking and they think they're through it. There's just more and more subtle ways." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The corkscrew applies. See [[development-as-corkscrew]].

## When Someone You Love Stops Caretaking

A specific instruction for the other side: when someone close to you is moving out of caretaking, they are likely to be clunky and harsh for a while. They have been preparing for attack their whole life because their truth produced attack from their original caregivers. Now they are testing whether attack will arrive.

"What looks like they're being dicks is really them preparing for attack." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The clean move from the receiving side: "I'm so glad that you're asking for what you want. And I just want you to know, I'm not going to attack you. There's no need to be defensive."

The instruction for the person making the transition: hold yourself with love and care as you do it. Do not defend. Allow attacks to land and use them as sandpaper. Most of the time, in a year or two, you will be surrounded by people who want to hear your truth and want to be sovereign next to you, not in you or through you.

## Caretaking Is a Developmental Stage to Embrace, Not Eliminate

A subtle point that is easy to miss in the work: the goal is not to eradicate caretaking. The goal is to learn to embrace it. Caretaking is one of the developmental stages a human moves through. Trying to skip it is like trying to pull a butterfly out of its cocoon before it is ready.

Asked whether he would prefer a vaccine for caretaking or for a virus, Joe declines both:

"I wouldn't pull the butterfly out of the cocoon because it won't be able to fly afterwards. For me, it would be like, do you build a vaccine for crawling or do you build a vaccine for, in a capacity to do mathematics in an 8-year-old? There are developmental steps that we need to go through. Like all systems require something to challenge them so that they can grow and be strong and evolve. If there's no challenge, there's no evolution. So I don't want to get rid of our challenges. I want to learn how to embrace them, how to learn how to embrace the death that we face, to learn how to embrace the caretaking that we do, to learn how to embrace the innocence that tries to get us to manage other people to feel safe. Like, that's what I want. It's not to get rid of it. It's to learn how to embrace it." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The framing of caretaking as "the innocence that tries to get us to manage other people to feel safe" is important. The caretaker is not a perpetrator. The caretaker is a frightened part of the self that learned this strategy for safety. The strategy gets refined over time, not amputated. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] for the broader teaching this expresses.

The practical consequence: when a person is working with their caretaking, the stance is not war. It is welcoming. The caretaking pattern is one of the things they are learning to love and let evolve, not one of the things they are trying to kill. See [[weaponized-not-caretaking]] for what happens when this distinction collapses.

## What Is Actually Compassionate

Joe's reframe of the apparent conflict between self-care and care for others: there is no conflict. What is actually most compassionate for you is also what is actually most compassionate for them. See [[what-is-truly-compassionate]].

The version of self-care that is in conflict with care for the other is shallow self-care (drinking, partying, withdrawing). Deep self-care converges with deep care for the other.

"What I see is that the thing that is true in us, that's the compassionate treatment of ourselves, is also the compassionate treatment of the person across from us." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The follow-up Joe asks when someone finally breaks through into their want: "And so what about that is not the most compassionate thing for the person who you've been caretaking?"

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone identifies as a helper or savior in their social group.
- When a leader feels responsible for team morale.
- When emotional exhaustion follows every deep conversation.
- When the same person keeps coming to you with the same problem.
- When a person is mistaking caretaking for loving.
- When someone is loving by saying yes when they want to say no.
- When someone is in a relationship with an angry, addicted, or unwell partner and feels trapped.
- When someone insists their family/work situation requires them to caretake.
- When someone is rehearsing how to deliver hard truth so the receiver will react well.
- When someone fears the other person cannot handle their honesty.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[empathy]]: the practice of being with, not managing.

- Toward [[what-is-truly-compassionate]]: the reframe that resolves the apparent conflict between self-care and care.
