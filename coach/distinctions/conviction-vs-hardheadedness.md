# Conviction vs. Hardheadedness

## The Distinction

Conviction and hardheadedness can look identical from outside. Both involve commitment to a direction. Both produce visible firmness. Structurally they are different, and the difference matters for the sustainability of the leadership they support.

**Conviction** holds where it is going without being rigid about how to get there. The destination is clear. The path is held with attunement. The person with conviction is listening to the environment, the people, the unfolding situation — and adjusting the route. The conviction is in the where, not the how.

**Hardheadedness** holds both the where and the how. It is the where-conviction with the how-flexibility removed. The person being hardheaded insists on a specific path because they have fused the destination with the route. The route IS the destination in their experience. Any deviation feels like betrayal of the conviction.

Hardheadedness is not the absence of conviction. It is a less effective form of it. People will sometimes follow hardheadedness because the conviction is real underneath. But the rigidity about the how costs the leader allies, accuracy, and adaptability.

"Sometimes there is no distinction. We will follow hardheadedness because there is a conviction to it. Hardheadedness is just not the most effective form of conviction." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## The Martin Luther King Illustration

The clearest example: Martin Luther King held conviction about where the movement was going — civil rights, equal treatment regardless of skin color. That conviction was firm. He could be called hardheaded about it in the sense that no argument was going to move him off it.

The how, however, was the opposite of hardheaded. The listening, the strategic shifts, the emotional intelligence in interactions, the response to circumstances as they unfolded — all of that was attuned, fluid, adaptive.

"You could say Martin Luther King was hardheaded about civil rights, hardheaded about being treated in an equal manner, no matter what color skin, but how he got there, listening, the interaction, having the emotional intelligence to interact with people, none of that was hardheaded. He had conviction in his process. He had conviction in where he wanted to land." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The example clarifies the distinction operationally. The conviction is about the destination and the values of how the journey is conducted. The flexibility is about the route between here and there.

## Why The Distinction Matters For Leadership

Two failure modes flow from confusing them:

1. **The person who refuses leadership because they associate it with hardheadedness.** They have seen hardheaded leaders impose their preferred routes on the team and produce friction. They do not want to be that person. So they refuse to step into conviction at all, mistaking the rigid version for the only version. Conviction without the route-rigidity is available to them. They have not seen it modeled.

2. **The leader who confuses hardheadedness with strength.** They believe that flexibility on the how looks like weakness on the where. So they double down on the route to demonstrate the conviction. The team loses the benefit of the team's intelligence. The decisions get worse. The leader interprets the worsening as confirmation that they need to hold the line harder. The cycle compounds.

The distinction provides the exit from both. Conviction in the where, openness in the how. Hardheadedness is sometimes useful tactically — when speed matters more than route-optimization, when the team needs decisiveness more than consultation. But it is not the most effective sustained form.

## The Functional Asymmetry

The asymmetry that makes the distinction load-bearing: hardheadedness about the where can survive flexibility about the how. The reverse is not true. Flexibility about the where erodes conviction. The leader who is willing to negotiate the destination has lost the conviction that gets followed.

This is why "be flexible about everything" is not the right teaching. The teaching is: hold the where with conviction, hold the how with openness. The where is what the team is following. The how is what the team is helping refine.

## How To Tell Which One Is Operating In Yourself

Ask: if the situation gave me a faster, cleaner, better-aligned route to the same destination, would I take it?

- If yes, with relief: conviction.
- If yes, with reluctance about losing "my way of doing it": probably hardheadedness with conviction underneath.
- If no: hardheadedness has taken over. The route has fused with the destination in your experience.

A second test: what feedback can I absorb without losing my conviction?

- If feedback about the route lands and I adjust: conviction.
- If feedback about the route lands as challenge to my conviction itself: hardheadedness.

The conviction-bearing leader can hear the team's read on the route without it threatening the destination. The hardheaded leader cannot — every challenge to the route registers as a challenge to the underlying conviction, because the two are not separated internally.

## The Connection To Process-Over-Result

A related teaching that lives in the same neighborhood: [[process-over-result]] and [[lead-with-your-process-not-the-results]]. The hardheaded leader holds tight to a specific result-path. The conviction-bearing leader holds the values that animate the process, and lets the results unfold from those.

This is also why conviction tends to look like principle-following more than goal-chasing in practice. The where the conviction is committed to is often a way of being, a value, a quality of how things get done — not a specific outcome. The outcome follows. The conviction is upstream of the outcome.

## What This Means For Reading Other Leaders

When evaluating leaders, the diagnostic is not "do they hold their direction firmly." Many people hold their direction firmly. The diagnostic is "how do they respond when the route gets challenged."

- The leader who responds with openness on the route while staying clear on the where: conviction.
- The leader who responds with rigidity on both: hardheadedness.
- The leader who responds with flexibility on both: probably not actually leading from conviction. The fluidity reads as conviction-less, and the team will not follow.
