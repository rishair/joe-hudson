# Lead AND Follow (Not One or the Other)

## The Distinction

A functional leader does both. They lead: they are first in, they take responsibility, the buck stops with them, they go first with their own vulnerability, wonder, and not-knowing. They follow: they actually listen to the wisdom of their executives and let them have authority and autonomy. Doing one without the other produces predictable forms of dysfunction.

A leader who only leads makes a tyranny -- decisions get made but expertise is not consulted, and the team has no real authority. A leader who only follows makes a vacuum -- the team is empowered in name but no one is actually deciding, no one is taking responsibility, no one is going first.

"Leaders who don't lead or follow, either one of those can create super dysfunctional teams." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## What "Lead" Means Here

Leading is not commanding. It is going first in the harder direction.

"Leading, being the first in, taking the responsibility, having the quote unquote the buck stop with them, being the person who leads the charge in their own kind of vulnerability, not kind of, their own vulnerability, their own wonder, their own not knowing." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The texture of leading is the leader making the move that no one else can make from their position: owning the decision, naming the unspoken thing, modeling the not-knowing the team is afraid to admit. See [[public-failure-as-leadership-modeling]] for a specific form of this.

"Not owning that role of, oh, I am the decision maker on these key components." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team (describing the failure of leading)

## What "Follow" Means Here

Following is not abdication. It is actively trusting and using the wisdom that exists in the team.

"When I mean follow is that they're actually listening to the wisdom of their executives or the people that they're working with and letting them have authority and autonomy, super important." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The texture of following is the leader genuinely consulting the team, letting the team's reads shape the leader's reads, and giving the team real authority over their domains. The follow is what makes the team's expertise into a usable resource.

## Why Both Are Required

The two functions are non-substitutable. Following without leading leaves the team without a decision-maker; the team can offer wisdom but cannot conclude. Leading without following leaves the leader making decisions without the relevant information; the team's expertise is wasted and the team feels unconsulted.

The both/and looks contradictory only if leadership is imagined as a single posture. In practice, the leader leads in some moments and follows in others, and the skill is knowing which the moment calls for. A team that produces functional outcomes is one where this oscillation is happening continuously.

## The Less Common Failure

Joe notes that the not-leading failure is the rarer of the two. Most dysfunctional leaders fail by not following -- by refusing to consult, by overriding expertise, by hoarding decisions. The leader who fails by not leading is less common but real: they hold space well, they listen well, and the team is left waiting for the decision that does not come.

"Without that kind of leadership, it's really hard for a team to be functional. Not owning that role of, oh, I am the decision maker on these key components. It's less common, but it happens." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Self-Knowledge Required

To do both, the leader has to know in any given moment which they are doing. The leader who thinks they are following but is actually waiting for permission has not followed; they have abdicated. The leader who thinks they are leading but is actually performing authority has not led; they have postured. The distinction is fine-grained and lives in the body more than in the calendar.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader presents a stagnant team and is doing one of the two well but not the other
- A leader complains that their team is not stepping up (often a follow problem -- the leader is not actually giving them authority)
- A leader complains that nothing gets decided (often a lead problem -- the leader is waiting for consensus they will never get)
- A team complains the leader is autocratic OR a team complains the leader is absent
