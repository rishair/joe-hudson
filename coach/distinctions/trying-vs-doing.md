# Trying vs. Doing

## The Two Sides

**Trying:** A clenched, effortful state that includes planning, bracing, and tensing without movement. The body is engaged but no action is taken. The system is loaded but not firing.

**Doing:** Action without the clenched bracing. Flow. The body moves in alignment with the impulse. There is no resistance fighting the action while the action is happening.

The somatic test from the [[hands-together-exercise]]: put the palms of your hands together in front of your face and try as hard as you can to pull them apart. "If your hands are apart right now, you're not trying, you're doing." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

When you try, every muscle engages in planning the movement without executing it. The system is in tension. When you do, the movement happens.

## Why the Distinction Matters

Most "productivity effort" is actually trying. Sitting at the desk gripping the work, beating yourself up about a to-do list, planning the email you have not written, rehearsing the conversation you have not had. The body is loaded with tension. No work is happening.

"As I did that, there's a sense of planning going on throughout my entire system, like imagining which muscles I would move and how to move my hands apart. And I can do all of that without actually moving at all. It feels like a good metaphor for beating myself up over a to-do list." -- Brett Kistler, The Art of Accomplishment

This is the experience of being "busy" without producing. Trying mistaken for doing.

## The Martial Arts Frame

A relaxed body that tenses at the moment of impact hits harder than a body that is tense the whole time. Conserving energy until the moment of action produces both more strength and more accuracy. Walking around tense all the time does not make a person more effective.

"You're whole body is relaxed until the moment of impact. So if I was going to tense the whole time and hit you, my punch is a lot less powerful than if I'm relaxed the whole time and then tense right before I hit you." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

The same principle applies to work. The system that is constantly bracing for the next demand is depleted at the moment of action. The system that rests between actions is fully available when action is needed.

## Trying as a Signal

Notice when you are trying. The signal is that the body is engaged but nothing is happening. You may be staring at a screen, walking around a room, or sitting in a meeting "concentrating." If no action is occurring, you are trying.

The intervention is not to add more effort. The intervention is to feel the opposite of the trying. See [[undoing-not-doing]] and [[hands-together-exercise]] for the somatic practice.

## What Someone Experiences

The exhaustion of trying is the exhaustion of running an engine that is not connected to wheels. The fuel burns. Nothing moves. At the end of the day, you are tired but no work has been produced.

The ease of doing is the surprise of seeing the result without remembering effort. The email was written. The conversation happened. The problem was solved. The body is not depleted because the body was not fighting itself during the doing.
