# Symptoms vs. Root Causes of Depression

## The Distinction

Most people working on depression are working on the symptoms. They try to think more positively, to see themselves more clearly, to focus better, to be less fatigued, to feel less stuck. Each of these is a symptom of the depression. Treating it directly produces partial relief at best.

The root causes are on four layers: the negative self-talk (intellectual), the repressed anger or sadness (emotional), the depleted nervous system (gut), and the disconnection (community). See [[depression-four-levels]]. Addressing these dissolves the symptoms as a byproduct. Addressing the symptoms while leaving the causes intact reinforces the underlying loop.

## What Counts as a Symptom

Joe lists the symptoms of depression specifically -- not as the definition, but as the visible surface:

- Pessimism
- Forever thinking ("it's never going to change," "it's always going to be like this," "how am I ever going to get out of it")
- The feeling of being stuck
- Short attention span
- Adrenal fatigue / low energy / "I don't have the energy to do something"
- [[soul-dysmorphia]] -- inability to see yourself clearly, friends telling you how awesome you are and you cannot believe it
- Negative self-talk that contradicts itself and is not based on data
- Constant self-attack

See [[symptoms-of-depression]] for the full list and the diagnostic pattern.

## What the Symptom-Treatment Trap Looks Like

The person notices the symptom. They try to fix the symptom directly.

- "I wish I wasn't so tired. I have to do something to stop being fatigued."
- "I just need to think positively."
- "I want to see myself more clearly."
- "I want to feel less stuck."
- "How do I become more optimistic?"
- "How do I focus?"

Each of these is reasonable. None of them work, because they are addressing the surface and leaving the cause running.

"They're constantly working on the symptoms, but they're not saying, 'Oh, what is it like to not speak to myself negatively? What is it like to move my emotions? What is it like to put myself constantly in places where my nervous system can relax? How do I stop believing the voice in my head?' Those things are what are far more likely to solve the problem than trying to say, how do I become more optimistic? Or how do I see myself more clearly? Or how do I focus? Or how do I not be fatigued? And so that's typically why people think it's really hard is because they're treating the symptoms instead of the cause." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

## The Root-Cause Questions

The reframe Joe offers replaces the symptom questions with cause questions:

| Symptom Question | Root-Cause Question |
|---|---|
| How do I become more optimistic? | What is it like to not speak to myself negatively? |
| How do I get unstuck? | What is it like to move my emotions? |
| How do I not be fatigued? | What is it like to put myself in places where my nervous system can relax? |
| How do I see myself more clearly? | How do I stop believing the voice in my head? |
| How do I focus better? | What is the negative self-talk costing me? |

The cause questions point to the work. The symptom questions point at the surface that the work would dissolve as a side effect.

## The Meta-Symptom: "There's Something Wrong With Me"

The deepest symptom that masquerades as a cause is the premise that there is something wrong with the person that needs to be fixed. This premise is the depression.

"The other thing I would say is that most, the other way to present the same thing you're saying is, you are -- in every way you've tried to fix yourself, you've assumed there's something wrong with you and that you need to be fixed, and this is how you're going to fix it, and that won't work." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

Brett's articulation in the same conversation: "the very premise of that, there's something wrong with me, is the exact kind of thought that triggers the fear in the nervous system, triggers the self-attack." Joe's response: "it literally is the depression. There's a way to think of it as there's something wrong with me is the depression. And so any fight to make, make yourself better is part of the self-fulfilling prophecy of depression."

This is structurally the same as [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]]: the work to fix the perceived brokenness reinforces the perceived brokenness. The fight to make yourself better is the depression.

## Can You Have a Plan?

The question this raises: does this mean no plan, no work, no method?

Joe's answer: you can have a plan. It just cannot start from "there is something wrong with me that I have to fix."

"You can have that. It just can't start with the assumption of there's something wrong with me that I have to fix. If it's, oh, my voice in the head is not competent and I need to help the voice in the head become competent, that's a very different approach than there's something wrong with me that I have to fix." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

The reframe: from "I am broken and need to be fixed" to "the voice in my head is using bad management techniques and needs better ones." The work continues. The premise changes. The reframe is the same as in [[voice-as-care]] and [[inherent-goodness]].

## Why This Distinction Matters For an AI Coach

The most common failure mode in coaching depression is to accept the person's framing ("how do I become more positive?") and try to help with that framing. The accepting compounds the problem. The AI coach has to notice that the question is a symptom question and gently reorient to the cause.

The reorientation is not a correction or a lecture. It is the move from "let's work on that" to "before we work on that, can we look at what's underneath that?" The person often experiences the reorientation as a relief because they have been failing at the symptom-fix for years and the cause-frame opens a door they did not know existed.

## The Selective Data Layer

A subtle version of the symptom-treatment trap: the depressed person says their negative thoughts have no data. The partial truth is that they do have data -- but it is selective data. Every negative thought has some basis in something true. The trap is treating the trueness of the data as evidence the thought is accurate.

"There's the data, like you're 40 and you don't own a house, right? And so that's true and it's data, but it's very selective data. It's not like -- it's not seeing everything that you have done, right? It's always looking at the negative side of the equation and never looking at the positive side of the equation. It's -- you're treating yourself the way you wouldn't treat somebody else typically." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

This is the symptom (negative thinking) that points at the cause (the inner critic is selectively pulling negative data). Treating the symptom would be "find positive data to counter the negative." Treating the cause is examining the selection mechanism itself: why is the voice only looking at one side? See [[lawyer-the-voice]].

## When This Distinction Applies

Beyond depression, this distinction has the same shape in other domains:

- Anxiety as a symptom of unfelt emotion vs. anxiety as the thing to manage
- Overwhelm as a symptom of broken flow vs. overwhelm as the problem
- Self-criticism as a symptom of held shame vs. self-criticism as something to argue with
- Procrastination as a symptom of avoidance of feeling vs. procrastination as a discipline problem

In each case, the symptom-treatment approach produces partial relief and reinforces the underlying cause. See [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]] for the broader principle.
