# Safety That Connects to Reality vs. Safety That Disconnects From Reality

## Definition

Safety is not a state of the world. Nothing is actually safe; the word names a story we construct to feel comfortable enough to act. That construction can be done well or badly. Done well, it brings the person into deeper contact with reality. Done badly, it disconnects them from reality so they can do something they otherwise couldn't.

"Safety is an illusion. Nothing is safe. We will call something safe if we feel comfortable, if we feel like we can be in flow, if we feel like we are capable of navigating the environment... if we feel like we are going to survive it." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The same word, "safety," refers to two opposite things depending on which function is being performed. The discrimination is the work.

## Safety as Contact With Reality

The first function: the person assesses the actual situation -- their capabilities, the environment, the variables, the worst-case outcomes -- and arrives at a felt sense that they can navigate what is in front of them. The safety here is the integration of full information into a body-felt readiness. Nothing has been hidden. Nothing has been dissociated. The body knows what it knows, the mind knows what it knows, and the readiness includes both.

Brett's BASE jumping practice is this form: imagining the jump in full detail, including the possible failures, feeling what each failure would feel like, integrating all of that, and arriving at a state where the body is genuinely prepared for any of the outcomes.

"As I'm preparing to do something, allowing myself to feel what it would be like to have that occur and recover from it, have that occur and not recover from it, have that occur and have that last, oh shit, moment before impact. And just having all of that, like all of that possibility moving through my body and integrating is something that allows me to, when I actually make the jump, if something goes a little bit awry, I know like my body knows what to do." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

This connects to [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] as a practice. The safety it produces is real because the worst-case outcomes have been pre-felt and integrated. The person is not avoiding the consequences of their action; they are operating with the consequences fully present.

## Safety as Dissociation From Reality

The second function: the person needs to act, the action carries risk, and they cannot face the risk. The mind constructs a story that the situation is safe so the body can proceed. The story does not match reality; it pacifies the part of the system that would otherwise refuse.

The clearest case: a person stands at the exit of a BASE jump they shouldn't make. They are afraid of disappointing the group more than they are afraid of the jump. The body holds the information that something is off. The mind builds a story -- "I'll be fine, this is safe" -- that lets them jump.

"Let's say that I really want to feel safe and make this jump because I'm with a group of people and I don't want them to have to climb down this mountain with me that we just climbed up... So I'm more afraid of disappointing people and not being seen than I am of the jump. Then I might just dissociate myself from some of the risks of the jump so that I reach this point where I'm like, aha, okay, I'm safe." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The "aha, okay, I'm safe" is the moment dissociation completes. The body's signal is no longer in awareness. The person acts. The risk does not change because the awareness has been dimmed; the outcome will be whatever the actual conditions produce, but the person will be less able to respond if something goes wrong because they have already turned off their contact with what is real.

## The Discrimination

The two forms feel similar from inside if the person is not paying attention. Both produce a sense of "okay, I can do this." The discrimination requires noticing which way the felt sense was reached.

**Safety as contact:** the felt sense arrived after the person looked at everything, including the worst-case outcomes. Anxieties were welcomed. Red flags were addressed or named as accepted risks. The body knows what is being faced.

**Safety as dissociation:** the felt sense arrived because something specific was put aside. There is a topic, a variable, a fear, a person's voice in the room that is not being included. The mind is working to produce the okay-ness rather than receiving it.

Brett's diagnostic question:

"How do you distinguish? A lot, something that we've said a lot in the sport... is that the other side of the coin of fear is excitement. And if I'm feeling a grounded level of excitement and anticipation, and I feel like my body is amped in such a way that is like metabolically prepared for what I'm about to do. And I feel that I'm in flow, and I feel like flow is accessible to me. And I'm also continuing -- I'm not just reaching that state because I'm closing, putting on the blinders. I'm reaching that state because I'm welcoming any red flags that there might be." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The somatic version of the question: is the body amped (contact) or bouncy (dissociation)? See [[grounded-excitement-vs-ungrounded-excitement]] for the full somatic discrimination.

The cognitive version: what am I not letting myself think about? If there is a topic that gets steered around, the safety has been constructed by exclusion. The excluded topic is the dissociation.

## Why This Matters More Than the Word "Safe"

The conventional use of "safe" treats it as a binary descriptor of the situation: this is safe, that isn't. Brett's framing replaces the binary with a question about the relationship between the person and the situation: how is the safety being produced?

This is why the conventional question "is this BASE jump safe?" has no answer. The answer is always "no" if safe means "no risk." The useful question is: is your relationship to this jump in contact with reality, or is it dissociated from reality? The risk is what it is. The variable is the person's relationship to the risk.

The same applies to business decisions. "Is this deal safe?" is the wrong question. "Are we in contact with the actual risks, or are we constructing a story about why we don't have to look at them?" is the right one.

Brett, on what people in the community keep asking:

"People will start to identify as like, oh, I'm a safe jumper, that person's not a safe jumper. Or this is a safe kind of jump and that's not a safe kind of jump. Or, is this jump safe for a beginner jumper like me? The answer is no to all of those things. But the answer is like, well, what are the risks and how do you as an organism interact with your environment given those conditions?" -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The question shifts from a property of the situation to a property of the relationship. That is the move.

## The Connection to Joe's "Safety" Skepticism

Joe has independently arrived at a parallel skepticism about the word "safety" -- though by a different route. See [[trust-vs-safety]]: the word safety is too available for manipulation in interpersonal contexts. "I don't feel safe" is often used as a demand for others to change.

Brett's frame about safety as a mental construction explains why the word is so manipulable. Safety is not a thing in the world; it is a story we tell. Stories can be wielded. Trust, by contrast, points at something more concrete (a track record, a pattern of behavior, the actual reliability of the person) and resists being wielded.

The two skepticisms converge: in interpersonal contexts, the word safety is used to control. In personal decision contexts, the idea of safety is used to dissociate. The cleaner orientation in both cases is to look at what is actually being said or felt under the safety frame.

## What Replaces "Is This Safe?"

The questions that produce real information:

1. **What are the risks here?** Real ones, not the ones I'm comfortable thinking about.
2. **What would have to happen for the worst-case outcome to occur?** Visualize and feel.
3. **Do I have the body history -- the experience, the reps, the rehearsed responses -- for what I'd need to do if something went wrong?**
4. **What am I not talking about right now?** Whatever the answer is, that is the dissociation.
5. **Is the excitement I feel grounded or bouncy?** See [[grounded-excitement-vs-ungrounded-excitement]].

A person who can sit with these questions and remain present is in contact with reality. The "safety" they arrive at is the real kind. A person who needs to skip a question to produce the felt sense of okay-ness is constructing the dissociative kind.
