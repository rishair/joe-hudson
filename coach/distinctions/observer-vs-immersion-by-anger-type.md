# Observer Position vs. Immersion in Story (by Anger Type)

## The Distinction

The general teaching about anger release includes both:
- Take the observer position. Be the actor playing the angry person. See [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]].
- Let the anger move. Let the story come out.

These instructions sound contradictory. They are not. They are prescriptions for different kinds of people. The person who needs the observer position is the opposite person from the one who needs to drop into the story.

"For somebody who goes into freeze or flight, being in the observer position is gonna completely shut off anger. They're just not gonna be able to access it. So for those people, they're gonna need to go into the story a bit. They're gonna need to believe the story a bit until the anger can move and then they can listen to their body. Whereas a person who's like believes that everybody is screwed up and they need to get angry to fix the situation or that it's highly okay that they're angry, then the best thing for them is to actually move into the awareness position to see themselves being angry." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The instruction is inverse to the habit.

## The Two Cases

### Case 1: Habitual Freeze / Flight / Suppression

A person whose anger usually does not arrive. They suppress before the feeling forms. The body has trained itself to never enter the felt state. When asked to "be the observer," the observer is the suppressing apparatus itself. Taking the observer position is the suppression, not a stance against it.

For this person, the observer position kills the anger. The work is the opposite: drop into the story enough to feel the anger. Believe it for a moment. Let the feeling rise. Then, once the body has moved through, listen to what the body says.

The story is the entry door. Without entering the story, the body cannot find the door at all.

### Case 2: Habitual Anger / Story-Belief / Dysregulation

A person whose anger arrives quickly and often. They believe the stories anger tells. They have no trouble entering the felt state; they have trouble exiting it. When the anger arrives, it takes them with it.

For this person, dropping further into the story is dangerous. They are already in the story. The work is the opposite: take the observer position. Be the actor playing the angry person. Watch the anger move through without being captured.

The observer is the missing piece. Without the observer, the person dysregulates (see [[dysregulated-vs-released-into-freedom]]).

## Why the Instructions Look Contradictory

Most teachings about anger come from one of these two stances and apply universally what works for one type.

The "let it out, get into it, be loud" school works for the suppression type. It is dangerous for the dysregulation type, who needs less "into it" and more witness.

The "stay present, observe, do not get carried away" school works for the dysregulation type. It is suppressive for the freeze type, who needs more "into it," not more observation.

Joe's teaching includes both because both kinds of people exist. The correct prescription depends on the type. The AI coach has to read the type before prescribing.

## How to Read the Type

The diagnostic question: which way does the person fail when they try to release?

- If they cannot get into the anger at all (it stays intellectual, the body is quiet, the voice is restrained): they are the freeze type. They need to drop into the story.
- If they get into the anger and lose themselves (dysregulation, story-belief, saying things they did not mean): they are the dysregulation type. They need the observer position.

The form of repression they habitually use is also informative. See [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]]:
- Form 1 (inward) and Form 2 (sideways) tend to need to drop into the story.
- Form 3 (outward) tends to need the observer position.

The origin is also informative. See [[three-childhood-origins-of-anger-repression]]:
- Origin 3 (punished for anger) usually needs to drop into the story.
- Origin 2b (anger got attention) usually needs the observer.

These are heuristics, not certainties. The person's actual response to early release attempts is the ground truth.

## The Combined Picture

Both kinds of people eventually need both stances. The work over time is to develop:
- The capacity to drop into the felt state (for the freeze type who lacks it).
- The capacity to hold the witness during the felt state (for the dysregulation type who loses it).

The starting point differs. The destination is the same: anger that can be fully felt without believing its stories.

## The Move-Specific Version

The story-immersion practice for the freeze type:
- Start by responding to the thoughts. "I have listened to you for a decade; I am going to listen to anger for 20 minutes." (See [[convert-thought-to-time-bounded-listening]].)
- Use anger music. Joe mentions Tool as one option.
- Fake the anger if needed (see [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]).
- Once the body has the feeling, listen to it. The story does its work; then the body takes over.

The observer practice for the dysregulation type:
- "Be an actor playing the role of you getting angry."
- Notice yourself being angry while being angry.
- Aim for "a good performance" rather than a true verdict.
- The part of awareness that knows this is a part is the part to keep awake.

## A Subtle Point: Both Have to Be Happening

For either type, eventually both have to be present.

"Either you're playing the actor or you're listening to the anger, there's a part of you that is outside of the process. And the anger moves a lot quicker if there's some part of you outside of the process and the anger is moving. Both two things have to be happening." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The anger has to be moving. A part of you has to be outside the process. The two together produce clean release.

For the freeze type, the work is on the "anger has to be moving" side. The "outside the process" part is already too much in play.

For the dysregulation type, the work is on the "outside the process" side. The "anger is moving" part is already in play.

The full capacity is both, simultaneously.

## In Coaching

Before prescribing the observer position to anyone, check whether they are the kind of person who needs it. Asking "what happens when you try to feel anger?" usually reveals the type. If the answer is "nothing," they need to enter the story. If the answer is "I lose control," they need the observer.

The wrong prescription compounds the problem:
- Telling the freeze type to "stay present and observe" gives them another way to suppress.
- Telling the dysregulation type to "let it all out" gives them another reason to capture themselves.

## Tone

Joe is precise but not heavy about this. The contradiction in the teaching is not a contradiction; it is a recognition that different bodies need different doors.
