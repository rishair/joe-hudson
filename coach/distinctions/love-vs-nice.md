# Love vs. Nice

## The Distinction

Nice is a behavior aimed at maintaining the appearance of harmony. Love is a deep welcoming that often requires speaking the hard truth. Confusing the two collapses love into people-pleasing.

"A lot of times people mistake being nice because they think that if they love somebody, there's not going to be conflict or something like that. But that's just not how love works." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## The Two Sides

**Nice:**
- Avoids conflict.
- Says what the other wants to hear.
- Protects the relationship from rupture by withholding truth.
- Performs care to manage the situation.
- The deliverer feels safe; the receiver gets pleasantness.
- Can be motivated by the deliverer's fear, not by love at all.

**Love (in this work):**
- Welcomes the other deeply.
- Says what is true, even when it is hard.
- Trusts the relationship to handle the truth.
- Comes from an open heart, not from a strategic stance.
- The deliverer may risk something. The receiver gets something real.
- Often looks sharp from the outside.

"Being compassionate is often a very sharp sword. Being compassionate is often saying a hard truth in a loving way with an open heart." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

## The Illustration: Alex Bell Told Joe He Lied

The reference case is from Joe's own teenage life. See [[examples/alex-bell-told-joe-he-lied]] for the full story.

In a single sentence, near the end of freshman year, a peer named Alex Bell told Joe: "Joe, we all know that you're lying all the time, and we would all like you so much more if you didn't."

Joe describes the moment as the most profound act of love he had experienced to that date. Joe's compulsive lying, which had been a fixed feature of his social behavior, stopped almost completely.

"That sure as fuck wasn't nice." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The act qualified as love because of three features:
1. It was true.
2. It was delivered with an open heart (Joe did not feel attacked; he felt seen).
3. It was costly for the giver. Alex was the only person in Joe's social world who said it.

Nice would have been to keep quiet, to laugh along, to maintain the surface. Nice would not have produced the change. Love did.

## What Nice Costs

People who default to nice do not get to give the gift Alex Bell gave Joe. They protect themselves from the discomfort of speaking the truth, and in doing so deprive the people around them of the welcoming that comes through truthful contact.

Nice also creates a relationship in which the other person cannot trust what is said. If you are always pleasant, your pleasantness carries no information. The kind word from someone who can also say the hard word means something. The kind word from someone who only ever says kind words means nothing.

This is why people-pleasing erodes intimacy. See [[people-pleasing]] (when this article exists). The pleasing partner protects themselves from the risk of truth, and the relationship slowly hollows.

## The Same Collapse Happens to "Compassion"

The same false equivalence that collapses love into niceness also collapses compassion into niceness. The three words tend to fuse: love = compassion = nice. They are not the same.

"Oftentimes what we think of compassion is, like, being nice. We think of being loving as being nice." -- Joe Hudson, The Boy Who Taught Me Truth

Joe specifies the contexts where he sees the collapse happen most clearly: online discourse (Twitter) and coaching environments. Both reward looking virtuous. The easiest way to look loving or compassionate without risking anything is to refuse to say anything that might upset anyone. The refusal gets praised. The actual love or compassion goes missing.

The two-part definition of compassion is the same as the test for love-not-nice: open heart AND truth. See [[compassion-as-truth-from-open-heart]] for the dedicated treatment.

## What This Does Not Mean

This distinction does not justify cruelty, harshness, or brutality dressed up as honesty. Many people who think they are "telling the hard truth" are actually venting or attacking.

The test is the same as for [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]: does the heart stay open while speaking the truth? Is the welcoming intact? If the truth is being delivered from a closed heart, from righteousness, from contempt, that is not love either. It is just the inverse of nice.

The sharp word from an open heart is love. The sharp word from a closed heart is just sharp.

It also does not mean every hard truth should be spoken at every moment. Discernment about timing, capacity, and consent still applies. The distinction is not "speak every hard thing always." It is "do not confuse withholding the hard thing with loving."

## In Coaching

When someone says "I just want to be nice to them," check whether nice is being used as a synonym for love, and check whether it is a cover for fear of conflict.

When someone is afraid that loving means they will not be able to say hard things, name the distinction. Love includes hard things. Love often is the hard thing.

When someone is being people-pleasing and calling it loving, the move is often to invite them into what they are afraid would happen if they spoke the truth. Usually the fear is rejection. Welcoming the fear of rejection is upstream of being able to risk the rejection.

## Connection to Mothers and Boundaries

A common cultural belief: love does not hold boundaries. Joe's counterexample: great mothers, who are the cultural archetype of love, hold boundaries constantly.

"Love is holding boundaries. Great mothers hold boundaries all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

Gandhi held boundaries. Mother Teresa held boundaries. The figures held up as examples of unconditional love were not nice in the conflict-avoidant sense. They were welcoming and uncompromising at the same time.
