# Intention vs. Goal

## The Two Sides

**Goal:** A specific outcome with a timeline, a measurement, and weight. Carries the implication that failing to hit the goal in the way specified is failure. Carries pressure. Often comes with a should attached.

**Intention:** Holding a direction in your consciousness without the weight. Like looking at a map and saying "Los Angeles is where I want to be." No grip on when, no grip on which road. Just a held knowing of the direction.

"I don't call it a goal, I don't call it a mandate. It's just holding it in your consciousness that this is where I want to be. It's like looking at a map and saying Los Angeles is where I want to be, or San Francisco is where I want to be. It doesn't require any more weight than that." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## Why It Matters

The implementation-agnostic quality is the whole point. An intention says "I am going there" without specifying when, how, or by which path. This frees the person to take whatever route emerges, to iterate, to adjust based on what is actually happening.

The Stockdale paradox illustrates the failure mode of goals with timelines. The POWs who got out were the ones who held the intention of freedom. The ones who died were the optimists who attached the intention to specific dates: "out by Christmas," "out by Easter." When the date passed and they were still in the camp, the framework collapsed and they collapsed with it. See [[pow-optimist]].

The intention survives any specific failed attempt. The goal-with-timeline does not.

## What an Intention Looks Like

An intention is a direction held lightly enough to:
- Take any of many possible paths
- Survive setbacks that would crush a goal
- Be expressed in the present tense rather than the future ("I am a person who...") See [[acting-as-if-already-true]]
- Be carried as orientation rather than burden

A goal often looks like:
- "I will hit $X by Y date"
- "I should have completed Z by next month"
- "If I don't get there, I have failed"

## Attitude Toward the Goal

A given goal can be held with many different internal stances. Each one produces different results in terms of energy, flexibility, and likelihood of arrival.

"You're running a company and you have a revenue goal of $100 million. You can hold that as I should get to $100 million. You can hold that goal as I want to get to $100 million. You can hold that goal as I will get to $100 million. You can hold that goal as I can't wait till I get to $100 million." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

Each attitude affects:
- Energy available for the work
- Rigidity in the approach
- Flexibility in the face of pivots
- Enjoyment of the process

The "I should" attitude is the most expensive and produces the worst long-term outcomes (though it can work short-term through force). The "I want" attitude is more efficient and more enjoyable. The "I will" attitude is more flexible than "I should" but more rigid than "I want." The "I can't wait" attitude carries the most energy but can shade into craving.

"You're not going to get there without the goal, but the most efficient attitude to get to the goal is to be in the want of it, not the should of it. It is to be in the enjoyment of it, not the rigidity of it." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Attachment vs. Intention

Attachment is the layer that gets added on top of intention. The intention is the want, the direction, the heading. The attachment is the grip on a specific form of arrival.

"You can have that intention without that craving, without that deep attachment. And if you don't have it, you're you're lucky to get anywhere. So that intention is really quite important. But if you're going to put attachment on top of that intention, on top of that want, then you are dragging. Then you are like throwing an anchor out and sailing across the ocean with your anchor out." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

Attached success is possible. Many executives succeed through deep attachment. But it is the slowest and least enjoyable route. The most efficient version is intention without attachment. See [[wanting-vs-craving]] for the related internal distinction.

## The Bigger Why

Successful CEOs that Joe has observed almost universally have an intention that points beyond the immediate goal. They are not attached to making money. They are attached to being the best, beating the competition, reducing carbon, dominating customer service. The money is the necessary step to the deeper attachment.

"If you look at every single CEO that I know who has been very, very successful, their intention wasn't to make money. They weren't attached to making money. What they were attached to was being the best or beating their competition or reducing carbon in the world or being the best at customer service. They had some sort of intention that got -- that was past this intention of just succeeding." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The mechanism: if you are attached to succeeding itself, the attachment grips the immediate outcome and produces rigidity. If you are attached to something beyond the immediate goal, the immediate goal becomes a step in service of the deeper intention, and the grip releases. The deeper why frees the work.

This is also why intention-based goals are more pivot-friendly. The intention "reduce carbon" survives the failure of any specific company. The goal "build this exact carbon product" does not.

## The Connection to Process

Intention pairs naturally with [[process-over-result]]. You hold the intention as orientation. You commit to the process of moving in that direction. You release attachment to any specific outcome arriving by any specific time. The form is allowed to change.

In business: hold the intention of building something valuable. Release the goal of "$10M ARR by Q3." Notice the difference in how the team operates. The intention orients without strangling. The goal often produces the very tension that prevents the result.

## What Someone Experiences

You can tell which one you are holding by checking the body. An intention sits in the chest as direction. A goal sits in the gut as pressure. An intention allows curiosity about how it will unfold. A goal produces anxiety about whether it will be met on schedule.

When you notice yourself bracing for a deadline that you set yourself, that is a goal. When you notice yourself moving toward something with steady, unhurried orientation, that is an intention.
