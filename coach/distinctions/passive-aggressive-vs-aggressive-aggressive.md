# Passive-Aggressive vs. Aggressive-Aggressive

## The Distinction

Both are anger aimed at a person. The difference is whether the anger is acknowledged as anger.

**Aggressive-aggressive** is open. "You're being a jerk." "I'm furious at you." The anger is named. The recipient knows what is happening. There is a thing in the room that can be met.

**Passive-aggressive** is covert. The anger is delivered through language that disclaims it. "I don't feel safe." "We didn't make introductions." "I just have some concerns." The aggression is real but it is wrapped in the language of safety, care, or process. The recipient cannot directly meet what is in the room because what is in the room is unacknowledged.

## Why the Distinction Matters in Coaching

Joe is on record that aggressive-aggressive is easier to work with. When someone is openly angry, the coach (or partner, or facilitator) can meet the anger directly: "Tell me about it. What's the wisdom in your anger? What do you care about?" The anger arrives at an open hand and tends to soften.

"I was really good when somebody was just like outright angry. They'd be like, 'Da da da da da.' And I'd be like, 'Yeah, tell me about it.' Like, please, I want to hear it. What's the wisdom in your anger? What do you care about that's making you like this? Like, I want to respect that. And they get angry, and then they would see that they were met, and like, it would all be done." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The passive-aggressive form is harder to meet because the aggression is denied by the form. Trying to meet "I don't feel safe" as anger requires first naming that it is anger, which the speaker may reject.

"But the ones that trapped me, especially early on, were the passive-aggressive ones, the ones where the aggression was done through the idea of safety, or, you know, we didn't make introductions, and so I don't feel safe. And it was hard for me to learn how to deal with that because there was just kind of this subtle attack that wasn't being acknowledged as an attack." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

## Why Passive Aggression Is the More Common Kink

People who have been hurt by anger as children often learn that direct anger is dangerous. They suppress the direct form and discover that the indirect form gets the same work done with less risk. Over time, this becomes the available expression of anger.

"There's a series of people out there who've really been hurt by another person's anger, and they are going to be upset that anybody is saying that anger could be a beautiful and good thing. And most likely because they've been hurt so bad by anger that they're not going to be aggressive about it. They'll be passive-aggressive about it. And so the most likely thing that we'll get is a series of subtle passive-aggressive attacks, you know, built under like, 'I don't feel safe with this episode.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

Both are kinked anger. See [[kinked-emotions]]. The passive form is just a more thoroughly disguised kink.

## What Passive Aggression Costs the Group

In a group setting (a workshop, a team, a family), passive aggression does damage that direct anger does not. The aggression is real, the group senses it, but no one can address what was not named. The energy hangs unaddressed and the work of the group slows.

"It would undermine the group, undermine everybody else's ability to get things done and to learn what they were there to learn." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

This is part of why the passive form is harder than the direct form. Direct anger spends itself when met. Indirect anger lingers because there is nothing concrete to spend.

## The Move That Works

Joe's move with passive aggression: invite the person to convert it to direct aggression. As an experiment.

"What I learned was to just say, 'Hey, great. I really hear that you're upset with something I've done. Can you help me by just being really just direct and angry with me about it?' Just as an experiment. Like just instead of being passive-aggressive, be aggressive-aggressive. And as soon as I learned that, you know, and if they were game, they would do that. And then they would have this big smile on their face and I'd have a big smile on their face and they would feel the freedom of actually getting in touch with their anger." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

Several things happen at once in this move:
- The unspoken aggression is named without the person being shamed.
- They are given permission to express it directly.
- The frame of "experiment" makes it lower-stakes.
- The actual experience of being met in direct anger often is a relief.

See [[invite-the-aggression-out]] for the move treated separately.

## The Key Caveat: Not Recommending Anger At People

This distinction is about identifying the kink. Joe is not recommending people get angry at other people. The point is that when anger is going to arrive at a person anyway (because it has been held), the open form is easier to meet and complete than the covert form. The fuller answer remains: release the anger in private (see [[get-angry-in-the-car]]), then have the conversation from clarity.

## In Coaching

When a person describes a conflict in which they "don't feel safe" or have "concerns," check whether anger is present underneath. The presence of safety-language about a non-physical situation often signals passive aggression.

When a person describes being on the receiving end of passive aggression and not knowing how to respond, the move is to make it visible: "I'm hearing that something is upsetting you. Can you say it directly?"

When a person reports they have a "team member who undermines everything quietly," check whether the team has a way to surface direct disagreement. Teams that punish direct anger train passive aggression.

## Tone

Both forms are kinked. Neither is morally worse than the other. The distinction is mechanical, not ethical. Joe describes his own experience of struggling with passive aggression matter-of-factly, not as a complaint.

- When direct anger has been suppressed and the system needs an alternative outlet.
