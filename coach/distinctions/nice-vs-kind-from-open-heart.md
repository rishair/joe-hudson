# Nice and Polite vs. Kind From an Open Heart

## The Distinction

Kindness comes from an open heart. The action arises because love is moving through you and reaches the other person. There is nothing being managed. Nothing is being performed.

Niceness and politeness are social-contract behaviors. The action arises because you are following a code about how people are supposed to be. The behavior may look the same as kindness from the outside. From the inside, it is being executed because of a rule, not because of love.

The distinction matters because the niceness/politeness register is the cultural condition in which passive aggression breeds. The kindness register is not.

"There's acting being kind because you have an open heart and you want to be kind. And then there's like being polite and being nice. And I noticed the more nice and polite a culture is, oftentimes the more passive-aggressive — passive aggression is built in." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Why Niceness Produces Passive Aggression

Niceness is governed by a code about what can and cannot be said. The code typically prohibits direct anger, direct disagreement, direct discomfort. When something arises that the code prohibits, the person cannot say it. The energy has to go somewhere. It goes into the indirect forms: gossip, talking behind backs, sarcasm, undermining, guilt-tripping.

"That very nice, everybody's very nice to us in this neighborhood, and like, that's the neighborhood where there's going to be a shit ton of gossip. That's the neighborhood where there's going to be a lot of people talking about each other behind their back." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The gossip is not a contradiction of the niceness. It is the niceness, in its other face. The two are the same system.

Kindness from an open heart does not produce this. When real disagreement is welcome, when anger is welcome, when discomfort is welcome, the energy has its direct channels. The covert forms do not need to develop.

"Whereas cultures where that isn't like, there's no expectation of civility, niceness, like in that way, if it's not heartfelt and it's like, yeah, it's totally okay to say, goddammit, that I'm upset with you. Then there's going to be like less passive aggression." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## The Test

The body knows the difference. Kindness from an open heart feels expansive — the giver feels something move through them. Niceness feels obligatory — the giver feels they are executing a behavior they are supposed to execute. Underneath niceness there is often a faint sense of resentment, accumulating across many small interactions.

The receiver also knows. Kindness from an open heart lands as connection. Niceness lands as something that does not quite touch you — pleasant, expected, ungenuine. Many people cannot articulate why "nice" people sometimes feel distancing. The reason is that nothing is actually being given. The form is being performed.

## The Niceness Contract

The deeper feature of nice cultures: they operate on an implicit contract about what is acceptable to do or say. Violating the contract incurs social cost. The contract may have nothing to do with whether anyone is being harmed — it is about how things look.

A specific instance Joe describes: he and his wife were spacing out at a coffee shop, which a couple nearby read as staring at them. The couple left muttering "that was so rude." Joe could see both sides — in their part of society, being looked at unwantedly registered as a violation. In other parts of society, the same behavior would be unremarkable.

"They're speaking about us because we were just staring out into the air, but we were staring in their direction. And so they felt washed. And I totally appreciate how like that, that I like, I could see someone going, yeah, that was, that's rude. But that's like, that's one of those like, oh, this is a societal norm that we have a contract in our little part of society that that's not okay." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The point is not that any specific contract is wrong. The point is that to the extent you are bound by the contract — to the extent it determines what you can and cannot do — you will accumulate passive aggression as you go.

"The more of those kind of like norms, the more of that like setup where this is what I can do and this is what I can't do. And it gets more and more complicated. Often you're going to get more passive aggression." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## What It Takes to Be Kind Rather Than Nice

The shift requires being willing to violate the niceness contract when violation is honest. This is scary. The contracts have social enforcement behind them. Choosing kindness over niceness means choosing the risk that you will be seen as rude, difficult, ungrateful, weird.

"If you don't want to do that, then you have to feel like you're not actually obligated to those norms, which is really, really scary." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The Buddhist temptation story Joe references: one of the temptations the Buddha faced was the temptation of societal norms — the reputation question, the "what will people think of you" pull. Falling for the temptation is what binds you to the niceness contract. Seeing through it is what frees you to be kind rather than nice.

The discrimination matters: "I can believe like, oh, if I hurt somebody, that's not okay, that's moral. But if it's just like these arbitrary rules that have been set up so that people can stay comfortable, if you feel those, then you're gonna, you're bound to be more and more passive-aggressive and be caught up in it."

There is real ethics. There is also the arbitrary surface of cultural rules. The work is to distinguish.

## The Migration Phenomenon

A predictable pattern: people who grow up inside niceness cultures often migrate to cities or scenes where the contract is looser. They want to make their own social norms because the inherited ones felt suffocating.

"I think our cities are full of young people who are like, I don't want to live in that fucking passive-aggressive society in the Midwest or in the South or in fucking Modesto, whatever. I don't want to live in that. And so they come to a place where they hope that they can find more freedom and make their own social norms." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

This is not a universal verdict on any specific region. It is the observation that the more elaborate the niceness contract, the stronger the impulse becomes for some people to leave it.

## In Coaching

When a person describes being chronically frustrated in their social or family environment, check for the niceness-vs-kindness distinction. If everyone is "very nice" and the person feels something is off, the passive aggression is usually built into the system, not arising from one bad actor.

When a person is trying to be kind and cannot understand why their kindness is not landing, check whether they are running niceness instead. The receiver feels the difference even when the giver cannot.

When a person reports they are "being too direct" or "people don't like that I say what I mean" — this is often the signal that they are in a niceness culture and that the discomfort they are causing is the discomfort of the contract being violated. The honest move is not necessarily to retreat into niceness. Sometimes it is to stay direct.

## Distinguishing From [[caring-vs-caretaking]]

This distinction sits next to [[caring-vs-caretaking]] but is not the same. Caring vs caretaking is about whether the helping action is being driven by love or by obligation/anxiety. Nice vs kind is about whether the social action is being driven by an open heart or by adherence to a code. The two distinctions interact: caretaking is often the niceness form of caring. But the underlying axes are different.

## Tone

Joe is somewhat dry when describing this. There is mild amusement when he names that the very nice neighborhood is also the gossip neighborhood. The teaching is not a condemnation of nice people. It is a structural observation: the form has a predictable downstream effect, and seeing the effect lets you choose differently.
