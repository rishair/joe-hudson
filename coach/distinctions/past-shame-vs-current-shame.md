# Past Shame vs. Current Shame

## The Confusion

People treat all shame as the same thing. It is not. Current shame and past shame have opposite functions. Collapsing them together leads to either suppressing all shame (which removes a useful signal) or wallowing in all shame (which locks patterns in place).

## The Distinction

**Current shame** is a signal that what you are doing right now is out of alignment with who you are. It functions like a compass. If you are stealing from someone, current shame tells you to stop. It is useful, immediate, and action-oriented.

"Current shame is actually quite useful. It's our signal that what we're doing right now isn't what we want to be doing. It's not in alignment with who we are." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

**Past shame** is the belief that brutalizing yourself over something you already did will prevent you from doing it again. It is a form of resistance to what happened, and resistance perpetuates the pattern. Studies consistently show that self-brutalization does not change future behavior. It condemns you to repeat the pattern.

"Past shame is this idea that if I brutalize myself, then it'll somehow change the way that I act in the future, which tons of studies show that that's just not true. And it's not really useful. And it's what really condemns us to repeat that pattern. It's a form of resistance, and so that pattern persists." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

## Why This Matters for Vulnerability

When someone is vulnerable about past actions, most people receive it with acceptance. The person reveals a past failure, and others can see they are doing the work. Past shame dissolves in [[vulnerability]] because others can hold it without needing to punish.

Current shame is different. If someone says "I am stealing from you right now," others are less likely to immediately accept. This is appropriate. Current shame is a call to change behavior, not to confess and be absolved.

Joe references movies set in prison ("The Work" and "What I Want My Words to Say to You") where convicted killers share their innermost experiences. The audience feels empathy, not condemnation, because the person is vulnerably facing their past shame rather than defending it.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is stuck in [[shame-drives-behavior]] cycles, the shame holding the pattern is almost always past shame.
- When someone confuses self-punishment with accountability.
- When vulnerability about past actions gets confused with ongoing harmful behavior.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Releasing past shame through vulnerability allows the behavior to change. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].
- Recognizing current shame as useful allows someone to act on it rather than suppress or wallow.
- Connects to [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: past shame is often the unfelt emotion holding the trauma pattern.
