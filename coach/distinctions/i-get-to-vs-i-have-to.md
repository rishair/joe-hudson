# "I Get To" vs. "I Have To" (The Lens That Reveals Whether You Love It)

## The Distinction

The same action can be lived as "I get to" or as "I have to." The action looks identical from the outside. The felt experience is opposite. The "I get to" version is alive, fueling, and sustainable. The "I have to" version is depleting, joyless, and corrosive over time.

The lens is one of the cleanest practical tools for breaking the grip of obligation. Asking yourself "do I want this?" before each significant action surfaces the obligation when it is operating and gives you the chance to either:

1. Reframe the action as a get-to (when you actually do want it but have been calling it a have-to), or
2. Recognize that you do not want it and decide what to do with that information.

"Put every one of those things through the frame of I get to choose this. Do I want to do this or do I not want to do it? As soon as like, oh, I have to do it because I need the money. Right. So to just put it through the frame of do I want this is a huge thing." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Same Job, the Same Marriage, the Same Art

The most powerful application is that the lens does not require changing your actions. A person who feels obligated to make money can keep making money, but from "I want to." A person who feels obligated to stay married can keep staying married, but from "I want to." A person who feels obligated to make art professionally can keep making art professionally, but from "I want to."

Joe's example from artist friends: Some artists who got record deals or gallery shows felt obligated, lost their love, and "bit the dust." Others were given the same opportunity and stayed in love with it. "I fucking still love it. I love getting up and playing rock and roll, or I love the painting that I'm doing. I feel so lucky that I get to do it. And even though there was an obligation to make an album and to go on tour, it never felt like an obligation."

Same record deal. Same gallery show. Same album. Different felt frame. Different outcome.

"It's that feeling of obligation." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Diagnostic Question

The question Joe uses to surface which frame the person is in:

"How, how do you imagine you could have continued in the air sports without feeling obligated professionally?" -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation (to Brett)

The structure of the question matters. It does not ask whether the person should change their job, marriage, or art. It asks whether they can continue what they are already doing without the obligation. The answer is almost always yes. The obligation is a felt addition, not a structural feature.

## Why It Is Not Just a Mindset Hack

This is easy to dismiss as positive thinking. It is not. The lens does not work as a verbal reframe ("just tell yourself you get to do it"). That kind of reframe runs the same obligation underneath the new language and produces nothing.

The lens works by surfacing the question "do I actually want this?" and being willing to feel the answer. If the answer is "yes, I want this and I had forgotten," the lens releases the obligation. If the answer is "no, I do not want this," the lens has revealed information that requires real change. Either way, the lens has produced clarity. The hack-version of "I get to" produces nothing because it skips the feeling step.

This is why the lens is paired with the next move below.

## The Power of No

The second move that allows the I-get-to frame to take hold is the willingness to say no.

"And then the second thing is saying no from time to time really, really helps. Like I get very, very deeply in touch with my want when I draw boundaries. Right. When I'm like, yeah, no, I can't work with you. I won't work in this company. It's just like, it's like, I feel this tremendous sense of empowerment of I'm doing what I want to be doing." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

The no is what makes the yes real. Without a credible no, every yes is suspect — possibly an obligation, possibly a want. With a credible no, the yes is verified as a want, because the alternative was available and not chosen.

This is also why the no often produces empowerment beyond the specific situation it addresses. Saying no breaks the implicit story that you have no choice, which is the story obligation runs on. See [[say-no-to-find-the-want]].

## The Brett Application: Choice and Agreement

Brett applied the lens to a stay-or-go decision in air sports: he could have continued in the sport professionally without falling into obligation by treating each opportunity as a choice and treating his agreements as agreements (which can be honored from want) rather than as obligations (which cannot).

"And, and if I make agreements, it doesn't mean that I'm obligated in a, in the sense like you describe. Like it's a, like it's not a new reality for me. It's an agreement. And I'm choosing to be there." -- Brett Kistler, Love and Obligation

The distinction between agreement and obligation matters. An agreement is something you entered into and continue to choose. An obligation is something that has trapped you. The same contract can be held either way. The frame is the variable.

## Why People Resist the Frame

The frame can feel naive when first heard, especially for people in genuinely constrained situations. "I have to keep this job because I need to feed my kids" is not paranoia; it is structural.

The frame does not deny the structure. It asks: given the structure, do I want to be doing this work? Often the answer is yes — feeding the kids is something the person genuinely wants. The "I have to" was disguising an "I get to" (I want to feed my kids; I get to use this work to do it). Once seen, the work changes character even though nothing in the job changed.

When the answer is no, the frame has revealed that the person is paying a real cost. That recognition is uncomfortable but valuable; it is the start of figuring out what they actually want to do.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that you have been living a life you actually want as if it were a sentence
- The surprise of finding that the answer to "do I want this?" is yes about something you assumed you were dragging yourself through
- The discomfort of finding that the answer is no and now you have to figure out what to do with that
- The shift in body state when you say "I get to" with sincerity and it actually lands
- The moment your no is honored — by you or by another — and you feel the freedom under the obligation
