# Is it that you're not feeling it, or are you repressing an emotion that comes up when you feel it?

## The Question

Asked after a person has put attention on a somatically loaded body part (the belly, the chest, the throat) and Joe has noticed a face change or other involuntary somatic response before the person reports anything.

The exact form:

- "Is it that you're not feeling it, or is that you are trying to repress an emotion that comes up when you feel it?"

The question is a binary. The two options are presented as the only two possibilities, with the first option (not-feeling) being implicitly the version the person walked in claiming, and the second option (active repression) being the version that fits the evidence Joe just observed.

The binary form does the work. The original claim was "I can't feel anything." That option remains on the table. But a second option has been added — one the person's body has just demonstrated in the room — and the person now has to choose. Choosing the first option requires explaining away the face change. Choosing the second option requires accepting that the wires are intact and the work is somewhere else entirely.

## When Joe Uses It

- Immediately after the [[attention-is-feeling]] sequence has run and the person has put attention on an emotionally loaded body part (most often the belly button or the chest).
- When Joe sees an involuntary response — face change, sharp breath, glaze of the eyes, micro-expression — before the person reports any felt sensation.
- When the person says "I don't know" or "I have no idea" in response to a question about what just happened in their body.
- When the [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] concern is being reframed in real time and the original wiring-problem framing needs to be replaced with the active-protection framing.
- When the difference between "the channel is closed" and "the channel is open but something else is shutting it" needs to be surfaced for the person to feel which one is actually happening.

The question is specifically not for someone who has shown no involuntary response. Without the live evidence of the face change, the binary loses its force — the person can answer "the first one" without any tension. The question requires the body to have just done something the person did not consciously cause.

## What It Produces

**A redefinition of the problem.** The person came in with one problem (the wires are not working). The question replaces that with two possible problems, only one of which matches the live evidence. The person almost always lands on the second option — "very possibly" or "yes, that" — and the work shifts from "build access" to "examine what's being pushed down."

**A reduction of the inability identity.** Before the question, the person could hold the identity "I'm someone who can't feel." After the question, the identity "I'm someone who pushes feeling back down" replaces it. The second identity is a doing the person can stop, not a being the person is.

**An opening to the underlying emotion.** Once the person has named that they are actively repressing rather than passively unable, the next move is to find what is being repressed. The belly-button attention can be re-deployed, this time with the explicit invitation to let what arises arise. See [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]] for the somatic interventions that often follow.

**Frequently: the unspoken admission "I don't want to."** When the question is asked gently and the binary is held open, some clients will answer something close to "yeah, I think I am." That naming is enough; the suppression cannot continue at the same intensity once it has been observed by the person who is doing it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says "very possibly" or hedged versions like "it's hard to discern the two":** treat the hedge as a yes. The person who genuinely had no idea what just happened would say "neither — I really don't know." The hedge is the recognition arriving slowly. Follow with an invitation to keep attending to the place and let what arises arise.

- **If the person says "yes, that one":** the work has moved. The new question is what is being pushed back. Often a short waiting and the emotion will surface. If the person needs a frame, [[joy-as-matriarch]] or [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]] can be deployed.

- **If the person says "no, it's the first one — I really feel nothing":** the body evidence and the verbal report are in conflict. Joe usually does not argue the conflict directly. He keeps the attention on the body location and waits. Often within thirty seconds the person notices something they previously did not. If nothing arrives, the read shifts toward [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] — the inability is older and more protective than the face change suggested, and the work changes.

- **If the person intellectualizes the question ("well, I think it could be either" / launches into analysis):** the intellectualizing is itself the answer. The mind has moved in to manage the situation. The intervention is to redirect to the body without further analysis. See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] and [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]].

## Why It Works

The question is built around a piece of live evidence the person did not produce on purpose. The face changed without their permission. The breath shifted without their direction. That involuntary movement is data that has no consistent place in the "I can't feel anything" frame. The question gives that data a place to live: it lives in the second option.

The binary form is not a false dichotomy. There may be other configurations (the person could be partially feeling and partially repressing; the face change could be unrelated to the belly-button attention). But the binary is precise enough for the work to advance. The person does not need to find the exact right answer; they need to find the answer that moves them off the "I have a wiring problem" identity. Both options do that — the first by being incorrect given the live evidence, the second by being correct.

The question also works because it does not require Joe to assert what is happening. Joe asks. The person answers. The shift in the person's frame is the person's shift, not Joe's. This is the same architecture as the [[attention-is-feeling]] sequence the question typically follows: change the frame by getting the person to state the new frame, not by telling them.

## Dialogue Example

The guest had just put attention on his belly button after Joe had walked him through the vocal-cord and right-pinky sequence. Joe saw the face change before the guest reported anything.

- **Joe:** "Yeah. So if you focus on feeling your belly button — whoa, what just happened there? Your face went—"
- **Guest:** "I, I have no idea, to be honest."
- **Joe:** "Like, so is it that you're not feeling it, or is that you are trying to repress an emotion that comes up when you feel it?"
- **Guest:** "Very possibly."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Guest:** "It's hard to discern the two."
- **Joe:** "You tell me, right? So if you, if you feel the belly button again — Okay, you can like put your finger right in it, then you know exactly where it's at. Okay. Yeah. So are you feeling your belly now? Sure."

*(Source: How Do I Feel My Body?)*

The "you tell me, right?" follow-up holds the responsibility on the person. The guest could answer with another hedge, but the hedge is now in service of finding out, not in service of avoiding the question. Joe then gives a small physical instruction (put your finger on it) that reduces the felt-access threshold and lets the body keep doing what it was already starting to do.

## Tone

Curious, light, not accusatory. The "whoa, what just happened there?" is delivered as surprise — Joe is reporting what he saw in real time, not unveiling a diagnosis he had stored up. The follow-up question carries the same curiosity. The guest is not being caught at anything; the guest is being asked to look at something the guest just did.

The "you tell me, right?" that follows a hedge is the moment of mild challenge. The person is being held to the responsibility of answering rather than escaping into "it's hard to know." But the challenge is delivered with warmth. The next sentence ("so if you feel the belly button again — okay, you can like put your finger right in it") immediately drops back to the practical.

## Relation to Other Questions and Moves

The question is the second beat of a two-beat sequence. The first beat is [[attention-is-feeling]] — the Socratic chain that establishes attention as the operational definition of feeling and gets the person to the belly button. The second beat is this question — the diagnostic binary that uses the live face change to redefine the problem.

The question is also related to [[whats-the-real-question]] — both questions reframe the presenting concern as something other than what the person walked in saying. The difference: [[whats-the-real-question]] surfaces a different topic the person is actually here to work on. This question surfaces a different shape of the same topic.

The question can be repeated across sessions with the same person as the work proceeds. Each time the body produces an involuntary response while the verbal channel reports nothing, the question is available. Over time, the person internalizes the diagnostic: "I noticed a face change just now; was I about to feel something I'm pushing back?" The question becomes a tool the person can apply to themselves.
