# What feels more in your purpose right now?

## The Question

The question dissolves the purpose-search by collapsing it to the present moment. The person has been looking for an external answer about what their purpose is. The question routes them back to felt-sense and to now.

Variations:
- "What feels more in your purpose right now?"
- "What has to change in this moment to make you feel closer to your purpose?"
- "What would feel more aligned right now?"
- "Where is more purpose-alignment available in this moment?"

"How can we acknowledge if we're living it in this moment? And if not, what has to change in this moment to make us feel closer to our purpose?" -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The question assumes purpose is always available, always being lived to some degree, and always responsive to a shift in the present moment. The person does not have to find their purpose first; the person has to feel into what would be more aligned right now.

## When Joe Uses It

The question is reached for when:

- The person is in the [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] search-frame and a smaller, present-moment question can collapse the search
- The person has been describing purpose as a future or external object
- The person is asking for help finding the right activity, and the question pivots from what-to-do to how-to-be-now
- After [[purpose-is-how-not-what]] has been named — this question operationalizes the how-frame
- After [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] has been read — the question reverses the external-validation direction by routing back to internal felt-sense
- When the person is paralyzed by the size of the purpose-question — the question shrinks the time-horizon from "my life" to "right now"

## What It Produces

The question changes the kind of answering. The person cannot answer it from the head in the usual way. The head was prepared to answer "what is my purpose?" with a future plan. The head is not prepared to answer "what feels more in purpose right now?" — the answer requires a felt-check.

Common responses:
- **The person pauses.** The question routes the answering through the body. The pause is the body coming online.
- **The person reports an immediate shift.** Often something small. "Stop trying to please you in this conversation." "Be more honest with my partner tonight." "Stop pretending I'm not tired." The shifts are small and specific.
- **The person resists with "I don't know."** This is often the head trying to answer a question the body could answer. The move is to slow down and let the body speak. "Don't think about it. What's the felt-direction?"
- **The person notices a misalignment they were not aware of.** The question surfaces what was just below conscious awareness — the small ways the person has been out of alignment in the present moment.

Often the person discovers the shift toward more-purpose is available immediately. The shift can be made in the next sentence, the next action, the next breath. The size of the shift required is small. The structural change is large because the orientation has shifted from "find the purpose later" to "live more of the purpose now."

## What Joe Does With the Response

The follow-up depends on what came up:

- **If a small shift is named:** Joe stays with the felt-sense of the shift. "Feel what it's like to be more in that. What's different?" The point is to anchor the felt-experience of being more aligned, so the person can recognize it again.
- **If the person resists with "I don't know":** Joe does not accept the resistance as final. "Don't think. What's the body saying right now?" or "If you didn't have to know, what would you do differently in the next ten minutes?"
- **If the person produces a big future answer ("I need to quit my job"):** Joe brings them back to the moment. "Maybe. But right now, in this conversation, what would feel more in your purpose?" The question is about the in-the-moment direction, not the major life decision.
- **If the person discovers misalignment they were doing without noticing:** Joe acknowledges the seeing. The seeing itself is the work. The person now has access to the felt-direction; they can choose to follow it or not.

The question is not asked once and answered once. It is asked, the answer is felt, the shift is enacted, and then the question can be asked again. "What feels more in your purpose right now?" is repeatable. Each iteration moves the person incrementally closer to alignment.

## The Question's Logic

The question assumes:
1. **Purpose is always available.** It is not located in the future or in another life. It is here.
2. **More-or-less-aligned is the relevant axis.** Not "in or out of purpose" — always partial, always shiftable.
3. **The body knows.** The felt-sense can read alignment without the mind needing to construct it.
4. **Shifts can be made now.** The person does not have to wait for conditions to change; the orientation can shift immediately.

"There's been no moment that I can find where I don't know if I'm more aligned or less aligned with my purpose." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

Joe is making a strong claim: the felt-sense of alignment is always readable. If the person says they cannot read it, the move is not to abandon the question but to slow down and let the body come online.

## The Distinction from "What Should I Do?"

"What should I do?" sends the person into deliberation, strategy, future-planning. The mind generates options. The body is not consulted. The answer arrives as a plan.

"What feels more in your purpose right now?" sends the person into felt-sense. The body is the consultant. The answer arrives as a direction, often non-verbal, that the person can either follow or not.

The first question keeps the person in the search-frame. The second question collapses the search-frame. Same surface concern; different question; different result.

For the AI coach: when tempted to ask "what should you do?" — substitute "what would feel more in your purpose right now?" The substitution alone changes the kind of conversation that follows.

## Tone

Quiet, slow, slightly spacious. The question is not asked rapidly; the person needs space to drop into the felt-sense before answering. The tone invites slowness rather than rewarding quick verbal answers. If the person produces a head-answer fast, the tone can stay quiet and the question can be asked again, lightly, to let the felt-answer have room.

When the person reports a shift, the tone matches the shift — often gentle, sometimes surprised, sometimes amused. The teaching is that the answer was right there the whole time. The tone honors that without dramatizing it.
