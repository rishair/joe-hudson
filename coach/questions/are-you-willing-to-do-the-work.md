# Are you both willing to do the work?

## The Question

When someone asks Joe whether a specific person is the right person for them -- the question that comes up constantly when someone is considering a relationship, dating someone new, or wondering whether to commit -- Joe does not engage the right-person frame. He redirects to the only criterion he treats as load-bearing.

"When somebody asks me, hey, do you think this is the right person? My answer is always the same. Are you both willing to do the work?" -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The question replaces the right-person assessment with the willingness-to-work assessment. Right-person is a property the conscious mind attempts to evaluate (does this person have the qualities). Willingness-to-work is a stance both partners can choose. The redirect moves the question from one neither person can really answer to one both people can.

## When Joe Uses It

- When someone asks "is this the right person for me?"
- When someone is considering whether to commit to a specific person
- When someone is in a relationship and wondering whether they should leave
- When someone is comparing a current partner to an idealized future partner
- When the right-person frame is operating and needs to be redirected
- When [[the-partner-checklist-doesnt-survive-the-actual-relationship]] is being addressed and a substitute criterion is needed
- When [[there-is-no-the-one]] has been delivered and the next question is what does matter

## What "The Work" Means

The work is not generic. Joe specifies what it consists of when he uses the question:

"If you're both willing to do the work, if you're both willing to look at yourselves, not work on each other, work on yourselves and find out how you can love yourself better and each other better. Yeah, it's going to work." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

Three parts are operative:

**1. Both willing.** Not one. Not the more-evolved partner trying to drag the less-evolved one along. Both. If only one is willing, the work cannot do what it does. This is part of why the question is plural: "are you both."

**2. Looking at yourselves, not at each other.** The trap that makes most relationship work go bad is the partners working on each other. The work the question is asking about is each partner looking at themselves. The partner is the mirror, not the project. See [[dont-position-yourself-as-your-partners-growth-guide]] and [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]].

**3. Finding out how to love yourself better and each other better.** The aim is not to fix the relationship's problems. The aim is the expansion of each partner's capacity to love -- self and other. The relationship is the field in which the expansion happens. The expansion is what makes the relationship deepen.

## The Forecast That Comes With the Question

When the question is answered with a clear yes from both, Joe forecasts the outcome with high confidence:

"Yeah, it's going to work. It might be bumpy as fuck." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The forecast has two parts. The work-will-deliver part: the relationship will function, will grow, will provide what relationships at their best provide. The bumpy part: the path will not be smooth. The work that the question is asking about is hard work. The friction is part of how the work happens.

Joe immediately cites his own marriage as the example:

"If you take Tara and my relationship, it got really bumpy in the first couple of years, like crazy. So, yeah, I mean, it could get bumpy, but you'll make it if that's the commitment, if that's really the commitment." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The bumpiness is not a sign the question was answered wrong. It is the work happening.

## What It Produces

When asked clearly, the question often produces:

- A pause. The person was expecting an evaluation of the partner. The pivot to their own willingness is unexpected.
- Recognition of the actual variable. The right-person frame had been organizing the assessment around qualities. The question reorients it to willingness, which is more workable.
- Sometimes immediate clarity. The person knows the answer -- or knows their partner's answer -- and the question surfaces it.
- Sometimes a more honest evaluation of the relationship. The willingness criterion is harder to fake than the right-person criterion. The person who could rationalize a right-person assessment cannot as easily rationalize "we are both willing to do the work" if they know they are not.
- A different conversation with the partner. The question is plural and requires the partner to also answer. The conversation moves from the person's internal assessment to a shared assessment.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If both are clearly willing:** The forecast follows. It will work. It will be bumpy. The commitment is real. The work that produces deepening over time is available. See [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]] and [[nre-returns-in-grounded-form-after-each-healing]].

**If one is willing and the other is not:** This is the most common difficult answer. The work cannot do its work unilaterally. The willing partner can do their own work in the relationship, but the deepening that the question is forecasting requires both. The realistic options become: stay and continue your own work without the relationship-level deepening, or recognize that what the question is pointing at is not available with this configuration.

**If neither is willing:** The relationship is a non-work relationship. This is a real option. Some relationships function on terms that do not require the work. The question reveals what is on the table.

**If the person cannot answer for themselves:** This is often the diagnostic that the readiness work has not yet been done. The willingness is not a momentary decision; it is the felt orientation of someone who has done enough internal work to be in a position to do the relationship-level work. If the answer to "are you willing" is unclear, the next move is usually the work on the person's own readiness. See [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]].

## Why This Question Beats the Right-Person Question

The right-person question is unanswerable in a way that the willingness question is not. The right-person question requires a comparison to an alternative that does not exist (a real comparison to all the other potential partners the person did not select). The willingness question requires only an honest report of what both partners are actually committed to.

The right-person question is also a defense in many configurations. As long as the question is "is this the right person," the answer can always be "I'm not sure yet, let me keep looking." The willingness question forces the question to be about the present configuration. It does not leave room for the search-frame to continue.

This question is therefore also a tool against [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]] in its right-person-search form. It is the operational version of [[there-is-no-the-one]]: if there is no the one, then the question of whether this is the one is the wrong question, and the right question is whether the two people are willing to make this the one through the work.

## What This Question Is Not

It is not a screen against difficult partners. The question does not predict the relationship will be easy. It predicts the relationship will be workable, which is different. Some partners who are willing to do the work are still going to be difficult to be with. The willingness is what makes the difficulty productive.

It is not a substitute for actual evaluation. The question does not say to ignore obvious incompatibilities, violations, or warning signs. It says that, among the things the conscious mind cannot reliably evaluate (whether this person is the one), the willingness criterion is the most reliable available signal.

It is not a guarantee. Joe's "it's going to work" is delivered with confidence but does not promise the relationship will continue forever in its current form. It promises the work will produce what the work produces -- deepening, expansion, capacity-growth. The relationship's specific trajectory is not predicted.

## Tone

Direct. Joe uses the same answer every time. The repetition is part of the teaching: this is the criterion. The tone is also slightly amused -- the question redirects from a question Joe knows people want him to answer (yes/no on the specific person) to a question they can answer themselves. The handoff is gentle but clear.
