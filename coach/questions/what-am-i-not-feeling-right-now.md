# What am I not feeling right now? / What do I need to feel right now?

## The Question

This is a question the person asks themselves, mid-fight, the moment they notice they are feeling superior to the person they are fighting with. It is the recovery move from inside one's own head when the fight is happening live.

- "What am I not feeling?"
- "What do I need to feel right now?"

The two phrasings are functionally equivalent. The first orients toward the avoided material ("what am I refusing"). The second orients toward the needed material ("what is being requested"). Joe uses both in close succession, suggesting the pair are interchangeable in this context.

"It's really important if you're noticing when you're in a fight, oh, I am thinking I'm better than the other person. The question is, what am I not feeling? What do I need to feel right now?" -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

## When to Use It

This is not a question Joe asks a client. It is a question the client learns to ask themselves, in real time, during the fight. The trigger is one specific moment: the moment you notice you are feeling superior to your partner.

The trigger sequence:

1. You are in a fight with your partner.
2. You notice a thought or felt sense that has the shape of "I am better than them" / "I am the rational one here" / "they are out of control and I am not."
3. The noticing itself is the cue.
4. You ask yourself: "what am I not feeling?"

The reason the superiority is the trigger: feeling-superior is the signature that emotion-avoidance is running. The judgment is the cognitive method by which the body is refusing to feel something. The question redirects the attention from the judgment (which is the symptom) to the avoided feeling (which is the cause). See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] and [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]] for the underlying structural account.

A second trigger: the felt sense, mid-fight, of seeing-something-out-of-the-corner-of-the-eye that you do not want to look at. Joe describes this as the small "ooh, maybe, oh" feeling — the awareness of the emotion that the avoidance is working to keep out of view. That awareness is the cue. The question turns toward it instead of away.

"You'll notice it. You're like, 'oh, I'm avoiding it during the fight.' Like, I — mm, yeah. And you kind of can see it out of the corner of your eye. And maybe you have that, like, thought, you know, the one where it's like, 'ooh, maybe, oh, oh.' And it's like, that's the thing. When you're in the fight, that's where you want to go because that's where your healing is going to be." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

## What It Produces

The question typically surfaces one of a small number of recurring answers, all in the family of emotions the person has been organizing the superiority to defend against:

- A felt sense of not-enough underneath the position of better-than. See [[superiority-and-shame-erode-together]] for the structural pairing.
- A specific shame the position is concealing — usually a shame about an old wound the present fight is firing the response to. See [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]].
- A fear of contact, of equality, of being vulnerable in the way the partner is being vulnerable.
- A grief about the way the fight is being lost (i.e., the fight is being won and the relationship is being lost in the same motion).
- An anger that has been suppressed in favor of the "calm rational" position.

The question does not require the answer to be precise. Even an imprecise answer ("something is here that I am not letting myself feel") is sufficient to redirect the attention. The redirection is the work. Once the attention is on the avoided feeling, the superiority loses its function and softens on its own.

## What to Do With the Answer

Once the avoided feeling has been located, the move is to feel it briefly, even for a few seconds, while still in the fight. See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] for the "2-4 seconds is enough" instruction.

"And by feeling that emotion, that's how you make the fight really productive." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

The feeling does not have to be discharged. It does not have to be expressed to the partner. It only has to be felt, in the body, briefly. The brief feeling is what reorganizes the fight from the inside.

What happens next varies. Sometimes the felt emotion can be named to the partner, which converts the fight from positional combat into shared seeing. See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]]. Sometimes the felt emotion simply allows the fight to land somewhere new, without the naming. Sometimes the feeling reveals a solution that was invisible from the superior position.

"That's where you want to go because that's where your healing is going to be. That's where the productivity is going to be. That's where you're both going to be able to find a solution that you couldn't see before." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

The solution-emerging-from-the-feeling is a specific structural claim: the rational position cannot see the solution because the rational position is being held precisely to avoid the feeling that contains the access to the solution. Feel the feeling, and the solution becomes visible. This inverts the surface narrative that calm rationality is what solves fights. The calm rationality is often what is blocking the solution.

## The Self-Coaching Context

This question is one of the cleanest examples of a self-coaching move. The conditions of use (mid-fight) do not allow for an external coach. The person has to be their own coach in the moment. The question is engineered for self-application: it is short, it is concrete, it points at something specific, and it has a clear next action.

The question can be embedded in the body as a reflex. The first few times, the person will not remember to ask it during a fight; they will remember after the fight is over and notice they could have asked it. With practice, the question starts to arrive in the moment. Eventually it becomes automatic: feel the superiority surge, ask the question, find the feeling, feel it briefly, return to the fight reorganized.

The progression is part of the work. The person who is asking this question consistently mid-fight is operating at a different developmental stage than the person who is still being puppeted by the superiority position. See [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]] for the broader context of where this capacity sits in the developmental sequence.

## Why This Is Not Just "Take a Breath"

Many couples' protocols teach a generic "pause and breathe" move at the moment of escalation. This question is not that move. It does not ask the person to regulate down to a calm position from which they can reason. It asks the person to redirect attention from the cognitive position (the superiority) to the felt content (the avoided emotion).

The breath move stays in the head (slowing down the head). This question moves out of the head into the body. The difference matters because the head-position is often the problem, not the dysregulation. The superior, calm, rational head is the very position that is preventing the fight from being productive. Slowing the head down does not address this; redirecting attention to the body does.

The two moves can be paired (regulate the breath, then ask the question), but the question is the active ingredient. See [[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]] for the regulation move and how it complements rather than substitutes for this question.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]]. That question is asked by the coach to the client about a decision being deliberated. This question is asked by the client to themselves about a fight being lived. The two share an underlying structure (find the avoided emotion to release the surface position) but they operate at different moments and on different material.

This is also not "what's wrong with me?" or "why am I being like this?" Both of those produce shame, which loops back into more superiority. This question is structurally different: it does not pathologize the superiority, it just treats it as a flag for unfelt feeling and asks the next question.

## Tone

Quiet, internal, not dramatic. The question is asked silently, mid-conversation, while the partner is still talking or while you are still talking. It is not announced. The partner does not need to know you are asking it. The work is interior.

Joe's framing carries no urgency about it ("oh, oh, maybe, oh" — the small awareness, not the trumpet). The question is small. The repetition is what does the work, not any single dramatic asking.
