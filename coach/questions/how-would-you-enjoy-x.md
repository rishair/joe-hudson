# How would you enjoy your X?

## The Question

When a difficult experience is named in the room — shame, sadness, stress, pain, helplessness, the inability to come up with an answer, the rushing through the day — apply the principle of enjoyment to that specific thing. Not enjoyment as escape from it. Enjoyment of it.

Variations, all from a single session:

- "How would you enjoy your shame?"
- "How would you enjoy your sadness?"
- "What stops you from enjoying chasing or avoiding?"
- "How do you, how do you run towards yourself in the time of stress? ... So how do you enjoy that?"
- "What would your chronic pain look like if you didn't pull the brakes?"
- "Is there anything about rushing through your day that can't be enjoyed?"
- "How do you enjoy oscillating in and out of [enjoyment]?"

The form is consistent. The X changes. The principle does not.

## When Joe Uses It

The question lives in a specific moment of the session: the person has named something difficult that is present in them, and the next reflex (theirs and most coaches') would be to fix it, soothe it, or move toward what is supposedly more enjoyable. Joe inverts the move. He turns the difficult thing into the candidate for enjoyment.

Contexts where Joe reaches for this:
- The person presents [[i-keep-postponing-enjoyment]] and is rushing toward an imagined future enjoyment. Apply enjoyment to the rushing itself.
- The person is in [[shame-as-disconnection]] and trying to perform their way out of the shame. Apply enjoyment to the shame.
- The person is in stress about a present question they cannot answer. Apply enjoyment to the stress.
- The person is in chronic pain and pulling the brakes against the pain. Apply enjoyment to the pain (and to the relationship with the pain).
- The person is in a victim-feeling and pushing against it. Apply enjoyment to being-a-victim-for-a-moment.
- The person is having difficulty being themselves in a particular context (fast-paced group conversation, performance situation, social setting). Apply enjoyment to the context itself.

## The Group-Conversation Application

A clean example of the question applied to performance anxiety in groups: when someone asks how to stay in [[view]] during fast-paced and complex group conversations, the hack Joe offers is to focus on enjoying yourself, not on the results of the interaction.

"Just focus on enjoying yourself, not on the results of the group interaction. How do I enjoy this group interaction, this fast-paced group interaction? How do you enjoy that? If you can focus there, a lot of this stuff goes away. Naturally." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The mechanism: the difficulty in a fast-paced group conversation is almost always coming from the partiality (the I in VIEW) -- the wanting to be liked, the wanting to say the right thing, the judging of one's own responses. Asking "how do I enjoy this?" redirects attention away from the partiality and toward the present experience of the conversation itself. The partiality cannot sustain itself when attention is on enjoyment.

The principle generalizes. Whenever someone reports difficulty with a context they cannot escape, redirecting them to "how would you enjoy this context?" tends to dissolve a substantial fraction of the difficulty without requiring direct work on whatever was producing it.

The question is the operational form of [[enjoyment-as-path]]. The principle states that enjoyment is the path; this question is what the principle looks like in a live exchange.

## What It Produces

The most common first response: confusion or pushback. The premise of the question is foreign. People are not in the habit of considering that the difficult thing could be enjoyed. The category seems wrong. Joe stays with the question. He does not soften it.

Once the person engages with the question, several things tend to happen:

- The body finds a small movement toward the thing. The client in the session described it as his body "kind of moving like grass in the wind." The micro-movement is the felt sense of beginning to allow the thing.
- The person discovers the thing was not what they thought. The shame, met, becomes connection. The pain, met, becomes spaciousness. The stress, met, becomes presence.
- The person reports a small surprise. "It doesn't feel nearly as bad as I thought."
- Sometimes the response is to identify what stops the enjoyment — and that answer is itself the next piece of work. See [[whats-stopping-you-from-enjoying-x]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person engages and finds a small allowing:** Joe notes it without celebrating. "Notice that." The noticing is the medicine.
- **If the person resists ("how could I enjoy that?"):** Joe holds the question gently. He does not argue. Sometimes he reframes: "What if you just enjoy the resistance to enjoying it?" The recursion is intentional.
- **If the person answers "I don't know":** Joe treats this as data, not a dead end. He may ask what stops them from enjoying *not knowing*. This is the same question one layer down.
- **If the person finds the enjoyment:** Joe stays in the moment. The person is having the experience the whole session was aimed at. Words would pull them out.
- **If the person uses the question to bypass the difficult feeling:** Joe notes this and brings them back. "Enjoying" is not the same as "fixing." The question is asking for enjoyment-of, not enjoyment-instead-of.

## The Recursive Form

A specific structural feature of this question: it applies to its own asking. The person asked "how would you enjoy your shame?" who freezes and says "I can't" can be asked "how do you enjoy not being able to?" The question can be applied to any state, including the state of not being able to apply it.

This is why the question is so durable as a coaching instrument. It does not require the person to be in any particular state. Wherever they are is the X. The question is "how do you enjoy *this* X?"

> "What stops you from enjoying your experience? Exactly. That's shame... What stops you from enjoying chasing or avoiding? What stops you from enjoying shame? What is the essential thing that's happening?"

The question and its inverse (see [[whats-stopping-you-from-enjoying-x]]) work as a pair. The first invites the move. The second surfaces what is blocking it. Both serve the same exploration.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I want to stop postponing enjoyment into an abstract imaginary future."
- **Joe:** "How about right now? How do you enjoy yourself right now?"
- **Person:** [Notes glimpses, oscillation, but reports current enjoyment]
- **Joe:** "Are you enjoying saying that?"
- **Person:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "So you can enjoy oscillating in and out of it."

And later, after the person has named chronic pain and shame about being an inconvenience:

- **Joe:** "Okay, so you're feeling the shame. What -- how would you enjoy your shame?"
- **Person:** "I mean, I feel like when I embrace it, I can -- you can see that my body's kind of moving like grass in the wind, kind of. I feel like I'm letting it move in a way, which feels kind of nice."
- *(Source: How Do I Stop Postponing My Enjoyment?)*

The question converts shame from an obstacle to enjoyment into an object of enjoyment. The body finds the answer the head could not.

## Tone

Curious, light, slightly playful. The question carries no judgment. Joe is not insisting the person enjoy anything. He is offering the question as an experiment. The energy is "what would happen if you tried this?" rather than "you should be able to do this."

There is also a structural lightness in the question: by asking about enjoyment of the difficult thing, Joe is implicitly communicating that the difficult thing is not a catastrophe. The shame, the pain, the stress — they are candidates for enjoyment, not threats requiring intervention. The framing itself does work.

## Why "How Would You" and Not "Can You"

The phrasing matters. "Can you enjoy your shame?" invites a yes-or-no. "How would you enjoy your shame?" presupposes the possibility and asks for the method. The presupposition does work. The mind, asked a how question, will start hunting for an answer. The mind, asked a can question, will often default to no.

This is the same grammatical move that powers [[how-what-questions]]. The presupposition is the coaching. The person is being invited to take the possibility seriously enough to look for the path.
