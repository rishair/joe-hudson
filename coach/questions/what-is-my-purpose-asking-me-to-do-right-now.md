# What Is My Purpose Asking Me to Do Right Now?

## The Question

A specific in-the-moment question that treats purpose as an active asker rather than as an alignment to be checked. The person stops asking "what is my purpose?" and starts asking "given that purpose is here and asking, what is it asking right now?"

Variations:
- "What is my purpose asking me to do right now?"
- "What would living my purpose look like in this moment?"
- "What would I need to say or do or listen to or understand to be living my purpose as fully as possible in this moment?"

"In this moment, you can run this little experiment. What is my purpose asking me to do right now? So you can be in a meeting and you can ask, what is my purpose asking me to do right now? You can be having dinner and you can ask, what is my purpose asking me to do right now? And you'll notice that there's an answer that comes through you." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The question is asked in real situations — in the middle of a meeting, in the middle of dinner, in the middle of whatever is already happening. It is not a planning question for later. It is an instruction-receiving question for now.

## What This Question Assumes

The question carries a specific posture that distinguishes it from related purpose-questions:

- **Purpose is active.** It is asking, not waiting to be discovered. The verb "asking" treats purpose as something with its own movement and direction, not as a static answer to be located.
- **Purpose is locally specific.** It has something specific to ask in this meeting, in this conversation, in this dinner. Not a general life-direction, but a specific next-step.
- **The answer arrives.** Joe says "you'll notice that there's an answer that comes through you." The question is not rhetorical. The answer is expected and reportedly delivered: "Oh, my purpose is to say this. My purpose is to do this. My purpose is to show somebody this thing or to listen really deeply."
- **The asking can be repeated.** Each moment offers a fresh ask. The instruction can shift moment to moment because the situation shifts moment to moment.

## Distinction From "What Feels More in Your Purpose Right Now?"

Both questions are in-the-moment purpose-questions. They are not duplicates; they have different operational shapes.

- **[[what-feels-more-in-purpose-right-now]]** is a felt-alignment check. The person reads how aligned they are with their purpose in this moment and notices what would shift the alignment. The unit is felt-sense; the answer is a direction.
- **What is my purpose asking me to do right now?** is an instruction-receive. The person treats purpose as an asker and listens for what it is asking. The unit is action; the answer is a next-step.

The alignment-check is good for diagnosis: how am I doing? The instruction-receive is good for action: what is being asked? The two are complementary. The alignment-check might be the first move; the instruction-receive might be the next. Or the instruction-receive can be used standalone when the person needs to know what to do now, not whether they are doing it right.

For the AI coach: the choice between the two depends on what the person needs. If they need orientation, use the alignment-check. If they need a next action, use the instruction-receive.

## When Joe Uses It

The question is reached for when:

- The person is in a real-time situation and needs to know what to do now, not what to plan for later
- The person has been doing the abstract purpose-search and the question shrinks the unit-of-purpose from "my life" to "this moment"
- The person is asking how to translate the felt-sense of purpose into an action
- The person has been [[mental-models-cant-tell-you-what-experience-feels-like]] modeling possible careers and needs an experiment-able instrument that produces actual data from the actual present
- The person is paralyzed by the size of the purpose-question — the question makes the question small enough to be answerable
- The person needs to practice receiving instruction from purpose rather than generating instruction from the head — the question puts them in the receive-mode

## What It Produces

Joe describes the kinds of answers that arrive:

"Oh, my purpose is to say this. My purpose is to do this. My purpose is to show somebody this thing or to listen really deeply. My purpose is here in this moment." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The answers tend to share specific features:

- **Small.** "Say this." "Listen deeply." "Show this thing." Not "quit your job"; not "change everything."
- **Available.** The answer is doable now, in the situation the person is in, without prerequisites.
- **Specific.** The answer points at one thing, not a general direction.
- **Surprising sometimes.** The person did not consciously plan the answer. It arrived. The "comes through you" framing is operative.

The accumulation of these small specific answers, followed repeatedly, produces the path. The path is not visible in advance; it is visible after enough next-steps have been taken in response to enough asks.

"If you start learning how to follow your purpose in the moment, then it becomes really clear what to do next. And that's the way the purpose shows up. It's like a path. If you're walking on a path in the woods and you don't have a map, which is the way purpose works... you're following your path and you know where to go when you take 10 more steps, and then you know where to go where you take 10 more steps." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The question is the asking-the-trail-for-the-next-ten-steps move. The trail answers. The next ten steps reveal the next ten steps.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The follow-up depends on what arrived:

- **If a specific small action is named:** support the doing. "Yes — do that." The point is not to analyze the answer; the point is to act on it and see what comes next.
- **If the person says "I don't know":** slow down. The head is trying to answer. Let the body or the asking come from a different place. "Don't think — just listen. What's asking?"
- **If the person produces a large life-decision ("I should quit my job"):** redirect to the smaller scale. "Maybe. But right now, in the next ten minutes, what is purpose asking? Start with that." The large decisions can wait; the smaller ones build the trust that the asks are reliable.
- **If the answer arrives and the person dismisses it as "too small":** name the dismissal. The smallness is appropriate. The accumulation of small asks produces the path. The dismissal is the head wanting the answer to be impressive enough to justify the years of search.

The question is repeatable. It can be asked many times a day. Each ask produces the next answer. The practice of asking-and-following is the practice of living purpose moment-to-moment. See [[purpose-checkin-alarm-practice]] for the operationalized form.

## The Question as Experiment

Joe explicitly frames the question as an experiment:

"In this moment, you can run this little experiment. What is my purpose asking me to do right now?" -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The experiment-framing is important. The person is not asked to believe that purpose is asking. The person is asked to test it: ask and see if an answer arrives. If it does, follow and see what happens. If it does not, the experiment failed for this moment and can be run again at the next moment.

The experiment-framing also removes the pressure to be right about the answer. An experiment can be wrong; that is what experiments produce — data, not conclusions. The person is freed from having to know in advance whether the answer is the right one. They can act on it, observe the consequences, and let the next ask be informed by the consequences.

See [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]] for the broader move and [[everything-is-already-an-experiment]] for the principle.

## Tone

Quiet, available, slightly amused. The question is offered as a thing to try, not as a teaching to receive. The amusement is at the structural smallness of the move — years of search resolved into thirty seconds of asking. The tone communicates that the simplicity is appropriate, not insufficient.

When the answer arrives and the person reports it, the tone matches the smallness of the answer. Not enthusiastic ("Yes! That's it!"), not dramatic ("Now you've found it!"), just steady acknowledgment: yes, that is what is asking, follow it.
