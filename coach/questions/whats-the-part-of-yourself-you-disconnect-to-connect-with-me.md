# What's the part of yourself you disconnect from to connect with me?

## The Question

The question in Joe's words. Multiple phrasings appear; the structural core is the same.

- "What's the thing that you feel like you have to contain to connect with me?"
- "What's the part of yourself you disconnect with so that you can connect with me?"
- "What's the part of yourself that you tell yourself you have to disconnect with to connect with me?"
- "What do you have to contain to be able to connect with me?"

The question contains a structural claim embedded in its grammar. The asker is asserting that connection is happening through a disconnection from self, not in spite of it. The person being asked is invited to look at what specifically gets cut off in the moment of reaching for connection.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person has presented a concern about their inability to bring their full self to a context (work, relationships, art) and the same containment is visibly operating in the coaching conversation. See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]] and [[the-cry-laugh-at-the-edge]].

- When the person has opened the session with an intellectual framing ("I need to make sentences that make sense") that is itself the disconnection-for-connection operating in real time.

- When the person describes the work-problem as a barrier to remove but is currently demonstrating the barrier in operation. The question relocates the work from the abstract barrier to the live operation.

- After the work-frame has been left explicitly ("let's leave your work aside for just a second, because if it's happening now, it's not just about work"). The question is the entry into the relational frame.

- When the person speaks well but does not seem to be in the speaking. The fluency itself is the channel through which the disconnection is being maintained.

- Not when the person is genuinely absent (dissociating, numb, blank). The question presumes a containment apparatus that is in active operation. If the person is not present at all, the question lands as confusing rather than as a precise probe.

## What It Produces

**The person locates the containment.** The question forces specificity. A vague sense of "I hold back" becomes a concrete answer: "all my emotions," "my sadness," "my anger," "the thing about my dad." The specificity is the work-material.

**The person often answers with their childhood material.** Once asked what they cut off to be here, the answer frequently surfaces the original install: "All emotions, because they were not met or not welcomed." The question goes directly to the installed material without first having to be routed through a childhood-history conversation.

**The fluency may break.** Once the person is asked to name what they cut off, the fluency that was the channel for the disconnection often pauses. The pause is the first crack in the apparatus. What comes through the crack is the material the apparatus has been holding.

**The containment becomes a relational operation, not a private one.** The question frames the containment as something done to make this connection (with the coach) possible. The frame is correct: the containment is always relational, even when it has been generalized so far that it operates in solitude. Returning it to its relational context makes it workable in the current relational context.

**The person sometimes resists or deflects.** Common deflections: explaining the childhood pattern in third person ("kids learn that..."), generalizing ("everyone has to do this in society"), going meta ("interesting question"). Each deflection is itself more apparatus and can be named.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person answers with the specific content they cut off (an emotion, a part, a kind of expression):** Joe stays with the specific content. He may name what he is seeing in the body. "I can see you holding it back right now actually." See [[the-cry-laugh-at-the-edge]]. The work moves into the live experience of the contained material.

- **If the person answers with a childhood pattern ("my dad was choleric so I learned all emotions were too much"):** Joe acknowledges and pivots back to the live moment. The childhood material is real but the work is happening now. He may make an empathy-guess to the childhood pattern. See [[empathy-guess-to-childhood-pattern]].

- **If the person answers intellectually ("I disconnect from my emotional self in order to function"):** Joe may refuse the frame and ask again. The intellectual answer is the apparatus answering instead of the person.

- **If the person resists the premise ("I'm not actually disconnecting"):** Joe lets it sit and watches the body. The body usually confirms the disconnection within a few exchanges. The verbal resistance is part of the apparatus.

- **If the person can answer with their whole body present:** the disconnection has already loosened. The answer that comes when the body is in is often surprising and load-bearing. Stay with it.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, after the woman has described wanting to bring her full self to her work and Joe has confirmed the containment is happening in the room right now:

- **Joe:** "So, so, so it sounds like what's happening is that you have a story that you have to contain. This cry-laughter that's always right there at the edge of your consciousness. Is that what I'm hearing? What — let's leave your work aside for just a second, because if it's happening now, it's not just about work. So what's the thing that you feel like you have to contain to connect with me?"
- **Person:** "I always try to connect first on a level, like on a logical intellectual level, because I need to make sentences that make sense for everyone."
- **Joe:** "No, you don't. That's not how the world works. Like, go check out two 3-year-olds meeting each other and playing. There's like, there's no sentences need to be exchanged. Most of our communication is non-verbal. So what's the question is still there. What's the thing that you feel like you have to contain to be able to connect with me? What's the part of yourself you disconnect with so that you can connect with me? I don't need your thinking. You just were thinking. I want, like, I want that from the whole body. What's the part of yourself that you have to dis— that you tell yourself you have to disconnect with to connect with me?"
- **Person:** "All emotions because they were not met or not welcomed or too much or too little or in the opposite. Like my father, for example, he was like very choleric, so he had like lots of emotions and it kind of hurt me when I was — so I just learned probably that, that all the emotions and when they're too much."

*(Source: I'm Not Responsible For How You Feel)*

The structural arc visible in the exchange: Joe asks the question. The first answer is the apparatus answering (intellectual connection strategy). Joe refuses the frame, uses the two-three-year-olds example, and re-asks the question with two reformulations and an explicit instruction ("I want that from the whole body"). The second answer arrives with the actual content (all emotions, because of the choleric father, because they were too much).

The reformulations matter. The same question asked three slightly different ways gives the person three different angles to receive it. The first phrasing usually gets the apparatus's first defense. The reformulations bypass the defense.

## Tone

Direct, but with a small softness. The question is not an interrogation. It is an invitation to look together at something the person has been doing in private for years. The tone is "let's look at this" rather than "tell me what you're hiding."

The question often has a slightly puzzled quality on Joe's part, as if he is genuinely asking. The puzzlement is appropriate because the apparatus is being run unconsciously; the person genuinely does not know what they cut off to be here. The asking-with-curiosity invites the looking.

When the question is re-asked after a deflection, the reformulation can carry a small firmness. "What's the thing you feel like you have to contain to be able to connect with me?" — said again, slower, with a small smile. The firmness is not pressure; it is the willingness to stay with the question across multiple iterations until the actual answer arrives.

## What This Question Is Not

- It is not a generic "what are you avoiding?" question. That question is too broad; this one is specific to the connection-to-this-person frame.
- It is not [[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]]. That question asks for the present-tense truth; this question asks for the specific operation of self-disconnection-in-service-of-connection.
- It is not a setup for the coach to tell the person what they're cutting off. The person has to answer it themselves. The coach's reading can be added later if useful, but the question's purpose is the person's own articulation.
- It is not a substitute for the live demonstration. The question often produces a verbal answer; the demonstration (the wordless invitation, the live containment) produces a bodily answer. Both may be needed.
