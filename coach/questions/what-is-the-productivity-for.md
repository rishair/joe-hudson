# What is the productivity for? / What's the forest through the trees?

## The Question

The question takes whatever the person has been doing (the grinding, the optimizing, the hustling, the pushing) and asks what all of it is ultimately meant to produce. Productivity is the trees. Something else is the forest. Most people who organize their lives around productivity have never identified the forest.

The question has multiple forms, each useful in different contexts:

- "What's all the productivity for, actually?"
- "If you could have everything you wanted and it didn't require you being productive, how would that be? Would you push that button?"
- "Is the productivity meant to make you rich, or is the idea that the productivity will make you happy, or is the productivity meant to make you rich so that you can be happy?"
- "Is the productivity meant so that people think you do a good job so Dad will love you?"
- "What role does safety play in this, if at all?"
- "If productivity is the trees, what's the forest?"

The question is structurally similar to [[what-was-x-going-to-get-you]] applied specifically to ongoing productivity behavior rather than to a long-finished pursuit. The difference: [[what-was-x-going-to-get-you]] looks backward at a strategy whose outcome the person already has. This question looks at a strategy the person is currently running.

## When Joe Uses It

The question appears when:

- The person reports being productive, busy, hustling, but is also reporting that something is off (no fulfillment, no purpose, "I can't wait till I can retire," low-grade depression underneath the success).
- The person is presenting a productivity problem ("I need to be more productive," "I'm overwhelmed by my to-do list") and the deeper move is to ask why all of this is even happening.
- The person has reached a target (sold the company, made the money, hit the milestone) and the expected feeling did not arrive.
- A leader is grinding their team and the team is producing, but the grinding itself has become the point.
- The person is describing the lifestyle they have built ("I have to," "I should," "I need to") and the structure of the lifestyle has eaten any sense of why it exists.
- Someone justifies a brutal week with "this is just what it takes" and the question is whether what they think they're getting from the brutality is actually being delivered.

## What It Produces

The first response to the question is often confusion. The person has not asked it before. The productivity has been autonomic for so long that the why has gone unexamined. The pause itself is part of the work.

When the person does answer, the answers cluster into a small set of underlying drivers:

- **Safety.** "I'm doing this so I'll be safe. If I work hard enough, the money / the position / the reputation will protect me from bad things."
- **Likability or approval.** "I'm doing this so people will think well of me. So my boss will be happy. So my parents will be proud. So I'll be loved."
- **Control of the world.** "I'm doing this so I can make the world go a certain way. So things will turn out how I want."
- **Happiness deferred.** "I'm doing this so eventually I can be happy. The productivity is the down payment on a future state."
- **Identity.** "I'm doing this because this is who I am. Without the productivity, I don't know what I am."

Each answer opens a different downstream conversation. None of them is "I'm doing this because the doing itself is meaningful and enjoyable to me right now." When that answer does come up, the productivity is not pathological and the question's work is mostly done.

## The Push-the-Button Variant

A particularly sharp form of the question:

"If you could have everything you wanted and it didn't require you being productive, how would that be? Would you push that button to allow that to happen?" -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The button-press question separates two things people often conflate: wanting the outcomes the productivity promises, and wanting the productivity itself. Many people would refuse the button. They would not actually want the outcomes if the productivity itself disappeared. That refusal exposes that the productivity is the goal, not the outcomes. The "I'm doing this to get X" framing was the cover story for "I'm doing this because the doing itself is what I am addicted to or identified with."

For people who would press the button, the question opens onto the next move: if you would press the button if you could, then the productivity is the cost you are paying for the outcomes, and the question becomes whether the productivity is actually producing the outcomes (often it is not) and whether the cost is worth it.

## The Implicit Forest Becomes Explicit

The downstream version of the question, after the underlying driver is named:

"There's a clue in the way that you are productive that hints to what the implicit forest is and then making it explicit." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

How someone is being productive reveals what they are productive for, even before they can name it:

- A person doing productivity from anxiety and shortness of breath is producing for safety. The forest is "be safe."
- A person doing productivity neediness, constantly checking how it lands, is producing for likability. The forest is "be loved."
- A person doing productivity from forcefulness, pushing the world into shape, is producing to control. The forest is "make the world be how I want."
- A person doing productivity from joy, presence, and creative engagement is producing because the producing itself is what they want to be doing. The forest is the doing.

The body's relationship to the productivity is the data. The driver is encoded in the somatic signature. The question makes explicit what the somatic signature has already been broadcasting.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the answer is "safety":** The follow-up question is "how safe is your productivity actually making you?" Often the answer reveals that the productivity is not producing safety at all -- it is producing the appearance of effort while leaving the person no more safe than before. The example Joe uses: middle managers who were doing the tasks their boss wanted, when the actual safety move was to make themselves the indispensable person who knew how to implement AI in their role. The first behavior is busy. The second is safe. They look similar from the outside. They are opposite at the level of leverage. See the [[middle-managers-and-ai]] example for the worked case.

**If the answer is "approval / likability":** The follow-up is to make visible what would happen if the approval did not arrive. What the person is actually after is usually the felt-state the approval was supposed to deliver. See [[what-was-x-going-to-get-you]] for the chain that follows. Imagine getting cut loose in a reorg and going to the next job saying "I was really good at making my last boss happy" -- the skill of approval-seeking does not transfer to anything that protects the person.

**If the answer is "happiness deferred":** The work moves toward [[how-do-i-be-happy]] and the structural teaching that pursuit-of-happiness does not produce happiness. The productivity-as-down-payment-on-happiness is a particular form of the misframe.

**If the answer is "I don't know":** The not-knowing is the work. Joe does not supply an answer. The discovery that someone has organized their entire life around a strategy whose endpoint they cannot articulate is often the point at which the strategy starts to dissolve.

**If the person resists the question:** The resistance is data. Resistance to "what is this all for?" is often resistance to the recognition that there might not be a good answer. Stay with the question. Let the resistance speak.

## The Daily-Level Form

The question scales down to a daily practice.

"Primarily it's, what am I doing this for? And it's that -- that's not a question you ask every month. It's a question you ask every day. That little thing. And for me, when I'm in our business and I watch a meeting, it's really clear if people are being productive in that meeting." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The daily-level question is not the full life question. It is the meeting-level, email-level, decision-level question: what is this for? Each instance of the question is small. The cumulative effect of asking it constantly is large. The work being done starts to align with what it is for, because the misalignments become visible at the moment they happen.

## The Disconnect Between Productivity and Fulfillment

The diagnostic value of the question becomes clearest when applied to people who have already reached the "successful" version of their productivity goals.

"You can see people who are ultra productive as far as getting tasks done, starting companies, even being successful, being rich, but there's no fulfillment. There's no sense of purpose. They say things like, 'I can't wait till I can retire' rather than 'I want to die doing this.'" -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The "I can't wait till I can retire" signature is the productivity-without-forest pattern at its endpoint. The person executed the strategy. The strategy produced the named outcomes. The endpoint state is not what was expected. The forest was never identified. The trees were the only thing actually being grown. The orchard was successful as an orchard and is providing no shade to anyone.

The contrasting signature, "I want to die doing this," is what productivity looks like when the forest is explicit and the trees are growing toward it. The productivity is enjoyable in the moment because it is what the person wants to be doing, not because it is what they have to do to get to what they want to be doing eventually.

## Tone

Slow, curious, slightly playful. The question is structurally large but Joe delivers it as a casual inquiry, not as a profound challenge. "What's all this productivity for, actually?" is asked in the same register as "what was that meeting for?" The non-loadedness of the delivery is part of why the question can be answered honestly. A loaded delivery would produce a defensive answer.

The exception is when the question needs to land harder against an entrenched identity. With someone who has built their entire identity around being productive, the playful delivery can bounce off. The shift then is to a quieter, slower delivery that gives the question time to land.
