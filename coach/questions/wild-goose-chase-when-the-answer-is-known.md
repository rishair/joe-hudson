# What's with the wild goose chase when you've had the answer?

## The Question

When the person has just acknowledged that they have already received the answer they have been searching for — usually through a past moment of having been seen, named, or shown by someone — surface the search itself as the puzzle. Why search when the answer is already known? The question makes the search visible as a choice rather than a necessity.

"What's the — wait, so why — what's with the wild goose chase when you've had the answer?" -- Joe Hudson, What Do I Offer The World?

The interruption-quality of the question matters. Joe interrupts himself ("what's the — wait, so why —") to underscore the structural strangeness of the searching-despite-knowing. The interruption mimics the listener's own bewilderment, once they see it.

## When Joe Uses It

The question comes out specifically when:

- The person has just described a past moment when their answer was clearly delivered to them
- The person has confirmed that the moment was significant ("one of the most singular moments of feeling seen")
- Despite having the answer, the person has continued to search for years
- The recognition that the answer has been there is just starting to land
- The person needs the disproportion (years of searching for a known answer) made visible to themselves

The question is for the moment when the past-data and the current-search are both present in the conversation, so the contradiction can be felt.

## What It Produces

The question typically produces:

- A pause. The person had not been holding the past-moment and the current-search in the same frame. The question puts them in the same frame.
- An attempt at explanation. "I think it's trying to fit into a framework that somebody else has created for how we articulate ourselves and for what validates us." The person often surfaces the [[contorting-into-someone-elses-framework]] pattern in their own words.
- Grief, sometimes, when the disproportion lands. Years spent searching for something already had. See [[grief-at-the-disproportion]].
- Relief, sometimes, when the search-pattern is recognized as a habit rather than a necessity. The recognition opens the option of stopping.

The question is structural rather than emotional. It does not push for a feeling. It points at a logical inconsistency and lets the person feel what they feel about it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The response usually contains the next move's material. The person's explanation surfaces the underlying pattern.

If they name the framework-fitting pattern: move to [[contorting-into-someone-elses-framework]] and ask how many times they have done this contortion. The recurrence-naming is what opens the apology offering.

If they name approval-seeking: move to [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] and surface that the search is not for the answer but for the validation.

If they name a deeper fear (of having to do something with the answer): move toward what the search has been protecting them from. The search-as-defense pattern.

If they cannot explain: the not-knowing is data. The search may be a long-standing reflex rather than a chosen strategy. Treat the reflex with curiosity, not judgment.

## Why This Question Works

The question works because it puts the contradiction in the same sentence. The person has been holding "I have the answer" (the past moment) and "I am still searching" (the current behavior) in separate compartments. Each, separately, makes sense. Together, they reveal the structural strangeness.

The interruption-quality ("what's the — wait, so why —") models the discovering of the contradiction. The coach is not delivering a prepared challenge. The coach is noticing, in real-time, that something doesn't fit. The modeling makes it easier for the person to notice the same thing.

The "wild goose chase" framing matters too. It does not pathologize the search. It does not blame. It just calls it what it is — a chase after something not where the searching is happening. Wild goose chases are a familiar pattern; the framing puts the person's specific search in a recognizable category that they can step back from.

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** Years ago, a friend introduced me, and the person he was introducing me to said, "oh, and what does she do?" And he just said, "she motivates people."
- **Joe:** Yeah.
- **Guest:** And it was one of the most singular moments of feeling seen that I've ever had.
- **Joe:** Well, there you go.
- **Guest:** Yeah.
- **Joe:** What's the — wait, so why — what's with the wild goose chase when you've had the answer?
- **Guest:** I think it's trying to fit into a framework that somebody else has created for how we articulate ourselves and for what validates us.
- **Joe:** How many times have you done that? How many times have you tried to put yourself in some framework that is going to apparently validate you to some—
- **Guest:** Oh my gosh, probably many. And it's always awkward contortionist experience.
- *(Source: What Do I Offer The World?)*

## Tone

Direct, slightly puzzled, warm. The puzzlement is real. The coach is genuinely curious about why the search continued when the answer was delivered. The curiosity is what makes the question land. A rhetorical-question version would be challenging; the genuine-curiosity version is inviting.

The "wait, so why" carries the puzzlement. The coach is sorting through what the person just said and noticing the structural oddness. The verbal stumbling is appropriate — the contradiction is genuinely surprising when first seen.

## What Not to Do With This Question

The question can be misused if the person has not actually had the answer delivered. If the past-moment was not really a clear seeing — if the person is just trying to be helpful by producing a memory that fits — the question lands as accusation rather than as inquiry. Make sure the past-moment is real before deploying the question.

The question can also be misused if it is asked too quickly. The person needs to feel the weight of the past-moment before being asked why they have been ignoring it. If the moment is named and then immediately questioned, the questioning skips the experience.

The question should not be repeated. If the first asking does not land, drop it. Repeating "but why are you still searching" becomes nagging. The first asking either opens the inquiry or it does not.
