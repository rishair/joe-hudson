# What do you think you need to be free?

## The Question

A diagnostic to surface the conditions a person has placed on their own freedom. The question is delivered as an instruction to make a list across four categories. The list itself is the work.

"They're going to be found in the things that you're working towards with anxiety, the things you beat yourself up for not having, the things you think are going to make you happy, or the things you're scared to lose. Basically, that's your list. Just write them down." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

The four categories:
1. What you are working toward with anxiety (not with grounded enthusiasm, but with the felt sense that you have to)
2. What you beat yourself up for not having
3. What you think will make you happy
4. What you are scared to lose

Each category catches a different shape of the same structure. Together they map the conditions the person has placed on their own freedom. The instruction "just write them down" matters. Holding the list in mind keeps it abstract. Writing it externalizes the structure where it can be looked at.

Variations Joe uses:
- "What's required for you to be free?"
- "What's on the list of things you think you need?"
- "What are you scared to lose? Because that's on the list too."

## When Joe Uses It

- When someone says "once I have X, then I'll be free" and Joe wants to surface the larger inventory of X's the person is carrying. See [[i-have-to-X-to-be-free]].
- When someone is in the post-acquisition phase of the prerequisite trap, having gotten the thing they thought would free them and now afraid of losing it. The fear-of-losing reveals that the prerequisite has not actually dissolved; it has just changed form.
- After establishing the principle [[nothing-is-required-for-freedom]] in conversation, as the operational follow-up. "Okay. Now you know nothing is required. So let's find your list of things you think are required."
- When someone is asking "how do I find what's blocking my freedom" and Joe wants to give them a method they can do alone.
- When the person is intellectualizing about freedom in the abstract and Joe wants to bring it into the specifics of their actual life.

## What It Produces

- A concrete, written list. The person discovers items they did not know they were carrying. Common surprises: "I thought I was over needing my parents' approval" turning out to still be near the top, or a condition the person had not articulated even to themselves becoming visible.
- A pattern across the items. Often the items have a theme. Money items cluster. Approval items cluster. Body items cluster. The cluster reveals which surrogate is doing the most work.
- The recognition that the list is the work, not the items. Removing the items is not the move. Seeing them as conditions placed on freedom is the move. The list reveals the structure. The structure is what is being held lightly going forward.
- A specific charge on one or two items. Most items are noticed and held loosely. One or two carry the heat. Those are the ones where the work runs deepest.
- A pause around items the person had been certain were not on the list. The "I don't care about money" person who notices money on their list. The "I'm not attached to my partner" person who notices the eggs-in-one-basket fear. The certainty is often the cover for the depth of the attachment.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the list is short and intellectualized:** the person is not actually doing the inventory; they are giving the right answer. Joe will name this and ask again, slower, with specifics. "What's the actual list, not the list you think you should have?"
- **If the list is long:** stay with the cluster pattern. What category has the most items? That category points at the surrogate the person is most identified with. Often money, often partner, often approval, often body, often awakening.
- **If one item is charged:** stop with that item. Trace the somatic feeling back. See [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] for the money form of this trace. The item is connected to an original imprint; the original imprint is what the work is on, not the item itself.
- **If the person identifies an item and immediately wants to remove the item:** redirect. The work is not to dismantle the conditions. The work is to see them as conditions and to question whether the freedom actually depends on them. The dismantling sometimes happens naturally. Often the conditions remain wanted (the person still wants the money, the partner, the body); what changes is the felt belief that freedom depends on them.
- **If the person discovers an item that surprises them:** stay with the surprise. Surprise is information that the condition was operating outside awareness. The seeing is the shift.
- **If the person discovers nothing they did not already know:** the list-making did not work for them in this moment. Either they are not yet ready for the surfacing, or the question needs to be asked differently. "What would you be scared to admit is on your list?" sometimes catches what the more general question missed.

## What It Does Not Do

The question does not by itself produce freedom. It produces visibility. The list is the diagnostic, not the cure. After the list, the work continues: with the principle [[nothing-is-required-for-freedom]], with feeling whatever the items are protecting against, with [[feel-your-way-to-freedom]], with the underlying surrogate analysis.

The question also does not require the items to be removed from the person's life. A person can want money and have money and still hold the money lightly enough that it is not a condition on their freedom. The list is about the felt structure, not about the inventory of possessions and goals.

## Tone

Practical. Direct. Almost like giving an assignment. There is no spiritual gravity to the delivery. The instruction "just write them down" is offered casually, the way you might suggest making a grocery list. The lightness is part of what makes the question land; treating it with ceremony makes it harder for the person to actually do it.

The follow-up to the response can have more depth as items get charged. The initial framing stays light. Heavy framing of the inventory invites performance; light framing invites honesty.
