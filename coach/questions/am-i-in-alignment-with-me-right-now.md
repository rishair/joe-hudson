# Am I in alignment with me right now?

## The Question

The question has two parts that operate as one move:

1. "Am I in alignment with me right now?"
2. "If not, what do I have to do to get into alignment?"

The two parts are required. The first part is the check. The second part is the correction. Without the correction, the check produces information but no movement. Without the check, there is nothing to correct toward.

"Then eventually that question just fades. And the only question that's left is, hey, am I acting in alignment right now? And if not, what do I have to do to get into alignment?" -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The question is presented as the operating-question that takes over once the belonging-search has been released. See [[belonging-is-the-wrong-question]] for the meta-frame.

Variations:
- "Are you in alignment with yourself right now?"
- "Is what you're about to do aligned with you?"
- "Where in this are you out of alignment with yourself?"
- "What would feel more aligned?"

## When Joe Uses It

This is an operating-question more than a single-use question. It is offered to the client as the new question they can run when the old questions (does this person like me, do I belong here, what should I do, what would they want me to do) start operating.

Specific situations where Joe reaches for it:

- After the person has accepted [[belonging-is-the-wrong-question]] and needs the replacement question to install
- When the person is running approval-seeking and needs an inward-facing operating question
- When the person is mid-decision and the decision-criteria are external (will they approve, will this be safe) and need to be replaced with the alignment-criteria
- When the person has been doing one of [[three-strategies-of-not-belonging]] and the strategy is now visible — the question replaces the strategy
- When the person is asking "what should I do?" in a way that suggests they want an external authority to decide for them

The question is also a related instrument to [[what-feels-more-in-purpose-right-now]] and shares family with [[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]]. The three questions point at slightly different inner referents (alignment, purpose, truth) but all operate by replacing external scanning with inward orientation.

## What It Produces

The question tends to produce a body-check rather than a head-answer. People often pause briefly. Sometimes they say something like "no, actually" and then notice what they had been doing.

When the answer is yes, the question confirms what was already happening; the person continues with more grounded clarity that what they are doing is theirs.

When the answer is no, the second part of the question (what do I have to do to get into alignment?) opens a small action. Often the action is very small: pause, breathe, say the thing that has been unsaid, drop the thing that doesn't fit. The smallness is part of why the question is sustainable as a daily operating-question.

The question does not produce drama. It is not a confessional question or a deep-feeling question. It is an instrument-panel question — a quick check, a small correction, continue.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person says yes (I am in alignment): Joe takes the answer as data and doesn't second-guess it. The person knows their own alignment better than the coach does.

If the person says no but doesn't know what to do: Joe sometimes follows with [[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]] or with [[how-do-you-want-to-be-with-them]], or sits in the not-knowing without trying to manufacture an action.

If the person says yes but the body says no (their stated alignment doesn't match their visible state): Joe will sometimes name the gap. The naming is gentle — the person is not lying, they are reporting the version they want to believe. The gap-naming opens the actual alignment-check.

If the person treats the question as too vague to answer: Joe sometimes makes it more specific by tying it to the immediate moment ("in the conversation you just described, were you in alignment?") or to a decision the person is weighing.

## Tone

Light. The question is offered as a tool, not a profound interrogation. The lightness is important — heavy delivery converts the question into a weight that the person feels they have to live up to, which reproduces the very problem (external pressure to perform alignment) that the question is meant to dissolve.

Joe sometimes asks it as a check-in mid-session, the way you might ask someone how their coffee is. The casualness is functional.

## When It Replaces a Different Question

The question is most useful when it is replacing a habitual question that has been running below conscious recognition. Common replacements:

- Replaces "do they like me?" with "am I in alignment?"
- Replaces "what do they want me to do?" with "what would alignment look like here?"
- Replaces "do I belong here?" with "am I being myself here?"
- Replaces "what's the right choice?" with "what's the aligned choice?"
- Replaces "am I doing this well enough?" with "am I doing this in a way that fits me?"

In each replacement, the criteria for evaluation move from outside to inside. The action that follows is generated from a different source.
