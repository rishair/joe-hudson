# What would happen if you stopped doing the caretaking thing?

## The Question

The question takes the specific caretaking behavior the person is doing and inverts it: what would happen if you simply stopped that, consistently, for a defined period?

The question is concrete. Not "what if you set better boundaries?" but "what if every time he started getting angry, you just got up and walked out? And you did that consistently for 10 weeks?"

The specificity is the lever. The vague version invites abstract agreement. The concrete version forces the person to actually imagine the consequence.

"Tell me what would happen if you, every time he started getting angry, you just got up and you just walked out. And you just consistently did this for, I don't know, let's call it 10 weeks. Every time he started losing his cool, you just got up and walked out. How long would he still be yelling at you?" -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

Variations:
- "What would happen if you stopped paying for his housing tomorrow?"
- "What if you stopped covering for her at work?"
- "What if you stopped reminding him about his appointments?"
- "What if every time she started crying you let her cry, just stayed present, did not try to fix it?"

## When Joe Uses It

When someone is in a caretaking dynamic and insists there is nothing they can do. The "I have to" or "I can't" framing is the trigger.

The question is also useful when the person can identify the caretaking but cannot see what life would look like without it. The blank where the caretaking has been is so habitual that the person cannot picture the alternative.

It is particularly useful when the person says the consequence of stopping would be catastrophic. The question makes them actually think through the consequence rather than reacting to it as a feeling.

Often paired with [[you-think-they-cant-handle-it]]: the person assumes the other will collapse if the caretaking stops. The question makes them check the assumption.

## What It Produces

Several typical responses:

**Recognition.** The person sees, often for the first time, that the caretaking is what is sustaining the dynamic. "Oh. He would stop. The anger is working because I'm managing around it. If I stopped, the anger would stop." This is the breakthrough moment. The trap reveals itself as a trade.

**Fear.** The person sees what they would have to feel if they stopped. The fear of the partner's anger fully landing. The grief of the addict's collapse. The terror of being attacked. This is useful data. The fear is what the caretaking was preventing them from feeling. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]].

**Insistence on exception.** "But you don't understand, my situation is different." The insistence is itself diagnostic. The person is protecting the dynamic, often the control the caretaking gives them. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] on the parallel between caretaker and bully both protecting their control.

**Practical reasoning.** The person starts working out what would actually happen. The reasoning often reveals that the feared outcome is less certain than they assumed. The angry partner might escalate, or might calm down. The addict might recover, or might collapse. The actual range of outcomes is wider than the catastrophic prediction.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person says "he would stop being angry," Joe holds the implication: the anger is being trained by the management. The path forward is to stop training it. Move toward [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]] or the leaving move.

If the person describes fear, Joe moves toward the fear itself. The fear is the door. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. Once the fear is felt, the impossibility of stopping the caretaking usually dissolves.

If the person insists on exception, Joe checks the exception. The check is the test in [[caretaking-prevents-the-bottom]]: do you actually have evidence that the caretaking is what is preventing the catastrophic outcome?

If the person catastrophizes, Joe asks them to feel the catastrophe in advance. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]]. The pre-feel often collapses the unbearability of the imagined consequence.

If the person says they do not know what would happen, Joe sits with the not-knowing. The not-knowing is itself an opening. The certainty that the caretaking is required dissolves into uncertainty. Uncertainty allows new action.

## Dialogue Example

From the source:

- **Person:** "My husband gets angry at me and I don't know what to do and there's nothing I can do."
- **Joe:** "Ah, BS, you like it."
- **Person:** (perplexed)
- **Joe:** "Tell me what would happen if you, every time he started getting angry, you just got up and you just walked out. And you just consistently did this for, I don't know, let's call it 10 weeks. Every time he started losing his cool, you just got up and walked out. How long would he still be yelling at you? Like, how would this work for him if this anger thing that he does stopped working?"

The move pattern:
1. Break the trapped frame with bluntness.
2. Concretize a specific anti-caretaking move.
3. Push for a duration that makes the experiment real.
4. Ask the person to actually think through the consequence.

## Why Concreteness Matters

The vague version ("what if you stopped enabling him?") fails because it stays at the level of concept. The person can agree with the principle and continue the behavior unchanged.

The concrete version ("what if you walked out every single time, for 10 weeks?") forces engagement with the actual mechanics. The person has to picture themselves doing it. The picture either produces clarity or produces resistance, and either response is useful.

The 10-week duration is also load-bearing. Most caretakers can imagine stopping for a day. They cannot imagine sustaining the stop. The duration forces the imagination to encounter what would actually happen over time, including the changes in the other person's behavior.

## What Joe Is Not Asking

He is not asking what the person should do. He is not telling them to leave or to walk out. He is asking them to actually think through the consequence of an action they have ruled out without considering.

The question is diagnostic, not prescriptive. The person may decide, after thinking it through, to continue the current behavior. But they will have made the decision rather than being driven by the unexamined "I have to."

## Tone

Direct, sometimes blunt. The trapped frame is a structural defense, and gentle handling tends to leave it intact. Joe sometimes uses humor or pointed challenge ("Ah, BS, you like it") to break the seriousness of the position.

The tone is not contemptuous. It is the tone of someone who genuinely believes the person has more agency than they are claiming. The challenge is delivered as a gift, not as an attack.
