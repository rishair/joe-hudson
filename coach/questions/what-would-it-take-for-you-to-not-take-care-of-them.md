# What would it take for you to be with them and not take care of them?

## The Question

The question in Joe's words:

"What would it take for you to be able to be with him and not take care of him? If I put you in a room with him, like, you're now back in an apartment together, you're married again, it's going to be a, um, a month together, and your only job is to not take care of him. Not take responsibility, not take ownership. But what would have to change in you to make that happen?"

The structure: take the caretaking pattern out of the deliberation about whether to stay or leave the partner, set up a hypothetical in which the caretaking is removed, and ask the person to discover what their internal state would have to be for that removal to be possible.

Variations:
- "If you were just in the same room with him for a month and your only job was not to take care of him, what would have to change in you?"
- "What would it take for you to be with [person] and not take responsibility for them?"
- "If I told you you could be in this relationship but you weren't allowed to manage his state, what would have to be different inside you?"

The hypothetical can be set in any relationship configuration: married, dating, working together, parenting, friendship. The variable Joe is making operational is the speaker's internal state, not the relationship's external form.

## When Joe Uses It

This question appears when:

- The person is presenting a stay-or-leave question about a relationship.
- The relationship has gone heavy and the speaker is described as the manager of the partner's state.
- The person is in their head deliberating about the relationship as if the relationship were the variable.
- The caretaking pattern is visible to Joe but not yet to the person.
- The person needs to feel the caretaking as the operative variable before any other work can land.

The question is asked early in the session, usually within the first few exchanges. It is a pivot move. It takes the conversation out of "should I stay?" and into "what would have to change in me?" — which is the workable question.

It is also used outside of stay-or-leave contexts whenever caretaking is showing up as the structure of the relationship the person is describing. The hypothetical-non-caretaking framing works equally well for parents, siblings, business partners, employees, friends.

## What It Produces

The question consistently produces a moment of recognition that the caretaking is the variable. The person realizes they cannot easily imagine the configuration. The caretaking is so habitual that imagining its absence is itself the difficulty.

In the source session, the person's first attempt at the answer was an honest "I think this takes me — like, I would have to — if I were taking care of him, I wouldn't be taking care of myself." The answer surfaces self-abandonment as the load-bearing structure: she cannot imagine not-caretaking him because not-caretaking him would require taking care of herself, which is the move she has not been making.

Joe's response: "Yeah, okay, there it is. Right."

The recognition is the move. The decision-frame is gone. The work is now the self-abandonment pattern.

Other common responses:

- "I don't know what I would do" — the absence of the management leaves a vacuum the person cannot imagine filling. This is data: the caretaking has been the entire structure of being-with-them.
- "I would have to trust myself" — the answer surfaces the trust issue. The caretaking is what was substituting for self-trust. See [[how-do-i-trust-myself]].
- "I would have to feel things I don't want to feel" — the answer surfaces the avoided emotion. The caretaking is the avoidance vehicle.
- "It feels slippery, I can almost get to it but not quite" — a body-level signal that the person is approaching the answer but the answer is held back. Joe will often stay with this and press a little: "What is it that you would have to face if you were responsible for him?"

## What Joe Does With the Response

The follow-up depends on what surfaces:

**If the answer surfaces self-abandonment ("I'd have to take care of myself"):** Joe lands the recognition and moves to the next layer. "And what are you trying — you're trying to manage him into happiness so that you can get what?" The question takes the caretaking forward to the strategy underneath. See [[what-are-you-trying-to-control-for]]. The strategy is usually: "to fall back in love with him" or "to get the man I used to know back."

**If the answer is "I don't know":** Joe sometimes presses for an attempt. "Just take a shot at the goal, even if it's fucked up." The press refuses the not-knowing as a stopping point. See [[give-me-a-good-attempt]].

**If the answer surfaces a feeling the person doesn't want to face:** Joe locates the feeling in the body and stays with it. The caretaking has been the avoidance of this feeling. The feeling is now the work. See [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]].

**If the answer is "it feels slippery":** Joe acknowledges the body signal and asks the second-layer question. "What is it that you would have to face if you were responsible for him? You're not, clearly. But if you were, what would you have to face?" The "you're not, clearly" preserves agency while still using the hypothetical to surface the avoided content.

The follow-up almost always lands on one of: self-abandonment, an avoided emotion, or the strategy of trading love for management. The question is shaped to produce that landing.

## Why This Question Works

The stay-or-leave question is structurally answerable only by the body's felt sense, and the body's felt sense is contaminated by the caretaking. Asking the head to choose is asking the head to do what only the unobstructed body can do.

The hypothetical-non-caretaking question removes the obstruction in imagination. The person is asked: in a world where you weren't doing the management, what would your internal state have to be? The question makes the caretaking visible by asking what its absence would require.

This is more useful than asking the person directly "are you caretaking him?" The direct question often produces denial or qualification. The hypothetical bypasses the defense. The person, trying to imagine the not-caretaking scenario, runs into the wall of what would have to change — and the wall is the diagnostic.

The question is also disarming because it removes the decision pressure. The person doesn't have to decide anything to answer the question. They just have to imagine. The imagining produces the recognition without requiring commitment. The commitment, if it comes, comes after the recognition.

## Tone

Curious, ground-level, almost casual. Joe is not setting a trap. The hypothetical is offered as a real exploration. The person is invited to think into it.

The "if I put you in a room with him for a month" framing is concrete enough to be felt. Joe sometimes adds detail — "you're married again, you're back in the apartment, you're together" — to ground the hypothetical in body-sense rather than abstraction.

The follow-up "What is it that you would have to face if you were responsible for him? You're not, clearly. But if you were, what would you have to face?" carries a small but important softening: "you're not, clearly." Joe is making sure the person doesn't take the hypothetical as an accusation. The accountability is held lightly while the diagnostic does its work.

When the person says "just the question, just the question" — meaning Joe pushed back on a tangent — that brevity is also part of the tone. The question is the work. Joe protects it from being detoured into therapy-talk about the theme of responsibility.

## Dialogue Example

From a trial-separation Q&A:

- **Person:** [describes managing husband's depression and anger, feeling more alive during the breakup window]
- **Joe:** "What would it take for you to be able to be with him and not take care of him? If I put you in a room with him, like, you, you're now back in an apartment together, you're married again, it's going to be a, um, a month together, and your only job is to not take care of him. Not take responsibility, not take ownership. But what would have to change in you to make that happen?"
- **Person:** "I think responsibility is the theme that I'm dealing with at the moment because it's like—"
- **Joe:** "just the question, just the question. What is it you would have to do to live with this man and not take responsibility?"
- **Person:** "There's something about like the trust in myself and the— I have this feeling like I can almost get to it inside of me, but it's like a little bit slippery."
- **Joe:** "What do you— what, what is it that you would— what is it that you would have to face if you were responsible for him? You're not, clearly. Like, he's his own man. But if you were, what would you have to face?"
- **Person:** "I think this takes me— like, I would have to— if I were taking care of him, I wouldn't be taking care of myself."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, okay, there it is. Right."

*(Source: Should I Stay With My Ex?)*

The dialogue shows the question working in compressed time. Within five exchanges, the caretaking pattern is named and the self-abandonment is on the table. The decision-frame is gone.
