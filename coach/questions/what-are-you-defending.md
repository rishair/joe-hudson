# What are you defending?

## The Question

The question takes several closely related forms, each prying open the defensive stance from a different angle.

- "What are you defending?"
- "What is the you that you're defending exactly?"
- "What's the thing you're actually protecting?"
- "What makes you think it's destroyable?"

The unifying move: make the object of defense visible to the defender. Defense almost always operates without the defender seeing what they are defending. Once seen, the defense often loses its grip.

"What are you defending? 'Cause we all say we're defending us. I'm defending myself. But what, like, what's the you that you're defending exactly?" -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

## When Joe Uses It

This question is reached for when someone is locked into a position from which they will not move. The position is being defended. The person experiences their stance as necessary, often as the only honest possibility.

Specific situations:
- A person in conflict who keeps explaining why they had to respond the way they did
- A person who feels misperceived and needs to correct the record
- A person in the [[fear-triangle]], particularly in bully or victim mode, where defense is the visible posture
- A person who is hardening (see [[hardening-means-personal]]) and cannot find their way back to softness
- A person stuck in a fight they cannot drop and cannot win

The question is also useful inwardly when a coach feels themselves defending something in the session.

## What It Produces

People rarely have a clean answer ready. The question reveals that what they thought they were defending is not a thing they can name. Once they try to name it, the structure of the defense becomes inspectable.

Common answers and the follow-ups they invite:
- "I'm defending myself." -> "What part of yourself, exactly?"
- "I'm defending the fact that I'm a good person." -> "What happens if someone doesn't see you as a good person? Who actually gets hurt?"
- "I'm defending my reputation." -> "What is reputation made of? What if it dissolved? What would actually be left?"
- "I don't know." -> Often the doorway. The defense was never about a known thing. It was the body's habit running.

The follow-up is the actual work. The question is the opener. The doorway is in what the person discovers they cannot quite name.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The arc of the inquiry: the more closely the person looks at what they are defending, the less defendable it appears. Either:

1. The thing being defended turns out to be something that cannot actually be destroyed (the self that survives all annihilation, in Joe's [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]] framing), in which case the defense is unnecessary.
2. The thing being defended turns out to be a constructed identity that the person is gripping. The defense is what is producing the suffering of being gripped, and releasing the grip is what opens space.

Either way, the defense relaxes. The person does not have to be convinced of anything. The looking does the work.

"There's nothing underneath any of them. There's nothing to defend." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

The line is not delivered as a teaching to memorize. It is the discovery the question is designed to produce. The coach does not need to give the answer. The question, followed through, gives the answer.

## What the Question Is Not

Not a Socratic gotcha. The intent is not to make the person feel foolish for defending something undefendable. The intent is to free them from defending something they did not realize they did not have to defend.

Not an argument that they should not defend themselves in real terms. Boundaries, exits, refusals, and self-protective actions are not what this question is targeting. The question targets the somatic and identity-level defense underneath the fight, not the practical actions a person may need to take.

Not a one-shot. The first answer is rarely the deepest. The question opens; the follow-ups deepen.

## Tone

Curious, not accusatory. Slow. Spacious. The defender is in a tight posture; the question needs to come in soft to be received rather than parried as another attack.

If the question is delivered with edge, it will be metabolized as one more attack on the defended thing, and the defense will strengthen. The question has to be asked from a place that is itself undefended.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** I'm not going to apologize. I'm just defending myself. They came at me first.
- **Joe:** Okay. What are you defending, exactly?
- **Person:** ...myself.
- **Joe:** What's the you that you're defending? What part of you?
- **Person:** (slows down) I guess... my reputation. The fact that I'm a good person.
- **Joe:** And what happens if they don't see you as a good person? Who actually gets hurt?
- *(adapted from The Power Dynamics of Fear)*
