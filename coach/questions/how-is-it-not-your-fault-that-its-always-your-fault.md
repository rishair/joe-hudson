# How is it not your fault that it's always your fault?

## The Question

The question in Joe's words:

"How is that not your fault? Um, how is it not your fault that it's always your fault, that you're always blaming yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?

The structure: take the chronic self-blame the person is reporting and apply the self-blame logic to itself. If everything is always your fault, then your always-blaming-yourself is also your fault. The recursion is the move.

Variants:
- "How is it not your fault that you're always blaming yourself?"
- "If everything is your fault, then how is it not your fault that you're stuck in this always-it's-me pattern?"
- "How come the one thing you don't blame yourself for is the blaming itself?"

The question is asked in plain tone, not as a gotcha. The recursion does the work on its own. The person hears it and the absurdity of the always-my-fault verdict begins to surface without the coach having to argue against it.

## When Joe Uses It

The question fires when a person has confirmed that they blame themselves a lot — not just for this specific event but as a general pattern. The setup is typically two exchanges:

- "How often do you blame yourself? How often is it your fault?"
- "A lot, probably."
- Then: "How is it not your fault that it's always your fault?"

Specific situations:

- After a person has framed a current event (a breakup, a fight, a failure) as their fault, and a follow-up question reveals they frame most things as their fault.
- When [[what-did-i-do-wrong-response-to-aggression]] is operating — the pattern of always asking what you did wrong, regardless of who was actually responsible.
- When the person is presenting [[i-cant-forgive-myself-for-hurting-them]] and the chronicity of the pattern needs to be surfaced before the specific instance can be worked.
- When the conversation needs to shift from "what did I do wrong in this specific event" to "what is this self-blame doing in my life."
- When the person has been doing the work for a while and the self-blame keeps re-arriving regardless of how much they have processed individual events. The pattern is the pattern.

The question is not for someone in acute self-criticism about a specific bad action they actually did. It is for someone whose self-blame extends past the available evidence — who blames themselves for things that were not their fault, or for things that were partially their fault as if they were entirely so. The recursion only lands when the chronicity is the problem.

## What It Produces

A pause. The person typically does not have an answer. The mind is built to handle "what did I do wrong?" — it is not built to handle "what makes you always think you did something wrong?" The recursion has no answer that does not undermine the premise.

The most common first response: "I'm not sure." Or silence. The body softens slightly. The verdict that has been running automatically becomes visible as a verdict.

The question opens the door for the next move: the choice between forgiving the specific instance and stopping the always-my-fault pattern entirely. See [[the-forgive-this-or-stop-blaming-everything-choice]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says "I don't know":** Stay with the don't-know for a moment. The don't-know is the wedge. Do not rush to answer it for them. Then move to the choice: "If I could give you a choice at the end of this call, you could realize how to forgive yourself for fucking up this relationship, or you could realize how to stop blaming yourself for everything. What would you choose?"
- **If the person tries to defend the pattern ("because I actually do mess up a lot"):** That is the pattern speaking. Reflect it: "So even your tendency to always say it's your fault is your fault." The absurdity has to be allowed to land.
- **If the person starts crying:** The body is recognizing the structure. Stay with the emotion. Do not redirect. The seeing is the work in this moment.
- **If the person intellectualizes ("I think it's because of my childhood"):** That is the rumination running on a new topic. Redirect to the felt experience: "Okay, but right now, in this moment, how is it not your fault that you're always blaming yourself?"

## What This Question Is Not

Not a debate question. The coach is not arguing that the person is wrong to blame themselves. The question does not say "you should stop." It just exposes the structural verdict and lets the person see it.

Not a setup for a corrective belief. The work is not to install "it is not actually always your fault" as the new conviction. It is to surface the always-my-fault as a pattern rather than as accurate self-assessment. The choice that comes after the question is what handles the corrective work, and even that is offered as a choice, not as a prescription.

Not a Socratic trap. The recursion is not a logic puzzle. The body recognizes the absurdity before the mind does. The question is felt, not solved.

## Dialogue Example

- **Client:** "I went through a breakup that was, I think, largely my fault. And I'm struggling. I feel like I betrayed myself..."
- **Joe:** "How often do you blame yourself? How often is it your fault?"
- **Client:** "A lot, probably. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "How is that not your fault? Um, how is it not your fault that it's always your fault, that you're always blaming yourself?"
- **Client:** "Um, I'm not sure."
- *(Source: Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?)*

The exchange shows the full move: presenting concern, the chronicity check, the inversion question, the "I'm not sure" that is the wedge. The next move in the session was the choice question. The recursion did its work in three exchanges and opened the door.

## Tone

Casual, almost amused. Joe asks the question the way you would ask a friend a question that has an obvious answer but the friend has not noticed yet. The "um" in his delivery ("Um, how is it not your fault") signals that he is thinking through it with the person, not delivering a verdict. The tone keeps the recursion from feeling like an attack.

The question is not delivered as challenge. It is delivered as recognition: "Have you noticed this? Let's look at it."
