# How do you want to be with them?

## The Question

The relational variant of [[how-do-you-want-to-be]]. When a person is locked into thinking about how the other person should change, or what the other person should do, or whether the relationship is salvageable on the basis of the other person's behavior — the question reorients the entire frame.

Variants:
- "How do you want to be with them?"
- "What's the relationship you want with your parents?" (when the person answers this with a controlling-them answer, the follow-up redirects: "No, I mean, you can't control anything about how they're going to do their thing in the world. What's — when you look at your parents, how do you want to be with them?")
- "How do you want to be with your partner?"
- "How do you want to be with your boss when this happens?"

The question takes the person out of the prediction-and-control loop about the other and into the only thing they can actually choose: their own way of being with the other.

## When Joe Uses It

The question fits a recognizable shape:

- The person is locked into prediction about the other ("they'll do X, then I'll have to do Y, then they'll do Z").
- The person is asking how to make the other person be different.
- The person is asking whether to be in the relationship, but the question is structurally about the other person's behavior.
- The person is at the threshold of a difficult relationship (reconnecting with estranged parents, considering staying with a partner, deciding about a job) and is paralyzed by uncertainty about what the other will do.
- The person has been in caretaking mode and has lost the question of their own stance.
- The first answer given is about what they want the other to do or be ("I want them to apologize," "I want them to see me"), not about how they themselves want to be.

The question particularly fits the **estranged-parents reconnection** shape, where the person wants contact and is afraid. The question moves the work from "will they be different" (which they often will not) to "how do I want to be with them, regardless of what they do."

## What It Produces

Often a pause, then a re-orientation. The person was solving the wrong problem (the other's behavior). The question reveals the actual problem (the person's own stance).

The answers tend to be value-shaped rather than outcome-shaped:
- "In the energy of love and acceptance."
- "Caring for myself within that."
- "Open. Curious. Not collapsed."
- "I want to be honest with him without expecting anything back."

The first answer is often the more habitual answer (the romantic-sounding one). Joe can land it without pressing, then ask for the second part — the part that includes the self. The full answer tends to be a both-and: open AND boundaried, soft AND solid, loving AND protected.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the answer is **about controlling the other** ("I want them to be respectful"): Joe redirects. "You can't control anything about how they're going to do their thing in the world." Then asks again. The redirect is the work.

- If the answer is **single-sided** ("in the energy of love and acceptance"): Joe accepts it, then waits for or invites the other side. Often the person produces it themselves: "And yet highly caring for myself within that." The both-and is the shape Joe is listening for.

- If the answer has **both sides naturally** (love AND boundary, open AND protected): Joe names the integration. "So you want to have an open-hearted, high-boundaried relationship?" The naming creates a frame the rest of the session can build on. See [[heart-opening-needs-boundaries]].

- If the answer is **"I don't know"**: Joe stays with the not-knowing. The cultural training is to know how the other should be, not how oneself wants to be. The not-knowing is sometimes the first honest contact with the question.

- If the answer is **immediately about how this feels impossible** ("I'd love that but I can't"): Joe shifts to the obstacle. What in the way is the work. See the coaching path in [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]].

## Dialogue Example

The clean form of the question and the redirect, from the parents-reconnection session:

- **Joe:** What's the relationship you want with your parents?
- **Person:** I would like to visit with them, hug them.
- **Joe:** No, I mean, you can't control anything about how they're going to do their thing in the world. What's — when you look at your parents, how do you want to be with them?
- **Person:** In the energy of love and acceptance.
- **Joe:** Great.
- **Person:** And yet highly, um, caring for myself within that.
- **Joe:** Yeah. So you want to have an open-hearted, high-boundaried relationship?
- *(Source: How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner)*

The first answer was about behavior ("visit, hug"). The redirect produced the question about being. The first response to the new question was the loving half. The second response, unprompted, brought the self in. Joe named the integration: open-hearted AND high-boundaried. That naming was the frame the rest of the session worked inside.

## Tone

Plain. Slightly firm in the redirect — Joe interrupts the first answer cleanly: "No, I mean..." The firmness is not cold; it is the firmness of someone who knows the question being avoided. The redirect is delivered as care: do not let the person solve the wrong problem.

After the redirect, the tone softens. Joe waits for the answer. He does not lead the person to the answer; he creates the question and lets the person find it.

## How This Differs From [[how-do-you-want-to-be]]

The general question is about character, life-shape, or moment-shape: who do you want to be in this life, in this body, in this moment.

The relational version is about a specific other or class of others: how do you want to be with this person, this kind of person, this relationship. The relational version is what to use when the relationship itself is the locus of the work.

The relational version is also the answer when the question of "should I be in this relationship" has been asked as a what-question. The how-with-them version is structurally a how-question wearing a relational-decision costume. The answer to "should I reconnect with my parents" is often clarified by first answering "how do you want to be with them if you do." The how reveals whether the contact is workable from the inside.

## Boundary Conditions

The question is misleading if the person is in immediate danger from the other. "How do you want to be with your abuser" is not the right question when the safety is the actual issue. The question presumes the contact is being chosen or considered, not being forced.

The question can also be deflected into spiritual bypass if the answer becomes "I want to be fully loving and accepting no matter what they do" without the self-inclusion piece. The full answer always includes both sides. The redirect when only the loving side is present: "and what about you in this?"
