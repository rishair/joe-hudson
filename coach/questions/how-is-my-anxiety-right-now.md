# How is my anxiety right now?

## The Question

A self-inquiry for working with anxiety in real time. The grammar is deliberately awkward.

- "How is my anxiety right now?"

Not "what" -- "how." Not "am I anxious" but "how is the anxiety."

"How is my anxiety right now? It's a great question. And it's a little awkward. The question's a little awkward. How is my anxiety right now? And it's designed that way because it... keeps the head out of the action a little bit, right?" -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

## Why the Awkwardness Matters

The grammatical awkwardness is doing work. The phrasing prevents the intellect from running its standard moves. The standard moves for "am I anxious" are:
- A yes/no answer
- A story about why
- A plan to fix it

The "how is" framing redirects to the felt sense. It asks for a quality, not a verdict. It treats the anxiety as something that has properties to be reported on, not something to be diagnosed and treated.

"I feel like that question goes more to the quality of the anxiety instead of a story around it." -- Brett Kistler, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

This puts the inquiry in the body rather than the head. The body knows how its anxiety is. The head knows whether to be alarmed about it. Asking "how" routes to the source of useful information.

## When Joe Uses It

Joe recommends this question as the close of the experiment of loving anxiety. After someone has decided to be with their anxiety as much as possible in a welcoming way, this question becomes the moment-by-moment check-in.

"I would recommend if you're gonna do the experiment, which I highly recommend you do, to be with your anxiety as much as possible in a way that you're grateful and you're embracing it. Then a great question is, how is my anxiety right now?" -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The question is also useful:
- When someone reports anxiety but has not located it in the body
- When the intellect is generating stories about the anxiety rather than feeling it
- When the practice of being with anxiety has slipped into observation from a distance
- When a person needs a low-stakes way to begin the inquiry without having to commit to a deep dive

## What It Produces

Asking the question well -- pausing, feeling, allowing whatever comes -- tends to produce one of several reports:

- A texture: "tingly," "tight," "buzzing," "cold"
- A location: "in my chest," "in my throat," "behind my eyes"
- A movement: "moving up," "settling down," "swirling"
- An intensity report: "lower than this morning," "spiking now"
- A surprise: the anxiety has changed since the last time it was checked

What it does not tend to produce: a list of reasons. The framing protects against that. If reasons start coming, they can be noted, but the question is not asking for them.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The response itself is the point. The question is not seeking an action. It is establishing contact with the felt sense.

- If the person reports a clear felt sense: stay with it. The reporting itself often softens the constriction.
- If the person reports a story instead of a felt sense: gently redirect. "And in your body, how is the anxiety?"
- If the person reports "I don't feel anything": treat as data. The numbness is the current state. See [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]].
- If the person reports the anxiety has shifted: notice without commenting on whether that is good or bad. The shift is just what is happening.

## Tone

Gentle, slightly playful. The awkwardness of the question is part of its character. Asking it with seriousness defeats its purpose. The question works better when it is asked with a slight smile, like checking in on an old friend.

## Connection to the Loving Practice

The question complements the practice of [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]]. The loving practice is the posture. The question is the moment-by-moment way of staying in the posture without it becoming performative.

A person practicing the loving posture without this question can drift into managing the anxiety. The question, asked periodically, returns the attention to direct contact with the actual anxiety rather than the mental representation of it.

## In Coaching

A useful question to teach a client for between-session work. The client can ask it anywhere -- at a desk, in a car, before a meeting. It takes ten seconds. It does not require closed eyes or formal practice. The lightness of the question matches the lightness of the holding that the broader practice is teaching.

When the question is reported back in session ("I tried asking how my anxiety was..."), the follow-up is to ask what was found. The reports often reveal whether the loving posture is operating or whether the management has crept back in.
