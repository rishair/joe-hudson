# What's the thing that, if it shifted in your life, would be the most profound positive movement?

## The Question

- "What's the thing that, if it shifted in your life, would be the most profound positive movement that you could conceive of?"

Variations:
- "What's the thing that, if it changed, would change everything?"
- "What do you actually want to work on?"

The full form is preferred over the short form. The length matters. "Profound positive movement that you could conceive of" pushes past the surface answer. "Most" pushes for the singular biggest thing. "If it shifted" implies it can shift. Every clause is doing work.

## When Joe Uses It

This is a topic-finder for the start of a session, used when:
- The person came in with a prepared intellectual topic that isn't alive (see [[intellectual-question-with-emotional-body]])
- The person says "I don't really know what I want to work on"
- The person is offering a small or surface topic and Joe senses there's a bigger one
- The person is looping through several possible topics without landing
- The original topic has been worked on enough that Joe wants to find the next layer

This question replaces the more common "what do you want to work on?" which often produces surface answers. The construction of this question -- "most profound positive movement you could conceive of" -- defeats the polite-small-topic response and reaches for the real one.

This question pairs with its inverse, [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]]. This one targets the positive vision; the other targets the negative absolute. Together they triangulate the core material.

## What It Produces

A pause, often. The person has to feel for the answer rather than reach for one. What comes out is usually one of:
- A relationship or family answer ("being closer to my partner," "being a better father")
- A self-relationship answer ("being able to trust myself," "feeling alive again")
- A capacity answer ("being able to feel," "being able to ask for what I want")
- A freedom answer ("not being trapped in this pattern")

Whatever comes out is the real topic. Joe treats it as the topic immediately. He doesn't say "tell me more about that" or "why does that matter." He says, in effect, "okay, that sounds like something to work on" and starts working on it.

If the answer is still surface ("I want to be more successful"), Joe will press further: "what about that would be the most profound shift?" The recursion continues until the actual material is named.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The most common move is acceptance and engagement. "Okay, that sounds like something to work on." The session shifts immediately to the new topic. The previous prepared topic is left behind.

The second move is to ask why this matters, but not the way most coaches ask it. Joe doesn't ask "why is that important to you?" -- which produces an intellectual answer. He asks something that brings the person into the felt sense of what they just named: "So how do you NOT trust yourself right now? You're talking. Decisions are happening. How is that not trust?" The question makes the named topic immediately operational.

If the answer reveals a frame Joe wants to invert (like "I want to trust my intuition" implying intuition is the work), Joe will sometimes start with the inversion: "How do you not trust it?" See [[how-do-you-not-trust-it]]. The new topic doesn't get accepted on its own terms. It gets examined.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** (after the prepared topic is set aside) "I feel like I've been trying to find this perfect question for you. No, I don't really know what that is."
- **Joe:** "What's the thing that, if it shifted in your life, would be the most profound positive movement that you could conceive of?"
- **Person:** "Being able to trust myself again."
- **Joe:** "Okay, that sounds like something to work on."
- *(Source: How Do I Trust Myself? -- One question pivots the entire session from intellectual abstraction to the real material.)*

## Tone

Direct, alert, slightly intent. The voice has a small lean in it. Joe is asking for something specific and doesn't want a polite answer. The question is delivered with the implicit signal: "tell me the real one."

The tone is not heavy. There's no portentousness. The question is asked the way you'd ask "what would you like to eat?" -- expecting an answer that the person actually knows, even if they have to feel for it.
