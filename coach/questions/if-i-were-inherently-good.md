# If I thought I was inherently good, how would I interpret that want?

## The Question

- "If I thought that I was inherently good, how would I interpret that want?"
- "If you saw yourself as inherently good, what would you make of that want?"
- "If you knew you were inherently good, what would this desire be telling you?"

The question is asked specifically in the moment a person has a want they feel ashamed of. The structure of the question forces a re-interpretation by changing the operating premise: instead of "I want X, therefore I am selfish/greedy/wrong," the person is asked to assume goodness and see what the want looks like from there.

## When Joe Uses It

When someone names a want and immediately layers shame on top of it. The shape: "I want X, but that's selfish/greedy/shallow/childish." The want has arrived already wrapped in a verdict. The question removes the verdict and asks them to look at the want fresh.

It is also useful when a person is in the early dismantling of installed shame around natural appetites (sex, money, ambition, pleasure, attention). The shame is loud enough that the underlying want cannot be heard cleanly. The reframe creates space to hear it.

The question is not asked rhetorically. It is asked to be answered. The answer is the work.

## What It Produces

The person usually goes through three stages in their answer:

1. **Resistance.** "I just want a billion dollars, what could be good about that?" The verdict is still operating.
2. **Translation.** When pressed, they begin to translate. "I want security. I want safety. I want to feel important. I want autonomy. I want to help people. I want to not have to worry."
3. **Recognition.** The thing under the want is something they immediately recognize as legitimate. The shame has been concealing it.

"So if it's like, oh, I want a billion dollars, okay, that's selfish, that's greedy, I should be ashamed of that, okay. And if you saw yourself as inherently good, what would you make of that want? Oh, I, I see that I want security. I see that I want to feel safe. I see that I want to be seen as important. I see that I want to help people. I see that I want to not be -- I want autonomy. And you would -- you could see what was behind the want." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

The recognition is the medicine. The person sees that the want they were ashamed of was a clean want in a disguised form. The shame was hiding the actual content from them.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person produces a translation, Joe stays there. The translated wants are the real material. Often more questions follow about each of the deeper wants individually, peeling them like an onion. See [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]] for what happens next.

If the person cannot produce a translation, that itself is data. The shame is doing heavy work and the inherent-goodness premise has not landed. Joe will often back up and address the inherent-goodness premise directly. See [[inherent-goodness]].

If the person uses the reframe to justify harmful behavior ("I want to control everything because I'm inherently good"), Joe stops them. This is the misuse of the question. See the cautionary section below.

## The Misuse and the Safeguard

The question is for re-interpreting a want, not for overriding the body's signal that an action is wrong. Joe is explicit about this:

"What I'm suggesting is more of when you feel the shame, interpret it through the lens of you're inherently good, not using 'I'm inherently good' to justify, to run your shame down and push it down and justify your behavior that feels like shit inside of your system." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

The diagnostic: the body. If the person uses the reframe and the body relaxes, the reframe is working. If the person uses the reframe and the body tightens, they are using the frame to suppress a natural signal. See [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]] for the underlying distinction.

"Your body doesn't like it. Like, there's no way that the person who's like, I want to control everything so that I feel safe, feels good in their body... those people are not -- in conflict in their body. They've cut themselves off from their body. Because if they felt their body, they would be screaming in pain." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

## Tone

Curious, almost playful. The question is asked the way you might ask someone to try on a different pair of glasses. The premise is not argued. It is offered as an experiment. The person is invited to look through it and report back what they see.
