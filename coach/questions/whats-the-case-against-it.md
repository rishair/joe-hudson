# Let me make the case for X. Now tell me what speaks against it.

## The Question

The two-part move. Joe's framing:

"Let me give a good case for question-based selling. Here it is. Now tell me what speaks against it." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

The leader makes the case for the change. Then explicitly invites the objections rather than pushing past them. The invitation is not rhetorical. The leader is asking to hear what is wrong with the proposal.

Variations:
- "Here's the case. What speaks against it?"
- "I want to do X. Tell me what's wrong with that."
- "Let me lay out why I think this. Now what are the reasons it might not work?"

## When Joe Uses It

When a leader is introducing a change and is about to default to convincing, motivating, or pushing the team into agreement. The convincing mode is [[enjoy-over-manage|management]]. The objection-invitation mode is flow.

The diagnostic for whether the leader needs this question: are they trying to push people into a result, or are they eager to find out what speaks against it? If they are pushing, they are managing. If they are inviting objections, they are listening to the river.

The question also surfaces when a leader has gotten "yes" too easily. Easy yes is suspicious. It often means the team has decided not to fight, not that the team is on board. The objection-invitation forces the real conversation that the easy yes was avoiding.

## What It Produces

Three things, in order:

**The list of obstacles you have to address.** The objections that surface are the things that have to be solved for the change to work. If the leader cannot address them, the proposal needed revision. Better to find this out before launching than after.

**Real buy-in.** When the objections have been heard and addressed, the team is genuinely on board, not performing alignment. The work that follows has the team's actual engagement behind it, not their compliance.

**Often, a better solution.** The objections frequently reveal a path the original proposal missed. The thing the team raised against the original is also the seed of the better version. The leader who invites objections gets to import the team's intelligence into the design.

"Then you address them, and then you're in the flow of the situation. And then most likely you're going to get a much better solution because the things that they want you to address are important to address. And if you can't address them, then they don't have real buy-in. And without real buy-in, they're not going to do as good of work. But without real buy-in, it probably means that there's a better solution out there." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

## What Joe Does With the Response

When objections surface:
- Take them seriously. Address each one directly. If you cannot address one, that is information about the proposal, not about the objector.
- Notice if you feel defensive. The defensiveness is the management instinct reactivating. The work is to stay in the inviting posture even when the objections sting.
- Consider that the objection may reveal the better solution. Hold the original loosely. The thing you wanted to do may need to morph into something the team's input made stronger.

When few or no objections surface:
- This is rarely good news. It usually means the team has not been invited to disagree before and does not yet trust the invitation. Ask again, differently. Wait for actual engagement.
- Check whether your case was so loaded with social pressure ("everyone agrees we need this") that the team felt they could not push back.
- Try framing it as an experiment with an exit ramp: "if we try this for three weeks and it doesn't work, we'll stop." See [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]]. The experimental framing makes objections easier to voice because they are not commitments.

## The Recursive Irony

The example Joe uses to introduce the move is itself an instance of the move. A sales team is being convinced to adopt question-based selling. The leader is doing it without asking any questions. The very mode of introducing the technique violates the technique.

The recursive failure is the diagnostic. Whenever the way you are doing something contradicts the thing you are trying to do, the way is the problem, not the resistance.

Question-based selling works because the salesperson follows the customer rather than managing the customer. The same logic applies to introducing question-based selling: ask the team what speaks against it rather than managing them into accepting it.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not asking for feedback. Feedback is solicited politely after the decision has been made. The "what speaks against it" question is asked before the decision is finalized, with the genuine willingness to change the decision based on the answer.

This is not playing devil's advocate. The leader is not pretending to want objections. They actually want them. The pretense version of this move is detected immediately and produces no real engagement.

This is not abdicating leadership. The leader has made the case. They have a position. They are inviting the team to test the position before locking it in. The leadership is in the inviting, not in the conceding.

## Tone

Open. Specifically *eager* for the objections, not braced for them. The shift from "I have to defend my idea" to "I want to find out what's wrong with my idea" is the entire move. The eagerness is felt by the team before any words are spoken.

If the leader cannot get to genuine eagerness, the move will not work. They will perform the question without the openness. The team will perform the objections without the depth. Both sides will leave the meeting having gone through motions without doing the work.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader is about to introduce a change and is feeling the urge to convince.
- A leader has been getting "yes" too easily from their team and the work has been suffering.
- A leader is operating in [[enjoy-over-manage|management mode]] and is looking for the practical pivot to flow.
- A team has stopped voicing objections and the leader wants to restore real engagement.
- A leader is about to spend significant resources on a decision and wants to stress-test it cheaply first.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[enjoy-over-manage]]: this question is one of the operational moves that distinguishes flow from management.
- Toward [[follow-the-demand]]: the objections are the team's actual demand surfacing. Following it is the principle in action.
- Toward [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]]: the experimental framing makes the "what speaks against it" question easier to ask honestly.
- Toward real buy-in, which produces better work, which produces less need for future management. The compound effect.
- Toward the team learning that objections are wanted, which changes the culture over time. See [[everybody-wants-the-same-kind-of-team]].
