# What Makes You Push Yourself?

## The Question

"What makes you push yourself?"

Variations:
- "What makes you try?"
- "What's the trying?"
- "What makes it that you have to push so hard?"

The question takes the live pushing or trying as a fact and asks for its source. It does not ask whether the person is pushing themselves (the pushing is already established). It does not ask the person to stop pushing. It asks what is driving the push.

## When Joe Uses It

This question follows [[come-to-me-without-contradiction]] or [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]] once the pushing or trying has been made visible in the room. The person has seen — at least briefly — that they are pushing themselves right now. The question then asks them to look at what is producing the push.

The question is also available when the person has named the trying themselves. "I'm trying to ask the right question" or "I keep trying to get it right" or "I push myself a lot" — any of these is an entry point.

The question is not asked rhetorically. Joe wants an answer. The answer is usually a specific belief, a specific fear, or a specific childhood structure. The answer is the material to work with next.

## What It Produces

The most common first answer is some version of "I'm afraid of doing something wrong." This is structurally significant — the pushing is being run to prevent "wrong." Joe often deploys [[tell-me-one-thing-youve-done-wrong-when-you-werent-pushing-yourself]] as the follow-up, which inverts the apparent logic.

Other common answers:
- "I had to be something else to be loved." The pushing was originally installed as the price of love. Joe pivots to [[why-dont-you-just-love-yourself-as-you-are-right-now]] — the cut-to-the-chase move.
- "I'll be rejected if I don't." The pushing is rejection-prevention. The work moves to feeling what gets pushed away.
- "Otherwise I won't get anything done." The pushing is the productivity-engine. The work moves to [[im-afraid-i-wont-get-anything-done]] and the brace as engine.
- "I don't know." The "I don't know" itself is sometimes the data — the system has run the pushing for so long that its source is invisible. The work moves to feeling the pushing somatically rather than naming it intellectually.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The response determines the next move. The pattern: locate the engine, then dissolve the engine, then watch what the pushing was preventing the person from doing arrive on its own.

- If the answer is "I'm afraid of doing something wrong": deploy [[tell-me-one-thing-youve-done-wrong-when-you-werent-pushing-yourself]]. The inversion exposes that the pushing IS the thing producing "wrong," not the prevention of it.
- If the answer is about love (had to be something else to be loved): pivot to [[why-dont-you-just-love-yourself-as-you-are-right-now]]. The cut-to-the-chase move. The pushing was a strategy for getting love; love is available now without the strategy.
- If the answer is about rejection: stay with what would be felt if the pushing stopped. Often the pushing is preventing the felt sense of rejection that already happened.
- If the answer is about productivity or function: see [[im-afraid-i-wont-get-anything-done]] for the broader concern this opens.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "What, what makes you push yourself?"
- **Client:** "So afraid of doing something wrong."
- **Joe:** "Tell me one thing you've done wrong when you aren't pushing yourself. You're pushing yourself right now. Question?"
- **Client:** "Feels like I haven't done anything wrong and I haven't pushed myself. Like nothing's coming to mind."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Now think about all the things that you think you've done wrong and how many of them are happening while you're pushing yourself."
- **Client:** "It's me, all of them."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Stop Trying So Hard)*

A second iteration in the same session:

- **Joe:** "So what makes you push yourself?"
- **Client:** "Like I had to be something else to be loved. The kid."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So why don't we just cut to the chase? Why don't you just love yourself as you are right now?"
- *(Source: Why You Can't Stop Trying So Hard)*

The two iterations show the question's diagnostic range. The first answer ("afraid of doing wrong") gets the inversion follow-up. The second answer ("had to be something else to be loved") gets the cut-to-the-chase pivot. The question itself is the same; the response determines the path.

## Tone

Plain, curious, slightly insistent. Joe is not asking rhetorically. He wants an actual answer. The voice is matter-of-fact. The question carries no charge against the pushing — the pushing is just a thing the person is doing, and the question is about its source.

The question is usually short. Joe does not pad it with caveats. "What makes you push yourself?" Five words. The brevity prevents the person from converting the question into a topic to discuss; it asks for a specific answer.
