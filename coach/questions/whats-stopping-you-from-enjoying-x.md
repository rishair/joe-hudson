# What stops you from enjoying X? / How would you NOT enjoy your X?

## The Question

The diagnostic inverse of [[how-would-you-enjoy-x]]. Where the positive question invites the move toward enjoyment, the negative question surfaces the specific mechanism blocking it. The answer to this question is almost always the operative material in the session.

Variations:

- "What stops you from enjoying [your shame / your pain / your experience]?"
- "How would you not enjoy your shame?"
- "What stops you from enjoying chasing or avoiding? What stops you from enjoying shame? What is the essential thing that's happening?"
- "What stops you from enjoying the sadness?"

## When Joe Uses It

Two specific moments:

1. **After the positive question has been asked and the person cannot find the move.** The "how would you enjoy your shame?" landed as a category error or produced silence. Joe inverts. "How would you NOT enjoy it?" The person, asked the inverse, can usually answer immediately. The thing they were going to do automatically is the thing they were unable to identify.

2. **As a structural diagnostic question.** Even when the person has found some enjoyment, Joe sometimes asks the inverse to make the blocking mechanism conscious. The person who can enjoy a feeling sometimes does not know what they were previously doing to stop themselves. Naming the obstacle makes the freedom durable.

The pairing is the move. The positive question and the negative question used in alternation map both the path forward and the mechanism that has been blocking the path. Either one alone is incomplete.

## What It Produces

The clarifying answer. The person, asked what stops them, names the specific brake: "convincing myself that I shouldn't feel it." "Believing the story that you despise me for it." "Pulling the brakes." "Going into my head to fix it." "Worrying I'll be a burden."

The named obstacle is then the next piece of material. The shame underneath, the story about being judged, the brake, the fixing-mode — whatever the person names, that is what the session can now work with.

> "What stops you from enjoying your experience? Exactly. That's shame."

In this exchange, Joe is naming the structural diagnostic: shame is, by definition, the thing that stops enjoyment of experience. The inverse question surfaces it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person names a story ("believing you despise me"):** Joe treats the story as a story, not as truth. The story is what is in the way. The work is on the story-as-obstacle, not on the content of the story.
- **If the person names a brake ("pulling the handbrake"):** Joe moves into the brake. What does it feel like? When did it start? What is it protecting against? See [[pulling-the-brakes]].
- **If the person names a fixing-mode ("I get really stuck in what interventions I need to make"):** Joe acknowledges that intervention can be needed and is not the brake; the brake is the rushing-into-head as escape from the body. The discrimination matters.
- **If the person names shame:** Joe treats this as the central material. The work moves to feeling the shame, applying enjoyment to the shame, finding what the shame is protecting against. See [[shame-as-disconnection]].
- **If the person cannot name what stops them:** Joe lets the body answer. "Just notice in your body what happens when you try to enjoy it." The somatic noticing produces information the head was not delivering.

## Why the Inverse Works When the Positive Doesn't

The positive question presupposes possibility. For someone who has been postponing enjoyment their whole life, the possibility itself can feel inaccessible. The inverse question does not require the possibility to be felt; it only requires the person to notice what they have already been doing.

A person can usually identify what they are doing to NOT-enjoy long before they can identify how to enjoy. The not-doing has been their lifelong practice. They know it well. The positive question asks them to do something new. The negative question asks them to notice something familiar.

The structural function of asking both: the inverse names the brake; the positive invites the release. The two together map the whole movement.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "Okay, so you're feeling the shame. What -- how would you enjoy your shame?"
- **Person:** [Names body movement, "feels kind of nice"]
- **Joe:** "Um, and right now, how would you not enjoy your shame?"
- **Person:** "Convincing myself that I shouldn't feel it, or that you despise me for it. Like believing that story. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "So what is it that stops you from enjoying your experience? Exactly. That's shame."
- *(Source: How Do I Stop Postponing My Enjoyment?)*

The exchange demonstrates the structural use: the positive question surfaces the small allowance; the inverse names the mechanism that has been preventing it; Joe then identifies that the mechanism is, by definition, shame.

## Tone

The question is asked the same way as its positive twin: curious, light, experimental. There is no implication of judgment. The person is not failing to enjoy. They are running a mechanism that prevents enjoyment, and the work is to see the mechanism.

The question almost has a tone of "show me how you do this." There is craft in it. Joe is asking the person to demonstrate the very thing that has been operating outside their awareness, so it can come into awareness and become optional.
