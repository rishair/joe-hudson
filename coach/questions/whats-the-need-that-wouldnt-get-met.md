# What's the need that wouldn't get met if you were welcoming?

## The Question

The full form Joe uses in the source session:

> "What's the need that wouldn't get met if you were welcoming?"

The shortened form when the context is established:

> "What's the need that wouldn't get met?"

The question assumes the pattern is doing a job. It asks: if welcoming replaced the pattern, what need that the pattern was serving would now go unmet? The question is asked sincerely — there is often a real need the pattern was serving, and the answer matters.

## When Joe Uses It

- After the person has identified a self-criticism, comparative mind, perfectionism, or other contracting pattern they want to release
- After the person has been welcoming themselves and is in contact with what the welcoming feels like, so they can compare it to what the pattern provides
- When the person is on the verge of dropping the pattern but is unconsciously holding onto it because of what it provides
- When the person says something like "I tell myself that if I didn't criticize myself, I wouldn't have the drive to improve" — the question makes the implicit need explicit
- After [[welcome-the-pattern-by-naming-its-brilliance]] has surfaced one function of the pattern, the question probes whether there are others
- Before [[what-would-make-it-that-you-dont-deserve-this]] as part of the closing sequence, to clear remaining structural blocks

## What It Produces

- The person usually names a need the pattern was serving: drive, performance, safety, the ability to compare and improve, protection from being attacked first
- Often the person immediately notices that the named need would actually be met under welcoming, not unmet — that the pattern was providing a worse version of what welcoming would provide better
- Sometimes the person discovers there is no need that would go unmet, which produces a kind of laughter or relief — "then what's the problem?"
- The question short-circuits the unconscious holding-onto-the-pattern by making the function explicit and testing whether the function is actually defensible

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person names a need:** ask the follow-up. "If you were welcoming that [function], how would that [feel]?" This tests whether the welcoming actually fails to meet the need or whether the welcoming meets it better.
- **If the person discovers the need would still be met:** stay with the discovery. The person has just dissolved one of the structural justifications for the pattern. Don't rush to the next thing.
- **If the person says "then what's the problem?":** this is the structural recognition that there is no held-onto reason for the pattern. Sit with it. The pattern's grip loosens at this exact moment.
- **If the person can't name a need:** the pattern may have been operating without an active function for a long time. Move toward [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] and let the welcoming work do the dissolving.
- **If the person names a need that genuinely wouldn't be met under welcoming:** take it seriously. Some patterns serve real needs (vigilance in unsafe environments, for example). The work is not always to drop the pattern. It is sometimes to honor what it does and find a more conscious way to meet the need.

## Dialogue Example

The exchange from the source session, after Joe has surfaced that the person criticizes themselves and resists the entire experience of welcoming:

- **Joe:** "So your question was, how do you love yourself, or how do you allow yourself the needs? So what's the need that wouldn't get met if you were welcoming?"
- **Guest:** "What's the need that wouldn't get met if I was [welcoming]?"
- **Joe:** "If any?"
- **Guest:** "A need to do better, to compare, to —"
- **Joe:** "If you were welcoming that, how would that —"
- **Guest:** "Then I wouldn't — then what's the problem?"
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

The "then what's the problem?" is the structural recognition. The guest realizes that the need (to do better, to compare) would still be met under welcoming — in fact, welcoming the comparative mind is the path to having it function without the self-attack. There is no need the welcoming fails to serve. The pattern's structural justification collapses in one sentence.

## Tone

Curious and unhurried. The question is not a setup. Joe is genuinely asking. The pattern might actually be serving a need, and if it is, the conversation needs to take that seriously. The unhurried tone signals that Joe is not trying to trick the person into a particular answer.

The "If any?" added in the source session is important — it gives the person permission to say "none." If the question is asked as if there must be a need, the person will manufacture one to comply. The "If any?" relaxes that pressure.

## Variations

- **"What does the self-criticism give you that welcoming wouldn't?"** — a slightly different framing for when the person is specifically attached to a critical voice as productive
- **"If you let that pattern go right now, what would you lose?"** — for when the person needs the loss made concrete before they can examine whether it's real
- **"What does the comparative mind do for you?"** — for when the specific pattern is the comparative mind and the function-naming needs to happen before the welcoming
- **"What would stop working in your life if you welcomed all of this?"** — for when the person frames the welcoming as something they're afraid would have practical costs
