# What's the scorecard that can't be taken away from you?

## The Question

- "What would be the internal scorecard that says I'm good, I'm valid, that can't be taken away from you?"
- "What's the scorecard that can't be taken away from you, the internal one?"
- "What if that's the scorecard? How much you trust yourself?"

The question has two phases. The first phase opens the inquiry: "what would the internal scorecard be?" The second phase, after the person locates something, often names self-trust specifically: "what if that's the scorecard?"

## When Joe Uses It

After [[what-if-there-was-no-scorecard]] has destabilized the external scorecard and the person has felt the somatic drop of its absence. The person has named "I'd be lost / rudderless." Joe has stayed with the felt absence. Now the move is to invite a relocation.

The question is not asked at the start. It is asked after the work of dissolving the external structure has been done. Asked too early, it produces an intellectual answer ("self-love, I guess"). Asked after the body has registered the absence, it produces a felt answer that the person can actually feel from.

The triggering reads:
- The scorecard has been named ("money / likes / status").
- The "what if there was no scorecard" question has been asked and the felt-absence has been registered.
- The person has tried at least one escape route (intellect, finding a new scorecard) and Joe has closed it.
- The person is now in a slightly spacious state, often visible somatically — they have stopped grasping for the next scorecard, even briefly.

The question is also useful as a standalone move when a person is reporting that their self-worth tracks an external metric. The question can be asked directly: "What's the version of that scorecard that can't be taken away?" The implicit invitation is that the scorecard structure is fine; the substrate is what needs to shift.

## What It Produces

Usually the first answer is something like:
- "My ability to love others."
- "My integrity."
- "My capacity to be present."
- "The way I show up."

These are usable starting points. The person has located something internal that functions as a metric. Joe often acknowledges these and then introduces self-trust as the deeper substrate:

- "What if that's the scorecard? How much you trust yourself? You put points on the board the more you trust yourself. Your bank account is self-trust."

The reframe to self-trust matters because:
1. **Self-trust is measurable internally.** The person can feel whether they trusted themselves in any given moment. The "ability to love others" is sometimes more abstract; self-trust is concrete in the body.
2. **Self-trust can't be revoked.** The market can drop. The likes can stop. The capacity to love others can be challenged by circumstance. The trust of self is the one substrate that the person has full sovereignty over.
3. **Self-trust often turns out to have been operating all along.** This becomes the next move: see [[ask-the-witnesses]] — "If I asked your wife how long you've been working on trusting yourself, what would she say?" The answer surfaces that the new scorecard is not a new project. See [[identity-lags-behind-growth]].

The response to the reframe is often a doubled feeling: "deflating and inspiring at the same time." Deflating because the work has already been done and the person did not give themselves credit. Inspiring because the work has already been done and the person did not give themselves credit. Same fact, two flavors. The body is sorting which to inhabit.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person locates an internal answer (love, integrity, presence):** Joe acknowledges it and then often pivots specifically to self-trust as the underlying substrate. The pivot is not a correction; it is a deepening. The person's first answer (love, integrity) is usually downstream of self-trust. Self-trust is what makes the loving or the integrity possible.

**If the person can't find an answer immediately:** Joe waits. The pause is part of the question. The body is searching. He may rephrase: "What's the thing that, no matter what happens externally, would still tell you you're good?" The rephrasing keeps the question alive without giving the answer.

**If the person says "but that can't be measured":** Joe agrees with the limit and reframes. "You know, it can't be measured accurately. But yeah, there's definitely a different sensation in one's body if you have a capacity to love, or if you have more or less of a capacity to love." The body has its own measurement that doesn't require external instruments.

**If the person resists the move toward self-trust ("I'm not sure I trust myself"):** Joe holds the position. "What if that's the scorecard? You put points on the board the more you trust yourself." The resistance is treated as information, not as an obstacle. The person is reporting where the work is.

**Once the person has accepted self-trust as the scorecard:** Joe moves to the demonstration that this scorecard has already been operating. See [[ask-the-witnesses]] for the move that surfaces the lifetime track record. The session typically ends here, with the person recognizing the new scorecard as the old practice.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "The thing that's interesting about yours, the scorecards you choose, they can be taken away from you. They're not completely within your control. What would be the internal scorecard that says I'm good, I'm valid, that can't be taken away from you?"
- **K:** "My ability to love others. That can't be taken away. It also can't be measured."
- **Joe:** "You know, it can't be measured accurately, but yeah, there's definitely a different sensation in one's body if you have a capacity to love, or if you have more or less of a capacity to love."
- *(Source: What's Your Scorecard? — the question, the first internal answer, and the body-measurement reframe.)*

Later, after working through more of the somatic territory:

- **Joe:** "So what if that's the scorecard? How much you trust yourself?"
- **K:** "Oof. That was both deflating and inspiring at the same time."
- **Joe:** "What did it deflate?"
- **K:** "Ah, it's so much easier to trust than a scorecard. The work's done for you. It's trust that, I'm not sure I trust myself."
- **Joe:** "What if that's a scorecard? You put points on the board the more you trust yourself. Points lead — your bank account is self-trust. Or your likes are self-trust."
- **K:** "I mean, feels pretty fucking awesome, which is weird because I always trusted myself when there was a scorecard. Give me a scorecard, I'm gonna find a way to rack up points on it. So there's tremendous amount of trust there. But when you remove that, I'm like, oh wait."
- *(Source: What's Your Scorecard? — the pivot to self-trust and the "I always trusted myself when there was a scorecard" recognition.)*

## Tone

Slightly mischievous, a bit pleased with the move. Joe is offering a way out that doesn't dismantle the person. The scorecard structure (which the person's identity has organized around) gets to stay. Only the substrate shifts. There is a kind of structural elegance to the move and Joe seems to enjoy it.

The tone shifts to steady when the person resists. When K says "I'm not sure I trust myself," Joe doesn't argue. He holds the proposed scorecard in place: "What if that's a scorecard? You put points on the board the more you trust yourself." The steadiness invites the person to try the proposal on rather than to defend against it.
