# Are you stuck or do you feel stuck?

## The Question

The opening diagnostic question for any stuck-presentation. The variant forms:

- "Are you stuck or do you feel stuck?"
- "Are we working on the fact that you're stuck, or are we working on the fact that you feel stuck?"
- "Are you trapped or do you feel trapped?"

The question surfaces the distinction that almost every stuck-presentation collapses: a feeling is being reported as if it were a fact. The question reopens the space between the feeling and the fact so the work can happen on the feeling rather than on the imagined fact.

The question gets reintroduced in the source as an example of the canonical opening move:

"This reminds me of the beginning of another coaching session that you had with another person who was stuck and your first question was, are you stuck or do you feel stuck? Because they came in saying, I feel stuck. Literally, that was what they said." -- Brett Kistler, How To Get Unstuck

The question's force comes from how immediately it changes what the rest of the session is about. Until the question is asked, the conversation is about a hypothetical fact that the person has been treating as immovable. After the question, the conversation is about a feeling that can be worked with.

## When Joe Uses It

This question shows up early -- usually within the first few minutes of a session where someone has presented with stuckness or trapped-ness. The reasons to use it:

- The person has come in saying "I feel stuck" but is talking about the situation as if it were the stuckness
- The person is describing circumstances they cannot change rather than feelings they could feel
- The coach is about to be pulled into circumstance-talk that will go nowhere
- The session needs to move to the layer where work is possible
- The person needs to make the distinction themselves so they own the shift

The question also belongs at moments later in the session when the person re-asserts the fact-frame. Someone in the middle of feeling-work who says "but I really am stuck" can be brought back with a soft repetition of the question. The distinction needs to keep being made until the work has had enough time to make it self-evident.

## What It Produces

The question produces one of three responses, each of which gives the coach useful information:

**Response one: the person picks "feel stuck."** This is the most common and the most productive. Once the person has named the work as work on a feeling, the session can move to "how does the stuckness feel?" and into the felt material. See [[emotional-movement-unsticks-stuckness]] and [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]].

**Response two: the person insists "I really am stuck."** The person is unwilling to make the distinction. This is information about their relationship to the felt state -- they are deeply identified with the stuck-fact. The coaching move is to surface counter-evidence: their life looked different a year ago, they just took the action of coming to this session, etc. See [[treating-felt-state-as-factual-reality]] for the read and the response.

**Response three: the person hesitates, becomes thoughtful, says "huh, I guess I feel stuck."** This is the most diagnostic. The hesitation indicates the distinction is new but landing. The shift from fact to feeling is happening in real time. The next move is to ask how the stuckness feels and proceed into the work.

The question does not require a specific answer to be useful. The asking itself surfaces the distinction. Even a defensive response to the question is information that the work has begun.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person picks "feel stuck," the immediate next move is:

"Great. And how does stuck feel?" -- Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

This is the entry into the felt material. The session has moved to the right layer. The next steps follow: [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]] if the felt material is being held just-below-tolerance; [[be-intimate-with-the-feeling]] if the felt material is available and needs contact; [[lets-go-there]] when the catastrophic image is named.

If the person resists the distinction, Joe surfaces counter-evidence the person already has:

"What did your life look like a year ago?" -- Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

The counter-evidence isn't designed to win an argument. It's designed to surface what the person already knows -- their life has been changing, they are not stuck-in-fact -- so the distinction can be felt rather than just argued. Once the counter-evidence is acknowledged, the question can be re-asked.

If the person agrees but with a "yes, but" qualifier ("yes, but for the last year it's felt stuck"), Joe stays gentle and persistent:

"Wait, I'm with you. I'm not gonna -- but, but you said you -- so you feel stuck. You just said it felt stuck. Okay, if your life looked a lot different, then it's not actually as stuck. I'm just saying, are we working on the fact that you're stuck, or are we working on the fact that you feel stuck?" -- Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

The persistence is important. The distinction is the prerequisite for the work; the work cannot proceed if the fact-frame stays operational. Joe re-asks until the person makes the choice.

## What the Question Does Structurally

The question is doing precise structural work:

1. **It separates the speaker from the report.** "I am stuck" identifies the speaker as the stuckness. "I feel stuck" leaves the speaker intact and locates the stuckness as something they are experiencing. The grammar of the second is workable; the grammar of the first is not.

2. **It moves the work to the right layer.** A "fact" can only be worked on by changing the fact -- by leaving the marriage, quitting the job, etc. A "feeling" can be worked on by feeling it. The question moves the work from the impossible layer (changing the fact) to the possible layer (feeling the feeling).

3. **It gives the person agency.** By asking which one we're working on, Joe is letting the person choose what kind of session this is. The choice is the first act of agency in a presentation that has been organized around having no agency. The act of choosing is itself unsticking.

4. **It opens the option-space.** The person who treats "I am stuck" as a fact has no options. The person who recognizes "I feel stuck" has many: feel it, amplify it, drop into it, work with it, let it move. The question opens the option-space.

5. **It signals the coach's stance.** Joe is not going to validate the fact-frame. The question signals that this conversation will not proceed on the basis of "you really are stuck and we need to figure out how to fix your unsticky situation." The signal sets the tone for the whole session.

## Dialogue Examples

The canonical exchange from the related session:

- **Person:** "I'm not happy with the current state of things... I feel stuck because the known is more familiar."
- **Joe:** "How long, what percentage of your life from 20 years old till today would that sentence be true?"
- **Person:** "Maybe 30 to 50. So I've taken a lot of risk, but I still keep finding myself in this space."
- **Joe:** "This what space of being unsatisfied with the way things are?"
- **Person:** "Yeah, feeling stuck, but feeling so terrified of making the next move that it's more familiar and safer to be in the stuck and unhappiness."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, I just want to point out that you just took an action of going into the unknown and proving that you're not stuck."
- *(Later)*
- **Joe:** "Are we working on the fact that you're stuck, or are we working on the fact that you feel stuck?"
- **Person:** "I think feeling stuck. Yeah, yeah, yeah."
- **Joe:** "Great. And how does stuck feel?"
- *(Source: I Am Scared Of The Unknown)*

The question lands after Joe has surfaced enough counter-evidence (the historical percentage, the present-tense action of being in the session) that the distinction is undeniable. Then the question completes the move and opens the work.

## Tone

Light, almost conversational, with a small lift on the second clause. The question is not delivered as a gotcha or a challenge. It is delivered as a clarifying question -- "let me understand what we're working on here." The light tone keeps the question from being a takedown.

When the person hesitates, the silence is patient. Joe is not rushing to fill the gap. The thinking-through is part of the work. The question needs room to land.

When the question has to be re-asked because the person re-asserted the fact-frame, the tone stays warm. The "I'm with you, I'm not gonna -- but" qualifier matters. Joe is not arguing. He is gently insisting on a distinction that the person can make.

## What This Question Is Not

It is not a denial of the felt experience. The point of the question is precisely to make the felt experience workable by separating it from the fact-frame that has been preventing the work. The question validates the feeling by giving it a place to be worked with.

It is not the same as asking "is your situation really that bad?" That question challenges the circumstances; this question challenges the framing. The circumstances may be genuinely difficult. The question is about whether they are stuck-as-fact or felt-as-stuck.

It is not the only opening move. Some stuck-presentations need other openings -- a question about what the person wants, a question about the felt sense, a question about the history. This question is useful when the fact-frame is strongly operational and needs to be surfaced before any other work can land.
