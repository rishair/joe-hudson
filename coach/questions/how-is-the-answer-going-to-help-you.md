# How is the answer going to help you?

## The Question

> "How is the answer to that question going to help you? What's the strategy of getting that question answered get you what?"

The question is asked in response to the person's opening question, before answering it. The move is to refuse the surface form of the question and ask what answering it would actually deliver. The presumption is that the question is a proxy for a felt experience the person is requesting, and the felt experience is the real subject.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person opens with a "how do I X?" question (how do I love myself, how do I trust myself, how do I stop doing X) that is framed as a request for technique
- When the question, taken at face value, would produce a teaching that bypasses the felt experience the person is actually after
- Before answering the question, to surface what an answer would do for the person — which often reveals that the person already has access to the thing they're asking about and is requesting permission to have it
- When the question is intellectually formulated but the person is visibly emotional, suggesting the question is a head-level proxy for a body-level request
- Early in a session, as the first move that converts a request for information into a request for experience

## What It Produces

- The person usually says something like "some way of [the thing I want]" — they articulate that they want the experience, not the answer
- The reframing makes visible that the question was a proxy for a felt request
- The person often realizes the question can be replaced by the direct request (instead of "how do I love myself?" the actual ask is "I want to love myself")
- The conversation pivots from teaching to demonstration; the next move is usually to have the person attempt the thing rather than explain it. See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] for the typical follow-up.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person articulates the underlying felt request:** acknowledge it and pivot to having them attempt the experience directly. "So the question isn't 'how do I love myself?' It's 'I want to love myself.'" Then: "Show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now."
- **If the person can't articulate what answering would give them:** the inability is the data. The question may have been an autopilot question rather than an actual request. Slow down and ask what they actually want from the session.
- **If the person resists the move and insists on having the question answered:** answer briefly and then return to the underlying request. Some persons need to feel the question respected before they can drop it.
- **If the person says "I want a technique":** stay curious. "Okay, and what would the technique do for you?" Usually the answer is "give me the experience of X" — at which point the pivot to demonstration is again available.

## Dialogue Example

The opening sequence from the source session:

- **Guest:** "Why do I tend to dismiss my accomplishments and also more than ignore my needs? I feel like I dismiss them, or feel like I'm not right to have them."
- **Joe:** "How is the answer to that question going to help you? What's the strategy of getting that question answered gets you what?"
- **Guest:** "I mean, my immediate reaction is some way of loving myself maybe more."
- **Joe:** "Okay. So what makes it — the question isn't, how do I love myself? Or —"
- **Guest:** "Yeah, yeah, I mean, it's literally that."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

The exchange converts the diagnostic question ("why do I do this") into a felt request ("some way of loving myself more"), which Joe then converts into a demonstration ("show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now").

## Tone

Curious, not challenging. The question is asked the way one might ask "what are you actually after here?" without irritation. It is a sincere inquiry into what the person is requesting, not a refusal to engage.

The pace is unhurried. Joe gives the person time to consider what an answer would actually do for them. The pause is part of the move — many people have never been asked this and need a beat to notice that they're asking a question they don't actually know what they'd do with the answer to.

## Variations

- **"What would you do with the answer?"** — a slightly more grounded variant
- **"What would change if you knew?"** — surfaces the felt expected change
- **"What's the thing you're actually asking for here?"** — more direct, when the person can handle the directness
- **"If you got the perfect answer right now, what would happen?"** — for when the person needs to imagine the outcome before they can see the underlying request

## Anti-Pattern Risk

The move can come across as dismissive if delivered without warmth. The person came with a real question. The move is not "your question is wrong." It is "let's get more specific about what you're actually after." The tone has to communicate that the question is being taken seriously, not deflected.

The other risk: using the move on every question. Some questions are just questions, and answering them directly is the right move. The question is for situations where the surface question is clearly a proxy for a felt experience and answering it at the surface level would bypass the actual work.
