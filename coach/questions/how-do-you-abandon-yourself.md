# How do you abandon yourself?

## The Question

Asked specifically — in a specific relationship, in a specific situation, in a specific moment of the present session. Not "do you abandon yourself" (which produces a yes/no answer) and not "what is your self-abandonment pattern" (which produces a theory). The question is asked about a concrete instance and expects a concrete answer.

Variants:

- "How do you abandon yourself in your relationship with your ex?"
- "How do you abandon yourself with the voice in your head?"
- "How do you abandon yourself in this situation?"
- "And how is that abandonment? How's that self-abandonment?" (follow-up that converts a generic answer into the specific naming)
- "Where does the abandonment happen for you here?"
- "How are you abandoning yourself right now?"

The question presupposes that self-abandonment is happening. It does not invite the person to evaluate whether they abandon themselves. It invites them to describe the specific shape of the abandonment in a specific context.

## When Joe Uses It

This question is reached for once the person has acknowledged, in some form, that something like self-abandonment is part of the picture. The acknowledgment can be explicit ("yeah, I've been abandoning myself") or implicit (the person describes a relational pattern in which their own wants get dismissed). Joe does not introduce the concept of self-abandonment cold. He uses the question once the conceptual territory is shared.

Specific situations:

- After the person describes a relationship in which they bend to the other person's wants and then get resentful. The bending IS the self-abandonment; the question makes it specific.
- After the person has described an inner debate in which one voice dismisses another. See [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]]. The question makes the dismissal visible as self-abandonment.
- In sequence across multiple contexts — asked first about an external relationship (the partner, the ex), then about an internal relationship (the inner voice). The sequence installs the recognition that the same pattern is running everywhere.
- When the person has been describing a decision-frame that is producing exhaustion. The exhaustion is often the energy cost of running the dismissal of the want. The question surfaces what is being dismissed.
- When [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]] is the operative principle and Joe needs to bring it down from concept to specific instance.

The question is also used to install the spectrum frame. By asking "how do you abandon yourself" in multiple contexts, the person discovers that the abandonment has a particular shape — and that shape can be present in any context. The recognition makes the abandonment something to be noticed and shifted in real time, not a fixed identity feature.

## What It Produces

The answer is usually concrete and quickly arrived at. The person knows how they abandon themselves; they have just never been asked.

In the source session, the two answers were:

- About the ex: "I tend to bend to what she wants and then get pissed about it."
- About the inner voice: "I just kind of look at it like, yeah, okay, like I just don't even entertain it. It's just a thing that happens on the side."

The two answers are different in content but identical in structure. Both describe a move of dismissal — bending without registering one's own preference, or refusing to entertain a voice that wants something. The person, asked once, then asked again in a different context, hears the structural identity of the two answers. That hearing is the work.

A common variant: the person gives a generic answer ("I just push it down") and Joe asks the follow-up "how is that self-abandonment?" The follow-up converts the generic into the specific by asking the person to name the abandonment in their own words. The naming makes the pattern legible in a way the generic could not.

The question sometimes produces a long pause. The pause is information — the person is looking at something they have not looked at in this way. The pause does not need to be filled. Joe waits.

The question sometimes produces tears. The recognition that one has been abandoning oneself in a specific way for a long time is a grief. Joe stays with the grief without rushing.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person gives a specific, in-the-body answer ("I bend to her and then get pissed"): Joe lets it sit. The naming is the work. He may move to the next context to install the pattern recognition, or he may stay with what the person has just named and ask what it's like to look at that.

If the person gives a generic or abstract answer ("I just don't take care of myself"): Joe asks the follow-up. "And how is that abandonment? How's that self-abandonment?" The follow-up requires the person to specify. The specifying is what installs the recognition.

If the person resists the question or says they don't abandon themselves: Joe does not insist. He may ask a related question that surfaces the same material differently — "where do you go when she's upset?" or "what happens to your wants in that conversation?" The avoidance of the direct question is itself information.

If the person gives an answer and immediately starts intellectualizing about why they do it: Joe redirects to the body. "Just notice what it feels like to have just named that." The intellectualizing is itself often the next round of the same self-abandonment — the explanation is leaving the felt experience to manage how it sounds.

After the answer, Joe often asks the question again in another context. The repetition installs the recognition that the pattern is the variable, not the context. "And with the voice in your head, how do you abandon yourself there?" The structural identity of the two answers across two contexts is the recognition Joe is going for.

The downstream move is usually [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]] or [[surface-the-spectrum-of-presence-in-real-time]] or [[self-love-makes-the-decision]] — the work moves from naming the abandonment to dissolving the framing that produces it.

## Dialogue Example

After the man has described his current difficult relationship with his ex:

- **Joe:** "So, so then, um, in your relationship with your ex, how do you abandon yourself?"
- **Person:** "Hmm, I tend to bend to what she wants and then get pissed about it."
- **Joe:** "Okay, awesome. And with the relationship with the voice in your head about you should be, but you shouldn't be in a relationship, how do you abandon yourself?"
- **Person:** "I just kind of look at it like, yeah, okay, like I just don't even entertain it. It's just a thing that happens on the side."
- **Joe:** "And how is that abandonment? How's that self-abandonment?"
- **Person:** "I mean, it's kind of just like ignoring something I want. It's like abandoning a genuine desire that I'm writing off as all the things I've said previously, right?"
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So is this an issue about relationships, or how much of this is just an issue about learning not to abandon yourself?"

*(Source: Why He's Afraid To Commit)*

The question is asked twice in close succession, in two different contexts. The "okay, awesome" between the two askings registers Joe's appreciation of the first answer without commentary on it. The "and how is that abandonment?" follow-up after the second answer is what produces the specific naming. The reframe at the end ("is this an issue about relationships, or...") uses the two answers as the basis for the larger move.

## Tone

Direct, warm, curious. The question is not soft, but it is not harsh. Joe is interested in the actual specific shape of the abandonment. He's not making the person bad for it. The compassionate stance is implicit in the asking; the person can feel that Joe expects them to have an answer and trusts that the answer is something worth knowing.

The follow-up ("how is that self-abandonment?") is asked with the same tone. It does not signal "you didn't answer well enough." It signals "say more — say it in your own words." The lack of disapproval keeps the channel open.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not "what do you do for self-care?" Self-care language operates in a different register. This question is about the specific way the self gets left behind in a specific moment, not about the practices used to compensate for chronic leaving.

This is not "do you have abandonment issues?" That framing treats self-abandonment as an identity feature. This question treats it as an active pattern with a specific shape in a specific moment.

This is not [[where-do-you-leave-yourself-in-this-conversation]] (if that question exists separately). The two are close, but this question is asked about a domain of life (the ex, the inner voice, the work, the family) and that question would be asked about the immediate session itself.
