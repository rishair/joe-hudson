# How is that not true about you?

## The Question

The question is asked in response to a judgment the person has delivered about someone else. The phrasing Joe uses:

- "How is that not true about you?"
- "That's a scary question for me to ask, but how is that not true about you?"
- "How is that not being wrapped up in yourself?"

Joe almost always pairs the question with an explicit flag that it is hard to ask. The flag is part of the instrument. It signals that the question is not an attack.

## When Joe Uses It

When the person has just delivered a judgment of someone else with energy. The intensity of the judgment is the signal that something is being projected.

Common triggers:
- The person says the authority figure is "wrapped up in themselves"
- The person says someone "doesn't see me" or "doesn't care"
- The person says someone is "controlling" or "manipulative"
- The person delivers any harsh characterization with charge

Not used when the judgment is delivered calmly as observation. The instrument requires the heat.

## What It Produces

Often a pause. Sometimes recognition: "oh, interesting." Sometimes defense: "no, it's way worse when they do it." Either response is useful.

Recognition opens the next layer. The person now sees something they were not seeing about themselves and the projection on the other softens.

Defense is also useful. It reveals how protected the quality is. Joe can then sit with the defense rather than push past it. Sometimes the defense itself is the doorway.

In the source session, the response moved through both: first recognition ("it would mean I'm judging the shit out of them"), then defense ("when they do it it's way worse, I'm a better person than that"), then deeper recognition ("I'm still wanting that one kind of attention I've decided I didn't get").

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person sees the mirror immediately: keep going. The next question is usually a more specific version: "How is that not being wrapped up in yourself?" The mirror gets sharper.

If the person defends: do not argue. Joe sometimes responds with a small affirmation of the defense ("yeah, so okay") and then loops back to the question from another angle. The defense is a layer to be welcomed, not punctured.

If the person collapses into self-criticism: redirect. The point is not to flip the judgment onto themselves. The point is to dissolve the projection so the actual dynamic can be seen.

If the person finds the deeper want underneath the judgment: follow that. The projection was protecting the want from view. Once the want is visible, the mirror has done its job.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I thought he was wrapped up in himself so much that he couldn't see anyone else."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. How is that not true about you? That's a scary question for me to ask, but how is that not true about you?"
- **Person:** "That you're wrapped up in yourself so much that I can't see others, or so much that you care whether people see you? Oh, interesting. I want to be seen, it seems like, again, by certain people."
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. Notice how Joe lets the person find the version that lands rather than specifying.)*

- **Person:** "When they do it, when they do it, obviously, Joe, it is way worse than when I do it. I'm a better person than that."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so, okay, so you're sitting in the meeting and the person's like, oh, this is exactly what I want from a leader."
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. Joe accepts the defense and pivots to the live example. The mirror has been planted; he does not need to win on it.)*

## Tone

Gentle and curious, with the meta-acknowledgment that it is a hard question to ask. The flag ("that's a scary question for me to ask") is essential. Without it, the question lands as accusation. With it, the question lands as shared exploration.
