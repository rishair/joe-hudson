# What would make it that you don't deserve this?

## The Question

> "Tell me, what would make it that you don't deserve this?"

The question is asked at the end of a session, after the person has been welcoming themselves with others present and the room has softened. "This" refers to the welcoming, the self-love, the being-seen, the spaciousness — whatever has been arriving in the moment.

The form is unusual. It is not "do you deserve this?" or "why do you deserve this?" It is "what would make it that you don't?" The question assumes the deservingness is the default and asks what could possibly negate it. The framing tilts the answer.

## When Joe Uses It

- At the end of a session where the work has landed and the person is in the welcoming
- After [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] has run through its loop multiple times and the welcoming is happening with witnesses present
- When the closing recognition needs a moment of explicit verification
- When the person has surfaced the [[shame-after-spaciousness]] pattern and is in the spaciousness without the shame
- As a final probe to surface any remaining structural blockers ("I don't deserve this because X") before they re-emerge after the session

## What It Produces

- The most common answer when the work has landed: "Nothing." Said with surprise, simplicity, or quiet certainty.
- Sometimes the person surfaces a specific block ("the thing I did" or "the way I am") that becomes the next layer of work
- When the answer is "nothing," the session can close. The person has verified their own deservingness directly rather than being told.
- The answer often produces a sense of completion. The question functioned as a final check; the check passed.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says "nothing":** that is the close. Let it land. Don't add to it.
- **If the person names a specific block (a behavior, an identity, a memory):** the block becomes the next thing to welcome. The session may need to continue, or the block becomes the homework. See [[loving-all-parts]] for the work of welcoming the specific part.
- **If the person says "I don't know" with hesitation:** this is often a remnant of the pattern firing under question pressure. Slow the pace, return to the welcoming, ask again.
- **If the person tries to argue themselves into not deserving it:** the argument is the pattern firing one more time. Don't fight the argument. Notice it with them, welcome it, and re-ask.

## Dialogue Example

The closing of the source session, after the welcoming has been running with others present:

- **Joe:** "And tell me, what would make it that you don't deserve this?"
- **Guest:** "Nothing."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

The exchange is short. The answer is one word. The work is done. Joe does not respond with explanation or elaboration. The recognition stands.

## Tone

Quiet, direct, present. The question is not a challenge. It is offered as a small final invitation to verify what is already there. The pace is unhurried — the question is asked after the welcoming has been happening for a while, in a moment of stillness.

The framing matters tonally as well as structurally. Asking "what would make it" rather than "do you deserve" or "why do you deserve" removes the burden of proof. The person doesn't have to justify deservingness; they only have to check whether anything specifically disqualifies them. The check is usually empty.

## Variations

- **"What would make you not loveable?"** — when the work has been about loveability specifically
- **"What would make it that you don't get to have this?"** — when the person has been in the "I'm not allowed to have it" frame
- **"What disqualifies you from this?"** — a more pointed form when the person has been operating from a sense of disqualification
- **"What's the reason you wouldn't deserve to be loved?"** — when the person has been in the love-deserving frame specifically

The basic move is consistent: invert the deservingness question so the burden falls on the disqualification rather than the qualification.

## Anti-Pattern Risk

The question can be misused as a debate prompt. If the coach (or AI coach) treats it as a way to argue the person into deservingness, the question stops working. The point is the empty answer arriving on its own — the person noticing that nothing actually disqualifies them. If the coach starts countering the person's reasons-why-they-don't-deserve-it, the answer becomes the coach's, not the person's, and the recognition doesn't land.

The other risk: asking the question too early, before the welcoming work has landed in the body. If the person is still in the head, the question produces a head-level answer ("well, I'm not perfect, so") that doesn't reach the deservingness layer at all. The question is for the close, not the open.
