# What made you phrase it around them instead of around your own pain?

## The Question

The question is asked early in a session — often within the first two or three turns — when the person has framed their presenting concern around the other person's experience instead of around their own pain.

- "What made you phrase it around your boyfriend instead of phrase it around your own pain and suffering regarding your indecision?"
- "What made you phrase it around him instead of around yourself?"
- "What made you make him the subject matter and not you?"
- "I notice you asked the question not like 'I'm in pain, how do I stop?' but like 'I'm driving him crazy.' What made you ask it that way?"

The question follows a setup that names the framing the speaker did not notice they were doing. The setup is the move: "What's interesting to me is that you asked me the question not like, hey, I'm in indecision, it's causing me pain, how do I stop? You asked me the question as like, I'm driving my boyfriend crazy, as if he's the subject matter, not you." The naming makes the framing visible. The question then asks the speaker to look at the framing they have just been shown they were doing.

## When Joe Uses It

This question fires on the read [[presenting-the-other-persons-pain-instead-of-your-own]]. The condition: the speaker frames the presenting concern around the impact on another person rather than around what they themselves are experiencing.

Specific situations:

- The speaker presents a concern about a relationship in which the other person's emotional state is the subject. "I'm driving my boyfriend crazy." "I keep hurting my partner." "My mom is upset with me."
- The speaker describes their own indecision, behavior, or pattern primarily through its effect on someone else. The own-experience is offstage.
- The presenting request is to *stop doing something to the other person*, not to feel something the speaker has not yet felt.
- The shame about the impact on the other person is showing up early in the presentation and is being used as the explanation for why help is being sought.

The crucial timing: ask the question in the first few turns, before the conversation has settled into engaging the surface frame. Once the conversation starts engaging the partner's pain as the unit of work, the framing has been honored and asking about the framing later lands as a course-correction rather than as the entry into the work.

The question can be paired with the prior history-question move ("when's the first time you had recognition of doing this kind of thing?") — establishing the chronic nature first, then surfacing the framing. The prior context makes the framing-question land with more weight because the configuration is already showing itself as long-running.

## What It Produces

The question reliably surfaces the avoided emotions in plain view. The speaker, asked to look at the framing, almost always answers with the specific emotional terrain the framing was covering.

In the source session, the answer arrived in one turn:

- **Person:** "The stuff around him feels more painful. Like, I feel a lot of shame. I'm scared that he's going to be mad at me. Like, I just want to stop doing things that upset him."

Three pieces of material in one answer: (1) the partner's pain is more accessible to the speaker than their own pain; (2) the shame is doing the work of keeping attention on the partner; (3) the avoidance is structured around fear of the partner's anger. All three are now on the table for the next moves.

The predictable shape of what the question produces:

1. The speaker pauses, often visibly noticing the framing for the first time.
2. The speaker explains the framing by naming what is harder to access (their own pain) and what is more accessible (the partner's pain or the speaker's shame about the partner's pain).
3. The fear of the other person's reaction surfaces — often as anger, sometimes as withdrawal, sometimes as rejection.
4. The configuration the framing was enacting becomes visible: the speaker has been measuring their own suffering through its visible impact on the other person.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The first move after the answer is to reframe the unit of work. "Maybe we work on that instead of the indecision because it seems like that's kind of the core." The presented symptom is moved off the table; the configuration the framing revealed becomes the work.

In the source session, Joe also names the structural inevitability of the indecision given the configuration: "Goodness gracious, of course you're indecisive." The naming validates the symptom as a logical consequence of the configuration rather than as a moral failing or a malfunction. The speaker can stop fighting the symptom and start looking at the configuration.

The follow-up move is usually some version of [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-they-re-pissed]] — surface the consequence-feeling that the management-of-the-other has been preventing. The answer to that question begins the work of locating the avoided emotion that the whole configuration has been organized around.

If the speaker resists the reframe and tries to keep working the surface concern, the move is to hold the line gently. "I hear you that the indecision feels like the thing. But I think the indecision is downstream of this other thing. Let's go there." The reframe is offered once and then re-offered if the speaker tries to return to the surface.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not [[how-do-you-abandon-yourself]]. That question is broader — it asks the speaker to identify where the self-abandonment is happening in their life or in a specific relationship. This question is sharper and more local: it points at the framing of the request itself as the abandonment in real time.

This is not a generic "tell me more about your own experience." That question invites narration. This question is diagnostic: it asks the speaker to look at the *shape* of the framing they just used, which surfaces the configuration faster than direct inquiry into their experience would.

This is not [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]]. That question is asked on each branch of a decision-fork to surface the symmetric avoidance. This question is asked when there is no decision-fork — the presentation is structured around the other person's experience without a binary, and the framing itself is the diagnostic.

## Tone

Curious, direct, observational. Not corrective. The framing the speaker used is treated as informative, not as wrong. The implicit message: the way you asked this question is interesting; it is telling me something; let's look at what it is telling us.

The "what's interesting to me" preamble matters. It signals that the framing is being treated as data rather than as a mistake. The speaker is not being told they should have asked it differently. They are being invited to look at the asking with the coach.

The pace matters too. Joe asks this question slowly, with space around it. The naming of the framing takes a moment to land; the question that follows needs space for the speaker to actually look. Asked fast, the question lands as a critique of the speaker's articulation. Asked with space, it lands as an invitation to notice something.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, after the initial presentation and some history-establishing:

- **Joe:** "What's interesting to me is that you asked me the question, not like, hey, I'm in indecision. It's causing me pain. How do I stop? You asked me the question. As like, I'm driving my boyfriend crazy, as if he's like the, the subject matter, not you. What made you phrase it around your boyfriend instead of phrase it around your own pain and suffering regarding your indecision?"
- **Person:** "The stuff around him feels more painful. Like, I feel a lot of shame. I'm scared that he's going to be mad at me. Like, I just want to stop doing things that upset him."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, right. So goodness gracious, of course you're indecisive. You want to live with that the rest of your life?"
- **Person:** "No."
- **Joe:** "So the question is, the question really, like, how much of the question is about how do I stop being indecisive, and how much of the question is how do I live with this man in a way in which I don't lose myself perpetually?"

The question opens the door. The reframe walks through it. By the fourth turn, the unit of work has changed from "the indecision" to "how do I live with this man without losing myself perpetually" — which is a question with a different answer.
