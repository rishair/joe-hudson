# How many attempts would you need before you had undeniable proof you couldn't do it?

## The Question

The question is posed in response to a person who says some version of "I don't trust myself to do this" or "my body doesn't believe I can do this" or "I'm not sure I can pull it off." The question forces the implicit standard out into the open by asking how many actual failed attempts would constitute conclusive evidence of the claim.

- "How many attempts would you have to have building an app until you were 100% certain that you weren't able to do it, build an app that made you able to be independent? How many times would you have to build, attempt to build an app and release it before you had undeniable proof that you couldn't do it?"
- "How many times would you have to fail before you had to admit you couldn't?"
- "Just give me a number. How many tries would it take?"

The question is a structural test. The person is operating from a felt belief that they cannot do the thing. The question asks them to put a number on the evidence required to prove the felt belief. The number they produce — almost always one, sometimes zero — exposes the absurdity of the standard they have been holding themselves to.

## When Joe Uses It

- The person is paralyzed at the threshold of starting their own work (side project, app, business, book, art).
- The person reports they "don't trust themselves" or their "body doesn't believe" they can do the thing.
- The person has not yet started, or has barely started, the work they say they want to do.
- A perfectionism standard is visibly operating but is not visible to the person.
- The framing "I have to get it right the first time" is implicit in how they talk about the work.
- The person knows intellectually that one attempt is not how things work but reports feeling otherwise.

The question is particularly diagnostic when the person has already named a specific concern — they want to build an app, start a business, launch a product — but cannot get themselves to begin. The unstarted state itself is the evidence that some implicit failure standard is operating. The question surfaces what that standard is.

## What It Produces

The honest answer is almost always: one. Sometimes zero.

The most informative thing about the answer is the gap between what the person says and what they intellectually know. They will often qualify the answer in real time:

> "I'd like to say many, but probably the, the honest answer is one." -- (Source: My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can)

The "I'd like to say many" is the intellectual standard the person can articulate when asked directly. The "but honestly one" is the felt standard their body has been operating from. The two are different. The question makes them notice the gap.

The person almost always knows, the moment they say "one," that the standard is structurally absurd. They are imposing on themselves a one-attempt rule that no rational person would accept as evidence of anything. The recognition tends to land as a small head-shake or a defensive follow-up: "I know that's not how it works. Yeah. But it still feels like there is so much on that one attempt."

The follow-up confession — "but it still feels like" — is itself the data. The felt standard persists even after the intellectual standard has been disowned. The work from here is at the felt level, not the cognitive level. See [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] for the structural reason.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The response is the diagnostic. From here Joe usually compresses the structure into a deliberately absurd statement that surfaces what the person has been doing without naming it as criticism:

> "It's good that you don't think you can do it because you can't, if that's true." -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The compression performs a small reductio. If the standard ("one failed attempt = definitive proof I can't") is correct, then the person's not-trying is rational. The not-trying is the correct response to the standard. The person sees themselves in the structure as someone behaving rationally given the absurd inputs, which is much easier to see than seeing themselves as someone behaving irrationally. The seeing produces the smile of recognition (see [[the-smile-of-recognition]]).

> "Look at that smile." -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The smile is the signal that the recognition has landed in the body. From here the path is open for the next move — usually [[offer-yourself-as-the-bad-boss-they-installed]] or [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]].

If the person tries to defend the one-attempt standard ("but it really does feel that way"), Joe does not argue. He validates the felt truth and then surfaces the next layer: where did the standard come from, who is the part of you holding it, why is this work loaded with so much that one failed attempt would mean everything. The exposure of the absurd standard is the start of the work, not the end of it.

## What This Question Is Not

It is not a debate about the actual failure rate of startups, books, or apps. The question is not seeking statistical information. The person knows the statistics. The question is exposing the gap between what the person knows and what they are operating from.

It is not an invitation for the person to commit to a specific number of attempts. The question is diagnostic; the commitment work happens elsewhere. Asking "okay, so you'll try ten times?" after the absurdity is exposed converts the recognition into a new should and undoes the work.

It is not a one-time question. The same person, asked the same question about a different domain a year later, often produces the same "honestly one" answer. The one-attempt standard is bounded to high-stakes work and may reassert itself on each new instance of high-stakes work. The question is portable.

## Dialogue Example

- **Client:** "I just don't know if I'm the right person, if I'm going to put in enough effort, if it's the right time."
- **Joe:** "How many attempts would you have to have building an app until you were 100% certain that you weren't able to do it, build an app that made you able to be independent? How many times would you have to build, attempt to build an app and release it before you had undeniable proof that you couldn't do it?"
- **Client:** "I'd like to say many, but probably the, the honest answer is one."
- **Joe:** "One. So if you built one app and you and that WAP wasn't successful, then that would be proof that you could not build an app."
- **Client:** "Like, I know that's not how it works. Yeah. But it still feels like there is so much on that one attempt or like, and it's not just yet, like it's with many attempts."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So that's really good. It's good that you don't think you can do it because you can't, if that's true. Look at that smile."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I really hate you right now, but I—"
- *(Source: My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can)*

The "I really hate you right now" is the verbal accompaniment to the smile. The recognition has landed, and the part of the person that has been protected by the one-attempt standard knows it has been seen. The hate is affectionate — it is the protest of the part that wanted the standard to remain invisible. The coaching can proceed from here.

## Tone

Direct, almost matter-of-fact. The question is asked the way a question about a real number would be asked. No theatrical setup. The casual delivery is what makes the absurdity of the answer audible — if the delivery were heavy, the person would feel they were being set up and would defend the standard. The casual delivery lets the person answer honestly.

After the answer, the slight playfulness of "it's good that you don't think you can do it because you can't, if that's true" gives the person room to smile rather than feeling caught out. The reductio is delivered as observation, not accusation.

## Variants

- "How many attempts would it take before you'd be willing to conclude you couldn't?"
- "Just give me a number. How many failures would constitute proof?"
- "If you tried five times and failed all five, would that be enough? Three times? One time?" (Walking down the scale to find where the implicit standard sits.)
- "What's the failure count at which you'd have definitive evidence you can't do this?"

The verbal form matters less than the structural move: ask for the number. The person's answer is the diagnostic.

## When the Move Misfires

- If the person produces a number like five or ten and means it, the standard being exposed is different — usually not the unstarted-perfectionism standard but something else (maybe a real assessment of their odds, or a fear of running out of resources). Adjust accordingly.
- If the person dodges by giving a methodological answer ("well, it depends on the market conditions"), the dodge is itself the data. The person is keeping the standard offstage. Bring them back: "Just for you, just your own personal standard, how many failed attempts would mean you couldn't do it?"
- If the person uses the question to launch a confessional spiral about all their failures, the diagnostic has surfaced shame rather than the perfectionism standard. Shift to the shame layer. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].
