# What were your parents scared of?

## The Question

Asked when a person is describing childhood material — what their parents did or didn't do, what they were or weren't given, what got installed in them as a result. The question redirects the inquiry from "what did they do to me" to "what was running them."

Joe's specific phrasing variants:

- "What were your parents scared of?"
- "What was the thing that they were scared of that had them teach you how to dissociate?"
- "What were they scared of that made them the kind of people who didn't — weren't able to attune to your sadness or your anger or your needs, your body?"

A specific corrective variant Joe uses when the person initially answers with something about themselves ("losing me," "me not being —"):

- "It's not you. It's not about you. What were they scared of that made them the kind of people who [did the thing]?"

The correction makes the question's structural intent explicit. The question is not "what fear about you ran them" but "what fear about themselves ran them." The redirect from you-as-the-content to them-as-the-system is the operative work.

## When Joe Uses It

After the person has described a pattern installed in childhood — they were taught to dissociate, taught not to feel, taught not to want, taught to perform. The pattern has been named. The question now is what installed it. The standard inquiry would ask "what did your parents do." This question asks something different: "what were they protecting themselves from when they did it."

Specific situational triggers:

- The person has named a childhood imprint and is starting to consolidate around the personal-injury frame ("they did this to me")
- The work needs to move from the description of what happened to the depersonalization of what happened
- The person is approaching shame about being broken by their parents and needs a structural read to release the shame
- The childhood pattern is real and accurate but the personal frame is locking the work
- The person is ready to see their parents as humans running their own fear rather than as the agents who damaged them

The question presupposes that the parents were running their own fear. If the parents were genuinely malicious or there is active abuse history that has not been processed, the question can land as a defense of the parents rather than as a depersonalization. In that case the prior work is feeling the child's anger before asking this question. See [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]].

## What It Produces

The first answer is usually wrong. The person produces an answer about themselves, because the standard frame is "what did they do to me." Joe corrects: "It's not you. It's not about you."

The corrected answer is usually quick and accurate once the redirect is in. In the source session, the person produced: "My dad is, uh, an emotionally scarred man. He's had a hell of a past, and I don't think he knows how to express his emotions properly. And, uh, my mom is overly emotional, and the loss of my grandmother really threw her for quite a roller coaster of a few years."

The answer reveals the structure: the dad's fear was about his own emotional scarring, the mom's fear was about her grief becoming uncontainable. Neither was about the child. The child was inside a system the parents were trying to manage with the tools they had.

The structural insight that follows: "So this — all of their activity wasn't about you. It wasn't personal." The person's typical response: "I think deep down I know that." The recognition that follows is the operative shift.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Once the depersonalizing answer has arrived, Joe extends with the body integration move. The intellectual knowing of "it wasn't personal" arrives first; the somatic integration takes longer. Joe's instruction:

"Yeah, let the rest of your body catch up. Something in your body knows it. Now we're just letting all the rest of the body catch up. Let it like literally seep into every cell of your body for a moment. What's the truth of who you are now that it's updated for a minute?"

This is [[let-the-body-catch-up]] — the move that takes the intellectual insight and walks it through the body until the body's report of who-the-person-is has updated. The question's full coaching value is unlocked by this follow-up. Without the integration move, the question produces a head-level reframe that does not land in the system. With the integration move, the person's felt identity updates in real time.

## Why It Works

The question performs a specific structural operation: it moves the explanation of the childhood event from the child as the cause to the parents' fear as the cause. The shift produces several effects:

1. **It releases the verdict on the self.** The child encoded the parents' behavior as a statement about who the child was ("there is something wrong with me that made them treat me this way"). The question reveals that the parents' behavior was a statement about who the parents were — specifically, what they were protecting themselves from.

2. **It humanizes the parents without absolving them.** The parents become humans running their own fear-based programming, not malevolent agents or perfect failures. They are not excused; they are placed in a system. The placement is what allows the child's verdict to release.

3. **It identifies the inheritance.** Once the parents' fear is named, the person can see what they have been carrying that was never theirs to carry. The dad's emotional scarring, the mom's unprocessed grief — these are the contents that got transmitted. Naming them makes them separable from the self.

4. **It opens the path to repair.** Once the inheritance is named, the person can begin to grieve, forgive, or distance — whichever the situation calls for — from a position of clarity rather than from inside the personal-injury frame.

The mechanism is structurally identical to [[the-pattern-is-not-personal]] applied upstream. That principle depersonalizes the patterns the person is currently running. This question depersonalizes the formation of those patterns by surfacing the parental fear that drove the formation.

## What the Question Is Not

- It is not a question about whether the parents loved the person. The parents may have loved them. The fear was operating in parallel with the love.
- It is not absolution. The parents' behavior was harmful regardless of what drove it. The question depersonalizes the cause; it does not erase the impact.
- It is not a request for forgiveness. The depersonalization can happen without forgiveness. Forgiveness is a separate work that happens on its own timeline if at all.
- It is not analysis. The answer is felt as much as thought. The right answer often arrives quickly because the body has known it for years. The intellect was the layer that hadn't been told.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session, showing the question, the corrective redirect, and the integration that follows:

- *(Joe has just introduced the Stone Face Baby experiment to explain the client's dissociation. The client confirms he is "very good at masking my emotions.")*
- **Joe:** "What was it? What was it about? What were your parents scared of? What was the thing that they were scared of that had them teach you how to dissociate?"
- **Client:** "Um, God, I don't know. Um, losing me. Um, me not being—"
- **Joe:** "It's not you. It's not about you. What were they scared of that made them the kind of people who didn't — weren't able to attune to your sadness or your anger or your needs, your body."
- **Client:** "I mean, my dad is, uh, an emotionally scarred man. He's had a hell of a past, and I don't think he knows how to express his emotions properly."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Client:** "And, uh, my mom is overly emotional and you know, the loss of my grandmother really threw her for quite a roller coaster of a few years."
- **Joe:** "Great, perfect. So this — all of their activity wasn't about you. It wasn't personal."
- **Client:** "I think deep down I know that."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, let the rest of your body catch up. Something in your body knows it. Now we're just letting all the rest of the body catch up. Let it like literally seep into every cell of your body for a moment. What's the truth of who you are now that it's updated for a minute?"
- **Client:** "I'm a big ball of love and wonder and just wanting and learning."
- *(Source: 2025-04-18_Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time)*

The arc shows the question's full work: the initial misplaced answer, the corrective redirect, the structural answer, the naming of the impersonal frame, the recognition of intellectual knowing, the body-catch-up move, and the updated self-report. The whole sequence took about two minutes.

## Tone

Direct, curious, neither warm nor cold. The question is asked as a request for information, not as a therapeutic intervention. The implicit message: "I want to know the answer to this; it will tell us both something."

The corrective redirect ("it's not you, it's not about you") is delivered firmly but without sharpness. The firmness matters because the redirect has to interrupt a strong default. The lack of sharpness matters because the redirect is not a correction of the person — it is a correction of the question they thought was being asked.

The post-answer naming ("Great, perfect") is more than encouragement. It is a confirmation that the structural answer has arrived. The "great" tells the person they have located the thing.
