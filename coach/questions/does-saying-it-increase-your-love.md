# Does saying it increase your love for them?

## The Question

The internal test for whether what you are about to say is a boundary or an ultimatum.

- "When you think about saying it to them, does it immediately allow you to love them more deeply?"
- "Does saying this open your heart toward them, or close it?"
- "If you rehearse saying this in your head, are you more in love with them or less?"
- "Does the saying of it increase your capacity to love them, regardless of how they respond?"

The question is asked of the speaker, about their own internal state, before the boundary is delivered.

"Just the saying it, no matter what their reaction is. Maybe their reaction is fuck you. And maybe their reaction is, oh, thank God you said that. It doesn't matter. Whatever it is, when you say it, your capacity to love them has increased." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## When Joe Uses It

When someone is about to draw a boundary and the language is starting to sound demanding or controlling. The question pulls the speaker back to their internal state before the boundary lands.

When someone reports that their boundaries are causing more conflict, not less. The likely diagnosis is that the boundaries are operating as ultimatums. The love test surfaces this directly.

When someone is in [[fear-triangle]] dynamics and using boundary language to manage another person. The question reveals whether the boundary is a real boundary or a power move.

When someone has been told to set boundaries and the framing has gone hostile. The question gives them a different success criterion than "make them stop doing the thing."

When [[boundaries]] or [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] has been introduced and the person needs a way to test their specific situation.

## What It Produces

Often surfaces immediate clarity. The person feels the difference between the two versions of the same statement in their body. The body knows even when the mind has convinced itself the statement is clean.

Sometimes produces resistance. "But they shouldn't be doing this. Of course I'm angry. How am I supposed to feel love when they are behaving like this?" The resistance is information: the person is mixing up the boundary with the resentment. The boundary is meant to address what you will do; the resentment is a separate piece of work. See [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]].

When the person honestly tries the test and the answer is "no, saying this closes my heart," the next move is usually to locate the fear or anger that is closing it. Boundary language layered over uninvestigated emotion almost always lands as ultimatum.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person says "yes, it opens my heart":** Joe trusts the body signal and supports the boundary. The boundary is likely clean.

**If the person says "no, it closes my heart, but I still need to say it":** Joe explores what is closing the heart. Often there is unfelt anger, hurt, or fear that needs to move before the boundary can be drawn cleanly. See [[don-t-do-emotion-at-people|dont-do-emotion-at-people]]: emotion released through the boundary is emotional abuse, not a boundary.

**If the person says "I don't know":** Joe slows down and walks through the imagined delivery in detail. "If you said it right now, to them, what happens in your chest?" The body knows when given the chance to register.

**If the person says "it depends on how they react":** That dependency is the diagnostic. A real boundary holds whether or not the other person responds well. If the test result depends on their reaction, the boundary is being used as a transactional tool rather than a clean statement.

**If the person claims to pass the test but the boundary lands as an ultimatum:** Joe gently reflects back what he heard. Sometimes the speaker's own perception of their internal state is off. The listener is also a useful data source.

## Why This Works as a Diagnostic

Love is not an emotion that can be faked into existence. If you have to pretend you love the person while drawing the boundary, you are not loving them. The body knows the difference.

The mechanism: a boundary drawn from love takes responsibility for your own response and leaves the other person fully free. There is nothing in that to close the heart. A boundary drawn from fear or anger is trying to control or punish the other person. There is everything in that to close the heart.

The question short-circuits the rationalization the mind is otherwise excellent at producing. Whatever logic justifies the controlling boundary, the body still registers closed.

See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] for the underlying distinction.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** I need to tell my partner that they have to stop checking their phone during dinner. That is my boundary.
- **Joe:** When you imagine saying that to them, what happens in your chest?
- **Person:** It tightens up. I feel pissed about having to say it.
- **Joe:** So that's not really a boundary. That's an ultimatum. The boundary is what you will do, not what they have to do. What is the actual want underneath? What are you actually asking for?
- *(Composite, based on the framework Joe uses in What's So Scary About Boundaries?)*

## Tone

Genuine. Slow. Not gotcha. The question is offered as a tool, not as a trap. Often delivered with the implied recognition that the person has been operating from a model where boundaries are firm-words-said-firmly, and this question is asking them to switch models.

Sometimes asked twice if the first answer was too fast. The body needs a moment to register. Snap answers usually reflect the mind, not the body.

## The Underlying Teaching

The question encodes the entire teaching of [[boundaries]] in a single test. Love and boundaries are not opposed; they are mutually required. A boundary that closes your heart is not actually serving the relationship, even if it accomplishes the surface goal of changing the other person's behavior. A boundary that opens your heart is serving the relationship even if it appears to be more restrictive on the surface.

The test relocates the success criterion. The point of the boundary is not to make the other person behave. The point is to preserve the conditions in which love can live. Behavior change may or may not follow. The relationship's health does not depend on the behavior change.
