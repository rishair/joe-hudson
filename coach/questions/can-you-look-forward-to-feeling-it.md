# Can you look forward to feeling it?

## The Question

The diagnostic question for whether emotional work has slipped back into management. Variations:

- "Can you actually look forward to feeling angry next time?"
- "Are you excited to feel sad when it comes up?"
- "Do you find yourself wanting to feel scared?"
- "When the emotion shows up next, will you greet it like a returning child or like an unwelcome guest?"
- "Can you welcome this without wanting it to leave?"

The internal test is whether the person finds themselves looking forward to feeling angry, scared, sad. When the answer is yes, they are no longer managing. When the answer is no, the work has slipped back into a more sophisticated form of management with the language of welcoming.

"It's literally the emotions are invited like children in your house, like, oh good, my kids are home. Like, it's like when that's the feeling, when I can't wait to feel sad, when I can't wait to feel angry, when I can't wait to feel scared, that's when there — you're not faking it." -- Joe Hudson, Stages of Emotional Development

## When Joe Uses It

The question is a calibration tool. Joe reaches for it when:

- The person is doing the right practices but the underlying pattern is not shifting. See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].
- The person reports "feeling" their emotions in a way that sounds like reporting rather than embodying.
- A person at later stages of [[stages-of-emotional-development]] wants to check whether their welcoming has become a strategy.
- A coaching session has just done an emotional release, and Joe wants to test whether the person is grateful it's over or grateful it happened.
- The person asks how to make an emotion go away faster.
- The person describes their emotional work using the vocabulary of optimization (more efficient release, faster processing, better technique).

## What It Produces

The question tends to produce a pause. The person checks internally and is honest with themselves about what they find.

Common responses:

- **"Honestly, no."** — The recognition itself is the move. The person sees the motivation directly. The next step is not to suddenly produce welcoming but to feel the resistance to welcoming as itself. See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].
- **"I want to say yes but I'm not sure."** — Indicates a person who has the concept of welcoming but has not yet found the felt sense of it. Useful for moving from intellectual frame to embodied stance.
- **"Yes, actually."** — When this is genuine (not performed), the welcoming is real. The work is moving. Joe accepts this and moves on.
- **"What if I never feel it again?"** — Surfaces the fear underneath the management. The question that wanted to be asked. Joe follows this thread.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person admits the motivation is to be done with the emotion, Joe does not push them to manufacture welcoming. The push would produce a performance. Instead, he treats the resistance to welcoming as the next emotion to feel. Often this is fear (of being overtaken) or shame (about having the emotion in the first place). Felt as itself, the resistance begins to release. Welcoming becomes possible from underneath rather than from above.

If the person produces enthusiastic welcoming language that sounds rehearsed, Joe asks again or asks slower. The question is meant to land in the body. A quick rehearsed answer indicates it landed in the intellect.

If the person genuinely cannot tell whether they are welcoming or managing, Joe offers a more concrete frame: imagine a friend is coming over you really want to see. What is the felt sense of that? Now bring up the emotion. Do you have the same felt sense, or a different one? This translates the question from abstract to comparative.

## The Image That Makes It Land

Joe pairs this question with an image: the emotions are children coming home. The matriarch waits to come last (see [[joy-as-matriarch]]) but all the children are returning. Do you have the felt sense of "oh good, my kids are home" when the emotion arrives?

The image is precise because it includes the warmth and the messiness. A returning child is not necessarily quiet. They make noise, demand attention, take up space. The welcoming includes the mess. If the person can welcome the emotion only when it behaves, the welcoming is conditional and the strategy has not shifted.

Brett's extension of the image: imagine you are preparing the house for all the children to come for a party, and one by one they arrive and you put them to work helping prepare for the party. The party never happens because you are too busy preparing for it. This is what happens when welcoming becomes the work-around: the welcoming itself becomes a form of arranging the emotion rather than being with it.

## Tone

Gentle, curious, slightly playful. The question is offered as a check, not a test. The playful tone matters: a grave version would push the person toward producing the right answer, which would defeat the purpose of the question.

## The Inquiry-Specific Form: You Get Excited Because You Get to Inquire More

The same diagnostic, applied to the practice of [[emotional-inquiry]] specifically. The marker is not just "I welcome the emotion" but "I am glad the emotion arrived because now there is more material for the inquiry."

"Pretty soon what you'll notice is that you'll be out in the world and this feeling will come up, and you'll be excited that the feelings come up because you get to inquire more, you get to learn more about it. And you get to play with it some more." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique

This is a more specific test than the general welcoming test because it specifies what the welcoming is in service of: continued inquiry, continued learning, continued play with the felt sense. The excitement is the excitement of a researcher whose subject just walked into the room. The same threshold (genuine vs. performed welcoming) applies, but anchored to an active engagement rather than passive acceptance.
