# What's the difference between overcoming fear and feeling fear?

## The Question

The full form: "What is the difference in your mind and your experience in your body between overcoming fear and feeling fear?"

The compact form: "What's the difference between overcoming the fear and feeling the fear?"

Variations:
- "In your body, what does it feel like to overcome a fear versus to feel one?"
- "When you push through fear versus welcome it, what's the somatic difference for you?"
- "Walk me through how the two feel different in your body."

The question forces a somatic answer. The intellectual answer (overcoming is denial, feeling is allowing) is well-known and easy to give. Asking specifically about the body lands the inquiry in a place the person cannot reach via concept.

## When Joe Uses It

The question is a coaching-interview instrument. Joe reaches for it when:

- A person is describing their journey with fear in language that has become well-worn or theoretical. The body specificity reopens the inquiry.
- The audience needs an accessible entry point. Joe asks it during interviews with the listener in mind: "the first question that comes to mind for me, particularly if I'm thinking about the audience..."
- The person has had a fear breakthrough but has not yet articulated what changed at the felt level. The question retrieves the felt experience.
- A person is conflating two different relationships with fear and the conflation is preventing them from seeing the actual shift in themselves.

Joe asking Brett during the welcoming-fear interview:

"The first question that comes to mind for me, particularly if I'm thinking about the audience, is what is the difference in your mind and your experience in your body between overcoming fear and feeling fear?" -- Joe Hudson, Welcoming Fear

## What It Produces

The answer almost always lands on [[fear-vs-excitement]] territory. The person describes:

- Overcoming fear as constriction, narrowing, closing in, pushing through, clenched jaw, held breath, performing-despite.
- Feeling fear as expansion, wider awareness, sharpening, breath present, performance enabled rather than performance achieved against the body.

The answer that Brett gives is the canonical one:

"When it's excitement, I'm feeling expansive and more aware and sharper. But if it's fear of something that like I should not be doing, if I'm in over my head or I'm like not paying attention to a variable consciously that subconsciously I'm aware is going on, like there's something wrong with my equipment or the conditions have changed and I'm just not letting myself see it, then the fear will actually feel more constricting and closing in. And so the difference between like overcoming it and welcoming it is the difference between standing on an edge and feeling the fear closing you down and just pushing through it anyway, or feeling the fear and then welcoming it and letting it and seeing how it transforms and how much it transforms into excitement." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

The body's answer carries content the mind's answer does not. The mind can talk about overcoming and feeling as concepts. The body knows that one of them costs something and the other does not.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person describes the constricted version with specificity: Joe confirms it and moves toward [[fear-as-signal-of-care]] or [[dont-step-on-the-signal]]. The signal has been described from inside; the next move is to name what the signal was carrying that was lost when it was overridden.

If the person describes the expansive version: Joe explores what made the shift possible. "What changed that let the fear be felt instead of overcome?" The shift moment is the most usable material in the interview.

If the person can describe one and not the other: Joe stays with the gap. Often the person has lived in one mode exclusively and is just beginning to encounter the other. Naming this is part of the work.

If the person answers conceptually rather than somatically: Joe redirects to the body. "I hear what you're saying about the concept. What does it feel like, physically, when you're overcoming versus feeling?" The somatic answer is what the question is reaching for.

## What Makes This a Joe Question Rather Than a Generic One

A generic coaching question would ask: "What's the difference between resisting and accepting?" That question gets a generic answer.

Joe's question has three specific features:

1. **The body is named explicitly.** "Your experience in your body" forecloses the intellectual answer.
2. **The pair is concrete.** "Overcoming" and "feeling" are not abstract; the person can locate them in specific moments of their own life.
3. **The audience is implicit.** Joe often asks this with a listener in mind. The question produces an answer that is useful to someone who has never been asked it.

## Tone

Direct, curious, light. Not an interrogation. The question is offered as the kind of inquiry the person might enjoy answering. Often phrased as "I'm curious" or "the first thing that comes to mind for me is..." -- the framing reduces the implicit pressure that the answer needs to be correct.
