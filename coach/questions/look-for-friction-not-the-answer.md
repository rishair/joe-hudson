# Where Do You Feel the Most Resistance and Friction in Living Your Purpose Right Now?

## The Question

A reframe Brett offered during the conversation that Joe immediately affirmed:

"If rather than, 'I'm looking for my purpose,' it's something more like, 'Where do I feel the most resistance and friction?' in living my purpose right now. And then let's go there." -- Brett Kistler, How To Find Your Purpose

Joe: "Yeah."
Brett: "Let's explore that."
Joe: "Let's explore that."

The question inverts the entire search frame. Instead of asking "what is my purpose?" — which presupposes its absence — the question asks "where in the purpose I am already living is the friction?" — which presupposes its presence.

Variants:

- "Where do you feel the most resistance and friction in living your purpose right now?"
- "Where in what you are doing is there friction?"
- "What is the friction asking you to surrender to?"
- "Where can the alignment be refined?"

## When Joe Uses It

- After [[purpose-is-recognized-not-found]] has been established — the person has accepted that purpose is already operating, and the next question is where the alignment can be refined.
- When someone is grinding in their work and reports the grind. The grind is the friction; the friction is the data; the question opens the inquiry.
- When the person presents the "I want to find my purpose" question and the reframe is needed to convert the search into an alignment-refinement.
- When [[purpose-asks-you-to-be-scary-then-effortless]] surfaces grind in phase two and the question is what to do with the grind.
- When the person is mid-activity and reporting that something is off, but cannot name what is off. The friction question localizes the offness.

## What It Produces

The question almost always produces a specific answer. The person knows where the friction is. They have been feeling it. They have just not been treating it as data — they have been treating it as evidence that they are in the wrong activity.

Common answers:

- "I'm doing it for approval and that's draining me." See [[do-it-for-yourself-to-find-purpose]].
- "I'm holding it as my identity and any threat feels personal." See [[purpose-becomes-non-personal-when-its-real]].
- "I keep deferring the scary part and doing the easy parts." See [[purpose-asks-you-to-be-scary-then-effortless]].
- "I'm trying to manage what people think of it instead of doing the thing." See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]].
- "I'm not actually saying yes to the call; I'm saying maybe."

Each answer is the location of the next refinement. The friction is not the problem; the friction is the map of where the work is.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The response is treated as data, not as a complaint. The friction is the diagnostic.

- If the friction is approval-seeking: the move is toward doing it for the doing itself. The audience moves inside. See [[do-it-for-yourself-to-find-purpose]] and [[chase-your-own-approval]].
- If the friction is identity-investment: the move is toward the non-personal quality. The activity is not the self; the activity happens through the self.
- If the friction is deferring the scary part: the move is to name the scary part directly and ask what is being avoided. The deferring is the avoidance.
- If the friction is performance for an audience: the move is to ask what would change if the audience were not watching. The performance is what is creating the friction.

In each case, the friction is the signal that more surrender is available. The exit is not less friction through avoiding the work; the exit is more alignment through surrendering further into the work.

## Why This Question Works

The question works because it presupposes the principle. It assumes the person is already in their purpose. It asks where the alignment can be refined. The assumption itself does work: the person has to drop the search-frame to answer the question. The drop is the work.

Compare to the search-frame question "what is my purpose?":

- The search question requires an answer the person does not have.
- The friction question requires an answer the person does have.
- The search question keeps the person in the abstract.
- The friction question puts the person in the concrete present.
- The search question reinforces the assumption that purpose is missing.
- The friction question reinforces the assumption that purpose is already operating, and the work is in the refinement.

The shift in question is small. The shift in framing is large. The friction question puts the person in a relationship with their actual living, in this moment, in a way the search question cannot.

## The "Right Now" Framing

The "right now" in the question matters. It pulls the inquiry out of abstraction.

- "Where is the friction in my life?" produces a general inventory.
- "Where is the friction right now?" produces a specific, immediate, actionable answer.

The right-now framing also matches the principle that purpose lives in the moment. See [[purpose-is-how-not-what]]. The how is always available right now; the friction in the how is always available right now; the refinement is always available right now. The question delivers the person to the place where the work is actually possible.

## Pairing With Other Questions

The friction question pairs well with several other questions:

- After: "What would happen if you didn't defend against the friction?" — asking the person to drop the defense and feel what is underneath.
- After: "What would the activity look like if the friction resolved?" — surfacing the felt-sense of the refined alignment.
- After: "Are you doing this for yourself or for someone else's approval?" — when the friction is approval-shaped.
- Before: "How aligned with your purpose do you feel in this moment, on a felt-sense level?" — establishing the more-or-less-aligned baseline before asking where the friction is.

The pairing creates a coaching path. Establish that the person can read alignment. Locate the friction. Surface what the friction is asking for. Make the refinement available.

## What This Question Replaces

The question is a direct replacement for several less-useful questions a coach might be tempted to ask:

- "What is your purpose?" → unanswerable from the search-frame; pulls into abstraction.
- "What do you want to do with your life?" → similar; produces strategy-options rather than alignment-data.
- "What are you most passionate about?" → requires the person to find a hidden passion rather than refine an obvious one.
- "What were you born to do?" → metaphysical; can produce grandiosity or paralysis.

In each case, the friction question is the more useful alternative when the person is already living in some way and the question is how to refine the living. The other questions can have a role earlier in the work, but they should not be the resting place of the inquiry. The friction question is.

## Boundary Conditions

The question presupposes the person has accepted that purpose is already operating in their life. If the person is fully invested in the search frame ("I haven't found my purpose yet"), the friction question can be deflected ("I can't tell you where the friction is in my purpose because I don't have one"). In that case, the move is to first establish [[purpose-is-recognized-not-found]] — usually through the felt-sense alignment check ("right now, do you feel more or less aligned with what feels purposeful to you?") — before asking the friction question.

The question also presupposes some basic capacity for self-observation. A person in acute distress, dissociation, or overwhelm cannot use the question productively. The question works best when the person is regulated enough to feel their own internal data.

## Tone

The question is offered with curiosity, not interrogation. Brett's "let's go there" and Joe's "let's explore that" capture the tone: collaborative, exploratory, slightly relieved. The reframe is light — the person has been carrying the search as a heavy burden, and the friction question is structurally lighter than the search question.

When the friction is named, the tone shifts to slower and more spacious. Naming a friction is often the first time the person has admitted it to themselves. Holding the space for the admission to land is part of the work.
