# What's Wrong With This Moment?

## The Question

When the person has been in fix-everything mode and the session has demonstrated being-with the coach in the room, the question collapses the fix-frame to the smallest possible field: this moment, right now.

The question is asked with a specific constraint that closes the escape hatches:

"You can't go to the past. You can't go to the future. Just what's wrong with this moment?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The constraint matters. The fix-everything mind operates by surveying the past for failures and the future for catastrophes. Limited to the present moment with no time-travel allowed, the mind cannot find a problem. The fix-pattern is structurally dependent on past-and-future scanning. Removing the scan removes the supply of things-wrong.

## When Joe Uses It

- After the demonstration move ([[make-the-session-itself-the-demonstration]]) has produced a felt sense of being-with in the room.
- When the person is starting to reach for a new problem to fix even as they are noticing the presence.
- When the person is in the experience and is leaving the experience by going back to the conceptual frame.
- When the work needs a small reality-test: in this actual second, with this body, in this room, what is the problem?
- As a tool to interrupt the constant present-time generation of problems-to-fix that runs underneath the fixing-pattern.

The question is also used with the anthill image as the setup. The anthill demonstrates what fix-from-presence looks like (you can't find anything wrong with an anthill); the question then applies the same posture to the present moment with the coach.

## The Anthill Setup

In the source session, the question was set up by a small visualization first.

"I want you to imagine looking at an anthill. And now I want you to imagine trying to fix the anthill."

The woman's response: "I cannot. I don't see anything wrong with the anthill, right?"

"Correct. Yeah. Yeah. And if you're in this moment, what's wrong? With me. What's wrong?"

The anthill is the analog of the present moment seen with presence. The anthill is what it is. There is nothing to fix because there is no fault to find. The same is true of the present moment when entered from presence rather than from the fixing-frame.

The setup also gives the person an experiential reference. They can feel, in the imagined anthill, what it is like to look at something without the fix-radar firing. They can then bring that posture to the present moment and find the same nothing-to-fix experience there.

## What It Produces

**The person tries to find a problem and discovers they have to leave the moment to find one.** The first answer is usually something like "well, I'm not fixing it fast enough" or "me not being able to sit here in this moment is what's wrong with my moments." Each attempted answer requires going to the past (I haven't done this before) or to the future (I won't be able to keep this up). Both moves leave the present moment, which is the constraint the question is enforcing.

**The person notices the structural feature of the fix-pattern.** The fix-pattern requires time-travel. The fix needs a past it failed to prevent or a future it is trying to prevent. The present moment, examined directly, contains no such material. The pattern's dependence on past-and-future becomes visible.

**The person can land in the moment.** Once the attempt to find what is wrong has failed several times, the system gives up the search. The giving-up is the landing. The present moment is suddenly available because no extraction-from-it is being attempted.

In the source session, the woman went through three rounds of trying to find what was wrong before naming what she was actually avoiding: "I am avoiding the silence. I am avoiding the peace."

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says nothing is wrong:** Joe stays. The recognition needs time to land. He may name it ("yeah, there you are in this moment") or just hold the silence.
- **If the person finds a problem that requires the past:** Joe gently constrains them back ("can't go to the past"). The naming of the rule is part of what makes the constraint felt.
- **If the person finds a problem that requires the future:** Same constraint. "Can't go to the future."
- **If the person says "me not being able to sit here is what's wrong":** Joe accepts this as data. The meta-problem (the failure to be here) is itself happening in the moment and can be the next material. Often this is the doorway to the underneath material ([[everything-i-touch-fails-drives-fixing]]).
- **If the person tries to fix the question:** Joe names the move. "Your mind just went to trying to fix it." See [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]].

The question is rarely deployed once. It is usually asked, the person attempts an escape, the escape is gently disallowed, the question is reasked. The repetition is the work. Each iteration narrows the field a little more.

## Why It Works

The question works because the fix-everything pattern is structurally dependent on time-extraction. The fixer needs to be slightly outside the present moment in order to register a problem; the registration of the problem is what triggers the fix-activity. The present moment, fully entered, has no field for the fix-pattern to operate in.

This is also why the question's constraint ("can't go to the past, can't go to the future") is the load-bearing piece. Without the constraint, the person will find something to fix immediately, because the fix-radar is always pre-loaded with material. With the constraint, the radar has nothing to scan. The person discovers experientially that the problems they were fixing are not in the present; they are imported from elsewhere.

The question is also a small disconfirmation experiment for the fix-pattern's core belief. The pattern operates on the assumption that there is always something wrong that needs fixing. The present-moment-constrained question disconfirms this assumption in a single trial. The fixer cannot find anything wrong in this moment. The next conclusion — that maybe nothing in any moment is what they thought it was — is the door the work has opened.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "I want you to imagine looking at an anthill. And now I want you to imagine trying to fix the anthill."
- **Guest:** "I cannot. I don't see anything wrong with the anthill, right?"
- **Joe:** "Correct. Yeah. Yeah. And if you're in this moment, what's wrong? With me. What's wrong?"
- **Guest:** "What's wrong with you? Nothing's wrong."
- **Joe:** "Oh, what's wrong in this moment? You can't go to the past. You can't go to the future. Just what's wrong with this moment?"
- **Guest:** "I feel I'm not fixing it fast enough."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, of course. ... Right. Fixing what? What's wrong with this moment?"
- **Guest:** "Me not being able to sit here in this moment is now what's becoming wrong with my moments."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And there, there you are in this moment. It just happened."
- *(Source: Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?)*

The arc takes four turns. The first attempt to find what's wrong requires the future ("not fixing it fast enough"). The constraint is gently re-enforced. The second attempt names the meta-problem ("not being able to sit here"). Joe receives this as evidence the person is now here. The arrival has happened in the speaking of the answer.

## Tone

Quiet, patient, slightly amused. The question is asked the way one might ask a small child where the cookie monster is hiding. There is a small playfulness — the asker already knows there is no problem to be found; the asking is to invite the asker to discover the same thing.

The constraint ("can't go to the past, can't go to the future") is delivered without heaviness. The constraint is a gentle reminder of the rules of the game, not a stern correction. The lightness lets the person keep trying without defensiveness.

## Variations

- "What's wrong right now?"
- "In this exact moment, what's wrong?"
- "If you can only look at right now — not before, not after — what is the problem?"
- "What's wrong with me right now?" (when the coach is the field being examined)
- "What's wrong with this room?" (when the room is the field being examined)
- "What's wrong with the anthill?" (the visualization variant; the anthill stands in for any present-moment object)

The variants share the same constraint: the question is about a specific, present, perceivable field. The constraint is what makes the question different from the broader "what's wrong with you" ([[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]]) or the moral-collapse refusal "what's wrong with that" ([[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]]). All three are in the what's-wrong family but operate on different fields.

## The Body-Grounded Teaching Variant

The question also appears outside coaching, as a standalone experiment in teaching contexts. The variant pairs the question with a brief body-awareness setup so the present moment is already somatically located before the question is asked.

"Take a moment, close your eyes, take another deep breath, big inhale, long exhale, and I want you to just feel your body right now. How does it feel in your feet and your knees and your eyes and your jaw? Like, what does it feel like? And just bring awareness to you here right now. And now just do this simple little experiment. Without going into the past or to the future, don't look for evidence from something that has happened a long time ago or in the last minute, and don't look for evidence for what you think is going to happen, what you imagine is going to happen in the future. Just with only the evidence that is right here, right now, find out if there's anything wrong with you. In this moment, what's wrong with you in this moment?" -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure Is What Makes Healing Possible

The teaching variant differs from the coaching variant in two ways. First, the body is grounded first — the question is asked of a system that has already arrived in present-moment sensation. Second, the time-travel constraint is explicit and verbal ("don't look for evidence from something that has happened a long time ago or in the last minute, and don't look for evidence for what you think is going to happen, what you imagine is going to happen in the future"). The constraint is more elaborated because there is no coach to gently re-enforce it in real time.

The teaching variant also clarifies what counts as discomfort:

"You might feel discomfort, but does that mean there's something wrong with you? Now, in this moment, the only moment that has ever existed, is this moment. What's wrong with you?" -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure Is What Makes Healing Possible

This addresses the most common evasion of the question: "well, I feel uncomfortable, that's what's wrong." The variant pre-empts the evasion by naming discomfort as not-the-same-thing-as-wrong. The discomfort can be present and there can still be nothing wrong. The question's force survives the discomfort exception.

The "the only moment that has ever existed" framing is also a structural reinforcement of the constraint. The past and future are not other moments to look in — they are not moments at all in this register. The only moment available is the one being examined. This collapses the search field absolutely.

## The Family of "What's Wrong" Questions

| Question | Field | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]] | The self as a whole | Demands the shame deliver content; reveals the shame has none. |
| [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] | A specific judged trait | Refuses the moral collapse on the trait; opens the trait as neutral. |
| **What's wrong with this moment?** | The present moment | Collapses the fix-frame; reveals the fix-pattern requires time-travel. |

The three questions are siblings. Each takes the structural "what's wrong with X?" form and uses it to dissolve a different version of the same anti-presence pattern. The session-level work often deploys two or three of them in sequence as the work moves from shame to moral-collapse-on-self to the actual present moment.
