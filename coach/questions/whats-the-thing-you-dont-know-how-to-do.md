# What's the thing you don't know how to do?

## The Question

After the client has described what they have been doing — usually some form of comfort-work, familiar tasks, things they know how to execute — ask them what specifically the thing they are avoiding is. The question's full form contrasts the known with the unknown:

"You know how to stock shelves. What's the thing you don't know how to do?" -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The question takes the comfort-work the client has just named and uses it as the implicit foil. The structure is: you have been doing X. X is a thing you know how to do. So what is the thing you don't know how to do — the thing the X is substituting for?

## When Joe Uses It

- After [[abstract-framing-as-defense]] has been interrupted and the client has named the comfort-work they keep returning to
- When the client has described the substitution pattern ("I go to what I know how to do instead of doing what I want to do")
- When [[complicating-it-as-the-distraction]] has been named and the structural work needs the actual edge to be located
- In [[i-keep-getting-in-my-own-way]] coaching paths, as the question that exposes the working-on-the-wrong-thing mechanism
- When the client has been building elaborate apparatus around what should be a simple unfamiliar action
- After the should-to-want substitution has happened, to move past the comfort-action and locate the actual avoided action

The question arrives in the middle phase of the session, after the structural framework has been established but before the action commitment. Its job is to surface the specific avoided thing that the rest of the work will be organized around.

## What It Produces

Often the client laughs or pauses. The question puts together two things they had not put together before. They knew they were doing the comfort-work; they had not explicitly named the avoided unfamiliar work. The question forces the naming.

What gets named is almost always something small and specific. Not "build the entire business" but "send the email to the first potential customer." Not "become an interior decorator" but "tell people I'll decorate their house." Not "write the book" but "write the first paragraph." The avoided thing tends to be tiny when stated cleanly — which makes the elaborate apparatus of avoidance suddenly visible as out of proportion to what is actually being avoided.

In the source session, the response surfaced the substitution structure clearly:

"Apparently I think the problem is, is like I've start— like even I've made my business model this roundabout workaround way, because you wouldn't think that having a shelf of things would get you to becoming an interior decorator, but that's where we're going with it." -- Client, Getting Out of Your Own Way

Joe's response named the architecture: "So you've even — the business that you aren't doing that's easy to do, you're doing so that you can get somewhere else instead of actually going to the place you want to go." The substitution had been doubled — the easy business was substituting for the harder business, which was itself substituting for the actual want (becoming an interior decorator).

The question made the doubled substitution visible. Without the contrast frame ("you know how to do X, what's the thing you don't know how to do?"), the client could have continued discussing the easy-business-as-route-to-the-harder-business without recognizing it as the avoidance.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client names a small concrete avoided action: Joe immediately moves toward making it doable. "How hard would it be right now to be like, I will [do the thing] for free?" See [[just-go-do-the-thing-stop-asking-permission]]. The smallness of the avoided action, once named, removes most of the imagined barriers.

If the client names something abstract ("step into my power," "be the person who can do this"): Joe pushes for more specific. "What's the actual action that would mean that?" The abstraction is one more iteration of the abstract framing the session was designed to escape; it has to be re-grounded.

If the client says they don't know what they don't know how to do: this is data. The avoidance has been thorough enough that the client has not let the avoided thing into view. Joe will often help by guessing — "is it asking for clients? Showing your work? Being seen?" — and watch which guess produces body activation. The body recognizes the actual avoided thing before the head does.

If the client names something that they actually do know how to do but have been pretending not to: Joe names this gently. "You know how to do that. What's the thing you actually don't know how to do?" The question can fire multiple times in a session until the genuine unfamiliar edge is located.

## Why This Works

The question rests on a structural insight from [[bound-the-behavior-find-where-it-doesnt-happen]]: the avoidance is almost always bounded. The person can act, just not on this specific thing. The contrast between where they can act and where they cannot is the diagnostic. The question is the contrast made operational.

The question also rests on the recognition that comfort-work is rarely random. People do not pick arbitrary tasks to do instead of the wanted work; they pick tasks that are related to the wanted work but require none of the unfamiliar action. The relatedness creates the illusion of progress while the actual edge stays untouched. Surfacing the unfamiliar edge by contrast with the comfort-work exposes the architecture.

The "you know how to do X" framing matters. The client cannot dismiss the comfort-work as unimportant — it has just been named as something they know how to do, which is true. The framing respects the competence the comfort-work demonstrates while still using it as the foil for what is being avoided. The client does not have to defend the comfort-work to engage with the question.

## Dialogue Example

The full exchange and its aftermath:

- **Joe:** "Yeah, so you're — it's stocking shelves. You know how to stock shelves?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, that's pretty simple."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, what's the thing you don't know how to do?"
- **Client:** "Apparently I think the problem is, is like I've start — like even I've made my business model this roundabout workaround way, because you wouldn't think that having a shelf of things would get you to becoming an interior decorator, um, but that's where we're going with it."
- **Joe:** "Oh yeah, okay. Yeah, yeah, right. So you're even right. So, so you've even — the business that you aren't doing that's easy to do, you're doing so that you can get somewhere else instead of actually going to the place you want to go."
- **Client:** "Yeah, yeah, yeah."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so how hard would it be right now to be like, I will interior decorate your home for free?"

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

In this sequence the question does its work in approximately thirty seconds. The client identifies that the comfort-work is itself substituting for a deeper want. Joe names the doubled substitution clearly. The client confirms. Then Joe immediately converts the now-visible avoided action into a small concrete doable thing. The work has moved from analyzing the pattern to specifying the action in about a minute.

## Tone

Curious, direct, slightly amused. The question is asked as a genuine inquiry — Joe does not already know the answer, and his interest in the answer is real. The amusement is at the structure of the pattern, not at the client.

The contrast framing ("you know how to do X") can be delivered lightly. The lightness signals that the comfort-work is not being judged. It is simply being noted as the implicit comparison. The client can engage without feeling that their previous efforts are being dismissed.

## What This Question Is Not

- It is not [[whats-the-question]]. That meta-question converts narration into inquiry. This question surfaces the avoided specific action within an already-active inquiry.

- It is not [[bound-the-behavior-find-where-it-doesnt-happen]]. That move bounds the avoidance by finding where the procrastination does not happen. This question goes the other direction — locates where the avoidance actually applies by using the non-avoided as the foil.

- It is not a question about skills. The client may or may not have the skills to do the unfamiliar thing. The question is about which thing has been getting worked on and which thing has been getting avoided, independent of whether the avoided thing requires unique skills.

- It is not a question about courage or willingness. Framing the avoided thing as something the client lacks the courage for would make courage the new project. The question is simpler — just naming what is being avoided so that it can be made small and doable.
