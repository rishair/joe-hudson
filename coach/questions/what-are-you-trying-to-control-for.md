# What are you trying to control for?

## The Question

The question in Joe's words, from the source session:

"What are you trying to control for? In the jealousy? If you could make something happen that would go, oh, I have that now, I don't really care about this relationship with her best friend, what's the thing you're trying to create?"

The structure: take the controlling behavior (jealousy, manipulation, pressure, frustration) and ask what specific outcome the behavior is reaching for. The behavior has a target. Surface the target.

Variations:
- "What are you trying to control for?"
- "If you got the thing you're after, what would change?"
- "What's the thing you're trying to create?"
- "What would have to happen for this jealousy to dissolve on its own?"

## When Joe Uses It

This question appears when:

- The client is engaged in controlling behavior (jealousy, micromanaging, hypervigilance, manipulation)
- The client has been describing the behavior as if it were autonomic ("I just can't stop feeling jealous")
- The client has been blaming the situation rather than locating their own want underneath it
- The work needs to move from the surface behavior to the underlying want or fear

The question pivots the conversation from the behavior to the want the behavior is trying to satisfy. This pivot is necessary because the behavior will not stop until the want is named and addressed.

## What It Produces

The question often produces a surprising answer that exposes the surrogate. In the source session, the client's first answer to "what's the thing you're trying to create?" was:

"Uh, a public company?"

The answer was off-target enough that Joe redirected: "No, no, with the relationship with your wife." The misfire was diagnostic — it showed that the controlling behavior was generalized, not specific to the wife/friend dynamic. The same controlling shape was running across multiple domains.

On the second attempt, the client got specific:

"I view this person to be a blocker in my life to the, my hopes and dreams."

Now the underlying want was visible: he wanted unimpeded movement toward his hopes and dreams, and he was experiencing this person as the obstacle. With the want named, Joe could then test whether the literal target (the friend's removal, the wife choosing him) would actually deliver the want. It would not. The friend wasn't actually the blocker. The defense against the no was the blocker.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The follow-up depends on the answer:

**If the client names an outcome the controlling behavior cannot actually produce:** Joe surfaces the gap. "If she was 100% supportive of you and was like, let me help you with the company, then that would be fine? Or do you actually have to create the company?" The question tests whether the named outcome is the real outcome. Often the client realizes the controlling behavior is aimed at the wrong target.

**If the client names a surrogate (money, status, the wife choosing them):** Joe points to what the surrogate is standing in for. The work moves to the underlying want — usually a feeling-state the client is trying to secure through external manipulation. See [[money-as-surrogate]] for the general pattern.

**If the client cannot answer the question:** Joe stays with the not-knowing. The not-knowing is information. The client is engaged in significant behavior aimed at an outcome they cannot articulate. That mismatch is itself the next thread.

**If the client gives an answer that reveals the pattern:** Joe names the pattern across domains. In the source session, once "blocker to my hopes and dreams" was named, Joe could connect it to the team dynamic. See [[same-defense-shows-up-in-marriage-and-team]].

## Why the Question Matters

Controlling behavior persists because it appears to have a purpose. The person thinks: "If I just control this, I will get what I want." The behavior runs on the implicit promise of an outcome.

When the question is asked and the outcome is named, two things become testable:

1. **Will the controlling behavior actually produce the named outcome?** Usually no. Jealousy will not make the wife choose. Micromanagement will not create alignment. The controlling behavior fails at its own stated goal.

2. **Is the named outcome the real outcome being sought?** Usually no. The named outcome is a surrogate for something more fundamental — a feeling-state, a sense of safety, a belief about the self.

Surfacing both of these collapses the implicit promise. The behavior loses its rationale. New behavior becomes possible because the old behavior is no longer being held in place by an unexamined goal.

## Distinct from "What do you want?"

The general "what do you want?" question is about surfacing desire in the abstract. This question is sharper: it takes a specific currently-running behavior and asks what outcome it is engineered to produce.

The specificity matters. "What do you want?" can produce an answer like "to be happy." That answer is too general to do work on. "What are you trying to control for with this jealousy?" produces an answer about the specific situation, which can then be tested and dissolved.

See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] for the related question that does the same move with "shoulds" rather than controlling behaviors.

## Tone

Direct, casual, conversational. The question is not therapeutic in tone. It is the tone of someone helping you debug a system you wrote. The behavior is doing something; let's figure out what; let's see if what it's doing is what you actually want.

The "what are you trying to control for?" framing is gentler than it sounds because "control for" is the language of measurement and engineering rather than the language of judgment. The client is not being accused of being controlling. They are being asked what variable their system is optimizing for. The framing makes the answer easier to give.

## Dialogue Example

From How Do I Love The "No"?:

- **Client:** "I've been somatically jealous of my wife's best friend. And after a year of therapy and a bunch of shit, anytime her name comes up, I can't get over it."
- **Joe:** [first surfaces the impact on the wife, then asks the question] "What are you trying to control for? In the jealousy? If you could make something happen that would go, oh, I have that now, I don't really care about this relationship with her best friend, what's the thing you're trying to create?"
- **Client:** "Uh, a public company?"
- **Joe:** "No, no, with the relationship with your wife."
- **Client:** "Yeah, like I view this person to be a blocker in my life to the, my hopes and dreams."
- **Joe:** [tests whether the named outcome is the real outcome] "Okay, so if this person was like 100% supportive of you and was like, let me help you with the company, then that would be fine? Or do you actually have to create the company?"
- **Client:** "Oh no, the company's created, but it's like she's like rowing in the other direction, you know what I mean? It's like an anti-capitalist type mindset that's influencing my fucking life."

The dialogue shows the question working: it surfaces the real target (the friend's anti-capitalist worldview, not her existence), which is then workable through the [[every-no-contains-a-better-answer]] principle.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a client is engaged in controlling behavior they cannot stop
- When a client describes a "blocker" without specifying what is being blocked from what
- When the surface behavior (jealousy, micromanagement, manipulation) is well-described but the underlying want is not
- When the work needs to move from blaming the situation to locating the personal want under the behavior

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward testing whether the named outcome is the real outcome ("if you got that, would the behavior stop?")
- Toward [[money-as-surrogate]] or similar surrogate-naming when the answer turns out to be a stand-in
- Toward [[every-no-contains-a-better-answer]] when the controlling behavior is rejection-of-the-no
- Toward [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]] when the controlling behavior is the same motion as keeping the wanted thing away
- Toward [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] when the answer surfaces a want that has been hidden under obligation
