# What makes you care more about their emotional state than yours?

## The Question

The question with its variants. Joe asks this when the person describes themselves carefully managing the partner's, boss's, or family member's emotions while leaving their own internal state unattended.

- "What makes you care more about their emotional state than yours?"
- "What makes it your job to take care of their emotional state?"
- "What would make it their job to take care of your emotional state?"

The third variant flips the same question in the opposite direction, which often makes the asymmetry visible in a way the first two do not.

"It's a great question to just ask yourself. What makes you care more about their emotional state than yours? Or the other version of this is, what makes it your job to take care of their emotional state? And what would make it their job to take care of your emotional state? Would be a great question, because that's what conflict avoidance is really doing, is you're managing somebody else's emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## When Joe Uses It

This question lands when the person has been describing some version of [[walking-on-eggshells]] or [[i-caretake-people]] -- a pattern of organizing their behavior around the other person's anticipated emotional reactions. The person often presents this as kindness or as good partnership. The question reframes it as a structural choice that has been made without examination.

Use when:

- The person describes carefully editing what they say to manage the partner's mood
- The person describes their day as organized around preventing the other person from getting upset
- The person describes their conflict avoidance as protection of the other person
- The person reports persistent exhaustion from emotional management work
- [[walking-on-eggshells]] has been named and the question is what is underneath the pattern
- A leader reports that they are not delivering hard feedback because of how the team member will react

The question is not designed to produce shame about the caretaking. It is designed to surface the structural fact that one person's emotional state has been prioritized over the other's, and to make that prioritization examinable.

## What It Produces

The question often produces a pause. The person has not framed their behavior in this language before. The framing exposes the asymmetry directly, which is usually uncomfortable.

Common responses and what they reveal:

- **"I just love them, I want them to be okay."** Surface response. Often opens to the deeper layer when followed up: "And what makes their being okay more important than your being okay?"
- **"They get really upset and it's hard."** Reveals that the calculus is partly about avoiding the experience of their upset. This opens the door to the work on capacity (see [[dont-make-it-your-job-to-prevent-their-triggers]]).
- **"I think I'm being kind."** Allows the reframe that the caretaking is actually preventing the other person from growing and preventing the relationship from being one in which the actual self can show up. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]].
- **"I don't know. I never thought about it that way."** The honest version. The structural choice has been made unconsciously. The question is the first time it has become visible.

The third variant ("what would make it their job to take care of your emotional state?") often produces an interesting answer. The person realizes they do not believe the other person should be responsible for their emotional state. The asymmetry becomes self-evidently strange.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Joe does not need an immediate answer. The question is a planted question. It works partly in the moment of asking and partly over time as the person notices the pattern in real situations.

In the moment:

- If the person opens into the underlying material (fear of the other's emotion, fear of loss, fear of being seen), follow that opening. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]] for one of the most common deeper layers.
- If the person stays at the surface, Joe usually does not push. He lets the question sit and moves to a related move, often the visualization of the cost of continuing the pattern.
- If the person tries to convert the question into an improvement project ("I need to stop caretaking"), Joe redirects to the felt experience. Conversion to project is itself a form of managing the discomfort the question produced.

The follow-on question often is: "Feel the pain of not being loved. You're not showing up, so no one can love you. Feel the pain of that." (See the practice for this in the related practice on visualizing the consequences.)

## Tone

Quiet. The question carries enough weight that it does not need delivery emphasis. Joe asks it as if it is simply a question, not a confrontation. The directness is structural, not tonal. The person hearing the question is given space to feel what it surfaces.
