# Aaron's First Day at De La Salle (The Mother Who Did Not Spare the Pain)

## The Story

The Story of Aaron Taylor's career hinged on a single moment when he was 15 years old. He had been kicked out of his mother's house at 14 for being a "DNF student" -- failing all his classes. After a week sleeping on a friend's floor using socks and underwear as a pillow, he came back. His mother used a Socratic conversation to walk him back from his stated goal (play pro football) through the choices and actions required to get there, helping him see for the first time the connection between his behavior in the present moment and the person he was becoming.

This produced a change. The family moved to Concord, California. His mother quit her 20-year job at Children's Hospital Oakland to take a different one paying twice as much. He enrolled at De La Salle High School, the football powerhouse with a 44-game win streak.

Then came day one of full pads.

"I get dropped off by somebody else's parents at the end of the day. My mom walk into the house. Hey honey, how'd it go? Don't say a word. Slam the door into my room. So she waited probably 10 minutes and I'm sobbing, sobbing, sobbing, sobbing. And she walks in. She goes, what happened?" -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

What Aaron said, through tears, was a complete collapse: "I went left, and the play was going right. Put my pads in. They just kept yelling at me and telling me I couldn't do it. You had to break the bad habits, and we don't do things like this here. And I just like, Mama, I'm so sorry we moved here. I can't do this. I can't do this."

His mother had moved her entire life to support this. She had quit her career, found a new house, uprooted her family. The natural thing to say next would be some form of reassurance, encouragement, problem-solving, or pep talk. She did none of these.

"She just let me go on and on, and I was so broken and so defeated and so scared. And she said to me, you know, after I got done, I looked up at her, she goes, 'You got to figure out if what you want is worth the price that you may have to pay to get it.' And she said, 'doesn't matter to me whether or not you play football or not. She said, but tomorrow morning I'll have breakfast ready for you, and if you get up, I'll know the decision you made. And if you sleep in, I also know the decision that you made. But either way, I won't say a word to you.' And she shut the door." -- Aaron Taylor's recollection, Feel Your Way to Freedom

## What His Mother Did Not Do

She did not:

- Reassure him that it would get better.
- Tell him to push through.
- Remind him of the sacrifices the family had made.
- Express disappointment.
- Convert his pain into a teaching moment with a lesson installed.
- Take responsibility for the decision.
- Make him feel that staying with football was what she wanted.
- Make him feel that quitting football was what she wanted.

She did one thing: she made the decision unambiguously his, and made it clear that either decision would be respected without commentary.

## The Moment of Choice

"Boy, there I was sitting on the edge of the bed sobbing in tears with this internal angst of what was I going to do? How could I go back? There's no way I could endure what I had just endured the previous day again. But somehow, some way, I got up and I went back to practice and I got my ass chewed again, but I made a block or two." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The pain was his. The decision was his. The capacity to get up was his. Everything that followed -- the cumulative blocks, the developing skill, "as it turned out, I was pretty damn good at football, I just didn't know it yet" -- followed from a decision he made that was actually his.

## Why This Was So Close to Never Becoming

"I think about that moment and everything -- every time I tell this story, I get emotional because everything that came after that was so close to never becoming. And I don't know what it was in me that got me up that next morning [...] And I think about Notre Dame and the friendships there and the surrogate mom that I met there. My father, uh, Green Bay, the Super Bowl, the financial freedom that's allowed me to do what I do for a living now, to enjoy more freedom, talking about sports on television, meeting my wife, my children, all of that, poof. Disappears." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The story's force comes from this: everything Aaron's life became rested on the choice he made at 15. The choice was real because his mother had not made it for him. If she had made it for him -- by spinning the disappointment, by reassuring him, by reminding him of her sacrifices, by telling him to get up tomorrow -- the choice would have been hers, not his. He might have gotten up, but he would have gotten up for her. The career that followed would have been carrying her.

By letting the decision be his, she preserved the developmental moment for him. The decision he made and the consequences he lived were his. The agency he discovered was his. The career that followed was his.

## The Larger Principle

This story is the canonical illustration of [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]]. Aaron's mother could see the pain. She could feel the stakes. She had every motive to intervene. She did not.

She did not because she understood, explicitly or intuitively, that intervening would rob something. The pain was producing the developmental moment. Removing the pain would prevent the gold. Letting the pain be his -- including potentially letting him choose to quit -- was the only way to let the gold be his.

The "I won't say a word" promise is critical. Most parents who give the choice to a child still telegraph the preferred answer through their face, their body language, their later behavior. Aaron's mother removed even that layer of influence. The decision was actually his, in a way that few decisions for 15-year-olds actually are.

## What Aaron Learned From This (Applied to His Own Children)

Aaron now applies this principle to his own sons, with difficulty:

"Maybe my 13 and 12-year-old, you know, who are really accomplished in what it is they do and both very good students, maybe I don't have to worry about them quite as much as my mom may be worried about me and just do the best I can, trust the process and let them find their own way down the mountain. I love to snowboard and I love to bomb it. My wife goes really slow. I used to get frustrated with her. We figured out that, hey, let's ride up together. I'mma bomb it. I'mma bomb it again and I'm going to meet you and then we're going to ride up together and like let her take her own way. Pick her own line down the mountain. She doesn't -- don't try to make her do what I want to do because that's the way I do it. Respect her journey." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The snowboarding metaphor extends the principle. You can love someone, you can be with them, you can ride the lift up with them. You cannot pick their line down the mountain.

## What This Example Illustrates

- [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]]: the canonical illustration of the principle.
- [[caretaking-prevents-the-bottom]]: what would have happened if the mother had spared him -- the decision would have been hers, the career would have been hers, the freedom would not have been his.
- [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]]: the 15-year-old underwent something like an annihilation. The version of himself that "couldn't do this" had to be either confirmed (quit) or contradicted (get up). Either way, an identity-extension was on the line.
- [[what-if-you-acted-exactly-how-you-want]]: the mother's framing creates the structural condition where the only relevant question is what Aaron actually wanted.

## When to Use This Example

This example fits when a coachee:

- Is a parent considering how to handle a child's developmental crisis.
- Is wrestling with whether to intervene in someone else's hard moment.
- Is the child in a situation where someone else is trying to make the decision for them.
- Is examining their own pivotal moment and the conditions that allowed them to make it.
- Is in a leadership role and considering how much to coach versus how much to let unfold.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A coachee describes wanting to spare their child or employee from pain.
- A coachee is wrestling with whether to make a decision for someone they love.
- A coachee describes their own developmental moment and wonders what allowed it.
- The principle [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] is alive and needs a story to anchor it.
