# Joe Calls His Dad to End the Criticism

## The Story

When Joe was younger, he read Fritz Perls' description of the upper dog ("you should do this, you should do this, why aren't you doing this") and the underdog ("yeah, I should, but I'm not going to, go fuck yourself"). The recognition opened a project. Joe did not try to make the voice stop. He changed his response to it. Every time the voice said "you should do this," he answered: "Oh, no thank you. You could treat me with more respect than that, please."

The voice softened. The shoulds came down. And, without planning for it, Joe started doing things he had not done before. Drawing boundaries was one of them.

About a year later, he called his father.

His father had a long-standing pattern: every time Joe came home, his father criticized him. The criticism had been so consistent that Joe did not want to come home. The father's response to that was to guilt-trip Joe into visiting. The structure was stable: criticism produces avoidance, avoidance produces guilt-tripping, guilt-tripping produces visits that produce more criticism.

Joe broke the structure with a single call.

"Hey, every time I come home, you're criticizing me, and I don't want to come home because who the heck wants to go to a place where they're criticized all the time? So I'm happy to come home if you can not criticize me. So just give me a call and say, 'Oh, hey, I'm ready to hang out with you without criticizing you,' and I'll be home." -- Joe Hudson, End Toxic Relationship Patterns

The call did not produce immediate change. The father did not call back. For a year, maybe a year and a half, the silence held. Then the father called and said "okay, I'm ready." Joe came home. The father did not criticize. There was one slip — by Joe's account, his father criticized him once, they both caught it, and his father stopped.

The criticism cycle ended. After decades of the same pattern, a one-time conversation closed it.

## What the Story Demonstrates

The story is the concrete demonstration of the principle that internal aggression and external aggression sit on the same machinery, and that working one side opens the door to working the other. See [[self-aggression-permits-others-aggression]] for the principle.

The structural sequence Joe describes:

1. **Read the Perls frame.** Recognize the upper dog / underdog structure of the inner conversation. See [[voice-in-the-head]] for the frame.
2. **Change the response, not the content.** Joe did not try to silence the voice. He answered it differently: "no thank you, you could treat me with more respect than that, please." See [[experimenting-with-the-voice]] for the broader practice this instance belongs to.
3. **The voice softens.** The should-language reduces over time. The inner relationship shifts.
4. **External boundaries start arising on their own.** Joe did not set himself a project to draw external boundaries. The boundaries came as a byproduct. The drawing of a line internally produced the capacity to draw lines externally.
5. **The external boundary closes the external pattern.** Once drawn, the boundary holds. The father, given the choice between stopping the criticism and not having Joe come home, eventually chose stopping.

The story makes the principle visible end-to-end. The internal shift was the prerequisite. The external boundary was the consequence. The relational change with the father was the consequence of the consequence.

## Why the Year-and-a-Half Matters

A specific detail worth holding: the father did not call back right away. The boundary did not produce immediate compliance. It produced a year and a half of silence.

This is important for an AI coach to know. The expectation that boundaries produce fast results is itself a piece of the same control logic that runs the original criticism pattern. The boundary is not a lever to get someone to behave. The boundary is a statement of what the person will and will not participate in. The other side's response is on its own timeline, and may never come.

Joe's father eventually called. Many fathers would not. The boundary still does its work either way. What the boundary closes for the boundary-setter is the participation in the dynamic. Whether the other person changes is a separate question. See [[boundaries]] for the broader treatment.

## The Catch-Once Detail

The father caught himself criticizing once after the boundary, and stopped. Both of them noticed. The criticism did not return.

This detail is also worth holding. A long-standing relational pattern that has cycled for decades can sometimes end in seconds once both parties have a shared awareness that it is happening. The pattern's life depended on its invisibility. The first time it became visible and was named, it lost the cover it had been running under. The father did not have to re-engineer his personality to stop criticizing; he just had to notice the pattern firing and decline to ride it.

This is [[awareness-as-change-agent]] operating on the relational scale. Bringing attention to the pattern is what changed it. The boundary made the pattern visible to both of them at the same time. The visibility itself was most of the work.

## Why This Story Is the Demonstration

Joe uses this story specifically as the concrete example for the teaching that to end toxic relationship patterns, the first piece is to stop having a toxic relationship with yourself. The story is selected because it shows the sequence in his own life: internal work first, external work second, external relational change as the result.

"That was the beginning of me seeing what actually needed to happen for me to stop abusive relationships, which was I had to stop having an abusive relationship with myself. That's the most critical piece. I had to see that the thing that I was creating in my life was also being created in me." -- Joe Hudson, End Toxic Relationship Patterns

The story is not a story about how to confront a parent. It is a story about how internal change produces the capacity for external change that then produces relational change. The confrontation with the father is the third event in the sequence; the inner-voice work is the first.

## The Asymmetry Frame Joe Used: "I Wouldn't Hang Out With Anybody Who Does That To Me"

When Joe tells the story in the 2025-12-16 context (framed as the home-doesn't-feel-like-home teaching), the specific phrasing of the boundary surfaces a structural argument worth holding separately. Joe did not call his dad and present the criticism as a personal failing. He pointed at the asymmetry:

"I just, I saw it. I just recognized it. And I called up my dad and I said, 'You know, every time I come home, you criticize me. You tell me what I'm doing wrong. You tell me what I need to do to be good enough. And I wouldn't hang out with anybody who does that to me. So I'm just not going to hang out with you if you're going to do that. But I love you and I really want to hang out with you. So just let me know when you're prepared to hang out with me. And not judge me, not criticize me, not try to manage me.'" -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

The asymmetry frame ("I wouldn't hang out with anybody who does that to me") is the structural lever. Most family relationships run on the assumption that the obligations of family override what would be obviously unacceptable behavior from anyone else. The frame names that exception and refuses to accept it. The dad is being asked to meet the same standard any non-family person would have to meet.

The phrasing also keeps the love explicit ("I love you and I really want to hang out with you") and puts the next move in the dad's hands ("just let me know when you're prepared"). This is a clean version of the boundary structure: name what you will not do, name that you still want the relationship, leave the door for the other person to choose differently.

## The Two-Years Variant of the Same Story

Joe tells this story across multiple episodes with slight variation in the silence-duration figure. In the 2025-03-07 telling (see [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]]), the silence was "like 18 months." In the 2025-12-16 telling, it was "one year passed, two years passed, but he finally called." The variation does not change the structural point — the boundary held across a long period of silence without Joe softening or relitigating. The "year, two years" framing emphasizes that the boundary holding was sustained across multiple years, not just months, and that the dad's eventual return required real time. Either telling makes the same point: boundaries are not levers for fast compliance, and the cost of holding them is the cost.

## When to Reach for This Story

- When someone is asking how to stop accepting bad treatment from a specific person, and they have not yet looked at their internal relationship.
- When someone has been doing inner work for a while and is asking why nothing external is changing.
- When the question of "how do I draw a boundary with my parent?" arrives without the prior question "how do I stop drawing that line on myself?"
- When someone is afraid that drawing an external boundary will explode the relationship; the story shows that it can also close a chapter that has been open for decades.
- When teaching the bidirectional principle of [[self-aggression-permits-others-aggression]] and a worked example is needed.
- When someone is dreading family visits and asking what to do — see [[i-dread-going-home]] and [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]].
- When demonstrating the asymmetry frame ("I wouldn't accept this from anyone else") as the structural lever for family boundaries.

## What the Story Does Not Say

The story does not say that every parent will respond the way Joe's father did. The story does not say that boundaries always work to close patterns. The story does not say that the internal work is sufficient and the external boundary is optional.

It says the internal work was the precondition for the external work, the external boundary was a separate action that had to be taken, and the relational outcome was a downstream consequence of both. Each step had to happen. Without the inner work, the external boundary would have been an attack disguised as a boundary, or would have been undermined by Joe's continued tolerance of self-criticism. Without the external boundary, the inner work would have remained inner, and the relational pattern would have continued.

The story is the both/and. Inside and outside. Each makes the other possible.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. Joe tells the story without dwelling on his father's character or the emotional weight of the call. The story is delivered as data: here is the sequence, here is what each step did, here is what happened. The lack of drama in the telling is part of the teaching. The pattern that loomed large for decades is named in a few sentences. The boundary that ended it is named in one quoted line. The integration ("he caught himself criticizing once and stopped") gets a single clause.

The proportion of the telling fits the proportion of the work. Most of the change happened in the inner-voice work that preceded the call. The call itself was the small visible event that the invisible work had made possible.
