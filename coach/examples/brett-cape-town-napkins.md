# Brett's Cape Town Napkins (Discovering the Stability That Was Already There)

## The Story

In 2009, Brett Kistler was 26. He had broken his arm skiing in Utah, then traveled to Africa with the broken arm. In Zambia he re-broke it doing motorcycle work helping smuggle refugees, and crashed. While he was there, his father lost his job and his health insurance. Brett had a couple hundred dollars and inconsistent freelance web work.

He took a bus from Zambia to Cape Town for the better medical care available there. He found a place to stay, paid rent for the week, and sank into the city for about a month. When he went to check his bank account, it was empty. The South African rand had nearly doubled in value against his currency in the time he had been there. He hadn't been tracking it. His savings were gone.

He was in a foreign country with no money, a broken arm, no health insurance, no job, and rent paid only for the current week.

He walked Long Street -- the main thoroughfare of Cape Town -- and wrote his name and phone number on napkins. He walked into bars and businesses. "Hey, I'm a web developer. Y'all's websites look like they could use a little bit of work. I'd love to help you out." He left the napkins.

He got one phone call. The client wasn't great. But the phone call came.

He survived.

## What the Story Shows

The capacity to start from absolute scratch in a brand-new place was already part of who Brett was. He didn't develop it in that moment; he discovered it. Before the moment, his identity had not catalogued this capacity. After the moment, it had.

"I still refer to that moment now where like, you know what, if I actually lost fully everything, it would suck. There's a lot that I love and that I'm attached to about like material possessions and, you know, access to financial resources that I've had in my life. And I also know that I can go back and just write down my name and phone number on napkins and do something from absolute scratch in a brand new place, in a brand new environment all over again." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The "I can go back and just" is what changed. The capacity was demonstrated once. The identity now included it. Every future fear about financial collapse could be met with the body's memory that survival was on the other side of it.

## What Joe Reaches For This Example For

This is the canonical illustration of [[identity-lags-behind-growth]]. The story is useful when:

- A person is afraid of a financial loss they have not pre-felt
- A person is in a limiting belief about not being able to start over
- A person is gripping external markers of security (savings, job, location) because they don't trust they could rebuild
- A person is being asked to make a brave decision and is treating the consequences as more catastrophic than they would actually be

The story reminds them: the capacity to navigate the worst-case is probably already in them. The work is to recognize it, not to develop it from nothing.

## The Related Insight

The Cape Town story also illustrates [[knowing-yourself-is-the-only-safety]]. The external markers had all collapsed -- savings gone, family safety net gone, body injured, foreign country. None of those externals were available to provide safety. What was available was Brett himself.

The home he could go to was himself. The home he could go to is yourself.

The story is one of the most direct illustrations of what that home looks like when it's tested.

## The Pattern Across His Life

Brett uses this story as an anchor. When he is in a high-stakes business decision, when he is at the exit point of a jump, when he is in an uncertain conversation, he can return to the Cape Town moment as evidence that the worst-case is survivable.

This is how internal stability accumulates over time: through demonstrated survival of difficulty, catalogued and remembered, available to be drawn on the next time.

"That's the kind of internal stability that might make us be like, hey, let me try something new, like walking a slackline or starting a business." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The Cape Town story is one chapter in the accumulating internal stability. See [[brett-slackline-metaphor]] for the somatic version of the same accumulation.

## Tone

Brett tells this story without drama. The events were dramatic; the telling is matter-of-fact. The point is not the heroism of the moment; the point is the recognition that came out of it. The dramatic delivery would obscure the actual teaching.

## Connection to Resources vs. Resourcefulness

The story is a direct demonstration of [[resources-vs-resourcefulness]]. The resources collapsed. The resourcefulness was what remained. The resourcefulness produced the next thing: the napkins, the calls, the client, the survival.

A person trying to solve a problem by accumulating more resources is not solving the problem at the level where the fear lives. The fear is about whether resourcefulness will hold if the resources fail. The only way to know is to test it. Brett tested it involuntarily and discovered it held.
