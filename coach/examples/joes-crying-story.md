# Joe Learning to Cry

## The Story

In his early twenties, Joe was looking through a photo album and saw a picture of himself as a child crying. The same photo showed him looking dumbfounded that someone was taking a picture of him while he was upset.

He realized: his parents had been uncomfortable with his emotions because they had been taught to be uncomfortable with their own. One of the ways they handled his crying was to tease him about it. Taking the photo was part of the teasing.

"I was like, ah, that's probably why I haven't cried in 14 years." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

He put the photo on his desk and decided to learn how to cry.

## The First Attempt

A year passed. He had not cried. The photo had not worked.

So he committed to a more deliberate effort. He drove to a remote trail, walked in, then walked three miles off-trail so that nobody could possibly see him. He was carrying that much shame about crying.

Then he faked it. He just sat there and faked crying. He did this for three months.

"I would just fake it. And I did that for like 3 months." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## The Turn

Eventually it became real. The fake crying gave the body a track to run on, and the real material found its way out.

When it became real, the relief was so great that he let it continue for four days. He cried while brushing his teeth. He could have stopped it, but his body had been waiting fourteen years and was not about to stop now.

"My body was just like, years of tension just evaporating in days." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## What Followed

Once he had access to tears, that access deepened. Then it deepened again. Eventually he arrived at: "Every single heartbreak of mine increases my capacity to love. I cannot wait for more heartbreak."

The thing that he could not access at all in his twenties became something he sought.

## What This Story Illustrates

1. **[[childhood-imprinting]]:** Parents who could not handle their own emotions taught him to hide his. The photo was a visible artifact of the programming.
2. **Faking can prime feeling.** In the early stages, especially when an emotion has been suppressed for years, the body may not know the shape of the motion. Faking gives it the shape.
3. **[[daily-emotional-yoga]]:** The three months in the woods was effectively daily emotional yoga. Time. Privacy. Permission. Repetition.
4. **The shame around emotion is itself programmed.** Joe walked three miles off-trail because of the shame, not because of any real risk of being seen. The shame was the programming asserting itself.
5. **[[stages-of-emotional-development]]:** Stage 1 to stage 2 in action. From "I don't even know I have these emotions" to "I can let them move."
6. **[[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]:** Fourteen years of unfelt material released in days once the door opened.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

- When someone says they cannot access an emotion they want to feel.
- When someone reports they have not cried in years.
- When someone is embarrassed about feeling weird or fake while trying to feel.
- When the shame around emotional expression is itself the obstacle.
- When someone needs evidence that faking it is a legitimate starting point.

## Tone

Self-deprecating, warm. Joe tells this on himself. The three miles off-trail is the kind of detail that makes the story humane. He is not presenting himself as having arrived emotionally fluent from the start. He had to fake-cry alone in the woods to get here.
