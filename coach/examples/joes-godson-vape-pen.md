# Joe's Godson and the Vape Pen

## The Story

Joe is at lunch with a friend (since high school) and the friend's son, who is Joe's godson. The friend, frustrated, tells Joe what his teenage son has been doing. The son stole $50 from him, bought a vape pen, and was vaping in a classroom. Five minutes later, the friend complains that the real problem with his son is that he has no ambition. He just does not want to do anything.

Joe interrupts. "What? Of course he wants to do something. Do you know how hard it is to do what he did?"

Joe walks through it. Stealing $50 required planning. Planning is ambition. Going through with it knowing he could get caught required commitment. Commitment is ambition. Then he procured the vape pen, which took effort. Then he had so much ambition that he did it in a classroom and got caught. "That is some CEO-level ambition."

The friend is now looking at Joe like "shut up, Joe." The godson is looking at Joe like "I should have visited my godfather more often."

Joe pivots to the son: "There's clearly ambition. It's just that you want him to be ambitious in one way, but he's ambitious in another. Let's look at how he's ambitious."

He asks the kid what he wants to do. The kid wants to play a particular sport, a next-generation laser tag game, which requires equipment that costs money. Joe goes with it. How are you going to afford this? Well, I'd have to get a job. What kind of job? This kind. That doesn't pay much. The kid starts thinking through the steps. Within a few minutes, he is enthusiastically planning out what he needs to do to get what he wants. Joe asks: "Well, how can your dad help?" And the kid starts telling his father what would actually help him be ambitious and get things done.

## The Point

The friend's framing: my son has no ambition. The friend's evidence: my son does not do the things I want him to do.

Joe's reframe: ambition is the ability and drive to plan, commit, and execute toward something wanted. The son demonstrably has all of that. He just has it pointed at vape pens and laser tag rather than school.

The error is not that the son lacks ambition. The error is that the father is treating "ambition" as a generic capacity that should be pointed wherever the father wants. Ambition is always pointed at something specific. The thing it is pointed at is the thing the person actually wants. See [[the-want]].

The hook for productive engagement is not "you should be more ambitious about school." It is "what do you actually want, and how do we move toward that?" The first frame produces resistance. The second produces the kind of engaged planning the father wanted in the first place.

## The Internal Application

Joe makes the move from external to internal at the end:

"We do that internally as well as externally. We're usually like the father in that story rather than the godfather in that story to ourselves. We're telling ourselves how to improve, what we need to do, instead of just paying attention to what the natural thing is. And if we follow that thread far enough down, it has far better results. It moves much quicker." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

The same pattern runs inside a single person. The internal critic plays the father role: you should be more disciplined, you should be more ambitious, you should care about the right things. The natural self plays the godson role: actually I want this very specific thing. Treating yourself like the critic does to you produces the same dynamic the father produced with his son.

## When Joe Reaches for It

Joe uses this story when:
- A parent (or self-critical adult) is frustrated by perceived lack of motivation.
- The conversation needs a concrete illustration of [[wants-over-shoulds]].
- A person is using improvement-language ("I should be more X") and missing the authentic want underneath.
- Reframing is needed: same data, different interpretation.

The story also functions as a demonstration of [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] in real time. Joe asks the kid "what do you want to do" and follows it through the planning. The kid does the work that "you should care about your future" would never produce.

## What Makes the Story Work

- The behavior the father condemns is, on inspection, evidence of the trait the father claims is missing.
- The reframe is not theoretical. Joe demonstrates it live by working with the kid in front of the father.
- The kid responds to being treated as already-ambitious by demonstrating ambition.
- The story translates to internal work. Most listeners will recognize themselves as both the father and the son.

## Tone

The telling is funny. Joe sets it up with self-aware acknowledgement ("this is going to be one of those stories that lets you know maybe you don't want to have me as a friend"). The humor is part of the teaching: the father's frame is so common that watching someone disrupt it lands as comedy.
