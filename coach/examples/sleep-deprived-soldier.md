# The Sleep-Deprived Soldier Study

## The Story

A U.S. Army study tested soldiers on the same task under two different conditions. The researchers used two versions of a comparable test to control for memorization.

**Condition 1:** Soldiers in boot camp, worked to exhaustion, sleep deprived. They took the test in that state.

**Condition 2:** Same soldiers, after a couple days of R&R. Not sleep deprived, not being rushed. They took the test in that state.

After both tests, the soldiers were asked: which test did you do better on, and which did you complete faster?

80% of the soldiers said they did better and faster on the first test, when they were sleep deprived.

100% of them actually scored better and completed faster on the second test, when they were rested.

"They took a group of soldiers in boot camp, and they just worked them to death and had them sleep deprived. And then they put them in front of the test, and they did the test. And then they let them rest for a couple days, R&R, and then they came back. They weren't sleep deprived, and they did the test. And then they asked the soldiers, of these two tests, which one did you think you scored better at, and which one do you think you did quicker? And 80% of the people thought that they had done quicker work and more accurate work on the first test when they were sleep deprived. And in actuality, 100% of them did better when they had rest and they were not being rushed through the whole situation." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## The Point It Illustrates

Humans have a mental illusion about efficiency that operates like an optical illusion. The feeling of being efficient and the reality of being efficient have diverged. When you are busy, sleep-deprived, and running on adrenaline, you feel productive. The data shows you are not.

The frenetic state has a phenomenology of efficiency. It does not have the actuality. This is why the cultural marker "I am so busy" is often evidence of inefficiency rather than evidence of accomplishment.

This study underwrites [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]]: the actual measure of human efficiency is not the felt sense of busyness, and not hours worked, but whether work landed and whether the person was integrated while doing it. Rested, calm, enjoying workers outperform exhausted, busy workers.

## When Joe Reaches for This

When someone insists their grinding work hours are necessary because they are "more productive that way." The study is the counter-evidence. The feeling of productivity in a depleted state is an illusion. The actual productivity is in the rested state.

When someone resists taking time off because they fear it will hurt output. The study shows the opposite.

When teaching [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] and needing a concrete demonstration that the cultural intuition about productivity is wrong.

When pairing with the multitasking research: people believe they multitask effectively while their actual performance degrades. Same illusion, different setting.

## The Connection to Cultural Frame

The illusion is supported by cultural messaging. In some cultures, working 60 hours is evidence of seriousness; in others, it is evidence of incompetence ("you needed 60 hours to do that?"). The frame that values busyness reinforces the illusion. The frame that values output relative to time exposes it.

The study is one piece of evidence that the busyness-equals-productivity frame is wrong on its own terms. Even soldiers, trained to function under stress, perform worse when stressed and depleted than when rested.
