# Joe's Parents and the Criticism Boundary

## The Story

Joe's parents had a habit of criticizing him constantly whenever he came home to visit. The criticism was passive-aggressive — not direct anger, not a stated complaint, just a steady drip of remarks about how he was doing things wrong. He had been receiving it as long as he had been visiting.

At some point he called them up and named it.

"Hey, I love you. I like coming home. But every time I come home, you criticize me. And I don't — why? I wouldn't do that. I wouldn't do that with anybody else in the world. Nobody else in the world would I be like, sign me up to go over to their house and be criticized for fucking 30 minutes or 2 hours or for half a day. I'm just not doing it. So I love you. I want to come over to your house, but I'm not going to do that until you can agree to stop criticizing me when I come over." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression: How To Identify & Respond

The boundary had two parts: the love (I love you, I want to come over) and the limit (I will not come over while this continues).

## What Happened Next

His parents did not call him. For approximately 18 months.

"They didn't call me for like 18 months. I think it was like 18 months. There was like silence. And then they were like, okay, we miss you enough. I guess they didn't say it that way, but they were like, okay. And then like the criticism pretty much stopped in the family." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression: How To Identify & Respond

The boundary held. Joe did not call them either, did not cave, did not soften the line, did not relitigate. The 18 months were the cost. After 18 months, the parents wanted the relationship back enough to change the behavior. The criticism in the family pretty much stopped.

## The Point It Illustrates

The story illustrates several distinct teachings at once:

**A boundary on passive aggression can be the same as a boundary on direct aggression.** The default response to passive aggression is to feel that the form is too vague to set a boundary about. Joe's point: the criticism is doing the same work as yelling. The same kind of boundary applies. "It's the same thing as if, like, I came home, my dad yelled at me all the time. I would say, hey, I'd love to see you, but it's not okay for you to yell at me, which I did at a different time in my relationship with my dad." (Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression: How To Identify & Respond)

**The boundary is about your behavior, not theirs.** "I'm not going to do that until you can agree to stop." The "I'm not going to" is what Joe is choosing. The "until you can agree to stop" is the condition under which he is willing to resume. He is not demanding they stop; he is naming what he will do as long as it continues.

**The cost can be substantial and the boundary still holds.** 18 months of silence is significant. The parents' wager was that Joe would buckle first. He did not. The structural feature of the boundary — that it is what he does, not what they have to do — is what made it possible to hold across that timeframe. If the boundary had been "you must stop criticizing me," the conversation would have happened immediately and probably ended badly. Because it was "I will not be there while this continues," there was nothing to negotiate.

**The boundary changed not only that relationship but the family pattern.** "Then like the criticism pretty much stopped in the family." A boundary held cleanly by one member of a system shifts the system. The change was not contained to the parents' behavior with Joe; it propagated.

**You wouldn't accept this from anyone else — why from family?** The clarifying frame is the comparison. "I wouldn't do that. I wouldn't do that with anybody else in the world. Nobody else in the world would I be like, sign me up to go over to their house and be criticized for fucking 30 minutes or 2 hours or for half a day." Family relationships often run on the assumption that obligations to family override what would be obviously unacceptable behavior from anyone else. The frame names the absurdity of that assumption.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone is on the receiving end of chronic passive-aggressive criticism from a parent or family member
- When someone says "but they're my mom" or "but it's just how they are" as the reason not to draw the boundary
- When someone is trying to address passive aggression by negotiating, arguing, or trying to convince and it has not worked
- When the question is whether a boundary about passive-aggressive behavior is even possible (it is)
- When someone is considering a long-term boundary and worried about the cost being too high
- When the conversation turns to whether boundaries actually change anything in long-standing family patterns
- When someone is in the middle of a long silence after drawing a family boundary and asking whether to call first

## What This Pairs With

This story pairs naturally with [[joes-dad-drinking-boundary]]. Both are boundaries Joe drew with his parents, both involved long periods of separation, both ultimately changed the relationship for the better, both were about Joe's behavior (what he would do) rather than demands on the parents (what they had to do).

The two stories also illustrate the dual-direction of the boundary teaching: the dad-drinking story is the canonical example of [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows|boundaries refining as capacity grows]]; the parents-criticism story is the canonical example of a passive-aggression boundary that holds at substantial cost and ultimately changes the system.

## The Specific Detail That Matters

The framing in the conversation Joe used with his parents started from love, not from grievance. "Hey, I love you. I like coming home." Not "you have a problem" or "you need to stop." The love is named first, the limit follows. This is structurally important: the boundary stayed [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against|a boundary with, not a boundary against]]. The 18 months of silence were not Joe punishing his parents. They were the structural consequence of Joe's choice, held without animosity.

The comparison ("nobody else in the world would I be like, sign me up to go over to their house and be criticized") is also important. It is a clarifying frame, not an attack. It surfaces the asymmetry without accusing the parents of being uniquely awful.

## What It Demonstrates About the Other Side

What the parents did during the 18 months is also part of the teaching. They did not call. They did not apologize. They did not change their behavior immediately. The boundary did not produce instant compliance. The boundary produced a long stalemate, and only after a long stalemate did the relationship resume on different terms.

This is consistent with the broader principle that boundaries are not about producing a desired behavior change in the other party. The parents could have not called for 30 years. The boundary still would have been the right move. The fact that they eventually came back is a happy outcome, not the point of the boundary. The point of the boundary was that Joe was no longer organizing his life around being criticized.

## The 2026-05-19 Retelling: The Boundary as the Operational Form of "I Can Have Love Without Criticism"

In the 2026-05-19 telling, Joe places the same boundary inside the broader teaching on pushing love away. He gives a slightly different age figure ("I wasn't a kid, I was 20-something years old") and frames the call as the spoken form of the recognition that love does not have to come with the wired-in companion.

"I called up my parents and I was like, hey, I love you. I want to hang out. I want to be your son. But every time I come over, you just criticize me nonstop. And nobody wants to be criticized. I don't want to come over if I'm criticized. So I'm happy to come over if you can stop criticizing me." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The 2026-05-19 telling is the canonical worked example of [[boundary-against-the-wired-pattern]] — the boundary statement that separates love from the companion (criticism) it got wired with in childhood. The structure is the three-component form: the love ("I love you. I want to hang out. I want to be your son"), the want (implicit in wanting to come over), and the limit ("I'm not coming over if I'm criticized").

Joe also adds two new specific framings in this retelling that the earlier sources do not:

**The scariness of the boundary as faith in a love the body has not yet experienced.** "Today I laugh at it because I'm like, of course I could draw that boundary. But then it was like life-changing. It was, it was the scariest thing, one of the scariest things I'd ever done. But what I was doing is I was making a stand saying, hey, I won't accept love in this variety anymore. I will only have love that comes without criticism. But the reason it was scary in me is because I was thinking I was saying I might not ever get love again. Because the only love I knew was with criticism." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The retelling makes explicit that the boundary required holding faith in a kind of love the body had no experiential memory of. The wiring had paired love with criticism so completely that refusing the criticism felt to the body like refusing love itself. The boundary was the structural action that allowed a different kind of love to become possible — but at the moment of taking it, the action looked indistinguishable from refusing love.

**What the body learned through the holding.** "In that process, I learned, oh, I can have love without criticism. And now, once I did that, I don't need to push love away as much anymore. Why do I need to push it away? Because it's actually safe." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The teaching here is the downstream effect: the boundary did not just change the parents' behavior. It changed Joe's body's wiring. The body learned, experientially, that love does not require criticism. Once that learning happened, the push-away strategies the body had been using to manage criticism-loaded love stopped being needed. The need to push love away decreased because love became safe.

This connects the example directly to [[three-ways-we-push-love-away]]: the strategies of chase, avoid, and fight are all responses to a love-imprint that paired love with something dangerous. When the boundary work updates the imprint, the push-away strategies lose their function. The mechanism is not willpower; it is the body's new evidence that the wired companion is not required for love to land.

The 18-month duration in the 2026-05-19 retelling is "almost a year and a half," consistent with the 2025-03-07 telling.

## The 2025-12-16 Retelling: The Boundary as Structural Response to the Home-Doesn't-Feel-Like-Home Pattern

In the 2025-12-16 telling, Joe places the same boundary in the broader context of why some families feel like home and others feel like an obligation. The boundary is reframed not as a response to passive aggression specifically but as a response to criticism and management more broadly — see [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]] for the principle the boundary operates on.

In that telling, Joe also gives a slightly different duration figure ("one year passed, two years passed") and emphasizes the explicit invitation back: "let me know when you're prepared to hang out with me. And not judge me, not criticize me, not try to manage me." The "not try to manage me" addition broadens the scope of the boundary from the criticism specifically to the whole field of management-as-relationship-dynamic.

The 2025-12-16 retelling makes explicit that this boundary is one available response to [[i-dread-going-home]] — the adult-child concern that the family home has become a place that produces dread because of the ongoing criticism and management. See [[joes-call-with-dad-about-criticism]] for the same boundary framed through the lens of inner-voice work and self-aggression-permits-others-aggression. The two retellings emphasize different aspects of the same boundary; both are useful depending on what the person is asking about.
