# Brett's Brother Scott

## The Story

In May 2023, Brett and his older brother Scott were co-facilitating a retreat together. During the retreat, Scott was diagnosed with stage 4 glioblastoma -- brain cancer with a short typical prognosis.

By December 2024, the brother had doubled his original prognosis. He was not doing well medically. By another measure, Brett reports, he was doing extraordinarily well.

The brother was living in Cleveland with his wife and two teenage children. Brett lived across the country. The diagnosis triggered a series of recognitions and reconfigurations in Brett's relationship to his brother, to his family, and to his own life.

## The Story That Was Running Underneath

Years earlier, Brett and Scott had gotten into skydiving together. They started a business together. They were going to travel the world, jump off things. Scott got married, had kids, and stayed in Cleveland. Brett went the other way.

For a long time, Brett was running a story: "I had left him behind and he was missing out." It was, Brett says, "subconsciously lodged." He had not seen how much it was organizing him. The story included a vague sense of guilt and a vague intention to someday go back and "free him" -- bring him into the business, take him on an adventure, do something to undo what felt like an abandonment.

The story did not survive the diagnosis.

"Very quickly upon hearing the news of his cancer, immediately that flipped. And I was like, he now has two kids in their early teens that he never could have had if he was on that adventure we were on together. The life that he lived was not a life where he stayed home. It's the life where he dove in to his family. And that flipped for me. I felt a sense of, not envy, but a sense of like, oh, wow, that fucking life that he's lived, man." -- Brett Kistler, How Death Brings Life Closer

The diagnosis dissolved the story in seconds. What had been "I escaped and he didn't" became "he dove in to his family in a way I never did." The same facts, reorganized by the felt-imminence of the loss.

## What Joe Names About This

Joe's read on the moment Brett describes is structural: when imminent death is acknowledged, the stories of who-was-supposed-to-do-what cannot survive. They get reorganized by what is actually here, which is the moment with the brother who is not dead today.

"It's like the only thing that can be there is the now, because it's the only thing you know you've got. He's not dead today. And so all you really have is, like, this moment with him. And somehow or another, our stories don't get to exist in that moment." -- Joe Hudson, How Death Brings Life Closer

The story Brett had been running required an indefinite future to make sense. "I left him behind" only makes sense if there is more story to come, in which the leaving will or will not be remedied, in which the brother does or does not catch up, in which Brett does or does not go back for him. When the indefinite future collapses, the indefinite-future-story collapses with it. What is left is the actual brother, actually here, actually with his actual kids, actually doing the life he chose.

## The Brother's Own Compression

Scott's experience of the diagnosis was its own version of the compression. Brett reports him saying, late in the illness:

"Yeah, I'm just lying in bed some days. I can't even move. I can't get out of bed. But when I just hear the door opening and closing with my kids running in and out of the house playing, I could never be happier." -- Scott, as quoted by Brett Kistler, How Death Brings Life Closer

The statement is structurally what [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] describes. The thing the brother had previously had to do something with -- the kids running through the house -- has become the thing itself. The doing has fallen away. The hearing of the door is the happiness. There is no preliminary, no preparation, no setup. The sound of the door is the life he is living, and he could never be happier.

Brett's response to this story-from-his-brother: "So my stories of what I require to enjoy my life, fucking gone." Joe's response: "Like, but my tea's too hot." The contrast between Scott's enjoyment from bed and the standard daily complaint about the temperature of the tea is what the example produces.

## The Brother's Goodbye-Like-It

A specific moment Brett describes: the last time he was with Scott and his sister, the brother held them both close. As the older brother of both of them, he said:

"Hey, it's been an honor to grow up with both of you."

Brett notes that he has never gotten this kind of moment with any of his many friends who died -- they "went around the corner and never came back." The slow-death of cancer offers what the sudden-death of BASE jumping accidents did not: the possibility of a real goodbye, said out loud, with everyone present.

This is the gift Brett names. The hurricane (slow, anticipated, devastating, with time to say things) versus the earthquake (fast, unanticipated, devastating, with nothing said). Both are loss. Only one offers the goodbye.

## The Deeper Relationship the Diagnosis Made Possible

After the retreat ended, Brett called Scott back and proposed an explicit reframe:

"Scott, so realistically, there's a reality where we could have spent the rest of our lives seeing each other every couple of years and, you know, making small talk around the kids or whatever. And having conversations about business and stuff going on in the world. And then there's a world where maybe you're not here for another year. And in that amount of time, there's a world where we can have a deeper relationship than we ever would have had otherwise. So let's lean into that." -- Brett Kistler, How Death Brings Life Closer

The proposition: use the diagnosis as the structure that makes the deeper relating possible. Joe's response: "Yeah, that's the opportunity. Because when that acknowledgement is there, then you get that deeper opportunity every time, every moment."

The opportunity is not specific to terminal diagnoses. The same acknowledgement -- that this person could be gone -- is structurally available in any relationship at any time. The diagnosis just makes the structure unavoidable. Once it is felt-acknowledged, the deeper relating becomes available. Without the acknowledgement, the small talk continues by default.

This is one of the moves in [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] applied to a sibling relationship. Pre-grieve the loss to be undefended in the relating that is still possible.

## What the Example Teaches

The story teaches three things at once:

1. **The story of who-left-whom dissolves when mortality is acknowledged.** Brett's "I escaped and he didn't" story could not survive the diagnosis. The facts that supported the story were the same. The interpretation collapsed because the future required to make the interpretation meaningful collapsed.

2. **The slow loss is a different gift than the sudden loss.** Brett's previous experience of death was sudden -- friends in extreme sports, no warning, no closure. The brother's cancer offers the opposite: time, conversation, the goodbye said. Both are losses; only the slow one allows the closure ritual.

3. **The dying person's compression can teach the living person.** Scott's "I could never be happier" from his bed is the lesson the living relatives receive. The dying person, by being fully in what is left, demonstrates the practice that the living relatives have been struggling to do in their normal lives.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

The example is most useful when:

- A person is in a long-running story about someone in their family that has been organizing them
- A person is preparing for a loss and is trying to figure out how to be in the remaining time
- A person is contrasting fast loss (accident, sudden death) and slow loss (cancer, neurodegeneration) and trying to understand the different work each requires
- A person is in caretaking mode and the question is what the dying person is actually offering rather than only what they need
- A person is in a daily-complaint frame and the example is needed to show what enjoyment looks like under far worse conditions
- The teaching of [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] needs a recent, concrete instance from someone the listener knows (Brett)
