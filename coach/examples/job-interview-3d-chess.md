# The Job Interview 3D Chess Story

## The Story

In his early 30s, Joe was looking for a job and going through job interviews. He kept getting rejected. With every rejection, he kept trying to game it harder. He kept asking himself: "what do they want from me, and how can I be that person so that they hire me?"

Every rejection hurt. But as he stayed with the hurt, he noticed it was actually hurting for two distinct reasons.

**The first reason: they rejected him.** This is the obvious surface pain.

**The second reason: he had already rejected himself.** Before the interview ever happened, by deciding to perform as someone else for the interview, he had said to himself "I don't trust myself. I don't trust being myself to get accepted." That self-rejection was actually more painful than the rejection by the interviewer. The interviewer was rejecting a performance. He had rejected himself.

**The third reason (he later identified): the unwinnable game.** He was trying to game something he had no idea how to game. He would look at the interviewer and try to be what they wanted. But he did not know what they wanted. They might want a patsy. They might want a super capable person. They might want someone subservient. They might want something that depended on what kind of mood they were in that day, on how much coffee they had had. He was trying to play 3D chess against an unknowable opponent in an unknowable game.

The compound stress: he was stressed because he was rejecting himself, and stressed because he was trying to play a game he could not win.

## Joe's Words

"I remember this moment where I — early 30s, I was looking for a job and I was doing job interviews and I was getting rejection after rejection after rejection. And I kept on trying to game it. Like, I kept on thinking, what do they want from me and how can I be that person so that they, that they, you know, hire me? And every time I got rejected, it hurt. But I started to notice that it hurt for two reasons. The first reason was that I got rejected. But the second thing is I rejected myself, that I actually had said, I'm — I don't trust myself. I don't trust being myself to get accepted. So I had rejected myself first, and that rejection was far more painful than the rejection of others." -- Joe Hudson, You're Not Giving People the Chance to Love You

"But what I also noticed is that there was another pain, which is that I was trying to game it. And I'm trying to game something that I have like no idea how to game. So I'm going into these job interviews and I'm looking at this person and I've thought through all the things I'm going to say before I get there, but I don't know what they want to hear. They might want a patsy. I don't know if that's what they want. They might want a super capable person. They might just want somebody who's subservient. I don't know what they want. And yet I think they do. And so I'm changing myself to give them something that I don't even know that I want. So there's a tremendous amount of stress in just trying to play that kind of 3D chess. And it's unwinnable because what they want might be determined by how much coffee they had that day. I just can't win that game." -- Joe Hudson, You're Not Giving People the Chance to Love You

## What the Story Illustrates

Three structural facts about the doing-to-be-valued strategy:

**1. Self-rejection precedes the externalized rejection.** The strategy of "perform as what they want" requires the actor to reject themselves first as the price of admission. The actor has decided that they are not okay as they are. The decision is made before any interaction with the audience. By the time the audience gives feedback (positive or negative), the actor has already done the more painful damage to themselves.

**2. The target is unknowable.** The strategy assumes that "what they want" is a stable, identifiable target that can be hit if the actor performs correctly. The story makes vivid how false this is. The interviewer's preferences are unknown to the interviewee. They may also be unknown to the interviewer (people often do not know what they want until they see it, and what they want depends on transient state variables like how much coffee they had). The 3D-chess image captures the absurdity: the strategy requires playing a game whose rules are not specified, against opponents whose preferences are not stable, with no way to verify that the chosen move is the right move.

**3. The strategy stacks two stresses on top of the original concern.** The original concern is "I want this job." The strategy adds: stress from self-rejection, stress from playing the unwinnable game. The strategy is not just failing to deliver the job; it is producing additional pain that would not be present if the actor simply went into the interview as themselves and let the interviewer respond to who they actually are.

The story is also a direct illustration of [[value-is-intrinsic-not-earned]] failing in real time. Joe was operating from the doing-to-be-valuable installation. The doing was the performance for the interviewer. The performance was supposed to produce the value (the job offer). The performance was not producing the value. The recognition that the strategy was structurally doomed had to come from inside the experience — which is what eventually allowed Joe to drop the strategy.

## The Coaching Move the Story Sets Up

The story makes available a specific coaching move when someone is preparing for an interview or similar approval-gated encounter:

"Before you go in there, notice: have you already decided that who you are isn't okay for this? If so, the rejection has already happened. They cannot reject you any worse than you have already rejected yourself."

The move surfaces the self-rejection that the strategy requires. Once seen, the actor can choose differently. The alternative is not "be cocky" or "be authentic as a strategy." The alternative is: go in as you are, let the interviewer respond to who you actually are, and let the result be the result. If you fit, you fit. If you do not fit, the interview has saved both of you time. The job that requires you to perform as someone else for the interview will require the performance throughout the role too. Getting it was not the win.

## What the Story Does Not Say

The story is not a claim that preparation is bad. Preparation is fine — knowing the company, knowing the role, having clear examples of your work. The story is about the difference between preparation (which is operating from "let me show them what I have") and management (which is operating from "let me be what they want"). The first lets you bring yourself; the second requires erasing yourself. The 3D-chess problem is specifically about the second.

The story is also not a claim that you will get every job by being yourself. You will not. But the jobs you get by being yourself will be jobs that want you, which is structurally different from jobs that want the performer you became to get them. The first set produces work that fits; the second set produces work that requires continued performance. See [[reputation-control-is-unwinnable]] for the related point about easy-to-maintain reputations.

## Variations

The story generalizes beyond job interviews. The same structure operates in:

- Dating: "what do they want me to be?" — the unwinnable game of being whoever the date is looking for
- Sales: "what do they want to buy?" — the unwinnable game of performing as the seller they will buy from
- Networking: "what kind of person do they want to know?" — the unwinnable game of being the right person in the room
- Pitching: "what investor do they want?" — the unwinnable game of being the founder the VC will fund
- Performance reviews: "what does my boss want to hear?" — the unwinnable game of producing the report that will get the rating

The story can be redeployed in any of these domains by substituting the context. The structural point — self-rejection first, unknowable target, compound stress — translates without modification.

## Tone

Joe tells this story conversationally, almost incidentally, as part of a larger teaching. The story is not labeled as a story; it is folded into the principle. The "early 30s, I was looking for a job" opener establishes that this is a personal experience. The shift to the structural insight ("but I started to notice that it hurt for two reasons") moves from narrative to teaching without ceremony.

The 3D-chess image is delivered with a slight wry energy. The absurdity of the situation is felt — Joe is not delivering this as solemn discovery. He is naming the absurdity of what he was doing then, in a way that lets the listener notice they may be doing the same thing now.
