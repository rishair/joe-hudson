# Jerry and the Lawyer Who Cried

## The Story

Jerry had been a VC for about 15 years. He was serving on too many boards. He recognized that he was addicted to the boards -- the addiction was being of service in a particular way, and underneath that, being needed.

A young lawyer came into his office for a networking conversation. The lawyer was networking his way toward a new job. The conversation was supposed to be transactional. Jerry asked him a question:

"Well, you seem miserable. Why did you become a lawyer in the first place?" -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The lawyer started to cry.

He started to tell Jerry about his father, and about pleasing his father, and about how the choice to become a lawyer had been the choice to please his father.

Jerry recognized something in real time. The thing the lawyer was describing -- the parental-pleasing pattern, the choosing of a career to meet someone else's need -- was something Jerry himself had been doing. The recognition was reciprocal. The lawyer's pattern was Jerry's pattern. The conversation in front of him was a mirror.

"I realized that that was something that I'd kind of grown up doing. It was at that moment that I realized that there was something about the alchemy of who I was and how I was that was repeating itself in a pattern. This is coaching before -- yeah." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## The Call That Landed

The recognition was the call. Not a thought about a possible career change. A felt knowing that the activity Jerry had just done -- asking the unexpected question, holding the space for the lawyer's tears, recognizing the pattern in himself in the same moment -- was the activity he was supposed to be doing. The conversation was not the side-thing during the networking meeting. The conversation was the actual thing.

The deliberation about whether to become a coach was effectively over before Jerry started consulting anyone. He picked up the phone and called Chris McConnell, who had been his coach when he was at JPMorgan.

"And I said, 'Chris, I think I want to be a coach.' And she said, 'Well, it's about damn time. I've been waiting for this phone call.'" -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The Chris-McConnell response is structurally important. Someone who knew Jerry well had already seen the call before Jerry had seen it. The "it's about damn time" is the outside acknowledgment of what was already structurally true. Jerry was not deciding to become a coach. Jerry was recognizing what he had already become and naming it out loud for the first time.

## The "I'll Coach on Wednesdays" Resistance

Jerry's initial framing of the transition was a hedge: he would keep being a VC and add coaching as a partial thing.

"I remember originally going, 'I'll coach on Wednesdays.' Yeah, yeah, yeah. That didn't work for long." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The hedge did not survive because the call was not the kind of thing that fits into a Wednesday. The call was the structural recognition of who Jerry was. Trying to fit it into a slot in the existing schedule was an attempt to make the call accommodate the existing identity. The existing identity could not accommodate the call. Something had to give. The Wednesday-coaching frame eventually dissolved into a full transition.

This is consistent with the broader principle in [[choicelessness-as-purpose-marker]]: the call is not optional, and the hedges that try to make it optional do not hold over time. Jerry's hedge held for a while because hedges always hold for a while. The hedge eventually failed because the call's pull was structurally greater than the hedge's accommodation.

## What This Story Illustrates

1. **The call can land in an unexpected moment.** Jerry was not looking for his call. He was in a networking meeting. The call landed in the middle of asking an unexpected question that turned out to not be a networking question. The call is structurally not in the calendar. It arrives when it arrives.

2. **The call is often a reciprocal recognition.** The lawyer's pattern was Jerry's pattern. The act of seeing the pattern in the lawyer was the act of seeing it in himself. The coaching moment and the self-recognition moment were the same moment.

3. **The lawyer-pleasing-his-father pattern is one of the most common patterns underneath career choices.** The recognition that the career was chosen for someone else's reasons is a common entry to the deeper work. See [[performing-for-love-becomes-savior-career]] for the broader version.

4. **Someone outside the person often sees the call before the person does.** Chris McConnell had been waiting for the phone call. The call was already structurally present before Jerry had named it. The outside witness is the verification. See [[the-retreat-woman-no-choice]] for the parallel story of Joe being seen by the retreat woman.

5. **The hedge ("I'll coach on Wednesdays") does not survive over time.** The call requires the full transition eventually. The hedge buys time. It does not become the actual stance.

6. **Coaching can happen before someone is officially a coach.** The lawyer received what was structurally a coaching session in a networking meeting. The frame of the meeting did not match what was happening. The not-yet-coach was already coaching. The official identity of "coach" was the catching up to what was already happening.

## The Underlying Recognition About the Self

The story carries a specific recognition that anchors the deeper principle. Jerry was a VC who had been on too many boards. He was addicted to that. The addiction was specifically to being of service in a particular way, and underneath that, being needed.

"And being of service in a different way." -- Jerry Colonna, naming what the addiction was

"Being of service. Being needed." -- Joe Hudson, completing the framing

The recognition was that the existing role was meeting an underlying need (being needed) in a way that was depleting. The new role would meet a structurally different orientation: being of service from the call rather than from the addiction. The shift from VC to coach was not a career change in the conventional sense. It was the resolution of an addiction that the existing role had been feeding.

This is why Jerry's story belongs in the same conversation as [[dont-coach-if-you-have-a-choice]]. The hedge of "I'll coach on Wednesdays while still doing VC" was effectively the addiction trying to negotiate with the call. The call won. The addiction did not.

## Tone

Jerry tells this story with warmth and self-recognition. The "I'll coach on Wednesdays" line is delivered with the gentle amusement of someone looking back at his earlier hedge with affection. The Chris-McConnell "it's about damn time" is delivered with the smile of someone who, in retrospect, can see that the outside witness had known what the inside was not yet ready to see. The story is not a triumph narrative. It is a recognition narrative -- the structure was already there; the seeing of the structure was the move.
