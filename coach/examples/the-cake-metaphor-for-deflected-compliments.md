# The Cake Metaphor for Deflected Compliments

## The Story

A coach is working with someone who cannot let compliments land. The coach offers a metaphor:

"If I was to show up and I'm like, here's this beautiful cake I baked you, and you went, mm-hmm, would I bring you — would I bring you another fucking cake?" — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The image: someone bakes you a cake, brings it to you, presents it, and you respond with a flat mm-hmm. The next question is whether they would bring you another cake. The answer is obvious. They would not.

The cake is the compliment. The mm-hmm is the deflection. The not-bringing-another-cake is the cost.

## The Extended Form

Later in the same session, Joe extends the metaphor when the client says they want to be appreciated but cannot receive appreciation:

"You're, you're, you're tossing their cake on the ground, and then you're like, why aren't people bringing me cake?" — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The extension is the diagnosis of the loop: the deflector throws the cake on the ground, then experiences themselves as not getting cake, then feels unappreciated, then deflects the next cake. The cake-throwing is what produces the cake-shortage. The deflector is the cause of the absence they are complaining about.

The instruction that follows:

"So why don't you practice letting it in? ... Stop, stop, stop. Yeah. Stop flipping the cake over." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The instruction is precise. Not "say thank you better." Not "be more open to compliments." *Stop flipping the cake over.* The cake-flipping is the move that has to stop. The receiving is what happens once the flipping stops.

## Why The Metaphor Works

Compliments are usually discussed in abstract terms: receiving, validation, recognition, approval. The abstraction makes the cost of deflection invisible. "I deflected the compliment" sounds like a small internal event with no consequences.

The cake metaphor makes the cost concrete. A cake is real. A baked cake represents time, attention, care, ingredients. Throwing it on the ground is a visible act with a visible consequence. The baker sees the cake hit the floor. The baker feels the rejection. The baker decides whether to bring another cake.

The cake also has a recipient-perspective insight. The deflector who feels they are not getting cake gets to see, in the metaphor, that they are the one throwing the cakes on the ground. The complaint about not getting cake and the action of throwing the cake away are the same person's behavior in the same room. The structure becomes inescapable.

## The Specific Lever

The metaphor's specific lever is the simple economics of repeated giving. Most people who deflect compliments have not consciously thought about whether the compliment-giver will keep giving compliments. The deflector assumes there is an inexhaustible supply.

The cake question makes the supply finite: "would I bring you another fucking cake?" The asker is the prospective baker. The asker is naming that they would stop. The deflector now has to reckon with the fact that their deflection-move is what is producing the long-term shortage of recognition they keep complaining about.

This is the same teaching as [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]] from the giver-side, and the same teaching as [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]] from the cost-to-the-giver side. The cake metaphor compresses all of it into a single image that lands faster than the explanations.

## How Joe Uses It

In the source session, the metaphor arrives at the moment Joe needs to:

1. Make the cost of deflection concrete
2. Bridge from the do-the-deflection-back demonstration (see [[do-the-deflection-back-to-show-them-how-they-do-it]]) to the body-level instruction (the lips-tightening observation)
3. Give the client a single image to take with them out of the session

The placement matters. The metaphor lands after the client has already seen the deflection (via the do-the-deflection-back move) and is open to understanding what the deflection is costing. Used too early, before the client can see the deflection, the metaphor would feel preachy. Used at this point, it lands as an observation of what the client just saw.

The metaphor also returns later in the session as the instruction: "stop flipping the cake over." The metaphor has become a shorthand the coach and client now share. The instruction is short because the metaphor did the long-form work earlier.

## What This Example Illustrates

- The cost of compliment-deflection is structural, not just internal
- The deflector is producing the recognition-shortage they are complaining about
- A concrete metaphor can land where abstract teaching bounces
- The same metaphor can function as cost-explanation and as in-session instruction
- The metaphor has a "would I bring you another" question that makes the giver-side perspective vivid

## When Joe Uses This Example

The setup:

- The client is deflecting compliments and cannot fully feel the cost of the deflection
- Verbal naming of the deflection has surfaced the move but not the consequences
- The work needs a concrete image to make the abstract cost visible
- The session has time for the image to be set up and extended

The metaphor is also useful as a take-away. Clients remember the cake longer than they remember explanations of the receiving-channel. The instruction "stop flipping the cake over" is easier to recall mid-life than "stop deflecting compliments." The image does the work between sessions.

## Tone

Concrete, slightly playful, with the occasional curse word. Joe's "would I bring you another fucking cake?" is delivered with weight on "another" and on "fucking" — the cursing marks the absurdity of expecting the baker to keep baking after the first cake has been thrown on the floor. The lightness is what allows the cost to land without shame.

The follow-up "you're tossing their cake on the ground, and then you're like, why aren't people bringing me cake?" is delivered with the same energy — naming the contradiction directly while keeping the tone observational rather than accusatory.

The instruction form "stop flipping the cake over" is delivered with mock-exasperation, almost like a parent telling a child to stop a familiar behavior. The mock-exasperation is part of what makes the instruction memorable.

## What This Example Is Not

- **It is not a guilt trip.** The metaphor is not "look what you've done to your loved ones." It is an illustration of the structural consequence of the deflection-move.
- **It is not a shaming device.** The cake-on-the-ground image, used wrongly, could install shame about previous deflections. Used right, it installs awareness of the move so future deflections can be interrupted.
- **It is not for every client.** Highly self-critical clients may need the metaphor delivered with extra care to avoid weaponizing it against themselves.
- **It is not a substitute for the body-level receiving work.** The metaphor makes the cost visible. The receiving still has to happen. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]].
