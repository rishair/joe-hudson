# Two Cups: Milk Into Water

## The Story

Dr. K tells the story in conversation with Joe. The version goes:

A seeker comes to the Buddha. He says: I have studied with a bunch of teachers. I am not enlightened yet. Help me out. The Buddha agrees. The Buddha starts teaching him things. The seeker keeps interrupting: but hold on, the other teacher said this. The Buddha keeps teaching. The seeker keeps interrupting: but the other teacher said this.

After a while, the Buddha tells him: come back tomorrow, and bring two cups. Bring a cup of water and bring a cup of milk. Both full.

The next day, the seeker brings the cups. The Buddha says: take the cup of milk, pour it into the cup of water.

The seeker, confused, does it. The milk overflows.

The point: if you want to learn something, you need to empty your mind first.

"If you want to sit with someone, you have to empty yourself." -- Alok Kanojia (Dr. K), Charisma isn't learned (presenting the story; Joe affirms throughout)

## What This Illustrates

The story is about the prerequisite for receiving anything. The two cups stand for two minds (the seeker's and the teacher's, or in coaching, the client's and the coach's). When the cup is full of the seeker's existing ideas, no new ideas can land. The teaching tries to pour in; what arrives just spills over the edges.

The illustration generalizes immediately to coaching, parenting, intimate conversation, conflict, listening. Anywhere one person wants to receive what is in another person, the prerequisite is the same: the receiver has to empty themselves enough for what the other is bringing to actually land.

Dr. K uses the story specifically to describe what Joe does when he sits with someone in coaching. The capacity Joe has to attune to a client -- to feel what they are feeling, to know what is in their body -- is downstream of the emptying. Joe is not full of his own material when he sits with someone. He has emptied. The client's material can therefore enter him. From the emptied position, he can attend to what is happening in the client.

"When Joe says he attends to people, the key thing there is that he is empty. So what the other person is enters him and then he attends to himself. But it's not what he feels. He is an instrument. He's like a radio wave. He's like a radio antenna picking up other things." -- Alok Kanojia, Charisma isn't learned

## When Joe Reaches For This Story (or the Underlying Move)

Joe does not himself tell the two-cups story in this transcript -- Dr. K does, in characterizing Joe's work. But the underlying move is constantly operating in Joe's coaching. When Joe describes what he is doing when he sits with someone:

"There's just, there's not a lot of I in the equation. So oftentimes at the end of it, I don't remember what happened. I don't know if it went well and I don't care. It's like none of that." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

This is the emptied-cup state in operational terms. The I is not in the equation. The remembering is not happening because the remembering self is not the one doing the work. The capacity to fully attend to the client requires the I to be absent. The two-cups story is the parable form of the same instruction.

## Why It Is Hard to Do with Familiar People

Dr. K names the operational difficulty in the same conversation: he can do this with random strangers; he can never do it with his wife. The moment he sits with his wife and she tells him how annoying he is, he is no longer empty. He is full of his own material, his history with her, his defense, his desire to be right.

"This is also why it's easier to talk to strangers and listen to strangers, because there's no ego, there's no relationship, right? You can talk to a stranger on a park bench, pour out your heart and soul, and it is easy to do because you -- there's an emptiness to it. There's no ego, there's no attachment." -- Alok Kanojia, Charisma isn't learned

The cup is fullest with the people closest to us. This is why the same coach who is profoundly attuned with clients cannot apply the same attunement at home. The cup is overflowing with the history. The work of emptying is harder where there is more to empty.

This is also why the work of relational practice (see [[expanding-sense-of-self]] for VIEW as relational meditation) is so much harder and so much more revealing than the work of practice in isolation. The isolation cup is easier to empty. The relational cup -- especially with intimates -- is the actual test.

## How This Differs From Suppression

A trap: making the cup empty by suppressing one's own material. This is not the move. Suppression produces a cup that looks empty but is actually pressurized. What enters has to compete with the pressurized material underneath. The attunement does not happen because the cup is not actually empty; it is just locked.

The actual move is more like the work that has already been done before the conversation began. The cup is not emptied at the moment of sitting with someone. The cup has been emptied through the long arc of inner work that has reduced what is loaded into it in the first place. The person who has fewer attachments, fewer unprocessed emotions, less defended identity walks into the conversation with less in the cup. That is what allows them to attend without effort.

This connects to [[dont-chase-spiritual-powers]]: the capacity to be empty enough to attend deeply to another person is a byproduct of inner work, not a technique to be deployed. The technique-level instruction ("empty your cup") fails when applied without the underlying work. The story is descriptive of what becomes possible after the work, not a shortcut around it.

## The Operational Use for the AI Coach

The story is reachable as a brief teaching when the situation calls for it. Specific moments:

- When someone wants to learn but keeps comparing every new teaching to what they already know.
- When someone wants to listen better and is approaching it as a technique to add rather than as a clearing-out.
- When someone is full of advice from many teachers and is bewildered about which to follow.
- When someone is asking how to be more present in conversation and listing techniques to perform presence.
- When the principle of emptying as the prerequisite to receiving needs a concrete image to carry it.

The image carries something words cannot. The visceral picture of milk overflowing from the cup of water lands the teaching in a way the abstract instruction "empty yourself" does not.
