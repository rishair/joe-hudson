# Joe's VC Realization (1/10 of the Work, Same Result)

## The Story

In the early part of his career, Joe was a venture capitalist. He noticed two patterns in himself: when he told himself to do X, Y, and Z, he procrastinated and didn't do them, or did them at the last minute. When he was doing something deeply aligned with what he wanted, there was no procrastination -- just doing, with an addictive quality to the doing itself.

Looking back at that period, the recognition that hit him: almost all of the work was unnecessary. He could have done one-tenth of what he did and gotten the exact same result.

"If I look back on that time in my life, almost all of it was unnecessary. Like, I could have literally done 1/10 of what I did when I was a venture capitalist to get the exact same result. And that recognition was huge for me. Oh, whoa." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

## What the Example Illustrates

This is the formative insight that produced much of Joe's later productivity teaching. Several principles are encoded in the story:

1. **[[spin-vs-pedaling]]:** the 90% he didn't need to do was pure spin. The remaining 10% was the actual pedaling that moved the bike. He couldn't see this in the moment because the spin felt like work.

2. **The wants-vs-shoulds observation.** Should-work produced procrastination. Want-work produced addiction-quality engagement. The body's response to the work is a reliable diagnostic for whether the work is aligned. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] and [[enjoyment-as-path]].

3. **[[productivity-is-the-trees-purpose-is-the-forest]]:** the 90% he didn't need to do was probably aimed at the wrong forest. The 10% that mattered was aimed at the real one. The reduction wasn't about working less; it was about working on what was actually aimed at the goal.

4. **[[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]]:** the realization is the upstream insight that produced this practice. Three things on the to-do list make most of the others unnecessary; the rest was the 90% Joe later identified.

## The Two Observations Joe Names

Joe identifies two specific patterns from the period:

**First: the procrastination signature.** "I told myself to do X, Y, and Z, and then I would procrastinate and not do those things, or do them at the last minute." -- Joe Hudson

Procrastination was the body's report on should-work. The work was assigned to himself; the body resisted. The resistance was data. He was trying to do work that was not aligned, and the body was registering the misalignment as resistance.

**Second: the addiction-quality signature.** "When I found that I was doing something that was really deeply aligned with what I wanted, there was no procrastination. There was just doing, and there was an addiction to that doing." -- Joe Hudson

Aligned work produced the opposite somatic signature. No resistance. A pull toward the doing. The body wanted to continue.

Together, the two observations form a working diagnostic. The body's response to a task is a more reliable indicator of whether the task is aligned than any verbal reasoning about it. The reasoning can justify any task as necessary. The body cannot be fooled the same way.

## The Recognition That Produced the Insight

The insight wasn't "I should have worked less." It was structural: most of the work he was doing was the wrong work, and identifying the right 10% was the actual job. The wrong 90% was driven by:

- Shoulds rather than wants.
- The cultural assumption that more activity = more value.
- The dopamine-completion pattern.
- Fear-based productivity aimed at undeclared safety.

The 10% that was actually productive was the work that pointed at the forest, that was aligned with what he wanted, that the body engaged with rather than resisted.

This is the same shape as [[doing-doesnt-equal-producing]]. Most of the doing was channel-throughput, residue, or misaligned should-work. The actual production happened in a much smaller fraction of the time.

## How Joe Uses This in Teaching

When someone is in fear that they can't do less because the work requires what they're doing, Joe can offer this as the personal-experience counter-example. He has done it. The 90% wasn't required. The recognition came in retrospect, not in the moment, and that retrospective recognition is what made the later practices (the three-things, the spin-check, the reflection-after-an-hour) possible.

The example is also useful for the inverse case: someone working enormous hours who believes they are being productive. The example shows that the same Joe who could have worked one-tenth as much was, in the moment, convinced he was working at the right level. The conviction in the moment is not reliable evidence. The retrospective view is more honest. The practices exist to produce the retrospective view in close-to-real-time, so the cost is paid in days rather than years.

## The Modern Analogue

Joe pairs this story with the contrast of his current work mode:

"I can be that productive. Last week I worked from -- you and I both worked doing a week-long -- we worked from 7:30 in the morning till 10, 10:30 at night average, sometimes longer, sometimes a little shorter, for 7 days straight. And this week I'll be doing 4 podcasts, 1 live performance running the business, seeing 8 clients, and still making time for my family, and still making sure that my girls are attended to, and that my marriage is healthy. And that happens." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The story is not "work less." It is "make the work the right work, and the energy holds." Joe currently works enormous hours. The difference from the VC period is not the volume; it is the alignment, the body-presence, the absence of inner-fight, the right pace. The 90% that he could have cut from the VC period was the misaligned and the fear-driven. The current 100% is mostly aligned, so the body can sustain it.

## When to Use This Example

- When a person is in [[i-keep-getting-stuff-done-but-nothing-changes]] and is convinced they cannot do less.
- When [[productivity-is-the-trees-purpose-is-the-forest]] is being introduced and the person needs an anchor for what the reduction looks like in practice.
- When someone is working from a should-engine and reports procrastination -- the example normalizes the procrastination as data rather than as a failing.
- When someone says "I work hard because that's what success requires" -- the example demonstrates that success in fact required a fraction of the work, and the rest was the unexamined assumption.

## What the Example Does Not Illustrate

The example does not illustrate that all work can be done in 1/10 of the time. The 10x reduction is specific to the VC context, where Joe was running the wrong engine on top of misaligned work. Different contexts will have different ratios. The principle is that retrospective examination usually reveals that some non-trivial fraction was unnecessary; the exact fraction varies.
