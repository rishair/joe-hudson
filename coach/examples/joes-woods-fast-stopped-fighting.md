# Joe's Woods Fast: The Moment He Stopped Fighting Himself

## The Story

Joe is 24 years old. He goes out into the woods to fast. He is looking over a ridgeline. He notices something happening in himself: he is fighting himself. Then he notices that he is fighting himself to stop fighting himself. Then he notices he is fighting himself to stop fighting himself to stop fighting himself. The regress goes on.

He stops.

"I was just like, yeah, this isn't gonna work. So I just stopped. And that was the first moment that I realized that it's not a matter of effort. It's a matter of acceptance. It's a matter of not efforting. It's the stopping of the trying. It is the trust." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

## What This Illustrates

This is the origin moment for what later becomes [[undoing-not-doing]] in Joe's framework. At 24, alone on a ridgeline, fasting, he runs the recursion of resistance all the way down -- and discovers that no level of effort exits effort. Every layer of fighting-against produces another layer to fight against. The only way out is to stop.

The realization is structural. It is not "I should be nicer to myself." It is not "I need a better technique to stop fighting myself." It is: the entire category of effort cannot solve this problem. Something other than effort is required. He names it as acceptance, as non-efforting, as the stopping of the trying, and finally as trust.

This is also the canonical illustration of [[layered-resistance]]: each new resistance to a resistance just adds another layer. Joe lived the recursion in real time and saw that it has no terminus inside effort. The only terminus is outside the category.

## The Quote on the Recursion

"I was noticing that I was fighting myself and then I was noticing that I was fighting myself to stop fighting myself. And then I was noticing I was fighting myself to stop fighting myself to stop fighting myself." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

The phrasing is precise. He does not say he was thinking about it. He was noticing it. The fight was happening at a level below thought and the awareness of it kept catching up.

## Why a Fast in the Woods

The setting matters. A fast in the woods strips away the usual ways the system distracts itself from its own activity. There is no work to do, no people to manage, no food to prepare. What remains is the activity of the mind, made visible because nothing else is happening. This is why insights of this kind frequently arrive in retreat-like conditions: the substrate the system normally rides on has been removed.

It also matters that Joe was 24. He had not yet built the conceptual frame he would later teach. He was not running a known technique. The realization arrived as a discovery, not as the application of a method he had been taught. This is part of why he can teach it with such conviction later: he found it before he had words for it.

## The Move: Just Stop

The exit is one move: stop. Not a sophisticated move. Not a sequence. Not a practice. Just stop trying.

"You just have to just stop. It's the only way." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

This is what makes [[trying-vs-doing]] possible to describe. There is a difference between trying and doing, and the difference becomes visible at the moment of stopping. What remains when the trying stops is the doing -- or the not-doing -- that was underneath.

The Hands Together exercise ([[hands-together-exercise]]) is a somatic version of the same insight: feel the difference between pressing palms together (trying) and letting them rest (undoing). The ridgeline moment is the felt-life version of the same teaching.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

When a person is in an effort loop trying to use willpower to stop using willpower. When the resistance to the resistance is now the dominant problem. When someone is asking "how do I stop fighting myself?" and the asking is itself another fight.

The story does the work the instruction cannot. The instruction is "just stop." But the instruction itself becomes another thing to try to do. The story slips past the trying because it is just a story about a 24-year-old on a hill who got tired of the regress.
