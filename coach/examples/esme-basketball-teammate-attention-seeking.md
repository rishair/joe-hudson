# Esme and the Basketball Teammate

## The Story

Esme was on a basketball team with a younger teammate (about three years younger). The teammate's behavior was difficult — constantly seeking attention, in ways that frustrated and distracted the rest of the team. Esme found her annoying. The behavior had set the teammate up as the team's outcast: the other players did not like her, she was being ostracized within the group.

Then one day the teammate asked Esme for a ride home. On the drive, the teammate started talking about her home life.

"She said it really clearly. She's like, 'I, I never feel like they pay me any attention.' I was like, oh, she's not feeling loved or cared for or feeling like her parents like have any time to give her attention, so she's looking for it in every other place and just continuing that pattern. It made me really, really sad." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The teammate had named her own situation cleanly. She did not feel her parents paid her any attention. The seeking-behavior at school and on the team was the same need showing up where she could get it met (or try to). The teammate was not strategically attention-seeking. She was a kid who had not been seen, looking for being-seen in the only places available.

## What Shifted for Esme

The information changed Esme's relationship to the teammate. Not the behavior — the behavior was still partially annoying. The frame shifted. Underneath the frustration was now a felt understanding of where the behavior came from.

"It just kind of turned into compassion. I mean, parts of her still bothered the fuck out of me. But under the frustration that I had towards her was like more of a deeper understanding of where that came from." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

Esme then took on a specific intention: to be one of the people who showed the teammate she was cared about. Not as parent-replacement. As a teammate who would offer the seeing-and-care the teammate was missing at home.

"I was like, oh, like I can't obviously be her parent by any means, but I can show her that she's loved and that she's cared for. Cuz the pattern that she's working out in with our team and everything makes her the outcast and ostracized and people don't like her and everything." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

## The Last Game

The teammate's response to Esme's small consistent offer of care arrived at the end of the season:

"We just had our last game and she was sobbing and gave me like the biggest hug and she's like, 'thank you for for making me feel like a part of this team.' And I didn't even feel like a part of the team necessarily, but it's just, no, you did. It just is such a, like, I don't know. It was really sweet." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The teammate had received what Esme had been offering and named it back to her. The teammate's experience of belonging on the team did not require the team itself to change. It required one person to consistently see and care for her. That one person, applied steadily across a season, was enough.

## What This Example Demonstrates

**1. The annoying behavior is the surface of a deeper unmet need.** The teammate was not a kid being annoying for the sake of it. She was a kid trying to get a need met where the need was supposed to be met (at home) and had not been. The behavior at school was the displaced version of the original missing thing.

This is the operational truth behind [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]]. A kid who does not feel liked at home will pursue feeling liked elsewhere, often through behaviors that make people not like them. The seeking creates the rejection it is trying to avoid. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the mechanism.

**2. The frustration the teammate produced was readable as her unmet need.** When Esme was frustrated by the teammate, the frustration itself was information. The behavior was producing rejection from everyone. The behavior was so consistent across contexts that it had to be running on something deep. The information from the ride home confirmed what the structural reading would have suggested. The behavior was a leak from somewhere the parent should have been but was not.

This is similar in structure to [[read-anger-and-help-as-fear]] — surface behaviors that produce reaction usually reveal underlying fear or unmet need. The teammate's attention-seeking is fear-of-not-mattering showing up sideways.

**3. The intervention did not require fixing the home situation.** Esme could not be the teammate's parent. The home situation was not her to repair. What she could do was offer, week after week, what the teammate was missing — being seen as a person worth seeing, being treated like she belonged. The cumulative effect across a season was enough to register as transformative for the teammate.

This is the practical demonstration of [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] applied at the small scale. The teammate did not need a therapist or a different family. She needed an ongoing relational experience of being valued. Esme provided it within the bounds of what she could offer.

**4. The Lowell-Tuttle pattern applied teen-to-teen.** Joe describes [[joe-and-lowell-tuttle]] as the experience that taught him how to see his daughters fully. Esme then applies the same pattern to her teammate — sees her more fully than her own family has, and the teammate shows up differently in response. The pattern is generational: Lowell saw Joe, Joe sees his daughters, and his daughters carry the move out into their own relationships.

## Why This Example Sits in the Parenting Domain

The example is not just about Esme being a good teammate. It is a worked example of how the absence of liking at home shows up in the kid's life elsewhere, and what it looks like for someone outside the home to provide a partial corrective.

For a parent in coaching, the example is a mirror. A parent whose kid is reported as annoying, attention-seeking, ostracized by their peers should not ask "what is wrong with my kid socially?" The first question is: does my kid feel that I pay them attention? Do they feel I like them? If the answer is unclear or no, the kid's social behavior is downstream of a felt sense of not-mattering at home. The work is not on the kid's social skills. The work is on the felt sense at home.

For a parent of a kid who is the Esme in the situation — a kid who is starting to recognize that some of her peers are not being met at home — the example also names the legitimate ground of the kid's compassion. The teen who can recognize the absence in another kid is often the teen who knows what presence feels like at home. Esme names the gratitude she felt:

"And like extraordinarily grateful at the same time of like, oh, I get to go to school and not need to seek the attention or the approval of the people around me cuz I feel secure and I, I like, I get what I need at home, you know?" -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The felt sense of being met at home produces both the security that protects the kid from chronic external-seeking and the capacity to recognize the pattern when it shows up in others.

## What This Reveals About Joe's Reading of the Field

Joe asks Esme, after she tells the story, how she can tell which kids are coming from home environments where the parents are fighting or distant. She answers: "It's incredibly clear." The kid's behavior at school registers as a reliable read of the home situation. The body language, the attention-seeking, the patterns of how the kid interacts — all of these are downstream of conditions the kid has been operating in.

This is consistent with [[criticism-posture-vs-shame-posture]]: the kid carries the home environment in their body and their behavior. A trained reader can see the home in the kid even before any of the home is described.

For a coach or therapist working with kids, the example normalizes this read. The kid in front of you is telling you about their home life through their behavior. The verbal report from the kid is a confirmation of what was already legible.

## When to Reach for This Example

- A parent describes their kid being ostracized at school or constantly seeking attention from peers
- A parent of a teen who is "annoying" their friends and the parent is wondering what is happening
- A coachee describing the kid version of themselves who was the difficult one
- A discussion of [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]] needs a teen-side example of what not-being-liked produces
- A coachee working with adolescents who is learning to read the home environment off the kid
- A discussion of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] applied to the social rejection produced by attention-seeking behavior

## Tone

Esme tells this story with genuine emotion — sadness for the teammate's situation, gratitude for her own. The story should be carried with both halves. The teammate's situation is sad. Esme's response is what a kid raised with seeing-and-care does, applied outward. The example does not romanticize Esme. It names the structural fact that being-met-at-home produces the capacity to meet others, and being-not-met-at-home produces the seeking-elsewhere pattern.
