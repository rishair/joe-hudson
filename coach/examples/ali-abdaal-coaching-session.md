# Ali Abdaal: 'I Hit the Bestseller List, Now What?'

## The Setup

Ali Abdaal -- doctor turned entrepreneur, 5+ million YouTube subscribers, author of *Feel Good Productivity* -- came to Joe one week after his book hit the New York Times bestseller list. The session opened with what Ali named as two topics: "what do I want to do with my life now?" and "I really struggle to feel my own emotions. When people ask me how I feel, that phrase does not even vaguely compute."

The session is a useful single-source example because (1) the same coaching move (apply connection to whatever you're doing) is repeated across multiple domains in sustained use, (2) the through-line of connection emerges visibly as the answer to both stated questions, (3) several reads and moves Joe uses with head-dominant high-achievers all appear in compressed form, and (4) Joe explicitly names the [[the-first-thing-they-say-is-the-kernel]] principle at the end.

## The Opening (Where the Kernel Was Set)

Ali's opening, after Joe asked what he wanted to address: "I just hit the New York Times bestseller list, and now I'm thinking, what do I want to do next?" Then, as the second topic: "I really struggle to feel my own emotions."

Both topics turn out to be the same topic. The head-dominant person whose long chase has just ended is also the person who has not been able to feel his emotions throughout the chase. The body-disconnect that allowed the chase to be sustained is now the gap that prevents the new chapter from being designed.

Joe did not collapse the two topics in the opening. He worked on the first one first. The work on the first one produced the bridge into the second one.

## The Opening Read: Always Knew What to Do Next

Joe's first move was to reflect what was structurally different about this moment.

"What strikes me about you is that you've probably always known what to do next. As far as, like, I can't imagine that there's been, like, a month of you really trying to figure out what you're going to do next instead of, like, you're actually doing something next."

Ali confirmed: from high school to med school to YouTube to the book, every next step had been on a conveyor belt. The current moment was the first time in his adult life that the conveyor belt had ended. The rules of the game had changed.

The reflection located what was actually happening: not "I am lost" but "the system that organized my next-step decisions has just completed its program for the first time in twenty years."

## The Money Fear Trace

Joe asked: "What, if anything, would stop you from living life on your own terms at this point?"

Ali stopped, closed his eyes, went inward, and answered: "I have these weird fears around money."

Joe immediately paused the content and went to the process. He asked Ali to describe his internal process during the pause. Ali's answer: zero somatic, 100% intellectual.

This was the moment Joe introduced [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]] and the small experiment of moving the chin up and down to test for somatic access. With chin down, the feeling was accessible. With chin up, it disappeared. Ali had been operating with his chin up.

Joe then traced the money fear to its origin. "If that fear existed in your system before you knew what money was, what would that fear be called?"

Ali's answer: "Some sort of fear of not being safe."

Joe followed with: "I want you to take a moment. Have the experience of lack of safety around money. Now, without a single thought whatsoever, trace that to the very first time you felt that particular set of sensations."

The trace did not surface a specific memory in the moment, but it surfaced something more useful: the sensation of trying to feel and not getting there. Joe redirected attention to that sensation as the first feelable thing. The constriction Ali located was just above the belly button, on the left rib side.

See [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]] for the practice and [[childhood-imprinting]] for the underlying mechanism.

## The Move That Did the Work: Apply Connection to Everything

Joe asked Ali to name his other wants. Ali listed: best shape of his life, hosting six mini-adventures with friends and family, in-person teaching, building a community, doing live coaching content, more in-person time with his team.

Joe's noticing landed in one sentence: "What I notice is everything that -- all of your goals are about connection. Connection to yourself or connection to others."

Ali had not seen this. The naming was the move. Once the through-line was visible, the design question changed. The question was no longer "what should I do next?" but "how do I do what I am already doing more connectedly?"

Joe then ran [[apply-connection-to-whatever-youre-doing]] in sustained form across four successively more mundane parts of Ali's business:

1. **The team work.** What would it look like to be more connected with the team? (Answer: quarterly in-person reset weeks, which Ali was already doing.)
2. **The teaching.** What would more connected teaching look like? (Answer: in-person seminars, content from what was alive for Ali rather than what served the algorithm.)
3. **The videos.** What would more connected video work look like? (Answer: building a community around the work, doing live coaching content, making videos he wanted to make.)
4. **Sponsorship segments.** What would more connected sponsorship work look like? (Answer: only working with brands he loved, reaching out to companies whose products he used, turning sponsorship segments into songs or poems.)

Each application produced multiple specific options within seconds. The pattern made the principle visible: the same frame applied to any task produced creative options the old frame did not. By the fourth task Ali said: "I think even just running it through that filter of like, what does 10% more connected look like -- I've got the answers."

## The 10% More Connected Experiment

Joe installed the daily practice form: be 10% more connected in everything you do. He named the Yap app as a possible reminder mechanism -- an app that produces a small sound at random intervals to interrupt the day and return attention to a question.

The instruction attached to the reminder: "How do I be 10% more connected right now?"

He paired this with the trust-the-body-to-integrate instruction. "If you actually allow it to hit your body and resonate in your body, oftentimes that works better than a list because you write it on a list. But do you remember to read the list and whatever happens next?"

See [[ten-percent-more-connected-experiment]] for the full practice.

## The Pivot to Emotional Access

After the connection work landed, Joe pivoted to the second topic: the inability to feel emotions. Ali had reported earlier that he had access to positive emotions like excitement but not to love or sorrow.

Joe set up the live contrast experiment. Bring up a memory that brings up love. Then a memory that brings up sorrow. Cycle between the two. Then hold both at once.

Ali located sorrow in a squeezy sensation just below the left ribcage. The love feeling was harder to locate at first, but with the contrast pass it emerged as an expansion outward from the lower ribs.

Joe used the moment to introduce two key teachings:

1. The story that led to the cut-off. Ali described the unrequited love in his first year of med school as the formative sorrow he had bypassed. Joe noted: "Somewhere in your college experience, you've made the choice to have success over connection. And now here you are, you've got your success and you're like, oh wait, I also want the connection piece."

2. The distinction between emotion-with-story and emotion-without-story. The story is not required for the emotion. Mammals without complex thought have emotions. The emotion can move without a meaning, and trying to find the meaning before allowing the emotion is what stagnates the emotion. See [[emotion-can-move-without-a-story]] and [[feeling-changes-story]].

## The Kernel Surfaces: "More to Be Done" as the Engine

As the conversation progressed, Ali named what was underneath both topics: "I feel like I have this constant sense of there is more that needs to be done."

This was the actual kernel. The post-achievement what's-next, the inability to feel, the money fear, the success-over-connection choice in college -- all of them ran on the same engine. The engine was the parental imprint that there was always more to do. Ali named the imprint directly: he and his brother could never let their mom see them playing video games because she would find a chore.

Joe asked what celebrating his New York Times bestseller win would look like in the most connected fashion possible. Ali's answer: sending thank-you messages and gifts to everyone who helped, and chilling out for a month with a fantasy fiction series.

At the description, Ali's eyes watered. Joe named it: "That's called gratitude."

Joe followed with the diagnostic question: if Ali actually did that -- the gratitude practice and the deep rest -- would he be further ahead or further behind two years from now? Ali's answer: "Definitely further ahead."

The exchange surfaced [[obligation-cuts-off-love]] in practice. The "more to be done" engine had been cutting off the felt experience of gratitude and rest. The cutting-off was the cost of the engine, not its benefit.

## The Closing Naming of the Arc

At the end of the session Joe explicitly named the kernel principle.

"I always know where the thing ends. For anybody listening to this, just like a quick little thing. I don't even know if we captured it in the recording, but the very first thing you said was, 'I just finished the New York Times bestseller list, and now I'm thinking, what do I want to do next?' And so the very first thing you said was the kernel. Always, almost always is the kernel of what is being worked on. Just, it's just a fascinating thing."

He added: "It's like people, it -- I'm following you. You bring that up and I'm like, oh, that's what we're here for. Right? Like, so that you, you know it, I don't know it."

See [[the-first-thing-they-say-is-the-kernel]] for the principle.

## What This Example Illustrates

- [[apply-connection-to-whatever-youre-doing]] applied across multiple successive tasks in a single session, showing that the move is portable.
- [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]] used live, with the immediate experiment of moving the chin to test somatic access.
- [[intellectual-process-with-no-somatic-component]] named directly, with the diagnostic question that distinguishes head-pause from body-pause.
- [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]] used in a head-dominant person whose trace produced not a memory but the sensation of trying-and-not-getting-there.
- The live contrast experiment for accessing differential emotions (love vs sorrow).

- The way the two stated topics turn out to be the same topic, worked from two angles.
- [[connection]] as the through-line that emerges visibly when wants are listed and the pattern is named.
- The 10% more connected experiment installed as a daily practice.

## Why Joe Could Move This Fast

A note on what made this session move at the pace it did. Ali came in with several things already in place:

- He had read or listened to enough of Joe's work to recognize the territory.
- He had already noticed the inability to feel emotions and named it as something he wanted to address.
- He was sincere -- he was not performing on a podcast. He was actually using the session to figure something out.
- He had a head-dominant nervous system that responded well to the intellectual frame followed by the somatic experiment.

These conditions are not always present. With a person who has less framework, less prior noticing, less sincerity, or a different nervous-system type, the same arc would take longer and look different. See [[take-access-points-wherever-you-can-get-them]] for the underlying principle.
