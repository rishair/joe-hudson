# The Widow With His Photos Still Up

## The Story

A man Joe knows lost his wife to a long, terrible disease. After her death, he met and partnered with a woman whose entire family — her husband and her two children — had been taken by a sneaker wave. Both of them had lost the most-loved people in their first lives. They came together in a second life, knowing what had happened to each other.

Joe's observation about their home: "I'm pretty sure the pictures aren't down in their house."

That is the entire example. The pictures of the dead first family are still up. The new marriage continues. The two facts coexist.

## What Joe Uses It For

The example arrives in the source session as a direct rebuttal to a common assumption the bereaved walk in with: that the only way to make room for a new love is to put away the photos, take down the mementos, dismantle the visible presence of the dead. The bereaved widow Joe is coaching has been imagining the eventual transition this way — "if I have another relationship in the future and stuff, there would be the photos would, you know, be come out of the frames or whatnot."

Joe offers the example as a counter-observation. There is a real configuration in which two bereaved people partner, both have suffered massive loss, and the photos of the dead first families remain visible in the home they share. The new relationship is not threatened by the visible presence of the dead. The new relationship is built on the shared recognition that the dead remain.

The example works because the configuration is extreme. It is not just one widower with photos up, which could be dismissed as someone who has not "moved on." It is two bereaved people, both having lost their entire first families, both keeping the photos visible, both partnering anyway. The extremity rules out the "they just haven't grieved enough" reading. The configuration exists. The bereaved widow being coached can see that the photos-stay-up version of the next chapter is not a fantasy.

## The Specific Function in the Source Session

The example is part of a longer move that culminates in the conduit teaching ([[the-dead-as-conduit-to-non-personal-love]]). The arc:

1. The widow says her life had been "about us" and needs to become "about me." Joe declines this frame.
2. Joe asks "what if you just continued saying this is for us?" The widow finds nothing requires it to be about her.
3. The widow concedes intellectually but pivots to the if-I-find-a-new-partner concern, which assumes the photos would have to come down.
4. Joe offers the example of the couple where the photos didn't come down.
5. The widow's response: "No, so I can still have him. Yeah. It's good news."
6. The teaching continues toward the conduit-to-non-personal-love instruction.

The example is the lever for step 5. Without the example, the if-I-find-a-new-partner concern remains as the unspoken next step that requires the let-go work the widow had been imagining. With the example, the next step is reconfigured. The new relationship does not require the dismantling of the visible presence of the dead.

## The Specific Quote

"I have, um, a person who reached out to me recently, and he lost his, um, wife. Same thing, very horrible disease. And then he found a woman who, um, entire family was taken by a wave. ... A sneaker wave. Two kids and, and husband. I, I'm pretty sure the pictures aren't down in their house." -- Joe Hudson, She Came Asking to Let Go, She Left with a Love That Doesn't End

The casual delivery is part of the function. Joe does not present this as a remarkable case study. He mentions it the way one would mention a person one happens to know. The casualness signals that the configuration is real and not exotic. The widow can recognize her future in it without having to be exceptional.

## Why It Lands

Several structural features make the example work where a generic teaching would not.

- **Extreme parallelism.** Both partners lost the most-loved people. The configuration cannot be dismissed as one person being more "evolved" than the other. They share the recognition.
- **Specific physical detail.** Sneaker wave. Two kids and a husband. The detail makes the example a real thing rather than a hypothetical.
- **The single observation about the pictures.** Joe does not describe their marriage in detail. He does not catalog what they do well. He names one specific physical fact about the house: the pictures are not down. The one detail does the work.
- **Tentative phrasing.** "I'm pretty sure the pictures aren't down." Joe does not claim certainty about their home. The tentativeness is what makes the example feel honest. He has not been to the house. He is making an informed guess. The informed guess is enough.
- **No moral.** Joe does not explain what the example means. He lets it sit. The widow does the interpretive work herself ("so I can still have him"). The work she does is what makes the recognition durable.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

- When the bereaved is assuming a new relationship requires the dismantling of the visible presence of the dead.
- When the bereaved is in the late-grief phase, ready to imagine a new chapter, but is constructing the chapter as requiring let-go work the body does not want to do.
- When the conversation is about photos, mementos, rings, or other physical evidence of the dead and whether those things would have to go.
- When the bereaved would benefit from one specific image that shows the alternative configuration is real and lived rather than theoretical.

## Tone

Joe delivers the example as a small offering rather than as a major teaching. The casualness is structural. A heavy delivery would frame the example as the answer; the casual delivery offers it as a piece of data the widow can do what she wants with.

The phrasing "I have, um, a person who reached out to me recently" is the standard Joe opener for an example. The "um" and the colloquial "reached out to me recently" signal that this is a real person Joe knows, not a polished anecdote. The signaling is what makes the example land as observation rather than as parable.
