# Joe's Stinky House Experiment

## The Point It Illustrates

The how is more important than the what. The path out of obligation is not to do less. The path is to learn to want what was previously done from should. When you remove the should without finding the want, life breaks down. When you find the want underneath, the same action becomes available without the obligation energy.

## The Story

Joe ran a personal experiment for one week (it extended to two): "I'm not going to do anything I don't want to do this week."

The first week, he stopped doing the things he had been doing out of should. He did not take out the trash. He did not do the other chores.

Then his house started to stink.

He did not want to live in a stinky house. So he had to find the want underneath the chore. He had to learn how to want to take out the trash. The action remained necessary. The relationship to the action had to change.

"I remember the first week I did that experiment, I didn't take out the trash, I didn't do all the things I didn't want to do. And then I realized I don't want to live with stinky house. And so I had to learn how to want to take out the trash, which changed everything for me. Where I realized that how I approach things was far more important than if I approached them, if I did them or not." -- Joe Hudson, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

## The Trash-Can Moment (The Canonical Version)

In the version Joe tells in the work-and-enjoyment episode, the story turns on a single concrete image: Joe standing in front of the trash can, smelling the stink, working out in real time what to do. The decision-point is in the body, not in the head. The contradiction between "I committed not to do anything I don't want to do" and "the trash needs taking out" is being held physically.

"I was looking at a trash can and it smelled. And so there I was staring at the trash can going, 'What do I do?' Because I committed to myself to only do what I enjoy. I was like, 'Huh, crap. The only way I'm getting out of this — 'cause I don't enjoy the stink — is to figure out how to enjoy taking out the trash.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work

What broke the contradiction was the discovery of a want already alive in the body: "I want this trash gone. I want it." Not "I have to take out the trash" but "I want to take out the trash because I want the smell to leave." The verb stayed the same. The fuel changed.

"That lesson to me — there was like this profound lesson of being able to get in contact, oh, I want this trash gone. I want it. It's not 'I have to take out the trash,' it's 'I want to take out the trash.' And as soon as I got in touch with that, I could learn to enjoy the thing because it was no longer an obligation, it came from my want." -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work

The structural sequence the story names:
1. The should is removed. The action stops.
2. The consequence of not acting arrives in the body (the stink).
3. The body finds a want that the should had been hiding (the want for the stink to be gone).
4. The want, named, becomes the new fuel for the same action.
5. The action, now fueled by want, becomes enjoyable.

This is the canonical micro-arc of [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] told with a single chore. The instruction "find the want underneath" is abstract; this story is what it looks like when the body actually does it.

## What This Story Establishes

The experiment is a structural test of [[wants-over-shoulds]]. Removing the should reveals what was actually wanted underneath. The trash does not need to be taken out because you should take it out. The trash needs to be taken out because you want a non-stinky house. The same action, different fuel.

The shift in fuel is the entire point. The action driven by should produces resentment, resistance, and procrastination. The action driven by want (even a quiet want like "I want a house that does not smell") produces ease. See [[enjoy-over-manage]].

The story also demonstrates that wants are not always loud or romantic. The want to live in a clean house is mundane. It is still a want. Finding the mundane want underneath the should is enough to reorient the relationship to the action.

## The Method

The experiment as a method:

1. For a defined period (Joe used a week; it extended to two), commit to not doing anything you do not want to do.
2. Notice what stops getting done.
3. Notice what consequences arise.
4. For each item that produces unwanted consequences, look for the want underneath the should. The want is the reason the unwanted consequence is unwanted.
5. Reorient the action around the want rather than around the should.

The experiment can fail in two ways. First, by collapsing back into should the moment a consequence arises ("I have to do this"). Second, by deciding the action is genuinely not wanted and letting the consequence stand ("I would rather live in a stinky house than take out the trash"). The first failure is the more common one. The second is rare and usually reveals a genuine misalignment that deserves attention.

## The Generalization

Joe's discovery generalized. "How I approach things was far more important than if I approached them, if I did them or not." The reorientation of fuel from should to want is the reorientation of the entire approach to action. It is the operational basis of [[how-do-you-want-to-be]]: the how is the want, the what is the action, and the how matters more.

This is also one of the experimental designs that produced the central insight of [[wants-over-shoulds]]. The principle is grounded in what happened in Joe's house when he ran the experiment. The principle did not come from theory.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a client describes a long list of things they "have to" do
- When the conversation has been about discipline and obligation and needs to shift to wants
- When someone is considering whether they should just stop a behavior versus reorient their relationship to it
- When the case for [[wants-over-shoulds]] needs an empirical example with stakes (the house actually stinks)
- When [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] needs a story to anchor it
- When a client is afraid that dropping shoulds will mean nothing gets done

## What the Story Preempts

The story preempts the most common objection to [[wants-over-shoulds]]: "If I only do what I want, nothing will get done." The story shows that the opposite is true. When you only do what you want, the things that need to be done reveal themselves as wanted. The want is already there, hidden under the should. The experiment surfaces the want by removing the cover.

The fear of doing nothing is the fear that without the should there is no engine. The experiment demonstrates that the want is the engine. The should was just borrowed pressure.
