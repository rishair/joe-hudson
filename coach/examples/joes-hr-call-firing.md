# Joe's HR Call: "We're Deciding If We're Going to Pay You Again"

## The Story

Joe is coaching an executive at a large organization. The head of human resources calls him.

The HR head opens with: "We're just deciding if we're gonna pay you again next year."

The opening is a power play. The implicit transaction: serve me, please me, align with my agenda, or you lose the contract. The threat is the lever. Joe's livelihood is the thing supposedly being held over the table.

Joe's response: "Oh, yeah. So I don't serve you. I serve the executive. That's my job. I'm not here to serve you. If you need me to serve you, I am the wrong coach for you because it's just way out of alignment for me."

The power play dissolves. There was no fear in Joe of losing the contract. The lever had nothing to grip. The HR head's stance, which required Joe's fear to function, lost its function.

"There was nothing in me that was worried about losing this thing. If this is what needs to happen, I'm happy to lose it because it's inconsistent with how I want to be in the world. There was, they didn't have any power over me." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

## What It Illustrates

The example is the cleanest demonstration available of [[empowered-vs-power]] in a real business setting. The HR head was operating from power: control through threat of loss. Joe was operating from empowerment: willingness to lose. The asymmetry collapsed the dynamic because the dynamic required Joe's compliance with the frame.

It also illustrates [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]] by negation. The HR head was scared (about something Joe doesn't elaborate on -- protecting their org, asserting authority, managing the coach as a vendor) and was defending themselves through what landed as attack. Joe could have read it as attack and defended. Instead he simply restated the actual relationship. The triangle did not engage because Joe did not take the position the dynamic required him to take.

## The Operative Move

Joe did not negotiate. He did not appease. He did not fight back. He did three things:

1. **Restated the actual job.** "I serve the executive." This made the implicit transaction visible. The transaction the HR head was pricing (Joe's service to HR) was not a transaction that was on the table.
2. **Acknowledged the structural conflict.** "If you need me to serve you, I am the wrong coach for you." This made the asymmetry explicit. It is not a fight, it is a mismatch.
3. **Named alignment as the criterion.** "It's just way out of alignment for me." This is the move that exits power dynamics generally. The criterion is alignment with self, not preservation of contract. The contract is downstream.

Note what is absent: defense, justification, attack, apology, hedging. The response is shorter than the threat. The brevity is part of why it works.

## The Underlying Stance

The stance behind the words: I would rather lose this work than do it from a place I do not want to be in.

"I'd rather have all the work that I'm doing in the world collapse than choose to get in a fight with somebody or think I have something to defend." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear

This is the operational version of [[principles-free-you]]. The principle is "I act from alignment, even at cost." Living by that principle removes the lever every power dynamic depends on.

## What This Example Is Not

It is not a how-to script. Said by someone still scared of losing the contract, the same words would be defense or bluff. The words work because the stance underneath them is real. There is no real loss to threaten. Without that, the words become another performance and the dynamic continues in a new shape.

It is not a recommendation to lose contracts. The contract may or may not have continued. Joe does not report the outcome of that conversation in this telling. The point of the story is not what happened with the work but what happened with the dynamic.

It is not contempt for HR or for the person calling. There is no anti-HR sentiment in the response. Just an accurate restatement of the actual reporting relationship. The dignity in the response is part of what disarms the power play.

## When to Use This Example

In coaching:
- When a client is being maneuvered into a power-over dynamic at work and feels they have no options
- When a client is afraid to lose something and the fear is the lever someone is using on them
- When the question "how do I respond to this without escalating" comes up
- When [[empowered-vs-power]] needs a business-context illustration that is not abstract

## Tone

Plain, almost casual in the retelling. The story is offered as a small example, not as heroism. Joe tells it as the kind of thing that happens regularly when one is unwilling to operate from fear. The casualness of the telling is part of the teaching.
