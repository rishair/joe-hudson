# The Catholic Dad Who Chose His Gay Son Over the Religion

## The Story

When Joe was living in Los Angeles, he had a friend, about 24 years old. The friend had been born and raised Catholic. The friend's father was a Catholic school teacher — not just nominally Catholic, professionally Catholic. The whole structure of the friend's upbringing was inside the framework.

At some point in his early twenties, the friend admitted to himself that he was gay. Joe was present for the whole arc of the realization and what followed. Eventually the friend decided he was going to tell his parents.

He came back after telling them, and he was telling Joe the story.

The father had started bawling when the son told him.

Joe's reaction was the obvious one. He said something like: "Oh man, that's horrible."

The son corrected him: "No, it was fantastic."

Then the son told Joe what the father had said. The father had cried, and then he had looked at his son and said:

"I can't be Catholic anymore. Like, I know that you wouldn't choose to be gay, and I'm not allowed to — like, then you have to go to hell according to my religion, and I can't abide by a religion where you would go to hell. Like, there must be something wrong." -- Joe Hudson reporting his friend's father, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

The father walked out of a lifetime of Catholicism (and a Catholic-school-teacher career) because the religion would have required him to condemn his son. He chose the actual son over the inherited framework.

Joe's tag on the story: "Guess what? That guy wants to go home, see his parents. He doesn't feel judged. He doesn't feel managed."

## What the Story Demonstrates

The story is the concrete example of [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]] in its positive form. The friend wants to go home and see his parents — not out of obligation, not because he should, but because the home is a place where he is not being judged or managed. That experience is what most people want and almost nobody articulates.

The story demonstrates several specific things at once:

**The father chose the actual person over the inherited framework.** The Catholicism was the structure that would have done the condemning. The father did not try to negotiate the structure into accommodating his son. He left the structure. The structural integrity of the framework was less important to him than the integrity of the relationship with his son. Most parents do not realize this is a choice they have.

**The father located what he actually believed by referring to his son.** "There must be something wrong" — meaning there must be something wrong with the framework, not with the son. The son's existence was the test of the framework. The framework failed the test. The father trusted his lived knowing of his son over the doctrine he had spent his life teaching.

**The bawling was not what it looked like from the outside.** Joe's initial assumption ("oh man, that's horrible") was the natural reading. The crying parent looks like the rejection. But the crying was about the father giving up the framework — it was the grief of letting go of a lifelong identity, not a rejection of the son. The son could feel the difference because the meaning of the tears was made explicit immediately by what came next.

**The relationship that resulted is what the principle predicts.** The son wants to come home. He does not dread the visits. He is not curating which parts of his life to share. He is not managing his parents' reactions. The home is a home.

## Why This Story Is the Counter-Example to the Pattern

Most family relationships do not have a dramatic moment like this. Most have a long slow accumulation of small judgments and small management moves. The cumulative effect of those small moves is the same as what would have happened in this story if the father had said "well, you're gay, but I still love you, and you know what the church teaches, and I just hope you find someone of the opposite sex eventually, and maybe try praying about it" — the son would not want to come home.

The story is useful precisely because the stakes are so visible. The father had a clean choice: the framework or the son. He chose the son. The clarity makes visible what is also happening in the background of most ordinary family interactions: small versions of the same choice, made the other way, accumulating into the dread that the kids carry into adulthood.

## What the Story Is Not

Not a story about Catholicism in particular. Substitute any framework that would require the parent to condemn or correct who the kid actually is. The framework could be a particular career expectation, a particular religious tradition, a particular political view, a particular set of standards about who they should partner with, what they should weigh, how they should look, what work they should do. The structural move is the same: the framework is the thing that would have done the criticizing; the parent's choice is whether the framework or the actual kid is more important.

Not a story that requires the parent to fully agree with the kid's life. The father in this story may or may not have arrived at a personal view that being gay was simply fine. The story is not about that. The story is about whether the parent's relationship to the kid is going to be mediated through a framework that requires correcting the kid. The father refused that mediation. That was enough.

Not a story claiming that all parents can or should give up their religion. The framework-shifting is not the move for everyone. The move underneath is: stop using the framework as the instrument for managing the actual person in front of you. There are many ways to do that without abandoning the framework. The dramatic version makes the structural move visible.

## When to Reach for This Story

- When a parent is wrestling with how to respond to a kid coming out, marrying someone they disapprove of, choosing a career they oppose, having a baby outside marriage, etc.
- When the question is whether to prioritize the framework or the actual person
- When the parent is asking how their kid could be happy with a life the parent does not approve of
- When the kid is preparing to tell a parent something they expect to be condemned for
- When teaching [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]] and a positive example is needed
- When teaching the difference between accepting the person and accepting every choice
- When someone presents that they cannot imagine their parent accepting them as they are
- When a parent presents that they love their kid but their framework requires them to disapprove of the kid's life

## Tone

Joe tells the story with restraint. The dramatic moment (the father giving up Catholicism) is delivered without amplification. The point is not the drama; the point is the structural move. The tone is: here is what is available, here is what some people actually do, here is what it produces. The story is presented as evidence, not as inspiration. The reader is invited to recognize the structural move and notice whether they have made it (or could make it) in the smaller, undramatic ways it shows up daily.
