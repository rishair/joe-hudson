# The Babysitter Apology

## The Story

Joe sees a former babysitter at lunch. The last time he saw her, a few years earlier, she looked unhappy. He tells her she looks great and that the last time he saw her she looked like she was "wilting a little." While Joe is eating with his 11-year-old daughter, the babysitter's mother comes over and chastises him for what he said.

Joe feels the sting of rejection for a moment. Then he walks over to the daughter (the babysitter) and says: "I'm sorry, I had no intention of offending you, and I'm sorry if I offended you." She says it is no problem, she just could not think of a recent time she felt bad. Joe explains he had not seen her for 2 or 3 years. She thanks him for apologizing. The mother is still upset, but the connection with the babysitter is restored.

## What It Illustrates

1. **Vulnerability does not end at the cliff.** The mind presents vulnerability as a single moment: say the thing, get rejected, it is over. In reality, you can always double down. After being chastised, Joe could have defended himself or walked away. Instead, he went back and was vulnerable again.

2. **The fear tells you there is a cliff.** "The fear tells us that there's this cliff and it's over at that cliff. Oh, we're going to be rejected. But it just never ends. There's just when you want to stop." -- Joe Hudson, Vulnerability -- Connection Course Series #5

3. **Sometimes stopping is the vulnerable thing.** Joe also notes: "Sometimes it's really vulnerable to say, I can't do this anymore." Vulnerability is not about always pressing forward.

## When To Reach for This

When someone assumes vulnerability is a single moment of exposure. When someone gets rejected after being vulnerable and concludes vulnerability does not work. The story shows that the rejection is not the end. There is always a next move, and the next move can be vulnerable too.
