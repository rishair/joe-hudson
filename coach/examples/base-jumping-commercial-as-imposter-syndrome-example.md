# The BASE Jumping Commercial in Switzerland

## The Story

Joe got a deal to do a BASE jumping commercial in Switzerland -- a major mecca for the sport. The production was substantial: full funding, a helicopter with a camera, professional crew, the full accoutrement of a high-production shoot.

Two problems: Joe had never been to that place in Switzerland. Joe had never organized a shoot like this before. The local BASE jumping community had the most experience and skill available -- they were the people who could have helped him organize the shoot well.

What Joe did: avoided contacting them. Routed around the experts. Organized the shoot in a way that tried to hide that he didn't know what he was doing.

"When I showed up to organize the shoot, I avoided contacting the people who were the most experienced and most skilled who could help me because I would have felt like an imposter. And so I, like, the way that I organized the thing was trying to kind of hide it." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The result: the production value suffered. The shoot still happened. They got the footage. But the work was visibly worse than it would have been had the local experts been engaged. The imposter-syndrome-driven hiding produced exactly the outcome the hiding was supposed to prevent.

## What Joe Realized Looking Back

"What would it have been like if I just showed up and I was like, yep, I have literally never done this before. And this particular thing we're doing is new to me. And I'm gonna do it. And I'm gonna ask for all the help that I can get." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The alternative was simple, available, and would have produced better work. The only thing in the way was the imposter feeling and the strategy of hiding it generated. The vulnerability of admitting "I've never done this before" was the move that would have unlocked the help, improved the production, and dissolved the imposter feeling -- because the feeling has nothing to attach to when the gap is openly admitted.

## What This Example Illustrates

The example is Joe's own first-person demonstration of several principles operating together:

- **[[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]]** -- The hiding behavior is the imposter act, and enacting it generated more imposter feeling, not less.
- **[[maintaining-false-persona-costs-more-than-incompetence]]** -- The cognitive overhead of maintaining the I-know-what-I'm-doing front consumed the bandwidth that would have improved the work. The production value loss was the operational cost.
- **[[imposter-syndrome-projects-incompetence-onto-the-people-who-hired-you]]** -- For Joe to be an imposter on the shoot he had been hired for, he had to assume the people who hired him didn't know what they were doing. The projection was implicit in the hiding.
- **[[trying-to-be-someone-other-than-yourself-is-the-actual-imposter-act]]** -- Joe was performing the persona of the one-who-knows-the-shoot. The performance was the imposter act. The not-having-done-this-before was not the imposter; the pretending was.
- **[[ask-for-all-the-help-you-can-get]]** -- The explicit alternative move Joe names looking back.

## Why This Example Works

The example is useful in coaching for several reasons:

1. **First-person.** Joe is naming his own pattern, not the client's. The story does not arrive as criticism.
2. **Mundane stakes.** It is not a life-or-death story. It is a normal work situation where the imposter syndrome produced a worse outcome. Most clients have analogous situations.
3. **Visible-in-hindsight.** The cost is obvious looking back, even if it was invisible in the moment. Clients can locate similar invisible-in-the-moment patterns in their own work.
4. **Simple alternative.** The alternative Joe names -- "yep, I've never done this before, gonna do it, gonna ask for help" -- is unambiguously available and obviously better. The simplicity makes the path forward concrete.

The story also normalizes the pattern. Joe ran it. Most successful, competent people have run it. The pattern is common; it is also costly; the cost is paid in worse work and more imposter feeling; the exit is vulnerability and asking for help.

## When To Use This Example

- When a client is describing hiding from experts or routing around the people who could help
- When a client is reporting that their work is suffering despite their effort
- When introducing [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]] and a concrete example would land
- When a client cannot see the cost of their imposter-driven behavior because it feels protective in the moment
- When normalizing the pattern -- the client realizes they are not uniquely broken; everyone has done this

The example is short and easily told. It is one of Joe's most useful first-person stories for the imposter syndrome work because it compresses several principles into a single concrete scene the client can hold in mind.

## Tone

Joe tells this with a slight self-deprecating humor. The pattern was costly and the cost was self-inflicted. The looking-back-from-after is the position of someone who has clearly seen what they were doing and learned from it -- not defensive, not regretful, just clear. The tone gives the client permission to look at their own analogous situations with the same clarity.
