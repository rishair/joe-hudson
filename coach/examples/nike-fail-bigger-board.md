# Nike's 'Fail Bigger' Board (Framing Iteration at the Front Door)

## The Story

Joe describes a Nike installation from the 1990s. Walking into Nike's Seattle headquarters, visitors encountered a sign the size of a sheet of plywood -- 4 feet by 8 feet -- mounted on the wall. The sign was constructed entirely of pushpins: black pushpins and white pushpins arranged to spell two words.

The words: "FAIL BIGGER."

Two words. Wall-sized. At the entrance. The first thing anyone saw walking in.

"Like, you'd walk into their main Seattle business headquarters and they had a 4x8, so like the size of a piece of plywood sign, all done by pushpin. And there was black pushpins and white pushpins and it just said, fail bigger. They immediately, you walk in and they have framed success as large failures. Boom." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

## What This Story Illustrates

This is the canonical example of framing iteration at the front door of an organization. The principle the sign embodied: success in this company is defined as how big you can fail. The bigger the failure, the bigger the attempt was. The biggest failures are the inputs of the biggest wins.

The framing was structural. It was not in a values document buried on a slide. It was not a quarterly memo from leadership. It was wall-sized, physical, and immediately visible. Every employee, every visitor, every prospective hire saw it before any other message about the company.

The medium was also part of the message. The sign was made of pushpins, not paint. The construction itself was iterative -- pushpins can be moved, rearranged, replaced. The medium said: the message itself is iterable. We mean this enough to display it. We do not mean it so fixedly that it cannot move.

See [[failure-is-an-idea]]. The sign was a structural intervention against the failure-frame. By literally redefining what failure meant at the threshold of the building, the company removed the silent governor on every employee's willingness to attempt the larger thing.

## The Mechanism: Front-Door Framing

Most companies want their employees to be more iterative. Most companies do not get more iteration. The gap is usually that the iteration value is announced in a memo that everyone forgets, while the daily signals -- performance reviews, recognition, water-cooler stories -- continue to reward outcomes and punish missed targets.

The Nike installation closed the gap. The framing was not a memo. It was the first thing every person saw. The framing carried the weight of physical presence. Each employee walked in past it. Each visitor saw it. The framing was operating in the background of every conversation that happened inside the building.

This is the structural form of [[reward-failure-in-the-organization]] applied to the architecture of the workplace. The reward signal was visible. It was unambiguous. It was placed exactly where the operating assumptions about success and failure get formed.

## What This Story Does Not Illustrate

The story does not claim Nike got everything right. The point is specifically about the framing intervention. The framing is necessary but not sufficient. A "fail bigger" sign without the operational follow-through -- actual rewards for bold attempts, actual celebration of generative failures, actual hiring decisions that select for iteration capacity -- becomes the kind of wall decoration that produces cynicism.

The story is also not a recommendation to literally install a sign. The recommendation is to put the iteration-framing somewhere it cannot be missed by anyone joining or operating inside the organization. The form matters less than the placement and the persistence.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

When teaching organizational application of the iterative mindset. The story is short, concrete, and visually memorable. Leaders can carry it back to their own organizations and ask: what is my "fail bigger" sign? Where would I place it?

When working with a CEO or leadership team that wants to install an iteration culture and is asking how to do it. The story is the first concrete example. The structural insight (framing at the front door, not in a memo) generalizes immediately.

When pairing with [[reward-failure-in-the-organization]] to show that the framing intervention is the upstream move and the reward structure is the downstream operationalization. The two together produce iteration culture; either alone produces cynicism.

When introducing [[team-as-scientific-method]] and the iteration discipline that makes the scientific method work. The Nike sign was a daily reminder that the company was running experiments, not seeking the right answers.

## What Made This Specific Sign Work

Three things, all transferable:

**Placement.** The sign was at the front door. Not in a conference room. Not in a values document. At the threshold every person crossed every day.

**Scale.** The sign was 4 feet by 8 feet. Big enough to be unmissable. Not modest. The size signaled that the company meant it.

**Construction.** The pushpin medium said iteration. Paint would have said permanence. The pushpins said: we built this and we can rebuild it. The medium reinforced the message.

A small framed quote in a conference room would not have done what the Nike sign did. The placement, scale, and construction were the message as much as the words were.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with a kind of admiring brevity. The cadence is "boom" -- the framing happened, the framing worked, the framing was the move. He does not editorialize. The example is offered as obvious-once-seen, the way a good structural move usually is.
