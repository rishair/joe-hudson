# Joe's Mill Valley List: The Shoulds That Didn't Change and the Wants That Did

## The Story

Young Joe is living in a shared house in Mill Valley, California. He has a single room. He has been on a self-development push and is being deliberately monistic about it. The room contains a desk. The bed is in the closet — he sleeps in the closet so the room can stay empty. The rest of the room is just big and full of light.

He is doing inner work. One day he decides to be deeply honest with himself and writes down every single thing he does not like about himself. Everything. He folds the list up, puts it away, and forgets about it.

Six to eight months later he finds the list. He reads through it. He notices something specific:

Every single thing on the list that had actually changed in the intervening months was something he had not told himself he should change. Every single thing he had told himself he should change had not changed at all.

"Every single thing on that list that had changed was not something that I told myself I should do. It just naturally changed. Everything I told myself I should do on that list hadn't changed at all." -- Joe Hudson, After 14 Years of Coaching

## The Point It Illustrates

This is the empirical seed of [[wants-over-shoulds]]. Joe did not discover the principle from a book or a teacher. He discovered it by accident from his own list. The list was a controlled experiment with a population of n=many items, all from the same person, all observed over the same time period. The variable that mattered was: did Joe attach a should to it?

The items he attached a should to did not move. The items he did not attach a should to moved on their own. The asymmetry was clean enough that the conclusion was unmissable.

The mechanism the story demonstrates was later named: shoulds operate as threats (see [[should-as-threat]]). The body responds to the threat with rebellion or submission, neither of which produces movement. Wants, by contrast, pull from inside. The natural impulse toward change does the work that the should was trying to manufacture.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone wants empirical evidence that shoulds don't work. The story is the controlled experiment.
- When someone is doing self-development and has a long list of things to change. The story is the warning: the things you push hardest on are the things that will move least.
- When someone is afraid that without shoulds nothing will change. The story is the counter-evidence: things changed without the shoulds. The wants did the work.
- When someone is in a long-running self-improvement project that is producing no movement. The story models the diagnostic: look at the list. What's stuck is what you've should-ed at. What's moved is what you let alone.

## The List Diagnostic as a Coaching Move

The story is generalizable as a coaching exercise. See [[list-things-trying-to-change-for-4-years]] for the explicit practice that derives from it.

The exercise: ask the client to list every behavior they have been trying to change for the last 4-10 years that has not changed. The instruction to apply to the list: ask of each item, "is this something I keep telling myself I should do?" Almost always the answer is yes. The persistence of the unchanged item across years is itself the evidence that the should is what is keeping it stuck.

The exercise's leverage comes from the asymmetry the story demonstrates. The client expects to be told they need to try harder. The diagnostic reveals that the trying is what is preventing the change. The intervention is to drop the should, not to increase the effort.

## The Living Conditions Matter to the Story

The empty room, the closet bed, the dedicated solitude — these are not background details. Joe was structuring his life to make the inner work as bare as possible. The list was written in conditions designed to allow honesty: no distractions, no audience, no comfort that might require him to soften the list. The conditions made the experiment cleaner than it would have been in a normal life.

This is worth noting for clients who try to do similar inner work in the middle of normal life and find that the items they write down are pre-filtered for what feels acceptable. The conditions of the writing affect the data. Joe's list worked because the conditions allowed an unflinching version of it.

The conditions are not a requirement to reproduce the experiment. But the principle is real: the cleaner the conditions for the honesty, the more useful the data. A client who wants to run a version of the experiment should set aside time and space that does not require performing the list for any audience, including their own self-image.

## The Connection to the Seed Stories

Like [[joes-photo-album-cried-14-years]], this story is one of the seed events of Joe's later coaching work. Both stories follow the same structure: young Joe doing self-work alone, stumbling onto something by accident, discovering an empirical fact that later became a teaching. Both are about a single observation that re-organized how Joe understood inner change.

The pairing is instructive for clients who think they need a teacher to find their next move. The two seed stories are both about a young person noticing what was happening in himself and trusting what he noticed. The teaching grew from the noticing. The noticing came first.

This is also why Joe is comfortable making the wants-over-shoulds claim as strongly as he does. He is not making it from theory. He is making it from a list with names on it of things that did and did not change, with the variable cleanly visible. The certainty in his teaching is grounded in the certainty the experiment produced.
