# Joe's Pleasing-Dad Money Story

## The Point It Illustrates

Many adult shoulds are not what they appear to be. The "should make money" feels current and rational. Underneath, it is often a much older should -- "should please my father" -- still running. Locating the actual origin of the should is what frees the person to find the actual want.

This is also an example of how a should can produce the appearance of motivation while actually preventing the work the person is trying to do.

## The Story

Joe was earlier in his venture capital career. He had started telling himself he should be making money. The should was somewhat foreign to him because money had not previously been important. The reasoning he gave himself was sensible: indebtedness to investors, doing a good job, being valuable.

Sitting in a hammock, he read news that a company formed with almost no money had sold for multiple billions of dollars. The reaction was visceral.

"It felt like just an absolute kick in my stomach, just like a whack in my stomach." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

He stopped. He noticed the feeling. He traced it back through his body -- not intellectually, but somatically -- to the first time he had felt it. The trace landed in his childhood: trying to please his father.

"It was not pleasurable at that time, you know, to please him, at least from my point of view, wasn't possible. And I saw that this whole money-making activity had nothing to do with actually making money. It had to -- and the should behind it had nothing to do with it. It was like this very early should that I had of should be pleasing my father, that that was a very ingrained should." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## The Translation

Once the origin of the should was visible, Joe asked the next question: what do I actually want here? The answer: he wanted to be part of creating great cultures for people to work in. Not money. Not approval. Culture.

"It changed my approach. It changed my ability to be effective. It just changed everything. As soon as I just moved from the should that was driven by an early feeling to a want which was very present and it was just very immediate. And all of a sudden, you know, everything started to open up and flourish in my life in a new way." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a client is grinding toward a goal that feels arbitrary or hollow
- When the should language is about a current adult metric (money, status, output) that probably traces to an older origin
- When the client is willing to do somatic tracing -- following the body sensation back to its earliest occurrence
- When the question "what do you actually want here?" needs an example to make it land

## The Coaching Technique Embedded in the Story

Two moves are visible:

1. **Trace the feeling back somatically.** When the should hits, the body has felt it before. Following the body memory to its origin reveals what the should is actually about.
2. **Then ask the want question.** Once the should is recognized as an old script rather than a current necessity, the question "what do I actually want here?" produces a clean answer rather than a recycled obligation.

This is the somatic version of [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]]. See also [[childhood-imprinting]] for the mechanism by which the original should got installed.
