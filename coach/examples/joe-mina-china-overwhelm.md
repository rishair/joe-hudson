# Joe and Mina in China (Overwhelmed by Being Loved)

## The Story

Joe and Mina were in China together. Mina was introducing Joe to her network there. They were visiting a group that had built a 50-story building in 3 days using a Lego-set-on-industrial-scale technology. They were sitting in the side office of this group, in a media-style conversation.

In the meeting, Mina was not being respected. The conversation was happening between the people who "knew something." Mina was not speaking up. Joe noticed.

Joe said the dynamic was unacceptable. Later, over coffee, Mina asked why he had said that. Joe said: "I'm here for you. I'm not here for this business meeting. I'm here for your growth."

In that moment, Mina got overwhelmed. The overwhelm was about being loved that much. The fear was about letting that level of love in.

"You were having coffee afterwards, and you're like, why is that unacceptable? And I was like, oh, because I'm here for you. I'm not here for this business meeting. I'm here for your growth. And you got overwhelmed in that moment because you were scared to let that much love in." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

Mina described what happened next: "You were about to say your next sentence, and I just burst out crying. And it was like loud. And you looked at me so surprised. You're like, what is happening? What just happened?"

"I was like, yeah, I've just never been loved like that, that I was more important than the content, the PowerPoint, the slides, or the business that was happening." -- Mina Lee, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The overwhelm did not get managed. It did not get pushed away. Mina allowed it to move through her, quite rapidly. The fear of being loved that much was felt, and then it passed.

What followed was a sustained flow state for the rest of their China travels. Every conversation turned into someone crying, having a realization, going "holy crap, what just happened?" Joe got on the floor of a 5-star duck restaurant in Beijing and begged a woman to stop apologizing to him. She did. The contacts they made paid off for Mina in future endeavors.

"From that point on, every conversation we had with anybody turned into somebody crying, somebody having a realization, somebody going, 'Holy crap, what just happened?'" -- Mina Lee, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

"That big smoothie and all of those contacts that we met then paid off for you in future endeavors. And that's how it works. If you can move through the overwhelm instead of tightening down, feel the feelings, and then all of a sudden that flow state comes in again." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## The Point It Illustrates

This story shows overwhelm produced by intensity that is not bad. The standard model of overwhelm is "too many demands, too little capacity." This is overwhelm from too much love being directed at someone whose capacity to receive love is not yet that large.

The standard intervention model would be "stop, slow down, regulate." That is not what happened. What happened was that the emotion that overwhelmed was felt, all the way through, quickly. The other side of the overwhelm was a flow state that lasted for days and produced both connection and concrete results.

The pairing with [[esme-math-final]] is important. In Esme's case, the overwhelm was external pressure, and the move was to let go of trying to control it. In Mina's case, the overwhelm was internal (the rising emotion at being loved), and the move was to let it move through. The same underlying principle in both: do not brace against the overwhelm, do not manage it, let what is happening happen. What is on the other side is flow.

"If you can move through the overwhelm instead of tightening down, feel the feelings, and then all of a sudden that flow state comes in again." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## What Specifically Worked

Mina did several things in fast sequence:

1. **She did not push away the rising emotion.** When the love hit and the fear of receiving it activated, she did not deflect, change the subject, or intellectualize. She let it surface.

2. **She felt it loudly.** The crying was not a controlled tear. It was loud. It was visible. It was full. This is contrary to the usual instinct to hide the emotion or apologize for it.

3. **She named what she had recognized.** "I've just never been loved like that, that I was more important than the content." Naming was not analysis. It was bringing the recognition into language so that it could be received.

4. **She did not perform okay-ness on the other side.** The crying happened. Then it was done. There was no extended discussion of what it meant or how to fix what had caused it. The emotion moved and the next thing began.

The whole sequence took minutes, not hours or days. Joe describes this as the model: when the overwhelm rises, let it move quickly through rather than dragging it out.

## The Fear of Being Loved

The specific overwhelm in this story is worth naming. It is not the overwhelm of having too much to do. It is the overwhelm of being seen and valued more than the person has ever been seen and valued.

This is one of the most common forms of unrecognized overwhelm. It often hides behind the more recognizable forms (busy-ness, work pressure, scheduling). Underneath the visible overwhelm, there is sometimes the deeper overwhelm of being loved by people whose love the person does not feel they can receive.

The pattern shows up in romantic relationships, in friendships, in professional mentorships. Someone is given love or attention that exceeds what they grew up with. The system reads the love as a threat. The threat produces something that looks like overwhelm. The underlying material is the unfelt grief of all the years that the love was not present.

See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]] for the relational version of this pattern. The fear of receiving love is one of the engines of the chase-and-distance dynamic.

## When Joe Reaches for This

When someone is overwhelmed by something good rather than something bad. Birthday parties. Receiving compliments. Being celebrated. Being loved more than the system has the capacity to easily receive.

When teaching that overwhelm is not always pathological. The story shows overwhelm as the leading edge of expansion of capacity. The capacity to receive love grew during this moment. The growth was uncomfortable. It was also exactly what was happening.

When pairing with [[esme-math-final]] to show the two directions of the same underlying mechanic. Releasing the management when overwhelmed by external pressure, allowing the emotion when overwhelmed by internal intensity. Same move, different presentation.

When someone is bracing against an emotion that is trying to move through them. The story shows that the brace is what makes the overwhelm long. The release is what makes it short.

## The Begging-on-the-Floor Sequel

Mina mentions a follow-up moment in the trip: at a duck restaurant in Beijing, a woman kept apologizing to Joe. Joe told her that if she apologized one more time, he would beg her. She apologized again. Joe got on the floor of the 5-star restaurant and begged her. She almost cried just to get him off the floor. She never said sorry again.

This sequel is part of the same flow state that opened when Mina let the overwhelm move through. The willingness to do the unexpected, the willingness to be ridiculous in service of someone, the willingness to break the social frame in service of love -- all of these were available in the flow state that followed the breakthrough.

The implication: when you move through your own overwhelm, you become more available to others in ways that produce the same kind of breakthrough for them. The flow state is contagious.
