# Joe's 7 Years of Weekly Reichian Breathwork

## The Story

For seven years — depending on travel schedule, either weekly or every other week, in hour-long sessions — Joe did breathwork in the pure Reich tradition with a teacher who has since passed away. She told Joe that she was one of the last practitioners doing the pure Reich breathwork without modification. Joe couldn't find anything to contradict that. Most of the lineages still practicing — including Lowen, which Joe respects — had taken the Reich work and modified it.

Joe's not arguing that the pure form was better. He says he's done many other kinds of breathwork in his life: coherent breathing, holotropic-influenced work, various Indian traditions ("I have a book this big of an Indian breathwork person, and I'll literally just open it up and be like, let's try that for a bit. And I'll try it and see what it does to my system"). The point isn't lineage purity. The point is the duration of sustained, weekly engagement with one practice over years.

## What Changed

When Jonny asks about Joe's own breathwork journey, Joe describes the cumulative effect of 7 years of consistent practice in concrete bodily terms — not in abstract spiritual ones:

"That absolutely changed my whole world. That's the 7 years that I did. I mean, it changed the way that I had an orgasm. It changed the way I laughed. It changed my capacity to — my resilience, my stress level. It changed so much. My armoring, the physical musculature armoring, it had a huge impact." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

The specific changes:
- The way he had an orgasm
- The way he laughed
- His resilience
- His stress level
- The physical musculature armoring throughout the body

These are nervous-system and somatic changes. They are not insights. They are not stories. They are alterations to how the body itself functioned at a baseline level. The body that Joe walked into the 7 years with was not the body he walked out with.

This is the concrete picture of what happens at the nervous-system layer of the work when it gets sustained attention. See [[the-work-as-three-levels-of-change]] for the structural frame.

## The Two-Phase Pattern: Early Ripeness, Then Long Eroding

Joe describes a two-phase pattern that is worth noting:

"A lot of the impact happened in the first little bit because I was very ripe. And then there was just something that happened over time, that there was an eroding and a wearing away that happened over time that was really useful." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

Two distinct kinds of change:

- **Early ripe-impact change.** The first months of the practice, the body releases what it has been holding and the person who is ready experiences large shifts. This is the dramatic phase.
- **Long-erosion change.** The years after the ripeness phase. The dramatic shifts slow down. What replaces them is a slow wearing away of armoring — old structural holding patterns that the body had treated as baseline finally start to dissolve.

Both phases matter. The first phase is what gets people excited and what most testimonials describe. The second phase is what produces the durable, body-level transformation that the first phase only hinted at. People who stop after the first phase get the ripeness benefit and miss the eroding benefit. The eroding only happens through sustained practice over years.

This is also why short, intense breathwork experiences — a single workshop, a week-long retreat — produce dramatic state changes that often don't translate into trait changes. See [[activation-release-rest-cycle]]. The ripeness fires. The eroding never happens because the practice doesn't continue.

## What This Refutes

This example refutes a couple of common assumptions:

- **Real transformation happens in one breakthrough.** No. Real transformation includes the dramatic-shift phase and then years of slow eroding. The eroding is most of the work.
- **Modality matters more than duration.** No. Joe is explicit that he's not advocating his particular lineage. He's pointing at what 7 years of consistent practice does, regardless of which specific tradition the practice came from.
- **You can fast-track somatic change.** No. The nervous system and the musculature update on biological timescales. The work has to be repeated and repeated and repeated for the body to actually rewire.
- **One specific breathwork is the answer.** No. Joe has done many. The duration mattered. The variety of approaches mattered less than the consistency.

## The Variety Point

Joe's description of his Indian breathwork book — "I'll literally just open it up and be like, let's try that for a bit. And I'll try it and see what it does to my system" — is also instructive. The sustained practice doesn't mean doing the same thing forever. It means staying in relationship with the practice over years while remaining curious about variations.

There's no orthodoxy in Joe's relationship to breath. He's done pure Reich, Lowen, holotropic-influenced work, coherent breathing, and assorted Indian traditions. The throughline is decades of breath as a working tool, not allegiance to any single lineage. The system he's worked on is his own; the practices are instruments.

## What Jonny Adds

Jonny adds a related observation about his own laugh and voice changing through breathwork:

"It's interesting you mentioned laugh. Both my laugh and my voice have changed. Even I listened to a podcast episode I did 4 years ago, and my voice sounds noticeably different then compared to how it sounds now. And I think a lot of that is because the tension in my lower belly and lower diaphragm, a lot of it's kind of been released." -- Jonny Miller, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

The same somatic-level change. The body that recorded the 4-year-old podcast was holding tension that the current body has released. The voice carries the release.

## When Joe Reaches For This

Joe uses his own breathwork journey when:
- Someone is asking what sustained practice actually does
- The conversation needs evidence that nervous-system-layer work is real and produces durable change
- Someone is asking how long this kind of work takes (the honest answer involves years)
- The point is being made that body-level change requires body-level practice over time
- The comparison is being drawn between dramatic breakthrough work and slow ongoing erosion
- The validity of breathwork specifically is being established

It's also one of Joe's rare moments of explicitly biographical disclosure — he's usually pointing at the work rather than himself. The example is offered because the question called for it, not as self-promotion.
