# Joe and Tara's 10-Minute Fight About the LA Show

## The Story

Four years into the relationship. Tara was doing some acting in LA. She had a show. Joe didn't come.

She came home furious.

In Joe's mind, in the moment, a thing happened that had not happened before: "Oh, this is true for her. Like this is how she sees the world. It doesn't have to be mine. It's hers."

He just sat there. Listened. Kept an open heart. Said "I'm sorry."

"It just all melted inside of like 10 minutes. What used to be hours of fighting, you know, like maybe even a day of fighting." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

## What Used to Happen

The same scene, before the shift, ran a different shape. Tara would come home angry. Joe would hear her story and start asking, internally: is this true or not true?

The binary trap:
- If true: I did something wrong.
- If not true: she's doing something wrong by yelling at me.

"And so then I was in this world where one of us had to do something wrong." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The fight then took hours. Sometimes a day. Both people defending, both people prosecuting, both people locked in a structure where the only exits were "I'm wrong" or "you're wrong."

## What Changed

The cognitive move that compressed the fight from a day to ten minutes is small and specific. Joe did not get better at apologizing. He did not get more empathetic in any general sense. He stopped treating Tara's anger as a claim about reality that he had to verify or refute. He started treating it as her experience of reality, which was real for her, and which his job was to understand, not to adjudicate.

The phrase that captures the move: "This is the way you see reality, this is the way I see reality, and how do we want to see reality together?"

The seeing-it-differently is not the problem. The forcing both people into the same seeing is the problem. With the forcing removed, the conversation that used to be a war becomes a conversation about two perspectives, neither of which is at risk.

## The Courtroom Language

Joe and Tara developed a shorthand for the old shape:

"We used to have this phrase and it was like, hey, let's not go into the courtroom. Courtroom was to figure out who had done what and what the truth was." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

Naming the structure made it a thing they could opt out of. "Let's not go into the courtroom" is a move either of them could make. It marks: we are about to start adjudicating, and adjudication is the trap, and we can choose not to enter it.

This is the operational form of [[dont-find-the-truth-find-their-truth]]. The courtroom is the metaphor for searching for THE truth. Stepping out of the courtroom is the practice of searching for their truth instead.

## What Joe Did Concretely

Three components, all present in the actual moment:

**1. Internal recognition.** "This is true for her. This is how she sees the world. It doesn't have to be mine. It's hers." This is the cognitive move that has to happen first. Without it, the listening that follows would have been technique.

**2. Open-hearted listening.** Not active-listening as a technique. Open-hearted. "I could just be there and like have an open heart and listen to her and hear her story." The body posture and the breath matched the internal recognition.

**3. The apology.** "I could just say I'm sorry. And I said I was sorry." The apology was not an admission that he was wrong. The apology was acknowledgment of her experience. He was sorry that she felt unmet, not sorry that he had failed to attend a show he had reasons not to attend.

The three components compress to: see her truth, receive it openly, acknowledge her experience. The fight that used to take a day was over in ten minutes.

## What This Story Illustrates

This is the canonical illustration of [[dont-find-the-truth-find-their-truth]] -- the move from searching for THE truth (who is right) to searching for HER truth (what is real for her). The principle is abstract. The story is the live form.

It also illustrates [[see-them-be-seen-find-alignment-instead-of-control]] in its compressed couples form. Joe's move in the story is the "see them" move, executed cleanly. The story is what the four-move frame produces when the first move is held without slipping into the agenda.

It is the upstream story for the [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] orientation: the experience of a fight collapsing from a day to ten minutes is part of what builds the destination orientation that fights can become anticipated rather than dreaded.

The story is also a demonstration of the [[shame-hot-potato]] not running. Tara comes home with the potato in her hand. Joe does not pick it up to throw back. She has nowhere to throw because there is no receiving body. The potato just sits down. The fight ends.

## What This Story Is Not

This is not "Joe gave Tara whatever she wanted." Joe did not promise to come to future shows. He did not say her anger was right. He did not abandon his own reasons for missing the show. The move is purely about the perception of her experience as real-for-her, not about agreement or compliance.

This is not "Joe was a doormat." See [[dont-find-the-truth-find-their-truth]] for the precise carve-out. Understanding the other person's truth does not require you to compromise yourself, change your actions, or pretend their interpretation is yours.

This is not a one-shot technique. This was a fight four years into a relationship in which two people had been training themselves on each other. The move worked because the internal recognition was actually present, not because Joe ran the script.

## How to Use This Story in Coaching

This is the cleanest example to deploy when:

**A person presents a recurring fight in their relationship.** Use the contrast: there is the shape that takes hours or days, and there is the shape that takes ten minutes. The difference is not how the partner showed up. The difference is whether you went into the courtroom or stepped out of it.

**A person is asking "how do I get my partner to see they are wrong?"** The question itself is the courtroom. The story illustrates the alternative: the fight ends faster when you stop trying to get them to see they are wrong and start trying to see what is true for them.

**A person says "but if I just listen, doesn't she win?"** The story demonstrates the third option that the win/lose binary obscures. Nobody won. There was no compromise. There was no agreement that Joe should have come to the show. The fight just ended because the engine was off.

**A person describes themselves trying to figure out the truth of what happened.** Name the courtroom. "It sounds like you are in the courtroom, trying to figure out what's true and what's not. Joe and Tara had a phrase: let's not go into the courtroom. What would it look like to just see her experience as hers, without needing to verify it?"

## The Generalization Joe Makes

Joe extends the story past the marriage:

"What I notice is that when two people are in a fight, this is such a critical thing. Two people in a fight, what both want is to be seen. Both of them want to feel seen. And what they're usually doing is they're just throwing shame at each other. They're like, you're wrong, you're wrong, you're wrong, you're wrong, you're wrong, you're wrong. And what they're saying is, I want to be seen. I want to be seen. I want to be seen." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The story is one instance of the general pattern. Two people both wanting to be seen. Neither one able to see while wanting to be seen. The exit is: one of them seeing first. The other almost immediately wants to see back.

"If you want to end a fight, just sit down and see their truth. Connect with where they're at... And as soon as someone feels seen, almost immediately they want to see the other person. And so the fight just almost instantaneously resolves." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The generalization is the "see-me method" the episode is titled after. The story is the proof of concept.

## Signal Questions

- The last fight you had with your partner: were you in the courtroom?
- What would it look like to see their experience as real-for-them, without needing to verify whether their version of events is the correct one?
- If you stopped trying to figure out who was right, what would you have to feel?
- The fights that take hours in your relationship: what shape do they take? What if they could take ten minutes?
- When your partner is angry, what story are you telling about whether their anger is justified or not? What if the question of whether it is justified is the wrong question?
