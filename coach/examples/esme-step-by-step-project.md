# Esme Asks Joe to Tell Her Exactly What to Do

## The Story

Joe's younger daughter Esme wanted to do a project. Joe agreed to support her. But the project was not getting done. Joe noticed his own frustration: "I don't really want to support you because it's not getting done."

Esme then asked something Joe had not expected. She said: what I need you to do is just tell me exactly what to do. I have never done this before. I have no idea what to do. I just need you to tell me step by step what to do the first time.

Joe paused. His default position was the opposite: I want you to be your own authority, I don't want to tell you how to do it. He realized that Esme was asking for something specific and reasonable. She had no template for this kind of project. She needed instruction to get the first cycle going.

They walked through a pipeline together. Step by step: find the cool experiences she could have, identify who to contact, write an email, who to send it to, how to send it. Joe read the email, gave feedback. Each step, he showed her. After the first cycle, Esme was running the process on her own. She did not need him.

## What the Example Illustrates

The principle of [[teach-teenagers-to-be-their-own-authority]] has an inverse failure mode. A parent so committed to not being the authority that they refuse to instruct when the child asks for instruction is also overriding the child's authority. The child has asked for something specific. Refusing the request is just as much an imposition as instructing unasked.

Esme's request was itself the operation of her authority. She had assessed her situation, identified what she needed, and asked for it cleanly. The right response to authority operating well is to meet the request, not to use the "I don't want to be your authority" principle to refuse it.

"I want you to be your own authority, right? And she was like, the authority in me says this is what I want, this is what I want." -- Joe Hudson (paraphrasing the exchange), Who Owns Your Authority?

The example clarifies the principle. The work is not to refuse to be an authority. The work is to install the child's own authority as the primary criterion. When that authority asks for instruction, the right response is to instruct. When that authority asks for space, the right response is to give space. The parent's job is to follow what the child's authority is asking for, not to enforce a meta-principle about authority.

## The Mechanism: Instruction Following Request Builds Authority

A parent who instructs without being asked installs the parent's voice as the third party in the child's evaluation. The child learns to consult the parent's voice for what to do.

A parent who refuses to instruct when asked teaches the child that asking for help is wrong, or that the parent will not be useful, or that the child should figure everything out alone.

A parent who instructs in response to a clean request teaches a third thing: that asking is legitimate, that help is available when needed, that the child gets to choose when to draw on the parent and when not to. The instruction in this case strengthens the child's authority because the child is in charge of when the instruction happens.

The structural pattern: instruction following an explicit request supports the child's authority. Instruction without request, or refusal to instruct when requested, both undermine the child's authority.

This pattern applies more broadly than parenting. See [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] for the coaching version: the coach offers when asked, holds back when the client has not asked, and follows the client's lead in what kind of engagement is wanted.

## Joe's Surprise as Part of the Story

The detail that Joe was surprised — "I was completely, I'm like, huh?" — is part of what the example demonstrates. Joe had a working model of how to parent toward authority, and the model would have led him to refuse the request. The surprise is the moment the model encountered the actual child in front of him and had to update.

A parenting principle held rigidly becomes its own constraint on the child. The child has to be allowed to ask for the thing the principle would refuse, and the parent has to be able to update the application of the principle in real time. The model is a starting point, not a destination.

## The Aftermath

Esme ran the project on her own after that first cycle. She did not need Joe's continued involvement. The instruction had served its function: getting the first cycle going, giving her the template, then stepping back.

The example completes itself: instruction was useful, was bounded, was followed by Esme's autonomous operation. The brief intervention did not install Joe as the ongoing authority. It installed Esme's capacity to run the process.

## When to Reach For This Example

- A parent who is committed to not telling their child what to do and is starting to refuse legitimate requests
- A parent who feels guilty for instructing their teenager and is questioning whether they violated the principle
- A coach working with a client who needs specific instruction and wondering whether to give it
- A discussion of [[teach-teenagers-to-be-their-own-authority]] that risks the principle being applied too rigidly
- A parent whose teenager seems to need scaffolding but who is unsure whether to provide it
- A teaching about authority that needs the corrective: the child's authority can request what looks like the opposite of authority development

## Tone

Joe tells the story with audible warmth and a small note of self-deprecation. The "I'm like, huh?" captures the moment of his model being interrupted by his daughter. The tone is the tone of a parent who learned something specific from the interaction and is willing to share both his initial reluctance and the eventual update. There is no shame in the story — the original instinct made sense, the update was the right response.
