# Joe's 26-Year-Old Brutal Honesty List

## The Point It Illustrates

The discovery that shoulds, not awareness, are what hold bad habits in place. Awareness alone changes most things. The things awareness does not change are precisely the things being held in place by a heavy should.

## The Story

At 26, Joe decided to be brutally honest with himself. He wrote down a list of everything about himself he did not want to admit. He folded the list and put it away.

Six months or a year later, he found the list and went through it. Most of the items had changed.

"And I went through the list and I was like, how many of these things have changed? And remarkably, like, most of them had. And I was like, wow, that's amazing. I did nothing and they just changed. Just the recognition of them changed. Awareness changed them." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

Then he looked at the items that had not changed. Each one shared a pattern.

"I looked through all the ones that hadn't changed, and to a T, each single one of them had a very heavy should attached to it. And that's when I started to realize that this way of managing ourselves by telling ourselves we should do things is just really ineffective." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## What This Story Establishes

Two principles converge in this story:

1. **Awareness is the primary change agent.** Most personal patterns shift when seen clearly. No effort, no technique, no plan. Just recognition. See [[awareness-as-change-agent]].
2. **Shoulds block change.** The items that resist awareness are the items that have a heavy should attached. The should is what makes them sticky. Without the should, the awareness would have done its work. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[shame-drives-behavior]].

The diagnostic flips conventional self-improvement on its head. People assume the harder a pattern is to change, the more discipline is required. The story says the opposite: the harder a pattern is to change, the more likely a should is the problem, and adding more should-energy makes it worse.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a client describes a pattern they have been "trying to change" for years without success
- When a client claims they have "tried everything" and nothing works
- When the conversation is about awareness vs. action and someone is dismissive of awareness as "just thinking about it"
- When the case for dropping shoulds needs an empirical example rather than an argument

## The Implicit Coaching Move

The story embeds an exercise that can be assigned: write down everything you do not want to admit about yourself. Wait. See what changes. Then look at what did not change and ask what should is sitting on top of it.
