# Esme at Whole Foods: "I'm Just Having My Emotions"

## The Story

Joe is at Whole Foods with 3-year-old Esme. Esme is in a full tantrum — kicking and screaming. Joe is not holding her tightly; he is acting as a physical blocker, positioned to keep her from knocking products off the shelves while she has her emotions.

A small older woman, a hippie type, approaches. She is concerned. She bends down to Esme and asks, "Are you okay, dear?"

Esme stops dead. She looks at the woman. In a calm voice, she says, "I'm just having my emotions."

Then she goes right back into the tantrum.

"This like little old hippie lady, she's like, are you okay, dear? And Esme just stops dead, looks at her and goes, I'm just having my emotions. And then she went back into like her temper tantrum. It's fucking awesome." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

## What Is Happening in the Story

A handful of things, each of which is the principle in action.

**The 3-year-old has an accurate model of what a tantrum is.** It is her emotions. They are happening. They are not a problem. She does not need to apologize for them. She does not need to be talked out of them. She does not need to be rescued from them. She is having her emotions, full stop.

**The parent is doing the right job.** Joe is not trying to stop the tantrum. He is not extracting an apology to the lady. He is not embarrassed. He is providing the only thing Esme actually needs in that moment: a container. He blocks the shelves so the tantrum cannot accidentally do damage. He does not block the tantrum.

**The kid has the language for it.** "I'm just having my emotions" is not a sentence a 3-year-old comes up with by accident. It is a sentence that comes from a household where emotions have been named correctly, repeatedly, by the parents. The child has absorbed the framing because it has been the family's framing.

**The stranger means well but is on a different script.** The hippie woman's question — "Are you okay, dear?" — is the standard adult intervention: this child appears to be in distress; let me check if she needs help. Esme's answer is essentially: I'm fine. This is just what is happening. The fact that she can say this at 3 is the diagnostic.

**The tantrum goes on.** Esme is not done. She returns to it. The brief contact with the stranger has not derailed her process. She is metabolizing what she needs to metabolize, on her schedule, in the place where she is safe to do it. See [[kids-release-emotions-where-they-feel-safe]].

## What This Example Illustrates

- **[[kids-release-emotions-where-they-feel-safe]]**: Whole Foods may not seem like a safe place for a tantrum, but with her father present and stable, Esme has the conditions she needs to release. The parent's presence is the safety, not the location.

- **[[hand-in-hand-parenting]]**: This is what stay-listening looks like in practice. The parent is with the child, providing structure (the physical blocking) without managing the emotion. The child moves through the emotion on her own.

- **[[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]]**: The child has internalized "I'm just having my emotions" because that has been the household's actual orientation. The words match the field. If the words had been said while the parents were actually anxious about every tantrum, the child would have absorbed the anxiety, not the framing.

- **[[stay-close-and-insist]]**: Joe stays close (physically blocking, present, not abandoning). He insists implicitly (this can happen here; the shelves are not negotiable; you can have your emotions).

- **The parent does not perform for the stranger.** Most parents in this situation would feel embarrassed and would try to manage the situation for the social audience — extracting an apology, hurriedly removing the child, shaming the child in some socially-legible way to communicate to the audience that the parent is "handling it." Joe is not doing any of this. He is present with his daughter. The stranger's concern is incidental. See [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]] for the underlying principle.

## What an AI Coach Can Take From This

Use this example when:

- A parent presents shame about a child's public meltdown
- A parent asks what to do when a child has a tantrum in a public place
- A parent is being told by others to "control" their child
- A parent is wondering what their child could possibly understand at 3 or 4 about their own emotions
- A parent is presenting the cost of caring about what other adults think about their parenting
- A coachee is illustrating to themselves what the work of a child raised in this way actually produces

The example is also useful as a counterexample to the common script that says young children cannot understand what is happening to them emotionally. They can. They will, if the parent stays out of the way and provides the framing without imposing it.

## The Larger Pattern The Example Belongs To

The example sits inside Joe's broader point about parenting in public, which is that the hard public moments are often the best ones. The airport, the restaurant, the Whole Foods — these are the places where the parent's own conditioning kicks in (people are watching; I have to make this stop) and where the child is most likely to need the parent to stay present.

"Yeah, those are the best moments too." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The public tantrum is also the place where you can identify, by reading the room, which adults were told as children that they could not cry. Those adults will be the aggravated ones. They are sometimes reacting to the child's emotion through their own repressed version of it.

"Have a kid cry on an airplane, then just look around, you can tell who was told they couldn't cry as kids. They're the aggravated ones in the airplane." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The public tantrum is, in this frame, a slow erosion of the parent's protective layer of caring about reputation. The parent who keeps doing this stops needing the reputation. The parent who manages the child for the audience keeps the reputation and loses something else.

## The Logical-Choice Frame That Made the Stance Possible

The third retelling of the story (in the Nathan Baschez interview) surfaces a small detail that did not appear in earlier tellings: before this incident, Joe had made a deliberate, logical choice that his reputation was not as important as his daughter's upbringing. The pre-commitment is the upstream condition for the stance he then took in the moment.

"I had made the logical choice — my reputation is not as important as my child's upbringing. So I don't, whatever, I'm gonna sit here and do this thing." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The naming matters because most parents have not made this choice. Without the pre-commitment, the moment of public tantrum activates whatever conditioning the parent has about being seen by other adults. The parent then manages the situation for the audience, which means managing the kid out of the emotion they are having. The kid does not get to complete the emotional cycle. The kid does, however, get to learn that the parent will sell them out for social approval.

The pre-commitment removes the negotiation in the moment. Joe is not deciding what to do as it happens. He has already decided. The actual decision happened earlier, in a moment of clarity, and the moment of tantrum is just executing on the prior decision. This is the same structural form as the [[pre-agree-on-the-handoff-signal-with-co-parent]] system: the difficult choice is made in advance so it does not have to be made under conditions where good choice is harder.

The pre-commitment also explains why Joe could be relaxed about the hippie lady's intervention. The intervention did not threaten anything Joe was protecting, because Joe had already decided his reputation was not the thing being protected. The audience could think whatever they wanted. The kid was having her emotions. The audience-management slot had been pre-collapsed.

## Boundary Conditions

The example is not a recommendation that every child should be allowed to have a 20-minute tantrum in the middle of any public space. The principle is about presence with the emotion, not about defending the child's right to tantrum without limit. Joe was physically blocking the shelves — there were boundaries. The boundaries protected the environment so the emotion could complete.

The example also presumes a child who has been raised with this kind of framing from infancy. A 3-year-old who has not been raised this way is unlikely to say "I'm just having my emotions" in the middle of a tantrum. The example points at what becomes possible, not what is automatic.

Light. The story is funny on its own. Joe tells it laughing. Hold that tone. The teaching is in the absurdity of a 3-year-old explaining her emotional life to a stranger mid-tantrum. The teaching is also in the parent who finds this delightful rather than embarrassing. Both layers matter.
