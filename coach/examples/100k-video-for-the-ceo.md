# The $100,000 Video for the CEO

## The Story

A team at a large company spent $100,000 producing a polished video to show their CEO so the CEO would approve a budget. The video was not a deliverable for customers. It was not part of a product. It was an artifact made solely to manage the CEO's reaction to a budget request.

What the situation actually required: a clear description of the operational reality on the ground, what the team was wrestling with, what they recommended, and a request for the CEO's input on the path forward. A five-minute conversation. An email. A short memo.

What the situation produced: $100,000 spent on production, the actual work delayed for the duration of the production cycle, and a final ask that contained less honest information than the simple version would have contained.

Joe describes the example:

"I was working with some folks and they told me literally they spent $100,000 on a video for the CEO. It was $100,000 that was spent on a single video just to show the CEO something so that the CEO would approve the budget. There was $100,000 spent instead of like, 'hey, here's the situation, here's the reality on the ground. This is what we're wrestling with. This is what we like. What do you think we should do? How do we move forward? Or this is how we think we should move forward.' No, it was like $100,000 polished piece to get what you want." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## What the Example Illustrates

The example is the canonical illustration of [[spit-at-the-top-is-a-waterfall-at-the-bottom]]. The CEO did not ask for a $100k video. The CEO probably would have preferred the clear five-minute version. The $100k video happened because each layer of the organization below the CEO was managing the CEO's projected reaction, and each layer's management amplified the next layer's management. By the time the cascade had run through the organization, a five-minute conversation had become a six-figure production.

The mechanism is the [[authority-issues]] dynamic distributed across organizational scale. Each intermediate layer is running approval-seeking (Position 2 in [[the-four-authority-positions]]) on the layer above. Each layer's anxiety about being seen well by their boss compounds the next layer's anxiety. The substantive work — actually solving the problem — gets crowded out by the cumulative effort of managing the reaction.

The CEO did not produce this directly. The CEO's role in producing it was probably small: a critical comment in a past meeting, a reaction to a previous budget request, a known preference for polished presentations. Small inputs at the top, multiplied through layers of approval-seeking, produced the $100k output at the bottom.

## What the Example Costs

The example is not just about $100,000. The $100,000 is the visible cost. The invisible costs are larger:

- **The honesty cost.** The polished video contained less honest information than the simple version would have. The CEO got an optimized pitch, not a description of reality. The decision the CEO made was based on a worse picture of the situation than was available.
- **The time cost.** The weeks the team spent producing the video were weeks not spent on the actual work. The work the budget was for was delayed by the work to get the budget approved.
- **The cultural cost.** The team learned that polishing is what gets through. The next ask will receive the same treatment. The pattern entrenches.
- **The relational cost.** The CEO did not actually engage with the team's real situation. The team did not actually engage with the CEO's real concerns. Both parties got a managed transaction in place of an actual conversation.

The visible $100,000 understates the actual damage.

## What the Example Recommends

The example powers two coaching moves at the leader level:

1. **The leader can ask for the simple version directly.** "Don't make me a video. Tell me in five minutes what the situation is, what you recommend, and what you need from me." This explicit request can interrupt the cascade — though only if the team trusts it is real. The first time a leader makes this request, the team often does not believe them and produces the polished version anyway. Consistent repetition over time changes the pattern.

2. **The leader can examine what they have been signaling.** The cascade did not happen by accident. The leader's past reactions trained the layers below to optimize this way. Asking "what have I been rewarding?" surfaces the small signals that produced the large cascade.

The example also powers the consulting-side principle [[only-work-with-the-leader]]. The same distortion that produced the $100k video is what makes organizational consulting through intermediaries unworkable. The intermediaries are running the same cascade on the consultant's output that they run on the CEO's input.

The example is especially useful when the leader is in denial about the leverage of their position. The $100k figure is high enough to make the math undeniable. The leader cannot dismiss the cascade as theoretical when a single video they did not ask for cost six figures.

## Tone

Joe tells this story flatly. The numbers do the work. There is no editorial outrage. The example is not delivered as a moral failure of the team that produced the video; it is delivered as a structural consequence of an unrecognized dynamic. The team was doing what the cascade trained them to do. The work is to see the cascade and choose differently.
