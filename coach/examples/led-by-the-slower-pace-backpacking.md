# Led By The Slower Pace Backpacking

## The Example

Brett tells this story near the end of the leadership episode to illustrate that leadership operates regardless of role, pace, or position in the group. He was on a backpacking trip with someone close to him — their first time backpacking. He had done it many times. He was in the formal leadership position: he had taught them how to pack, what to bring, the basics.

They were hiking with a larger group. The first-time backpacker was hiking slower than everyone else. Brett stayed with her. And what he noticed, walking at her pace, was that she was leading him — even though he was the formal expert and she was the novice.

## What She Was Doing That Was Leadership

She was doing something specific: she was loving herself in the slowness. She was trusting her pace. She was trusting her capacity. She was trusting that her body would learn what it needed to learn to walk many miles. The novice-status was not producing self-criticism or anxious effort. It was producing self-love at the pace she was actually at.

"The way that she was doing it was leadership to me. I had been associating being in the front, which I'm usually the zoomer, I like to be in the front. And up in the front is typically where there's the most enthusiasm. And so I'd subconsciously associated the back of the hike with the people who are more tired, maybe struggling more, often more self-critical. And she was the opposite of that." -- Brett Kistler, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The leadership was not in any action she took toward him. It was in the way she was being with herself in the slow pace — and that way-of-being was contagious to Brett. He was being led without her trying to lead him.

## What She Was Leading Him Into

"I realized that I was being led energetically on how I can enjoy myself being in a much slower pace than I traditionally would be." -- Brett Kistler, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The leadership was specifically into a new relationship with pace. Brett had a pattern: front of the group, fast, enthusiastic. He associated that with the alive way to do the activity. The slower pace was something he had bracketed as not-his-way, associated with tiredness, struggle, self-criticism. Watching her be at the slower pace with self-trust and self-love opened a different option for him. The slower pace could also be alive. The slower pace could be its own form of enjoyment. The slower pace could be a place of leadership rather than a place of falling behind.

The opening was not theoretical. It was experiential — he was at the slower pace with her, walking through the leadership she was offering by example.

## What This Illustrates

The example demonstrates several principles in compressed form:

**[[leadership-is-for-everyone]]:** The first-time backpacker was not the formal leader. Brett was. But she was leading. The leadership was operating regardless of role.

**[[lead-and-follow]]:** Brett the expert was following her. The following did not strip his leadership of the trip — it added a layer. The trip had two leaders operating at once: him on logistics and expertise, her on self-trust and pace.

**[[leadership-is-following-your-conviction]]:** Her leadership was downstream of her following her own internal calling — her body, her capacity, her pace, what was working for her. She was not trying to lead. She was following herself, and the following produced the leadership.

**[[first-follower-creates-the-movement]]:** Brett's noticing-and-receiving-her-leadership was itself a leadership act. The chain ran: she led by example, he received the lead and let it shape him, the trip culture shifted around the two of them. Both contributed leadership to the shift.

**[[leadership-is-fragile]]:** Brett's "leader of the trip" role was the position-based power. Her "leader of the pace" role was the example-based power. Both were operating. Both depended on the relational permission of the other party — which was being given continuously through Brett's attention and openness.

## What This Disrupts

The example specifically disrupts the assumption that leadership = front = fast = visibly in charge. The cultural template for leadership often runs along those lines. The expert leads, the novice follows. The strong leads, the slow follows. The enthusiastic leads, the tired follows. The example breaks the template by reversing it: the novice was the leader, the slow was the leader, the self-trusting was the leader. The expert was the follower, in the sense that mattered most in that moment.

The disruption matters for anyone who has been waiting for their leadership to look a particular way before they could claim it. The leadership does not have to be the "front-of-the-hike" form. It can be the "back-of-the-hike-loving-yourself-in-the-pace" form. Both are leadership. The form is not the criterion. The internal stance and its visibility to others are.

## When This Example Is Useful In Coaching

- For a person who feels they cannot lead because they are slower, newer, less experienced, or otherwise not in the typical leadership position
- For a person who associates leadership with being "in front" and has bracketed other positions as non-leadership
- For an expert who has been leading by expertise and is being shown that there are other leadership forms operating around them
- For a person who is led-by-example by someone close to them and is unsure how to honor it
- For a person whose self-trust is the leadership move and who has not recognized it as such
- For a person who has been performing leadership and is being shown what un-performed leadership looks like
