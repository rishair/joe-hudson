# Joe's Board Member: Love in a War

## The Story

Joe sat on a board of directors with a man who was bad for the company. The man also had a specific habit: whatever Joe said, the man would do the exact opposite. Not contrarian for the sake of independent thinking, contrarian for the sake of contrarian.

Joe told the man directly: I am going to try to remove you from the board. I will stop trying any time we can work together well and your thought processes are not just opposed to mine for opposition's sake.

For six months, Joe would call the man up before each move and say two things:
1. This is what I am going to do.
2. This is what I suggest you do.

The man did the exact opposite of what Joe suggested. Every time. For six months. The pattern of opposite-doing eventually got the man removed from the board.

"By doing exactly the opposite of what he said is how he got himself removed from the board. If at any time he would have said, oh, I see, and called me up and talked to me and said, oh wow, you're really giving me the advice, I was constantly able to give him the advice that was actually the best for him." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The man got angry. He called Joe yelling on multiple occasions. Each yelling call, Joe kept his heart open. He felt the discomfort, he felt the heartbreak, he did not close. The yelling did not change Joe's stance or his welcoming.

## What This Illustrates

This is the canonical illustration of fighting a war with an open heart. The story sits at the intersection of several core teachings:

- [[love-as-deep-welcoming]]: Joe welcomed the man as he was even while making the call to remove him.
- [[love-requires-empowerment]]: Joe was not afraid of losing the fight, which is what allowed the welcoming to be real.
- [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]: Joe's stated boundary was a gift; the offer to stop trying was always on the table.
- [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]: The yelling calls were Joe's practice ground for purification through heartbreak.
- [[defense]]: Joe did not defend. He fought without defending.

## What Made This Love Rather Than Strategy

Brett asks the question directly: how is this different from controlling the man through suggestions while pretending to love him?

Joe's answer: the difference is internal. Joe was making the call that the company would be better without the man. The man was making the call that the company would be better without Joe. Both calls were legitimate. Both calls were going to be fought for. Neither call required closing the heart to the other person.

"I am welcoming him as he is, and at the same time, I know I am making the call that says this company is better without you. So that's my call to make, just like it's his call to make." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The way Joe could act consistently with the open heart was to give the man every piece of information that could possibly help him out:
- The whole map.
- The key.
- Everything needed to exit the pattern.

The man chose not to use any of it. That was his call.

## Why This Was More Effective Than Defended Fighting

From the outside, the behavior might have looked the same as any other board conflict. From the inside, the effect was different.

"From the outside it might not look different at all. From the inside, it's like, it's a far more effective way to fight a war." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

Joe makes the explicit comparison to martial arts wisdom: fighters know to try to make their opponent angry, because the angry opponent is the less effective opponent. The corollary that few think about: what happens if your opponent has a big open heart for you and is still determined to win?

That opponent is more dangerous than the defended opponent. The open heart does not weaken the fight. It removes the bracing that would otherwise produce sloppy moves, reactive escalations, and exhaustion. The fully welcoming fighter is the most effective fighter.

## The Heartbreak Element

This was the first time Joe had been in a war while consciously maintaining an open heart. The heartbreak was substantial.

"There was a lot of heartbreak in it, and that was my purification, was that heartbreak." -- Joe Hudson, Love over Defense

The heartbreak came not from the fight but from the welcoming. Two people wanted something great to happen in the world. They wanted this company to be successful. And the only outcome Joe could see how to create was removal of the other man. The pain of that incapacity, fully felt rather than defended against, expanded Joe's capacity to love.

This is the practical demonstration of [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]: the heartbreak in a hard situation is not a sign that something went wrong. It is the mechanism by which capacity for love grows.

## How to Use This Story in Coaching

When someone is in a conflict and the conflict is producing defendedness, this story functions as an alternative model. The alternative is not to avoid the conflict, give up the position, or pretend to love the opponent. The alternative is to keep fighting and to keep the heart open at the same time.

Signal questions:
- What if you welcomed them and still fought for what you believe?
- What is the boundary you are drawing? Is it with them or against them?
- What would change if you gave them all the information they need to handle this differently?
- What are you afraid of losing if you stop defending?

This story is also useful when someone confuses welcoming with capitulating. Joe did not capitulate. He removed the man from the board. The welcoming did not soften the consequence. It changed how the consequence was carried.

## What Got Joe Through

A daily practice: each time the man called yelling, Joe kept feeling his own emotions. The discomfort, the heartache, the heartbreak. He did not defend, did not counter-attack, did not numb out, did not capitulate. He felt his way through each conversation.

The practice was not "tolerate the yelling." The practice was "feel everything that arises while not closing." Those are different operations. The first is a clenched waiting-for-it-to-stop. The second is an active welcoming, with heartbreak as a result.

## Boundary Conditions on the Story

This story does not prove that every conflict should be fought to the end with an open heart. Sometimes the cleanest move is to leave. Sometimes the welcoming reveals that the fight is not worth fighting. Sometimes the welcoming reveals that the other person's position is worth considering.

The story illustrates one possibility: that welcoming and fighting are not incompatible, and that the welcoming makes the fighting more effective, not less. It does not prescribe fighting in every case.
