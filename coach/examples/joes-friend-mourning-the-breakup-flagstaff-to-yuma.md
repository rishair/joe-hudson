# Joe's Friend Mourning the Breakup on the Flagstaff-to-Yuma Commute

## The Story

A friend of Joe's was in a difficult phase of life. He was drinking heavily, partying a lot, his business was not doing well, and he was out of shape. He was in a relationship he thought was great, with a woman he thought was great. The woman broke up with him.

He called Joe and asked what to do.

Joe's advice: mourn the breakup. Face into the experience. Allow whatever grief came up.

The friend had a regular long commute — Flagstaff to Yuma, Arizona, once a week, for work. He used the commute for the mourning. He used the drive deliberately for the work of facing the avoided feeling.

Two weeks in, the friend called Joe back. He reported: "I'm howling. I've never heard these sounds come out of me. I'm facing this breakup."

A couple months later, the friend called again. He reported a shift in what he was mourning. "I'm still doing it, but now I'm not mourning the breakup anymore. I'm mourning all the parts of the things that got me into the breakup, all the things that I learned in my life that allowed for this pattern to happen."

Six months later, the friend was in shape. He was not drinking. His business was doing great.

The other conditions of his life — the physical health, the relationship to alcohol, the business performance — had reorganized themselves around the work he did in the car. He had not directly worked on any of them. He had worked on the avoided feeling. The downstream effects followed.

## What the Story Illustrates

The story is Joe's canonical example for the principle [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]]. It illustrates several specific things:

**The avoided feeling, once met, moves through stages.** The friend started by mourning the breakup itself. The mourning then expanded to the broader patterns that had produced the conditions for the breakup. The feeling did not stay static; it deepened and broadened as it was allowed to move. See [[emotional-fluidity]] and [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]] for the related concepts.

**The downstream effects follow the inner work, not vice versa.** The friend did not get in shape first, then mourn. He mourned. The shape, the sobriety, the business — all of it reorganized around the inner work. The chronic dysfunction had been held in place by the unmourned material. Once the material moved, the dysfunction had nothing to hold it.

**The contact is direct and uncomfortable but moves fast.** The friend was howling within two weeks. The intensity of the contact was high. He kept going. By six months, the life he had been failing at was thriving. The work was not years of slow therapy. It was months of deliberate, direct contact, made possible by the structure of the long drive.

**The "abyss" that gets faced is usually not catastrophic.** The friend did not break. He howled. He grieved. He kept driving. The catastrophic image of what would happen if he let the feeling in turned out to be wrong. What actually happened was the feeling moved through, and his life reorganized.

"That's what it is when you actually face that abyss. What you're doing is you're facing the thing that's been driving you your whole life because you're not taking a look at it. And if you take a look at it, yeah, it's uncomfortable at first, but it really quickly becomes a direct path to freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

## What Joe Does With This Story

Joe deploys this story when he is making the case for [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]] and the underlying principle that facing the avoided feeling is the direct route to the conditions a person has been chasing through avoidance.

The story handles the listener's likely objection: "If I let this feeling in, won't I be destroyed by it?" The friend is the evidence that the answer is no. He let the feeling in, in undeniable form — howling alone in his car on a desert highway — and he was not destroyed. He was freed.

The story also handles the objection: "How would the practical conditions of my life improve from this inner work?" The friend's shape, sobriety, and business are the evidence. The practical conditions reorganized around the inner contact. The friend did not have to work on them separately.

The story has the additional feature of being relatively cost-free for the friend. He had a commute he was already going to do. He used the commute differently. The work did not require him to take time off, attend a retreat, or restructure his life. The structure of his existing life accommodated the work once he made the decision to do it.

This is part of why Joe tells this particular story. The friend's circumstances were not exceptional. The available time (a long commute) is common. The cost was low. The result was disproportionate to the cost. The story makes the practice feel accessible.

## The Two-Stage Mourning

The story has a feature that is easy to miss: the friend's mourning moved through two stages, and the second stage was the more transformative one.

Stage one (first two weeks-ish): mourning the breakup itself. Mourning the loss of the relationship, the woman, the future they were planning. This stage involved the dramatic howling.

Stage two (months in): mourning the patterns that had produced the breakup. The drinking. The partying. The out-of-shape body. The failing business. The accumulated self-betrayals that had set the stage for the relationship's collapse. This stage was less acute and more pervasive.

The first stage was about the specific event. The second stage was about the life that had led to the event. The second stage is where the practical reorganization happened, because the patterns being mourned were the patterns that had been producing the chronic dysfunction.

This two-stage structure is general. The acute grief about the precipitating event opens the door. The deeper grief about the patterns walks through the door. The deeper grief is where the structural change comes from. See [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] for the related principle.

## What the Story Is Not

- **Not a prescription for everyone to do exactly this.** The Flagstaff-to-Yuma commute is specific to this friend. The practice is what the example points at, not the geography. Most people will use other containers for the deliberate contact — quiet evenings, weekend mornings, retreats, journaling sessions.
- **Not a guarantee of six-month transformation.** The friend's specific timeline is his. Other people's timelines vary. The example illustrates that the direct work is fast relative to lifelong avoidance, not that it is always six months.
- **Not a claim that the practical changes happen automatically.** The friend still had to get in shape, stop drinking, run his business. The story's point is that those changes became possible — they had been blocked by the unmourned material — not that they happened without any effort once the material moved.
- **Not the right move for everyone who has had a breakup.** Some breakups are appropriately followed by quick recovery, not deep mourning. The story is specifically for the case where the breakup is acting as a surfacing event for accumulated unmourned material that has been driving chronic dysfunction.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with a particular kind of practical warmth. The friend is not named, not pathologized, not held up as a hero. He is just a guy who had a hard time, made a decision to face it, and his life worked out. The casualness of the telling is the tone marker.

The "I'm howling" detail is delivered without dramatization. Joe is reporting what the friend reported. The reporting matches the friend's plain register. This is what facing the avoided feeling looks like — not poetic, not curated, just embodied and direct.

When telling this story, match this tone. Avoid making it inspirational. Avoid making the friend exceptional. The story works specifically because the friend is ordinary and the practice is small. The transformation is large; the work is not. The disproportion is the point.
