# The Baboon Leadership Study

## The Story

Researchers put tracking collars on a troop of baboons to figure out how the troop decided which direction to travel each morning. The assumption going in was that the alpha male led the troop. The data did not show this.

What the data showed: anyone in the troop could lead. The least dominant female could be the one the troop followed. The most dominant male could be the one the troop followed. The variable that predicted whether a particular baboon's chosen direction got followed was not status. It was *how* the baboon moved.

The baboons who got followed walked with directness. They moved at a reasonable clip. They moved with confidence. There was no hesitation in the body. When a baboon set off in this manner, the troop followed. When a baboon set off uncertainly, even an alpha male, the troop did not follow.

Joe describes the study:

"They put all these tracking collars on baboons, and I love this metaphor. And every morning, the baboons would come down off the tree or whatever, and they would decide which way to go. And so they were trying to figure out how the fuck they decided which way to go. Who led them? Was it the alpha male? And it turned out it was anybody. Sometime the least dominant female would lead the way, and sometime the most dominant male would lead the way. And so they would sit there and what would happen is like some people would start going this way and the troop wouldn't listen. And then somebody would go this way, the troop wouldn't listen. Then somebody would go. And what they literally found was the baboon that did it with the most directness, cuz that's what you could track. They were just like, yep, this is what we're doing. And they went at a reasonable clip and they moved with confidence. Then the whole troop followed." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## What the Example Illustrates

The example serves several teachings at once.

**Authority is not always positional.** The alpha-male baboon is still the alpha-male baboon. But leadership in the moment-to-moment sense is distributed. Anyone in the group can lead if they move with the right qualities. The formal hierarchy and the live leadership are not the same thing.

"That is the nature of how humans work. Now that alpha male baboon is still the alpha male baboon, but the leadership came as much from him as anybody else in the, in the group." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**Directness and confidence are what get followed.** The example specifies the qualities precisely: directness, reasonable pace, confidence. Not status, not aggression, not seniority. The group responds to the way a person moves, not to who they are.

**The leader cannot impose against the group.** The CEO who wants something to happen and finds it not happening is in the position of the baboon who started in a direction the troop did not follow. The leader is real; the group is responding to other signals. To recognize this without fighting it is the work.

"You can see that a CEO wants something to be this way, but it's not that way. And they're like, 'why isn't it that way?' Because the culture is doing something different. And to recognize that is really — it's quite lovely to, when you really see like, 'oh, if you harness that instead of fight it as a leader,' it's a really amazing, it's magical." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**There is wisdom in the group greater than any one person's.** The baboons are not following the most knowledgeable individual. They are following the felt confidence of whoever is moving with clarity in the moment. The aggregated direction the troop ends up taking is better than the average direction any single baboon would have chosen alone.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

- A leader who is frustrated that their preferred direction is not being followed by their team
- A coachee who believes leadership requires positional authority
- A coachee being introduced to the difference between empowerment and power (see [[empowered-vs-power]])
- A subordinate who is being coached to speak truth upward and needs the model of how truth is followed (it has to be delivered with directness, not with fear — see [[name-the-authoritys-fear]])
- A leader who is over-asserting their positional authority and not getting movement
- A facilitator who is finding that their room is not coalescing around their direction

The example is especially useful when the coachee needs to understand that the *quality* of how they show up in the room is what determines whether they are followed — not the role they hold. This applies upward (the subordinate who speaks truth to the CEO) and downward (the leader who needs the team to coalesce).

## The Connection to Speaking Truth Upward

The example also illuminates why speaking the hard truth to authority figures requires undefendedness. If the subordinate speaks the truth scared, the truth will not be received — the same way the troop did not follow the baboon who walked uncertainly.

"If you're scared, if you say the truth to the authority figure and you're scared, they're never going to trust it. Just like you didn't trust the CEO who's acting out of fear in the top of the room. They're looking for the baboon who's walking confidently in that direction. You know what I mean there? And if you're scared of it, they're not gonna follow." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The principle is general: confidence and directness are what get followed, regardless of which direction the truth is being spoken. Speaking truth from clarity gets received; speaking truth from fear does not. See [[name-the-authoritys-fear]] for the move and the prerequisite of having pre-felt the consequence.

## The Connection to Founder Defensiveness

Joe also uses the example to point at why a founder defending their product in a public talk loses the room. The founder is the one in the room with positional authority. They have what looks like the alpha-male spot. But their defensive posture is the same as the baboon walking uncertainly. The room responds to the body, not to the formal role.

The contrast Joe sketches: a founder who responds to critical questions with "oh wow, that's some really cool criticism. Can you tell me more? How would you improve the product?" would keep the room — that is the directness-and-confidence response. A founder who defends ("my product is great, you don't understand") loses the room, regardless of their status.

## Tone

Joe describes this example with appreciation for what the data revealed. The framing is "this is how it actually works" — not a moral claim, an observation about the mechanics of group movement. The implicit message for the leader: stop trying to force outcomes through positional authority; the live leadership is happening on a different axis.
