# The CIA Sabotage Manual (Decisions By Board of Six)

## The Story

A document published by the CIA (originally the OSS Simple Sabotage Field Manual) describes how to destroy an organization from within without doing anything overtly disruptive. Among the recommendations: ensure that decisions are made by boards or committees of at least six people, requiring consensus.

The point is that the recipe is publicly available because it works. An organization that has structured itself to make decisions by six-person consensus has, intentionally or not, adopted a sabotage protocol. The decisions slow, the conflict gets diluted, the accountability disperses, and the organization grinds.

"Reminds me of that CIA document that we've pointed to a couple of times in the podcast that showed how to destroy an organization is to make sure that it's board-controlled decisions with at least six people. Yeah, reaching consensus or something." -- Brett Kistler / Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Point Joe Uses It To Illustrate

The example is a counter to the assumption that more voices in a decision is always better. There is a specific scale at which a decision becomes either degraded by the number of voices or impossible to make at all. The CIA recipe weaponizes this knowledge.

The application to functional teams: a team needs to know what its decision-making structure is. Decisions by consensus of large groups are usually structurally sabotaged, whether anyone intended them to be or not. Decisions by single owners with the right input from the right voices move at the speed organizations actually need.

This is the design parallel to the principle that voices should be heard inside the team but not weighted equally when the people involved are not contributing to the solution.

"People should all have their voice heard if they're willing to contribute and put effort and they're trying to solve the problem. But if you're just standing on the outside and you think you have the right for your voice to be heard and you're not actually addressing the problem, it's not your thing to do. It might be great for someone to get your advice, you might have great stuff, but you shouldn't have power over the project. So it gets really dysfunctional in teams when people want their voices heard and think that their voices should be heard, but they're actually not contributing to the solution." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The CIA manual's recommendation operationalizes this dysfunction. Make the deciders many. Give them all veto. Require consensus. Watch the organization stop.

## When Joe Reaches For It

Joe reaches for this example when:

- A team is discussing how to make decisions and trending toward "we should all decide together"
- A leader is being criticized for not being inclusive enough in decisions
- An organization is moving slowly and the leader is wondering why
- A founder is designing decision-making structure for the first time

The example provides external validation for the discomfort the leader may feel about the "more inclusion is always better" position. A government intelligence agency has publicly documented that the recipe for destroying an organization is, in part, to widely distribute consensus-required decision-making. The example gives the leader cover to choose the structure their judgment is pointing them toward.

## What This Does Not Argue

The example does not argue against inclusion in decisions. It argues against a specific failure mode of inclusion: requiring consensus among many for decisions that should be made by fewer.

The [[aristotle-project]] at Google (which Joe references in the same conversation) found that teams in which everyone expressed themselves outperformed teams in which they did not. That finding and the CIA manual are not in contradiction. The first is about voices being heard in the work. The second is about decision authority. The first should be high; the second is best held by fewer people who are accountable for the outcome.

A high-performing team has wide input and narrow decision authority. The CIA manual sabotages the second. The Aristotle Project commends the first. The functional team has both.

## Tone

The example is delivered with mild humor by Brett. Joe agrees, then uses it to make the substantive point. The example's value is in the surprise (a government manual on how to destroy organizations exists and recommends this) which makes the point memorable.
