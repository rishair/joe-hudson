# Three Ways to Fire Someone

## The Story

Joe uses three contrasting versions of the same hard conversation -- firing an employee -- to illustrate the difference between defended impersonality, expressed emotion, and the truly impersonal stance.

## Version 1: "It's Just Business"

The speaker says: "We had to let you go. It's just business. Nothing personal."

What this is doing: the speaker has taken the firing personally, feels bad about it, and is hardening themselves to push the bad feeling down. The phrase asks the listener to also not feel anything. It is an instruction to both parties to skip the emotion.

What this communicates underneath: "I don't trust you to be able to handle my telling you the real reason that I'm firing you." (This is Brett's clarification in the episode.) The closure is presented as kindness; it is actually self-protection.

The mark of this version: hardening. The speaker had to become cold to deliver it. See [[hardening-means-personal]].

## Version 2: Expressed Emotion

The speaker says: "I feel really sorry that we couldn't do it. We wanted to and we tried and we weren't able to keep you here."

What this is doing: the speaker is staying in their own emotion through the conversation. This is more honest than Version 1. The listener is given a real human experience to receive.

The limit of this version: it centers the speaker's feelings about firing the person. The listener still has to manage the speaker's emotional state at the same time as receiving the firing. Not bad, but not quite the cleanest move.

## Version 3: Truly Impersonal

The speaker says:

"This was our expectation of what you were to do here. We made that really clear to you, and those expectations aren't being met. My experience is that you're either capable of doing what we asked, in which case you're choosing not to, which means you know on some level this isn't the right job for you and you're making sure it doesn't last. Or secondly, you're not capable of it, and therefore you must always be feeling like you're not doing a good enough job, which also isn't a great thing for your world. So it's clearly not a match. How do we move forward if this job isn't a match? How do we help you? What do you need so that you can get to where you want to go in your life next? And how can we be of service in a way that's mutually beneficial?"

What this is doing: stating the situation as the situation. Naming what is true for the speaker, true for the listener, and true for the relationship. Staying in collaboration even through the ending. Treating the firing as a structural mismatch rather than as a verdict.

The mark of this version: no hardening required. The speaker is open. The listener is treated as capable of receiving the truth. The relationship can continue in a different form.

"That's the least personal of those three things. It's like, this is the situation that we find ourselves in." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

## The Point Joe Is Making

The thing Version 1 is avoiding is the feeling of feeling bad for firing the person. The speaker hardens to avoid that feeling, becomes an asshole to do it. The hardening is the problem. See [[hardening-means-personal]].

The thing Version 3 demonstrates is that the conversation can be conducted without the hardening. The speaker stays with whatever emotion is moving through, names the structural reality, and leaves the listener with usable information and continued relationship.

## When Joe Reaches For This Example

- When someone is preparing a hard conversation and defaulting to "I'll just have to be professional about it"
- When someone has delivered a "just business" message and reports the conversation went poorly
- When teaching the distinction in [[just-business-vs-impersonal]]
- When teaching the diagnostic in [[hardening-means-personal]]
- When the broader topic is depersonalizing money or work in a way that does not produce coldness

## The Generalization

The example works for any hard conversation, not only firing. The same three options exist for breaking up, declining an offer, raising a difficult subject with a partner or family member, ending a contract, refusing a request.

The diagnostic is the same: did you have to harden to say it? If yes, you took it personally. If no, the conversation will land cleaner and the relationship will survive better.
