# Joe's Abandonment Mapping

## The Story

For reasons in his childhood, Joe felt emotionally abandoned. He did not want to feel it. So he built a whole world to avoid it.

In the process of building that world, he kept recreating the conditions that produced the original abandonment. He attracted people who would emotionally abandon him. Over and over. Different people, different bodies, different backgrounds. Same dynamic.

"I created a whole world to not feel that. But in the way I created that whole world, like everybody, I reintroduced it over and over and over again." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## The Original Installation

The imprint installed in the first 7 years. Joe's mother was a working professor at a time when that was unusual for upper-middle-class Connecticut women. She had picked her husband partly because he would allow her to have a career and kids. She was overwhelmed. Her parenting style was scheduled, rigid; she would train Joe when to go to the bathroom so she could change diapers on a schedule. The functional message at a pre-verbal age: my needs are not going to get met.

"It took me probably until my early 30s, late 20s, that I even had needs. You know, when people were like, 'Oh, do you have any needs?' And I'd say, 'Yes, water, food, and shelter. Air maybe.' And I had no idea that there was needs out there to thrive. It was only needs to survive in my mind." -- Joe Hudson, Joe Hudson's Story (Part I)

The emotional needs were also unacceptable. Some of his earliest memories were being made fun of for crying. So the imprint was layered: physical needs are on a schedule, emotional needs are not allowed, and the relational nurturing he wanted from his mother was largely unavailable except when he was sick. Joe enjoyed being sick as a child because illness was the one condition under which he received the attunement he was hungry for.

Joe's specific cognitive frame for what this taught him: "I was learning deeply that I had to do it all myself. That there was nobody who was going to be there for me and that I was going to be emotionally abandoned, that love was emotional abandonment."

That sentence — *love was emotional abandonment* — is the core of the imprint. It is the equation that organized decades of behavior. The adult who keeps choosing emotionally unavailable partners, who keeps engineering distance just as intimacy threatens to land, who finds it nearly impossible to receive support — they are not failing at love. They are succeeding at love as it was originally defined for them. See [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]].

## The Self-Reliance Branch

The other branch that grew from the same imprint was extreme self-reliance. If the people you need are not going to be there, the only sustainable response is to need no one. This worked. It built a career. It also installed the wall that made intimacy nearly impossible later.

"When you get taught you have to be self-reliant, it's really great and it makes you successful in business in many ways, or can. But what it also does is it makes it really hard to let people help you because you never trust it, because you get angry a lot. So there was a whole bunch of anger issues that I had to deal with. There was a whole bunch of self-reliance issues that allowed it really, really difficult for people to be able to support me. Allowed intimacy was really challenging, to let love in was really challenging, all those things, and then constantly creating it so people would emotionally abandon me." -- Joe Hudson, Joe Hudson's Story (Part I)

The same imprint produced the [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] concern that shows up in countless leaders, and the abandonment-recreation loop. They are not two patterns. They are two faces of the same imprint.

## The Specific Mechanism: Anger as the Recreation Engine

Joe later named the specific behavior that did the recreating. The anger was the engine. The moment he sensed he was about to be abandoned, he would get angry. The anger was an attempt to avoid feeling the abandonment that was arriving. The anger then produced more abandonment, because being yelled at causes people to leave.

"I used to, when I was recreating the pattern of emotional abandonment, I would get angry whenever I saw that I was starting to get abandoned as a way to avoid feeling that feeling. Obviously the anger made more people abandon me, 'cause who wants to be around someone who's yelling at them? And therefore I'd invite it in." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Intensity (2022)

This is the same general structure that operates in any [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]] pattern. The behavior that is meant to prevent the feared outcome produces the feared outcome. The avoidance is the mechanism of recreation. Naming the specific behavior matters because it makes the pattern visible: a person can usually identify what they do when they sense the feared thing approaching. That behavior is almost always doing the engineering.

## The Turn

The pattern continued until he was willing to fall in love with the abandonment itself. Not to tolerate it. To welcome it. To want it.

"I recreated people who would emotionally abandon me over and over again until I fell in love with the abandonment. And once I fell in love with it, my system didn't need to recreate it. I had found homeostasis." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

After falling in love with abandonment, the recreation stopped. The body had found its homeostasis. The unfelt emotion was no longer unfelt. There was no need to keep generating situations that might give him the opportunity to feel it.

## The General Principle

This is the operational shape of [[trauma-mapping]]: the things we are most scared of are the things we are subtly inviting into our lives. The avoidance generates the conditions for the feared thing. The body keeps trying to complete the loop.

"The things that we are most scared of are the things that we're subtly inviting into our life. So if we're most scared of feeling helpless, we will invite helplessness unconsciously into our life so that we have that opportunity to feel it." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

The loop completes when the emotion is finally felt. Then the conditions stop being recreated.

## The Pattern Interrupt: The Friend's Sentence

The configuration that had been operating invisibly for decades got named in a single sentence by a friend. Joe describes years of being angry that people would not show up for him. The grievance had the certainty of confirmed experience. The pattern interrupt:

"One day I'm sitting there complaining about how people don't show up for me, and my friend was like, 'Yeah, you make that impossible.' I was like, 'What?'" -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The friend did not argue with the experience. The friend named the configuration that was producing the experience. The sentence was brief, unflattering, accurate, and delivered by a friend rather than a professional. All four properties were part of why it landed. See [[you-make-that-impossible]] for the developed example article.

The recognition opened the work, but the work took years. The integration involved feeling the emotions that had been compartmentalized (anger and sadness in particular — sadness was the hardest at the beginning), welcoming the feeling of abandonment rather than fleeing from it, testing vulnerability in low-stakes situations and being surprised when help arrived, and repeating the test until the body's predictions updated. The Rumi line that Joe quotes from this period captures the testable theory: "all a nursing mother wants is to hear a baby's cry." The universe was waiting to come and provide — but only when the cry was actually cried.

The further mechanism the recognition revealed: the self-reliance was not just refusing help; it was actively sabotaging the help that did arrive. The fear of being let down produced behavior (double-checking, distrust, repeated verification) that made it impossible for help to actually function. See [[double-checking-help]]. The configuration was self-fulfilling — the predictions about people not being there were being produced by the behavior the predictions justified.

## The Surprising Source of Help

Once the configuration started to release, Joe noticed a specific structural fact about where help came from. The first time he raised money — for a short film — the people he had been generous to over the years almost entirely did not contribute. The people who did contribute were other self-reliant givers — people who had never needed Joe for anything, who were positioned as peers rather than as recipients.

"I remember I was raising money for a short film. It was the first time that I ever went and raised money, and all the people who I'd been so generous to, almost none of them helped me raise money. And all the people who were super self-reliant — now that I think about it, I hadn't thought about it this way — they were generous folks, but they never needed me for anything." -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The lesson: relationships in which one person has been positioned as the giver cannot easily pivot to bidirectionality when the giver finally asks. The recipients have been receiving; they are not positioned to give. The asking arrives as a violation of the established shape of the relationship. The help that does arrive often comes from outside the established relational shapes. See [[giving-to-avoid-receiving]] for the developed pattern article.

## The Pattern in Other People

Joe describes the same dynamic in many situations:

- The friend who has been dating the same person six times in a row, in different bodies.
- The person who has picked seven different partners who all cheated on them.
- The CEO who keeps recreating the conditions for the helplessness they have been avoiding.

In each case, the conscious mind is bewildered. "How did I keep ending up here?" The body is not bewildered. It knows exactly what it is doing: creating opportunities to feel the unfelt emotion.

## Why Falling in Love, Not Just Tolerating

Joe is specific about the language: he fell in love with abandonment. He did not just tolerate it or accept it. The body needs to actively welcome the previously rejected emotion for the pattern to dissolve. Tolerating is still resistance, just quieter. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

The language that recurs is "looked forward to." Not endured. Welcomed.

## What This Story Illustrates

1. **[[trauma-mapping]]:** The unconscious recreation of the original wound.
2. **[[what-we-fear-we-invite]]:** Fear of the feeling generates the conditions for the feeling.
3. **[[familiar-over-safe]]:** The body returns to the known emotional baseline.
4. **[[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]:** The pattern dissolves when the emotion is welcomed.
5. **[[childhood-imprinting]]:** The original imprint set the conditions for decades of recreation.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

- When someone describes repeating the same relational pattern.
- When someone cannot understand why they keep ending up in the same situation.
- When someone is fighting the recurrence of a feared dynamic rather than feeling what is underneath it.
- When the concept of "falling in love with" a feared emotion needs a concrete example.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, instructive. Joe uses this as a teaching example. He is not dwelling in the abandonment story; he is using it to show the mechanism.
