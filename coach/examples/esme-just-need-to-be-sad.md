# Esme: "Dad, I Just Need to Be Sad. You Don't Need to Take Care of That."

## The Story

Joe's teenage daughter Esme was having a hard time. Joe started saying something to comfort her, edging toward what he describes as the territory where his caretaking impulse takes over — the territory where he is doing more than being present, where he is trying to make the bad feeling go away.

She named it directly, in real time.

"Just the other night, I remember you were having a hard time and I was saying something to, to comfort you, I think, and maybe make you feel better. I was definitely on that edge where we don't particularly like going. And you looked at me and you're like, 'Dad, I just need to be sad. You don't need to take care of that.'" -- Joe Hudson, Joe and His Daughter Discuss Fatherhood

Joe's response in the retelling: "wow."

## What the Daughter Is Doing

Three things happen in her sentence, each worth examining.

**She is naming her actual need.** She does not need to be cheered up. She does not need a reframe. She does not need to be reassured. She needs to be sad. The need is for the emotion itself, not for the relief from it. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for the underlying principle.

**She is releasing her father from a role he was about to take.** "You don't need to take care of that." She is explicitly removing the caretaking task from his shoulders. This is not "leave me alone." It is "you can be with me without trying to fix this." She is offering him a different way to be present, one she can use.

**She is doing it without softening.** No "I'm sorry but" or "I don't mean to be rude but." Just the direct statement. The cleanness of the delivery is itself evidence of how she was raised — she has the capacity to tell her father something he might not want to hear without padding it with apology. See [[upright-undefended-love]] for the related principle about delivering truth from an empowered posture.

## What Joe Is Doing in Response

Joe does not defend, does not argue, does not get hurt. He recognizes the move and names it as "wow" — the felt sense of being caught in a pattern by someone he loves, and finding the catch worth meeting rather than worth defending against.

The implicit teaching Joe is enacting: when your child correctly names a thing you are doing, the right response is acknowledgment, not defense. The defensive response would have been "I'm just trying to help" or "I want to make sure you're okay." Either of those would have re-centered Joe's caretaking need over the daughter's stated need. The acknowledgment leaves the daughter's space hers.

## The Caretaking Pattern This Surfaces

Joe describes the edge he was on as territory "we don't particularly like going." The "we" is interesting. It suggests this is a pattern that runs in him (and perhaps in his family) where the caretaking impulse activates around someone else's sadness. The daughter catching it is also Joe being given information about his own residual caretaking pattern — even a parent who has done extensive work on caretaking will still have moments when the impulse activates, and the relationships where the activation is most visible are the closest ones.

This is consistent with [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]]: the child surfaces the parent's pattern in real time. The parent who can receive the surfacing without defense gets to keep working on the pattern. The parent who defends gets to keep the pattern.

## What This Example Illustrates

- **[[caring-vs-caretaking]]**: the daughter is asking for care (presence with the sadness) and refusing caretaking (management of the sadness). The distinction is named, in the moment, by the person on the receiving end of the attempted caretaking.

- **The empowered child outcome from emotional parenting**: the daughter can identify what she needs, articulate it without softening, and deliver it to her father without fearing his reaction. This is the parenting outcome that the work is aimed at.

- **Caretaking activates around sadness specifically**: Joe describes the edge as one he doesn't like going to. The caretaking impulse is often strongest with sadness because the parent's own difficulty being with sadness gets activated. See [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]] for a related anti-pattern where the impulse to comfort skips over the necessary work.

- **The teenager as teacher**: Joe explicitly frames raising the children as "one of the greatest teachers that I've ever had." This example is the teaching happening in real time. The teacher is 17 years old. The student is the father. See [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]].

## The Coaching Move Hidden Inside

What the daughter does is, structurally, what Joe does in coaching when someone is trying to escape an emotion. She redirects the person who is trying to manage the emotion (Joe, in this case) back to letting the emotion be what it is. She is teaching her father how to be with her in her sadness, by refusing the version of being-with that is actually about her father's comfort.

For an AI coach observing this: this is the move when someone is asking for comfort but actually needs presence with the feeling. The honest response to "I just need to be sad" is not to comfort. It is to be present with the sadness. The coach who skips this and offers comfort has chosen their own comfort over the client's actual need.

## What Joe Says This Reveals About His Daughter

Joe immediately frames the daughter's statement as evidence of her level of understanding.

"That's your level of understanding." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and His Daughter Discuss Fatherhood

The understanding being named is not intellectual. It is the felt knowledge of what she needs and the relational confidence to ask for it. This is the kind of emotional literacy the work is aimed at producing. A 17-year-old who can do this is, by Joe's reading, ahead of "tons and tons of adults that don't have it."

The implication for parents: producing a child who can say "I just need to be sad. You don't need to take care of that." is one of the meaningful parenting outcomes. Not a child who is always happy. A child who can name what they are feeling and what they need from the people around them while they are feeling it.

## When to Use This Example

This example fits when a coachee:

- Is a parent who feels the caretaking impulse around their child's emotion and is trying to learn how to be present without managing.
- Is a partner who has been correctly identified as caretaking and is trying to understand the difference between care and caretaking.
- Is a coach learning when their attempt to comfort is actually managing.
- Is a person who suspects they have been receiving caretaking when what they wanted was presence.
- Is a parent who is reaching for an example of what emotional parenting actually produces in the child.

## Boundary Conditions

The example is not a recommendation that every sad person should tell others to stop trying to comfort them. It is a recommendation for the parent who can recognize the caretaking impulse in themselves and the receiver who can recognize when caretaking is being offered instead of presence. The daughter has the capacity to name it cleanly because of how she was raised. A child who has not been raised this way may not have access to this move, and may also not have any other move available except acceptance of the caretaking. The example points at what becomes possible, not what should be demanded.

## The Alternate Telling: "Dad, Just Let Me Have My Emotions"

In a later 2025 podcast, Joe retells the moment with a slightly different phrasing — almost certainly the same incident, remembered through a different angle. The phrasing carries the same teaching with a different verb:

"I was having an off day and she was having a hard time and she was like going through it and I was like trying to give her advice to like make her feel better. And she was like, Dad, just let me have my emotions. I was like, oh, fuck." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Fixing Your Partner

The two phrasings name the same boundary from two angles:
- "I just need to be sad. You don't need to take care of that." names the need and releases the caretaker from the role.
- "Just let me have my emotions" names the request and removes the unrequested intervention.

The "oh, fuck" reaction in the later telling is Joe's compressed naming of the recognition. The same recognition the daughter gave him produced the same internal shock the parent feels when a beloved is correctly identifying a pattern in them.

## This Moment as the Origin of a Larger Teaching

The 2025 retelling is structurally important because Joe frames the moment as the origin of a teaching he then generalizes across all close relationships:

"I realized like that is the cause of so much tension in almost every relationship that I see... usually what happens is somebody feels like they're responsible for the other person's emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Fixing Your Partner

The daughter's correction was not just feedback about one parenting moment. It was the moment Joe identified the pattern that produces "so much tension in almost every relationship that I see" — the assumption of responsibility for the other person's emotional state. The example sits at the head of a teaching arc that includes [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]], [[caring-vs-caretaking]], and [[nobody-wants-to-have-sex-with-a-kid]]. The 17-year-old delivered the data; Joe spent the years after that working out the consequences across relational domains.

For an AI coach: this example is not just an illustration of one parent-child boundary. It is the seed of the entire emotional-responsibility teaching applied to romantic partnership. When a coachee is presenting "I'm trying to manage my partner's feelings and it's not working," the example can be used both as a precedent (someone correctly named this in real time) and as a frame (the underlying teaching emerged from this kind of moment).
