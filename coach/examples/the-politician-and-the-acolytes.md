# The Politician and the Acolytes

## The Story

Imagine watching a politician you do not trust. Maybe you actively dislike them. They are on a stage delivering a speech. The whole speech is dubious. You see it. You can hear the moves, the omissions, the bad logic.

Then the camera pans to the crowd. The crowd is full of acolytes. They are nodding. They are saying "Yeah, that's true. Yeah, that's true. Yeah, that's true." Each "yeah" is reinforcing the politician's authority. The acolytes are how the speech becomes power.

Now flip the frame. The voice in your head is the politician. You are the acolyte.

"You have a politician, you probably don't like them, and you watch that politician talk and you see a whole bunch of acolytes going, 'Yeah, yeah, that's true, that's true, that's true.' And you're like, 'Ah, man, that's not true.' Yeah, so the politician is the voice in your head and unless you're not acting like an acolyte, you're probably gonna buy into what the voice in the head says." -- Joe Hudson, How to Break Free From Overthinking

## The Point It Illustrates

The voice does not have inherent authority. Most of its authority comes from the acolyte-response of the listener. The voice says "you should lose weight," and the standard internal response is the acolyte's nod: "yeah, I should." The nod is what installs the statement as truth.

If you can hear the same statement and respond the way you would respond to a politician you do not trust — with skepticism, with an actual ear for the false logic, with no automatic agreement — the voice loses its grip in real time.

The metaphor sharpens what is at stake in the response. The default response is not neutral. It is active reinforcement. Treating the voice as if it were obviously authoritative is the same operation as the crowd treating the politician as obviously authoritative. The thing that holds up is not the speech. It is the crowd.

## How Joe Uses It

The metaphor lands particularly well for people who are politically opinionated. Most people have at least one public figure whose pronouncements they have no impulse to take seriously. Borrowing that same internal stance — the one that already exists for the politician — and pointing it at the voice in the head is a transposition the person can do without learning a new skill.

This is also why one of the experiments in [[experimenting-with-the-voice]] is to nickname the voice after a politician you cannot stand. The nickname makes the transposition automatic. Once the voice has the same name as the person you would never believe, the acolyte's nod is much harder to perform.

## The Connection to Locating the Untruth

The acolyte's nod prevents the basic move of [[untruth-in-every-thought]]. You cannot find the small untruth in what the voice is saying if you have already agreed with the whole statement. The acolyte is committed before inquiry begins.

Withdrawing the acolyte response is what opens the space for the lawyer to do its job. See [[lawyer-the-voice]]. The lawyer cannot cross-examine a statement that the listener has already pre-validated.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When the person reports that they "can't help believing" what the voice says.
- When the person treats the voice as a source of information rather than a process to investigate.
- When the person needs a fresh frame for why the voice retains its power despite years of awareness.
- When teaching the basic move of withdrawing automatic agreement.

## Tone

Slightly mischievous. The politician choice is deliberate — Joe picks the figure the listener will most cleanly reject. The energy of the metaphor is "you already know how to do this, you just have not been applying it inward."
