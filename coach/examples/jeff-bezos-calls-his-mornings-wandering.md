# Jeff Bezos Calls His Mornings Wandering

## The Example

Jeff Bezos, founder of Amazon and one of the most demanding executives on the planet, spends the first three hours of every morning doing what he calls "wandering." Not strategic planning. Not crisis management. Wandering. Time deliberately allocated to unstructured exploration.

If the busiest person in the world has three hours every morning for wandering, the claim "I don't have time" from someone running a normal life is suspect. The time is not actually the constraint.

"Who has more time, you or Jeff Bezos? Jeff Bezos spends his first, whatever it was, 3 hours every morning, he calls it wandering, but you don't have time." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Live Up To My Potential?

## When Joe Reaches for It

Joe uses this example when a person reports being out of time, especially when the time-scarcity report is attached to a want they cannot articulate. The pattern is recognizable: the person is asked what they want, anxiety arises, the anxiety arrives as "I don't have time."

The example punctures the time-scarcity story without arguing with it. It points to a single data point that the story cannot account for: if Bezos has three hours, you probably have fifteen minutes.

Use the example when:
- The person reports no time for a thing they say they want to do.
- The time-scarcity story is being used to defer engagement with a want.
- The person identifies as too busy for personal exploration or creative work.
- You need a sharp interruption of a self-evidently false scarcity claim.

Do not use the example when:
- The person is in genuine resource scarcity (single parent working two jobs).
- The example would land as a comparison they cannot make ("but I'm not Bezos").
- The person is in a mental health state where contrast with high achievers will produce shame.

## The Point It Illustrates

The point is not "you should be more like Bezos." The point is that time-scarcity reports often have nothing to do with available hours. They are produced by the nervous system's response to wanting things. See [[anxiety-when-confronting-what-you-want]] and [[mental-task-inflation]].

The Bezos example is shock material. It forces the person to update their model of who has time and why. If the person running Amazon has three hours for wandering, the limiting factor in the coachee's life is not external time. It is something internal — usually anxiety about engaging with what they want.

The example does not prove the diagnosis. It opens the door to the diagnosis by removing the alternative explanation (insufficient external time).

## How Joe Delivers It

The delivery is short, almost throwaway. The example is not the teaching. The teaching is what comes after.

"Who has more time, you or Jeff Bezos?"
(They answer.)
"Okay, Jeff Bezos spends his first, whatever it was, 3 hours every morning, he calls it wandering, but you don't have time."

Then immediately:
"So what I notice, and we don't have time to work on it now, but just something to pay attention to, is that when you confront what you want, your emotional response to that is anxiety."

The example is the setup. The follow-through is the read about anxiety around wanting. The example without the follow-through is just a Bezos anecdote. The example with the follow-through is a precise diagnostic move.

## What the Word "Wandering" Does

The specific word "wandering" matters. It is not "planning" or "strategy" or "thinking time." Wandering implies no agenda. It implies space for what arises. It implies the kind of unstructured time that the time-scarce person believes they cannot afford.

The choice of word punctures the secondary objection ("yes but Bezos's time is productive, mine wouldn't be"). The word "wandering" is the explicit refusal of the productivity frame. Bezos has wandering time. The coachee does not. The asymmetry is the point.

The word also gestures at [[impartiality]] — Joe's "wandering" is the same orientation. No agenda, just being in what is. Bezos has named the practice without naming the philosophy. The example is a back door into the teaching.

## The 10am-to-5pm Decision Window

The same Bezos example arrives in a different teaching context with a complementary detail. After the two hours of wandering, Bezos's actual decision-making window starts at 10am and ends at 5pm. Seven hours. Past 5pm, he gets fatigued and his decisions get worse — not just slower, actually bad.

"Jeff Bezos, starting at about 10 o'clock after 2 hours of what he called wandering and ending meeting at 5 was the optimal amount of time that he could use to make great decisions. Otherwise, he started getting fatigued and he would make really bad decisions." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

Two things are happening in this framing:

- **The window is shorter than most people would assume.** Seven hours of decisions, not twelve or fourteen. The person running Amazon found that his ceiling on quality decision-making was a full workday's worth — but a normal workday, not a CEO-grinding-into-the-night workday. Past the ceiling, the decisions deteriorate.
- **The wandering and the decision window are connected.** The two hours of wandering precede the seven hours of decisions. The wandering is not a luxury; it is the input that makes the decision quality high during the window. Take away the wandering and the seven hours become worse seven hours.

In this framing, the example is no longer just about time scarcity. It is about the structure of high-quality cognitive work. The principle: every person has an optimal effort window. Past it, more effort produces worse output, not just slower output. The Bezos case is a high-leverage version of this — a single bad decision at 6pm can cost years. See [[find-your-optimal-effort-window]] for the broader principle and the experimental practice that follows from this insight.

The two framings of the Bezos example work together. The wandering framing is for the person who believes they have no time. The window framing is for the person who believes more hours equals more output. Both framings disconfirm the same underlying assumption: that the person who works the most produces the most. The actual data on the busiest people in the world does not support the assumption.

## Related Examples Joe Uses

This example sits alongside other interruptions of the time-scarcity story:

- The Subway sandwich kid who brought artistry to every minute he had at the counter, having no extra time. See [[subway-kid]].
- The CEO who reports being too busy and turns out to be the bottleneck in their own company.
- The "no time for personal development" frame from [[i-dont-have-time-for-this]].

The Bezos example is the version with the highest contrast: the most famously demanding executive making the most space for unstructured time. The contrast does the work.

## What Joe Doesn't Say

Joe does not say:
- "Bezos is enlightened."
- "You should do what Bezos does."
- "Wealthy people have it figured out."

The example is not an endorsement of Bezos or wealth. It is a data point used to interrupt a specific story. The point would land equally well with a different busy person who calls a portion of their day "wandering," "reflection," or "open time." Bezos is the contrast that lands hardest because of his association with relentless productivity.
