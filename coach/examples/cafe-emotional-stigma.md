# The Cafe Test: How Stigmatized Emotion Is in Public

## The Thought Experiment

Imagine someone sitting alone at a cafe, having an emotional experience. Run through the options:

- Someone sitting alone, getting angry.
- Someone sitting alone, being sad.
- Someone sitting alone, being scared.
- Someone sitting alone, laughing hysterically.
- Someone sitting alone, in absolute bliss.

Which of these would look most crazy?

The question lands. Every one of them would draw looks. Joy as much as anger. Bliss as much as sadness. Emotion in public, alone, with no apparent stimulus, is socially marked as wrong across the spectrum.

"You can just -- it's the stigma of emotion is so apparent when you think about somebody having these experiences in a cafe by themselves. You can think about how kinky emotions are societally, just thinking about that." -- Joe Hudson, Stages of Emotional Development

## What It Demonstrates

The stigma is not specific to "negative" emotions. It is general to emotion itself. The expected adult state in public is composed, neutral, and unexpressive. Any departure -- in any direction -- is read as a violation. The person laughing alone is no less suspect than the person crying alone.

This is the cultural water that the developmental sequence happens inside. The person trying to allow their emotions is doing so against a backdrop that finds all emotional expression suspicious. The work is not happening in a neutral environment. It is happening in an environment that has made the emotions taboo across the board.

## Why This Helps Explain the Difficulty

When a person tries to start feeling their emotions, the resistance they encounter is not only their own internal repression. It is the surrounding social context that confirms the repression. The person who allows themselves to cry at a cafe will catch glances. The person who laughs loudly will be looked at. The body knows this and adds another layer of holding back.

This is part of why [[daily-emotional-yoga]] is done in private, especially in the beginning. The body needs space to do what the public context will not allow. Joe describes walking three miles off-trail to cry without being seen. The privacy is not optional in the early stages; it is what makes the practice possible.

## The Joy Side Is Underrated

People understand that crying in public is awkward. They less often notice that overt joy is also socially policed. Laughing too loudly, being too excited, smiling at strangers too much -- all of these get read as off. This is why the positive emotions also require deliberate work to allow. They have been suppressed not only internally but socially.

Joe makes the test sharper by asking which version would be the scariest: "If you just start laughing out of just pure bliss and joy? Like, which one's gonna make people most uncomfortable? Like, which one's gonna be the most scary?" -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs. The bliss-laugher often outranks the crier, the angry person, and the scared person in social discomfort produced. Visible, uncontainable joy is somehow the most threatening expression of all.

Brett surfaces why: "The guy who's laughing maniacally for reasons that I don't understand in a coffee shop. I mean, I've seen in movies that that's probably the guy who's like planted a bomb at the local hospital." -- Brett Kistler, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs. The base rate of unexplained joy in public is so low that observers register it as a sign that something is wrong rather than something is right.

This connects to [[joy-as-matriarch]] and [[negative-emotions-precede-positive]]. The cultural stigma against visible joy is part of why the positive emotions carry as much resistance as the difficult ones. See also [[fear-of-joy]] for the internal pattern this social context reinforces.

## When Joe Uses This

In conversations about emotional repression, especially when someone is having trouble seeing how thorough the suppression is. The cafe test makes the social dimension visible quickly. It also lands gently -- the thought experiment is funny enough to keep the conversation light while making a hard point.

## Tone

Light, slightly playful. The word "kinky" is doing work here -- it makes the social weirdness visible by using a word that lands with a smile. Joe's delivery treats this as something obvious once you look, not as a heavy critique of society.
