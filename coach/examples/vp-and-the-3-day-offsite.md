# The VP and the 3-Day Offsite

## The Story

Joe was recently with a vice president of a large organization who was skipping the 3-day offsite of the CEO and all the vice presidents. Joe asked how she felt about it.

Her response: she could ask Claude (the AI) what the meeting summary would be, and the summary would be about 80% accurate. She would have gotten 80% of what she was meant to get out of the meeting. Everyone who went would also only get 80% of what they were meant to get out of it. She could tell Joe what was going to happen, how it would happen. It was all a known quantity.

"I was recently with a vice president of an organization, big organization, and she was missing the 3-day offsite of -- is where the CEO and all the vice presidents sit down. I said, whoa, how do you feel about that? Her response was, I could ask Claude what the meeting summary is and it would be 80% accurate, and I would have gotten 80% of what I was meant to get out of it. And everybody who went will only also get 80% of what they were meant to get out of it. She's like, I could literally tell you what's going to happen, how it's going to happen. It's all, it's all a known quantity." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

Joe's commentary: "Oh wow, so I'm just thinking about that. That is each vice president making $2 million a year. Like, how much does that -- how much does that 3 days cost? Like, what did that move forward?"

Brett's add-on: "And what were they distracted from?"

## What the Example Illustrates

This is the highest-status, highest-cost version of [[spin-vs-pedaling]] visible in the modern professional world. A meeting at the very top of a large organization, with the most expensive talent in the company, producing 80% of a known outcome. The remaining 20% is the supposed point of the meeting, but the cost of producing it is enormous.

Several principles converge:

1. **[[spin-vs-pedaling]]:** the offsite was high-effort and low forward-motion. The pedaling was intense; the bike barely moved. The fact that a $2-million-a-year executive could legitimately conclude "I can predict the entire meeting" and choose not to attend is the indicator that the meeting had reached pure spin.

2. **[[productivity-is-the-trees-purpose-is-the-forest]]:** the offsite was happening because offsites happen. The forest -- what the offsite was supposed to be for -- had been lost. The trees of "we hold quarterly offsites" had subsumed the forest of "we need a moment where the leadership team aligns on the deep questions."

3. **[[what-is-the-productivity-for]]:** the question is the unasked one at the heart of the offsite. If "what is this offsite for?" had been asked rigorously by the organizers, the offsite would have been designed differently. Without the question, the offsite became another iteration of itself.

4. **[[invigorated-vs-depleted-as-functionality-measure]]:** the offsite was depleting. The VP could detect this in advance. The body knew. The structure of the meeting was producing depletion, not invigoration.

## The Counterfactual Joe Names

"And how could you have made that super productive where she was looking forward to going and she knew that it was critical to her business? And that's the underlying question. And it's in each meeting, it's in each decision. Is this how we're supposed to be spending time? Is this a great use of my time?" -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The counterfactual is not "she should have skipped." The counterfactual is "the offsite should have been designed so that she wanted to go and knew it was critical." The principle the counterfactual illustrates: the test of any meeting is whether the people who would benefit from it most are looking forward to it. If a critical VP can sit out a 3-day executive offsite without loss, the offsite is not what it claims to be.

## The Frequency-of-Question Point

Joe uses the example to land a specific principle about frequency:

"It's in each meeting, it's in each decision. Is this how we're supposed to be spending time? Is this a great use of my time?" -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The question is not asked once. It is asked in every meeting, in every decision. The reason the offsite reached pure spin is that the question had stopped being asked. Each previous offsite had been added to the calendar by inertia. The inertia produced the predictable, low-leverage meeting that any executive could simulate by querying an AI.

## The Companion Example (The 100 Middle Managers)

In the same teaching, Joe pairs this story with [[100-middle-managers-redundant-vs-safe]]. The two examples illustrate the cost of unexamined productivity from opposite ends of the corporate hierarchy. The middle managers were doing the wrong work because they had not asked what the work was for. The VP was opting out of work that wasn't producing anything because she had asked the question and gotten an honest answer.

The two examples together show that the principle operates at every level of an organization. Unasked, the question of "what is this for?" produces middle managers making themselves redundant and VPs spending 3 days predicting what each other will say. Asked, the question reorganizes the work toward leverage.

## How Joe Uses the Example in Coaching

This example is useful when:

- A leader is defending a recurring meeting on the grounds that "we've always had it."
- A team has accumulated meeting-bloat and cannot tell what should be cut.
- A person is in [[i-keep-getting-stuff-done-but-nothing-changes]] at the meeting-attendance layer specifically.
- A person needs permission to skip a meeting and the implicit cultural rule is that important people attend important meetings.

The example shows that opting out is not always disengagement; sometimes it is the appropriate response to a meeting that has lost its purpose. It also shows that the better solution is to design the meeting so opting out feels like a loss. Either move is better than passive attendance.

## What the Example Does Not Illustrate

The example does not illustrate that all offsites are pointless. Some offsites are essential. The example illustrates what happens to an offsite when the forest is missing. With the forest, the same 3 days can be transformative. Without it, the 3 days are the most expensive group simulation of a known outcome that the company will produce that quarter.
