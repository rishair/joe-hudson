# Joe's Daughter and the New Boyfriend (Avoidance of Hurt Invites Hurt)

## The Story

Joe's daughter has been dating someone new and she is really liking him. She learns about a previous girlfriend or fling of his. The information sets off a fear: maybe he is playing her, maybe he is not into her as much as she is into him.

She handles it in a way that looks like good communication on the surface. She tells him: this is what I am afraid of, what is going on? He responds well -- says he is committed, wants her to be his girlfriend. She is reassured.

Telling Joe about it over dinner, she does the conversational version: she defends the boyfriend a little too much, justifies that he is not going to hurt her. Joe notices the defense. He asks: what are you doing? Why do you need to defend him? What is really going on?

She sits with it, then starts to cry. The recognition: this is my own fear. This is me being scared I am going to get hurt, and I am trying to avoid getting hurt.

## What Joe Then Teaches

In this particular case it worked out -- the boyfriend was, by his own account, actually committed. But Joe uses the moment to point at the underlying mechanism:

"Yeah, so I want to teach you something. In this particular case, it worked out well, but the reality is that acting on that fear made it more likely that he was going to abandon you... Because you're more likely to come off needy and she has broken up with a lot of guys who are needy. So she knows that world, right? And so you're more likely to come off needy, which means somebody who's self-possessed isn't going to be attracted to you. And you also ended up pushing, potentially could have pushed him into something that he wasn't ready for. And the other thing is that, like, if you can take or leave him, if you can be in that fear yourself and, you know, learn to welcome it, then you're self-possessed, and that's a harder person to leave, right?" -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

The action she took (the reaching toward the boyfriend, the seeking of reassurance, the questioning, the post-hoc defending) was driven by an unwillingness to feel the abandonment fear inside her own body. The reaching is the very behavior that produces the outcome -- pushes self-possessed people away, signals neediness, sometimes pushes the partner into a premature commitment they then break.

The corrective is not to suppress the fear. The corrective is to feel the fear fully. The self-possession that develops from being able to take or leave the boyfriend is itself the trait that protects the relationship.

## What This Illustrates

This is a clean live illustration of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The avoidance of the emotional experience (being hurt, being abandoned) is precisely the engine that produces the experience. The teaching is structurally identical to Joe's own abandonment-mapping story (see [[joes-abandonment-mapping]]) but on a much smaller, more daily scale.

It also illustrates the texture of [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]]. The reaching that signals anxious attachment is the very behavior that pushes the partner away. The recognition is not "stop reaching"; the recognition is "the reaching is the avoidance of feeling the fear; feel the fear instead and the reaching dissolves."

The story is useful in coaching because it is small-scale and benign. In this case the boyfriend was actually committed and the consequences were minor. The teaching lands because the listener can see the mechanism without the catastrophe muddying the lesson.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

- When a person is reaching toward a partner to seek reassurance and naming the reaching as care
- When a person is describing the right-looking communication move but the underlying fear is still driving
- When a parent or coach is showing a young person the mechanism of avoidance for the first time
- When a person needs to see [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] in its small-scale daily form, not just in dramatic life patterns

## The Coach's Move

Joe's specific in-moment move was to notice the defensive overreach in the daughter's telling and ask the open question: "What are you doing? Why do you need to defend him?" The question puts the attention back on the daughter's own system. The intellectual conversation about the boyfriend collapses; the actual feeling underneath becomes available. The crying that followed is what made the recognition possible.

The move is a small one but it shows how Joe coaches family members in everyday conversation: he does not lecture, he asks the question that surfaces the feeling, and he lets the feeling carry the recognition.

## Tone

Tender and direct. Joe tells this story with the warmth of a parent recounting a moment of seeing his kid clearly. The teaching that follows is not delivered as critique of his daughter's behavior; it is offered as a gift she earned by being willing to cry at the table.
