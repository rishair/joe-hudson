# Sarah's "I Love Your Frustration"

## The Story

Joe was beginning to lead a company that was growing in size. The them-and-us dynamic was starting to creep in — the felt experience of being the authority on one side and the team on the other, separated. The early authority-issue dynamics he describes elsewhere were beginning to install in his own leadership.

He hit a moment of frustration. He expressed it. Sarah's response was four words that dissolved the entire structure.

"As I was starting and being a leader in a company of a bigger size, and I was starting to not feel seen and there was like a little bit of a there's the them and us and me and them, that kind of authority issue thing coming up. I got really frustrated one day and Sarah was like, 'oh, I love your frustration.' And poof, all the separation totally went away. It's us, it's us, it's we're doing this together. I have a role, you have a role, but we're humans together." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## What the Example Illustrates

**Being seen dissolves the authority separation.** The structure Joe was beginning to inhabit — leader-and-team, them-and-us — was held together by his unfeltness. The moment Sarah welcomed his actual emotion, the structure collapsed. The roles remained; the separation did not.

The dynamic is exactly what gets named in [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] and [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]] from the receiving side. The new piece this example surfaces is what happens when the welcoming is offered to the *leader*, by a member of the team. The same move that works downward (the leader welcoming the team's emotion) works upward (the team welcoming the leader's emotion). The structure of authority issues does not survive being seen in either direction.

**Frustration is not a problem to manage; it is information.** Sarah's response treated Joe's frustration as data the team needed, not as a behavior the leader needed to suppress. The framing she offered was implicit: this frustration is telling us something important; let's pay attention.

"It's like, 'oh yeah, I love your frustration, Joe. It's telling us that there's something important that we're missing.'" -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues (paraphrasing Sarah's stance)

This is the inverse of the default response. The default response to a leader's frustration is anxiety: did I do something wrong, is the leader going to take it out on me, do I need to manage them. Sarah's response was none of these. The result was that the frustration could be useful instead of just disruptive.

**The cultural conditions that made this possible.** Joe explicitly names that Sarah could respond this way partly because the culture he had built made it possible. The team had been trained — by Joe — that emotions were welcome. Sarah was deploying the culture's stance on Joe himself. The leader had made the conditions; the team used them in service of the leader.

"It's just like, that's where the culture affected me. And I'm sure that happened because I've made the culture a safe place to be able to do that." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

This is the dance Joe describes between leader and culture. The leader shapes the culture; the culture, when it works, then shapes the leader. The two are coupled.

## What Changed for Joe After

Joe names that the experience shifted his approach to leadership in a specific operational way: when things are going wrong in the company, his first move is now to make sure everyone feels seen.

"And I noticed whenever things are going wrong in the company, my first move now is to make sure everybody feels seen." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The Sarah-frustration moment functioned as a felt teaching about what the work actually is. The work is not to manage the team's behavior; it is to make sure everyone — including the leader — feels seen. The seeing is what makes the rest of the dynamics dissolve.

"Feeling seen is not always comfortable. You know, feeling seen sometimes is uncomfortable, but if everybody feels seen, then whatever the politics are, they go away." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

- A leader who is starting to feel the them-and-us creep in and does not know what to do about it
- A leader who is treating their own emotions as something to manage in front of the team
- A team member who is afraid to welcome their leader's difficult emotion and needs the model
- A coachee being introduced to the bidirectional nature of [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]]
- A leader who has not yet recognized that the conditions they create for the team also condition what the team can offer them back
- A teaching moment about [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]] that needs the inverse example: the team that sees the leader

## The Position-4 Reversal

The example is particularly useful as the live alternative to Position 4 in [[the-four-authority-positions]] — the alone-at-the-top leader. Position 4 is what happens when the leader's emotion is not welcome and the team's projection is not interrupted. The Sarah moment is what happens when both are corrected at once.

The Position-4 leader is alone because they cannot be met. The Sarah moment shows what being met looks like: a four-word response that converts the felt isolation into felt partnership. The leader did not have to perform anything. The leader did not have to apologize. The leader had to express their actual state and have it received.

This is also why [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]] and [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]] are not the only Position-4 moves. The deepest move is the one that requires no move at all: be available to be seen, in a culture where seeing is possible.

## Tone

Joe describes this moment with warmth and a touch of awe. The transformation was felt — "poof, all the separation totally went away." The framing is not "Sarah did the right technique"; it is "Sarah saw me, and the seeing rearranged everything." The example carries the specific quality of a felt event that re-shaped a stance.
