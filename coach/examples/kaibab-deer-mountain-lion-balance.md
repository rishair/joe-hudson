# The Kaibab Plateau Deer (How Killing the Predators "To Save the Deer" Crashed the Deer)

## The Story

The Kaibab Plateau sits on one side of the Grand Canyon. Because of its elevation, the plateau was isolated enough that the deer there had become their own species. Aldo Leopold, one of the early American environmentalists, looked at the situation and decided he wanted to save the deer. The plan was straightforward: shoot the mountain lions that were eating them.

"There's this guy named Leopold. He was on the Kaibab Plateau, which is one of the plateaus on one of the sides of the Grand Canyon. And because it was this high place that animals couldn't get on and off, they had their own species of deer. And as a good early environmentalist, he said, I want to save the deer and I'm going to shoot all the mountain lions. So they shot all the mountain lions. The deer's population went from -- don't quote me, but something like 30,000 to 100,000 to 300,000 to like 600, because there was a natural balance that was with the deer and the mountain lion." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

The numerical arc — 30,000 to 100,000 to 300,000 to 600 — is the entire teaching in four numbers. The deer population exploded because nothing was eating them, then crashed because the deer had eaten through everything the land could feed them. The intervention designed to save the deer destroyed them.

(Joe attributes the story to Leopold and offers the numbers as illustrative. The historical record of the Kaibab deer irruption is contested in its details, but the structural pattern — predator removal producing population irruption followed by crash from habitat collapse — is well-documented in wildlife ecology, including in Leopold's own writing.)

## What the Example Illustrates

The story illustrates a single principle in vivid form: in any living system, what looks like the problem is often the structure that keeps the system alive. Removing what looks like the problem does not solve the problem; it collapses the system.

The mountain lions looked like the threat to the deer. The mountain lions were the structure that kept the deer population sustainable. Without the mountain lions, the deer destroyed their own food supply and crashed harder than predation would ever have caused.

The diagnostic move the story unlocks: when a system has a chronic-looking dysfunction (the deer being eaten), the dysfunction is often the system regulating itself. Intervening against the regulation produces a worse outcome than the regulation was producing.

## How the Example Extends

The Kaibab story extends to many domains where well-intentioned intervention against one element of a system collapses the system:

**Internal psychology.** A person decides that their anger is the problem and works to eliminate it. The anger was holding their boundaries. Without it, they get overrun. The eventual breakdown is worse than any anger would have been. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] — the part of the system being targeted was usually doing useful work.

**Relationships.** A couple decides that their fights are the problem and works to never fight. The small fights had been releasing accumulated friction. Without them, the friction builds. The eventual rupture is the divorce, not the fight. See [[black-swan-small-collapses-prevent-big-ones]].

**Organizations.** A leader decides that a particular conflict-prone individual is the problem and removes them. The conflict-prone individual had been the one surfacing the disagreements the rest of the team was suppressing. Without them, the disagreements go underground. The eventual implosion is worse than the conflict was.

**Society and culture.** A movement decides that a particular group or behavior is the problem and works to eliminate it. The targeted element was often part of a homeostatic structure that other elements depended on. The unintended consequences are often catastrophic.

**Parenting.** A parent decides that the child's resistance is the problem and works to eliminate it. The resistance was the child's developing capacity for self-direction. Without it, the child becomes either compliant or rebellious in worse forms later.

## The Companion to the Forest Fire

The Kaibab story is the natural-systems companion to [[forest-fire-as-system-reset]]. The forest fire story says: the destruction is part of the system; suppressing it makes the eventual destruction worse. The Kaibab story says: the predator is part of the system; eliminating it crashes the prey.

Both stories install the same underlying recognition: living systems are held together by what looks like its threats. The threats are not threats. They are the architecture of homeostasis. The intervention against the architecture is what produces the catastrophe.

Together, the two stories form a two-step argument:

1. Forest fire: the disturbance you fear is the maintenance the system requires.
2. Kaibab: the threat you fight is the regulation the system requires.

A listener who has accepted both is structurally prepared to receive [[shift-the-environment-not-the-actor]] as the operational principle: do not attack the wolf, shift the environment that the wolf and deer both arise within.

## The "Holding Wisdom" Frame

The framing Joe uses inside the Kaibab story:

"All of them are holding wisdom. The wolf holds wisdom, the deer holds wisdom, the forest fire holds wisdom. The environment allows for wolves, the environment allows for deer. And so when you think about the opportunity, the opportunity is in shifting the environment. It's not in attacking the wolf and saying the wolf is bad." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

The phrasing matters. Joe is not saying the mountain lion is good or that the deer should be eaten. He is saying the mountain lion is doing what mountain lions do, and the deer is doing what deer do, and the configuration of the environment is what determines whether the doing produces a livable balance or a catastrophic imbalance.

This is a foundational orientation for the kind of coaching Joe does. The behaviors a person hates in themselves are often doing something. The behaviors a person hates in others are often doing something. The leverage is rarely in eliminating the behavior. The leverage is in changing the conditions the behavior emerges from. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] for the personal-development application.

## When the Example Is Useful in Coaching

The Kaibab story is useful when:

- A person has identified a single element of their life or themselves as "the problem" and is trying to eliminate it
- A leader is about to fire the troublemaker and the trouble is actually surfacing real material the team is otherwise suppressing
- A parent is about to crack down on the child's resistance and the resistance is appropriate developmental friction
- A couple is about to declare one of them "the problem" and the dynamic is held by both
- A person is about to surgically remove a "negative" emotion from themselves and the emotion is holding something necessary
- A leader of a country, movement, or organization is about to eliminate a faction that is doing necessary regulatory work in the system

The conversational move is to tell the story and let the listener see the parallel. The story works precisely because the listener will, on hearing 30,000 to 100,000 to 300,000 to 600, recognize that the intervention did not produce what the intervener wanted. The recognition is then available for transfer to the listener's own intervention plan.

## What the Example Does Not Say

It does not say predators should never be managed. It does not say all interventions in ecological systems are wrong. The story is about a specific kind of well-intentioned single-target intervention against a regulating element of a system, and the predictable failure of that intervention. Other kinds of management (corridor protection, invasive species removal, restoration of historical fire regimes) sometimes do produce the intended outcomes.

It does not say wolves never need to be killed in individual cases. The principle is about the systems-level pattern, not about every individual decision.

It does not say humans should not intervene in their internal or relational systems. The principle is about how to think about the intervention. Intervening at the environment level (the conditions the patterns emerge from) is different from intervening at the actor level (the pattern itself). The former usually works. The latter usually produces the deer-crash.

## Tone

The story is told with quiet matter-of-factness. Joe does not editorialize. The numerical arc — 30,000 to 100,000 to 300,000 to 600 — does the rhetorical work. The slight self-deprecation ("don't quote me") protects against the listener arguing the specific numbers and keeps attention on the pattern. The "good early environmentalist" framing is important: Leopold was not stupid or malicious. The intervention was thoughtful, well-intentioned, and catastrophic. That combination is the story's actual teaching.
