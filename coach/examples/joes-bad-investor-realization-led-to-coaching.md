# Joe Was a Bad Investor (Which Is How the Coaching Career Started)

## The Story

Joe spent 12 years doing venture capital, primarily in agricultural and environmental investing (see [[joes-transition-from-art-to-venture-capital]] for how he got there). He was, by his own report, a bad investor.

The specific reason he was a bad investor: he was picking entrepreneurs who needed him.

The structural requirement of good venture investing is the opposite. The entrepreneur you want to back is one who does not need you, who probably finds you to be a nuisance, who is going to build the company with or without your involvement and your check is the lubricant rather than the substrate. Joe was selecting against this. His pattern of needing-to-be-needed — installed by his alcoholic father whose love and approval Joe had spent his childhood trying to earn — was operating as the selection criterion.

"What I didn't understand is what you're looking for is an entrepreneur who doesn't need you. That's what you're actually looking for. And my background was to be needed. You know, like that's what gave me some sense of validation. And I see this a lot in the business world today, being useful, showing that I can contribute was a really important part of my identity. And so I would pick things that needed me, which is a really bad way to invest venture money. What you really want to pick is people who don't fucking need you, who find you to be a nuisance. And so because of that, a lot of the investments we were making weren't working." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

## The Move That Followed

The investments were not working. Joe's options were limited. He could not change the structural fact of the bad selection — those investments had already been made. The only thing he could do was try to make the existing investments work by changing the entrepreneurs.

"And so the only thing I could do was to like coach them on changing consciousness. And so through that whole process, I just got into the coaching world of like, oh, here's all the stuff I've learned about myself, and here's how I see that apply to business, and here's how a business tool, if you put a tilt of self-awareness into it, actually becomes a more powerful business tool. I didn't know if it would, but I, I kept on doing the experiments because I knew I needed that. That's kind of self-development. Business had to be a form of self-development for me, or I was going to lose interest in it." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The coaching career began as instrumental. It was the lever Joe had to make his bad investments work. He started bringing the consciousness work he had been doing in himself for years into business contexts because the business contexts were what he was being asked to fix.

## Why The Instrumental Origin Mattered

The instrumental origin is structurally important to how the work developed. Joe was not building a coaching practice from a desire to teach. He was building a coaching practice from the necessity of making business work. The pressure of needing the businesses to actually function meant that the coaching had to actually produce business results. The coaching could not be self-help that made the entrepreneurs feel better; it had to be coaching that made the companies make money.

This is the substrate that produced Joe's specific style of coaching: relentlessly practical, allergic to spiritual bypass, designed to make the work the person was actually trying to do measurably better. The pressure of needing the investments to perform meant that "feel-better coaching" was structurally insufficient. The coaching had to be load-bearing for the business.

The principle this points to: the coaching that has to produce results for a reason outside the coaching itself develops different muscles than the coaching that exists for its own sake.

## The Word-Of-Mouth Compounding

The coaching worked. Entrepreneurs Joe coached recommended Joe to other entrepreneurs. People in Silicon Valley started telling other people: "You gotta get coached by this person." Joe found himself with more people wanting coaching than he had time for.

The small groups started. Joe describes the third small group as the one Brett (the host) was part of. The groups eventually multiplied. At some point, Joe was teaching enough that other people were saying he should teach more formally. Joe resisted for a long time. The resistance was twofold: he had watched teachers get stuck — "they're on the side of a mountain holding onto a rope, helping everybody get to where they are, but they're not climbing still" — and he did not yet feel he understood himself enough to teach, having watched people teaching who clearly did not have the understanding and were doing damage.

The teaching happened anyway. The pressure that drove it was the same pressure that drove the original coaching: making the work work for the people who were already paying for it.

## The Joke That Compresses The Arc

"So you did all this to get better at capitalism?"
"Exactly. Capitalism. Yes, exactly. So in short, capitalism has made me what I am today." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The joke compresses the actual mechanism. Joe did not become a coach because he heard a calling to coach. He became a coach because his investments needed him to. The coaching that emerged from that necessity became the work he is now best known for.

The implication is not cynical. The implication is that the coaching practice has its origin in the friction-rich, results-required world of venture investing rather than in the lower-stakes world of personal development. The work that came out of that origin has the particular shape it has — practical, results-oriented, allergic to bypass — because the conditions of its origin required that shape.

## The Pattern This Story Illustrates

This is one specific instance of a broader pattern: calling often arrives not through a clear vision but through a structural necessity that forces the practitioner into the work they are eventually going to do. The conscious mind frames the work as a calling, but the actual mechanism is that something in the practitioner's life was structurally requiring them to develop a capacity they did not yet have. The calling-feel arrives later, in retrospect.

This is the same structure as [[joes-calling-to-have-kids]] — the calling arrived as a knocking, not as a decision. It is also the same structure as [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] — the move that opened up Joe's relationship with money came from a recognition that something else was operating, not from a deliberate decision to change his relationship with money.

The principle: pay attention to the structural necessities your life is putting you in. They are often the leading edge of the work you are going to be doing for the next decade or more.

## The Self-Need Recognition

The other dimension of this story is what Joe is naming about his own pattern. The need-to-be-needed that made him a bad investor was a specific pattern he could see clearly only in retrospect, after it had cost him a lot of investment performance. Once he could see the pattern, the coaching work could also include working with that pattern in himself. The investments were partly the data that revealed the pattern.

This is the same mechanism as the WhatsApp gut-punch story (see [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]]): the body's somatic data revealed an internal structure that the conscious mind had not yet seen. The investments not working revealed the need-to-be-needed selection mechanism. The body, in different ways, kept giving Joe the information he needed to see the patterns running underneath.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When the conversation is about how a career arc actually develops — through necessity, not through vision
- When a client is wondering whether they need to have a clear calling before they can act
- When the principle is being made that the work that has to produce results for a reason outside itself develops different capacities than the work that exists for its own sake
- When the structural advantage of the need-to-be-needed pattern is being looked at honestly (it makes the practitioner valuable; it also distorts their selection)
- When a leader is choosing collaborators by who needs them and the structural cost of the selection criterion needs an example
- When a coach is questioning whether their work has to come from a pure-calling place to be real

## Tone

Self-deprecating and instructive. Joe tells this without claiming a hidden wisdom in having been a bad investor. He was a bad investor. The fact that the coaching career emerged from the bad investing is the structural irony of the arc, not a vindication of having been bad at the original work.
