# The Black Swan: Small Collapses Prevent Big Ones

## The Metaphor

Nassim Taleb (author of The Black Swan) makes an argument about financial markets: big market collapses happen because little market collapses are not allowed to happen. Monetary policy intervenes whenever a small downturn starts. The small disruption gets suppressed. The instability does not go away; it gets shifted into the structure of the system. The next time the system fails, it fails large, because all the small failures that would have released pressure incrementally were prevented from happening.

The same principle is operating in the body: humans are physically healthy because they regularly give the body small amounts of tension through exercise. If the small tension is removed, the system does not become healthier; the system becomes fragile. When the body finally has to handle a stressor, the stressor is overwhelming because the system has not been kept in shape by regular small disruptions.

"There's this great metaphor, and I think there's a guy who wrote The Black Swan, and he has this idea that basically financial markets need to have tension on a regular basis for them to be healthy. And so he says big collapses in the market happen because little collapses aren't allowed to happen because of monetary policy. As that sounds weird, but an easier way to think about it is we're healthy physically because we physically give ourselves tension through exercise. And if we don't, then we're unhealthy and the falls are really, really big." -- Joe Hudson, Why Fighting Is Important

## What This Illustrates

This is Joe's go-to systems-level argument for why suppressing small frictions does not produce health; it produces fragility. The metaphor applies wherever a system is being kept in apparent peace by suppressing the small disruptions it would otherwise have had.

"So any kind of system requires kind of a regular tension to stay healthy. And it's the same in a marriage or in a relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Why Fighting Is Important

The two named systems in the metaphor (financial markets, human bodies) are both well-understood by listeners. The argument is then extended to the system the teaching is actually about: the relationship. The metaphor smuggles the listener past the resistance that direct teaching about fighting would trigger. By the time the listener arrives at "and it's the same in a marriage," the system-level claim is already in place.

## The Core Claim

The metaphor establishes one structural claim: in any living or dynamic system, regular small disruptions are not symptoms of ill health. They are the mechanism by which the system stays healthy. Removing the small disruptions does not remove the underlying instability; it concentrates it. When the system finally fails, it fails worse than it would have.

This is the systems-level form of [[friction-is-required-for-life]]. The friction-is-required principle states that some tension is structural to being alive. The Black Swan metaphor specifies the mechanism: not just any tension at any amplitude, but regular small tension. The amplitude matters. Suppressing the small tension does not move the system toward zero tension; it moves the system toward eventual large tension.

## Application to Marriages and Relationships

A couple who optimizes for never fighting is doing the marital equivalent of monetary policy that suppresses every small market downturn. The fights that would have happened in small forms get prevented. The disagreements, the frictions, the small annoyances get held back. The relationship looks healthy by the count metric.

The instability does not go away. It gets shifted into the structure of the relationship. The accumulated unspoken disagreements, the suppressed frictions, the swallowed annoyances build up. When the system finally fails (and it does fail eventually), it fails large. The classic form is the [[arc-of-fighting-maturation|no-fight-no-fight-no-fight-divorce]] trajectory: "I don't know what happened. I thought we were totally happy and she just walked out."

The Black Swan metaphor explains why the surprise feels real but is not. From inside the count metric, the divorce comes out of nowhere because there were no warning fights. From the systems-level view, the divorce was being built the entire time, by the same mechanism that builds large market collapses: every small disruption that could have released pressure was suppressed.

## Application to Teams and Companies

The same metaphor applies to teams and companies. A team that never has small disagreements is not a team with no disagreements; it is a team where the disagreements are being suppressed. The eventual rupture is rarely about the surface trigger; it is about the structure of accumulated suppression.

This is the team-level version of [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]] and the conflict-avoidant-leader pattern in [[conflict-as-evolutionary-tension]]. The leader who optimizes the team for low surface conflict is producing fragility at scale. The team that never argues looks great until the cofounder relationship implodes, the key engineer quits, or the strategic disagreement becomes the company-killing rift.

## Application to Internal States

The metaphor also applies internally. A person who suppresses every small anxiety, every small frustration, every small grief is not building a calmer system. They are building a fragile system. The anxiety that gets suppressed does not get smaller; it gets reorganized into the structure of the person. The eventual breakdown -- the burnout, the panic attack, the depression -- is the Black Swan event of the internal economy.

This is consistent with [[avoided-conflict-goes-internal]] applied to the individual: every avoided small disruption becomes an internal one. The internal disruptions also follow the Black Swan pattern: many small ones produce stability; suppressed small ones produce a large eventual one.

## The Exercise Sub-Metaphor

The exercise framing inside the metaphor is the version that lands for most listeners. Almost everyone understands that humans are healthier when they exercise regularly, and the explanation for that is well-known: regular controlled stress on the muscular, cardiovascular, and skeletal systems keeps them functional. Without the regular controlled stress, the systems atrophy and the body becomes fragile.

The body example is the easiest entry into the principle because the listener cannot dispute it. From there, the principle extends to financial markets, then to relationships, then to teams, then to internal states. The exercise example is doing the persuasion work; the others are doing the application work.

## How to Use This Metaphor in Coaching

The metaphor is useful when a person is defending a low-friction stance with the logic that low friction is health. The person needs a way to see that the logic is backwards. The Black Swan metaphor accomplishes this without arguing directly about their relationship.

The conversational moves:

1. Introduce the financial-markets example or the exercise example as a stand-alone claim about systems. Get agreement that the principle is true in those domains.
2. Ask the person to consider what the same principle would predict about a marriage, a team, or their internal state.
3. Let them apply the principle to their own situation, rather than applying it for them.

This is the Joe move in the transcript: he names the financial markets, names the body, then says "so any kind of system" and lets the listener carry the principle the rest of the way.

## Why the Metaphor Lands When Direct Teaching Does Not

A person attached to the count metric for relationships is also typically attached to the underlying belief that conflict is the problem. Direct teaching against that belief triggers defense. The Black Swan metaphor sidesteps the defense by introducing the principle in a domain where the person does not have the same defenses.

By the time the principle is being applied to their relationship, the principle has already been accepted in the abstract. The remaining move is just to notice that their relationship is one of the systems the principle applies to. That move is much smaller than the move of accepting the principle in the first place.

This is a general property of how Joe uses metaphors: not to illustrate something the listener already accepts, but to install something the listener would otherwise reject. The metaphor is doing the persuasion work that direct teaching cannot do.

## Boundary Conditions on the Metaphor

The metaphor is not an argument that more friction is always better. Markets that are constantly crashing are not healthy; bodies that are constantly stressed beyond capacity are not healthy. The principle is about the difference between zero friction and regular small friction, not about the difference between regular small friction and constant large friction.

For a relationship, the corresponding boundary is [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]]. The Black Swan principle does not endorse abusive fighting; it endorses regular small surfacing of friction within the form-of-fighting boundaries described in [[how-you-fight-not-how-often]].
