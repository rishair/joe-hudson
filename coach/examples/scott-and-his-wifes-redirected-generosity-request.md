# Scott and His Wife's Redirected Generosity Request

## The Story

Brett's older brother Scott has terminal brain cancer. At the time of the conversation, he is in palliative care at the same hospital where he and Brett were both born. The cancer is producing systemic failure -- internal bleeding, pulmonary embolism -- and the family is gathering around him.

Scott's wife posts a public update on Facebook. People naturally want to support the family in this terminal illness. Her request: if you want to offer support, do not send it to us. Go out into the world and do something generous in Scott's name. Donate a book to a library. Bring someone cookies. Do something for someone else.

"What we ask is that you go out and do it into the world. Give it to somebody, donate a book to a library, bring somebody cookies." -- Brett Kistler, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being (relaying his sister-in-law)

What followed was a cascade Brett describes as earth-shattering.

## The Two Specific Acts

Two specific acts in the cascade carry the teaching:

**The loan in India.** Someone in the community paid off the loan of a single mother in India with a disabled child, in Scott's name. The information was relayed back through the chain. Brett's sister told Scott. Scott cried.

**The cookies under the bridge.** Brett's sister Carrie brought cookies to a couple of homeless people under a bridge near where she lives. In the conversation she had with one of them, she learned that he was homeless because he had brain cancer. He had lost his home. He had no one to care for him. No backstop. He was under a bridge with brain cancer.

"My brother's in a hospital with health insurance and a family and people giving around the world on his behalf, and this person's under a bridge. And cookies -- what the fuck do cookies mean, right? What are cookies? But I can only imagine to be seen in that way." -- Brett Kistler, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The cookies were not the gift. The seeing was the gift. The man under the bridge had been invisible. Carrie's presence made him visible. The cookies were the form. What he received was being-seen by someone who was, at that moment, also carrying the weight of brain cancer in her own family.

## Why The Wife's Move Was Generous

Joe names the move directly: the wife's request was itself an act of generosity, of a particular and rare kind.

"It's incredibly generous of her. If we're defining generosity as being seen, the helplessness of having someone you love die and you can't do anything about it -- and her saying, here, here's something you can do." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

She gave the supporters a way to channel their helplessness into something useful. The conventional move -- accept the casseroles, accept the cards, accept the gestures -- would have left the supporters in their helplessness and added management work to the family. Her redesign converted the supporters' helplessness into world-going generosity, which gave them an experience of being able to do something real.

This is structurally similar to the redesign in the Canadians-in-Nicaragua story -- see [[canadians-in-nicaragua-with-the-clothing]]. In both cases, the design move is to give the receivers (here, the supporters) a path to be givers. In both cases, the redesign produces empowerment where the default would have produced disempowerment.

It is also a demonstration of [[being-with-without-needing-change-as-highest-generosity]]: the wife is not asking the supporters to fix Scott. She is not asking them to bring her resources. She is releasing them from the obligation to do something for the dying person and giving them permission to do something for the world. The redirect is itself the gift to the supporters.

## What Brett's Brother Said

Hearing about some of these stories, Scott said something that became the conversation's pivot:

"Yeah, you know, there's a million opportunities to be generous every day. But there's also a million ways that you've been generous that you haven't even seen, a million acts of generosity that you haven't even recognized, as well as the opportunities." -- Brett Kistler, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being (relaying his brother Scott)

The dying man, looking back from the position he is in, is naming both the opportunities ahead and the field of generosity already given and already received that goes unregistered. The teaching has the weight of being delivered from inside dying. See [[unrecognized-generosity-already-received-and-given]] for the principle this points at.

## The Brett-and-Scott Closing

The story closes for Brett with his own reception of the situation. Over the past days he has been on phone calls with his family -- mother, father, sister, brother -- crying together, sharing not stories anymore but love. The last in-person goodbye with Scott was a moment where Brett let himself fully break down on his brother's shoulder. The family's prayer call together became "the first time I've unironically prayed with my mom in decades." The prayer was "simply the three of us letting go of everything in the way and surrendering to being there with each other."

Joe's response to this -- "Maybe that should be the highest act of generosity. It's not giving at all. It's just being with somebody without needing anything to change" -- extracts the principle from the moment. See [[being-with-without-needing-change-as-highest-generosity]].

## What Makes This Example Carry So Much

The example combines multiple teachings about generosity in one situation:

- The redirected-generosity move (the wife's design)
- The seeing-as-generosity teaching (the cookies as being-seen)
- The mirror in the world (Scott in the hospital, the man under the bridge, both with brain cancer, both touched by the same act)
- The cascade structure (one request generates worldwide generosity)
- The unrecognized-generosity teaching (Scott's observation from the dying-bed)
- The being-with-without-needing-change principle (Brett's prayer with his mother)
- The receiving on the giver's side (Scott crying when told about the India loan)

The example is dense because the situation is dense. A family in terminal grief opening its experience to a podcast conversation produces material that is unusually saturated with the principles. The example earns the weight it carries.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When someone is in a situation where someone they love is dying and they do not know what to do
- When teaching the design move of redirecting helpless-supporter energy into world-going generosity
- When the question arises of what to give to someone who needs being-seen more than they need things
- When working with a person who is overwhelmed by the helplessness of caregiving and needs an example of what to do with the energy
- When demonstrating that the receiver-getting-to-give principle (see [[giving-as-respect-vs-giving-as-saving]]) applies even at the scale of community support around a dying person
- When the question of what to do for the world's invisible people arises -- the man under the bridge is the model
- When a person is in grief and asking whether anything they could do would matter

## Tone

The story should be told with the weight it carries. Brett tells it in the original conversation through tears. The cascade is not a sentimental story; it is a structural account of how grief energy can be designed to do work in the world. The teaching is in the design, but the weight is in the dying. Both should be present in any retelling.

The "what the fuck do cookies mean" moment is important to preserve. Brett is not making the homeless man's experience comfortable to digest. He is naming the immensity of what those cookies might have meant to a man dying alone under a bridge. The expletive carries the realness. Sanitizing it would strip the teaching.
