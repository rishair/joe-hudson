# The VP Who Feared Starting Her Own Company

## The Story

A vice president of a really big organization came to Joe worried about starting her own company. The worry was framed as a risk concern: she might fail. The staying-in-her-role position was framed, by her, as the responsible-safe choice. The starting-the-company position was framed as the risky one.

Joe did not engage the surface choice. He ran a series of questions designed to surface the actual risk calculation, which was inverted from what she had been calculating.

"I was once working with a vice president of a really big organization, and she was worried about starting her own company. She was like, ah, yep, maybe I'll fail if I start this company. And so I started asking her a series of questions. I started saying, well, let's say 2 years you fail. Are you more hireable or less hireable than before? And the answer was more hireable. She had more skills, she understood more, she had more of an entrepreneurial spirit. All those things would make her more effective as a vice president in any big company. So she's more hireable. And said, yeah. And what do you think about your ability to succeed in a big company if you had started your own thing? Oh, I could succeed a lot more. And so there's this perceived risk. There's this potential risk, but it's actually a lot less risky than staying as a vice president in a company. And when we looked at that, it became really clear. I said, like, what's the lifespan of a vice president in your company? And it wasn't very long. It was like maybe 3 or 4 years. She'd already been in it for 2 years. So they fired as a vice president or failed as a CEO -- like, which one's actually the bigger risk?" -- Joe Hudson, The More Stuck You Feel, The Closer You Are To Freedom

## The Sequence of Questions

The example is structured as a worked demonstration of the [[fear-of-consequences-creates-stuck]] mechanism being unwound through specific questions. The sequence:

**Question 1: If you fail in two years, are you more or less hireable than before?**

The answer was more hireable. The act of trying produces skill, perspective, and entrepreneurial capacity that translate back into any future role. The "failure" of the company is not a destruction of her career; it is an addition to her career. The first question started inverting the calculation by showing that the worst-case outcome of the "risky" path produces a better position than the starting position.

**Question 2: If you had started your own thing, what would your ability to succeed in a big company be?**

Greater. The autonomy, the breadth of skill, the willingness to operate without a defined role -- all of these compound the value she would bring back to a corporate role if she ever wanted to return. The second question reinforces the first: not only is the worst-case outcome better than the starting point, but the success-case outcome also makes the supposedly "safe" return-path easier than it was before.

**Question 3: What is the lifespan of a vice president in your company?**

Three or four years. She had been in the role for two. The "safe" position has a built-in expiration. Staying was not the no-risk position she had been treating it as; it was a position with a one-to-two-year remaining horizon, after which she would be in transition regardless. The third question reveals that the "stay" choice she had been framing as the absence of action was actually a choice that itself had a high probability of producing the exact outcome she was afraid of (loss of position) on a comparable or shorter timeline than the venture.

**Question 4 (implied): So which one is actually the bigger risk?**

Fired as a VP, after staying in a position with no growth, with the same skills she had two years ago. Or failed as a CEO, with new skills, new networks, new experience, and the entrepreneurial spirit that makes her more valuable than she was. The framing question collapsed the original safety-vs-risk frame and revealed that the "risky" move was the less risky one.

## What This Example Demonstrates

The example is a worked case of three principles operating together:

**[[safety-is-in-evolution-not-stagnation]]:** The felt sense of staying-is-safe was inverted by the actual math. Stagnation produced a higher-risk position than iteration did. The VP's body was reading the situation backwards; the questions surfaced the reality.

**[[fear-of-consequences-creates-stuck]]:** The VP had collapsed her option-space to a binary (stay-VP vs. start-the-company) and was rejecting the latter because of its perceived consequence (failure). The questions did not engage the binary; they questioned the consequence-calculation underneath the binary. Once the consequence was reframed (failing as CEO is better than getting fired as VP), the binary dissolved.

**[[failure-is-an-idea]]:** The "failure" Joe used in the question ("let's say in two years you fail") was the VP's frame. Joe accepted the frame as the starting point and then asked what that "failure" would actually produce. The answer surfaced that even the failure produced a better position than the staying. The frame the VP was operating under was a construction that did not survive examination.

## What Joe Did Not Do

He did not give her advice about whether to start the company. The work was not the decision; the work was surfacing the actual calculation. Once the actual calculation was visible, the decision became obvious without his needing to recommend it.

He did not engage her fear directly. He did not ask her what she would feel if she failed, or work the somatic layer. The cognitive-layer entry was the right entry for this VP; she could meet the questions and respond from her own knowledge. The recognition was hers, not his.

He did not reassure her that she would succeed. The questions were not optimistic. They were structural. They surfaced what would actually happen in the various possible futures, without making any particular future seem more likely. The reframe did not depend on the venture succeeding; it depended on seeing that the worst-case outcome of the venture was better than the expected outcome of staying.

He did not extend the conversation past the recognition. Once the calculation was inverted, the work was done. The decision, the action, the timing -- all of those were the VP's to determine on her own. The coaching value was in making the actual situation visible, not in advising what to do with the visibility.

## When This Example Lands

The example lands in the coaching room when:

- A client is describing a career or business decision as "stay-safe vs. take-risk"
- The client has the felt sense that the "stay" option is the responsible choice
- The client can articulate what they are afraid of about the "risk" option
- The client has the cognitive bandwidth to engage a series of questions about consequences
- The example's specificity (a VP, an organization, a startup) is close enough to the client's situation to feel relevant

The example is less useful when:

- The decision is genuinely high-stakes in a way the example does not capture (the "risky" move would actually be financially ruinous; the "safe" move has a much longer horizon than the VP role did)
- The client is in the felt-amplification work and the cognitive example would interrupt the body's process
- The client has the resources to remain in the "safe" position indefinitely and the safety-vs-iteration framing requires the longer time-horizon to matter
- The "stay" position is genuinely growth-producing and the client is just afraid of growth -- in which case the example would mis-diagnose the situation

## The Universal Lever in the Example

The universal lever the example demonstrates: ask what would actually happen in each possible future, on the longer timeline, with the realistic accumulation of skills and experience that each path produces. The fear-frame collapses time and treats the worst moment of each option as the permanent state. The questions extend time and let the longer arc be seen.

This is the structural inverse of the [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]] / [[fear-of-consequences-creates-stuck]] mechanism. The mechanism produces stuckness by collapsing the option-space and freezing time at the bad moment. The intervention restores the option-space and extends time past the bad moment. The result is that the calculation the person had been running on artificially narrowed terms can be re-run on the actual terms, and the actual terms usually reveal that the supposedly safe move is the riskier one.

## Tone

Joe narrates the example with the cadence of "this is a clean case I keep in mind." The voice is matter-of-fact: this is what happened, these are the questions, this is what was surfaced. He does not editorialize about whether she eventually started the company. The point is not the outcome; the point is the calculation. The example is offered as a worked demonstration, not as an inspiring story.

The questions themselves are delivered as if Joe is recalling the conversation, with the cadence of natural curiosity rather than coaching-question theater. The "what's the lifespan of a vice president in your company?" question, in particular, has the quality of asking-the-obvious-that-no-one-was-asking. The casual register is part of the move; it makes the recognition feel like a discovery rather than a teaching.
