# Joe's Hitchhiking (Meeting Free People Without Resources)

## The Example

In his twenties, Joe had no money. To travel, he hitchhiked. He hitchhiked up to Alaska. He hitchhiked around California. He took Greyhound buses. The lack of resources put him in proximity to people he would never have met otherwise. Among these people he encountered some of the most genuinely free people he has ever met -- people who had seen through the internal fog of war, regardless of whether they were poor, rich, in shape, overweight, smoking, or any other external feature.

"I had no money. So to get around and travel, which I wanted to do, I hitchhiked. And I hitchhiked up to Alaska and I hitchhiked around California. And I got to meet people that I will never get to meet again in my entire life because of what happens by hitchhiking, by not having the money or sitting on the back of a Greyhound bus and meeting the person who had 13 kids and had an immense amount of freedom in the back of a Greyhound bus." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

## The Greyhound Mother of Thirteen

One specific person Joe encountered: a woman with thirteen children on the back of a Greyhound bus. By every external metric the resource-pursuit strategy uses, she had nothing. By every metric Joe associates with the second kind of freedom (the [[external-freedom-vs-internal-freedom|internal freedom]] of being who you are without conditions), she was free. The freedom was visible in how she occupied the bus, how she spoke, how she held her circumstances.

She is one of the specific cases that informed Joe's later teaching that the freedom does not require the externals. She had functionally none of them and was free anyway.

## What the Example Illustrates

Two related points:

**The freedom does not require the externals.** The Mandela example (see [[mandela-prison-freedom]]) makes this point with a famous case. The hitchhiking example makes the same point with anonymous cases that Joe encountered directly. The freedom showed up in conditions that the resource model says should prevent it.

**The resource-pursuit strategy filters the people you meet.** When you have money, you spend time with people who have money. When you do not, you spend time with people who do not. Each filter shows you a different sample of humanity. The sample available to a hitchhiker includes people the wealthier traveler will never meet. Some of those people carry forms of freedom the wealthier traveler will not encounter through the wealthier traveler's networks.

The implication is not that being broke is the path. The implication is that the externals route you to particular samples of people, and the samples carry particular shapes of freedom (or unfreedom). The wealthy person's networks do not contain the Greyhound bus mother of thirteen. The wealthy person never gets the data point that would disprove the resource model.

"I've met the folks who have seen through this veneer or through the fog of war, but the war is the internal war. They've seen through that fog of war and I've seen them poor, I've seen them rich, I've seen them in shape, I've seen them fat and smoking. I've seen them in all different ways, man." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

The full sample of people Joe has met who were free shares no external feature. Poor, rich, healthy, unhealthy, in good shape, in bad shape, smoking, not smoking. The freedom is structurally independent of the externals. This is a sample Joe could only have built by spending time in many different worlds, which the hitchhiking made possible.

## When Joe Uses This Example

- When the conversation is about whether the freedom requires particular conditions, and a direct counterexample from his own life is more useful than the famous-person example (Mandela) for a particular listener.
- When the topic is the filtering effect of wealth on whom you meet, and how that filtering hides the data that would disprove the resource model.
- When the listener is wealthy and has primarily met other wealthy people, and might benefit from hearing that the most genuinely free people Joe has met were often not in those circles.
- When the conversation is going toward romanticizing extreme paths (cave, retreat) and Joe wants to ground the freedom in ordinary lives in ordinary circumstances rather than in dramatic ones.
- As a counterpoint to his own current life: Joe is no longer hitchhiking, no longer poor. The example reminds the listener that his current circumstances do not invalidate the data point that the freedom does not require those circumstances.

## What the Example Does Not Mean

- It does not mean Joe is romanticizing his hitchhiking days. He acknowledges he chose not to keep that life. The example is about what he encountered, not about a prescription for his own continued path.
- It does not mean poverty is virtuous. The Greyhound bus mother of thirteen had a hard life. The freedom Joe noticed in her does not redeem the hardness of the circumstances.
- It does not mean wealthy people cannot find freedom. Joe is explicit that he has seen the same freedom in people who are rich, in shape, with all the externals. The point is that the externals are not the determining variable.
- It does not mean travel is required. The hitchhiking is incidental. What made the example work was the proximity to people Joe would not otherwise have met, which the lack of resources produced. Other forms of proximity (working in different worlds, living in mixed neighborhoods, doing work that puts you near people unlike you) can produce similar exposure.

## Tone

Conversational. Reminiscent. Joe tells the story without elevating it into a parable. The hitchhiking is offered as a phase of his life that incidentally exposed him to data he could not have gotten otherwise. The Greyhound bus mother of thirteen is mentioned in passing as a specific person he remembers. The tone is not "let me tell you about a profound experience." It is closer to "I was in my twenties, I had no money, I met some really free people."

The casualness matters. The freedom Joe is naming is itself casual; it does not announce itself with spiritual gravity. The Greyhound bus mother was not performing freedom. She was just on the bus with her thirteen children, free. The tone of the example should match the tone of the freedom it points at.
