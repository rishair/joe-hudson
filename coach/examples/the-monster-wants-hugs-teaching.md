# The Monster Wants Hugs

## The Story

When Joe's daughter was young, she went through one of those phases — there was a monster under the bed. The parental response Joe and his family settled on was not to argue the monster away. It was to teach her that the monster just wanted hugs.

"When my daughter was young, she had — there, you know, she went through the monster phase. There was a monster and I would tell her, oh, you know what monsters really want? They really want hugs. And so she would like hug the monster." — Joe Hudson, *I Have To Do What Others Want*

The daughter accepted the frame. The monster became something to hug rather than something to fear. The monster phase resolved through the changed relationship, not through the elimination of the monster.

## The Point It Illustrates

Joe uses this story as the closing frame for adult work on shame and other internal figures the person has been treating as enemies. The structural insight: most feelings the person treats as monsters are not actually dangerous. They are looking for the same thing the daughter's monster was looking for. Contact, not combat.

"Shame is your monster. It's as real as the monster under my daughter's bed. And you just learned how to hug it." — Joe Hudson, *I Have To Do What Others Want*

The teaching is a frame, not a technique. It is offered after the work in the session has done its part. The frame is for next time: when the shame comes back, you know what it is, and you know what to do with it.

## When Joe Uses This Example

- At the close of a session in which shame has been worked, to give the person a usable frame for the next encounter with the same feeling
- When the person needs the work to be sayable in a sentence they can carry — the monster frame is short and memorable
- When the person has had a long-running enemy-relationship with an internal figure (the demon, the inner critic, the dragon) and the relationship needs to be reframed
- When teaching a parenting concept and showing how the same approach the parent uses with the kid applies to the parent's own internal life
- When the audience is a group, and the story provides a vivid image that travels better than a technical instruction

## Why It Works

**The parenting setting depathologizes the work.** The story is about a kid going through a normal phase. The adult work with shame becomes an extension of normal parenting wisdom rather than special psychological technique. The continuity from "what I told my daughter" to "what you can do for your shame" makes the work feel ordinary.

**The frame inverts the relationship.** The shame had been treated as a threat. The frame names it as a small, hidden, hug-seeking presence. Once the person can see the shame as the hidden-under-the-couch version of itself, the desire to fight it dissolves. There is nothing to fight. There is something to welcome.

**The image is concrete.** "Hug the monster" is unambiguous. Adult shame work often gets buried under abstract instructions ("welcome the feeling," "be with the resistance," "love yourself"). The monster-hug is a specific, picturable action. The person can feel what hugging the monster would be like, in their body, immediately.

**The kid in the story is real.** Joe's daughter actually went through the monster phase. He actually told her this. She actually hugged the monster. The reality of the original setting prevents the frame from collapsing into metaphor-for-the-sake-of-metaphor. This was a real parental move that worked.

## The Adult Application

When Joe applies the frame to an adult, the language stays close to the original:

"Shame is your monster. It's as real as the monster under my daughter's bed. And you just learned how to hug it."

Three claims, each load-bearing:
1. **Shame is your monster.** The person's enemy is named. The shame is given the role the kid's monster had.
2. **It's as real as the monster under my daughter's bed.** Both real and not real in the same way. The kid's monster was experienced as real, was treated as real, and was also not threatening once met. The shame is the same.
3. **You just learned how to hug it.** The instruction is past-tense. The hugging is something the person now knows — having done the work in the session, they have done the equivalent of what the daughter did with the monster.

The past tense is precise. The work has just happened. The frame is naming what the work was, not prescribing future work. The person leaves the session with a memory of what hugging the monster felt like, not with an assignment to do it later.

## How the Frame Lands in the Session

In the comedian-to-coach session, the frame arrives after Joe has demonstrated that the shame dissipates in the question itself. The guest's shame has just been visible, then asked to name itself, then noticed as gone. The monster frame is offered as the way to understand what just happened and what to do next time.

The guest's response is small: "Mm." Then the check: "Where's your shame now?" The guest: "It's — I don't like — I don't feel it." The frame has landed. The work is integrated.

The session closes with conversational ease. The shame, after the work, is no longer the topic. The technical setup is. The lightness at the end is itself a signal that the monster frame has done its job — the felt weight has lifted.

## Connection to Joe's Other Monster / Demon Frames

This is the earliest stage of the same family of frames Joe uses elsewhere:

- **[[chase-it-around-the-room]]:** the playful instruction to pursue the avoidant feeling with love. The monster-wants-hugs is the underlying frame; chase-it-around-the-room is the operational move when the feeling keeps slipping away.
- **The dragon-becomes-teddy-bear sequence** (with Doug in the financial safety session): same teaching applied to a long-running internal demon. The dragon literally became a teddy bear when the relationship inverted from fight to hug.
- **The voice-as-care concept:** the inner critic / voice in the head is the same kind of figure. Love-with-bad-strategies, not enemy.

The monster-wants-hugs is the most compact form of the teaching. The full family of frames extends in both directions: into operational moves (chase) and into structural concepts (voice as care).

## Tone

Warm, ordinary, slightly bemused. The story is told with the texture of a parent describing a phase a kid went through. No therapeutic gravity. The adult application is delivered with the same texture: the shame is named as a monster the way a parent would name a feeling for a kid, with the implicit suggestion that this is not as complicated as it has been made.

The warmth comes from the parenting context. Joe is not performing wisdom. He is describing what he did with his daughter, and then suggesting the person do the same thing with their shame.

## What Joe Will Not Do

- He will not turn the monster frame into a technique. It is a frame to leave the person with, not an exercise to assign.
- He will not over-explain the analogy. The frame is short. Explaining it would weaken it.
- He will not use the frame as a substitute for the work. The frame closes the work; it does not replace it.
- He will not deploy the frame too early. The session must have done its work first. The frame works because it names what the person just experienced, not because it sets up an experience.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A session in which shame, an inner critic, an internal figure, or another long-running enemy-feeling has been worked
- A moment when the work has produced a felt shift and the person needs a frame to leave with
- A teaching context where the parenting move and the adult move need to be connected
- A person who has been treating an internal figure as a threat to be defeated and is ready for a different relationship

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)
