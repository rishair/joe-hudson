# You Make That Impossible

## The Story

Joe spent years complaining that nobody would show up for him. The complaint had the texture of grievance and the certainty of confirmed evidence — he had decades of experience of people not being there. His self-reliance was his solution to this experiential fact: if no one is going to be there for me, I have to be there for me.

The pattern interrupt arrived from a friend in a single sentence:

"I remember just getting so angry because nobody would show up for me. And one day I'm sitting there complaining about how people don't show up for me, and my friend was like, 'Yeah, you make that impossible.' I was like, 'What?'" -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The friend did not argue with Joe's experience. The friend did not soothe the grievance. The friend did not list the times people had been there. The friend named the configuration: *you make it impossible for people to show up for you*. The sentence did the work that argument and soothing would have failed to do.

## Why the Sentence Worked

The configuration the sentence named was structurally invisible to Joe from inside it. He was looking out at the people who were not showing up. He was not looking at his own behavior that was producing their not-showing-up. The friend's sentence put the camera behind Joe and pointed it at him.

The sentence has several properties that made it effective:

1. **It was short.** The friend did not explain, justify, or build a case. The bare statement of the structural fact let the recognition land without intellectual scaffolding.
2. **It was unflattering.** The sentence asserted that the person presenting as the victim of others' failures was actually producing those failures. Most coaching language softens this kind of claim. The friend did not soften it.
3. **It came from a friend.** A coach making the same observation might have landed as a technique. The friend making it landed as a friend telling a friend the truth.
4. **It was true.** The sentence was an accurate description of the configuration. False reframes do not produce recognition; accurate reframes do.

The slow-motion click that followed was the recognition of the configuration. The "what?" was the system's first encounter with the structural truth that had been operating invisibly for decades.

## What the Sentence Opened

Joe describes the immediate consequences of the recognition:

"When you have that strong thing of self-reliance where because people didn't show up for you, there's a way that that hurt. That self-reliance hurt that, oh, I have to do this for myself. And so you don't want to open up a door where somebody can let you down again. And so you start acting like you got it all together. I got this. I got it." -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The recognition that he was the one making it impossible opened the door to seeing the mechanism: the "I got it I got it I got it" performance was a wall against being let down again. The wall worked. It also prevented the help.

The further consequence — and this is the harder layer:

"If someone does help you because you're scared of having them let you down, you double-check their work. Do they really have it? Are you really going to be there?... It's like asking a teenager to do something 6 times. They're just like, fuck off. And they don't do it right." -- Joe Hudson, Self-Reliance Is A Trap

The wall was not just a refusal of help. It was an active sabotage of help that did arrive. The fear of being let down produced behavior (double-checking, distrust, repeated checking-in) that made it impossible for the help to actually function. The configuration was self-fulfilling.

## What Made the Recognition Take

The friend's sentence opened the recognition. The work that followed was years of integration. Joe describes the elements of that work:

1. **Feeling the emotions that had been compartmentalized** — anger, sadness, hurt, helplessness. Sadness was the hardest at the beginning. Learning to cry was a major part of the change.
2. **Welcoming the feeling of abandonment** — not just tolerating it but inviting it. See [[joes-abandonment-mapping]] for the developed form: "I recreated people who would emotionally abandon me over and over again until I fell in love with the abandonment."
3. **Testing vulnerability in low-stakes situations.** Joe describes asking for help and being surprised that help arrived: "I need help. And somebody came to help me. I was like, what's going on?" The surprise was the data the configuration's predictions could not accommodate.
4. **Repeating the test until the body believed.** One instance of help arriving did not undo decades of conditioning. The repetition over years allowed the body to update its prediction.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

- When someone is presenting [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] and is locating the cause entirely in other people's failures
- When the configuration is invisible to the person inside it and the camera needs to be turned around
- When a person has been told "you're self-reliant" before but has not received it as a description of their own active behavior
- When the work of letting someone close is the next step and the person needs a model for how the recognition could land
- After the reads of [[self-reliance-emotional-signature]] are in the room and the example illustrates what the recognition looks like

The story has several built-in features that make it useful in coaching:

- It is delivered by a friend, not by a coach. The person hearing it can imagine the same recognition arriving outside the coaching relationship, which models how the work continues after the session ends.
- It is brief. The whole story can be told in three sentences. The brevity matches the structural truth that the recognition itself is often a short event with long consequences.
- It does not dwell on the wound. Joe tells the story to illustrate the mechanism, not to process the original pain. The example is teaching, not catharsis.

## What the Story Illustrates

1. **[[self-reliance-pushes-people-away]]** — the configuration that produces the not-showing-up that the self-reliant person experiences as the cause.
2. **[[joes-abandonment-mapping]]** — the structural context for why the configuration installed in the first place.
3. The general principle that some patterns can only be seen from outside the configuration. The friend was outside; Joe was inside; the friend could see what Joe could not. Coaching often functions in the same way.
4. The further principle that the right sentence at the right moment can open work that years of internal reflection had not opened. The recognition is a discrete event. Looking for the right sentence is sometimes more useful than looking for more time inside the configuration.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, instructive. Joe tells the story as autobiography that illustrates the mechanism. The original feeling state — the grievance, the anger, the years of complaint — is named without re-entering. The story has weight because Joe is reporting from the other side of the recognition, not still processing it.
