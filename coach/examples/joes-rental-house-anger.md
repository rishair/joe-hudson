# Joe's Rental House: The Kick in the Stomach

## The Story

Joe and his wife Tara were looking for a place to live during the financial crash. Joe was holding off on buying because he had done the math on short-term loans and knew the bottom of the market would come in 2011. They wanted to rent in the meantime.

It took a long time to find a rental. They became friends with the neighbors who owned the property. Then one day the neighbor yelled at Joe and Tara's daughter. Tara was not having it. The neighbors kicked them out.

Every time Joe drove past the house after that, he felt it.

"I just got this kick in the stomach. I just got this kick, like, bam. And I would just drive quicker, just try to push it down, try not to feel that experience." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

For some period of time, this was the pattern. The kick arrived. Joe sped up and pushed it down.

Then one day he flipped it.

"And one day I was like, you know what, no, no, no, no, no, I am going to feel this experience. So every time I drove by the house, when I felt that kick, I would just stop the car and I would feel it and I would invite it. And I just like, yeah, okay, I want to, I want to like milk this thing for everything. Like, I'm not going to avoid this, I'm going to run right into it." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

He drove past every day. He stopped every time. He let the kick happen and stayed with it.

About two or three weeks in, the kick stopped. Driving past the house produced love.

"And then all of a sudden it was like I could drive past that house with nothing but love. I had felt through all the pain of it, and there was nothing that I was trying to avoid. There was no more resistance to it." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The story had changed in the process.

"My story changed from these people are assholes who screwed us to I could see their pain, I could see their anger, I could see, I could see all of them. It wasn't just one aspect of them anymore that I was looking at." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

## What This Illustrates

This is the clean example of [[feeling-changes-story]]. Joe did not work on the story. He did not reframe it. He did not try to find compassion for the neighbors or argue himself into understanding their behavior. He just let the body feel the kick every time it arrived.

The story changed on its own. The new story (seeing all of them) was the byproduct of the emotion being felt. The wider perspective was not available to Joe at the start of the work. It was what was waiting underneath the unfelt anger.

The example also shows the practical mechanics:
- The trigger (driving past the house) provided a reliable activation
- The location (private, in the car) allowed the feeling to move
- The frequency (daily) gave the body repeated chances to complete the cycle
- The intention (invite, milk, run into it) was active welcoming, not just tolerance
- The duration (2-3 weeks) was modest -- not months, not minutes

## The Key Move: "Milk This Thing for Everything"

The active welcoming is what made this work. Joe was not just allowing the kick. He was inviting it, pursuing it, wanting more of it. The orientation was the opposite of what he had been doing (speeding up to escape it).

This is [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] in operation -- specifically the "looked forward to" quality that distinguishes welcoming from tolerating. The kick was not bearable; it was sought.

## Why It Worked in 2-3 Weeks

Joe's emotional system already had practice with this kind of work. For someone newer to this practice, the same kind of trigger might take longer to clear, or might require professional support. The 2-3 week timeline is not the universal speed of this kind of work; it is the speed for someone with experience.

What is more transferable is the structure: a reliable trigger, a private moment, an active invitation, daily repetition. These elements support the work for anyone, even if the timeline varies.

## The Story That Replaced the Story

Notably, the new story was not "these are great people who didn't do anything wrong." The new story was wider: "I could see all of them." Their pain, their anger -- the things that drove their behavior -- became visible at the same time as their humanity.

This is the typical pattern of post-release clarity. The change is not toward a positive interpretation. It is toward a more complete one. The kink in the perception unkinked, and what came through was the fuller picture.

See [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]] for why the post-release perception is structured this way.

## The Same Story Told for Enjoying the Uncomfortable

Joe tells this story in different episodes with different framings. In one version it illustrates [[feeling-changes-story]] (the rental-house people stopped being assholes once the anger had moved). In another, told a few months later, it illustrates [[enjoyment-as-path]] -- specifically the idea that the path includes learning to enjoy the very things you have been telling yourself are unbearable.

"It got to the point where like I would literally drive by the house to hope that that feeling would come back so I could be with it and I could love it and I could spend time with it, I could attend to it. I learned how to enjoy the thing that I thought was uncomfortable." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

This second framing is structurally identical to the first. He drove past the house. He let the kick happen. The feeling moved. The story changed. The only difference is the lesson the teller draws out at the end. In one telling, the lesson is what becomes possible when emotion moves; in the other, the lesson is the orientation that makes that movement available -- looking forward to the feeling rather than fleeing it. The two framings are companions, not contradictions.

## How to Use This Story in Coaching

When a person is stuck in a narrow story about another person and the story has not budged despite cognitive work, this example offers the alternate route. The work is not on the story. The work is on the felt sensation that the story keeps reactivating.

The pattern transfers to many situations:
- A coworker whose presence in the building produces a tightening in the chest
- An ex whose name in a message produces a wave of something
- A childhood home that produces a specific body response on driving past
- Any reliable trigger that the person has been avoiding

The instruction is the same: find a private moment near the trigger, invite the sensation, stay with it, repeat daily. The story will change when the body is done.

This example is especially useful for someone who is sophisticated cognitively and has already worked the situation intellectually without it shifting. It gives them a different channel without dismissing their analysis.
