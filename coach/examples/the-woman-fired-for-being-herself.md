# The Woman Fired For Being Herself

## The Story

Joe was working with a woman who was high up in one of the big companies — not the CEO, so she could be hired and fired, which she was worried about. She came to him asking whether she should keep her job or quit.

Joe redirected: "I don't think that's the question. I think the question is, who do you have to be in your job to be happy with your job?" See [[who-do-you-have-to-be-in-your-job-to-be-happy-with-your-job]] for the move.

She took the question on as a practice. Every day she said the things she hadn't said the day before that were true to her. She accepted that there would be political consequences. She accepted that she might make enemies. She decided to be herself.

"She just every day said, these are the things that I didn't say yesterday that are true to me. I'm going to say them today. And she was just full of courage. She said, yep, politically there's a consequence, but I don't care. Yep, I might make enemies, but I don't care. I'm going to be myself." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The being-herself moved through two phases. Phase one was defensive: she was being herself but with a guard up, putting herself slightly above, protecting through the way she was showing up. Phase two was the dropping of the defense — being herself in a way that included no posture, no above-or-below, just the simple version.

"It first started very defensive, like she was herself, but there was a defense to it. There was a way in which she was protecting herself. There's a way she was putting herself above, and she started to let that drop and just started to be herself. So if something hurt, she said, ouch. If she was offended, she said, I was offended. If she disagreed, she disagreed. But she didn't do it with any kind of punch. She just was like, oh yeah, I see it a little bit differently. And maybe she'd ask some how, what questions like we do in the Connection Course." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

And she got fired.

Joe was surprised — by his own report, around 80% of the time the executives who run this experiment rise through the ranks rather than get fired. This was one of the 20% cases.

When Joe asked her how the firing landed:

"I remember talking to her and I said, well, what, what, what, what is that like for you? She goes, oh, it's great. I was surprised again. I was like, 'What?' And she was like, 'Yeah, it's great. To be fired for being myself is the best possible way to be fired.'" -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

What she had learned in the experiment: when she was being herself, people could trust her. The I'm-on-the-outside fell away. The I'm-better-than fell away. The I'm-hiding fell away. The pushing-to-see-if-you-still-love-me fell away. It was all just her. The firing didn't take that learning back.

## What This Story Illustrates

The example is the canonical worked illustration of several teachings at once:

- [[who-do-you-have-to-be-in-your-job-to-be-happy-with-your-job]] — the reframe that started the experiment
- [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]] — the experiment that ran
- [[be-yourself-despite-consequences-undoes-stuckness]] — the principle that grounded the willingness
- [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]] — the world responded by reorganizing (in this case, by reorganizing the person out of the job)
- [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]] — the inversion: this is the 20% case where the feared consequence did happen, and the example is still useful precisely because the person was fine
- [[freedom-is-not-absence-of-consequences]] — freedom and consequences coexist

The example is unusually useful because it covers the failure case. Most worked examples of being-yourself-at-work end with the person rising through the ranks. This one ends with the person getting fired. The point: even in the failure case, the experiment was the right thing to do, and the person reported feeling great afterward.

The reason the firing didn't break the example: the person was not being herself in order to keep the job. She was being herself because being herself was what she wanted. The job was the context, not the goal. When the context closed, the want remained, and the next context would be one that fit better.

## The Two Phases of Being Herself

The example also illustrates a subtler structural observation: being yourself has a phase-1 defensive version and a phase-2 dropped-defense version, and the second one is the destination.

Phase 1: she was being herself but with the I'm-better-than mode active. The being-herself was still a posture against the environment. She was being defensible-herself rather than just herself.

Phase 2: the defense dropped. The being-herself became simply: if it hurt, she said ouch; if she was offended, she said so; if she disagreed, she disagreed. No punch. Just the simple version. She used the [[how-what-questions]] from the Connection Course as part of the move.

The phase-1-to-phase-2 transition matters because many people who try the being-yourself experiment stop at phase 1 and report mixed results. Phase 1 is necessary as a stepping stone but it has its own cost (the people around can feel the defense even when the words are honest). Phase 2 is where the real signal change happens. Phase 2 is what [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]] describes.

## The Reframe of the Firing

The most-quoted line is: "To be fired for being myself is the best possible way to be fired."

The reframe is structurally important. The conventional reading of a firing is: I did something wrong, the organization punished me, this is bad. The reframe is: I did the right thing for me, the organization couldn't hold it, the organization-rejection is information about fit, not a verdict on my value.

The reframe holds because the person had a clear inner referent for what she was doing. She wasn't being-herself as a strategy to be liked; she was being-herself because alignment with herself had become the operating question. See [[am-i-in-alignment-with-me-right-now]]. The firing didn't disrupt the operating-question, so the firing couldn't disrupt the meaning of the experiment.

## When to Reach for This Example

The example is useful when:

- A person is considering a be-yourself-at-work experiment and is asking what might happen
- A person is afraid of the worst-case scenario (getting fired) and the worst-case scenario is what blocks the experiment
- A person is in [[i-dont-belong-here]] at work and the work-version of the teaching is being assembled
- A person has run a partial version of the experiment and is in the phase-1 defensive version
- A person needs to see that the failure case is not actually a failure case
- A teaching on [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]] needs an example where the reorganization closed a door rather than opened one

The example is also useful for executives, leaders, and people in high-stakes work contexts. The "20% case" makes it credible — Joe is not pretending the experiment is risk-free. The structural point is that the experiment is still the right move even in the 20% case.

## What the Example Does Not Say

It does not say firings are always good. It does not say being yourself is a strategy to optimize for any particular outcome. It does not say the woman wouldn't have preferred to keep the job. It says: she did the experiment, the experiment had a consequence she would not have chosen, and the experiment was still the right thing to do because the alternative (continuing to suppress herself to keep the job) was a worse outcome than the firing.

The example also does not pathologize the organization that fired her. The organization did what organizations do when someone changes their signal: it didn't have the capacity to hold the new signal, so the relationship ended. That is just how it goes sometimes. The next context will be a better fit.

## Tone

Joe tells this with a slight grin in his voice. The "I was surprised again" beats are part of the comedy — the coach being surprised by the client's response is itself a teaching about how the work actually goes (you do not control the outcome, even when you have asked the question that starts the experiment).

The tone is direct and grounded. Joe does not romanticize the firing. He notes that 80% of the time the outcome is different. The fact that this is the 20% case is named. The point lands because Joe is honest about the cost.
