# The Nail in the Head (The Woman Who Just Wants to Talk About It)

## The Story

A short YouTube video Joe references as the cleanest illustration of the fix-vs-listen mismatch. A woman has a literal nail in her forehead. She wants to talk about it. Her partner keeps offering to remove the nail. She refuses. She just wants to talk about how painful it is.

"There's this video on YouTube with this woman with a nail in the head and she's like, 'I just want to talk about having a nail in the head.' He's like, 'Can I just remove the nail in the head?' She's like, 'No, I just wanted — this is just painful.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

## The Point It Illustrates

The metaphor works because the visual is absurd. From the outside, the partner's impulse looks completely reasonable — there is a nail, removing it would solve the problem. From the inside the same impulse is the exact thing the speaker does not want.

The function of the absurdity is to make the fix-impulse visible in its naked form. In daily life, the fix-impulse is invisible because the problem being fixed feels real and solvable. The fixer cannot see that the speaker is not asking for the fix. The fixer sees only the problem and the available solution.

The nail-in-the-head image strips that away. There is no ambiguity about whether the fix would work — yes, removing the nail would resolve the physical issue. There is no ambiguity about what the speaker wants — she has stated explicitly that she does not want the fix. And the fixer still keeps offering. The image is funny because the fixing impulse is operating below the level of what the situation calls for. It is automatic.

This is what most fix-attempts look like inside relationships, just less visible. The husband solving the work problem his wife is describing. The wife giving advice about the conflict her husband is venting about. The friend offering reframes when the friend is asking to be heard. In each case, the fixer is operating from the same automatic impulse the cartoon partner operates from. The speaker's actual request is invisible because the program is running.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When teaching the three-option ask (see [[ask-what-they-want-before-responding]]). The metaphor makes the underlying mismatch immediately legible.
- When working with a coachee who cannot see that their partner is not asking for help. The image gives them an external object to point at.
- When working with a coachee who feels frustrated that they keep being told they "didn't listen" even though they offered great advice. The frustration is exactly what the cartoon partner is feeling. Recognizing themselves in the cartoon is what produces the shift.
- When teaching the broader principle that we run programs instead of attuning. See [[attune-to-the-actual-want-not-the-program]].

## What the Metaphor Captures That Direct Teaching Doesn't

The teaching "ask what they want before responding" is correct but does not produce immediate behavioral change in most people. The reason is that the fixer cannot feel the fixing impulse as alien — it feels like care, it feels like usefulness, it feels like love. The teaching arrives as instruction and the fixing continues because the underlying impulse has not been seen.

The metaphor produces what the teaching cannot: a moment of recognition where the fixer sees their own behavior from the outside and laughs at it. The laughter is the shift. After the laughter, the next time the impulse fires, the fixer can name it ("oh, I'm doing the nail thing right now") and choose differently.

This is why Joe uses it as the opener and the closer of the teaching on attunement. The metaphor frames everything that follows.

## Joe's Own Position in the Story

Notable: when Joe references this metaphor, he is describing his own past experience, not the experience of clients. He is the cartoon partner. The frustration he describes about the early phase of practicing just-listening with Tara — "I felt so frickin frustrated, I was like, grrr" — is the felt experience of being the partner trying to pull the nail out while the woman keeps saying she just wants to talk.

The first-person framing matters because it removes the metaphor from the realm of "those other people do this" and locates it in the universal human experience. Everyone has been the cartoon partner. The recognition is not "those bad partners do this," it is "I do this, all the time, and now I can see it."

## Tone

Light, slightly self-deprecating. Joe does not deliver this with a heavy hand. The metaphor is offered as comedy, and the comedy is what allows the recognition to land without shame.
