# The Boeing-to-Ford CEO Who Promoted the Bad-News Bringer

## The Story

A CEO who had previously run Boeing took over Ford. Ford was losing money. The CEO sat in his meetings, and for months, every executive showed up with the same message: everything is great. He waited. Months passed. Finally, one executive came to him with bad news.

The CEO said: "Okay, you're COO."

The promotion was the message. The man who would tell the truth got promoted instantly. The implicit instruction to the rest of the company was unmistakable. The culture of "everything is great" was being replaced with a culture that explicitly rewarded the people willing to surface reality.

"There's a great example of the guy who ran Boeing whose name escapes me, but when he went to Ford, he just waited until somebody gave him bad news. I think it took months and then somebody gave him bad news and he's like, okay, you're COO. Because it was all, everybody was showing up, everything's great. It's like, no, it's not great. The company's losing money." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Point Joe Uses It To Illustrate

A leader cannot just say they want bad news. The culture of "everything is great" exists for a reason: somewhere in the company's history, bringing bad news was punished. The reasons may be invisible to current leadership but they are operative. People will not bring bad news because they have learned bringing bad news is dangerous.

The leader who wants to change this has to demonstrate it, not announce it. Promotion of the first bad-news bringer is one of the few signals that cannot be misread. The signal arrives without ambiguity. The cost (the message about whatever decisions the previous executives made and did not surface) is part of the lesson the company needed.

The example connects to [[trust-as-multifaceted|trust]] in its communicative dimension. The company had no trust that bad news could be brought to the leader. The new leader's job was to rebuild that dimension. Words would not do it; behavior had to.

It also connects to [[no-doctrine-leadership]]: the leader was creating the conditions in which the truth would emerge by rewarding truth-telling and refusing to reward false-positive reports.

## What the Story Reveals About the Old Culture

For months, no executive could surface bad news. This is not a comment on those executives; it is a comment on the culture they were operating in. They had learned, accurately, that bringing bad news got them fired or sidelined or marginalized. They were rationally protecting themselves.

The leader who replaces such a culture has to recognize this. The executives are not stupid or cowardly. They are responding to a structure that punished truth. The structure has to be replaced before the people will trust the new conditions.

The promotion is the structural replacement. The new structure says: bringing bad news leads to advancement. The old structure said: bringing bad news leads to expulsion. Once the new structure is observably operating, the executives reorient. Not immediately; they have to see whether the new structure persists. But the promotion is the first credible signal.

## When Joe Reaches For It

Joe reaches for this example when:

- A leader is asking how to get their team to be more honest with them
- A leader is frustrated that they keep getting filtered information
- A leader has announced they want directness and is confused that the directness has not appeared
- A team is in a culture of "everything is great" reports and the dysfunction has accumulated below the reporting layer

The example collapses the "they should just tell me" position. The team will not just tell you. The team will tell you what experience has taught them is safe to tell you. Changing what is safe to tell requires changing the consequences. Words about wanting honesty are not the consequences; promotions and dismissals and visible reactions are.

## The Subtler Move

The CEO did not announce the new policy. He just waited and then promoted. The absence of the announcement is part of the move. An announcement would have been received as words. The promotion was received as data.

Leaders who try to short-circuit this by announcing their new commitment to honesty get the experience the executives expect. The team waits to see if it is real. If the next person who brings bad news gets the standard reaction, the announcement is filed under "things leadership says but does not mean." If the next person gets promoted, the announcement was retroactively credible.

The order matters. Behavior first, then the announcement is redundant. Announcement first, behavior has to be even more dramatic to overcome the skepticism the announcement created.

## Tone

Joe delivers this as illustrative. The story is short. The point is sharp. The example is a hand-pointing-at-the-moon: do not get caught up in the specific company or executive; see the structural move.
