# Stacy and the Conversation With Her Father

## The Point It Illustrates

Returning to the original source of an abandonment wound requires two things at once: full preparation for the conversation and total release of outcome attachment. Either alone fails. Preparation without release produces a performance; release without preparation produces avoidance. The combination is what allowed Stacy Brown-Philpot to drop into a real exchange with the father whose absence had shaped her life.

This is also a canonical example of how the eight-out-of-ten pattern shows up in the highest-stakes conversations. The feared outcome (continued rejection, denial, indifference) did not materialize. The actual outcome was a watershed.

## The Background

Stacy grew up in Detroit in a house of four generations of women after her parents divorced. The lesson installed was independence: depend only on yourself. The lesson served her career, helping her become CEO of TaskRabbit and a sought-after board member. The lesson also installed a deep felt sense of abandonment that organized her work life into perpetual proving:

"It was just a recognition that I knew I could be a better person. I could show up better as a mother, as a wife, if I process some of the things that happened to me as a child. And over the course of 2 years, as I unpacked what really was holding me back and what I was fighting for, I think I spent 2 years of really working with Joe on understanding why I was always trying to prove something to somebody. And fight for something." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot, Stacy Brown-Philpot on Reclaiming Abandonment

The proving traced back to abandonment:

"Underneath that was this feeling that I needed to prove something because if I didn't, I would be abandoned again and again and again. And as I went back to the original source of where that started, that's really where it started." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot

## The Preparation

She and her father had developed a good adult relationship over a decade. They loved each other. The unspoken conversation had still not happened. She decided to go to Detroit in July 2021 specifically to have it.

She prepared methodically. A written script. Notes. Rehearsal of every possible outcome.

"I wrote -- I had a script. I wrote it down. I took many notes. Joe is laughing because he knows. I practiced. I practiced all possible scenarios, like how this conversation could go. It could go really good. It could be really bad. He could reject me again. I don't know what was going to happen." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot

The preparation served a particular function. It was not preparation to control the conversation. It was preparation to feel through every possible outcome in advance so that she would arrive without grip.

## The Release

The key move was not the script itself. The key move was becoming okay with any outcome before the conversation began.

"And I became OK with any outcome, meaning if there was no watershed moment, if he did pretend like nothing ever happened, I became okay with that possible outcome. And so because I think because I was okay with any outcome, I just freely spoke." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot

The relationship between preparation and release: working through each possible outcome in advance was how she got to okay. Not by deciding to be okay. By actually feeling what each outcome would be like and discovering that she could survive any of them. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] for the underlying practice.

The release was what made the speaking real. Without it, the script would have been a defense. With it, the script became a structure inside which she could speak truth without controlling the response.

## What Happened

They were going to take a drive. They never left the parking lot.

She told him that because of how they had grown up, she had never thought she mattered to him because he was not around. He cried. She cried. He said: "You've always mattered to me." He told her about things she had done as a child that had helped him change his life, things she had never known about.

"It was a moment where we were going to take a drive, Brett, and go and have this conversation. And we actually never left the parking lot because he just opened up and both of us were in tears just sharing. And he said to me, you've always mattered to me. And I don't think I knew what I expected from that conversation. All I wanted him to know was that how he made me feel. But when he said, you've always mattered to me, and in fact, there are some things that you did when you were a kid that helped me change my life for the better that I never knew about, that helped me see not only can I just make this statement to the person who I felt abandoned me and just feel the release, but also get information about what his experience was that just totally flipped everything I thought about what was happening to me as a child. And so that pivotal moment for me was just a release of like freedom and knowledge that the one person I thought didn't care about me as much actually really did. And it, it changed my perspective on how I saw basically everybody else." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot

The release was not about the apology. The release was that she had said it. The apology was the eight-out-of-ten pattern showing up: the feared outcome (rejection, denial) did not materialize, and a far better outcome did. See [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]].

## What Then Changed

The shift propagated outward. Where her CEO mode had been the fighter, the prover, the one who would take the mountain first, the post-conversation Stacy could deliver hard news with care:

"I want all of them to be successful, but we're not going to be the person who -- the company that provides the capital. And so when we pass on a deal and it's someone that I know, I talk about, I want to stay connected with you. I want you to be successful. And here's why we're not going to invest right now. It's a very different conversation. And almost always, the response that I get is a thank you, which is weird because I'm basically saying we're not going to give you money." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot

The capacity to deliver no with love came from having delivered her own unspoken truth to its original target.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When a client knows there is an unspoken conversation with a parent that wants to happen
- When the client is over-preparing and missing the release component
- When the client is under-preparing and treating the release as if it could be reached without first feeling through the outcomes
- When the fear of the conversation is itself organized around the feared rejection rather than the unmet need
- When a CEO client is locked in a proving pattern that traces to abandonment
- When the question is whether to wait until the relationship "is ready" -- Stacy and her father had been getting along for a decade before she did this

## The Practice Embedded in the Story

Three components, in order:

1. **Decide what you want to say.** Write it. The clarity matters.
2. **Pre-feel every possible response.** Not to plan reactions. To feel through what each outcome would actually be like. The work is somatic, not strategic. Until you can hold each outcome without grip, you are not ready.
3. **Speak from the release.** Once the outcomes have been felt, speak freely. The script becomes a structure rather than a defense.

See [[prepare-and-release-the-difficult-conversation]] for the full treatment of the practice.

## Why This Belongs in the Trauma-Mapping Lineage

This is the canonical example of going back to the original source rather than working only with the surrogate. Stacy could have worked endlessly on workplace abandonment patterns (the manager who did not promote her, the colleague who left) and made some progress. The deeper release required addressing the source. See [[trauma-mapping]] and [[reclaiming-abandonment]].

"All these things go back to abandonment. At one point, I had to go back to the original source, which was the relationship with my father." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot
