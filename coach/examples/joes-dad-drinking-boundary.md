# Joe's Dad and the Drinking Boundary

## The Story

Joe's father was a drinker. Joe's boundary with him evolved across three stages over time:

**Stage 1: total separation.** "I'm not going to be around you."

**Stage 2: conditional separation.** "I'm not going to be around you when you're drinking."

**Stage 3: behavior-specific.** "I'm not going to be around you when you're behaving in a certain way because of the drinking."

Each stage was the correct boundary for that point in Joe's development. The earlier stages were not "wrong" or "immature"; they were what was needed to do the underlying work of convincing himself he deserved the protection.

"With my dad, it was, I'm not gonna be around you. And then once I realized that, oh, I don't have to be around his drinking. But I miss my dad. I like to be around my dad. Okay, I'm gonna be around my dad, just not while he is drinking. Oh, I don't really mind that he is drinking as long as he's not being a prick. Okay. You know, but I had to learn that I could not be around his drinking. And that's why the boundary was such like a huge tool." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## The Point It Illustrates

The story is Joe's canonical example of how [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows|boundaries refine as capacity grows]]. The first boundary is doing the dual work of stopping the harm and establishing the underlying self-worth. The later boundaries can be more discriminating because the self-worth is no longer in question.

It also illustrates the principle that boundaries are about what the speaker will do, not what the other has to do. At every stage, the boundary was about Joe's behavior (being around his dad or not), never a demand for his dad to change.

And it illustrates [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]: "each one of them allowed me to love him better." The boundary was not a wall against the father. It was the structure that made love sustainable.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone is drawing a first-time boundary with a parent, partner, or other long-standing relationship
- When someone is judging their own boundary as too strict and wanting to soften it before they have the capacity
- When discussing addiction and family relationships
- When the conversation turns to how boundaries change over time
- When someone is trying to skip directly to the "advanced" boundary without doing the early-stage work
- When working with someone whose self-worth in the relationship is the actual issue underneath the visible behavioral one

## The Specific Detail That Matters

The strictness of Stage 1 was not about the father's behavior. It was about Joe's belief in his own deserving. The "I'm not going to be around you" had to be that absolute because Joe had to convince himself that the absence-from-his-father option was even on the table.

"It was really important for me to have that really big strict boundary at the beginning of just, I'm not gonna be around you, because I needed to convince myself that it was okay for me not to be around that person." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

Without that stage, the later stages would not have been possible. A premature "I'll be around you sometimes under certain conditions" would have collapsed back into the original dynamic because the underlying conviction was not yet in place.

## The Final Stage Discriminator

By Stage 3, the drinking itself had stopped being the issue. The behavior was. This is a meaningful refinement: it acknowledges that the drinking was a contributing condition but not the actual cause of the rupture, and it makes the boundary more accurate to what Joe actually needed protection from.

The progression also implies what was happening internally for Joe across these stages: he was building capacity to be around someone whose behavior he could not predict, then capacity to be around drinking specifically, then capacity to discriminate between drinking that affected him and drinking that did not. Each stage represented an internal expansion.
