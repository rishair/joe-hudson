# Joe's Funeral Poem for Kase

## The Poem

Joe wrote this poem at Kase's funeral. Kase was the mentor Joe describes in [[case-the-consultant]] -- the man whose way of operating in the business world gave Joe the first concrete example of [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]] and ultimately seeded [[view]].

The poem, as Joe recited it in conversation with Brett:

"At your funeral, even in the full light of recognition that we cannot survive this life, we cannot help but pretend we are forever. We see there hasn't been a second we have survived, ceaseless in delivering our every dying moments that we throw together and project into an unknown future. I fall to my knees in gratitude for this illusion of me. It is what has allowed me to laugh and cry with you, my friend, and it's what allows me to feel the endless depth of missing you now." -- Joe Hudson, How Death Brings Life Closer

## What the Poem Says

The poem holds two truths simultaneously without collapsing either one. The first truth: there is no continuous self that survives any moment. The "every dying moments" phrasing is not metaphorical -- it is the structural fact that each moment of self is a fresh construction, projected into an unknown future, and gone. The continuity is the projection, not the substance.

The second truth: the illusion of the continuous self is what makes the relationship possible. Without the illusion, there would have been no Joe to laugh with Kase, no Joe to cry with Kase, no Joe to miss Kase now. The gratitude is for the illusion, not despite it.

The poem refuses the standard split between "spiritual truth" (no self) and "human experience" (relationship, love, loss). It holds both as real and asks one to be grateful for the apparent contradiction. The illusion is what allowed the love. The love is what reveals the missing. The missing is endless precisely because the love was real, in the only way love is ever real -- through the illusion that makes a someone available to love.

## The Specific Phrases Worth Holding

**"In the full light of recognition that we cannot survive this life."** Not "that life is short" or "that we will eventually die." The phrasing is more radical: we cannot survive this life. Life itself is the thing we do not survive. The framing collapses the distinction between death-at-the-end and dying-throughout. Both are this-life-not-surviving.

**"Ceaseless in delivering our every dying moments that we throw together and project into an unknown future."** The continuous self is described as an activity, not a thing. We are ceaseless in the delivery. We throw the moments together. We project them forward. The "self" is the verb, not the noun. The dying is the basic operation; the projection forward is what makes it feel like continuity.

**"I fall to my knees in gratitude for this illusion of me."** The gratitude is named explicitly as gratitude for an illusion. Not for what the illusion hides. For the illusion itself. The illusion is the gift. Without it, no laughing, no crying, no missing.

**"The endless depth of missing you now."** The missing is endless. The depth is endless. The "now" is what locates the endless missing in this moment. The grief is not a remainder of something that ended. It is the live form of the relationship continuing, which is the structure named in [[death-is-not-the-end-of-the-relationship]].

## Why the Poem Belongs at the Funeral

The poem is what Joe brought to the gathering that was meant to mark Kase's death. It is not consoling in the standard sense. It does not say Kase is in a better place, that we will see him again, that his memory will endure. It says that the illusion that produced the relationship was always an illusion, and that the gratitude is for the illusion, and that the missing is endless.

The reason this lands at a funeral, in a way the standard consolations do not, is that it tells the truth that the funeral attendees are already feeling but do not have language for. The room knows that Kase is gone. The room knows that the goneness is not going to be undone. The room knows that the love does not get to be repaired by the goneness being explained away. The poem meets all of that and adds: the structure of what just happened reveals the structure of what was always happening. We have been dying together the whole time. The dying is what made the love possible. The gratitude for the love includes the gratitude for the structure.

This is what [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] looks like when expressed in a few sentences. The compression is in the poem.

## What the Poem Teaches About Grief

The poem teaches that the depth of the missing is the depth of the relationship continuing, not a sign that the relationship is over. The endless depth of missing Kase is the endless presence of the relationship Joe had with Kase. The grief is the relationship in its current form.

This is the principle that [[death-is-not-the-end-of-the-relationship]] articulates in prose. The poem condenses it: the missing is endless because the relationship is endless, in the only way relationships ever are -- through the projection of continuous selves that recognize one another, love one another, and now miss one another.

The grief in the poem is not the grief that needs to be processed and finished. It is the grief that gets to be the living edge of the continued relating. The endlessness of the depth is the gift, not the curse.

## What the Poem Teaches About Self

The phrasing "this illusion of me" is the most direct statement Joe has put on record about what he understands the self to be. The self is an illusion. The illusion is grateful-for. The illusion is the basis of every relationship the person has ever had.

The standard spiritual move is to point at the illusion of the self and call it the obstacle. See through the illusion, the move goes, and you are free. The poem makes a different move. It points at the illusion, sees through it, and then thanks the illusion. The seeing-through does not require the illusion to be discarded. It enables a different relationship to the illusion -- one in which the illusion is the gift rather than the problem.

This is structurally consistent with [[humility]]: the dissolution of taking-life-personally does not require the dissolution of the apparent self. It requires the dissolution of the gripping that treats the apparent self as the thing one is. The apparent self can continue to operate, gratefully, without being mistaken for what one is.

## When Joe Reaches for This Poem

Joe reaches for the poem in conversation when a few specific conditions converge:

- The conversation has touched the felt-reality of someone's death or imminent death
- The conversation has reached the question of what acknowledging death actually does
- The need is for something more compressed than prose, that lands as a felt-recognition rather than as an argument
- The teaching about the illusion-of-self needs to be paired with the gratitude-for-the-illusion so it does not collapse into nihilism

In the December 2024 conversation with Brett, the poem arrives in response to Brett's question about whether stories are there to prevent us from feeling the closeness of death. Joe affirms that they are, and then offers the poem as the report from the other side of that recognition -- the place where the closeness of death is felt and the stories are recognized as the gift they always were.

## The Performance of the Poem

Joe recites the poem slowly. Brett's response after the recitation is "Oh, wow. Yeah." The pause is part of the recitation. The poem lands not by being argued but by being held in the moment until what it points at becomes felt.

The poem is not delivered with the standard funeral-poem cadence of mourning. It is delivered with the same matter-of-fact warmth Joe brings to ordinary observations. The matter-of-factness is what makes the content available. If the poem were performed as tragic, the content would be subordinated to the performance. The lightness of the delivery lets the content be the thing.
