# The Tire Shop Guy and the Aggressive Customer (Holding the Container)

## The Story

Joe is at dinner with a friend who runs a tire shop. The friend has been getting angry often at work. He tells Joe about the kind of customer he encounters: a guy in a $150,000 car who shows up at 5:45 when the shop closed at 5:30, banging on the door, treating the staff like obstacles. The friend, instead of folding or matching the aggression with reactive aggression, holds the container. He is present with the customer's anger without disappearing into it and without escalating against it.

The result: that same customer, at the end of the interaction, asks for his phone number. Says he wants to stay in touch. Wants to hang out.

This happens four times a week.

"Last night I'm having dinner with this guy, and he's like, he works at a tire shop, and he is like, he's been a friend for a while. He's like, oh, I'm gonna do whatever I want, bro. Like, he's just like that kind of guy, and I freaking love it. There is like, there's no lack of authenticity, and he just says whatever he thinks. I just love that. And so we're sitting there talking and he's talking about how working at this tire shop is not doing well for him because he's getting angry often. But then he tells me the story. He's like, yeah, these customers come in and they're absolutely like assholes. He's like, and I'm just like, oh, what do I do? He goes, and then they always are like, yeah, here's my phone number. I'd like to keep in touch at the end of it." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

"This happens 4 times a week where I literally am like, hey, hold the container. And this person who started off as like the asshole in like the $150,000 car who's like 'Come on, open the door, it's only 5:45, you closed at 5:30,' ends up saying, 'Hey, can we hang out?' You know, at the end of it." -- Tire shop friend, recounted by Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## The Point Joe Uses This For

The example illustrates [[aggression-amplifies-against-conflict-avoidance]] in its inverse form. The aggressive customer's nervous system is searching for someone who can hold the energy they are bringing. Most service workers fold, which intensifies the search; or they fight back, which escalates the dynamic. The tire shop guy does neither. He stays present with the aggression. The customer's body registers "this person can hold me," the aggression dissolves, and what is left is the human being who wants connection.

"I was like, let me explain what -- like I was like geeking out. I'm like, psychologically, let me explain to you how that works. And it's that thing of like, he, they feel like, oh, my anger is safe here. Like, I can have anger and this person's not going to fold. And so I actually want this person in my life." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The four-times-a-week frequency is part of what makes the example land. This is not a one-time anecdote. The mechanism is reliable. The same customer profile, met the same way, produces the same outcome with statistical regularity.

## The Refinement Beat

Joe and Brett extend the example to show the developmental edge. The tire shop guy is doing this with his own anger as the holding-tool. The next-level refinement would be holding the same energy without the personal charge of anger -- being present with the customer's aggression without the bracing that comes from taking it personally.

"I would say that the thing of like, if he could hold that same energy without the anger, he would be a lot less constricted. And I think that's about -- for him, it's not beating himself up for the anger, but it's also about not taking stuff personally. So slightly different thing that's happening there. But yes, if he could love the anger, it would show up with less ho to it." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

Both versions work. The angry-but-present version is significantly better than the folding version or the escalating version. The non-personal version is the more developed form.

## Why Joe Reaches for This Example

The story is useful because it makes the counterintuitive principle visible. Conflict avoidance produces more conflict; meeting aggression with presence produces less. The principle is hard to feel as true when it stays abstract. The image of a guy in a $150,000 car going from banging on the door to asking to hang out gives the body something to recognize.

The example also avoids the trap of pure self-help framing. The tire shop guy is not a yoga teacher. He is a regular guy who runs a tire shop and "freaking" says whatever he thinks. The mechanism is not exclusive to people who have done years of inner work.

## What This Is Not

This is not advice to be passive with aggressive people. The tire shop guy is not being nice. He is being present. The energy of his presence includes the firmness of his anger; he is not pretending the situation is okay or absorbing the abuse silently. The container he holds is a real container.

This is also not a recipe for tolerating actual abuse. The customers in this story are situationally aggressive, not structurally dangerous. The mechanism described here works with the situational aggression that comes from someone whose nervous system is dysregulated and searching for containment. It does not work with someone who is engineering harm. See [[definition-of-asshole]] for the relevant distinction.
