# Marriage as "Two People Thinking They're Not Good Enough Coming Together to Find Out That They Are"

## The Definition

Joe uses an aphorism he heard somewhere -- he could not remember the source -- as one of the more honest definitions of what marriage is.

"Two people thinking that they're not good enough coming together in an attempt to try to find out that they're good enough." -- Joe Hudson (paraphrasing an aphorism), Finding a Romantic Partner

The definition is striking because it refuses the standard romantic frame (two perfect-for-each-other people finding each other) and refuses the standard cynical frame (marriage as social contract). It names the actual psychological work that most marriages are doing whether or not the partners are conscious of it.

## What the Definition Captures

The definition has three operational components:

**1. The partners arrive thinking they are not good enough.** This is almost universally true. Most people enter long-term partnership carrying some version of "I am not enough" -- not pretty enough, not lovable enough, not worth being chosen, not worth being stayed with. The unconscious belief is what is being brought to the relationship.

**2. They come together in an attempt to find out they are good enough.** The relationship is, at its psychological core, an attempt to disprove the not-enough belief. The partner's choosing them, staying with them, loving them, becomes (consciously or unconsciously) the proposed evidence that they are good enough after all.

**3. The attempt requires a specific kind of partner.** Not any partner can supply this. The body selects a partner whose own not-enough material interlocks with theirs in a specific way (see [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]]). The partner who arrives is the one who can carry the matched not-enoughness in the way that will allow the work.

The definition therefore explains the precision of partner selection (it is not random, it is the body's intelligence about its own healing trajectory) and the difficulty of long-term partnership (the not-enough material is being surfaced by the relationship, often painfully) and the potential of long-term partnership (the not-enough material can actually be worked through in the field of the relationship).

## Why Joe Likes This Definition

The definition is unsentimental and accurate. It does not romanticize marriage and it does not dismiss it. It names what is actually happening underneath the surface.

It also resolves the apparent paradox of why partners who are obviously not the "ideal match" by external criteria end up together and stay together. The standard criteria for compatibility (interests, values, attractiveness) are not what selects the partner. The not-enough fit is what selects the partner. The body is doing precise work that the conscious mind cannot see.

The definition is also the structural foundation for [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]] as the relationship criterion. If marriage is two people trying to find out they are good enough, then the question of whether to commit is the question of whether both are willing to do the work that the finding-out requires. The right-person frame becomes irrelevant; the willingness frame becomes the only thing that matters.

## What This Definition Does in Coaching

For the person evaluating a relationship: it offers a different frame for "is this the right person." The question becomes "is this the person with whom my not-enough material has surfaced precisely enough that we could do the work together." The right-person criterion is replaced with the trauma-interlock-plus-willingness criterion.

For the person frustrated by recurring patterns in their relationship: it relocates the source of the patterns. The patterns are the not-enough material the relationship has been doing its work on. The patterns are not signs the relationship is wrong; they are the work the relationship was for.

For the person whose partner is "not who I would have chosen": it reframes the partner as exactly who the body chose for the work. The conscious mind would not have chosen this person. The body did. The body's selection is precise even when the conscious mind cannot see why.

For the person considering leaving: it surfaces what would have to happen if they left without doing the work. The not-enough material would travel with them. The next relationship would have the same shape. See [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]].

## The Connection to Self-Rejection

The definition links directly to [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]]. The not-enough belief that the partners bring to the relationship is the same self-rejection that operates as the "I'm not enough to be loved" frame. The relationship is the field in which this self-rejection becomes workable.

The relationship's potential is not that the partner will resolve the self-rejection by loving the person enough. That dynamic does not work in the long run; no amount of partner-love can substitute for the work on the self-rejection. The relationship's potential is that, in the field of partnership, the self-rejection can be surfaced, met, and worked through.

The work is each partner's own. The relationship is the container. See [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]] and [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]].

## When Joe Uses This Example

- When someone is in the right-person frame and the structure of partnership needs to be reframed
- When someone is trying to evaluate a partner against criteria and the deeper mechanism of selection needs to be named
- When [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] is being introduced and a concrete framing for the body's selection is useful
- When someone is considering whether to leave a difficult relationship and the question of what the difficulty is for is on the table
- When the trauma-matching dynamic is showing up and the partner's role as the body's chosen interlock needs to be named without pathologizing

## What This Example Is Not

It is not a romantic ideal. The definition is bleak in its way. Marriage is not described as the meeting of two whole people but as the meeting of two people who do not believe they are whole. The acceptance of the bleakness is part of what makes the definition useful. It is operating at the level reality is actually at.

It is not a guarantee of resolution. The definition says the partners come together to find out they are good enough; it does not say they will. Many marriages do not deliver the finding-out. The definition names what the marriage is attempting to do, not what every marriage achieves. The achievement depends on the work both partners are willing to do.

It is not a substitute for evaluating actual fit. The definition operates at the deep psychological level. There are also surface-level questions (do these two people have compatible life situations, values, intentions) that are real. The definition is useful at the level it operates at and does not replace the other levels of evaluation.

## Tone

Quoted, not asserted. Joe attributes the definition to someone else and is careful to say he cannot remember who. The tone is the tone of someone passing on a teaching they think is true. The lightness of "let's see if I can remember it" softens what would otherwise be a stark claim.

## What Underneath the Fights Reveals (Joe and Tara on What Every Fight Was Really About)

In the Modern Relationships interview, Joe and Tara are asked what they were always fighting about. Their answer matches this definition's framework precisely. The surface content was variable. The underneath was consistent.

"In a way it's, do you love me? Do you love me? Do you love me? Are we safe? Am I safe? Am I safe? Always. Like, can we attach? Am I attached? Am I attached? Are you attached? Are you attached?" -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

Joe adds: "Who's in charge? Who's in charge?"

And: "Wherever we had both had shame that were perfectly interlocking and feeling unseen. That was a huge part of it." -- Joe Hudson, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The "perfectly interlocking shame" is the structural form this definition predicts. Two people who each carry "I am not enough" find each other precisely because the not-enoughness fits together in a way that surfaces it in both. The fights are the form the surfacing takes. The fights' content (pick a topic) is the cover; the underlying processing is the "do you love me / are we safe / am I attached" inquiry, which is the "am I good enough to be loved, kept safe, attached to" inquiry in slightly disaggregated form.

The teaching cleanly supports the definition. The marriage's psychological work is the finding-out about being good enough. The fights are the medium of the work. The interlocking shame is the structural mechanism by which the partner is precisely the one who can deliver the work. See [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] for the mechanism.
