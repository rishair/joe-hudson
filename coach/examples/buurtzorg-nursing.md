# The Buurtzorg Nursing Company

## The Story

(Joe refers to the company as "Hertzog" and acknowledges he is bad at pronouncing it. The company in *Reinventing Organizations* is Buurtzorg, a Dutch home-nursing organization. Joe's larger point holds regardless of pronunciation.)

In Holland, community nursing was privatized. The competitive structure was efficiency. How long should it take to get to a patient's house? How long to administer the shot? How long to return to base? Payment was structured to reward the most efficient nurses by these measures. The whole industry was running on improve-improve-improve.

A new company came in and made a different choice. Their stated job was not "be as quick as possible." Their stated job was to help people become self-reliant. Each visit was structured around moving the patient toward not needing the visit.

The result: roughly 40-60% more efficient than competitors (Joe could not remember the exact number). The frame designed to ignore efficiency produced higher efficiency than the frame optimized for it.

## Why It Worked

The competitors hooked their incentive structure onto a goal that was not authentically motivating to the nurses. Nurses do not get into nursing to administer the maximum number of shots per hour. They get into nursing because they want to help people.

The new company hooked into what was already there. Wanting to help people is part of nursing nature. Wanting people to become self-reliant is a deeper form of help than wanting to administer shots quickly. The work became aligned with what the nurses wanted to do anyway.

When the work aligns with the authentic want, effort drops and output rises. This is [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] at the organizational level: enjoyment is the most efficient fuel.

"They hooked onto that natural thing, and then that led to natural improvement. They weren't trying to improve in some unnatural way." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The Larger Principle: Hook Into Multiple Authentic Drives

Joe extends the example to a nuance. As soon as he says altruism is human nature, someone will object that self-interest is human nature. Both are true. The most effective companies hook into both simultaneously.

"It's our nature to be altruistic, and it's our nature to be self-interested, and it's our nature to want to be rewarded, and it's our nature to want our team to win, and it's our nature for us to win. Companies that are becoming the most efficient companies are hooking onto all of that." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

The Buurtzorg case meets all of these:
- Altruism: the work serves patients into self-reliance.
- Self-interest: the model produced higher pay and better work conditions.
- Team-winning: the team outperformed competition.
- Individual reward: Joe notes that nurses had agency over their own compensation.

The frame "improve nursing efficiency" only hooks into one driver: complying with measurement. The frame "help people become self-reliant" hooks into a stack of authentic motivations at once.

## The Negative Example: The Debt Collector

Brett brings in a complementary example from a contemporary debt collection agency. The standard industry approach: maximize recovery, buy debt cheaply, pressure debtors. The contrarian approach: be transparent with the debtor about what was paid for the debt and what is expected, then collaborate on creative solutions.

The transparent approach produces:
- Better recovery rates.
- Testimonials from debtors saying "I wish my debt had been bought by this company."
- A different relationship to the work for the collectors themselves.

Joe extends this: compartmentalization is the cost. When a debt collector compartmentalizes their heart to be "professional," the debtor reciprocates by compartmentalizing theirs. The interaction becomes harder, not easier. The compartmentalization makes everyone less effective.

"People think they have to compartmentalize themselves to do business, and that compartmentalization, that inauthenticity, makes you less efficient. It might make things easier to do in the short term, but absolutely harder to do in the long term, because you're basically asking anybody you interact with to compartmentalize themselves that same way." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## When Joe Reaches for It

Joe uses this example when:
- A business or team is in optimize-everything mode and the optimization is producing diminishing returns.
- A leader is wondering whether authenticity is "soft" or whether it scales.
- The conversation needs evidence that the authentic frame is not opposed to performance but enables it.
- An executive is considering whether to restructure incentives around something other than the obvious metric.

The example is the business-level demonstration of [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]. The personal-level demonstration is [[joes-hacky-sack]]. Both show the same dynamic: authentic alignment outperforms forced optimization.
