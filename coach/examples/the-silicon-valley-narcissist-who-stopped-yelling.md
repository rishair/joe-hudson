# The Silicon Valley Narcissist Who Stopped Yelling at Her Team

## The Story

A woman executive in Silicon Valley is working with Joe. Her team has to meet every two weeks with one of the most famous Silicon Valley narcissists. He runs the meetings by yelling — "You're screwing up, you're fucking up" — yelling and screaming at people. She is scared. Her body reads the anger as evidence she has done something wrong.

One day she walks into the meeting and says:

"Hey, I see that what you're pointing at is really important. I see your vision, and I see how important it is for us to listen to you. And for us to help you get that vision across. However, when you yell at us, it makes it harder for us to help you. So if you could please not yell at us so that we can be more effective, I'd appreciate it."

He stops yelling at her team. He keeps yelling at the other teams. Her team works with him for the next year without yelling. When she leaves to start her own company, he agrees to be on the board.

"And so for the next year they worked together, there was no yelling that happened. And then she left to start another company. And he actually agreed to be on the board of that company. So their relationship got very good very quickly, and it's because he felt safe with her for the first time." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

## What She Did Operationally

The move has a specific structure:

**1. She named the vision he was reaching for.** "I see that what you're pointing at is really important. I see your vision." This is the empathic move — she got that he cared about something. The yelling was the form; the underlying transmission was "this matters and I need you to take it seriously." She received that part first.

**2. She named her wanting to help him achieve it.** "I see how important it is for us to listen to you. And for us to help you get that vision across." She was not telling him to fix himself. She was aligning with what he was trying to do.

**3. Then she named the specific obstacle.** "However, when you yell at us, it makes it harder for us to help you." Not "stop yelling at us because it's wrong." Not "you're being abusive." A simple causal statement: this behavior interferes with the outcome you want.

**4. She made the ask in service of his goal.** "So if you could please not yell at us so that we can be more effective." The ask was framed in his terms — his effectiveness, his vision, what he wanted.

**5. She did all of this without fear in her voice.** This is the structural prerequisite — see "What She Had to Have Done First" below. The move only works because the body is not communicating fear underneath the words.

## What This Illustrates

**Containment of a narcissist looks different from VIEW with a non-narcissist.** A regular use of [[view]] with this person would have been exploited — the vulnerability would have become the leverage point. What worked was a boundary delivered with empathy for what he was actually after. See [[contain-the-narcissist]]. The empathy was real (she did see his vision) but it was paired with structural firmness about what would and would not work.

**The narcissist's anger is asking for engagement.** Underneath the yelling is "see me, take this seriously, don't run away from me." She did exactly that. She stepped in, named that she saw him, and offered partnership. This dissolved the function the yelling was serving. See [[anger-is-an-act-of-vulnerability]] and [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]].

**The boundary that holds creates the safety the narcissist's nervous system has been looking for.** The yelling was a containment test. Other teams failed it — they got smaller, more scared, more compliant, which meant the yelling escalated. Her team passed it — she did not get smaller, she stayed with him, and the yelling stopped being necessary. The narcissist whose behavior keeps working is being kept in a state where the nervous system cannot rest. See [[contain-the-narcissist]] for the deeper treatment.

**He could only do this because he felt safe with her for the first time.** Joe's framing: their relationship got very good very quickly *because* he felt safe with her. The yelling-everyone-else dynamic and the not-yelling-at-her-team dynamic happened in parallel for a year. The variable was her capacity to be present without being scared. The relationship that resulted was one of the strongest professional relationships in his life — she eventually got him to be on her board.

**The pattern Joe sees in containment work also produces relationship deepening.** This is not just "she got him to stop yelling." She got him as a board member. The capacity to hold the boundary while staying open-hearted produced more connection, not less. The containment is what made deep connection possible. Without it, the relationship would have remained adversarial.

## What She Had to Have Done First

The story is rarely tellable without the prerequisite, which Joe pairs with it in the source episode: she had been working on her own anger before this moment.

"The woman that I worked with to get good with the anger. She would drive home every day on her commute and she would yell for like 10 or 15 minutes a day, just getting all of her anger out so that the anger stopped being scary. It stopped being something that was a way to control somebody or be controlled. It just became, oh, this is an energy that's moving and I can actually accept it. And as she learned to love her own anger, she could learn to love this man's anger." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

The 10-15 minutes of nightly yelling on the commute was what made the meeting move possible. Without it, the body would have gone into freeze in the meeting room and the words would have been impossible to find. With it, she could be in the same room as a yelling narcissist and stay in her own state. The vision-naming move emerges from the underlying capacity; the words are downstream.

This is the same pattern as [[joes-angry-executive-commute]] — nightly anger release on the commute produces empowerment that shows up in the daytime relationship. Joe sometimes tells the two stories together because they are the same mechanism showing up in different contexts. The executive-commute story illustrates the capacity-building; this story illustrates what the capacity makes possible.

## What Did Not Work for the Other Teams

The other teams stayed scared. Their pattern was the default response to a narcissist's yelling: get smaller, comply faster, perform helpfulness, walk on eggshells. Each of these moves confirmed for the narcissist's nervous system that the environment was not contained. The behavior escalated. The other teams continued to be yelled at for the entire time.

Joe does not explicitly diagnose what was different about her, but the implicit contrast is between the receiver who had done their own anger work and the receivers who had not. The story functions as the worked example of [[self-boundary-mirrors-external-boundary]] — the only times you cannot draw a boundary with someone else is when you cannot draw the boundary with yourself. The other teams could not draw the boundary because they were still tolerating internal versions of the same treatment.

## When Joe Tells This Story

- When teaching how to handle a boss or partner whose anger has been scaring the listener.
- When introducing the principle that containment is what the narcissist's nervous system is actually asking for.
- When showing that the boundary delivered with empathy lands differently than the boundary delivered with fear or aggression.
- When demonstrating the operational shape of [[anger-is-an-act-of-vulnerability]] — what to receive when you receive the anger.
- When paired with [[joes-angry-executive-commute]] to show both halves of the move: building the capacity, then deploying it.
- When showing what changes when the receiver of anger is no longer scared of anger as an energy.

## What This Is Not

This is not a generic "stand up to your bully" story. The move did not work because she confronted him; the move worked because she empathized with what he was actually reaching for, stayed in her own state, and offered partnership rather than capitulation or counter-attack. The confrontation framing misses the empathy that made it land.

This is not permission to attempt this without the underlying capacity. A person who tries this move while still scared of the angry person's anger will have their fear read by the angry person and the move will fail. The capacity is the prerequisite. See [[dont-should-yourself-to-welcome-anger]].

This is not a universal narcissism move. With a different narcissist on a different day with a different team configuration, the same words could have produced a different response. The story illustrates a principle (containment + empathy + boundary) rather than a script.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with calm appreciation. The narcissist is not the villain. The executive is not the hero. The story is structurally interesting — what happened in the room, why it worked, what made it possible. The tone is "here is something I have seen work" rather than "here is the right way to handle these people."
