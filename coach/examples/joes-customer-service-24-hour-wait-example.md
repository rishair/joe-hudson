# Joe's 24-Hour Wait on Customer Service

## The Story

Early in Joe's working life, when he had a portfolio of website clients, he discovered that if he didn't respond immediately to client questions, the clients often resolved the problem themselves. Within 24 hours, what had felt like an urgent inquiry frequently dissolved without his involvement. He turned this observation into an operational pattern: deliberately wait a day before responding to see if the question was still alive.

> "When I was early in doing like customer service for — I had a whole bunch of website clients and I'd get questions from them. I started to learn that if I didn't respond within the first 24 hours, often they resolved their own problem. So I just started running a pattern of just not responding for a day and seeing if the question was still alive." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The pattern looks like procrastination from outside. Joe is sitting on customer questions instead of responding immediately. Most operators would call this poor service. The actual structure was different: the wait was a deliberate strategy that produced better outcomes for both Joe and the clients. The clients learned to solve their own problems. The questions that genuinely needed Joe's involvement still got addressed. The total work decreased and the client autonomy increased.

## The Point Joe Is Making

The example illustrates a structural feature of action that the "respond immediately to everything" frame misses: **sometimes non-action is the more effective action.** The wait is not procrastination from inability. The wait is the response.

> "There was a deeper wisdom that was just there's a, there's a better way here that involves non-action. That leads to a smoother solution. And I could operationalize that in a far more effective and, you know, honest way with my clients." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The phrase that carries the principle: **operationalize the non-action.** Once Joe noticed the pattern, he made it explicit. He didn't have to keep questioning whether the delay was procrastination because he had named it as the strategy. The non-action became a chosen response rather than a failed action.

This is the bridge from procrastination to prioritization. The same delay, labeled correctly, is a deliberate operational choice. Labeled incorrectly, it produces self-attack. The labeling is the difference. See [[prioritization-vs-procrastination]] for the broader distinction.

## How This Connects to Procrastination Work

The example sits in the procrastination-is-wisdom territory. The thing Joe initially might have called procrastination ("I should have responded already") turned out to contain a wisdom: the question often didn't need response. The wisdom was about timing and about the nature of the work itself — much of customer service is actually customer self-service that gets short-circuited when the operator responds too quickly.

> "Oftentimes we're learning the natural rhythm of things, and that requires some experiment, that requires some not doing and some doing, and then you find that thing, it's never procrastination unless you're beating yourself up." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The example is also a small business-strategy lesson tucked inside the procrastination teaching. Operators who default to immediate response are doing work that doesn't need to be done. Operators who experiment with timing learn what the actual response cadence should be. The experimentation is what produces the calibration. The fear of "letting clients down" prevents the experimentation that would have shown that the clients are not let down — they often prefer the structure where they solve their own problems.

## What This Example Does in Coaching

When a client is in pure should-frame about responding to every email, every text, every request, the example surfaces that the default of immediate response is not necessarily the most effective response. The client may be doing twice the work because they are responding to inquiries that would have resolved themselves. The wait, properly named, is not procrastination; it is operational calibration.

For coaches, the example also illustrates the broader principle that operational habits should be experimented with rather than inherited. The "always respond immediately" frame is one of many inherited operating defaults that may not actually serve the work. Other examples: always answer the phone, always be reachable on Slack, always reply within the hour. Each could be deliberately experimented with, and the experimentation often reveals that a different pattern produces better outcomes.

The deeper teaching this example carries: **the rhythm of the work is something to learn, not something to assume.** Most chronic-overwork patterns are driven by inherited assumptions about response cadence that were never tested. The test, in many cases, would reveal that less responsive operation produces equal or better outcomes with substantially less work.
