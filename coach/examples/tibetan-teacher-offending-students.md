# The Tibetan Teacher Who Offends the Student

## The Example

Joe describes a form of Tibetan Buddhist teaching with two stages. In the first stage, the teacher and student build deep connection and friendship. In the second stage, the teacher does everything possible to offend the student. The offense is the teaching.

"If you look at Tibetan teaching, like one form of the Tibetan teaching, they take the monks in and the first stage of the teaching is we're friends and you build this deep connection. And then the second stage of the teaching is they do everything they possibly can to offend the student." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The student is not informed in advance. The not-informing is structural; if the student knew the offense was coming, the ego would protect itself from being exposed.

"Is the student informed up front?"
"No, of course not."

## The Point It Illustrates

Offense reveals where the ego still runs the show. Whatever in the student is still defended, still gripped, still organized around an identity is what gets activated when offended. The offense is therefore a diagnostic tool. It points to what has not yet dissolved.

"Because if I offend you, then I am pointing to an ego that still is running your life." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

This is why a tradition that values awakening would build offense into the curriculum. The student cannot see their own attachments without something hitting them. The teacher provides the hits.

## When Joe Reaches for It

Joe uses this example when a person is making the case that being kind requires not offending anyone, or when a person is trying to figure out how to be themselves without producing any friction. The example reframes offense as service rather than harm.

It is particularly useful as a counter to:

- "I have to be kind."
- "Being myself would hurt them."
- "If I love them I won't offend them."
- "Spiritual people don't make others uncomfortable."

The example presses on the assumption that kindness and avoidance of offense are the same thing. They are not. In some traditions, kindness IS the offense.

## The Consent Caveat

Joe is careful to qualify the example. The Tibetan teaching has an implicit consent structure: the student has entered a teaching relationship and trusts the teacher to teach. The offense happens within a frame.

"Granted, that's a consensual agreement to some degree, like you're there for the teaching and you trust the teacher to teach you." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The point is not that Joe recommends randomly offending people for their growth. The point is that within trusting relationships, offense has a function that conflict avoidance prevents.

## How It Connects

This example is closely linked to [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]]. The Tibetan teacher is enacting the principle: by being willing to offend, the teacher gives the student the chance to encounter their own ego. By NOT being willing to offend, a teacher would deprive the student of that chance.

It also illustrates [[trust-requires-conflict]] from a different angle: the deep first-stage connection makes the second-stage offense bearable. The relationship has been built with enough trust to hold the conflict productively.

## Joe's Application

"There's truth to it in every aspect of this, right? That if you are constantly avoiding the conflict, then you are stopping the chance for people to learn. And you see this in companies all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The Tibetan example becomes a bridge to the organizational form: conflict-avoidant leaders sit on stagnant problems while leaders who welcome conflict produce innovation. The dynamic is structural, not cultural; it shows up in monasteries and in companies because it is about how growth works in humans.

## Tone

Joe tells the story matter-of-factly, almost casually. The unusualness of the example does the work. The listener has to consider that a serious spiritual tradition treats offense as part of the curriculum, which loosens the assumption that all offense is bad.
