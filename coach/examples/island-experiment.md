# The Island Experiment (12 People Who Unconditionally Love You)

## The Setup

A thought experiment Joe uses to point at what real community does and why nearly everyone, once they imagine it clearly, wants it.

"Imagine you were dropped onto an island and there was 12 people who unconditionally loved you. They drew boundaries with you. They — wasn't like they were just accepting your shit, but they loved you unconditionally. They accepted where you were and they loved you unconditionally. And now if you imagine that you were stuck on that island for a decade, right, and you're with these basic kind of like saints almost, like people who are just like in that kind of deep love, like, who are you at the end of that decade. Like, where, where, you know, where's your fear? Where's your love? Where's your — like, how do you interact in the world? How do you see yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

The features that do the work:

- Twelve, not one. The unconditional love does not depend on any single relationship being functional that day.
- Unconditional, but with boundaries. The people are not pushovers. They love you while drawing limits with your behavior when needed. See [[boundaries-with-vs-against]] for the distinction the experiment leans on.
- A decade. Enough time that the conditions become the new baseline, not a peak experience.
- Acceptance of where you are. The love is not contingent on your becoming something different.

## The Point

The experiment is not about the island. It is about the kind of person those conditions would produce. Once a person sits with the question, the answer is intuitive: they would be unrecognizably less afraid, less defended, more open, more in their love. Their relationship to fear, to love, to themselves and others would be qualitatively different.

The reason the experiment matters: those conditions are not exotic. They are the conditions a real community generates inside ordinary life. The experiment makes vivid what a person is missing when they say they have community but it is the surface kind. It also makes vivid why people are willing to give up money and power for the real thing once they have tasted it.

## When Joe Uses It

- When someone is dismissing the value of community, or treating community as a nice-to-have
- When someone is in a stifling community and confusing that for what community is
- When someone is asking why they should bother with the work of vulnerability and depth in a group
- As a counter to the cultural story that what people need is more privacy, more independence, more self-reliance

## What It Produces

The experiment cannot be argued with intellectually. The person's own answer to the question does the work. They imagine the conditions, they sense the person they would become, and they feel the gap between that and their current life. The gap is the motivation. No further persuasion is needed.

"That's why everybody wants it, because they know if they're met there, what becomes of them — they become the thing that they truly are. And so I think that's the thing that really pulls people, and they — and people can sniff it out." -- Joe Hudson

## Internal and External Versions Are the Same Project

A useful second turn of the experiment, after the person has felt into who they would become: notice that the internal voice is almost never 12 saints loving the person unconditionally. It is usually the inverse — judgment, performance demands, conditional acceptance. The work that the external island would do over a decade is the same work that [[loving-all-parts]] of oneself does internally. The external island and the internal one are the same project.

The third turn: the experiment also functions as a mirror for what conditions the person is currently providing for others in their life. If 12 people on the island loved you unconditionally and drew clean boundaries with you, are you doing that for anyone? The asymmetry between what one wants from others and what one is offering tends to become visible. That visibility is often where the actual community-building starts: by becoming a node such an island can form around.
