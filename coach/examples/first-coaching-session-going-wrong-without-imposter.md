# Joe's First Public Coaching Session Going Wrong

## The Story

One of Joe's first public coaching sessions, well before he started coaching for a living, went badly. The session was public; friends were there to watch.

The client had a strong resistance pattern and wanted a lot of attention. The session collapsed. Joe could not get traction. Things went visibly wrong.

After the session, Joe's friends gave him direct feedback: "you maybe shouldn't do this for a living." The session was that visibly off.

"I remember one of my first public coaching sessions, everything went fucking wrong. I had somebody there who was like deeply into the resistance thing and wanted a lot of attention. And like, it just went horribly wrong. Like it was, I mean, I had friends there and they were like, you maybe shouldn't do this for a living. And like all of that crap happened." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

From the perspective of the standard imposter-syndrome model, this is exactly the scene that would install or amplify imposter syndrome. A public failure, in the domain the person wants to work in, with respected peers visibly judging it as bad. The standard prediction: the person carries deep imposter syndrome about coaching from that point forward.

Joe's actual experience: no imposter syndrome arrived.

"There was nothing in me at that moment that felt like I was an imposter. I was just like, bad shot. There's things for me to learn. What do I do? I started talking to people like, what are your tricks in this kind of situation? I started to learn. I started to feel into what that was. I felt the grief of that experience, but never was I thinking, oh, I'm an imposter here." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

## Why the Imposter Syndrome Didn't Land

The reason Joe gives: the deep self-investigation work he had done -- the inquiry into "what am I" -- had produced a sense of self that was not vulnerable to the standard imposter-installation mechanism. The conditional-worth structure that would have made the failure a verdict on his worth was no longer running. The failure could be just a failure, not evidence of being a fraud.

"It's because my sense of self was like, I couldn't particularly question it anymore because that the question of what I am essentially and identifying with that was there. And I've noticed that generally with everybody who's gone through that process of really investigating what am I and how do I be deeply authentic, they do not experience imposter syndrome. And maybe some, but like, it's like maybe a little bit, but not even close to the levels that they have." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The self-investigation had produced access to a sense of self that did not depend on performance outcomes. The session going wrong did not threaten that sense, because the sense was not built on session outcomes in the first place. The failure could be received as data ("here are things to learn, here are skills to develop") without triggering the worth-question that imposter syndrome runs on.

## What This Example Demonstrates

The example demonstrates several things:

1. **The conditional-worth structure is the engine of imposter syndrome.** When that structure is loosened (through self-investigation, through repair work), the events that would trigger imposter syndrome simply do not. The events are the same; the structure receiving them is different.

2. **Self-investigation work has measurable downstream effects.** The "what am I" inquiry can sound abstract. The example shows what its absence-of-imposter-syndrome looks like in a specific high-stakes situation that would have triggered it before.

3. **Failure can be received as information.** The failure-as-verdict-on-worth interpretation is not the only one available. The failure-as-data-to-learn-from interpretation is just as available, and is what is left when the worth-question stops firing.

4. **What would have been a stop-doing-this moment becomes a what-do-I-learn moment.** Joe could have absorbed the friends' feedback as confirmation that he should not coach. Instead he received it as one input among others, and continued. The continuing was not heroic; it was just the natural next step when the failure had not been received as a verdict.

The example also functions as the demonstration of [[confidence-is-connection-to-self-while-acting]]. The confidence Joe had in continuing was not built from the session going well. It was the state-of-being that the self-investigation had produced. The state survived the perceived failure because the state did not depend on performance outcomes.

## When To Use This Example

- When demonstrating the long-arc value of self-investigation work
- When a client has had a perceived failure and is rebuilding from it
- When a client is asking whether the deep inner work has practical value
- When introducing [[what-am-i]] as the inquiry method
- When working with [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] and a client wants to know what the work eventually produces
- When demonstrating that confidence as state-of-being can survive what skill-built confidence cannot

The example is also useful for normalizing failure as part of the coaching arc. Joe's first sessions were not good. The friends' feedback was honest and unflattering. The work proceeded anyway. The client can locate similar moments in their own arc.

## Tone

Joe tells this matter-of-factly. The "everything went fucking wrong" is plain reporting, not dramatized. The friends' feedback is reported without resentment. The continuing-anyway is reported without heroism. The whole story has a quality of "this happened, this is what it was like, this is what I noticed about my own experience."

The flatness of the telling is part of what makes it useful. The standard narrative of "I overcame imposter syndrome through deep inner work" would be a different and less useful story. This story is "I didn't have imposter syndrome to overcome because the structure that would have produced it had already been loosened." The flatness shows what the absence of the structure looks like.
