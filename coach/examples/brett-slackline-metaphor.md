# The Slackline Metaphor (Learning to Fall Off Is Learning Internal Stability)

## The Metaphor

A slackline is a one-inch piece of webbing tied between two trees, or set up between two cliffs a thousand feet in the air. When a beginner first stands on it, their legs wiggle and shudder, and they fall off almost instantly. They wonder how they will ever walk it.

The metaphor Brett offers: slacklining is actually the art of learning to fall off.

"The line is not insecure at all. The line is just perfectly stable. The only insecurity that's being brought into the system is what you're bringing to it. It's your muscles overcorrecting. It's your top-down trying to think your way through it. And initially, yeah, you're gonna fall, but the more you do it, the more internal stability you develop, and then the less overcorrecting your legs need to do." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The line doesn't change. The body changes. As the body learns to fall and recover, the overcorrecting stops. The natural point of balance becomes accessible. The walk becomes possible.

"And then you find that the balance is just a natural -- there's just a natural point of balance on the line. The line wants to balance, your body wants to balance, and it will just do it. And that's when you start getting to the point where you can walk across, you know, mile-long lines." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

## What the Metaphor Teaches

Several layers of teaching live in this image.

**The instability is in you, not in the situation.** The line is stable. The instability is the body's overcorrection. This is true of many situations people experience as unstable: the situation is what it is; the felt instability is what the system brings to it through trying too hard.

**Trying harder makes it worse.** The beginner's instinct is to grip more tightly, control more rigorously, think their way through. Each of these adds insecurity to the system. The skill is to do less, to trust the natural balance more.

This connects to [[trying-vs-doing]] and [[undoing-not-doing]]. The slackline can only be walked when the trying stops.

**Stability is built through repetition of falling, not through avoidance of falling.** Every fall is a development. The body learns each time. The person who is too afraid to fall never builds the stability to walk.

**Internal stability is somatic, not intellectual.** The body learns the stability. The mind cannot reason the body into balance. Standing on a slackline is the moment the mind discovers its own limits as a stabilizing instrument; the body's wisdom is what makes it possible.

## What It Looks Like From Outside

People who walk mile-long slacklines look magical to observers. "How do you do it?" they ask.

The answer is undramatic: years of falling. Years of letting the body learn. Years of trusting the natural balance until the natural balance became reliably accessible.

The visible skill is the tip of the accumulated body history. The body history is the actual answer.

"People just see it like, this is amazing, how do you do it? And it's like, that's a really good metaphor for the internal security that... the more internal security you develop, and this can occur in physical reality, could occur in your social reality or business, the more secure in yourself you are, the less you need any specific external conditions to be any certain way. And the more you'll be able to remain in flow through whatever conditions end up occurring." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

## The Generalization to Internal Stability

Brett uses the metaphor to point at what internal stability is in general. The structure is the same in every domain:

- Initial fear and overcorrection in any new situation
- Many small failures that the body learns from
- A gradual reduction of the muscular gripping
- A discovery that there was a natural balance available all along
- A capacity that, once developed, generalizes beyond the original situation

The person who has developed internal stability in one domain has developed something that travels. Slackline stability and the stability to start a business and the stability to navigate a difficult conversation are not separate skills. They are the same underlying body capacity expressed in different terrain.

## What Joe Reaches For This Metaphor For

This is the canonical illustration of what internal stability is, how it develops, and why trying to control externals never produces it. The metaphor is useful when:

- A person is trying to make a situation safe by controlling external conditions
- A person is trying harder and harder and producing worse and worse results
- A person is afraid of failing and the fear of failing is preventing the failures that would build the capacity
- A person is in a beginner stage and treating their wobble as evidence they cannot do this
- A person is impressed by someone else's apparent stability and missing the years of accumulated falls underneath it

## The Joe Caveat

Joe interjects mid-metaphor to head off a misreading:

"What's interesting to me is there's somebody listening to this can be like, okay, I have to learn to be balanced so that I can be secure, which is in itself an overcorrection and a lack of security." -- Joe Hudson, But... Is It Safe?

The misreading: "stability is the goal, let me try harder to be stable." This is exactly the overcorrection that the metaphor warns against. The point is not to seek stability through more effort. The point is that stability is built by letting yourself fall, and that the trying-to-not-fall is what produces the falling.

Joe then pivots Brett toward an example of a real-life fall that taught stability. This is where the conversation moves to [[brett-cape-town-napkins]]: the involuntary fall that produced the accumulated security.

## The Pattern Across Brett's Life

Brett uses the slackline metaphor as the somatic anchor. The Cape Town story is the life-level evidence of the same development. Both stories teach the same underlying point: stability is accumulated through demonstrated survival of disruption, not through avoidance of disruption.

## Tone

The metaphor is delivered with the calm of someone who has walked many slacklines. The point is not the dramatic achievement of the mile-long line. The point is the unglamorous process of falling and learning. The tone matches the teaching: undramatic, somatic, grounded.
