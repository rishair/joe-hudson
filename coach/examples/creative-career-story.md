# Joe's Creative Career Story

## The Story

Joe was working in international stock lending early in his career. He decided what he wanted was a creative career, something he saw as far away and requiring years of effort to attain. He spent seven years managing his way toward that outcome.

He reached it. He was working on a TV show, had written something, and was being brought in to train to direct. After a two-week stint, he saw it clearly: the creative career was the same thing he had run away from. One person had all the power. Everyone worked long hours. Everyone was unhappy. The lead actor said: "Every time I come into work, it's like having a piece of my soul ripped out." Everyone wanted to be doing something else. Most stayed because of the money.

"I had spent all these years of my life trying to manage this outcome, and I had finally achieved it, but I didn't have any idea that it wasn't the thing that I wanted." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy over Manage

## The Shift

Joe made a decision: instead of managing toward outcomes, he would say yes to whatever showed up. If he enjoyed it, he would keep saying yes. If he did not enjoy it, he would say no. More opportunities appeared. He kept picking the more enjoyable ones.

"Everything took off -- my money, my happiness, my career, everything -- in ways that I couldn't even have expected. I had surrendered, and I allowed that surrender into my own enjoyment to lead my life." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy over Manage

## The Point It Illustrates

Management of outcomes produces a life that matches the plan, not a life that matches the person. Joe neither enjoyed stock lending, nor enjoyed trying to become an artist, nor enjoyed being the artist. The managed outcome was technically achieved but worthless. Enjoyment as a compass produced results management never could.

## When Joe Reaches for This

When someone is gripping a specific career outcome, life plan, or vision of success. When someone has achieved what they wanted and finds it empty. When the distinction between [[enjoy-over-manage]] needs a concrete illustration.
