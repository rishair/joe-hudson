# Joe's Creative Career Story

## The Story

Joe started in international stock lending — 5 a.m. until 3 or 4 in the afternoon, yelling into phones, in a soundproof room they called "the cage." The texture was specific and recognizable: one person in charge, everyone working long hours, everybody unhappy because they were not getting enough sleep, everybody snappy and chasing the next thing, nobody feeling deeply empowered. He looked at the structure and concluded: this is misery, I want out. The body delivered its own verdict — he broke out in hives from the stress.

The exit required preparation. He had been successful and had saved a lot. He figured out how to live on less money. The savings became the runway for the next chapter.

What he wanted instead was to be an artist. He spent seven years chasing it — cajoling, figuring out, managing. He eventually landed an observing-director slot on a TV show, having produced his own short film, on track to direct an episode.

He walked onto the set and saw it. Everybody was working hard. Everybody was miserable. Nobody wanted to be where they were; they all wanted to be in the next place. One person made all the decisions. Everybody was unhappy. The lead actor said: "Every time I come into work, it's like having a piece of my soul ripped out."

"Holy crap, this is just like investment banking. And so I have been running for 7 years towards an idea of what I want, only to find out that I was running towards the thing I was running away from." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The seven years of chasing had delivered him to the structural duplicate of what he had been escaping. The activity changed; the form of misery did not.

"I had spent all these years of my life trying to manage this outcome, and I had finally achieved it, but I didn't have any idea that it wasn't the thing that I wanted." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy over Manage

A sharper version of the same recognition, told later: "It dawned on me, I am running away from the same thing that I was running to, and I had no idea that that's what was happening. And it was in the search for purpose that all this was occurring." -- Joe Hudson, You Won't Find Your Purpose By Searching For It

The "no idea" is the operative phrase. The seven years of seeking did not produce visibility into what was being sought. The reaching toward an image of art-as-the-thing was simultaneously the reaching away from the structure of misery — and the image did not specify the structure, so the structure was inherited unchanged. The visibility arrived only at the moment of arrival, when the structure of the new chapter could be compared side-by-side to the structure of the old.

## The Shift

The shift was a single move: stop chasing a mental model of what would make him happy. Say yes to whatever was presented. Say no to what he did not enjoy. Let the narrowing happen on its own.

"Any opportunity that was put in front of me, I said yes. And I did metalworking, I did woodworking, I did this large-scale video art installation for this rich guy. I did all sorts of crazy things. And then I got so busy that I could only say yes to the things that were really exciting to me. And that's what I did. I just kept on saying yes to the things that were exciting and just narrowed down more and more and more." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The narrowing logic is operationally specific. The filter is not "what I think I should do." The filter is "what intrigues me." The intrigue produces the yes. Enough yeses produce too-full. Too-full produces the inability to do everything. The inability forces the next filter: "the most enjoyable stuff." That filter does the next narrowing without anyone needing to plan it.

"What I started doing was just saying yes to anything that intrigued me. I just was like, yep, I'm going to do that. Yes, I'm going to do that. Yes, I'm going to do that. And all of a sudden, my time was too full. I couldn't do everything that I was being asked to do. And so then I just did the most enjoyable stuff. And it went from whatever I was doing to making large-scale video art installations, to working for a big family office, to being a venture capitalist, to running a philanthropy, doing those two things at the same time." -- guest, You Won't Find Your Purpose By Searching For It

The two filters operate in sequence — intrigue, then enjoyment. The first opens the field; the second tightens it. Either filter alone is insufficient. Intrigue without the enjoyment filter produces dispersion; enjoyment without the intrigue filter produces the safety-default. The combination produces specificity that no one could have plotted in advance.

The narrowing was emergent, not planned. The yeses produced enough opportunity that the criterion for the next yes had to tighten. Enough yeses tightening enough times produced specificity that no upfront plan could have predicted.

"Everything took off -- my money, my happiness, my career, everything -- in ways that I couldn't even have expected. I had surrendered, and I allowed that surrender into my own enjoyment to lead my life." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy over Manage

## The Pivot Through a Conversation

One of those yeses — a large video art installation for a wealthy man — produced an unplanned consequence. Joe was talking to the man about what he thought was going to happen with oil, gold, and the stock market, drawing on his old investment-banking experience. The man offered him a job.

"He said, 'Wow, you know, you really understand money and I would love you to come and work with me.' I said, 'No, that doesn't make, that's not exciting to me. So I can't say yes to that.' And then he said, 'Well, why don't you think about exactly what would make you excited and then come back to me?' Which is like a huge gift. And I'm forever grateful for this man." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

Joe took three or four months to answer. The answer that arrived was specific: he wanted to make a huge change in climate and in consciousness, particularly with kids. The man said yes. Joe did venture capital for climate — businesses Joe thought could change the world via the change-agent of technology and capital — and ran a nonprofit foundation for kids.

The pivot was made possible by two things at once: the willingness to refuse a yes that did not resonate, and the willingness to take three months to find what would resonate. Without the refusal, the bank job would have absorbed him back into the misery-structure he had already left twice. Without the patience to find what would resonate, he would have substituted another mental model.

## The Next Evolution

Fifteen years later the venture-and-foundation work stopped feeling like the purpose-thread.

"For 15 years after that, I just kept on doing those things, kept on saying yes, until one day I was like, oh, this isn't feeling fulfilling anymore. This isn't actually what I want to be doing. And at that exact same time, enough people were asking me to coach them that I realized, oh, I want to say yes to this. And that became the next purpose in my life, because as you evolve, your purpose changes." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The pattern that produced the venture-capital chapter is the same pattern that produced the coaching chapter. Say yes to what is exciting. Let the next thing emerge from the saturation of the current thing. The form changes; the through-line — contributing to society, helping people see themselves more clearly — does not.

See [[purpose-is-an-evolution-not-a-destination]] for the principle the arc demonstrates.

## The Retrospective Recognition

Joe describes a felt moment that recurs in his life: looking back at the path and seeing every step retroactively as leading to where he stands.

"Every time in my life that I have this moment of, oh, here I am, I look back and I realize everything I did, the video that I did is now helping me in this YouTube video. The venture capital that I did is helping me understand how to build this business. Everything that I did in my past was leading to this moment." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The video work helps him with video now. The investment-banking work helps him build his coaching business now. The art installations, the foundation, the climate-VC chapter — all of it is operational equipment for what he is currently doing. Looking forward, none of it was visibly leading to this. Looking back, all of it was.

This is the marker of the path-not-map shape: the navigation is not visible in advance, but it is visible after the fact. The walk is what produced the route. The route was not pre-installed.

## The Point It Illustrates

Several principles are visible in this arc:

1. **You cannot know from a mental model what an experience will feel like.** The seven years toward art were premised on a thought about being an artist. The reality of being on set produced different information than the thought had promised. See [[mental-models-cant-tell-you-what-experience-feels-like]].

2. **Chasing the idea of a thing can deliver you to the structural duplicate of what you were escaping.** The misery of investment banking and the misery of TV directing were the same misery in different costumes. The chase did not solve the misery because the chase was not targeting the actual variable. See [[running-toward-the-thing-youre-running-from]].

3. **Saying yes to what is presented and letting the narrowing happen produces specificity that planning cannot.** The climate-venture-capital-plus-kids-foundation answer was not a plan; it was a residue of years of saying yes and narrowing. See [[enjoy-over-manage]].

4. **Purpose is an evolution, not a destination.** The art chapter, the venture chapter, the coaching chapter — three chapters, one through-line. The expectation that one of them was supposed to be the final answer would have produced the suffering of trying to stay in a chapter past its life. See [[purpose-is-an-evolution-not-a-destination]].

5. **Retrospect reveals the through-line that prospect cannot.** The constancy of "contribute to society, help people see themselves more clearly" was invisible at year five and visible at year thirty. The visibility comes from accumulated evidence, not from prior clarity. See [[purpose-is-how-not-what]].

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone is gripping a specific career outcome, life plan, or vision of success.
- When someone has achieved what they wanted and finds it empty.
- When the distinction between [[enjoy-over-manage]] needs a concrete illustration.
- When someone is chasing a mental model of a profession they have not actually been inside and Joe wants to surface that the model is not the reality.
- When someone is presenting [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] with years of trying and asking what to do — the story models the alternative: stop chasing, start saying yes to what is presented, let the narrowing produce the specificity.
- When someone is trying to install a single permanent answer and feels they should already have it — the story shows the answer evolves and that is the form.
