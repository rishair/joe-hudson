# Joe's High-Waisted Jeans Decoy

## The Story

For years, Joe loudly and consistently complained to Esme that he hated high-waisted jeans. His mom used to wear them, they were ugly, they did not look good on anyone. Esme, naturally, started wearing high-waisted jeans. The super-super-high ones. For years. She would strut by him in the morning wearing them, and he would make a fuss about it.

It was all a setup. Joe did not actually hate high-waisted jeans. He had constructed a decoy target so Esme could rebel against him in a low-stakes, safe direction.

"You gave me something to rebel against as a child because you knew it was a natural part of a thing. You're like, I'm gonna tell you I hate high-waisted jeans just so that you can wear them and be in rebellion against me for that, and so it doesn't come out in some weird other sideways way. And I wore high-waisted jeans for, for years." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

When Esme found out, about a year and a half to two years before the recording, she was first in pure shock, then thought: "what else is he making up?"

Joe's reflection on the move: "Oh my God, I made such a fuss about how I hated that." Acknowledged as a little duplicitous, but pretty funny — and effective.

## Why the Move Worked

The move solves a real parenting problem. Teenagers have a developmental need to push off against something. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]] for the swimming-pool-edge metaphor: the teen's job is to push off the edge. If there is nothing visible to push off, the pushing can come out in directions that are actually consequential — drugs, dangerous behavior, severed relationships.

Joe's solution was to provide an artificial, harmless target. Something the parent could plausibly disapprove of, that the teen could enact rebellion through, with no real damage to anyone. The pushing energy got directed at the jeans. Other things — Esme's actual relationship with her parents, her schoolwork, her safety — did not need to absorb the same energy.

The structural function: the developmental need was met without the cost of the developmental need being met against something that mattered.

## What Made It Defensible

The duplicity is real and Joe acknowledges it. The defensibility comes from several conditions:

- **The stakes were low.** Wearing high-waisted jeans had no real downside for Esme. The rebellion target was harmless.
- **The intent was care.** The move was structured to channel a developmental energy into a safe outlet, not to manipulate Esme into a parental preference.
- **It was eventually revealed.** Joe told Esme the truth at some point. The deception was not permanent. The revelation was part of the relationship Esme could process.
- **The relational ground was strong enough to hold it.** Esme's response to discovering it was "what else is he making up" — a reasonable reaction — and then, eventually, recognition of what the move was doing for her. The trust survived the revelation because the trust was robust.

The move would not be defensible if the stakes were higher, the intent was control, the deception was permanent, or the relationship could not absorb the revelation. Those conditions matter.

## The Larger Reflection on Rebellion

Joe contrasts his own teenage rebellion with what he tried to do for Esme. His own rebellion was hardcore — running away from home, getting kicked out, the green mohawk, eventually shipped off to boarding school. The rebellion was hardcore because there was nothing safe to push against. The whole structure of his parents' relationship to him was rejecting parts of who he was. The pushing had to go all-out because the squeeze was all-out.

"My rebellion was so hardcore as a teen. I think that was part of it, is that like, well, my teenage years just fucking sucked." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The high-waisted jeans decoy is, in some sense, a corrective for what Joe's own parents did not provide. They did not give him a low-stakes target. The pushing came out in high-stakes ways because the low-stakes channel did not exist.

The move is also, structurally, a recognition: teen rebellion is going to happen, and the parent can either pretend it should not happen and watch it come out sideways, or honor that it is a developmental need and engineer something for it to push against that does not cost anything.

This is the same principle as [[honor-the-patterns-intent]]: the pattern (rebellion) has a legitimate developmental function. Suppressing it does not work. Channeling it toward something harmless lets the function get fulfilled without the destructive cost.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

- When a parent of a teen is afraid of the rebellion that is coming and is trying to figure out how to prevent it
- When a parent realizes their teen is pushing against everything and they want a way to take some of the pressure off
- When a coachee is processing their own teenage rebellion and wondering why it had to be so destructive
- When the conversation is about how parents can honor developmental needs without controlling outcomes
- When the room could use the humor — the story is funny, and the humor itself is part of the teaching

## What Makes the Story Effective as Teaching

- It is funny. The image of Esme strutting in high-waisted jeans while Joe complains works on its own.
- It is a little subversive — the parent is being duplicitous, which is unexpected from a parenting teaching.
- It contains a real principle (rebellion is a developmental need that can be channeled) wrapped in a memorable image.
- It demonstrates that the parent's relationship to the teen's rebellion can be playful and engineered rather than reactive.
- The reveal — that it was all "for the plot" — adds another layer about the relationship being strong enough to hold the prank.

## What This Story Does Not Recommend

The story is not a recommendation for systematic parental deception. It is a recommendation that the parent recognize the developmental need for rebellion and can sometimes engineer harmless targets. The principle scales down — the parent can also just be slightly mock-disapproving of harmless things the teen does, without an elaborate decoy operation. The mock disapproval gives the teen something to push against in the conversation without the parent being actually controlling.

The story also does not say the parent should orchestrate the teen's whole life. The decoy is one channel; the teen still gets to push against real things when those come up. The move is one tool in a larger relationship that is built on [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]] and the other principles. A decoy without the broader respect-based relationship would be manipulation; inside the respect relationship, it is play.

## Tone

The telling is light, funny, slightly self-deprecating. Joe acknowledges the duplicity ("a little duplicitous") and finds it funny in retrospect. Esme is also amused. Hold that tone: this is a story about parental playfulness in service of a real developmental need, not a lecture about parenting technique.
