# The Asshole at Bodega Bay (Joe's Friend Calls Him an Asshole and It Lands)

## The Story

Joe is sitting at Bodega Bay. The tide is low and the bay looks a little swampy. The sun is going down, and the light is reflecting orange on the water.

His friend looks at him and says: "God, Joe, you're an asshole."

Joe's first reaction is the defensive one. "No, I'm not. Why would you call me that? What are you doing?"

The friend does not back down. "Ah, just relax, Joe. I still love you. You don't have to be upset that you're an asshole. You're just an asshole. Are you trying to tell me you're, like, not an asshole ever? You're an asshole. What's the problem?"

Joe describes the moment that follows: "Oh, and this moment happened where I realized, oh yeah, of course I'm an asshole. Everybody is an asshole at some point. And I can love that part of myself. I don't have to be ashamed of that part of myself. I don't have to defend that part of myself. I can just love that part of myself." -- Joe Hudson, After 14 Years of Coaching

What happens in the three months after: "The more that I loved that part of myself, the less I was that. That thing that I was resisting in myself continued, insisted that it existed because I was resisting it. And as I started to be gentle with it, as I started to love it, it transformed, it changed."

## The Point It Illustrates

Two principles in one story.

**First**, [[defense-vs-love]] as a live diagnostic. The reflex to defend is the marker that something is being half-believed. If a friend told Joe he was a unicorn, Joe would not defend himself. The defense activated because some part of Joe knew that "asshole" landed. The defense is the receipt of the half-belief.

**Second**, [[love-is-the-best-change-agent]] in action. Joe stopped defending and started loving the asshole part. Over three months, the behavior the part was generating reduced. Not because Joe tried to stop being an asshole. Because the part stopped needing to insist on itself once it was no longer being rejected.

The story is the operational demonstration of [[loving-all-parts]]. The mechanism the principle claims — that loving a rejected part allows it to move — is shown working in real time, in a specific person, in a specific timeframe, with a specific result.

## What the Friend Did Right

The friend's framing is worth studying. Three moves stacked in the recovery sentence:

1. **"Just relax, Joe."** — names the defensiveness without making it bad. Cues Joe to notice he is in defense.
2. **"I still love you."** — establishes that the naming is not a withdrawal of love. The relational base is stable. The naming is happening on top of love, not as a replacement for it.
3. **"You're just an asshole."** — refuses to soften the original observation. Does not retract. Does not minimize. The accuracy is held.

Add the closing question — "Are you trying to tell me you're not an asshole ever?" — and the friend has done the entire job of the move-from-defense-to-love for Joe. The question makes the implausibility of Joe's defense visible. Joe has to either claim he is never an asshole (which he knows is false) or release the defense. The friend has structured the choice so that release is the only honest move.

This is also a model for how to deliver hard feedback to someone in defense. The structure: name the defense, restate the love, hold the observation, ask the question that exposes the implausibility of the defended position. The combination produces the choice without forcing it.

## The Three-Month Window

The detail that "the more I loved that part of myself, the less I was that" over the next three months is worth keeping in the story. The transformation is not instantaneous. The shift happens in the moment at Bodega Bay. The behavioral change unfolds over months as the loved part stops needing to assert itself.

This timeframe matters for coaching. A client who has just had a similar realization will not see the behavior change in the next 48 hours. The realization is the inflection. The unwinding takes weeks or months. The work is to not re-attack the part during the unwinding, which would re-install the resistance that was driving the behavior.

If the client returns in a week and reports that the behavior is still happening, the coaching move is not "you must not have really gotten it." The move is "are you still loving the part, or did you start trying to change it again?" The loving has to continue past the initial recognition. The change agent is the sustained welcoming, not the moment of insight.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone is defending themselves against a piece of feedback that the defending itself reveals to be accurate. The story demonstrates the alternative to the defense.
- When someone has identified a part of themselves they reject and want to know what to do about it. The story is the operational answer: love it.
- When someone is in a long-running effort to change a behavior and the behavior is not changing. The story diagnoses why (the resistance is what is keeping the behavior in place) and points at the alternative (love the part that does the behavior).
- When someone wants to know whether love-as-change-agent is real or just a teaching. The story is the personal evidence with the specific timeframe and result.
- When someone confuses loving a part with endorsing the behavior. The story disambiguates: loving the asshole part did not make Joe more of an asshole. It made him less.

## The Two-Step Recognition

The story can be used as a template for the two-step move it demonstrates:

**Step 1: Notice the defense as the marker.** When you find yourself defending against an accusation, the defense itself is the data. You are defending because you half-believe it. If you did not half-believe it, you would not need to defend.

**Step 2: Drop the defense and love the part.** The defense is what keeps the rejected part frozen. Without the defense, the part is available to be loved. Love what is actually there, including the asshole part. The loving does not endorse the behavior; it removes the resistance that was sustaining it.

The two steps are sequential. The first is a recognition. The second is a stance. Most people stop at the recognition ("yeah, I see I was being defensive") without taking the second step ("now I love this part of me"). The second step is where the change happens.

## The Connection to the Seven-Patterns Framework

This story is positioned in the "After 14 Years of Coaching" video as the canonical worked example of the love-over-defense pattern (the seventh of the seven patterns Joe sees holding people back). See [[seven-limiting-beliefs]] for the framework and [[defense-vs-love]] for the distinction.

The position of the story in the framework matters. Love-over-defense is the most profound of the seven because it is the operational form of [[inherent-goodness]]. The asshole story makes the abstraction concrete: the part you are defending is a part you secretly think is bad. The work is not to prove it isn't bad. The work is to love it anyway, which dissolves both the defense and the bad-ness premise simultaneously.
