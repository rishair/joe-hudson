# Joe's Calling to Have Kids ('Knock, Knock, Knock, It's Time')

## The Story

For years Joe was a clear no on having kids. Tara, around age 30, started saying she wanted kids. Joe kept saying no, no, no, no. Then something shifted. Not through deliberation. Not through Tara convincing him. Not through a reasoned reconsideration. The shift was experiential -- a felt sense that the time had arrived.

"It was clear, like, oh, this is the -- the kid wants to come now. So for me, the odd experience, and it feels kind of woo-woo, but I can't deny what's true, is that like, it literally just felt like, oh, it's time, this wants to happen. So it was more of a calling and less of an intellectual decision to, okay, now's the time to have kids. It was like, literally felt like, it felt like, okay, now is the time." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

He and Tara had been having unprotected sex for years, being as careful as you can while being unprotected. The moment the calling arrived, they stopped being careful. Tara was pregnant immediately.

"We had been having unprotected sex for years. We were being as careful as you can being unprotected. But then we were like, okay, now it's time. I was like, now it's time to -- I was like, yeah, I feel it, it's time. And boom, like one, one, she was pregnant. It was just like that. It was so clear." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

## The Felt Quality of the Call

What made this striking, in retrospect, was the cleanness of the shift. Joe had expected the move from no to yes to be reluctant -- a gradual giving-in, a kicking-and-screaming concession to Tara's wanting. It wasn't.

"It was surprisingly like, yep, this is the time, I can't deny it. It was surprisingly that. It was like, I would have expected kicking and screaming because I was so -- I was like, no, no, no, for so long. And then when it hit yes, it was just so clear." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The felt sensation he describes is auditory and arriving from outside: "knock, knock, knock, knock, it's time, let me in." This is the calling. Not an internal calculation. Something requesting entry.

The same thing happened with Oona, their second child. Both times the call was clear and mutual. He and Tara could each feel the timing.

## The Asymmetry With the Third Kid

The third child arrived under different conditions. Tara was a clear yes. Joe was a clear no. The asymmetry was real and the call did not arrive for both of them at once. Joe is direct about naming this as a different experience from the first two:

"Now the third kid was like -- Tara was like a very clear yes, and I was a very clear no. And that was, that was a different thing. But both Oona and Esme, it was like a clear calling, like, this is it, this is the time, we're doing this now." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The story does not collapse into a single template. Sometimes both partners feel the call together. Sometimes one feels it and the other does not. The example is not "the call always comes for both of you." The example is "when the call comes, you can feel it; when it does not come, you can feel that too."

## The Financial Story That Did Not Hold

Joe also names the financial fear that almost stopped him before the call came. He believed he and Tara were not financially stable enough to have kids. The belief did not survive the actual experience.

"There was no intellectual decision being made. It was just very clear no, and then it was a very clear yes. And I wasn't really contemplating whether I should or shouldn't. I definitely remember feeling like we're not financially stable enough to have kids. Yeah, but we sure as hell were not financially stable enough when we conceived Esme. Like, between the moment of conceiving Esme and her birth, like, I went from like in from major debt just from like living because I was meditating all the time to completely out of debt in that 9-month period. And making like the best salary I'd ever made." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The structural observation he draws from this: people figure out how to take care of the kids who arrive. The financial fear is rarely the actual block. He cites the saying that "babies come with loaves of bread under their arms" -- the survival happens. The fear of not being ready is a different conversation from the actual outcome.

## What the Story Illustrates

The story is one of Joe's primary illustrations of [[enjoyment-as-surrender]] applied to life-direction decisions. The call is not chosen. The call is recognized. The work is not deliberation; the work is listening for the call and acting on it when it arrives.

It also illustrates [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]]. The years Joe spent saying no were not a decision in the sense of weighing pros and cons. They were a clear felt no. The shift to yes was also not a decision. It was a clear felt yes. The thing that was never present was the experience of deliberating, of trying to decide.

## When Joe Uses This Story

Joe reaches for this story when someone is sitting in a major life-direction question and trying to deliberate their way to clarity. The example does not say "wait until the call comes." It says: there is a category of major decisions that respond to listening rather than reasoning, and this is what listening can look like.

The story is particularly useful for the kids-or-no-kids fork (see [[should-we-have-kids]]). The presenting concern often arrives in deliberation language. Joe's example replaces the deliberation frame with the calling frame. The person can hear what it might be like to recognize a call rather than to manufacture an answer.

The story is also useful for non-parenting life-direction questions: should I take this job, should I leave this relationship, should I move countries, should I start this thing. The same pattern can apply. The deliberation has been going on for years. The call has not come. The work might not be more deliberation. The work might be sitting still long enough for the call to arrive or for the absence of the call to become its own clarity.

## What the Story Does Not Promise

The story does not promise that everyone gets a call. Some people do not have kids. Some people deliberate forever. Some couples have one partner who hears the call and one who does not, and have to navigate that asymmetry in real ways.

The story also does not promise the call will be reassuring. Joe's first call to have kids did not include any guarantee that he was financially ready, emotionally prepared, or going to do it well. The call arrived; it did not include reassurance.

## The Pattern Beyond Parenting

The same structure shows up in other big shifts Joe describes. The shift was not a decision; it was a recognition. The internal pull arrived; he followed it. This is the operational pattern of [[listening-to-impulse]] and [[enjoyment-as-surrender]] in the context of life direction.

The kids-calling story is the most legible example because the outcome is unambiguous (Esme exists, Oona exists) and the cleanness of the timing is striking (she was pregnant immediately after the call arrived). For listeners who are wary of the "calling" language, the cleanness of the outcome makes the example land.

## When to Tell This Story

- A coachee in the kids-or-no-kids deliberation
- A coachee in a different life-direction deliberation that has gone on for years without resolution
- A coachee who is trying to reason their way to a decision that is not responding to reason
- A discussion of [[enjoyment-as-surrender]] where a concrete example is needed
- A discussion of [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]] in the context of major life shifts
- A pre-parent who is asking how the decision to have kids actually gets made by people who do it well

## When Not to Tell This Story

The story can be misheard as "wait for the call and don't act until it comes." That is not the teaching. The teaching is that some decisions arrive as calls and the listener should be available to that mode of arrival. For some people, in some situations, the work is action, not listening. The story is not a prescription. It is an illustration of one way these things can come.

The story can also be misheard as "Joe and Tara had a magical experience that you should expect to have." Joe is careful to name that the third kid did not come with that same shared call. The example resists being made into a guarantee.
