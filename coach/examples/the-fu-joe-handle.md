# The @fu_joe Handle

## The Story

Joe's Twitter handle is @fu_joe (short for "Fuck You Joe"). The origin and the function reveal two distinct uses of the practice of self-mockery.

The origin came from live VIEW sessions. When Joe would go around a room asking people to practice VIEW, they would get self-conscious -- they had to do it "right" in front of him. The self-consciousness blocked the practice. Joe discovered a hack: if the person looked at him and said "Fuck you, Joe," the self-consciousness broke. They would laugh. They would release the little anger that was behind the self-consciousness. They could go back to the practice unguarded.

The handle institutionalized the hack. The handle is a continuous public invitation to take anger out on him as a way to dissolve the self-consciousness people feel about being seen by an authority figure. It is also a structural reminder, in his own life, that he is not the authority figure people might be projecting onto him.

"The Fuck You Joe handle came from -- what I noticed when I was doing live sessions was, every time I got in front of somebody who was practicing VIEW and I'd go around the room, they'd get self-conscious like they had to do it right. So one of the hacks that I found, so somebody wouldn't be self-conscious, is if they just looked at me and said 'Fuck you, Joe,' then all of a sudden they wouldn't be. They'd laugh, they'd get out that little anger that's behind the self-consciousness, and they could go back to it. So it was an invitation to take anger out on me as a way for them not to be self-conscious." -- Joe Hudson, Humility Q&A

## What It Demonstrates

The example demonstrates three things at once.

**1. The mechanism of self-consciousness.** Self-consciousness in front of an authority figure is held together by suppressed anger and the need to perform correctness. Saying "fuck you" directly to the authority figure releases the anger and pops the performance. The person is freed to actually be present.

**2. The use of self-mockery as a structural tool.** Joe is not being self-deprecating for its own sake. The handle does work. It makes him less of a target for projection, less of a halo, less of an authority figure. It is a deliberate move to keep the relational field clear.

**3. The connection between humility and humor.** A person who can be the butt of the joke without defending against it is a person whose identity is not in the way. The willingness to be made fun of -- and to invite it -- is a structural signal of humility. The willingness to make fun of yourself is the same signal from the other direction.

## Why It Works (Without Becoming Its Own Identity)

A risk with self-mockery: it can become its own identity. "I am the teacher who is humble enough to make fun of myself." The performance of humility is itself the trap [[the-arrogantly-humble-paradox]] warns against.

The handle avoids the trap because of how it is held. It is not a flag. It is a continuous, low-grade joke. It is connected to a specific practical use (the live-session hack). It is not deployed as evidence of Joe's humility. It just exists, like the teenage daughters who make fun of him exist. The function is structural, not performative.

The structural difference: the self-mocker who is using the self-mockery as identity will defend the self-mockery when challenged. The self-mocker whose self-mockery is structural will not notice or care if you say "you are just being humble for show." Either it is or it isn't. There is no defense.

## Related Live-Session Application

The "fuck you Joe" hack from live sessions is itself a coaching move worth noting. When a student is too self-conscious to practice in front of you, the move to dissolve the self-consciousness is not "you do not need to be self-conscious." That feeds the self-consciousness. The move is to invite the suppressed anger out directly. Once the anger has moved, the self-consciousness is gone.

This is a specific instance of [[loving-the-resistance]]: working with what is actually present (the suppressed anger and the projection of authority) rather than trying to talk the person out of it. The hack is a small, applied form of that general principle.

## The Point

Self-mockery, deployed structurally rather than performatively, is one of the few reliable ongoing correctives to the gravitational pull toward becoming the authority-figure that public roles produce. The @fu_joe handle is one operational form of this. The teenage daughters are another. The being-roasted-by-close-friends is another. Each operates on the same mechanism: continuous, low-grade reminders that the role is not the person.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with affection and amusement. He likes the handle. He likes the hack. The tone is light. The lightness itself is part of the teaching -- humility done well is not somber. It carries laughter inside it.
