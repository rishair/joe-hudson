# The Friend in Bodega Bay Who Called Joe a Dick

## The Story

Joe describes a moment from his own life. He was sitting in Bodega Bay with a friend. The friend looked at him and said, "You know, Joe, you're a dick."

Joe defended. "No, I'm not." Then started to qualify, to soften, to make the conversation comfortable.

The friend would not let the comfort happen. But also did not push. He just stayed with the naming, repeating it gently, with no investment in Joe accepting it: "Ah, geez. Can we not, can we not — that's all unnecessary. It's no problem. You're a dick. Like, I love you just the same. Why, why worry about this? Just admit you're a dick."

"He wasn't trying to change me. He wasn't moving, you know, it might have from the outside, my, maybe somebody said it's really impartial. He would want me to say that I was a dick, but what was clear was that he was just gonna love me. He was open-hearted towards me no matter what, even if I sat there and kept on being defensive." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

## What This Illustrates

The friend was demonstrating [[speak-the-elephant-from-open-heart-not-fear]] in the most challenging form — naming something about Joe directly, without managing how Joe would receive it, without needing Joe to accept it, without losing the love that grounded the naming.

The teaching: you can say ridiculous-sounding things ("you're a dick") and have them land as love if the inner posture is right. The content does not have to be diplomatic. The inner ground does.

"That's the interesting piece. The interesting piece is that you can say ridiculous stuff if you can keep an open heart and not buy into the shame game, not buy into the blame game. And maybe you have to play that to 3 rounds, right? What do you mean I look like shit? Do you know who I am? Yeah, I know, but come on, you look like shit. There's no problem. We're going to fix it. But geez, like, you all right? I'm concerned about you as a human. This isn't a personal thing." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

## The Three-Round Pattern

The story exemplifies a specific pattern that often unfolds when the elephant is named openheartedly. The listener defends. The speaker stays open. The listener defends again. The speaker stays open. By round three, the defense has nowhere to go.

In Joe's story:

- **Round 1.** Friend: "You're a dick." Joe: "No, I'm not." (Defense)
- **Round 2.** Friend: "I love you the same. Just admit you're a dick." Joe: still resisting. (Defense)
- **Round 3.** Friend: still openhearted. The defense has nothing to fight against because the friend is not attacking.

The defense requires an attacker. Without an attacker, the defense exhausts itself. The friend's continued openheartedness was the move that disarmed the defense.

## What the Friend Did Not Do

The friend did not:

- Get hurt that Joe defended
- Push harder when Joe resisted
- Withdraw the naming as a way of soothing Joe
- Make the conversation a referendum on whether Joe was a good person
- Try to extract Joe's admission
- Make Joe wrong for defending

He just kept being there. Kept loving Joe. Kept noting the observation. The non-attachment to Joe's response was what allowed Joe to actually hear the observation.

## What This Says About Receiving Hard Truth

The story is also instructive for the receiving side. Joe's defense was natural and predictable. The defense did not mean the friend's observation was wrong. The defense was just what bodies do when something lands.

The friend's response to the defense — staying open, not retracting, not pushing — is what created the space for Joe to eventually take the observation in. Joe does not describe in this telling whether he ever admitted to being a dick. The teaching is not contingent on the admission. The teaching is in the friend's posture.

A different friend, hearing Joe defend, might have backed off ("oh, sorry, I didn't mean it, you're not a dick"). The retraction would have collapsed the moment. Joe would have learned: he can defend his way out of feedback. The truth-friend who delivers the observation and then retracts it is not actually delivering an observation; they are testing whether they can deliver it without consequences.

The friend who delivers and stays is doing the work. The work is offering the observation with no demand and no retraction. The receiver can do with it what they will. The offering remains intact.

## The Connection to "I'm None of My Own Business"

The friend's posture overlaps with [[im-none-of-my-own-business]] — both the speaker and the listener stance. The friend is not in his own business about whether Joe accepts him. Joe, ideally, would not be in his own business about whether he is "the kind of person who is a dick." The lightness in the story is the lightness that comes from neither person being too invested in themselves as the subject.

The naming becomes possible because the friend is not invested in Joe's response. The acceptance becomes possible because Joe is not invested in his own identity remaining intact. The two postures match. The conversation can be playful.

## The Connection to Sarah's Quote

Joe uses this story as the bridge to the "I'm none of my own goddamn business" teaching. The friend was demonstrating the receiver-side of that posture — he was not making Joe's response his business. The naming was offered. Joe's reception was Joe's affair.

"It was that same place that he came from. Right? There was nothing that he, he didn't, he wasn't trying to change me. He wasn't moving, you know, it might have from the outside, my, maybe somebody said it's really impartial. He would want me to say that I was a dick, but what was clear was that he was just gonna love me." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The impartiality is critical. The friend wanted nothing from Joe in particular. He had no preference for Joe's response. He had no agenda to change Joe. He just named what he saw and continued to love.

## How This Story Functions in the Teaching

Joe uses this story when he needs to show:

- The naming can be of something seemingly insulting ("you're a dick") and still be loving
- The receiver's defense is not the failure of the naming; it is just the receiver's defense
- Staying with the naming through multiple rounds of defense is the work
- The naming requires impartiality about the response
- Friendship at this depth includes the capacity to say things that would, in shallower friendship, be unsayable

The story is the personal-scale complement to [[makeup-artist-and-the-rock-star]]. Both illustrate openhearted naming. The makeup artist is the stranger version (low-stakes for the relationship, high-stakes for the moment). The friend is the intimate version (high-stakes for the relationship, lower-stakes for the moment). Both work because of the inner posture.

## When Joe Reaches For It

Joe uses this story when:

- Demonstrating that openhearted naming can include content that would normally be inflammatory
- Showing the receiver-side of the practice — what to do when the named person defends
- Illustrating impartiality in interpersonal naming
- Teaching that the speaker does not need the listener to agree for the naming to be complete
- Pointing to the kind of friendship that includes the capacity for this depth of honesty

## Tone

Joe tells this story with affection. The friend is not named in the telling but is clearly important to Joe. The story is delivered with the lightness that is part of the teaching — calling Joe a dick is funny, not tragic, because the love underneath the naming was secure. The humor is part of how the naming lands.
