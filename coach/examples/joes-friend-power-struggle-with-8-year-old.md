# Joe's Friend Having a Power Struggle With His 8-Year-Old

## The Story

Joe is on his friend's farm. The friend is having a bad parenting moment — a power struggle with his 8-year-old daughter. Joe, watching, says directly: "Dude, she's 8. Your job is to never have a power struggle with your 8-year-old."

"You might think like, oh, it'd be cool to be friends with Joe. It is not always cool to be friends with Joe. And so I'm hanging out on this guy's farm, and he's having a bad parenting moment. We all have them. He's having a bad parenting moment, and he's having a power struggle with his 8-year-old. And I'm like, dude, she's 8. Like, your job is to never have a power struggle with your 8-year-old, right? You're telling her that, that you're not in control by having a power struggle." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The opening aside — "it is not always cool to be friends with Joe" — is Joe naming that he is the friend who will say this out loud. The teaching that follows is what makes the moment worth being uncomfortable.

## What Is Actually Happening

Joe's read of the moment: the friend is not having a power struggle with his daughter because the daughter is the problem. The friend is replicating, in the present, what was modeled to him in his own childhood.

"What you're really doing is you're replicating that when you were a kid, love came with a fight. There was always a fight." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The dad's body, in the moment of conflict with the child, is doing what his body learned love does. His parents and he fought; the fights were the contact; the contact was the love. As an adult parent, when contact with his child needs to happen, the body reaches for the form of contact it learned — the fight. The power struggle is not the dad failing at parenting in a discrete moment. It is the love-imprint operating on the next generation.

## What the Daughter Gets

The teaching's sharpest point is what the daughter receives from the configuration. She does not get love. She gets one of two things:

"How does that daughter grow up? The daughter grows up feeling like she was forced and pushed into stuff. And so she's going to obviously rebel or she's going to say, okay, whatever you say, Dad, but it's not going to be love. He's not going to get love. He might get compliance, he might get rebellion, but he's not going to get love. And that's his way of even with his daughter wanting the love and pushing away the love at the same time." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The two options the daughter has — compliance or rebellion — are both not-love. Compliance is submission to power. Rebellion is reaction against power. Neither is the contact the dad presumably wants. The fight is the form he is using to try to get love and it is the form that guarantees love will not arrive.

This is the worked-out version, in parenting, of the fight category in [[three-ways-we-push-love-away]]: the fight is the trained love-form, the fight produces the conditions where love cannot land, both parties leave the configuration with less of what they wanted.

## What the Daughter's Future Looks Like

The teaching extends past the moment. The daughter, having had her childhood love wired with fights, will likely grow into an adult whose body recognizes love-as-fight. She may:

- Choose partners who fight with her constantly (the wiring is recognized)
- Find peaceful partners boring (the wiring is missing)
- Start fights with partners who are kind to her (the body produces the wiring when it does not arrive)
- Treat any contact that is not contestation as not-love
- Or operate in the avoid posture instead — refusing close contact entirely because all close contact feels like an incoming fight

The dad's power struggle in the moment is not contained to the moment. It is one of the installation events for the daughter's adult love-pattern. The pattern that the dad himself is operating from is being passed forward. See [[love-pattern-mimics-childhood-love-pattern]] for the broader principle this is one specific instance of.

## What "Your Job Is to Never Have a Power Struggle With Your 8-Year-Old" Means

Joe's direct instruction is operationally useful, not just rhetorical. The 8-year-old does not have the developmental capacity to be in an even contest with a parent. A power struggle with an 8-year-old is the parent collapsing the asymmetry of authority and pretending it is a contest. The pretense is what produces the wiring.

The dad has authority. The dad does not need to fight to use it. The dad's resort to fighting signals to the child that something other than authority is being expressed — usually the dad's own unresolved relationship to authority from his own childhood. The child reads the dad's loss of asymmetry as a sign that something is wrong with the parent, with the situation, or with herself.

"You're telling her that you're not in control by having a power struggle." The parent who knows they have authority does not need to win a fight to demonstrate it. The fight is itself the demonstration that the authority is being doubted internally. The parenting move is to operate from the authority that does not need to be fought for.

## The Point It Illustrates

**Love-came-with-fight is one of the three primary forms of pushing love away** (see [[three-ways-we-push-love-away]]). The dad is running the fight category. He is reaching for contact with his daughter through the form his body learned was contact, and the form guarantees the contact will not produce the love either side wants.

**The wiring passes generationally.** The dad's parents had it. The dad has it. The daughter is being installed with it now. Each generation passes the form forward unless someone in the chain does the work to separate love from the fight.

**The fight cannot produce love. It can produce compliance or rebellion.** This is the structural point. Compliance is not love. Rebellion is not love. Both are responses to power, not exchanges of love. The fighter is reaching for love with an instrument that cannot deliver it.

**The parent and child both want love and both push it away in the same moment.** The dad wants connection with his daughter. The daughter wants connection with her dad. The fight is the form both bodies have been trained to use. The fight prevents the connection both want. The push-away is happening on both sides simultaneously, through the same configuration.

**The work to interrupt the generational pass is to recognize the wiring in the moment of operating it.** The dad cannot stop the wiring by intending to. The wiring fires faster than intention. What can change is the recognition of what the fight is doing. The recognition does not have to be perfect or constant; even partial recognition begins to open space for a different move. The recognition of the wiring is the first move in [[what-was-wired-with-love-needs-untying]].

## The Comparison to the Adult Versions

The power struggle on the farm is the same structure as:

- Couples who fight constantly about everything (same fight-as-love wiring)
- Workplaces where every interaction with a boss is a power contest (same wiring extended into authority relationships)
- Friendships that are entirely about who is right (same wiring extended into peer relationships)
- The internal version: the self-fight, the inner critic that the person constantly battles (see [[negative-self-talk-reveals-love-imprint]])

The dad's moment with the 8-year-old is the cleanest visible form of the structure because the asymmetry between parent and 8-year-old makes the absurdity of the fight legible. The adult-to-adult versions look more like reasonable disputes. The structural mechanism is the same.

## The Specific Detail That Makes the Example Work

The 8-year-old's age matters. The teaching depends on the asymmetry between parent and 8-year-old being large enough that the fight cannot be defended as a real contest. A power struggle with a teenager raises the question of whether the teen is asking for the contest, whether the parent is responding to actual provocation, whether the parent and teen are negotiating an evolving authority relationship. A power struggle with an 8-year-old does not have those plausible defenses. The asymmetry is so large that the fight is unambiguously the parent's wiring at work.

The friend-on-the-farm setting also matters. Joe is not in a coaching session. He is a friend at a house. The "it is not always cool to be friends with Joe" framing is Joe acknowledging that the teaching is being delivered uninvited, in a context where it would be easier to say nothing. The directness of friend-to-friend is what allows the teaching to be specific enough to land. The friend is not asking for a coaching observation; the friend gets one anyway, because Joe has decided the moment is worth being uncomfortable about.
