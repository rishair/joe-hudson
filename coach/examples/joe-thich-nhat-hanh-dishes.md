# Joe and the Thich Nhat Hanh Dishes

## The Story

Joe hated doing the dishes. It was the chore he did not want to do. Then he read Thich Nhat Hanh describing doing the dishes as a pleasurable experience: warm water, soap, the sensation of it. Joe tried it. The dishes became pleasurable. The pleasure was there the whole time. The thing he had been calling a chore had been a candidate for pleasure all along.

The realization extended immediately. If the dishes could be pleasurable, anything could be. He started running an experiment: how much pleasure could he extract from any given moment? Not by doing pleasurable things — by noticing what was pleasurable in whatever was already happening.

"I started experimenting with pleasure. A lot. I remember just experimenting and saying, how much pleasure can I extract from life? And the amazing thing about it was that it wasn't what I thought it was going to be, right? I thought it was going to be like having sex all the time or something." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The expected form of pleasure-chasing — sex, intoxication, peak experiences — was not what showed up. The thing that showed up was different. Pleasure that has to be chased is not the same thing as pleasure that is always available. The first cannot be sustained because the conditions for it cannot be sustained. The second can be, because the conditions are just the noticing.

## The Forgetting

A second beat of the story that Joe keeps in. After the initial breakthrough, the practice fell away.

"And it was like a month later, I'm like, wait a second, what the fuck just happened? Like, a month ago I realized that I could be in pleasure all the time, and now it's a month later and I did it like once. Like, what, what, what was that?" -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The forgetting is part of the teaching. The insight does not install itself once and stay installed. The system reverts to its old configuration even after a major realization. The practice has to be re-entered repeatedly. This is also why a one-time exposure to the teaching is rarely sufficient — the body has to come back to it many times before the orientation becomes the default.

## The "It's an Avoidance" Realization

After the second pass, Joe arrived at the structural insight that organizes the teaching:

"Once you realize you can have it anytime, it's an avoidance. Why am I avoiding this? Because it's the only option." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The framing flips. Before the realization, not feeling pleasure is the default and feeling it is an exception requiring effort. After the realization, pleasure is the default and not feeling it is an active operation of avoidance. The question "why am I not feeling pleasure right now?" becomes a real question — there is now an answer in terms of what the system is doing to push the pleasure away.

This is the seed of the broader teaching that pleasure-capacity is trainable and pleasure-not-being-felt is a clamp, not a circumstance. See [[pleasure-capacity]] and [[fear-of-joy]].

## What the Story Establishes

The story is the origin point for several distinct teaching points:

- **Pleasure is available in any moment if attention can land on the sensation that is there.** The dishes work as a test case because the dishes are mundane. If the dishes can be pleasurable, the universal claim becomes plausible.
- **Pleasure-chasing is not what the practice is.** What Joe expected (sex, peak experiences) is not what arrived. Real pleasure-practice is closer to the dishes than to the Lamborghini. See [[chasing-pleasure-is-avoidance]].
- **The practice has to be re-entered.** The first breakthrough does not install. The teaching has to be returned to.
- **Not-feeling-pleasure is the active operation.** Once pleasure is known to be available, the absence of pleasure becomes data about what the system is doing rather than data about what the world is providing.

## Why Joe Tells This

The story functions as the personal credential behind the teaching. Joe is not relaying something he read; he is reporting an experiment he ran in his own life. The arc — hated the chore, read the framing, tried it, found it, lost it, found it again — is recognizable enough that listeners can map their own potential experiment onto it.

The story is also disarming. The opening is unglamorous (a man who hates the dishes). The realization is small (water and soap are nice). The teaching that follows (pleasure is the natural state when not being clamped) is large. The small opening keeps the large teaching from sounding like a sales pitch.

## Tone

Light, casual. The story is told without ceremony. The "wait, what the fuck just happened" beat is told for the laugh. The seriousness of the teaching is carried entirely by the small concrete details — warm water, soap, dishes — rather than by emphatic delivery.
