# The Healthcare CEO Turnaround (Connection 5x'd the Company)

## The Story

A healthcare company in Joe's investment portfolio brought in a new CEO. The situation was really a turnaround, though she had not done a turnaround before. The standard turnaround playbook is to fire half or more of the people in the first six months and re-establish the business with a new team.

She did not do that. Instead, she made connection the operating principle.

- Weekly surveys went out to employees.
- She constantly connected with customers and with employees.
- She kept attention on whether people were happy and connected to the work.
- She tracked the felt state of the organization continuously, not as an HR exercise but as the central management activity.

In two years she 5x'd the company.

"It was just a tremendous thing to watch her. And it was her number -- she was more prioritized on that. You know, as a CEO, what you notice is you have to focus on like 2 or 3 things and the thought process is, 'If I focus on these 2 or 3 things, everything else will work around it.' And she was just maniacal on that sense of connection. And feeling connected and being connected herself. And it was just, it was amazing to watch that turnaround." -- Joe Hudson, Connection: A State Beyond States

## What She Did Differently

CEOs choose two or three things to focus on, with the implicit theory that focus on those things will make everything else fall into place. Most CEOs in a turnaround pick things like cost reduction, revenue growth, product simplification. She picked connection. Not as a soft adjacent value. As the central operating principle.

The implication: the cost reduction, revenue growth, and product improvements were not explicit goals. They were outcomes that arose when the connection was right. The leverage point was connection. The metrics were downstream.

This is consistent with [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]]: the CEO's actual operating principle becomes the organization's culture. She was not asking her people to do something she was not doing herself. She was being connected, and the company became one where people were connected.

## Connection to Southwest Airlines

Joe ties the story to a more famous example: Southwest Airlines, which historically operated under the principle of employees first, customers second, shareholders third.

"If your employees feel that sense of connection with the company, then the customers will be happy. If the customers are happy, then the shareholders will be happy. It's how they formed that business." -- Joe Hudson

The structural insight is the same: connection at the upstream point (employees) cascades through the system. Trying to optimize for the downstream point (shareholders) without the upstream connection produces neither the connection nor the financial result.

The healthcare CEO worked the same principle. Connection was the leverage point. The turnaround was the result.

## Why This Example Matters

In the connection podcast, the healthcare CEO story is the third of three pieces of evidence that converged for Joe and produced [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]] as a principle. The personal example came from [[case-the-consultant]]. The data came from the [[harvard-grant-study]]. The scaled business application came from the healthcare CEO.

The function of the example is to show that the principle scales. Case operated as an individual. The Harvard study aggregates individuals. The healthcare CEO is the principle running at the scale of a company.

Together the three pieces of evidence cover the three levels at which someone might want proof: individual, statistical, and organizational. After all three, the principle is hard to write off.

## What This Example Is Not

The example is not a recommendation to never do layoffs. Layoffs are sometimes the right move. The instruction is not "always avoid layoffs"; it is "do not let the playbook substitute for actually understanding what your organization needs."

The CEO did the unusual thing because connection-as-principle led her to it. A different organization with different needs might require a different choice. The point is that the principle is what made the choice obvious to her, not that connection-as-principle always produces no layoffs.

## When Joe Uses This Example

Joe uses this example when the person is skeptical that connection works in business. The objection is usually some version of "that's nice for marriages but it doesn't apply to running a company." The story addresses the objection directly: a company applied it as the central operating principle and 5x'd in two years.

The example is particularly useful in the context of turnaround conversations, leadership coaching, or any situation where the person is being told to do the harsh thing because it is the standard playbook.
