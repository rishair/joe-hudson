# The Vietnam Malnutrition Story (Positive Deviance)

## The Story

A man went to Vietnam with Save the Children or a similar organization. He had $50,000 and six months. His mandate: stop malnutrition in children across an entire county. The local officials met him at the airport with the warning that if he failed, he would be kicked out.

The problem looked impossible. The budget would not cover food distribution at the required scale. The classic approach -- buy food, distribute it -- could not arithmetically produce the required outcome.

Instead of starting with what was wrong, he started with what was right. He went village to village looking for one where the children were not malnourished. Eventually he found one.

In that village, the families:
- Split the same amount of rice into three meals instead of two
- Took a specific weed out of the rice paddy and chopped it up very finely, then added it to the rice
- Added microscopic shrimp from the paddies

All the inputs were already locally available. There was no missing resource. The village had figured out the recipe.

He took his $50,000 and used it differently. He brought mothers from the other villages into the working village. He showed them what the working village did and the measurable difference in their children. The mothers carried the practice back. The whole county changed how it fed its children. The problem was solved.

"He goes into the first village, second village, and he finally finds a village where the kids aren't malnourished. And he's like, 'Oh, what's happening here?' And he found out that instead of the same amount of rice in 2 meals, it was in 3 meals. That they took this weed out of the garden and they -- out of the rice paddy and they chopped it up really finely, put it in, and then they took these like little microscopic shrimp and put them in. And so they had -- it was just all right there available." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

"He took that $50,000 and he took the moms from all the other villages, brought them in with some kids and said, look, if you do this, this is measurably what your kids will be different as if you compare to do this. And all of a sudden, all the whole county changed their way of feeding their children, and he solved the problem." -- Joe Hudson

## What the Story Illustrates

The story is the canonical example of [[see-whats-right-to-build-on-it]] as a problem-solving method. The first-path approach -- buy food, distribute food -- was budget-impossible. The second-path approach -- find what is already working and propagate it -- was budget-trivial.

The structural lesson: when a problem is everywhere, it is often not everywhere. There is usually an exception. The exception contains the solution. The work is to find the exception and study what makes it exceptional.

This is sometimes called positive deviance in the development literature. Joe uses the story as an instance of the broader principle that seeing what is right reveals solutions that seeing what is wrong cannot.

## The Counterfactual

The alternate version of the story is salient and worth naming. If the same man had taken the $50,000 and tried to use it to buy food and distribute it, he would have failed. The math would not have worked. He would have left without solving the problem.

"I could imagine an alternate story where he tried to use that $50,000 to purchase food and bring it to the kids in all those other communities and that the scalability of that with that money just wouldn't have done it." -- Brett Kistler, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

"Yeah, yeah, like whatever, there's, I think hundreds of billions of dollars used that way in Africa and didn't work." -- Joe Hudson

The first-path approach is not just locally suboptimal. It is the approach that has consumed hundreds of billions of dollars of international development aid with poor results. The story is not just a clever anecdote; it is a small-scale demonstration of a structural failure in the dominant problem-solving paradigm.

## The Mechanism

Why did the working village work? Not because of an outside intervention. Not because of new resources. Because of a local innovation that nobody outside the village had ever asked about. The resources were already present. The resourcefulness was the variable.

This is [[resources-vs-resourcefulness]] applied to a development problem. The other villages had the same rice, the same paddies, the same weeds, the same shrimp. They did not have the recipe. The $50,000 spent on showing them the recipe was a leveraged investment in resourcefulness rather than in resources.

The constraint of the budget forced the second-path solution. With unlimited money, the first-path approach (buy food) would have been available and probably chosen, with worse results. See [[constraints-increase-creativity]].

## When Joe Uses It

Joe brings out this story when:

- A leader is trying to solve a problem with brute-force resource application and is hitting the limit
- A team is in problem-listing mode and not finding solutions
- The teaching is on the negativity bias and a concrete illustration is needed
- Someone is resisting gratitude or positive scanning as Pollyanna-ish, and the story shows that the positive scan was the only thing that solved a real, hard, measurable problem
- A coaching context where the person believes their situation is hopeless because "everything is wrong" -- the story interrupts the totalizing frame

## The Point It Lands

The point is not "be positive." The point is that the data you collect determines the solutions you can generate. If the only data you collect is what is wrong, you can generate only solutions that address what is wrong. If you also collect what is right, you can generate solutions that propagate what is already working.

The asymmetry is large. The first approach is bounded by the cost of intervening from outside. The second approach is bounded by the cost of finding and propagating what is already happening inside. The second is usually much cheaper and often much more effective.
