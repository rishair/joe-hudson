# The Two Cups Parable

## The Story

A man comes to the Buddha. He has studied with many teachers. He is not enlightened yet. He asks for help.

The Buddha agrees to teach him. The next day, the Buddha begins to teach. The man interrupts: "but hold on a second, I studied with this other guy and he said this."

The Buddha tries again. The next day, the same thing: "but I studied with this other guy and he did this."

After several days of this, the Buddha tells him: come back tomorrow and bring two cups. One full of water, one full of milk.

The man returns with the two cups, both full. The Buddha says: now pour the cup of milk into the cup of water.

The man does. The milk overflows. There is no room for the milk in a cup that is already full of water.

The Buddha's point: if you want to learn something, you need to empty your mind first.

## The Source

Dr. K tells the parable in conversation with Joe and Charlie Hooper, in the context of explaining how Joe is able to sit with people the way he does. Joe affirms the teaching and recognizes himself in it.

"So I'd encourage everybody to pause the video right now and do this at home. Take a cup of milk, take a cup of water, pour the cup of milk into the cup of water. Have them both full at the beginning. What happens? The milk overflows. If you want to learn something, you need to empty your mind first. If you want to sit with someone, you have to empty yourself." -- Alok Kanojia, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

## What the Parable Points To

The parable is about two related operations that have the same structure.

**Learning.** A mind that is already full of opinions, knowledge, prior teachings, and self-references cannot take in new material. Each new piece of information arrives at a mind that immediately compares it to what is already there, classifies it, defends against it, or assimilates it into the existing frame. The new material does not actually enter; only the version of it that the existing mind accepted enters. This is not learning. This is the existing mind processing the new material into more of itself.

Real learning requires the mind to empty enough that the new material can enter without being immediately transformed into what is already known. The emptying is the precondition. The teaching is what fills the emptied space, if any teaching is offered. Without the emptying, no teaching can land, regardless of how skillful the teacher is.

**Sitting with someone.** A coach, therapist, friend, or partner who shows up full of themselves cannot take in what the other person is actually offering. The full self is busy with its own thoughts, its own framing, its own response-construction. The attention is divided between what is being offered and what the listener is doing internally. What the listener registers of the other person is the filtered, partial version that made it through.

Real attending to another person requires emptying first. The emptying is what makes attention available. Once attention is available, the other person enters, and the listener can attend to themselves from inside the other person's offering — which is the operational mechanism of empathic attunement.

## The Same Operation Twice

The parable points at a single structural feature of receiving, applied to two domains. The full cup cannot receive milk because there is no room. The full self cannot receive teachings because there is no room. The full self cannot receive another person because there is no room.

The teaching, in any of its forms, is the same: empty first, then receive. The order matters. Trying to receive without emptying does not produce receiving; it produces a distorted version of what was offered, shaped by what was already in the cup.

## How Joe Uses This in Practice

In the same conversation, Joe describes his own coaching state in terms that align with the parable. The relevant features:

- "I'm vulnerable in the listening and in the asking of questions."
- "There's a deep impartiality in me."
- "I don't think I need to get that person anywhere. I'm not there to help them."
- "There's just not a lot of I in the equation."
- "Oftentimes at the end of it, I don't remember what happened. I don't know if it went well, and I don't care."

Each of these is an operational description of the emptied state. The lack of I in the equation is the structural emptiness the parable points at. The not-remembering-what-happened is the signature of having been present rather than having been processing. The not-needing-to-get-the-person-anywhere is the absence of the agenda that, if present, would have been filling the cup.

Dr. K names the same state in his own work:

"The more empty you become, the more that you remove your ego from the equation, then you can attend fully to someone else... He is an instrument. He's like a radio wave. He's like a radio antenna picking up other things. But the more that you have your own shit going on within yourself..." -- Alok Kanojia, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The antenna metaphor extends the parable. The empty cup is what the antenna is — a structure with nothing of its own occupying the channel. The other person's signal can pass through. The signal that comes back from the listener is responsive to what the other person actually offered, not to what the listener was already thinking.

## When the Cup Cannot Be Emptied

A specific failure mode Joe names: it is much harder to do this with someone you are in close relationship with than with a stranger.

"I can do this with random strangers. I can never do it with my wife. The moment I sit with my wife and she tells me how annoying I am, I'm like, 'no, I'm not.' Yeah, I am adding something to the equation." -- Alok Kanojia, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma (and Joe affirms the same in his own life)

The mechanism: the closer the relationship, the more the listener has invested in being a certain way with the speaker. The investment fills the cup. The emptying that is possible with a stranger (where there is nothing to defend) becomes impossible with a partner (where everything is in play).

This is also why people pour their hearts out to strangers in airports and not to their spouses. The stranger has the empty cup. The spouse has the full one. The stranger can attend; the spouse is busy with their own stake in the conversation.

The implication for the coach: the coach has an unfair advantage with the client that the client's loved ones do not have. The advantage is the emptiness that is structurally available to the coach and structurally unavailable to the people who actually live with the client. The coach should not mistake this advantage for special skill. It is a function of the relationship's structure.

## When Joe Reaches For This Parable

- When someone is asking how the coach is able to do what they do, and the answer requires explaining the emptied state.
- When someone is teaching others and finding that their students are not learning — the diagnostic is often that the students' cups are too full, not that the teaching is wrong.
- When someone is struggling to receive teachings they intellectually agree with — the cup is full of the agreement, which is itself blocking the actual receiving.
- When someone is trying to coach their partner and getting nowhere — the cup is too full of the relationship to attend cleanly.
- When the broader question of how to listen deeply arises (see [[listening]]) and a concrete image is needed.

## The Limit of the Parable

The parable can be misread to suggest that the listener should become a blank slate, with no perspective, no experience, no self. That is not what the parable points at. The empty cup is a cup, with shape and form. It is not the absence of a vessel. The emptying is of the *contents* of the cup at the moment of receiving, not of the cup itself.

The mature coach has years of experience, refined intuition, and a clear sense of what they know. The emptying is not the dissolution of any of that. It is the temporary clearing of the active processing during the moment of attending, so that the other person enters before the processing engages. The processing returns afterward, now informed by what was actually received.

This matters because the misreading produces a kind of coach who pretends to have no perspective and ends up being useless. The empty cup is the structure that lets perspective be useful when it is offered. Without the emptying, the perspective is offered prematurely and lands on what the coach assumed rather than on what is actually there.

## The Coachee's Side of the Parable

A second turn: the parable also applies to the person being coached. A coachee who arrives with a cup full of self-explanation, prior diagnoses, and committed framings cannot receive the coach's offering. The coach's most skillful intervention will be overflowed by the existing contents of the cup. The work, in such cases, often starts with creating conditions for the coachee's cup to empty — through questions that surface the contents, through reflective stillness, through the kind of attention that does not engage the existing contents as if they were the actual question.

See [[have-them-receive-your-listening]] for the related move: letting the coachee feel being listened to is one way of emptying their cup, because the listening received reveals to them how much had been filling the cup that they were not aware of.
