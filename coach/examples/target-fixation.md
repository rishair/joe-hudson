# Target Fixation: The Parachute and the Tree

## The Example

Brett offers this as a metaphor in the projection conversation, and Joe immediately embraces it ("That's a beautiful metaphor. I really like that metaphor").

If you are flying a parachute and you want to land in a field, but there is a tree in the field, where you look determines where you go. Look at the tree, you will hit the tree. Same with riding a motorcycle around a corner: if you look into the ditch, you go into the ditch. The body steers toward where attention is fixed.

## The Point It Illustrates

This is the embodied version of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] and the perceptual version of [[projections]]. The fear of the bad outcome focuses attention on the bad outcome. The focused attention organizes the body, the choices, the perceptual filter — and produces the bad outcome the person was trying to avoid.

"There've been lots of times in my life where I've seen some kind of disaster coming in business or like in a relationship and I'm like not wanting it, but I'm just like scared of it, which makes me think of it more, which makes me see the evidence of it more and not see the other paths available to me. And then the thing happens." -- Brett Kistler, Your World is a Projection

The mechanism is not mystical. It is not "the universe brings you what you focus on" in any magical sense. It is much more concrete: where attention goes, the body follows; what attention searches for, the perceptual system finds; what perception confirms, behavior reinforces.

## When Joe Reaches for This

This example is useful when someone is caught in a self-fulfilling fear pattern and the abstract framing of "what we fear we invite" has not landed. The parachute makes the principle physical. Anyone who has driven a car or ridden a bike has felt this. The metaphor moves the principle from theory into known experience.

Use it when:
- A person reports working hard to avoid an outcome and getting that outcome anyway
- A person is in vigilance about a relationship pattern and the vigilance is the dynamic
- The intellectual frame is not landing because the person experiences themselves as the careful one trying to avoid disaster
- A person needs to understand that scanning for the bad thing is itself the steering input

## The Friend at the Job

Joe pairs the metaphor with a concrete example. A friend believes he is bad and incapable. He has to do something at his job. He does not want to feel the anger of his boss. So he does not do the thing he needs to do to make sure the job is done right. Then by not doing the thing, he produces more evidence that he is bad and incapable.

"He's trying to avoid the feeling of being bad and incapable by being yelled at by his boss. As we see ourselves a certain way, subconsciously or consciously, we're recreating that over and over and over again." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

The same target-fixation dynamic at the level of identity. The fear of being seen as bad and incapable steers behavior in exactly the direction that produces being bad and incapable.

## What to Avoid When Using It

Do not let the metaphor become a license for blame. The point is not "you brought this on yourself" but "the attention itself is part of the system, so we can work with the attention." The person did not choose to fixate on the tree. Their nervous system did, for reasons that go back to childhood. The metaphor opens the seeing; the work goes back to [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

Also do not push the metaphor too far. Real life is messier than parachuting. Not every bad outcome was target-fixated into existence. The metaphor is most precise for cycles where the same outcome keeps appearing despite explicit attempts to avoid it.

## Why It Works as a Bridge

The metaphor takes a concept that can feel mystical or accusatory ("you create your reality") and makes it physical and obvious. People resist "you attracted this." They do not resist "look at the tree, hit the tree." Then once the physical version is accepted, the same mechanism can be traced upward into the emotional and relational layers where the same dynamic operates with different details.
