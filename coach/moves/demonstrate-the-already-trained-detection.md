# Demonstrate the Already-Trained Detection

## The Move

The person is in a vigilant scanning posture, working hard to detect a category of threat they have been hurt by before (narcissists, manipulators, people who will betray them, people who will leave). They believe the scanning is what is keeping them safe and what is building the detection capacity.

Ask them to perform the detection lightly, in a low-stakes way, right now. The performance is decisive: they can do it. They do it with a smile. The demonstration exposes the redundancy of the vigilance.

The phrasing as Joe uses it, in the live-Q&A version where the participant is on Zoom:

"Well, do me a favor and just like go to gallery view and just scan through the pictures. And I want you just for a minute — I don't want you to try. I just want you to see and tell me who has more narcissistic tendencies and who has less."

The lightness is structural. "I don't want you to try" is the key instruction. The trying-to-detect is the vigilance. The not-trying-and-still-seeing is the demonstration. The person performs the detection at a glance, with a smile, and the redundancy becomes visible to both of them at once.

## When To Use It

After a person has presented vigilance, scanning, or hypervigilance as the protective behavior they believe they need to maintain. Most commonly:

- Someone fresh out of a relationship with a narcissist who is afraid of dating again because they might miss the signs.
- Someone afraid of trusting people in general after a specific betrayal.
- Someone who has been over-evaluating new colleagues, friends, or potential partners.
- Someone whose protective scanning has become a daily cost ("my life is becoming hell because of this").
- Someone who self-identifies as hypervigilant and has framed it as a trauma response that requires the vigilance to remain safe.

Use the move when:
- The person has actual exposure to the thing they are scanning for (they have been with a narcissist, they have been betrayed, they have been the target).
- The room or medium permits a small experiment (a coaching session, a Zoom call with other people visible, a coffee shop with strangers around).
- There is enough rapport that the person will play with the experiment rather than treat it as a test.
- The vigilance is clearly redundant — the person obviously already has the capacity, they are just not crediting themselves.

Do NOT use the move when:
- The person is in active danger and the vigilance is genuinely load-bearing (they are still in contact with the narcissist, the betrayer is still in their environment).
- The person has not yet had the actual exposure that would have trained the detection (someone who is afraid of narcissists without having been with one is in a different situation — the work there is different).
- The person is dysregulated and a small experiment would be experienced as a test rather than a demonstration.

## What It Produces

The person almost always succeeds at the demonstration. They sort the gallery (or the room, or the imagined population) into more-narcissistic and less-narcissistic categories within seconds. Their face usually softens or smiles. There is often a small laugh of recognition.

The shift is structural: the claim "I can't trust myself to spot them" cannot be maintained while sitting in the felt experience of having just spotted them.

The other thing that happens: the lightness of the demonstration directly counters the heaviness of the scanning posture. The person has been treating the detection work as serious, exhausting, perhaps impossible. They have just done it lightly, in seconds, with a smile. The dissonance between the felt experience of the demonstration and the felt experience of the daily scanning is the leverage.

The natural next move is [[whats-the-need-for-the-defense]]. Once the capacity is shown to be online, the defense becomes structurally incoherent. The question opens the door to what the defense has been actually doing.

## Dialogue Example

The cleanest version is from the source session:

- **Person:** "I want to stay really open for myself, for other people, and I'm enjoying life tremendously. And at the same time, now I'm struggling with huge fear and constant watching out for all the possible signs of narcissists around me. And I notice that my life becomes sort of hell because of that."

- **Joe:** "Well, do me a favor and, um, just like go to gallery view and just scan through the pictures. And I want you just for a minute, I want you to just, um — I don't want you to try. I just want you to see and tell me who has more narcissistic tendencies and who has less."

- **Person:** "Who has more narcissistic what?"

- **Joe:** "Tendencies and who has less. Oh my God."

- **Person:** "I'm not going to damage people, right?"

- **Joe:** "No, you're not going to say it out loud."

- **Person:** (does it, with a smile, with a giggle)

- **Joe:** "Um, yeah, so you're doing this with a smile on your face and a giggle. Yeah, and you're probably not doing a bad job. If you've been with a narcissist, you can kind of probably spot them, like at least like, oh, good percentage, bad percentage, low percentage, high percentage, all with a smile on your face. So what's, what, what's the need for the defense?"

- *(Source: How Do I Not Get Ruined By Narcissism?)*

Notice the explicit reflection Joe makes after the demonstration: "you're doing this with a smile on your face and a giggle." Joe names what just happened. The naming makes the demonstration legible to the person. Without the naming, the person might dismiss what just happened as a small thing. With the naming, the contrast between the smile-and-giggle of the demonstration and the my-life-is-hell of the scanning is impossible to miss.

The naming move ("you're doing this with a smile") is itself part of the technique. The demonstration without the naming is incomplete.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **When the person succeeds easily and acknowledges it:** Joe moves directly to [[whats-the-need-for-the-defense]]. The structure is in place. The defense's incoherence can now be named.

- **When the person succeeds but resists ("but it might be too late," "I might be wrong"):** Joe meets the resistance as more material, not as failure of the move. He may extend with another piece of evidence ("you spotted them just now") or pivot to the underlying fear ("how much of this is fear of feeling the hurt that you already have suffered?"). See [[how-much-of-this-is-fear-of-feeling-the-hurt-you-already-have]].

- **When the person hesitates to do the experiment:** Joe lowers the stakes further. "You don't have to say it out loud." "Just notice — no one will know." The hesitation often dissolves once it is clear that no judgment is being made about the people being scanned. The reluctance is often about being seen as judgmental, not about the difficulty of the task.

- **When the person cannot do it (rare):** this is data. Either the person has not actually had the exposure that would train the detection, or the dissociation from the original wound is severe enough that the detection has been split off from awareness. Both cases require a different move. The most common version: the person can do it but does not trust their answer. That is a different problem — see [[how-do-i-trust-myself]] for the trust-collapse pattern.

- **When the demonstration triggers tears or grief:** stay. The detection demonstration has done its work by surfacing what the vigilance was protecting against. The next move is to let the grief move. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]].

## Tone

Light, playful, slightly silly. The energy is "let's do a small experiment that is going to be fun" rather than "let's prove your scanning is wrong."

The exclamation "Oh my God" Joe makes after asking the question is part of the lightness. It punctures any seriousness that the request might have carried. The participant's "I'm not going to damage people, right?" is a joke; Joe's "No, you're not going to say it out loud" is a joke back. The shared lightness is the container for the work.

The lightness is also structurally important. A heavy delivery of the same exercise would feel like a test, and the person would treat it as one — performing carefully, qualifying their answers, hedging. The light delivery makes it a game. Games produce honest answers because nothing is at stake.

## Why It Works

People in a vigilant scanning posture are running an exhausting protective routine. Their experience of the detection work is that it is hard, requires constant attention, and could fail at any moment. They have come to believe the routine is necessary.

The actual detection capacity is fully online. It was trained by the original exposure (the relationship, the betrayal, the year of dealing with the parent). The training is complete. The scanning is not training. The scanning is something else.

The demonstration makes this distinction felt rather than argued. The person sees themselves do the detection lightly, in seconds. They feel the gap between the demonstration and the daily scanning. The gap is the data. The gap forces a different question: if the detection is already online without the scanning, what is the scanning for?

This is the same structural insight as [[point-at-the-present-evidence]] — the person is already doing the thing they fear they cannot do — applied specifically to detection of a feared threat type. The capacity is in the room. The capacity has been here the whole time. The vigilance is downstream of something else, and naming what becomes the work.

## Variations for Different Defenses

- **For narcissism vigilance:** "Look around and tell me who has more narcissistic tendencies and who has less."
- **For untrustworthy-person vigilance:** "Of the people you can see, who would you trust more and who would you trust less?"
- **For about-to-leave-me scanning in relationships:** "Of the people you know, who is the type who would leave and who would stay?"
- **For about-to-be-angry detection:** "Right now, who in the room is more likely to be angry and who is calmer? See — you can read that fast."
- **For incompetence detection in colleagues:** "Of your team, who would you trust to do this and who wouldn't you? See — you already know."

The principle is the same: ask the person to perform the detection lightly, in the room, on a benign sample. The performance demonstrates the capacity is fully online. The demonstration makes the vigilance structurally incoherent.

## Why the Smile Is Part of the Evidence

A specific detail worth naming. Joe's reflection — "you're doing this with a smile on your face and a giggle" — is not incidental. The smile is part of the evidence the move produces.

If the detection were as serious as the person's scanning treats it, the demonstration would not be possible with a smile. The smile shows that the detection itself is light. The heaviness has been added by the protective frame, not generated by the detection.

When Joe names the smile, the person feels it. They were not aware they were smiling. The fact that they smiled while doing the thing they thought was so serious is itself information. The body knows the detection is easy. The mind has been treating it as hard.

This is also why the move requires the in-the-room observation. A purely verbal version ("you could already spot them, right?") loses the smile and loses the leverage. The body has to actually do the detection, the smile has to actually appear, and the appearance has to be named.
