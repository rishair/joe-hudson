# Love the Rejected Part of Me — Practice on the Coach First

## The Move

When a client cannot love a rejected part of themselves (the victim, the weak one, the helpless one, the failure, whatever the part is), have them practice loving it in you first. You — the coach — host the rejected part. They love it in you. Once they have found the felt-sense of loving the part in someone else, they turn the same loving toward themselves.

The sequence:

1. The client names what they cannot love in themselves.
2. The coach offers to host the part. "I love your victim. I'm okay with it being me."
3. The client is asked to love the part as it lives in the coach. "Love, love, love the helpless and non-capable victim in me."
4. The client finds the felt-sense of loving that part (because the part is being held by a different person, it is easier to love).
5. Then: "turn it toward yourself."
6. The same loving is redirected at the client's own version of the part.

"Yeah, see if you can love the powerless, weak part of me for a minute." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

"Love, love, love the helpless and non-capable victim in me." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

"And then just turn it towards yourself. There's a wall, same wall that stops the rage." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

## Why This Move Works

The client cannot love a part of themselves because the part has been forbidden for the entire history of the self. Asking them to love it directly runs into the entire architecture of rejection that has organized around the part. The body has no template for loving this part because the body has practiced rejecting it instead.

The move bypasses the architecture by relocating the part. The same part, held by a different person, does not trigger the same rejection. The client may even love it easily in someone else — they love their own younger sibling who is weak, their own friend who has been a victim, their own daughter who is helpless. The capacity to love the part exists; it has been blocked specifically at the self.

Once the client has located the felt-sense of loving the part — in the coach, in the room, in real time — the felt-sense is now available. The next move is to redirect: turn the same loving at the same part in yourself. The body knows what the loving feels like now. The redirection has a destination it can find.

This is the relational analog of [[universalize-the-trait]] and the cunning-reframe in [[loving-all-parts]]. The structural insight is the same: the part is loveable; the block is specific to loving it in oneself; finding the loveability elsewhere first establishes the channel.

What makes the move work in coaching specifically is that the coach is willing to literally host the part. Not "imagine someone you love has this part" — actually take the part on. The coach says "I'll be the victim. Love it in me." The body responds to the live offer in a way it would not respond to an imagined exercise.

## How to Run the Move

1. **Wait until the part has been named.** The client has to specify what they cannot love — the victim, the helpless one, the weak one, the failure, the angry child, whatever. The naming sets up the host-target.

2. **Offer to host it.** "I love your victim. I'm okay with it being me." The offer is not metaphorical. You are taking the part on in the room as your own. The body needs to feel that you are not pretending.

3. **Direct the loving at you.** "Love the helpless and non-capable victim in me." Repeat. The repetition gives the body time to find the channel.

4. **Stop them from talking.** "Stop talking, stop talking. Do it, do it, do it." The client will often try to verbally process the experiment. Block the verbal exit so the body can do the work.

5. **Track for landing.** When the client's body softens — when they actually do love the part in you — there is a felt change. The breath shifts. The eyes get tender. The body finds the loving.

6. **Redirect.** "Now turn it toward yourself." The instruction is short. The body has just located the loving; it can be turned. The client may try to immediately argue ("how do I"). Block the argument: "no how-to, just do it."

7. **Name the structural insight.** "There's a wall, same wall that stops the rage." The recognition that the love-block and the rage-block are the same defense gives the body the bigger frame. The work just done on the love is the same work that was done on the rage. Both are about the same wall coming down.

## What to Watch For

The move is landing when:

- The client looks at you while loving the part and the look changes quality. The eyes get soft. The face opens.
- The body posture changes — less constricted, more available.
- When you redirect ("turn it toward yourself"), the client does not have to ask how. The body knows.
- The client reports a felt-sense ("oh, I can do that for other people but not for me") — the recognition of the structural block is itself the work.

The move is not landing when:

- The client stays in the head. "I can intellectually understand loving that part of you, but..." The body has not yet found the felt-sense. Slow down. Have them just sit with you and look at you and see if any loving is there.
- The client tries to love the part in the abstract ("I love all victims everywhere"). The abstraction has bypassed the live offer. Bring it back to specifically loving this part in this person right now.
- The client gets very self-critical when asked to turn it toward themselves. The introject is still operating. May need to return to [[fuck-you-im-not-X-against-the-introjected-belief]] before the love-move can land.
- The client says "I can't" with a quality of certainty. The wall is intact. The work for this session may be in just noticing the wall — the breakthrough may come later.

## The "Same Wall" Recognition

A specific consequence of running this move after rage-work: the same wall stops both. The client has just done rage work and got something to move; now the love-work hits the same block. The coach names it.

"There's a wall, same wall that stops the rage." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

The recognition is the bridge into [[rage-and-love-are-the-same-pulsing-aliveness]]. The rage and the love are the same energy. The block to expressing the rage and the block to receiving/turning the love are the same block. Working one side opens the other. The body that just felt the rage flow can now feel the love flow because the same channel is open.

This is why the love-move often works fastest immediately after rage-work has moved. The system is open. The wall has just come down. The love can be directed through the same opening.

## Variations

**The cunning-reframe variant.** Joe also offers this move with adult-to-child reframing: "can you imagine a 5-year-old girl who is cunning?" The 5-year-old is loveable; the cunning is loveable in the 5-year-old; therefore the cunning is loveable. See [[loving-all-parts]]. The host-in-the-coach version is the same structural move with a different proxy — the coach instead of the 5-year-old.

**The mother/parent variant.** "Love it the way you love your daughter when she is weak." Uses an existing love-relationship as the proxy. Works when the client has children or close family members they obviously love.

**The "I love your X" variant.** "I love your anger. I love your fear. I love your shame." The coach speaks the love directly at the part. The client receives the loving from the coach. The receiving is the model for self-loving.

**The full sequence with the rejected-part dialogue.**
- Coach: "I love your victim."
- Client: "I don't want to love the victim."
- Coach: "Can you love the powerless weak part of me?"
- Client: "Yeah, other people I love."
- Coach: "Do it. Love the helpless and non-capable victim in me."
- (Client finds it.)
- Coach: "Now turn it toward yourself."

## Tone

Warm. The move is not delivered with the same intensity as the rage-work. After the rage moves, the room softens. The coach's tone follows the softening.

The "stop talking, stop talking, do it" is firm but not harsh. The firmness is keeping the body in the work; the warmth is the field the work is happening in.

When the loving lands, the coach can be quiet. The body is doing the work; the coach is just present.

## Distinction from [[love-yourself-the-way-you-always-wanted]]

This move and [[love-yourself-the-way-you-always-wanted]] are complementary.

- **Love yourself the way you always wanted** uses the 3-year-old felt-sense as the access point. The client locates what they wanted but did not get from their parents, then gives that to themselves.

- **Love the rejected part in the coach first** uses the live coaching relationship as the access point. The client loves the part in the coach, then turns the same loving on themselves.

The first works for general access to self-love when the question is "but how?" The second works specifically when there is a rejected part the client cannot love directly, and they need a proxy to find the felt-sense first.

Both are practices in the larger category of [[loving-all-parts]]. The proxy moves are needed because the entire architecture of rejection has organized against the direct move. The proxy locates the loving where the rejection is not blocking, then redirects.
