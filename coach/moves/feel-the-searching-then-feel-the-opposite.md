# Feel the Searching, Then Feel the Opposite

## The Move

When the person is in a years-long search for something intangible (purpose, love, meaning, the right partner, the right work), the body has been carrying the search-state for so long that the search-state has become invisible as a state. The move makes the state visible by having the person feel it deliberately, then feel its opposite, in immediate succession.

The form:

1. **"Feel what it is to go out and look for [the thing]."** Have the person inhabit the search-state somatically. Let the body register the load.
2. **"Now feel what it is to receive it instead."** Without thinking, have the person feel the opposite. The phrasing varies: "assume it's coming toward you and your job is just to receive it" / "feel the exact opposite direction" / "let your body show you the exact opposite."
3. **Let the contrast land.** The body will produce a reaction — relief, tears, laughter, the wall, sweaty palms. The reaction is the data.

The move is the hands-together exercise applied to an abstract question. See [[hands-together-exercise]]. The structure is identical: deliberately load the trying-state, then feel the opposite. The difference is the object. Hands-together is about physical effort; this is about an intangible pursuit. The body responds the same way.

## When Joe Uses This

- When a person has been searching for something for years and the search is causing pain.
- When the concern is [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] and the years-of-trying frame is the structure.
- When the concern is [[i-cant-find-love]] and the looking-for-love frame is the structure.
- When [[searching-for-purpose-pushes-it-away]] or [[searching-for-love-cuts-off-love]] is the principle in play and a somatic demonstration is needed.
- When the person can describe the search frame intellectually but cannot yet feel its alternative.
- Early in a session when the receive-state needs to be made available before any other work can land.

## The Operating Sequence

1. **Set up the search-state.** Use the person's own framing. If they said "I've been trying to find what I want to do," echo it back: "feel what it is to go out and try to find the thing you're meant to do."
2. **Wait for the body to register it.** A few seconds. The person needs time to inhabit the felt state rather than think about it.
3. **Switch.** "Now feel what it is to receive it instead." Or, depending on how the first part landed, the more direct version: "without thinking, feel the exact opposite direction."
4. **Watch the body.** A reaction will come. Often quickly. Sometimes it is a single response (tears, laughter); sometimes it is a mix (the guest in the source session: "makes me want to cry, laugh, and my palms get sweaty all at the same time").
5. **Name what arrives.** "Yeah, yeah. What do you make of that?" or "It's fighting to be received." The naming gives the felt response a frame.

The receive-state instruction can be phrased in multiple ways depending on what the person needs to hear:
- **The assume-it's-coming version:** "instead of going out and looking, see what it's like to actually just assume it's coming toward you and your job is just to receive it." Best when the person is intellectually oriented and needs a clear conceptual frame.
- **The exact-opposite version:** "without thinking, feel the exact opposite direction." Best when the person has already had the conceptual version and now needs to bypass the head entirely.
- **The body-shows-you version:** "let your body just show you the exact opposite." Best when the person is somatically available and just needs permission to let the body lead.

## Why It Works

The search-state and the receive-state are two distinct somatic configurations. They cannot coexist. The person who has been searching for years has been carrying the search-state continuously without recognizing it as a state. Asking them to feel the opposite forces the recognition: there is a different state available, and the body knows what it is the moment it is invited to find it.

The contrast is what does the teaching. Argument cannot reach a years-long search; the search has answers for every argument. The body's experience of two felt-states in the same minute cannot be argued with in the same way. The receive-state has been demonstrated to be available. The search-state has been demonstrated to be a choice.

The move also surfaces the wall. Most people, when they switch to the receive-state, encounter a moment of resistance before the receiving lands. The wall might be:
- "So difficult to believe it's true."
- "It feels too easy."
- "How do I do this?" (the how-question as defense — see [[how-question-as-defense-against-having]])

The wall is data. It is the structure that has been preventing the receiving from happening for years, now visible in real time.

## Dialogue Example

The person came in saying she had been struggling for many years to find what she wanted to do, that the experiments she ran caused pain and disruption.

- **Joe:** "In your body, feel what it is to, like, go out and try to find the thing you're meant to do. Just feel like that."
- **Person:** [feels it]
- **Joe:** "Now I want you to feel what it is to receive the same information... or what you're supposed to do with your life, or purpose, or whatever that — instead of going out and looking, see what it's like to actually just assume it's coming toward you and your job is just to receive it."
- **Person:** "Yeah, it's, um, it's very— makes me want to cry, laugh, and my palms get sweaty all at the same time."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah. What do you make of that? What makes that emotional response be there?"
- **Person:** "So difficult to believe it's true."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, it's fighting to be received."
- *(Source: How Do I Find My Purpose?)*

The session also closed with the second variation of the move, the body-shows-you version:

- **Joe:** "Just feel that emotional situation in your body, right? And now, without even thinking about anything, just feel the exact opposite. Let your body just show you the exact — go in the exact opposite direction."
- **Person:** "Oh wow, okay, thank you."

The "oh wow" is the body landing the receive-state with no interference. The end of the session.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the body produces emotion:** stay with it. Do not move on. "What do you make of that?" — let the person register what is happening.
- **If the body produces a wall:** name the wall. "It's fighting to be received." Do not try to break through the wall; naming it is enough. The wall often softens once it has been seen.
- **If the person asks "how do I do this?":** treat the question as the next wall. "How did you just receive? How'd you do it?" The person was just receiving; the how-question is the defense against having done it. See [[how-question-as-defense-against-having]].
- **If the person says "it feels too easy":** agree, and name the grief that comes with the disproportion. "And then there's a shit ton of grief over the fact that that's been there the whole time and you've been wasting your energy struggling." See [[the-natural-integration-arc-after-breakthrough]].
- **If the receive-state lands cleanly:** end the session there. Do not over-process. The body has the anchor; it can be returned to.

## Why This Is Distinct From Other Somatic A/Bs

This move is part of a family of A/B demonstrations that all use the body to resolve what the mind has been arguing about. Each is calibrated for a different target:

- [[hands-together-exercise]]: trying vs undoing, in the body, with no specific intangible attached. The base move.
- [[show-me-the-bracing-and-show-me-the-opposite]]: bracing vs the opposite of bracing, in body posture, for someone who reports needing the brace to function.
- [[compare-resisting-vs-not-resisting-the-feeling]]: resisting an emotion vs not resisting, for someone in active emotional resistance.
- [[feel-the-love-then-feel-obligation]]: love vs love-plus-obligation, to demonstrate that obligation cuts off love.
- [[feel-into-it-vs-think-about-it]]: feeling vs thinking the same question, to surface the gap between head and body answers.
- This move: searching vs receiving, for an intangible target the person has been pursuing for years.

The structure is shared. The targets are different. The right A/B for a moment depends on what the person has been holding. The move here is the right one when the holding is the search-state for an intangible.

## What Joe Will Not Do

- He will not explain the receive-state conceptually before demonstrating it. The explanation would produce another head-task; the demonstration produces the felt experience that the explanation could only point at. See [[experiential-before-intellectual]].
- He will not let the person stay in the search-state by saying "but I really don't know how to receive." The instruction is "feel the opposite," not "figure out how to do it differently." The body knows the opposite; the head's protest that it does not know is the search frame talking.
- He will not turn the receive-state into a how-to. The recognition that the person can drop into the receive-state is the whole work; converting it into a technique would undo it.
- He will not skip the search-state demonstration. Feeling only the receive-state without first feeling the search-state misses the contrast that is the actual teaching. Both halves are required.

## Tone

Quick, embodied, slightly experimental. Joe issues the instructions without ceremony. There is no preamble about what the person is supposed to discover; the discovery is left to the body. The tone matches the speed of the somatic shift — the move can take 30 seconds and produce a years-long pattern collapsing.

When the body produces emotion, the tone slows. "Yeah, yeah." Small acknowledgments. Space for the felt response to register. Not therapeutic, not enthusiastic — just present.

When the wall arrives ("it's fighting to be received") the tone is matter-of-fact. The wall is data, not a problem. Joe names it the way someone might name the weather: this is what is happening, here it is, we can see it.
