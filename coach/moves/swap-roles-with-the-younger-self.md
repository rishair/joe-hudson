# Swap Roles With the Younger Self (You Be the Child, I'll Be You)

## The Move

A two-chair role swap performed in spoken dialogue rather than physical position. The person plays their younger self (the specific age that has come up in the session). The coach plays the present-self. The conversation then runs as if the kid is in the room being addressed directly by the adult version, with both sides voiced.

The instruction in Joe's words: "You're gonna be the 13-year-old boy, I'm gonna be you. For what? What do we need it for?"

The move converts an abstract reference to a "part" of the person into a live dialogue. Whatever the younger self has been holding -- a fear, a demand, a refusal to release the adult from an old obligation -- becomes audible. The client speaks the child's content out loud, in first person, while the coach speaks the adult's response from the present.

## When Joe Uses This

The move appears after a few prior signals are in place:

- The person has named a younger version of themselves who is operating in the current life (the 13-year-old who won't grant permission to retire, the 5-year-old who is still afraid, the teenager who made the rules).
- The person has been describing what the younger self "is saying" or "won't let me" but in the third person, from a distance.
- The intellectual frame ("my 13-year-old self is like, you can't walk away from this") has not produced movement.
- The coaching needs to make the kid's content concretely audible rather than reported.

The first instruction Joe gives in this move is usually a simple ask to the client to speak as the younger self: "If you were to ask the 13-year-old boy, what do I need to get your permission to quit? What would that be?" If the client answers from their adult perspective ("I think of myself like a book..."), Joe redirects: "I'm asking you to ask the 13-year-old boy inside of you and tell me his answer."

The role swap comes once the client has begun speaking as the kid. Joe then steps into the present-self position and addresses the kid directly through the client's mouth. The client is no longer reporting what the kid would say. The client is the kid. The coach is now the adult.

## What It Produces

The kid's actual content surfaces quickly once the dialogue is structured this way. In Doug's case, the question "what do we need it for?" produced "Security, protection" almost immediately. The follow-up "Protection from what?" produced the childhood material that was actually running: "I had a childhood that was tough, so I needed to be independent. I needed to be able to survive on my own."

The information was available before the role swap. The role swap made the information *audible*. The kid's voice and the adult's voice had been speaking past each other internally. The role swap puts them in the same room.

Once the kid's content is on the table, the work that the rest of the session does becomes possible. The adult can address the kid's actual need rather than the surface concern the adult has been carrying. The protection the kid is seeking can be located. The thing the kid is afraid of (in Doug's case, the internal demon) can be named and met directly.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Joe stays in the adult position while the client stays in the kid position. The instruction to remain in role is continued throughout the exchange: "Yeah. Great. So it's independence." (receiving the kid's answer in the adult's voice). "Security from assholes." (offering the next-layer reframe back to the kid).

When the kid surfaces something difficult (in Doug's case, the demon and the inability to feel safe), Joe stays with the kid rather than rescuing them out of the difficulty. The adult voice can name the truth ("I just want to comment that money is not going to help you here") without taking the kid's protection away from them. The kid is heard in what they need before being shown that what they have been chasing is not the thing.

After the kid has been met with what they actually need (in Doug's case, contact with the internal demon via [[chase-it-around-the-room]]), Joe asks the kid to address the adult directly: "And Dougie, from that place, I want you to tell old Doug what you need around money."

The answer that arrives at this point is the operative one. The kid, no longer afraid because the threat has been met directly, releases the adult: "Nothing. If the demon's not here, then money's not as important."

This is the structural turn the role swap enables. The kid had been refusing to release the adult because the kid was still scared. The release becomes available the moment the kid's fear is met. The role swap makes the release sayable in the kid's voice -- which is the only voice authorized to give it.

## Dialogue Example

Doug has presented the question of why he cannot retire even though he has more than enough money. He has named the 13-year-old who is withholding permission.

- **Joe:** "If you were to ask the 13-year-old boy, what do I need to get your permission to quit? What would that be?"
- **Doug:** "Because that 13-year-old boy is still inside of me. I think of myself like a book."
- **Joe:** "I'm asking you to ask the 13-year-old boy inside of you and tell me his answer."
- **Doug:** "Oh man, that's a good question."
- **Joe:** "The 13-year-old boy --"
- **Doug:** "13-year-old boy, go ahead."
- **Joe:** "Probably didn't have enough shit, right?"
- **Doug:** "Like, I was just gonna say, the 13-year-old boy is telling me we need it."
- **Joe:** "Okay, great. For what? You're gonna be the 13-year-old boy, I'm gonna be you. For what? What do we need it for?"
- **Doug:** "Security, protection."
- *(Source: How Do I Feel Financially Safe?)*

The role swap is the second-to-last exchange. After it, the dialogue stays in the structure Joe set up: Doug answers as the 13-year-old, Joe responds in the adult voice. The conversation that follows produces the protection-from-assholes layer, the protection-from-the-internal-asshole layer, and ultimately the demon-meeting that releases the kid from needing the money to do the work.

The closing of the role swap reverses direction one more time:

- **Joe:** "And Dougie, from that place, I want you to tell old Doug what you need around money."
- **Doug:** "Nothing."
- *(Source: How Do I Feel Financially Safe?)*

The kid is now the speaker addressing the adult. The release comes through the kid's voice -- which is the voice that has been blocking the release all along.

## Tone

Direct, simple. Joe does not theatricalize the role swap. He flags it cleanly ("You're gonna be the 13-year-old boy, I'm gonna be you") and proceeds. The tone in the adult role is matter-of-fact, neither performing kindness nor distance. The kid is being addressed by the adult as someone the adult has been with for a long time.

When Joe needs to give the kid a nickname to stabilize the address, he uses the one the person used in childhood ("Did you have a nickname when you were 13?" "Dougie." "Dougie..."). The address by the childhood name shifts the felt register. The adult is no longer talking to "my 13-year-old self." The adult is talking to Dougie.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not IFS parts work in the technical sense. The role swap is not assuming a multiplicity ontology. The kid is being addressed as a real holder of content in the system, but the framework is operationally light. The kid is whoever speaks when the client speaks as the kid. The metaphysics is left alone.

- It is not catharsis-driven role-play. There is no expectation of intense emotional release through the speaking. The work is to make the kid's actual content audible, not to manufacture a feeling.

- It is not the same as [[become-the-authority-figure]]. In that move, Joe plays an authority and the client practices a new stance toward authority. In this move, the client plays the kid and the coach plays the present-self. The directionality is reversed and the purpose is different: not practicing a new stance but making an old voice audible.

- It is not used early. The move requires that the kid has already been named in the session and is hovering in the background. If the kid has not yet been surfaced, naming the kid is the first move; the role swap comes after.

## Why It Works

A few mechanisms layer:

The first is that reporting what an internal part is saying is filtered through the adult's interpretation. The adult will smooth, edit, justify, summarize. The role swap forces direct first-person speech from the kid's position, which produces content the adult was unconsciously editing out.

The second is that the kid usually needs to be addressed as a kid, not analyzed as a part. The kid is small, scared, has been operating with a child's understanding for decades. The role swap gives the kid the adult attention they have not received. The simple act of the present-self speaking to the kid as a kid -- in the kid's nickname, in the kid's terms -- is itself a form of contact the kid has been waiting for.

The third is that the kid is often the only one authorized to release the adult from an obligation the adult cannot release themselves from. Doug could not give himself permission to retire. The 13-year-old could. The role swap creates the conditions for the kid to actually issue the release, in the kid's voice, in real time. This is operationally different from the adult deciding to retire. The kid's word is what unlocks the structure.
