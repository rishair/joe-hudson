# Use the Live Group as the Love Field

## The Move

When a client reports isolation, an inability to feel connection, or a wall (often shame) standing in the way of receiving love, and there is a live group present (a Q&A, a workshop, a group session), use the group itself as the field for the work. Do not coach about how to find love or how to feel connection. Have the client find it in the room, right now, with the people who are actually present.

The sequence Joe runs:

1. **Anchor to the room.** "Stay with me and stay with keeping your heart open to all these folks. Let's not rationalize anything for a minute. Let's just feel."
2. **Have them look for love in the faces.** "Do you see anybody loving you right now, even though you're a stranger on a [call]? How much can you notice that?"
3. **Pick one face. Don't walk away.** "Take a look at one of those people. Don't walk away. Like, sit in that, savor it for a minute."
4. **Have them thank that person.** "And you don't even have to say their name. Just see what it's like to just say thank you to them."
5. **Recalibrate the receiving.** "Now again, thank them, but this time thank them like as if you deserve the love."
6. **Pick another person, deepen.** "So pick another person real quick that you can see loving you right now. Can you find one? Yeah. And what can you say to them that brings you even closer to connection?"
7. **Note what arises.** When something like "I love you" arises and the client says they cannot say it to strangers in normal life, normalize the want and demonstrate that here, it is permitted.
8. **Spread it out.** "Love all these people, love them up. Even the person who's not paying attention to you... And love them just the way they are. And breathe. Don't forget to breathe."

The whole sequence takes perhaps two minutes. The product is not a discussion of love; it is the felt experience of love in the room, with real people who are actually there.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the client is in a live group and the presenting concern is loneliness, isolation, inability to feel love, or shame blocking connection
- When [[shame-blocks-connection-in-real-time]] has been named and the work is to demonstrate that the connection is available right now
- When the client reports they cannot find connection in their life (a new city, a thin friend group, a recent loss) and the available room is the disconfirming evidence
- When the client describes wanting to say loving things to people but believing it is not okay — and the group is willing to receive
- When the client has been intellectualizing about connection and needs a somatic experience of it
- When the work needs a felt anchor before harder material (like anger underneath the love) can be approached safely

The move is not for individual sessions where there is no group. The solo version is different — see [[chase-it-around-the-room]] and [[scan-body-for-what-cant-be-loved]] for solo love-work.

## What It Produces

The client moves from intellectual report of trust ("I've been listening to your podcast for a couple years") into felt connection with specific faces. The body changes — relief, a smile, sometimes tears. The reported isolation begins to thin, not because the situation has changed, but because the somatic experience of "I cannot feel love" gets contradicted by a present-moment instance of feeling it.

The move also functions as a baseline anchor for the rest of the session. Once the client has felt love in the room, that felt state becomes a place to return to. Later in the session, when something harder arises (anger at an ex, shame about a behavior), Joe can return the client to looking at the faces. The felt anchor is reachable because it was established with the real people in the real room.

A specific secondary effect: the client often discovers that they have been wanting to say loving things to others ("I love you is the only thing that really comes up") and have been suppressing it. The room becomes a place where the suppressed loving language can be voiced and received without the social cost the client feared. This itself loosens the architecture of the shame that has been suppressing the loving language in the rest of their life.

## Why It Works

Three mechanisms are operating:

**Shame depends on hiding.** The client carries a felt sense of not being okay to be seen. When they look at faces and find love rather than rejection, the felt sense gets disconfirmed by present evidence. The shame cannot maintain its claim that connection is impossible when connection is actively happening.

**Love is felt, not concluded.** The client may believe they love people but cannot feel it. Having them look at one specific face — not "love" in the abstract but this face, this person, this expression — produces the felt experience that the abstract concept does not. The body needs a specific other to register love. The room provides specific others.

**Group amplification.** With a single face, the love is one data point. With many faces, the data is multiplied. The client cannot dismiss any one face ("well, they are a coach, of course they care") when there are dozens of faces. The plurality breaks the dismissal.

The move also leans on the [[container-of-love]] dynamic. The group, by being present and attentive, becomes a container. The container holds what the client could not have held alone. The witness function is the felt opposite of the shame's claim that being seen would result in rejection.

## The Recalibration Step Is Critical

Stage 5 — "thank them like as if you deserve the love" — is the move's deepest piece. The client's first "thank you" is usually delivered from the position of someone who is grateful but not entitled to what they are thanking for. The body posture is slightly stooped, the voice slightly small. The instruction to thank them again, "like as if you deserve the love," asks the client to receive at a different level.

The recalibration usually produces visible change. The body straightens slightly. The voice fills. The thank-you becomes something offered from equal ground rather than from below. This is the moment the love actually lands — the previous thank-you was the body still managing the receiving, still keeping the channel narrow. The recalibration opens the channel.

This step also demonstrates the [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] move applied to in-room receiving. The first thank-you was deflected even as it was said; the recalibrated thank-you is permeable.

## The Spread-Out Step

Stage 8 — "love all these people, love them up. Even the person who's not paying attention to you" — extends the receiving into broadcast. Once the client has received from one face and another, the work expands to loving everyone in view, regardless of whether each individual is registering or responding.

This step matters because it removes the contingency. If the client can only love the people who are visibly loving them back, the love is still transactional. Loving the person who is doing other work on the side of the screen — and loving them "just the way they are" — practices the unconditional form. The love does not require any specific response to keep flowing.

The instruction to breathe ("And breathe. Don't forget to breathe") is the somatic anchor that keeps the work in the body. People who are practicing intense relational states for the first time often hold their breath, which closes the channel they have just opened. The breath instruction keeps the body open.

## The Move Sets Up the Next Move

In the divorce session, the love practice with the group is what makes the subsequent anger work possible. Once the client has the felt anchor of love available in the room, Joe extends the practice to the person the client has been most cut off from: "now I want you to do that with your ex-wife." The same move — find the love — applied to the avoided figure surfaces what was underneath all along: love and anger together. "I know there's love, but there's a lot of anger."

This sequence — establish love in the available field, then extend to the difficult figure — is a general pattern. The group work creates the felt template. Once the template is present, it can be applied to the figures the client has been unable to apply it to in their inner work. The room becomes the practice space, and what is practiced becomes the access point for harder relationships.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** stay, just stay with me and stay with keeping your heart open to all these folks. Let's not rationalize anything for a minute. Let's just feel like — do you see any — do you see anybody loving you right now, even though you're a stranger on a [call]? Can you see how, how much can you notice that?
- **Client:** Yeah, I see it.
- **Joe:** Yeah. And if — take a look at one of those people. Don't walk away. Like, sit in that, savor it for a minute. And you don't even have to say their name. Just see what it's like to just say thank you to them.
- **Client:** Thank you.
- **Joe:** And again, thank them, but this time thank them like as if you deserve the love—
- **Client:** yeah, thank you, thank you for being here.
- **Joe:** Yeah, what happened to that fear and that shame?
- **Client:** It is a little bit, yeah.
- **Joe:** So pick another person real quick that you can see loving you right now. Can you find one?
- **Client:** Yeah.
- **Joe:** Yeah. And what can you say to them that brings you even closer to connection? Your connection, not theirs. They don't even know who you are. They don't even know that you're speaking to them. It doesn't really matter.
- **Client:** I love you is the only thing that really comes up.
- **Joe:** Yeah.
- **Client:** And it's something that I often find myself wanting to say to people, yeah, but I know it's not okay to just go out and say that to strangers.
- **Joe:** I know. I think I created this entire organization so that I could say that to strangers.
- *(Source: I Can't Get Over My Ex)*

The exchange shows the full arc — anchor to the room, see love, savor one face, thank, recalibrate the thank, pick another, find what wants to come ("I love you"), normalize the want. The whole sequence takes under a minute and produces the felt anchor that the rest of the session builds on.

## Tone

Active and present-tense. Joe stays in the right-now of the room — "right now," "real quick," "this minute." The instructions are short and somatic ("Don't walk away. Like, sit in that, savor it for a minute"). The tone has none of the reverence that would make the practice feel ceremonial; it has the quality of someone showing a person how to do something they already know how to do but have not realized they could.

There is also a casualness to the language about the group ("all these folks," "even the person who's not paying attention to you") that prevents the practice from becoming precious. The room is not a sacred space; it is a real space full of regular people, and the love is happening with regular people. The client's body learns: I can do this with regular people in regular spaces. The transferability to life outside the session depends on this framing.

## What Not To Do

- **Do not let the client stay in intellectual report.** "I trust you because I've listened for two years" is not the felt experience the move is trying to produce. Re-anchor to the somatic: "But how is it right now?"
- **Do not skip the recalibration step.** The first thank-you is rarely the work. The "as if you deserve the love" is the work.
- **Do not push the client past their capacity.** If they cannot find one face that is loving them, that itself is data. Stay with the inability — what is the wall, where is the wall — rather than performing the love practice through the wall.
- **Do not let the practice become a routine.** The same sequence done as a routine does not produce the felt anchor. The move depends on Joe's actual present attention to what the client is experiencing in each moment, adjusting based on what the body is showing.
- **Do not deploy without [[shame-blocks-connection-in-real-time]] or a similar read having been confirmed.** The move presupposes that connection is the work and love in the room is the field. If the work is somewhere else, the move is off-topic.
- **Do not extend the practice to the avoided figure too quickly.** The group work first. Only once the felt anchor is in place can the difficult figure (the ex, the parent, the lost person) be brought in. Premature extension overshoots the body's capacity.

## The Compressed Variant (Anti-Shame in a Self-Blame Session)

In the breakup-self-blame session, Joe uses a faster, more directive version of the move when the client is composing himself against tears. The full sequence (anchor → look → savor → thank → recalibrate → another → spread out → breathe) is compressed into a single push:

"Fuck that. Look at everybody. Look at me and, and love me. Love yourself. This is the antidote to your shame addiction. Yeah, see that I'm loving you. Look at the other faces, see that they're loving you right now, or in awe of you, or check them out. The shame is like, fucking, it's your matrix. It's not even true. Like, there's like — nobody has — nobody thinks you have anything to be ashamed of." -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?

The compressed variant is used when the client has already started producing the somatic opposite of shame ([[feel-the-shame-then-feel-the-opposite]] has been run, the chest has raised, the love has started arriving) and the work is to extend and protect the production against the shame about to reassert. The directness ("fuck that," "look at everybody") cuts through the composing in real time.

The compressed variant has a specific addition that the longer form does not always include: the explicit naming of the shame as a matrix. The matrix-naming is the cognitive shorthand that the body needs to dismiss the shame's claim. The shame says "you should be ashamed in front of all these people." Joe's matrix-naming says "the shame is making the rules; the people in the room are not actually thinking what the shame is telling you they're thinking." The reality-check is what allows the body to keep producing the love-state instead of returning to the composing-state.

The trade-off: the compressed variant is more directive and depends on Joe's authority being already established. The longer form is more spacious and self-discovered. The compressed form is for acute moments in active sessions; the longer form is for opening the field at the start.
