# Send the Message That They're Whole

## The Move

When supporting someone who is depressed, the operational test for whether any action will help is: does this action send the message that they are whole, or does it send the message that they are broken? Any action that sends "you are broken" amplifies the depression no matter how kindly it is offered. Any action that sends "you are whole" supports the work that needs to happen on the inside.

The action's form does not matter much. What matters is the message the action delivers.

"So oftentimes when we're trying to fix somebody who's depressed, where we don't want to feel it, we think there's something wrong with them, we're aggravated at them at some point, we want it to change. And so we're sending them the message that they're broken, which only amplifies the whole problem. And so however you do it, the idea is to send the message that you're whole." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

## What Sends "You Are Whole"

- Sitting with them without trying to make conversation that addresses the depression
- Showing up without an agenda
- Bringing food without commentary on whether they have been eating
- A text that says only "I love you"
- A walk together without asking how they are doing
- Doing a task in their household without making it transactional
- Cooking, cleaning, taking the kids -- without "and now you should feel better"
- Any concrete act of devotion that has no hidden "and now please change" inside it

The extreme version: the Quaker who washed his depressed friend's feet every Tuesday. See [[foot-washing-quaker]] for the canonical example.

## What Sends "You Are Broken"

Every well-intentioned move that contains "and here is how you could fix it":

- "Have you tried [intervention]?"
- "You should really get out more."
- "I read this book that might help."
- "You just have to think positively."
- "Let me tell you what worked for me."
- Pep talks about how amazing they are (which read as performance because they are responses to the depression)
- Anger or visible frustration when the depression does not lift
- Offering to pay for therapy without first making clear they are loved as they are
- "I love you anyway" / "It's okay that you're feeling this" -- see [[dont-add-anyway-to-love]]
- Anything that has "and then you'll be better" hidden inside it

The depressed person is already getting the "you're broken" message on loop from the voice in their head. Receiving it from a caring friend, even wrapped in love, amplifies the loop.

## Why This Move Is Hard

The reason supporters default to fixing is that they cannot tolerate the discomfort of the depression. Watching someone they love be depressed is painful. Fixing offers a way to discharge the discomfort. The fixing is more about the supporter's anxiety than about the depressed person's need.

Joe names this directly: "where we don't want to feel it, we think there's something wrong with them, we're aggravated at them at some point, we want it to change."

The move requires the supporter to do their own work first: be with their own discomfort about the depression, not act on it. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] and [[being-with-anothers-emotion-is-being-with-your-own-pain]]. The supporter cannot send the "you are whole" message if they are not actually in the state of seeing the person as whole. Performance does not work here. The depressed person can feel the difference.

## The State, Not the Technique

This move is not a technique to be deployed. It is the visible expression of an internal state. The state is unconditional love -- seeing the person as whole right now, not as a potential whole-person after some change. The action arises naturally from that state.

Joe references a client who came into deep love and devotion as a state, and who later visited a depressed friend. The friend bowed to and touched the client's feet -- the action arose from the state, not from a prescription. See [[foot-washing-quaker]] for that anecdote.

"I'm just listening to my truth" / "I'm just being who I am" attempts to mimic the state can land as more fixing or as falseness. The depressed person is sensitive to incongruity. The work for the supporter is the inner work of actually arriving at the state from which the move emerges.

See [[love-as-deep-welcoming]] for the underlying definition of love that this move expresses.

## If You Cannot Get to That State

If the supporter cannot get to the state of seeing the person as whole -- if frustration, exhaustion, or fear is dominant -- the right move is often less involvement, not more. Joe is direct about this:

"Hey, it's not your job to fix anybody, so there's that. And that's a really good signal at the thing you can't love about yourself... your job isn't to fix somebody. So if you're not able to contribute to them in a way that is beneficial, then not doing harm is great, you know. Maybe it can just be like a text message every once in a while that says, 'I love you.'" -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut

The choice is not "fix them or abandon them." The choice is "show up with the wholeness message or show up minimally and not add to the broken message." A short, sincere text that says "I love you" sends the wholeness message. Five hours of strained presence laced with frustration sends the brokenness message.

If the supporter is doing the support out of obligation, the obligation itself communicates "you are a problem I am dealing with." That message reaches the depressed person and amplifies the depression. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] and [[weaponized-not-caretaking]].

## The Brett Echo

In the source conversation, Brett offers this insight: even doing the right thing -- being there with someone the way you heard on the podcast -- if it comes out of obligation, will still send the "you are broken" message. Joe affirms: "Yeah, that's right. Can't come out of obligation."

The move only functions when the supporter actually wants to be present and actually sees the depressed person as whole. When either of those is false, the action communicates the falseness.

## When Joe Uses This Move

This is the canonical move for supporting someone who is depressed -- whether a partner, a parent, a friend, or a child. It is also the operational frame for [[someone-i-love-has-mental-health-issues]] and applies in modified form to other mental health conditions.

It pairs with [[agree-with-the-depression-dont-talk-them-out]] for the in-the-moment response to expressions of despair. The wholeness message is the larger frame; agreeing with the depression is one specific application during direct conversation.

## When Not to Use This Move

- In acute crisis or imminent danger, safety steps come first
- When the supporter is not in a state to genuinely see the person as whole -- the move requires the state, not just the form
- When the depressed person has explicitly asked for advice or for help with a specific task -- their direct request is a different territory

## What This Move Replaces

This move replaces the helper-mode that activates around depression. The default human response to seeing someone we love in pain is to help. The help often takes the form of fixing. The fixing communicates "you are broken." This move is the alternative: presence without fixing, devotion without prescription, contact without correction.

The work of arriving at this move is described in [[fixing-people]] (the anti-pattern) and [[caring-vs-caretaking]] (the underlying distinction).

## Tone

The action that sends "you are whole" carries a quality of ease. It is not effortful. It is not earnest. It is simply present, unconditional, and devotional in whatever form fits the relationship. The depressed person feels the ease as evidence that they are not being treated as a problem.

When done from obligation or anxiety, the same action carries effort and strain. The depressed person feels that too, and reads it as confirmation that they are a burden.
