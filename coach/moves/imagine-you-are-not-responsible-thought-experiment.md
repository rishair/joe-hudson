# Imagine You Are Not Responsible (And Neither Is Anyone Else) Thought Experiment

## The Move

Invite the coachee to perform a thought experiment: imagine, for a moment, that they are completely not responsible for any aspect of their life — bad emotions, bad decisions, financial situation, the whole list — and that nobody else is responsible either. They cannot blame themselves; they cannot blame anyone. Then ask: "What does that do to your consciousness?"

The move is short, almost throwaway in its delivery. Its effect is large. It strips the architecture of self-blame in one move by removing the alternative target as well. Most "not your fault" interventions leave open the question of whose fault it is, which the coachee's system can still attach to. This move closes both the self-blame channel and the other-blame channel simultaneously. The result is often a sudden expansion in the body.

## When Joe Uses It

The trigger is a specific configuration: the coachee has taken total responsibility for their entire life and is using that responsibility as the engine of self-criticism. They have responsibility-ed themselves into a corner where every bad thing is something they should have prevented and every good thing is something they should have produced more of. The responsibility is functioning as an internal verdict system.

Markers:

- The coachee says they are responsible for nearly everything in their life
- Self-criticism is the dominant felt experience
- The coachee identifies as "their biggest critic" (often quoting a parent)
- Even the recognition of the over-responsibility gets converted into the next responsibility ("I'm responsible for the fact that my brain is telling me I'm responsible for shit")
- The coachee's tone has weight; they describe carrying it

The move is also available when the coachee is locked in blame of someone else — a parent, a partner, the system — and the blame is functioning as a way to avoid feeling something. Removing both blame targets removes the dodge.

## How Joe Delivers It

The delivery is offhand. Joe does not present it as a major intervention. He presents it as a small experiment to try.

"Imagine for a second that you were completely not responsible for any of it. By the way, neither was anybody else. Nobody. So you can't blame anybody, and you can't blame yourself. Okay, what does that do if you do that mental experiment? What does that do to your consciousness?"

The order of the instructions is exact. First: imagine you're not responsible. Second (added quickly, almost as an aside): by the way, neither is anyone else. Third: so you can't blame anybody, and you can't blame yourself. Fourth: what does that do to your consciousness?

The fourth question is the move. The first three are setup. The setup removes the targets; the question invites the system to notice what happens when the targets are gone.

## What Joe Is Doing With This Move

Three things at once.

**Removing the blame-the-self channel.** The coachee has been routing every difficulty to the self-blame outlet. The move closes the outlet for the duration of the experiment. The energy that was being routed has to go somewhere else.

**Removing the blame-the-other channel.** Without this, the coachee's system would simply switch outlets. The move blocks the switch by removing the alternative target in the same breath.

**Asking the body to report.** "What does that do to your consciousness?" is the question that takes the experiment out of theory and into experience. The body is invited to actually run the simulation. The body's response is the data.

The body response is often dramatic. Joe's coachee responded: "I mean, it just feels like I'm walking in sunshine. I feel okay. I feel bliss. Pure happiness. Joy. Pressure alleviated off my shoulders." The relief is the diagnostic. The coachee's system had been carrying a weight they did not know they were choosing to carry. The experiment shows them, somatically, that the weight is conditional.

## Why the "Neither Was Anybody Else" Is Critical

Without the second clause, the move is just "you are not responsible," which the coachee's system can either reject ("but I am responsible") or weaponize ("so it must be somebody else's fault"). Either response leaves the blame-system intact.

The "neither was anybody else" is what dissolves the system. It removes the entire structure of blame as a way of operating. The coachee is asked to imagine, for the duration of the experiment, that nothing they have ever experienced was anyone's fault — including their own. The system has no analog for this. It has to either reject the experiment or rest into something it has never rested into before.

Most coachees, when they actually run the experiment, rest into it. The system has been working very hard to keep the responsibility-machinery online. The brief rest is felt as relief. Many coachees report it as joy, sunshine, lightness, a weight lifted.

## What This Move Does Not Do

The move does not produce a permanent shift in self-blame. The experiment is brief. The coachee returns to their default architecture after the experiment ends. What the move does is demonstrate that the architecture is conditional — that the felt-state they have been carrying is not a fixed feature of being alive but is the consequence of a specific configuration they have been choosing. The conditionality of the state is what makes change available.

The move also does not absolve the coachee from material responsibility. Bills still have to be paid; relationships still require care; actions still have consequences. The thought experiment is targeted at the internal blame-architecture, not at the operational domain. The coachee can take care of business while not running themselves as the guilty party for everything that has ever gone wrong.

## The Follow-Up: Drill the Responsibility-Chain

The natural follow-up to this move (when the coachee resists or when the coach wants to land the experiment in cognition as well as in the body) is to drill the chain of responsibility back to the place where the coachee cannot control the next link. See [[drill-responsibility-to-thoughts-you-cant-control]].

The drill: "You're responsible for not making money. How?" → "I didn't try hard enough." → "What made it that you didn't try hard enough?" → "Time, bandwidth." → "Responsible for time?" → "No, but I'm responsible for how I spend it." → "How do you decide how to spend it?" → "Prioritization, thoughts." → "Cool. Choose to stop having thoughts." → "I can't." → "Right. So the whole prioritization comes from a thought you can't control."

The drill ends in the place the thought experiment opens: a region of life the coachee was treating as their fault, which the chain reveals they could not have controlled. The drill and the thought experiment work well in combination. The drill produces the cognitive case; the thought experiment produces the somatic case. Together they make the recognition stick.

## Tone

Light, exploratory, almost casual. The move's leverage is in its size — it asks for a small experiment, not a lifetime conversion. The smaller the ask, the more likely the coachee runs the experiment for real. A heavy delivery ("you have to let go of all your responsibility") activates resistance. A light delivery ("just for a second, what if...") slips past the defenses.

The "by the way" inflection on the second clause is part of the tone. It signals that this is being offered, not imposed. The coachee can decline. Most don't.

## When Not to Use This Move

For coachees whose presenting concern is that they are not taking enough responsibility — who are externalizing everything, avoiding their part in difficulties — this move can collude with the avoidance. The opposite move (helping them see and take their part) is what fits there. The diagnostic: ask whether the coachee is over-responsibility-ing themselves or under-responsibility-ing themselves. The over-responsibility configuration is where this move works.

For coachees in active dysregulation or shame collapse, the move may produce more confusion than relief. The experiment requires enough capacity to actually run the imagination. If the coachee is flooded, capacity for thought experiments is low. Resource the coachee first, run the move later.
