# Surface the Assumed Dichotomy (What Makes That a Dichotomy Instead of a Both-And?)

## The Move

When the person frames their question as a choice between two options, ask what makes them assume it has to be a choice.

The form Joe uses:
- "What makes that a dichotomy instead of a both-and?"
- "What makes you assume a dichotomy?"
- "Is that really an either-or?"
- "What makes those two things not be the same thing?"

The move does not answer the question. It questions the structure of the question. The person has packaged their inquiry as a fork in the road; the move asks why they think there is a fork.

## When Joe Uses It

When the person's question has an embedded assumption of opposition that is doing the work the surface question pretends to do. The diagnostic: the question feels stuck not because the answer is hard but because both answers are unsatisfying. This is usually the signal that the framing is wrong.

Triggering signals:
- "Is it X or is it Y?" framing for two things that might both be true.
- "I don't know if I should A or B" where A and B are not actually mutually exclusive.
- "Either I keep doing this or I'm going to lose that" framing for situations where both could coexist.
- The person reaches an impasse trying to choose and Joe notices the impasse is in the framing.
- The person uses "but" between two clauses that could be joined with "and." See [[and-vs-but]].

The move is especially useful when the person is in a problem-solving mode that depends on the dichotomy being real. The dichotomy is what makes the problem feel intractable. Surfacing it dissolves the intractability.

It is not used to invalidate the person's question. It is used to make visible the assumption that is making the question stuck. After the surfacing, the original question often re-poses itself in a different shape that has an answer.

## What It Produces

The person usually pauses. The intellect that has been building arguments for one side or the other has nothing to build against. The mind has to actually examine what made the dichotomy feel real.

Common responses:
- "Huh. I don't know what makes it a dichotomy."
- "I guess it doesn't have to be."
- A specific answer that itself becomes the material: "Because if I don't push, I'll stagnate." That assumption can then be examined directly. See [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] and [[nature-improves-itself]].
- An attempt to defend the dichotomy: "But they really are different." This is also useful — it surfaces what the person is defending. Often the defense reveals a fear or an identity that depends on the binary being real.

The most productive responses surface a downstream assumption that has been operating invisibly. K's response in the session showed this:

- **K:** "I think that there is some element of not moving is — I guess the first thing was, not moving is bad. And so the dichotomy is that being and just being with that internal compass is insufficient if it doesn't actually move you to some other place."

The surfacing revealed the operating assumption: "not moving is bad." That assumption is itself addressable. The dichotomy ("is it being or is it going-somewhere?") dissolved when the assumption ("not moving is bad") was named, because the assumption is what created the opposition. With the assumption surfaced, the next move could engage it directly. See [[nature-improves-itself]] and [[tell-the-unchanging-part-it-has-to-move]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person can't articulate the assumption:** Joe waits. Sometimes the person needs time to find what was operating. He may rephrase: "What would have to be true for it to need to be a dichotomy?" The rephrasing keeps the question alive without giving the answer.

**If the person names a specific assumption:** Joe usually moves to the assumption rather than back to the original question. The assumption is what was producing the impasse. Once it is named, it can be examined. The original question often resolves itself once the assumption is examined.

**If the person says "you're right, it doesn't have to be a dichotomy":** Joe checks whether this is intellectual agreement or felt recognition. Intellectual agreement leaves the dichotomy operating in the body. Joe may say: "Yeah, what's the both-and look like?" The image of the both-and grounds the recognition somatically.

**If the person defends the dichotomy:** Joe lets the defense be heard. He doesn't argue. The defense usually reveals the fear that is making the binary feel necessary. The fear becomes the material.

**If the dichotomy turns out to be real:** Sometimes the person examines it and concludes it is actually a real either-or. This is also a useful outcome. The person now knows they are making a real choice rather than a constructed one. The clarity itself is the gain. Joe does not insist that all dichotomies are false; he just makes sure the person has examined whether this one is.

## Dialogue Example

From the K session, the move at the moment K had constructed a being-vs-doing fork:

- **K:** "I can't tell if it's — this is just the place, meaning there is nowhere to go, or just trusting it or listening to it, it will get me to where it will get me to the place, if that makes sense. So like, is it just this way of existing, or is it this way of existing, being that eventually reveals what the ultimate destination?"
- **Joe:** "What makes that a dichotomy instead of a both-and? What you're saying there, there's an assumption of a dichotomy. What makes you assume a dichotomy?"
- **K:** "I think that there is some element of not moving is — I guess the first thing was like, not moving is bad. And so the dichotomy is that being and just being with that internal compass is insufficient if it doesn't actually move you to some other place."
- *(Source: What's Your Scorecard? — the dichotomy surfacing produces the embedded assumption "not moving is bad," which becomes the next material.)*

The work the move did: K's question ("is it just being or is it being-that-leads-somewhere?") was stuck because both answers were unsatisfying. The first felt like stagnation; the second smuggled in the very scorecard logic the session was dismantling. The dichotomy was the trap. Surfacing it produced the assumption underneath ("not moving is bad") which Joe could then engage. The original question no longer needed to be answered, because the framing that produced it had dissolved.

## Tone

Curious, slightly interested. Not gotcha. Not impatient with the person for having a dichotomy. The energy is "huh, what is making that feel like a fork to you?" rather than "you're framing this wrong."

The question is asked once and then waited on. Joe does not pile on. He does not stack a second question on top before the first has been processed. The question is short. The silence after it is long.

When the person surfaces the assumption, Joe receives it with the same flatness with which he asked the original question. He does not say "ha, see, that was the issue." The neutrality lets the person own the recognition.
