# Can you hang out with it?

## The Move

When a person has just named a fear (or any difficult feeling) and the impulse is to talk about it, analyze it, or move past it, the move is to ask whether they can hang out with it. The phrasing is light. "Can you hang out with the fear?" "Can you stay with that?" "Just be with it." "Let your breath move through it."

This is the move that follows the fear arriving. [[lets-go-there]] is the invitation to enter the avoided territory. This move is what happens once inside: the staying. The work of hanging out is the work that allows the feeling to do its sequencing -- to become whatever it wants to become next, on its own timing, without being managed.

Variations from the transcript:
- "Can you hang out with the fear?"
- "Just hang with it."
- "Letting yourself be in that down deeper. And just hang out. Nowhere to go, just hanging with it."
- "Can you let your whole body just have that aliveness and electricity?"
- "Can you stay hanging out with that?"
- "Staying with that grief... see how if at all it wants to move. So you're letting it move through the system if it wants to."
- "If your body wants to have that shake, give it -- let it sequence."

The phrase choices share a quality: low pressure, no agenda about what should happen next, permission for the feeling to stay or shift on its own.

## When This Move Comes

- Immediately after a person names a fear, sadness, or other feeling.
- When the person reports a sensation in the body and the next move would be to interpret it rather than feel it.
- When a feeling has just dropped down a level ("I feel like I just dropped down deeper") and the impulse is to talk about the drop.
- When the body is doing something (shaking, tearing up, softening) and the move is to let the body finish what it is doing rather than interrupt with words.
- When a person has been intellectualizing and finally arrives at the felt sense -- the move is to not let them leave.

It often follows the [[lets-go-there]] invitation. Once inside the avoided territory, hanging out is what happens next. It can also follow [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]] or [[what-does-your-heart-say]].

## What It Produces

The feeling moves. Not always in the direction expected.

Fear becomes sadness. Sadness becomes grief. Grief becomes aliveness. The aliveness becomes a felt impulse to say something to a person in the room. The impulse becomes a vow.

Or: fear becomes deeper fear. The body shakes. The shaking is the fear leaving. Underneath the fear is grief about needing to be safe and stable for someone else.

The pattern: when the feeling is hung out with, it almost always becomes something else within minutes. When it is managed or analyzed, it stays as it was. The move's reliability is what makes it the workhorse of emotional inquiry coaching.

The transcript shows the sequence cleanly:
1. Fear named (eggs in one basket).
2. "Can you hang out with the fear?"
3. Sadness arrives. ("I noticed there's a lot of sadness immediately.")
4. "Yeah. And if you hang out with that and just be with it..."
5. Grief arrives, with memory of a previous partner who died.
6. The grief becomes wanting to pour herself out toward her current partner.
7. The wanting becomes a sentence said directly to him: "I want you to want to be here with me as much as I want to be here with you."
8. The fear is no longer there. "It's not currently feeling like fear. It feels like aliveness and kind of electricity."
9. "Can you let your whole body just have that aliveness and electricity?"

Each step is a hang-out. The coach does not direct the sequence. The sequence happens because the feeling is allowed to do what it does.

## What the Coach Does Next

Once the person is hanging out with the feeling, the coach mostly stays quiet. Brief check-ins. "What's that?" "What's happening?" The check-ins are not analysis. They are invitations to report what is presently happening so the felt sense stays articulated.

If the person moves into story or interpretation, the coach gently redirects: "Just be with the feeling." "What's the body doing?" "Let your breath move through it."

If the person reports a new feeling (sadness arriving on top of fear, for example), the coach repeats the move: "Can you hang out with that?" "Stay with the sadness." The same move applies at each layer.

If the body wants to do something (shake, cry, exhale loudly), the coach gives explicit permission: "If your body wants to have that shake, let it sequence." This is important because adult bodies often hold the impulse back without permission to release.

If the feeling produces an impulse to turn toward a person in the room and say something, the coach surfaces that: "What does that feeling want to say to him?" See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]] for the couples-coaching variant.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "It feels like all my eggs are in this basket."
- **Coach:** "Can you hang out with the fear? Because it is true, right? Just breathe and hang out with it."
- **Person:** "I noticed there's a lot of sadness immediately."
- **Coach:** "Yeah. And if you hang out with that and just be with it and let your breath move through it..."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I feel like I just dropped down deeper."
- **Coach:** "Yeah. Great. So letting yourself be in that down deeper. And just hang out. Nowhere to go, just hanging with it. What's that?"
- *(Source: Marriage, Love, and Death. The coach does not narrate, interpret, or push. The feeling moves on its own each time it is hung out with.)*

## Tone

Spacious, slow, low. Almost casual. The phrase "hang out" is intentionally informal. It removes the felt pressure of "you must feel this completely." Hanging out is what you do with a friend on a porch. Nothing has to happen. The feeling is not a problem to solve. It is company to keep.

The coach's body matches: relaxed, unrushed, low voice. Eye contact when possible, soft when not. The instruction "let your breath move through it" is paired with the coach's own audible slow breath, modeling the rhythm.

The opposite tone -- intense, focused, "now go INTO it" -- collapses the move. Hanging out cannot be done with effort. The light touch is what lets the feeling stay long enough to move.

## Why "Hang Out" Specifically

The phrase carries no instruction about outcome. "Feel it fully" implies a target. "Process it" implies a procedure. "Hang out with it" implies presence without project.

This matters because the feeling will not move under pressure to move. The body's emotional sequencing is autonomic; it happens when the field is safe enough to let it happen. "Hang out" creates the safety. Anything that signals "the feeling needs to be done with" prevents the safety, which prevents the moving.

This is also why the move is repeatable. After the feeling moves to a new layer, the coach asks again: can you hang out with this? The repetition is not stalling. It is the recognition that each new layer is its own hangout. The work is not a single immersion. It is a sequence of stayings.
