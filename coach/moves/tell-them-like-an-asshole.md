# Tell Them Like an Asshole, Then Like Not an Asshole

## The Move

When working with a leader and their team in the room, ask the leader to say exactly what they want in three ways: the unhedged "asshole" version, the kind non-asshole version, and the hedging-without-saying-it version. Let everyone observe the contrast.

"I'll actually do work with CEOs and with their team, and I'll say, okay, just tell them exactly what you want in the word, like in a complete asshole way, and then do it in a not asshole way, and then do it like when you're hedging, you don't want to tell them, but you're telling them, and you just see the CEO recognize at that moment how much of a relief it is to their people when he's just telling them what to do, or she's just telling them what to do, or when she's being clear that this is one of those places where we collaborate and I don't want to tell you what to do." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## What It Produces

The exercise produces immediate, visible reactions in the team:

- The "asshole" version surprises them with relief. The leader is finally being clear. Even if the content is uncomfortable, the clarity is a gift.
- The "non-asshole" version, said cleanly, lands as effective leadership.
- The "hedging" version produces the visible discomfort the team usually lives in. Watching it from outside makes the cost visible.

The leader, observing the contrast, recognizes how much energy the team has been spending decoding the hedged version. The recognition is the moment the move is working for.

## Why It Works

Leaders who hide their wants believe the hiding is protecting their team from the leader's "negative" wants (the want to control, the want to override, the want to be in charge). The hiding is actually the burden. The team is doing the work of figuring out what the leader actually wants, on top of their actual work.

The three-versions exercise breaks the illusion. The team's relief at the unhedged version proves that the directness is welcome, not threatening. The team's discomfort during the hedged version proves that the hedging is the cost, not the kindness.

See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] for the mechanism the move is interrupting. The leader's hidden want is the source of the "guck" in the system. Making the want sayable removes the source.

## The Three Deliveries

**Asshole delivery:** Exactly what you want, with no softening, no relational tact, no qualifiers. "I want this done my way and I do not want input on it." "I want you to stop talking and listen." "I do not actually want collaboration on this; I just want compliance."

**Non-asshole delivery:** The same content, with relational tact and respect for the people receiving it, but the want is still fully named. "I have a clear vision on this one and I would like to make the call myself. I welcome your input on other things but I want this one to be mine." "I want to share a decision rather than discuss it." "I'm asking for collaboration here, and on this one I want to make the final call."

**Hedging delivery:** The leader's actual current pattern. The performance of asking for input while signaling the answer. The questions whose answers are predetermined. The "what do you think?" that has only one acceptable response.

The contrast among the three is the point. Leaders rarely see their hedging clearly until they do it adjacent to the directness they have been avoiding.

## Why the Asshole Version First

If the order is asshole → non-asshole → hedging, the team gets to feel the relief of clarity first. The clarity becomes the reference point. The hedging then registers as a deviation from possible.

If the hedging comes first, the team's tolerance kicks in and the leader's discomfort with the asshole version dominates. The order matters.

## Variation: Reveal the Two Modes

For leaders who actually want a mix of collaborative and authoritative styles depending on the decision, the exercise extends to:

"This is one of the times I want collaboration. I want your input. I will make the final call after hearing you."

"This is one of the times I have a decision. I am sharing it, not asking. I welcome your thoughts but they will not change my mind."

Both are owned. Both are useful. The team can navigate both. What is unmanageable is the leader who claims the first while behaving as the second.

## What the Move Reveals to the Leader

The internal experience the leader usually reports:

- "I did not realize how much I was hiding."
- "Saying it directly was easier than I expected."
- "I can see the team relaxing."
- "I was protecting them from something that was not actually a threat."

This recognition is often more impactful than any conceptual teaching about leadership. The leader feels the cost of the hiding and the lightness of the owning in the same five minutes.

## When to Use This Move

- A leader is described by their team as inscrutable, political, or hard to read
- A leader is asking for more collaboration but the team is becoming more passive
- A meeting includes a leader and their team and the leader is hedging visibly
- A leader is wrestling with whether to be "more directive" without being controlling
- After [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] has been named and the leader is ready to experiment

## What Not to Do With the Move

- Do not use it to embarrass the leader. The point is to make the contrast visible, not to humiliate.
- Do not skip the non-asshole version. The asshole version is for contrast, not as the recommended delivery.
- Do not use it before establishing enough trust that the leader can risk the asshole delivery.
- Do not let the team applaud the asshole delivery as license to be cruel. The point is owned wants, not unloaded brutality.

## The Tone

Joe brings this move with a playful, experimental tone. "Just try saying it the asshole way" is light. The lightness gives the leader permission to actually try it. A heavy or moralistic delivery would produce the hedging the move is trying to interrupt.
