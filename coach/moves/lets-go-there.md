# Let's Go There

## The Move

When a person names something they've been avoiding -- a fear, an abyss, a feeling they've been keeping at bay -- Joe responds with "let's go there." Sometimes "let's go." The phrase is short and concrete.

The function of the phrase is to convert the avoided thing from something talked-about into something stepped-into. The person was naming it from outside. The invitation is to enter it from inside.

The phrase often repeats. "Let's go there." (Person continues describing.) "Let's go." (Person continues.) "Yeah, let's go." The repetition is not impatience. It is companionship -- the steady offered hand.

## When Joe Uses It

This move comes at specific moments:
- After the person names the deepest fear ("some sort of spiral into madness")
- After the person describes the territory they've been avoiding ("an abyss in me")
- After the person reaches the edge of a feeling and pauses
- When the person says "I don't want to feel this" or "I'm scared to go there"
- When the next move would be to feel into the avoided thing rather than to talk more about it

It usually comes after [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]] has produced the answer. The question opens the door. The move walks the person through.

The move is also used as a recovery when the person retreats. If the person starts to describe the avoided thing and then pulls back into story or analysis, Joe brings them back with "let's go." This is companionship plus mild insistence.

## What It Produces

A pause. The person was talking about the thing; now they have to feel into it. The pause is the transition. Sometimes the pause is long. Joe doesn't fill it.

What comes next is often surprise. The thing they were avoiding turns out to be different than expected. The spiral isn't there. The madness isn't there. What's there is sensation, breath, body. The thing they were running from for years takes a few seconds to step into and is not what they thought.

Sometimes what comes is the actual feeling that was being avoided -- sadness arrives, anger arrives, a wail comes. The move opens the channel. The feeling moves.

Sometimes what comes is peace. The avoided abyss turns out to be peace. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]]. This is the most common surprise.

## What Joe Does Next

Once the person is in the territory, Joe stays quiet. He doesn't narrate, interpret, or guide. The work is the person being in the thing. Talking would pull them back out.

If the person comes back out into thinking, Joe names it: "You got quiet. You're making it the enemy again." This is friendly redirection.

Once the person reports what is actually there ("just sensation," "peace," "nothing"), Joe names the structural fact if useful. "What if this is what you're avoiding? What's the scary thing?" This helps the person see what just happened so the experience can become available again later.

## Why "Let's" Rather Than "Go"

The move is "let's go," not "go." The plural matters. Joe is not instructing the person to do something. He is going with them. The first-person plural makes the move companionable rather than directive.

The companionability matters because the avoided territory is, by definition, what the person has been alone with. The fear of going there is the fear of being alone with it again. "Let's" signals that the going-in is shared. The person is not being sent in alone. The presence of another person -- who is steady, who is not scared, who is going in too -- is what makes the going possible.

This is also why Joe uses the move with his own body. His posture stays relaxed. His voice is low. He is modeling that the territory is not scary. The going-with is somatic, not just verbal.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "What's the ultimate thing that you're running from?"
- **Person:** "Like some sort of spiral effect. I've seen people I love..."
- **Joe:** "Let's go there."
- **Person:** "Like spiral into depression or..."
- **Joe:** "Let's go."
- **Person:** "Spiral into madness."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, let's go. There's an abyss in you that you're avoiding, and your fear is that if you go into that..."
- **Person:** "I won't come out."
- **Joe:** "You won't come out. So let's go. Can you find it?"
- **Person:** "Oh yeah, yeah, it's there."
- **Joe:** "So see what it's like to just step in the middle of it."
- *(Source: How Do I Trust Myself? -- The repeated "let's go" walks the person from naming the fear to entering it.)*

## Tone

Quiet, slow, low. The voice has no urgency. The pacing widens. The "let's go" is delivered the way you would invite someone into a deep pool that you have been in many times.

There is no enthusiasm in it. Enthusiasm would feel performative. There is no gravity in it. Gravity would make the territory feel more dangerous. There is just steady invitation.
