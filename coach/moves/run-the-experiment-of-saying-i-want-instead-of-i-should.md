# Run the Experiment of Saying 'I Want' Instead of 'I Should'

## The Move

The person uses the word "should" in a sentence. Catch it immediately. Have them say the same sentence with "I want" substituted for "I should." Then ask which feels better.

The move is small, fast, and produces an immediate felt difference. It is not a teaching about should-vs-want; it is a live experiment that has the body do the work the teaching would only describe.

The form Joe uses:

- **Client:** "So, um, today I should be doing my business."
- **Joe:** "Well, there's a good thing that will stop you right there."
- **Client:** "Saying I should."
- **Joe:** "Uh-huh."
- **Client:** "Okay."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Well, so let's just, let's just run the experiment. You should do what?"
- **Client:** "I should go stock my shelf."
- **Joe:** "Okay, cool. Now say I want to go stock my shelf."
- **Client:** "I want to go stock my shelf."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Which feels better?"
- **Client:** "Like I want to."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

The sequence has six steps:
1. The client uses a should.
2. Joe catches it and signals that the should itself is the obstacle ("there's a good thing that will stop you right there").
3. Joe frames what is about to happen as an experiment, lowering the stakes.
4. Joe asks for the specific should to be named in full ("you should do what?").
5. The client says the specific should.
6. Joe asks the client to say the same sentence with "I want" instead.
7. Joe asks which feels better.

Step 7 is the move's payload. The body has just felt both sentences. The body knows which one was lighter. The client's report ("like I want to") is the felt diagnostic the rest of the work can rest on.

## When Joe Uses This

- The moment any should arrives in the client's speech, regardless of what else is happening in the session
- After [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] has been introduced conceptually and a live demonstration is needed to ground it
- When the client believes intellectually that wants are better than shoulds but the body has not yet experienced the difference
- When the client has been should-ing themselves about a specific concrete action and needs the should mechanism made visible in real time
- When the client is in [[i-keep-getting-in-my-own-way]] mode and the structural work has produced a concrete avoided action, which then immediately gets framed as a should
- When the should-to-want translation is being taught and needs to land somatically, not just conceptually

The move is fastest at the moment the should arrives. Letting it pass and circling back means having to reconstruct the felt context. Catching it in real time makes the substitution maximally clean.

## Why It Works

The should produces body contraction. See [[should-as-threat]]. The want produces body opening. See [[wants-over-shoulds]]. The two states are different at the somatic level. The body knows which one feels which way without any explanation.

When the same sentence is said with the two different framings within seconds of each other, the body cannot help noticing the difference. The brain might want to insist they are the same — they refer to the same action, after all — but the body reports back what it actually felt. The body's report is the data the rest of the work uses.

The "which feels better?" question is the move's pivot. The question forces the client to consult the body rather than the head. A heady answer ("well, theoretically the want should feel better") would not work. The body has just had two distinct experiences and is being asked to compare them. The comparison produces an honest answer because the body's recent direct experience is more available than any conceptual position.

The "let's just run the experiment" framing is important. The word "experiment" reduces the stakes. The client is not being asked to commit to anything, not being asked to believe anything, not being asked to do anything different in their life. They are being asked to try a sentence. Trying a sentence is small enough that resistance does not fire. Once the sentence is tried, the felt difference is in the body, and the rest of the work has its evidence.

## What It Produces

Almost always: an immediate report that the want-version feels better. The report can be a single word ("yeah") or a short phrase ("like I want to"). The body has done the work; the words just confirm what already happened.

Occasionally: the client says they feel no difference. This is data. Either the should is so deeply installed that the want-form is being said in a should-voice (the body cannot hear the difference because both are being delivered with the same energy), or the underlying want is genuinely not there (the action is one the client does not actually want to take and the should was a more accurate report). Either way, the next move is to investigate what the body actually feels about the action — not to insist on the want.

Sometimes: the client laughs or smiles when saying the want-version. The laugh or smile is the body's recognition that something just shifted. This is the strongest version of the experiment landing.

Rarely: the client refuses the experiment. The refusal is itself the data. The should has a function the client is not ready to give up. The work then moves to what the should is providing — usually some form of safety or identity-preservation — rather than insisting on the substitution.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client reports the want-version feels better: Joe affirms briefly and uses the experience as the entry to the next layer. In the source session, Joe immediately moved to the teaching that the should is shame and shame stops behavior. The felt experience was the predicate for the teaching to land — the client had just felt the should slow them down and the want unlock them.

If the client reports no difference: Joe usually checks whether the want-version was being said in a should-voice. "Try saying it again, but this time really feel that you want it." If the difference still does not appear, Joe explores whether the underlying action is wanted at all.

If the client laughs or smiles: Joe stays with the body for a beat. The laugh is information about what is releasing. Letting it land before moving on respects the body's response.

If the client refuses: Joe pivots to investigating the function of the should. "What would happen if you said it as a want?" The investigation surfaces what the should has been protecting against, which becomes the new material to work with.

## What Comes Next

Once the felt difference is established, the work has a foundation. The standard next move is to teach the mechanism explicitly:

"The should is usually a good way to stop things. Should is a shame. Shame is a way that you stop behavior, right? I shame you to get you to stop. Should is shame. Shame stops behavior typically." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The teaching lands because the body has just confirmed it. The client felt the should stop them and the want unlock them. The teaching is now the explanation for what they just experienced rather than an abstract claim about how shoulds work.

The teaching can be followed by an anticipatory naming: "Now you'll come to me in two weeks and you'll be like, how do I get rid of my shame so I can start my business? I see you coming." This naming preempts the next loop — the loop where the want-vs-should insight becomes the new self-improvement project. The preempting lands as humor when delivered well, which keeps the insight from being instrumentalized.

## Tone

Light, quick, slightly playful. The "experiment" framing keeps the move from carrying too much weight. The "which feels better?" question is asked the way you might ask someone to compare two flavors of ice cream — with genuine curiosity, no investment in the outcome, expecting the body to know.

The catch ("there's a good thing that will stop you right there") can be delivered with a small smile. The smile signals that the should has been spotted but is not being condemned. The condemnation would invoke a new should ("I should stop should-ing"), which would defeat the move.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]]. That move locates the want that the should is sitting on, often through translation ("I should call my mom" → "I want to feel connected to her"). This move is more elementary — it just changes the verb in the same sentence. The two moves serve different points; this one is the entry, that one is the deepening.

- It is not [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]]. That move surfaces wants that have been blocked by shame, often by having the client speak progressively closer versions of the underlying desire. This move handles the simpler case where the want is right there and just needs to be voiced in want-form.

- It is not [[should-vs-want-body-check]]. That move asks the client to feel the difference between should and want without any verbal substitution. This move uses the verbal substitution as the somatic prompt.

- It is not a teaching about shoulds. The teaching can follow, but the move itself is just an experiment. Treating it as a teaching defeats the purpose — the client should be doing the experiment, not learning from it.

- It is not for every should. Some shoulds are useful. Functional shoulds ("I should pay my taxes by the deadline") are calculations, not threats, and the substitution does not produce a different felt state because there is no real want underneath. The move is for shoulds attached to actions the client actually wants to take but is being slowed down by the framing.

## Dialogue Example

The fuller arc, including the teaching that follows:

- **Joe:** "So, so, if, if, if you just started doing your business today, right now, what would stop you?"
- **Client:** "So, um, today I should be doing my business."
- **Joe:** "Well, there's a good thing that will stop you right there."
- **Client:** "Saying I should."
- **Joe:** "Uh-huh."
- **Client:** "Okay."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Well, so let's just, let's just run the experiment. You should do what?"
- **Client:** "I should go stock my shelf."
- **Joe:** "Okay, cool. Now say I want to go stock my shelf."
- **Client:** "I want to go stock my shelf."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Which feels better?"
- **Client:** "Like I want to."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So yeah, that's what I'm saying. The should is usually a good way to stop things. Should is a shame. Shame is a way that you stop behavior, right? I shame you to get you to stop. Should is shame. Shame stops behavior typically. Okay, so we remove shame from the equation, but now you have another thing to do that'll distract you from actually doing the business."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

The complete sequence shows the move's full arc: the catch, the experiment, the felt-difference confirmation, the teaching landing on the felt foundation, and the immediate preempting of the next loop (the "now you'll come to me in two weeks and ask how to get rid of shame" anticipation, which arrives in the next exchange).
