# Give Feedback in Service to Something Greater Than the Person

## The Move

When you have to tell someone they have a shortcoming, tell them in service to a larger purpose — the role they are growing into, the vision they hold, the impact they want to have, the relationship they care about. The shortcoming is named directly. The naming is anchored to something bigger than the person themselves.

The structural form: "You have the potential to be a really great [doctor, leader, partner, friend]. But as long as [specific shortcoming] is going on, you're never going to get there."

The two clauses do specific work. The first names what the person is moving toward — the larger thing the feedback is in service to. The second names the specific thing that is currently blocking the path. The shortcoming is not the topic; the path is the topic. The shortcoming is the obstacle on the path.

"So what works really well is when you tell someone they have a shortcoming, but tell them what it is in service to. So, 'hey, this is what you're good at,' and be authentic. It's not a compliment sandwich. And, like, 'you have the potential to be a really great doctor, but as long as this is going on, you're never going to get there.'" -- Alok Kanojia, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma (Joe affirms throughout the same exchange)

## Why This Differs From the Compliment Sandwich

A compliment sandwich is bread (compliment), shit (criticism), bread (compliment). The form is intended to soften the blow. The recipient sees through it instantly. The bread is recognized as decoration. The shit is the message. The compliments are read as evidence that the speaker thinks the recipient is too weak to receive the criticism directly.

"You're calling them weak. You're saying, hey, oh yeah, you can't handle it." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The sandwich communicates two things at once: the criticism, and the judgment that the recipient is fragile. The second message often does more damage than the first. The recipient feels both criticized and condescended to. They lose trust in the speaker — both because the compliments are recognized as instrumental (and therefore false) and because the implied judgment about their fragility is itself a small attack.

The in-service-to-something-greater frame avoids both failures. There is no false compliment. The recipient is treated as strong enough to receive the criticism directly. The criticism is anchored to a larger purpose the recipient already cares about, which is the actual reason the criticism matters. The structure is honest at every level.

## Why the Larger Purpose Disarms the Ego

The reason this move works structurally has to do with what the ego is doing in the moment of receiving feedback. The ego arises to protect against negative emotions. When feedback arrives as "you are bad" or "this is wrong with you," the negative emotion is shame, and the ego activates to defend against the shame. The defense is what blocks the feedback from being heard.

When feedback arrives anchored to a larger purpose — when the criticism is heard as "this is what is in the way of you becoming the thing you are reaching for" — the ego has less to defend. The criticism is not about whether the person is good or bad. It is about whether a specific behavior is functional for a goal the person has chosen. The ego does not need to defend against information about functionality. It needs to defend against verdicts on worth.

"You can give a judgment, and oftentimes judgment comes with ego. But if you can tease those things apart, and the way you disable the ego is, what is this in service to? You will be a doctor one day. Yeah, that will be great. But if you give your medication, if you give patients medications that they're allergic to, you will never be a great doctor." -- Alok Kanojia, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The "what is this in service to" is the operational lever. It is the question that, when answered explicitly in the feedback delivery, separates the judgment about the behavior from the judgment about the person. The behavior is judged; the person is being supported toward their own goal. The ego has nothing to defend.

## Joe's Companion Observation

In the same exchange, Joe adds the heart-side observation that completes the structural picture:

"When ego is involved, when I'm going to call you bad, it's because we don't want to feel something. I do that to another person. When I'm in that level of judgment with people, what I notice is it's because I don't want to feel something." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The companion principle: the move to call someone bad — to deliver feedback in the verdict form rather than the in-service-to form — is the giver's defense against a feeling the giver does not want to feel. The shit sandwich is structurally the same: the speaker cannot tolerate the discomfort of delivering the criticism cleanly, so they bury it in compliments to manage their own discomfort. The compliment sandwich is the speaker's ego-defense in feedback form.

The move to give feedback in service to something greater requires the speaker to have moved through their own discomfort enough that they do not need the cushioning structure. The speaker can be in the room with the criticism honestly, because they are not protecting themselves from the discharge of giving it.

See [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]] for the broader version: judgment is always protecting from an emotion underneath it. The judgment that arrives in feedback is no different. The clean feedback requires the speaker to have already metabolized whatever the dirty feedback would have discharged.

## What This Move Produces

When delivered well, the move produces:

- The recipient hears the criticism without defensiveness.
- The recipient feels supported toward their own goal, not attacked.
- The relationship between speaker and recipient strengthens through the difficult conversation.
- The behavior in question often changes, because the recipient now has a structural reason to change it (it blocks what they want) rather than a punitive reason (it is bad).

The contrast with the compliment sandwich, which often produces:

- The recipient hears the criticism and discounts the compliments.
- The recipient feels condescended to and slightly attacked.
- The relationship erodes slightly.
- The behavior often does not change, because the criticism was wrapped in a structure the recipient learned to distrust.

## The Joe Example: "You're a Dick"

Joe gives a parallel example from his own life. Someone told him, years ago, "Joe, you're a dick." Joe protested. The man said: "Don't fight it. There's no problem with it." Charlie adds: "You're just a dick." Joe found that he could take the feedback because there was nothing to defend against.

"I could take that feedback because there was nothing to defend against. The way that you're — because there was no 'you're wrong, you have to change.' There was no shame coming at me. There was no trying to — and what I'm hearing from you is that that's some version of that is what's happening and giving that level of feedback. And my experience is we give that level of feedback when we are not trying to not feel something." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The example is structurally the same as the Dr. K version, with a slight angle. In the Dr. K version, the speaker anchors the feedback to something the recipient is moving toward (becoming a great doctor). In the Joe version, the speaker does not anchor the feedback to anything — they just deliver it as observation, with no shame, no demand for change, no implication of badness. Both work. The common feature is the absence of the giver's discharge in the delivery. The recipient is not being made to take the giver's discomfort.

The two versions can be used in different contexts. The in-service-to-something-greater frame works especially well in mentorship, leadership, parenting, and any context where the recipient has a goal the speaker can credibly anchor the feedback to. The pure-observation frame works in contexts where the relationship is mature enough that the recipient can hear feedback as information without needing the anchoring.

## When to Use This Move

- Giving feedback to direct reports, mentees, students, or anyone in a developmental relationship.
- Parenting — naming what the child is moving toward and what behavior is in the way.
- Coaching — naming the larger thing the client wants and what is currently blocking it.
- Couples — naming the relationship the speaker is committed to and what the partner is doing that is making it harder to have the relationship both want.
- Any moment when the urge to use a compliment sandwich is rising. The urge is the signal that the move is needed.

## When This Move Does Not Apply

The move requires that the speaker actually be in service to something greater than themselves. If the speaker is just trying to discharge their own irritation, the in-service-to frame is theater — it dresses up the discharge in respectable clothing. The recipient still feels the underlying discharge and the dressing makes it worse, because the dressing is now also dishonest.

The internal check: am I delivering this for the recipient's path, or am I delivering this so I can stop feeling what I am feeling? If the second, do the feeling work first. The clean feedback can only come after the speaker's own material has been moved.

A second check: is the larger thing real? If the speaker is naming a vision the recipient does not actually hold (telling someone they have the potential to be a great doctor when the person does not want to be a doctor), the frame becomes manipulation. The larger purpose must be one the recipient has identified as their own. Otherwise the speaker is using "this is what you want" to control the recipient's behavior, which is its own form of attack.

## Tone

Direct, warm, clear. The move does not require softness; it requires honesty. The warmth comes from the genuine investment in the recipient's path, not from softening the criticism. The clarity comes from naming both the path and the obstacle cleanly. The recipient feels seen — both as someone with a goal and as someone with a specific blocker that needs addressing.

If the tone slides into hesitation, qualification, or the apologetic structure, the move has lost its medicine. The hesitation is the speaker's discomfort showing up. Return to the internal check and re-deliver from a cleaner place.
