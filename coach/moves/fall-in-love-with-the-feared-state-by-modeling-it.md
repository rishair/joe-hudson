# Fall in Love With the Feared State By Modeling Your Own Love of It

## The Move

The client has surfaced a feared state -- aimlessness, helplessness, sadness, boredom, panic, purposelessness. The standard instruction would be to invite the client to feel into the state and find it tolerable. The instruction-form often fails because the client cannot generate a felt-love of the state on instruction; they can perform it but the body knows it is performance.

The move is different. Instead of instructing the client to love the feared state, the coach demonstrates their own actual love of the state. Out loud. With specificity. With body-evidence. The coach is not modeling tolerance. The coach is modeling preference -- "I would choose this state over its supposed opposite."

The form Joe uses for aimlessness in the source session:

"All these people that on the thing, like, who's not aimless? I mean, I am dedicated to building this business and, and being of service to folks here and doing this stuff. Right. But 10,000 years, 1,000 years, it just looks like an, an ant, you know, building an anthill, just like everybody else."

And then, with full body-evidence:

"What's wrong with feeling aimless? I love the feeling of aimlessness. I love it. If you told me you have a choice, Joe, you can drive aimlessly, or you can drive in rush hour to get to work on time. I'm gonna drive aimlessly all day long." -- Joe Hudson, "I Should Be More Productive" Is Killing You

The structure has three components. First, the cosmic-scale view that undermines the fear's content. Second, the direct statement of the coach's love ("I love the feeling of X"). Third, a vivid concrete preference that demonstrates the love at body-scale ("if you offered me X or its opposite, I'd choose X").

## When Joe Uses This

The move fires when:

- The client has surfaced a feared felt-state and is locked in resistance to it.
- The client has tried to "let themselves feel it" and is producing performance rather than felt-contact.
- The client's frame is "this state is bad/dangerous/should be avoided" and a conceptual reframe alone won't shift it.
- The coach actually does love the state in question. (The move fails if the coach is performing love. Clients read the performance.)
- The descent has reached a bottom and the client needs evidence that the bottom is liveable.
- The body has been informed that the feared state will produce some catastrophe; the move offers counter-evidence in body-form.

The move is especially useful when the feared state is something the culture treats as a problem (aimlessness, boredom, helplessness, uncertainty, having no plan). The cultural frame is reinforcing the client's avoidance. The coach modeling love of the same state is a direct counter-vote.

The canonical sequence in the source session:

- **Client:** "Uh, maybe when you ask me that question initially, what springs to mind immediately was like aimlessness."
- **Joe:** "Great. Let's feel aimlessness for a bit. Yeah, what's happening in your tummy right now?"
- *(Client tries; spaces out.)*
- *(Joe names the avoidance: should as first distraction, spacing out as second.)*
- **Joe:** "All these people that on the thing, like, who's not aimless? ... 10,000 years, 1,000 years, it just looks like an, an ant, you know, building an anthill..."
- **Joe:** "I love the feeling of aimlessness. I love it. If you told me you have a choice, Joe, you can drive aimlessly, or you can drive in rush hour to get to work on time. I'm gonna drive aimlessly all day long."
- **Client:** *(small shift; "fair")*
- **Joe:** "You're talking, you're not feeling, you're not doing the feeling part. Feel the aimlessness."

The "feel the aimlessness" instruction at the end works better after the modeling than it would have worked at the start. The field has been loaded with the coach's love of the state. The client now has someone in the room who confirms the state can be loved. The instruction lands into that field rather than into an empty one.

## What It Produces

When the move works:

- The client's body shifts. The shift is often subtle -- a softening, a small smile of recognition, a willingness to try the felt-contact that was previously refused.
- The cultural frame around the feared state weakens. The client has met someone who does not share the cultural frame, which makes the frame less monolithic.
- The instruction to "feel" the state, when issued after the modeling, can be received. Before the modeling, the same instruction usually produces performance or refusal.
- The client may report a body-level confusion ("you actually love that?") followed by a small permission ("...okay") that opens the next layer of work.

When the move fails:

- The client treats it as a personality observation about the coach ("interesting, you love aimlessness, I don't") without registering it as evidence about the state itself.
- The client experiences the modeling as pressure ("the coach loves this, so I should") which converts the move into a new should and re-triggers the original avoidance. See [[dont-make-stop-judging-into-a-should]].
- The coach is not actually in love with the state and is performing love; the client reads the performance and the move adds distrust to the avoidance.
- The cosmic-scale reframe lands as nihilistic and triggers a defensive attachment to "but there has to be a point."

The most common failure mode is the second one (pressure as new should). The mitigation: the modeling has to be delivered without an expectation that the client will adopt it. The coach loves the state; the client may or may not. The non-attachment is what makes the modeling receivable.

## Why It Works

The move works at the level of the relational field, not at the level of the conceptual frame. A felt-state is loaded with all the cultural and personal meanings that have attached to it. When the client tries to "feel" the state, those meanings come with it, and the meanings produce the avoidance. The conceptual reframe ("aimlessness isn't actually bad") cannot displace the meanings because the meanings are body-level, not concept-level.

The coach's actual love of the state is a different kind of evidence. It is a body-level vote that the state is liveable. The client's body registers the coach's body. The cultural meanings are still there, but now there is a counter-vote from a person who is sitting in the same room without those meanings. The field around the feared state has been changed.

This is the same mechanism as [[love-the-thing-you-fear-cures-the-phobia]] applied to the inviting-the-client side. The phobia-cure principle says the cure is to love the fear. The teaching-question is: how does the client get to a love they have never had access to? One reliable route is for the coach to be in the love first. The client borrows the love long enough to try it. If the trial produces a real shift, the borrowed love becomes the client's own.

The move is also a structural instance of [[demonstrate-the-already-trained-detection]] and similar coach-as-instrument moves: the coach uses their own felt-state as the operational tool, not just as the diagnostic. The coach's body is in the room. What the coach's body is doing matters.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the modeling produces a body shift: immediately direct attention back to the felt-state. "Now feel it." The window of openness is brief. The coach's modeling has loaded the field; the next move uses the loaded field to invite the contact that was previously refused.

If the modeling produces a small "fair" or "okay" without a body shift: that is the head accepting the reframe without the body coming along. Joe's response in the source session: "you're talking, you're not feeling, you're not doing the feeling part. Feel the aimlessness." The intellectual concession is acknowledged and then pushed past. The move was not about the head agreeing. The move was about the body shifting.

If the modeling produces resistance ("but I'm not you, I can't just love it"): meet the resistance directly. The resistance usually contains the actual structure of the avoidance ("I can't love it because if I did, X would happen"). Surface the X, and X becomes the next layer of work.

If the modeling produces nothing: the move did not land. Possibilities: the coach was performing rather than loving; the feared state has not been surfaced specifically enough; the layer above is too active for the love to reach. Drop the move, go back to the descent, surface the actual blocker.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not VIEW-as-strategy. The coach's love has to be real. If the coach is using "I love this state" as a technique to get the client to love it, the client reads the technique. See [[view-as-technique]].
- This is not self-disclosure-as-therapy. The coach is not sharing a personal story about how they came to love the state. The modeling is in-the-moment ("I love this") and operational, not narrative.
- This is not advice. The modeling does not say "you should love this." It says "I do love this." The "I do" is the move; the "you should" would convert it into a should and lose the medicine.
- This is not the coach making the session about themselves. The modeling is brief and pointed. A single sentence, sometimes two, is usually enough. The session immediately returns to the client.

## Variations of the Move

The basic form: name the feared state, state your love of it, give a concrete preference-based example.

Variations by feared state:

- **Aimlessness:** "I love driving aimlessly. If I had a choice between driving aimlessly and driving to work on time in rush hour, I'd choose aimlessly all day."
- **Boredom:** "Boredom is the best. I save up for it. Most of my best ideas come in boredom."
- **Helplessness:** "Helplessness has been one of my greatest teachers. I welcome it now when it comes."
- **Uncertainty:** "Uncertainty is where everything alive happens. The certain part is the past."
- **Loneliness:** "I love being alone. I structure my life to get more of it."

The specifics vary; the structure is the same. State the love. Give a preference-based example. Let the body-level evidence work.

The cosmic-scale form (used in the source session): undermine the supposed importance of the thing being protected against. "10,000 years, it just looks like an anthill." This pairs well with the love-of-the-feared-state because it removes the felt-stakes that have been justifying the avoidance.

## What Distinguishes This from Bypass

Bypass would be: "Don't worry, aimlessness is fine, just feel it." That move treats the feared state as not actually being feared, which contradicts the client's experience and produces resistance.

This move treats the fear as real and gives it counter-evidence at the body level. The fear is not denied; it is met with a competing body-level signal from someone who is in the room. The bypass-frame says "your fear isn't valid." This move says "your fear is real and I want you to know that this state can be loved -- here's me loving it right now."

The distinction matters because clients can tell the difference. The bypass adds shame ("I shouldn't be afraid of this") on top of the fear. This move adds permission ("the state I'm afraid of is loved by someone I'm in connection with right now") underneath the fear.

## Tone

The tone has to match the actual love. If the coach loves the state with quiet enthusiasm, the modeling should be quietly enthusiastic. If the coach loves the state with humor, the modeling should be funny. Joe in the source session is funny: the rush-hour-versus-aimless-driving choice is delivered with a smile. The humor signals that the coach's love is real -- you don't joke about something you are performing love for.

The tone is also not solemn or therapeutic. The cultural frame around feared states wants the conversation to be solemn ("yes, aimlessness is a serious matter we must approach with reverence"). The casual-love tone collapses the solemnity and signals that the state is normal life, not a special territory requiring special equipment.
