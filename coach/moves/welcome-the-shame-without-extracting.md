# Welcome the Shame Without Asking Them to Stop Feeling It

## The Move

When someone in your presence -- a child, a partner, an employee, a client -- is in shame, the move is two-part and easy to get wrong by only doing one half:

1. Communicate that there is nothing in you that wants them to feel ashamed. Say it explicitly. "I see that you're ashamed, and I want you to know there's nothing in me that wants you to be ashamed."
2. Also communicate that they are welcome to feel the shame if they want to, and that you can be with them in it. "And I can be with you while you're ashamed."

Both halves are necessary. Without the first, the person feels their shame is being amplified by your judgment. Without the second, the person feels a new pressure -- "now I should stop feeling ashamed" -- which is itself a new shame about their shame.

Joe describes this with his daughters:

"I will literally say that to them. I'm like, 'I see that you're ashamed and I want you to know there's nothing in me that wants you to be ashamed. You're welcome to be ashamed, and I can be with you while you're ashamed, but I just want you to know there's nothing in me that wants you to be ashamed.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

## Why Both Halves

Joe is explicit that he initially got this wrong. He sometimes communicated only the first half, which created exactly the new shame layer he was trying to prevent:

"I did that once or twice. Yeah, exactly. Their shame was so uncomfortable for me that I wanted them to stop it." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

Brett names the failure mode:

"Otherwise people could be like coming from a place of trigger and frustration with somebody who's in a shame cycle around them, be like, stop feeling shame. And then that can just create a new whole new shame pattern around the shame." -- Brett Kistler, The Anatomy of Shame

The trap is that the shame in another person is uncomfortable to witness. Wanting them to stop feeling it is often about your own discomfort, not their wellbeing. The corrective move is the second half: "you're welcome to feel it, I'm here with you." This communicates that you can hold the shame, not that they have to manage it for your comfort.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not pretending you have no reaction to whatever they did. The move is about the shame itself, not about the action the shame is wrapped around. You can simultaneously:

- Not want them to do the thing again
- End a relationship over the thing if it warrants it
- Not want them to feel shame about it

"I will say to them, hey, I don't want you to do that again, and I might even stop our relationship, and I don't want you to feel ashamed. And the reason I don't want them to feel ashamed is because that's just going to recreate the behavior." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

The logic: shame reinforces the very behavior the shame is wrapped around. See [[shame-drives-behavior]]. Wanting them not to feel shame is wanting them to be free of the engine of the behavior. It is not approval of the action.

It is also not the move for someone in a chronic shame loop who is hurting you. In that case the move is to draw a clear boundary. See [[caretaking-prevents-the-bottom]]. The welcome-without-extracting move is for relationship repair and presence, not for absorbing repeated harm.

## When To Use This Move

- A child has done something they feel terrible about
- A partner is apologizing with heavy shame and wants to be made to feel okay
- An employee has made a mistake and is collapsing into self-criticism
- A coaching client is in active shame about something they have revealed
- A friend or family member is in a chronic shame loop and the pull is to either fix them or reject them

The move is the alternative to both fixing and rejecting. It is presence with the shame, without asking the shame to leave for your benefit, while also not endorsing the shame as accurate or necessary.

## Tone

Soft. The first half ("there's nothing in me that wants you to be ashamed") works only if the body language matches the words. A clenched delivery makes the words sound like a command. The delivery has to come from the place that is genuinely free of wanting them to be in shame.

The second half ("you're welcome to feel it") has to be neutral, not encouraging. You are not asking them to wallow. You are removing the pressure to perform okayness.

The composite tone is the tone of someone holding a door open. The person can walk through if they want. They can stay in the room with the shame if they want. Either is fine. You are not pushing them either direction.

## What This Move Produces

In children: the natural moral compass shows up faster, not slower. See [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]]. The child can process what happened without also having to defend against the parent's judgment.

In adult relationships: the shame stops being a third party in the room. The conversation can be about what happened, what is wanted, what is true, rather than about managing the shame.

In coaching: the client can stay in the difficult material without dissociating. The move creates the conditions for [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] to happen safely.

In Joe's experience speaking about his own daughters: "It makes me like misty inside to just think that I'm able to give that gift to my girls." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame. The move has a felt quality for the giver as well. Holding shame for another person without trying to remove it is itself an experience of love.
