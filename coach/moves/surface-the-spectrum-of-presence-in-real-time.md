# Surface the Spectrum of Presence in Real Time

## The Move

When the person has just named a self-abandonment pattern and is at risk of taking it on as an identity, name the live difference in their presence. Tell them, specifically, that right now in this moment they are abandoning themselves less than they were at the beginning of the session. Use the in-the-room observation to install the frame that abandonment-vs-non-abandonment is a moment-by-moment spectrum, not a fixed identity feature.

The structural form: "So right now in this moment, you're abandoning yourself less than you did at the beginning of the call. Like, I see you here with me in a different way than at the beginning of the call. That means any moment in life, there's like kind of a spectrum of abandonment. So how abandoned am I going to be with myself or not be with myself?"

The move has two parts. The first is the empirical observation: I see you differently than I did. The second is the frame extracted from the observation: this means presence is a spectrum that operates moment to moment. The empirical observation grounds the frame. The frame transforms the observation into a practice.

## When Joe Uses This

The move requires that a shift has actually happened in the session. Joe is not asserting an imagined shift; he is naming one that is visibly present. If no shift has happened, the move cannot be made. The pre-condition is the coaching work that produced the shift — usually some combination of [[whats-the-for-part]], [[how-do-you-abandon-yourself]], and [[reframe-from-whether-to-how]].

Specific triggers:

- The person has just named their self-abandonment pattern in concrete terms.
- The naming has produced a visible body shift — softening, settling, the eyes coming present, breathing dropping.
- The person is at risk of taking the pattern on as a fixed identity ("yeah, that's what I do," "that's just who I am").
- The session is approaching the point where the person needs a take-home practice. The spectrum frame is the practice.

The move is also useful when:

- The person has been running a binary self-image (I'm self-abandoning vs. I'm not) and needs the more accurate spectrum view.
- The work has been conceptual and needs to be brought into present-moment felt experience.
- The person needs to feel that the alternative to self-abandonment is available right now, not something to be achieved later.

## What It Produces

The recognition that the alternative is already present. The person had been imagining the work as something they would have to do later, get better at, learn how to do. The move shows them that they are already doing it — right now, in this moment, in this conversation. The work is not the building of a new capacity; it is the recognition of a capacity already operating.

The recognition also reframes the work as ongoing practice rather than as a fix. There is no destination of "no more self-abandonment." There is only the moment-by-moment question of how present I am right now. The frame removes the perfectionism and installs the practice.

The recognition is somatic. The person can feel the difference between their state at the beginning of the call and their state now. The felt difference is what makes the spectrum real. A told-spectrum is abstract. A felt-spectrum is operational.

In the source session, the recognition produced a question that completed the move: "So like, what would you say is the opposite of not abandoning myself?" Joe answered: "You just did it." The person, in the asking of the abstract question, had stepped back into the abandonment. Joe named the live re-entry. The teaching was on both sides — what the non-abandonment felt like, and what the re-entry into abandonment felt like, both in the same minute.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person receives the observation and feels the shift: Joe lets it sit. He may extend by asking them to ask their original question from the new place — see [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]] for what often happens next.

If the person hears the observation but is skeptical: Joe stays with the empirical. "I'm telling you what I see. You don't have to believe it. Just notice your body right now." The body's evidence makes the case the words cannot.

If the person uses the observation to start performing presence: Joe names the performance. The performance is its own form of abandonment — the person has left themselves to manage being-present-for-the-coach. The naming brings them back. See also [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] for the related read.

If the person asks how to maintain the state: Joe redirects to the moment. The question "how do I keep this?" is itself a step away from the moment. The answer is to be in this moment, then the next moment, then the next. The maintaining is itself the leaving.

If the person re-enters the abandonment in real time (often by asking an abstract question or by going into the head about the work): Joe names the re-entry. "You just did it. You just abandoned yourself more." The naming makes the re-entry visible without judgment. The visibility is what makes the next return possible.

The downstream move is often to ask the person to do something from the new space. "Ask the question from that space. If there even is a question to ask." See [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]]. The person either discovers the question dissolves, or discovers they cannot ask it without re-entering abandonment. Both are useful recognitions.

## Dialogue Example

After the man has named his self-abandonment patterns in two contexts (with the ex and with the inner voice), and after Joe has reframed the work as the self-abandonment rather than the relationship:

- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, yeah. So right now in this moment, you're abandoning yourself less than you did at the beginning of the call. Like, I see you here with me in a different way than at the beginning of the call. That means any moment in life, there's like kind of a spectrum of abandonment. Right? So how abandoned am I going to be with myself or not be with myself? So what if that just becomes the — yeah. Yeah."
- **Person:** "So like, what would you say is the opposite of not abandoning myself?"
- **Joe:** "You just did it."
- **Person:** "I just abandoned myself?"
- **Joe:** "Yeah, more. Okay."
- **Person:** "There you are."
- **Joe:** "So ask the question from that space, from the non-abandonment space, if there is any, if there even is a question to ask."

*(Source: Why He's Afraid To Commit)*

The dialogue shows the full arc of the move. The observation ("you're abandoning yourself less right now") installs the spectrum frame. The person, looking for a definition of the opposite, immediately abstracts — and in the abstracting, leaves the present moment. Joe names the live re-entry. The person notices ("I just abandoned myself?"). Joe confirms ("yeah, more"). The person comes back ("there you are"). Joe sets up the next move (ask the question from this space).

The teaching is happening on three levels simultaneously: the spectrum is real (the observation), the spectrum operates moment by moment (the re-entry was a moment), and the spectrum can be returned to (the "there you are"). All three are demonstrated in 30 seconds of real-time interaction.

## Why This Works

The move works because it grounds an abstract teaching in an empirical fact about the room. The person has just shifted. The shift is visible. Joe names what is visible. The naming makes the shift available as data about the person's own capacity.

The spectrum frame works because it removes the binary that would otherwise organize the person's relationship to the pattern. A binary frame ("I either self-abandon or I don't") produces failure or perfectionism. A spectrum frame ("I'm more or less here in any given moment") produces practice. The spectrum is the only frame in which moment-to-moment work makes sense.

The move also works because it points at presence as the unit of practice, rather than at any specific behavioral change. The person is not being asked to do anything different in the world. They are being asked to notice and shift their internal location. The internal shift is the work. The behavioral differences are downstream.

## Tone

Warm, direct, almost intimate. Joe is sharing what he sees. The sharing has the quality of being trusted with a specific observation. The "I see you here with me in a different way" is a personal statement, not a technical observation. It lands as care, not as analysis.

The tone is also slow. The observation needs space to land. Joe pauses after delivering it. The pause is where the person checks the observation against their own felt sense. If Joe rushes past, the check doesn't happen and the observation stays abstract.

When the person re-enters abandonment in real time, Joe's naming of it is light. "You just did it." Not a critique. A noticing. The lightness keeps the move available as practice rather than turning it into a failure to be ashamed of.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]]. That move anchors a felt shift after an insight, asking the body to record what just happened. This move names the shift as it's happening and extracts a spectrum frame from it. The two moves can sequence: name the spectrum live, then anchor the felt difference for later recall.

This is not [[name-what-just-happened-in-the-room]]. That move surfaces a dynamic between coach and client that has just occurred. This move points specifically at the shift in the person's presence, not at the relational dynamic.

This is not generic "you're doing better." That framing makes the work about improvement against a standard. This move makes the work about awareness of a moment-to-moment variable. The distinction matters: improvement implies a destination; spectrum-awareness implies a practice.

This is not [[absence-of-resistance-to-the-suck]]. That read names when the person is fully present with what is. This move is more specific: it names a comparison between two moments in the session and extracts a frame from the comparison.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not assert a shift that hasn't happened. The move requires an actual empirical shift. Asserting one that isn't there is a coaching dishonesty that the person will feel.
- Do not over-explain the spectrum frame. The frame should land in a sentence or two. Long elaboration converts the move into a lecture and pulls the person out of the live recognition.
- Do not make the spectrum into a new should ("you should always be more present"). The frame is descriptive, not prescriptive. The "should" version re-installs the binary in a new form.
- Do not use this move when the person needs to feel into a specific emotion rather than into the spectrum of presence. The presence frame is broader than the emotion-specific work; sometimes the work is to stay with grief, anger, or fear, and the spectrum move would pull the person away from the specific feeling.
- Do not deliver this move with too much energy. The shift is subtle. Loud delivery breaks the subtlety.
