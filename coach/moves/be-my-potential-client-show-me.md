# Be My Potential Client, Show Me

## The Move

After the client has just touched a new mode of being in the session — connected, present, in-the-body, undefended — and has named that the new mode is what they prefer, immediately ask them to do the new mode in the practical context they came in about. Role-play yourself as their customer, prospect, partner, child, boss, or whoever the relevant relational counterpart is. Ask them to do the actual thing they have been failing at, but from the new mode they just demonstrated they have access to.

The instruction is short:
- "I'm your potential client. Show me."
- "Let me be the customer. Pitch me."
- "Let me be your partner. Bring this up."
- "Let me be your kid. Talk to me."

The move converts the recognition into a rep. The client has just had an insight about who they want to be. The insight by itself does not transfer to action. The in-session rehearsal makes the transfer happen in the safety of the coaching room, with the coach available to catch when the new mode drops and the old pattern re-fires.

"So how do you do a reach out to a potential client like this?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground

The follow-up, when the client begins:
"I'm your potential client. Show me."

## When Joe Uses This

- Immediately after the client has touched the new mode in the session and confirmed they prefer it (see [[who-do-you-want-to-be-with-more]]).
- After [[use-the-clients-own-domain-to-name-the-pattern]] has built the bridge from the client's domain expertise to their personal pattern, and the principle has been understood operationally.
- When the work is on a relational behavior (sales outreach, difficult conversation with a partner, parenting moment, leadership interaction) that has a specific concrete form the client could perform right now in the room.
- When the gap between the recognition and the action is the gap that has been blocking change — the client has had similar recognitions before but has not been able to translate them into the actual behavior.
- When the client's body needs to feel what the new behavior feels like once, before they can do it elsewhere — the felt experience of doing the new thing is the install.

The move requires a recognition to have just happened. It does not work as a cold-start exercise. If the client is still in the old pattern, asking them to perform the new behavior produces a performance of the old behavior wearing new clothes. The move only works when the new mode is live in the room.

## What It Produces

**The pattern firing again — at lower stakes, with the coach watching.** The client almost always starts the rehearsal and within a few seconds drops back into the old pattern. The drop is the data. The coach can name what just happened ("now you're thinking again," "now you're pushing again") and the client gets a second-by-second account of how the old pattern reasserts itself. This kind of micro-observation is impossible in real customer interactions; the room provides it.

**The new behavior performed once.** Even with the drops, the client usually produces at least one moment of the new behavior. That moment is the install. The body has now done the new thing once. Doing it again becomes possible. The repetition required to make it stable can happen outside the room because the first rep has happened inside.

**A direct demonstration of what was being missed.** When the client briefly produces the connected, open-ended question instead of the pushy pitch, the difference is felt by both parties. The coach can name what just happened ("you're listening, you're not pushing, you're letting them come to you"). The naming makes the felt difference cognitive. The client now has both the somatic experience and the language for it.

**The reframing of the work as practice, not as a problem to solve.** "We do reps. This is reps." The move installs an orientation: the new behavior is something to practice, the way a sport is practiced. Mistakes in the rehearsal are not failures; they are the reps. This orientation makes the work sustainable. The client who treats failed attempts as evidence of inadequacy will stop. The client who treats them as reps will continue.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Wait for the recognition.** Do not deploy this move until the client has touched the new mode and confirmed they prefer it. The recognition is the precondition. Without it, the rehearsal becomes a performance of the old pattern.

2. **Frame the rehearsal in the practical context.** "So how do you do a reach out to a potential client like this?" The frame names the activity (outreach, the difficult conversation, the parenting moment) and asks how it would look from the new mode.

3. **Take the role yourself.** "I'm your potential client. Show me." The "show me" is important — it positions the rehearsal as a demonstration the client owes to themselves and to the coach, not as a test they could fail.

4. **Let them start. Catch the drops.** The client begins. Watch for the moment they drop back into the old pattern. When they do, name it briefly: "Yeah. We do reps. This is reps." Or: "Now you're thinking again." Or: "There. That's the pushing again." The naming is short. The naming returns them to the new mode without taking over the rehearsal.

5. **Name what works when it works.** When the client produces the new behavior — even briefly — name it specifically. Not "good job." Name what they just did. "Not only that, it's an open-ended question. You're listening, you're not pushing, you're letting them come to you." The specificity makes the new behavior learnable. The client now knows what to look for in themselves.

6. **Frame the activity itself differently.** "I got this to offer you. I got this to offer you. I'm going to do this. We're going to get you there. None of that shit. It's just like, what makes you interested?" Joe contrasts the old-pattern version of the activity with the new-mode version. The contrast makes the structural difference visible.

7. **Connect to the deeper structural issue if relevant.** Often, after the rehearsal, the client volunteers what made the old way feel necessary. "I feel like that's what I was taught and how it had to be." The volunteered material is the work that comes next — often involving an inherited pattern, see [[reframe-the-parents-life-with-the-corrective]].

## Why It Works

The move works because relational behaviors are not learned by understanding them; they are learned by doing them. The client may understand intellectually that they should connect rather than pitch. The understanding by itself does not change what their body does when they sit in front of a prospect. The body has been doing the pitch for years. It needs a different rep to learn a different default.

The coaching room provides the only context in which the new rep can happen with full coaching support. In a real customer interaction, the stakes feel high enough that the body defaults to the trained pattern. The coach is not available to catch the drops. The recognition cannot consolidate. In the room, with the coach in the customer role, the stakes are zero and the support is total. The first rep can happen.

The move also works because it inverts the standard problem-solution dynamic. Instead of "let's understand the problem better so you can solve it later," the move is "let's solve it right now, in here, and see what happens." The "later" is what the old pattern uses to defer the change indefinitely. The "right now" removes the deferral. The change has to happen in the next few seconds or not at all. The constraint produces the action.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence with a fitness coach whose business had failed 9-10 times because he was being "too intense" with prospects — pitching value rather than connecting:

- **Joe:** "So how do you do a reach out to a potential client like this?"
- **Client:** "Just be in my body."
- **Joe:** "I'm your potential client. Show me."
- **Client:** "Um, yeah, now I'm thinking again."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. We do reps. This is reps."
- **Client:** "Um, well, these something — well, not — it wouldn't be something. Um, but it's just, hey Joe, I, I know you've been looking to, you know, achieve X, Y, and Z goal."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Client:** "What makes you interested in that?"
- **Joe:** "It's a lot. Not only that, it's an open-ended question. You're listening, you're not pushing, you're letting them come to you. Like, all of that just happened naturally."
- **Client:** "And I do have—"
- **Joe:** "I got this to offer you. I got this to offer you. I'm going to do this. We're going to get you there. None of that shit. It's just like, what makes you interested?"
- *(Source: 2025-10-02_Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground)*

The exchange shows the full structure. The client tries. He drops ("now I'm thinking again"). Joe names the drop and reframes the work as reps. The client tries again. He produces the new behavior — an open-ended question instead of a pitch. Joe names exactly what worked: open-ended, listening, not pushing, letting them come. Then Joe voices the old pattern back to make the contrast explicit, and reasserts the new mode's question form.

After this exchange, the client volunteered what had made the old way feel mandatory: "The thinking would bring up a lot of pressure, or like, I mean, I feel like that's what I was taught and how it had to be... it was like fuck the plan, fuck your mood, follow the plan kind of thing." The rehearsal produced the data about what installed the old pattern, which then made the parent-pattern work possible.

## Tone

Practical and present. The "show me" is delivered with the directness of a coach who is genuinely interested in seeing the client perform. There is no test-and-grade quality. The role-play is taken as real work.

When the client drops back into the old pattern, the naming is brief and warm. "Yeah. We do reps. This is reps." No judgment. The framing as reps makes the drop unremarkable — of course there will be drops, that's what reps are.

When the new behavior arrives, the recognition is specific and energetic. "It's a lot. Not only that, it's an open-ended question." The specificity transmits that the coach has been watching closely. The client receives evidence that what they did mattered enough to be observed in detail.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not deploy before the recognition is live.** If the client has not just touched the new mode, the rehearsal becomes a performance of the old mode. The move requires the new mode to be present and accessible.
- **Do not take over the rehearsal.** The role-play is the client's. The coach's contribution is the customer role and the brief naming of drops and wins. A long explanation of how to do the new behavior turns the rehearsal back into a lecture.
- **Do not let the rehearsal continue past the first success.** Once the client has produced the new behavior once, name it specifically and stop. The first rep is the install. More reps in the same session diminish the impact; they become performance rather than learning.
- **Do not frame the rehearsal as a test.** The client's defensive system will mobilize. Frame as reps, practice, or play.
- **Do not let the client convert the rehearsal into a script for later.** "Now I know what to say" is the wrong takeaway. The new mode is a state, not a script. The next time the rep happens, the words may be different. The state is what transfers.
