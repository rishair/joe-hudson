# Ask What They Want Before Responding (Listen, Questions, or Advice?)

## The Move

When someone you love comes to you with a problem, a feeling, a story, or a hard day, pause before you respond and ask them which kind of response they want. Joe and Tara use three explicit options:

- Do you want me to just listen?
- Do you want some questions?
- Do you want advice?

The move is small. The reframe it produces is structural. The default in most relationships is that the listener runs a program — solve the problem, cook the food, fix the situation — without ever checking whether that is what the speaker actually wants. The three-option ask interrupts the program and converts the response from automatic to chosen.

"Do you just want to be listened to? Do you want some questions or do you want advice?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

## When To Use It

- When your partner comes to you with something and you feel the pull to respond a particular way.
- When you notice your partner getting cranky at how you are responding, mid-conversation. The crankiness is the data that you have run your default program rather than the one they wanted.
- When you would normally jump straight to advice and you suspect the person wants something else.
- When you would normally just listen and you suspect the person actually wants advice.
- Early in the practice, before either partner has developed the language to ask for what they want unilaterally.
- In any conversation where the response-mode mismatch is producing friction.

The mid-conversation version is critical. The ask does not have to happen at the start. Joe's example is interrupting his own running response: "Okay, wait, wait, wait. Do you want, what do you want? Do you want me to just listen? Do you want me to give you some advice? You want some questions?" The interrupt is the move when the read is "I am running the wrong program right now."

## What It Produces

- The speaker becomes conscious of what kind of response they actually want, which they often have not articulated to themselves.
- The mismatch where the husband solves while the wife wants to be heard (or the reverse) gets resolved at the source rather than after years of accumulation.
- The default program (give advice, fix, cook, solve) gets interrupted and seen as a program rather than as the natural response.
- The listener stops feeling responsible for a particular outcome and starts feeling responsible only for delivering what was requested.
- Resentment that comes from the speaker feeling managed/belittled/undermined drops because the speaker authored the response-type.
- Resentment that comes from the listener feeling unappreciated for their fixing drops because the listener is no longer fixing without consent.

Joe's specific report from years of doing this with Tara: he and Tara almost never want each other to coach or psychiatrist them. The discovery was structural — they assumed they wanted coaching from each other because that was the default mode. Once the ask was in place, the actual want became visible. The actual want, almost every time, was just to be with each other.

"We had this particular thing around that where we realized that what we almost never wanted was the other person to coach us or to be our psychiatrist. It's like, no, we want each other to be with each other." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

## The Felt Shift on the Listener Side

The most surprising consequence of the practice is what happens to the listener. The original assumption — "I am responsible for fixing this for them, I have to figure out the right thing to say, I have to make her happy" — gets exposed and dropped. What replaces it is the discovery that being with the person is actually easier than fixing them.

"This is so easy. Like, I don't have to do shit. I can just sit there and be loving and listen to her and she will sort out the whole thing herself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

"I used to think I had to do so much. I had this feeling of like, I'm responsible for her happiness. And now it's like, oh no, like I can just be with her. It's just that easy." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

This is the operational form of [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] and [[responsibility-stops-love]]. The three-option ask is the practical move that surfaces and drops the responsibility-for-her-happiness configuration. The speaker is not asking for the listener to take responsibility for the outcome. They are asking for one specific thing. The listener delivers that one specific thing. The configuration of who is responsible for whom gets reset, conversation by conversation.

## What Makes It Hard at First

The listening option is the hardest one for most people, especially for partners who have spent years in the help/fix/solve mode. Joe's own report:

"One of the coolest things was at the beginning, the listening was like so hard. I'm like, I got advice, I got advice... I felt so frickin frustrated. I was like, grrr." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

The frustration is not a sign the practice is wrong. It is the sign that the default program is being interrupted and the body has not yet developed the capacity to receive without producing. The discomfort is what learning looks like at this stage. See [[nail-in-the-head-metaphor]] for the canonical illustration of how absurd the impulse-to-fix looks when the speaker just wants to be heard.

The advice option also requires honesty in the other direction. Some partners default to listen-mode because giving advice feels intrusive, and they need to be asked to give the advice they actually have. The three options are not weighted; "give advice" is as valid a request as "just listen."

## Dialogue Example

A reconstructed shape of how this lands inside a conversation:

- **Tara:** [describes a hard situation at work]
- **Joe:** [starts to offer a solution]
- **Tara:** [gets a little cranky]
- **Joe:** Okay, wait, wait, wait. What do you want? Do you want me to just listen? Do you want me to give you some advice? You want some questions?
- **Tara:** I just want you to listen.
- **Joe:** [shifts into listen-mode]

The crucial detail is the noticing — Joe sees Tara get cranky and treats the crankiness as data about the response-type mismatch, not as a problem with Tara. The interrupt is non-defensive. The question is sincere. The reset is fast.

In Joe and Tara's current practice, after years of this, the ask itself has become rare because the defaults have shifted. They occasionally ask each other for coaching maybe once every two months. The rest of the time, the default has become be-with-each-other rather than fix-each-other. The asking was the training wheels; the configuration it installed is what stays.

## The Bigger Reframe This Move Carries

The three-option ask is a small move that names a much larger principle: most of what we do in relationships is running a program from past conditioning, not actually attuning to what the person in front of us wants. See [[attune-to-the-actual-want-not-the-program]] for the principle. The move is the operational interrupt that makes the program visible and chosen rather than automatic.

It also expresses [[different-languages-of-love]] from the response-mode angle. The husband's love-language may be "I help by solving." The wife's love-language at this moment may be "I want to be heard." Both are valid forms of love; the mismatch is the mismatch of channels. The three-option ask is the most direct channel-matching tool available.

## What Joe Does NOT Do With This Move

- Joe does not make the three-option ask a rule that has to be performed every time. The ask is a training-wheels move that drops away once the attunement has been built. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]] for the parallel principle about turning listening into a should.
- Joe does not weaponize the ask. ("Well, you didn't ask me which kind of response I wanted, so I'm going to be hurt.") The ask is offered, not demanded.
- Joe does not use the ask to avoid being present. ("I asked what you wanted, you said listen, so I'm just going to be passive.") Listening is active receiving, not passive presence. See [[loving-presence-as-defense]].
- Joe does not pre-decide what the partner wants and pretend to ask. The ask is real or it is not the move.

## Tone

Plain, practical, slightly amused at how simple it is. Joe presents this as a small thing that produced an outsized change. The teaching arrives without preciousness. The implicit message is that most couples could install this in a week and it would change the texture of their relationship.
