# Ask the Three Accountability Questions

## The Move

When something committed to did not get done, the leader asks three questions, in order:

1. **What made you not get that done?**
2. **What are you going to do to get it done?**
3. **What can I and/or the team support you in to get it done?**

The three together form an accountability conversation that surfaces what blocked the work, surfaces what the person plans to do about it, and surfaces what support they need. The conversation is welcoming, not threatening. The tone is the move as much as the questions are.

"My version of accountability is, what made you not get that done? What are you gonna do to get it done? What can I and/or the team support you in, in getting that done?" -- Joe Hudson

The three questions replace the usual default of accountability-as-trouble. Most people experience "I need to hold you accountable" as "you are about to get in trouble." The three questions do the actual work of accountability — what happened, what's next, what's needed — without invoking the trouble frame.

## Why the Tone Carries Half the Move

The same question can be threatening or welcoming depending on the tone. The interviewer in the conversation makes the point and Joe agrees immediately:

"What made that not get done could be really threatening. What made that not get done could be a welcoming, an invitation for understanding." -- the dialogue

The questions are not magic. A leader who runs the three questions with a punishing tone has produced the same effect as the usual accountability-as-trouble move with extra steps. The questions work only when the inner posture is genuine curiosity about what blocked the work, not interrogation dressed in soft language.

The diagnostic for whether the leader is in the right posture: are they actually curious about the answer, or are they preparing the next move based on what they expect to hear? If preparing, the tone will leak through regardless of the words.

## The Three Components

**1. What made you not get that done?** This is the diagnostic question. It surfaces the actual blocker — was it resources, was it understanding, was it competing priorities, was it personal life, was it that the work was harder than expected? The leader needs the information. The team member needs the chance to name it without the conversation immediately turning into criticism.

The question is open-ended on purpose. It does not presuppose blame ("why didn't you get it done?") and does not presuppose excuse ("what came up?"). It asks what made the not-doing happen. The agent is the situation as much as the person.

**2. What are you going to do to get it done?** This is the agency question. It hands the response back to the team member. The leader is not solving the problem; the leader is asking the team member what they intend to do. The team member who has a real plan brings it forward. The team member who does not surfaces that they need help with the next step, which is information for the third question.

The question also tests for ownership. A team member who says "I don't know" or "I think it's not actually possible" is providing data about the work and about themselves. The leader registers the data and proceeds accordingly.

**3. What can I and/or the team support you in, in getting that done?** This is the support question. It signals that the leader and team are on the same side as the person doing the work. The accountability is not the leader policing the team; it is the team helping each person succeed at what they committed to.

The question also opens a real channel. If the team member needs a resource, the leader can provide it. If they need a constraint lifted, the leader can lift it. If they need a different teammate to take on a piece, the leader can facilitate. The support question makes the leader genuinely useful in the conversation rather than just evaluative.

## The Reframe: Accountability Is Not Trouble

The most common failure mode in accountability is the conflation with trouble. Joe surfaces this directly:

"Most people think someone's going to get in trouble. Like, that's what they think. Oh, holding accountable is equal to trouble, or it's equal to control. ... Like, there's a lot of times this happens, like, outside of business, just like the idea of holding someone accountable is basically, I don't like what that person did, or they're scaring me and I want to manage them." -- Joe Hudson

"Everybody wants a team that's accountable, but nobody wants to be held accountable." -- Joe Hudson

The three-questions form interrupts the trouble frame. The conversation has a specific shape that is recognizably not the trouble shape. The team member who has been through the conversation a few times learns that being "held accountable" by this leader is not a punishment ritual. It is a real working conversation about how to make the thing happen.

## The Excellent Form: The Team Accountable to Each Other

The highest form of the move is when the leader does not have to run it because the team runs it on each other. The leader has installed the structure; the team uses it horizontally.

"Excellent accountability is when I'm not doing it, when the team is doing it to each other. Because in reality, and this is incredibly — this is an advanced tool, but if you can create a structure, again, a structure where the team holds itself accountable, it is far better for the organization because that's what's actually happening." -- Joe Hudson

The reasoning: when a team member fails to deliver, they did not fail the leader. They failed the team. The basketball analogy:

"If I'm playing on a basketball team and somebody misses all their 3-pointers, like, you didn't fail the coach, you failed the team, you failed the fans, you failed everybody. So why is it only the coach holding you accountable? That doesn't make any sense." -- Joe Hudson

The leader-only accountability structure is structurally misaligned with the actual relationship of failure to consequence. The team-runs-accountability structure is aligned.

## What the Move Is Not

**Not a script.** The three questions are the shape, not the exact words. The leader can phrase them differently as long as the three things get surfaced. "What's making this hard?" "What's your next move?" "What do you need from me?" is the same move in different words.

**Not unconditional acceptance.** The leader asks the questions, but the leader can also follow them with a real assessment. If the answer is excuses, the leader names that. If the answer reveals the work is not going to happen, the leader can say "this isn't working." The three-questions form opens the conversation; it does not pre-determine the conclusion.

"Sometimes it's gonna be, hey, I wanna know what makes that, that it's not, that doesn't get done, and, I gotta say, if I'm paying you $200,000 a year, my expectation is that that gets done. Like, that's way too much of an investment for me to have to track this or follow up on this." -- Joe Hudson

The form is welcoming. The form does not prevent the leader from naming what they need.

**Not a way to avoid hard conversations.** Some accountability conversations end with "this isn't the right role for you" or "this isn't working out." The three-questions form does not avoid these. It just ensures that even those harder conversations begin from a position of clear information about what actually happened.

**Not curiosity that accepts everything.** Curiosity does not mean every answer is fine. The leader can be curious about the answer and still find the answer insufficient. The two are not in tension.

"It doesn't mean you're not curious. It doesn't mean you're not full of wonder. It doesn't mean, oh, what happened? Oh wait, those are excuses. I see that you're giving me excuses, and I can only tell you that that lowers my confidence." -- Joe Hudson

The leader stays curious through the answer and then names what the answer revealed. The curiosity is the input; the assessment is the output.

## Where the Conversation Goes From Here

Based on what the three questions surface, the conversation goes one of several places.

- If the blocker was situational and the person has a clear next move: the leader confirms the support, schedules a check-in, and ends the conversation.
- If the person has lost belief in their ability to deliver: the work moves to the underlying belief or capacity issue. The accountability question opened a deeper conversation.
- If the answers are excuses: the leader names this directly. "I see you're giving me excuses. That lowers my confidence." The accountability conversation has surfaced a pattern that needs addressing. See [[excuses-lower-confidence-falling-down-raises-it]].
- If the person reveals that the role isn't a fit: the conversation moves toward [[it-just-isnt-a-match]] — a matter-of-fact recognition rather than a punishment.
- If the answer reveals a structural problem: the leader's next move is structural redesign rather than continued accountability on the individual.

## Tone

Open-hearted and curious. Joe's actual delivery is gentle in shape but firm in expectation. The questions are real questions. The leader is genuinely interested in the answer. The leader is also genuinely expecting the work to get done. Both are present without contradiction.

The most important thing about the tone is what it is not: not threatening, not interrogative, not setting up the next blow. The team member who has been through the conversation should feel that the leader is on their side and wants them to succeed, and that the leader is also clear that the work needs to happen.
