# Welcome Yourself in the Conversation

## The Move

Have the person welcome themselves right now, in the room, in the conversation. Watch what happens. The welcoming will start, briefly, and then stop. Name what stopped it. Send them back. Watch the next stop. Name that too.

The session structure is a loop:
1. **Welcome.** "Show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now."
2. **Stop.** The welcoming will halt — often within seconds. The person's face changes. The energy contracts. Something pulls them out.
3. **Name the stop.** "What happened? What'd you do?" The person reports what they noticed (got quiet, started trying, looked away, judged themselves).
4. **Return.** "Let's go back. Go back to welcoming yourself and notice what makes you stop." The person re-enters the welcoming.
5. **Repeat indefinitely.** Each loop produces new data about the specific mechanism that pulls the person out of self-love. The pattern of stops is the pattern of the wound.

The eventual instruction: "The idea now is that you're going to welcome yourself in this whole conversation." The welcoming becomes the throughline rather than a discrete exercise. Whatever arises during the conversation (sadness, shame, comparison, the trying to do it right) gets welcomed in real time.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person asks "how do I love myself?" framed as a question expecting a technique. Most people who ask this already know how to welcome themselves briefly. The demonstration reveals the access and the lockout.
- When the person identifies behaviors that look like self-rejection (dismissing accomplishments, ignoring needs, beating themselves up) and asks how to stop them. The move bypasses the behaviors and goes to the underlying capacity.
- When the person has done self-love work before and reports "knowing it but not living it." This move makes the not-living-it visible in real time.
- After the more conceptual moves (refuse the question, ask what answering would get them) have surfaced the request as a request for felt experience. The move converts the request into the experience itself.

## What It Produces

- Within seconds, the person can usually access self-welcoming briefly. The access is not the thing being missing — it is present.
- The welcoming stops, almost always, within seconds of starting. The stop is the data.
- A series of stops reveals the pattern: same kind of pulling-out each time, or a sequence of different kinds (first the trying, then the quieting, then the comparison, then the shame).
- The person often reports the welcoming feels light, easy, like they can move — followed immediately by the contraction.
- Naming the stops one by one builds a real-time map of the wound, which the person can see and feel rather than just understand.

The cumulative effect: the person stops trying to fix the dismissing-accomplishments behavior and starts to notice the stops directly. The work becomes meeting each stop with welcoming rather than fighting any one of them.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person welcomes themselves and then stops:** Don't ask why. Ask what just happened. "What'd you do?" Then send them back.
- **If the person reports trying to figure out the welcoming:** Name the trying as the pattern. "There's some trying that's going on in your system right now. The first time you welcomed yourself, it was an immediate [thing]." Then: welcome the trying. See [[welcome-the-trying]].
- **If shame arises after the welcoming:** Name the [[shame-after-spaciousness]] pattern. Then welcome the shame too. "When that shame arises, welcome that too."
- **If the person identifies a recurring obstacle (comparative mind, self-judgment, defensiveness):** Model the welcoming move on it specifically. "I welcome my comparative mind. It helps me be good in work. There's a brilliance in it." See [[welcome-the-pattern-by-naming-its-brilliance]].
- **If the person can welcome alone but not in the room:** Name the false-humility-with-others pattern. Then re-prompt the welcoming with people present. See [[the-good-alone-false-humility-with-others-read]].
- **If the person stays in the welcoming and the room softens:** Don't interrupt with the next teaching. Sit with it. Notice the redness in the face, the eyes being met by others. "Notice how much this is love, self-love. Eyes open. Look at them."

## Dialogue Example

The opening of the loop, compressed:

- **Joe:** "Show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now. Oh, you did it for a second. What happened?"
- **Guest:** "It's in here for a second, and then it [pulled away]."
- **Joe:** "Let's go back. Go back to welcoming yourself and notice what makes you stop. Just be with it. You're gonna — yeah, there it is. What happened? What'd you do?"
- **Guest:** "I feel like I just quieted."
- **Joe:** "So, okay, trying to — how is it to hold a quiet and a welcoming at the same time? So what I notice is you're — there's some trying that's going on in your system right now. There's some trying to figure it out. But the first time you welcomed yourself, it was an immediate [thing]."
- **Guest:** "I feel like I try a lot. I feel like it's sort of my [way]."
- **Joe:** "So welcome, welcome the trying."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

Later in the same session, the loop extends through multiple iterations and Joe consolidates:

- **Joe:** "Keep welcoming yourself. The idea now is that you're going to welcome yourself in this whole conversation. There's no reason to stop. And it's going to bring some crazy-ass emotions up, as you said."
- **Joe:** "So back to welcoming. Welcome the trying. Welcome the trying. And then just name the things that come up and then tell me how you're welcoming them."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

The closing recognition, after the loop has been running for the whole conversation:

- **Joe:** "And notice how much this is love, self-love. Eyes open. Yeah, look at them."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

## Tone

Gentle, patient, persistent. The pace is slow. Joe does not move the work along when the person stops welcoming — he sends them back. The repetition is not punitive; it is generative. Each return is a new chance to notice the stop.

There is a small warmth in the naming of the stops. "What'd you do?" is curious, not accusatory. "There it is" is delighted, like spotting a bird. The lightness in the tone matters because the work is not about catching the person doing it wrong. It is about making the pattern available to be welcomed.

When the room becomes spacious and the person is in the welcoming with others present, Joe does not narrate or analyze. He says "Notice how much this is love" and lets the person be in it. The teaching is the experience, not the description.

## Variations

- **For people who present self-criticism as their problem:** instead of trying to fix the self-criticism, run this move and notice that the self-criticism is one of the things that stops the welcoming. Welcome it too. See [[welcome-the-pattern-by-naming-its-brilliance]].
- **For people in couples coaching:** the partner can be brought in to welcome them too. See [[have-them-say-i-want-all-of-me-loved]] for the sibling move that uses a single spoken statement.
- **For people who report "I know this already":** the sibling move [[you-know-this-place-this-is-not-your-first-time-here]] acknowledges the knowing while still running the loop.
- **For groups:** the welcoming-in-the-conversation move can be extended to welcoming the whole group. "Just be with everybody here on the call with that welcoming, that deep welcoming."

## Anti-Pattern Risk

If the move becomes a discrete technique done once at the start of the session, it loses its function. The whole point is that the welcoming runs through the entire conversation and becomes the new orientation. Stopping it after one round resets the person back to the head-level engagement that the move is designed to escape.

The other risk: trying to make the welcoming happen "correctly." Welcoming is not a posture or a state to achieve. It is whatever the body does when it lets in what is here. If the coach (or AI coach) starts evaluating whether the welcoming is "real enough" or "deep enough," the evaluation becomes the new pattern pulling the person out of the welcoming.
