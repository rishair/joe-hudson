# Feel the Love, Then Feel the Obligation (The Live Trade Demonstration)

## The Move

In a single live exchange, surface the felt experience of love for a specific person, then have the client add the sense of obligation toward that same person. The client feels the love disappear in their own body. The principle that obligation cuts off love is no longer a teaching to be argued with. It is a felt fact.

The structure is two-step:

1. "Get in contact with the love that you once had for [person]." Wait for the client to report when they feel it.
2. "Okay, now I want you to feel responsible for [person]'s happiness." Watch what happens.
3. (Often:) "What happened to the love?"

The client invariably reports the love disappearing, dissipating, retreating. The disappearance is the move. There is no further explanation needed.

"So do me a favor. Get in contact with the love that you once had for your wife... maybe you still have some. Yeah, tell me when you feel it... Okay, now I want you to feel responsible for her happiness." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

"That's easy." -- the client

"What happened to the love?" -- Joe Hudson

"Disappeared, dissipated, retreated deep into a—" -- the client

"So you stopped loving her to take responsibility for her is what I'm hearing you say. Motherfucker." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## When Joe Uses It

- When a client is presenting a relationship — usually a marriage, a parental relationship, a long friendship, or a business partnership — that has gone flat
- When the client describes the relationship in the language of duty, responsibility, or obligation
- When the client says they "still love" the person but cannot feel the love in the moment
- When the client is wrestling with a stay-or-leave question and the obligation is part of what is keeping them stuck
- When [[i-caretake-people]] is the presenting pattern and the cost has not yet been seen
- When the client has intellectually grasped that obligation is not love but has not felt the trade

The move requires that there be some residual love available to find. If the relationship is fully closed, the love-finding step does not produce anything. In that case the diagnostic is different — the closure itself is the topic.

## What It Produces

The most common response: the client feels the love disappear in real time and is briefly stunned. The stunning is the recognition landing in the body before the intellect can rationalize it away.

A second common response: the client tries to argue with the experience. "But I do love her, I'm just being responsible." Joe does not engage with the argument. He insists on the felt experience. "I want you to actually feel it. This is an emotional experience, okay? So it's not a guess. It's like every time you took responsibility, acted out of obligation it stopped the love that you felt for her."

A third common response: tears. The recognition that the client has been doing this for years often surfaces grief — for the love that was traded, for the years of trade, for the person on the other side of the relationship who has been receiving obligation instead of love.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the love disappeared, Joe names the trade in plain language and lets the client sit with it. He does not rush to a solution. The recognition itself is the work.

If the client argues with the experience, Joe redirects to the body. "This is not a guess. Feel it." He refuses to engage at the conceptual level until the somatic experience has been honored.

If the client cries, Joe stays with the crying. The grief is part of the recognition. Often this is the moment when [[grief-as-gateway-to-self-love]] becomes available for the person, because the love they have been trading is most visible now.

If the client cannot find the love in step one, Joe does not force it. The inability is data. The relationship may be more closed than the client realized, or the love may be deeper underneath layers that need attention first. See [[reclaiming-abandonment]] and [[heartbreak-is-the-love-underneath-the-blockers]].

## The Variations

The move can be run with any object the client has obligation toward, not only a partner.

- "Feel the love you had for your work when you started. Now feel obligated to make it succeed. What happens?"
- "Feel the love you have for your children. Now feel responsible for their happiness. What happens?"
- "Feel the appreciation you have for money. Now feel obligated to make money. What happens?"
- "Feel the love you have for yourself in this moment. Now should yourself to do the thing you keep avoiding. What happens?"

In each variation the result is the same: the felt love disappears under the obligation. The move makes the principle in [[obligation-cuts-off-love]] viscerally available across any domain.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not run this move as proof of a theory.** The move only works if Joe is genuinely curious about what will happen. If it is run to "show" the client something, it becomes a manipulation and the client senses it. See [[wonder-as-strategy]].
- **Do not skip the love-finding step.** The trade is only visible if the love was felt first. Going straight to "you feel obligated" misses the point.
- **Do not rush past the disappearance.** The disappearance is the entire teaching. Pausing here lets the recognition land in the body. Moving on too fast turns it back into an idea.
- **Do not provide the solution.** Once the trade is felt, the solution is not "stop being obligated." The seeing of the trade is the work. See [[obligation-cuts-off-love]] for why the prescription is not the principle.

## Tone

Direct, sometimes sharp, occasionally profane. The move is alive — Joe is genuinely waiting to see what will happen in the client's body. The "motherfucker" that landed for the divorce client is a marker of the live quality. The tone is not solemn but it is unwavering on the somatic instruction: this is not a thought experiment, it is something to feel.

## Dialogue Example (Divorce Client)

- **Joe:** And where does obligation sit in your body?
- **Client:** Chest. Upper arms.
- **Joe:** Feel it for a minute. And what happens if you're just like — if you see that as the part of you that never got loved? And love it.
- **Client:** I can get to acceptance. It's hard to get to loving it.
- **Joe:** Is that what you're doing every time you accept responsibility for somebody else? You get to acceptance, but you don't actually get to love them?
- **Client:** Yeah, I think that's, that's fair. I can accept myself. I'm not sure I can get to loving myself.
- **Joe:** So do me a favor. Get in contact with the love that you once had for your wife... maybe you still have some. Yeah, tell me when you feel it.
- **Client:** [signals]
- **Joe:** Okay, now I want you to feel responsible for her happiness.
- **Client:** That's easy.
- **Joe:** What happened to the love?
- **Client:** Disappeared, dissipated, retreated deep into a—
- **Joe:** So you stopped loving her to take responsibility for her is what I'm hearing you say. Motherfucker.

## The Compressed Q&A Variant

In a shorter coaching window (a Q&A session of seven minutes or less), Joe sometimes uses a compressed version of the move that does not require the explicit "find the love / now add obligation" toggle. The compression works by asking a single harder question that contains the trade implicitly:

"You're living in this apartment for him for a month, you can't take care of him, and you have to feel love. What's that gonna require from you?" -- Joe Hudson, Should I Stay With My Ex?

The structure: instead of running the toggle and watching the love disappear, Joe poses a hypothetical that requires both removal of the obligation AND presence of the love. The client, trying to imagine this state, immediately surfaces the requirement: "Monumental amounts of unconditional love for myself."

The compressed version produces the same recognition as the toggle but takes one exchange instead of three. The client is doing the same somatic work — feeling what would be required to maintain love in the absence of obligation — but the work is done internally rather than demonstrated externally.

Use the compressed version when:
- The coaching window is short (under ten minutes)
- The client is already somatically available (can find the love quickly)
- The toggle would take too long to run cleanly in the available time

Use the full version when:
- The coaching window allows for it
- The client is in their head and needs the live somatic disappearance to make the trade felt
- The client has been intellectually agreeing with the principle but has not yet felt it
- The work needs to land with the sharp "motherfucker" quality of the live disappearance

Both versions are doing the same teaching. The choice between them is about the available time and the client's current somatic availability.
