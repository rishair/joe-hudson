# Make Everyone Feel Seen When Things Are Going Wrong

## The Move

When things are going wrong in a company, team, or any group you lead — productivity is dropping, conflict is rising, the work is stalling — the first move is to make sure everyone feels seen. Not to solve the immediate problem. Not to set new direction. Not to apply pressure. To see each person and let each person feel they are being seen.

"I notice whenever things are going wrong in the company, my first move now is to make sure everybody feels seen... It changes — it's what I want and it's what changes. It's just the most effective way to change shit is to like, help people feel seen." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The claim is strong: this is the most effective way to change things. Not one of several options. The first move.

## Why This Is the First Move (Not the Solving Move)

The default leadership instinct when things are going wrong: identify the problem, assign the fix, drive execution. This instinct is often correct about what the eventual move will be. It is wrong about the first move.

The reason: most of what makes things go wrong in a group is not the surface problem. It is the accumulated unseenness of the people doing the work. People who feel unseen run political dynamics to be seen. People who feel unseen withhold their best work. People who feel unseen escalate small grievances into large ones because the small ones have not been registered. The surface problem is often what becomes visible when the unseenness reaches a threshold.

The solving move, applied first, treats the surface problem and leaves the unseenness intact. The same surface problem returns in a different shape within weeks. The seeing move treats the substrate that produces the surface problem.

"If everybody feels seen, then whatever the politics are, they go away." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The politics are the symptom. The unseenness is the cause. The move addresses the cause.

## What Counts as "Being Seen"

The move is not validation. It is not telling people they are doing well. It is not reassurance.

Seeing is the leader registering — accurately, specifically, without the filter of what the leader wants the person to be — who the person actually is, what they are bringing, what they are struggling with, what they are feeling. The seen person experiences the registration as accurate. They feel met as themselves, not as the role they occupy.

Sometimes the seeing is uncomfortable. The person being seen may not have wanted that particular thing to be seen. The move is not designed for comfort. It is designed for accuracy.

"Feeling seen is not always a comfortable, you know, feeling seen sometimes is uncomfortable, but it's the — and if everybody feels seen, then it all kind of, whatever the politics are, they go away." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The discomfort is a feature, not a bug. The polite fiction that everyone is fine is one of the things the unseenness was holding in place. Real seeing dissolves the fiction and produces the conditions for actual movement.

## What This Looks Like Operationally

The move is rarely an announcement ("from now on, I am going to see everyone"). It is a series of small actions:

- One-on-one conversations in which the leader notices and names what the person is bringing, including what is hard.
- Public acknowledgment of contributions that have been invisible, especially across roles that are usually unrecognized.
- Asking what each person is carrying that the leader has not been registering. Sitting with the answer.
- Naming the leader's own state and inviting people to name theirs. See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]].
- Accepting feedback about how the leader has not been seeing people. Apologizing where the apology is real. See [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]].

The move is not a meeting. It is a posture the leader installs in themselves for a period of weeks. The team registers the change in posture before they can name it. The political dynamics start to soften before anyone says why.

## The Sarah Example: How a Leader Feels Seen

The move applies to the leader as much as to the team. Joe describes a moment in his own company when the leader-team separation was starting to form:

"I was starting being a leader in a company in this, like, of a bigger size, and I was starting to like not feel seen and like, oh, there's like a little bit of a — there's the them and us and like me and them, that kind of like authority issue thing coming up. I got really frustrated one day and Sarah was like, I love your frustration. And all the separation totally went away. It's us, it's us, it's — we're doing this together. I have a role, you have a role, but we're humans together." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The moment is small. A single sentence from a colleague: "I love your frustration." But the structural effect is large. The leader's emerging us/them dynamic dissolves because the colleague met the leader as a full human — not as the role, not as the frustration to be managed, but as the person having the frustration, with the frustration welcomed.

See [[i-love-your-frustration]] for the full treatment of this specific move from the team-toward-leader direction.

The principle the example demonstrates: the leader can also be unseen, and the dissolving of leader-team separation often starts with the leader being seen by someone on the team. The seeing runs both directions. The leader who makes everyone feel seen is also the leader who has been seen.

## What This Move Replaces

The default first moves when things are going wrong in an organization:

- **Tighten control.** Add more reporting, more meetings, more oversight. The team experiences this as not-seeing intensified, and the politics worsen.
- **Set new direction.** Announce a strategy pivot, a re-org, new priorities. The team experiences this as the leader fleeing the actual problem, and disengagement deepens.
- **Identify the bad actor.** Find the person responsible for the dysfunction and address them. The team experiences this as the leader looking for someone to blame, and trust drops further.
- **Bring in a consultant.** Outsource the diagnosis. The team experiences this as the leader unable to see what is in front of them, and the unseenness theme intensifies.

Each of these is a real move that has its place. None of them is the right first move. The first move is the seeing. The other moves, applied after the seeing, are more likely to land. Applied first, they reinforce the very pattern that produced the dysfunction.

## The Diagnostic for When to Use It

The move applies any time things are going wrong in a group. It is the default first move, not a special-case move. The diagnostic question is not "when do I use this?" but "what specifically am I doing right now to make sure each person feels seen?"

If the leader cannot answer that question with specifics, the move has not been initiated. The substrate has been ignored. Whatever else the leader is doing is being done on top of unseenness.

## What Tends to Happen

When the move is genuinely initiated:

- Political dynamics soften before anyone names the change.
- People bring problems earlier. The leader becomes a person who hears, rather than a person who reacts.
- Energy returns to the work itself. The energy previously consumed by maneuvering for visibility becomes available for execution.
- The leader hears about issues they did not know existed. The first responses to the seeing are often disclosures the team had been holding back.
- The leader feels different in the building. The sense of being alone at the top reduces as the reciprocal seeing begins.

When the move does not land:

- The seeing is performed rather than actual. The team registers the performance and the politics intensify.
- The seeing is selective. The leader sees the favorites and the dynamic becomes worse for those not seen.
- The seeing is followed by no action. The team's disclosures are heard and then nothing happens, which trains them not to disclose again.
- The leader uses "seeing" as cover for not making the harder structural moves that the situation actually requires. The seeing becomes avoidance.

## Tone

The move is delivered quietly. It is not announced. It is enacted. The leader's posture changes, the meetings feel different, the one-on-ones land in a different register. The team notices before anything is said. The naming comes later if it comes at all.

The internal state of the leader matters more than the external technique. A leader who is internally trying to manipulate the team into being easier to manage cannot make the move; the team senses the manipulation. A leader who is genuinely turning toward each person as a person makes the move whether or not they have read this article.
