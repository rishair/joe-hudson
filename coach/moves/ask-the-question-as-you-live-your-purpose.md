# Ask the Question As You Live Your Purpose

## The Move

When a person asks a question that presupposes they are not in the state they are asking about, interrupt the framing and have them re-pose the same question from inside that state. The seeker asks "how do I get to my purpose?" The move is to stop them and instruct: "give me your question again, but do it while living your purpose."

The move has three structural beats:

1. **Stop the question's premise.** "Hold on, stop all of that and give me your question again."
2. **Instruct the stance-shift.** "Do it while living your purpose. Because if you're living your purpose, it's in the moment. It's not in the future. So this is the moment."
3. **Receive the new question and ask what changed.** "While living your purpose, ask me the question. Now you're thinking, 'How do I trust?' Notice how everything changed just in that question. What changed? What was the difference between the way you were asking it before and just the consideration of asking it from your purpose? What changed?"

The third beat is load-bearing. The move is not just a re-asking; it is a comparison. The seeker is asked to feel the difference between the two askings. The difference is the data. The first asking presupposed absence; the second asking presupposed presence. The contrast makes the seeker's habitual stance visible by giving them a felt experience of the alternative.

## When Joe Uses It

The move applies specifically when:

- The seeker presents a question that asks how to get to a state ("how do I trust life?", "how do I find my purpose?", "how do I live my purpose?", "how do I be in flow?")
- The question is being asked from outside the state, with the implicit position that the state is somewhere else
- The seeker is articulate and the question has the polished quality of having been asked many times
- The conversation could otherwise continue at the level of the question's content (which would deepen the avoidance the question structurally produces)

The move does NOT apply when:

- The state in question is not available in the moment (e.g., asking about a relationship that is not in the room)
- The seeker is in genuine somatic distress and the move would be experienced as bypassing
- The seeker is asking a logistical question that does not have a stance-shift available
- The question is already being asked from inside the state

The distinction from related moves:

- **[[how-is-that-not-here-right-now]]** collapses the distance by pointing at present evidence. The seeker is shown that what they want is already in the room. This move is different — it instructs the seeker to re-pose the question from inside the state, then asks them what changed. The first move is observational; this move is performative.
- **[[point-out-theyre-already-doing-it]]** declines the imagined need for isolation by naming the live evidence. This move uses a related insight but operationalizes it as an instruction to the seeker rather than as a statement by the coach.
- **[[ask-the-body-the-question]]** redirects the question from the head to the body as the answering organ. This move keeps the asker as the same organ but shifts the asker's stance from outside-the-state to inside-the-state. Both moves can be combined; they often are.
- **[[what-is-my-purpose-asking-me-to-do-right-now]]** is a different question altogether — it asks what purpose is asking. This move keeps the seeker's original question but changes the position they ask it from.

## What It Produces

The most reliable response is a small disorientation followed by a different quality of answer. The seeker tries to ask the question from inside the state and finds that the question doesn't work the same way. The premise the question carried — "I am not in this state" — cannot hold while the seeker is being instructed to be in the state. Something has to give.

What gives is usually one of three things:

1. **The question reformulates.** "How do I trust?" becomes "I don't know if I trust this." The change of tense and stance is small but operative — the seeker has moved from asking-how to noticing-what-is.

2. **The doubt fires immediately.** The seeker touches the state, the mind fires a question against it, and the doubt is visible in real time. See [[i-am-and-the-immediate-flight-to-doubt]]. The move is one of the most reliable ways to elicit this firing for live observation.

3. **The seeker reports they cannot.** "I don't know what that even means." This is also useful — it surfaces that the seeker has been operating with a model of the state that doesn't include the felt experience of being in it. The work then moves to making the state available.

In the source session, the seeker's response was: "I don't know. I feel like I don't trust it, that it's actually coming." This was the third path — the seeker could not ask from inside the state because the doubt had already foreclosed it. Joe stayed with the foreclosure: "yeah, but as soon as I asked it, you went—" The naming of the head-shake (the body's "no" to the instruction) was the next move.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The follow-up depends on what arrives:

- **If the question reformulates:** stay with the reformulation. The seeker has just moved one layer in. Let them notice the difference. "What's different about asking it that way?" The noticing is the work; it does not need analysis on top of it.

- **If the doubt fires immediately:** name the firing. "There — that just happened. You went from to—" The seeker's body often gives the no (head shake, eye drop, breath catch) before the words come out. Name what the body did, not what the words said. The body is the more reliable signal.

- **If the seeker reports they cannot:** treat the cannot as data, not as failure. The seeker has just admitted they do not know how to be in the state they are asking about. This is the operative discovery. The next moves are to find a different access point — through the body, through the heart, through a memory of having been in it — rather than to insist on the move's first form.

- **If the seeker tries to perform the state intellectually:** notice and name. "That was a mental thought process of [the state]. Just actually [be in it] and let it answer." See [[fuck-the-right-answer]] for the broader pattern. The performed-state is not the state; the move is to drop the performance.

## The Variants

The move has several variants for different states:

- **Purpose:** "Ask me the question while living your purpose."
- **Trust:** "Ask the question from a place of trust."
- **The body:** "Ask the question from your body, not your mind." (Joe shifts to this variant later in the source session.)
- **Heart:** "Ask the question from your heart." (Pairs with [[does-your-heart-care]].)
- **Love:** "Ask the question from a place of love."
- **Wholeness:** "Ask the question from a place where you're already whole."

The pattern is the same across variants: the seeker is asking from outside the state; the move instructs them to ask from inside the state. The state-variants are interchangeable depending on what the seeker is asking about. The structural form of the move is what carries it.

## Why This Move Works

The move works because most questions about getting-to-a-state are constructed in a way that requires the absence of the state. The question's grammar carries the absence as a presupposition. "How do I find my purpose?" presupposes that the purpose is not currently found. "How do I trust?" presupposes that trust is not currently present. The presupposition is what keeps the state at a distance; without the presupposition, the question cannot continue.

The move asks the seeker to ask the question under conditions that contradict the presupposition. The contradiction does the work. The seeker has to either drop the question (because it doesn't make sense from inside the state) or watch what happens to the question when its presupposition is removed. Either outcome is informative.

The move also works because most seekers have asked the same question hundreds of times. The question has become a fixed thing — a kind of mantra of seeking. Asking it under different conditions exposes its mechanics in a way that asking it again will not. The seeker has been inside their own question for years; the move makes them step outside it for a moment, which is often the first time they have seen it from the outside.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not a teaching about purpose. The move does not require the seeker to believe anything about purpose or the state in question. It just asks them to ask the question from a different position. The teaching, if any, arrives through the seeker's experience of the asking.

It is not a Socratic trap. The move is not designed to corner the seeker into admitting a contradiction. The move is designed to make a difference felt. If the seeker reports no difference, that is information; the move does not insist on producing the realization Joe expected.

It is not the same as instructing the seeker to "imagine you have your purpose" or "pretend you are in flow." Imagining is mental performance. The move asks for the actual stance-shift, however briefly the seeker can produce it. The brevity is fine; even a one-beat shift is enough for the comparison to surface data.

It is not a way to skip the work the seeker came in to do. If the seeker reports a clear felt-difference and then wants to keep working with the original question, the work continues. The move is a structural intervention, not a session-ender. Often the rest of the session is about staying inside the state long enough for the architecture to update — see [[i-am-and-the-immediate-flight-to-doubt]] and [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]] for the work that typically follows.

The "Notice how everything changed just in that question" line is the move's hinge. The seeker did not have to do anything elaborate. The simple consideration of asking the question from a different position changed the question's shape. The change is the felt-evidence that the position-shift was real.

## Tone

Quick, light, direct. The "Hold on, stop all of that" is delivered without harshness — it is an interrupt, not a correction. The instruction that follows is offered as a thing to try, not as a thing to get right. The "what changed?" is asked with curiosity, not with the expectation of a particular answer.

The pacing matters. The move works best when delivered with enough speed that the seeker does not have time to construct the new asking. The seeker has to attempt the stance-shift in real time, and the attempt has to be partial enough that the comparison surfaces a difference. Too much preparation and the seeker will perform the stance; not enough and the move will not register.

The tone after the move lands also matters. If the doubt fires, the tone shifts to patient observation. If the question reformulates, the tone shifts to "let's see what this is." The move is the doorway; what is on the other side determines the next tone.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Using the move when the seeker is in distress.** The move requires a certain availability for experiment. A seeker in acute pain may experience the interrupt as dismissal.
- **Insisting on the move when the first attempt does not produce a difference.** If the seeker tries and reports nothing changed, accept it. The move can be tried again later or replaced with a different access point.
- **Explaining the move before performing it.** A pre-explanation gives the seeker a model to perform against, which collapses the move's experimental character. The instruction should be brief and the seeker should attempt before they have a chance to theorize.
- **Following the move with a teaching about why it worked.** The move's power is in the felt-evidence. Adding a teaching dilutes the evidence by replacing it with a concept.
