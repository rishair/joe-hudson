# Write to Your Team Like You Write to a Customer

## The Move

When writing internal communication -- a leadership letter, a policy announcement, a strategy update -- write it the way you would write a marketing email to a customer you actually want to keep. Ask: what is the problem I am solving for them? What are their likely objections? What is the thing that would make them care about reading this? What would respect their attention?

The move is a deliberate reframe of the audience. Internal communication often gets written from the implicit stance of "I am the authority, I have decided, I am informing." Marketing email gets written from the stance of "I am asking for your attention, your engagement matters, I owe you the respect of giving you a real reason to care." The two stances produce very different documents about the same content.

"If I was writing that letter to my folks, it would be like a marketing email. Oh, what's the thing that's gonna make them read this? What's the thing that they care about? What's the problem I'm solving for them? This is, this is, these, these are the customers. These are the people. These are the users. How do I, how do I look at what their, what their objections are? How do I respect them the same way I'd respect a customer?" -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

## When to Use It

The move applies to any internal communication where:

- The leader is asking the team to do something different
- The leader is communicating a decision that affects the team
- The leader is trying to land a culture change
- The leader is publishing a policy, a value, a principle, or a strategy
- The leader is writing anything that they want the team to actually engage with rather than just skim

The move applies regardless of company size. A 10-person startup needs this as much as a Fortune 100 leader. The dynamic is the same.

## The Anti-Pattern It Corrects

The anti-pattern Joe describes is the leader writing "I've said it, therefore it shall be" -- the message whose form treats the audience as already obligated by the leader's having said the thing.

A Fortune 100 example: a CEO sends a letter saying the company is becoming too bureaucratic and execution is dropping, and therefore the company needs to change. The form of the letter is itself bureaucratic. The team that received it laughed at it for being one of the worst-written letters they had seen. The content was about reducing bureaucracy; the form was bureaucracy.

"Instead of, oh, I've, I've told them we are now going to be less bureaucratic and therefore it shall be less bureaucratic. And the email itself was like the most bureaucratic email because it wasn't, it wasn't like showing that respect. It was, well, I've said it, therefore it shall be. And obviously it's not gonna work that way." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The form of the communication trains the team in what to expect. A bureaucratic email about becoming less bureaucratic teaches the team that the leadership doesn't actually mean it. A respectful, customer-grade email about the same topic teaches the team that the leadership is modeling the change in the very act of communicating it.

## The Specific Questions to Ask Before Writing

Before sending an internal message, run the marketing checklist:

- **What is the thing that's going to make them read this?** Not what I want them to read. What would they want to read.
- **What's the problem I'm solving for them?** Not the problem I'm solving for the company in the abstract. The problem in their specific experience.
- **What are their objections?** Anticipate them. Address them. Treat them as legitimate.
- **What respect am I owing them?** They are people whose attention I am asking for. What would acknowledge that?
- **What is the call to action and is it clear?** Not what they "should" do because I said so. What I am inviting them to do and why it serves them.

The checklist is not different from what a competent marketer would do for an external email. The leverage is in applying the same discipline to internal audiences. Most leaders do not.

## Why This Is a Deeper Move Than It Sounds

The move is not a writing tip. It is a relational reframe. Treating employees with the respect you would give customers requires you to actually see them as people whose engagement is voluntary in the relevant sense -- you cannot command engagement; you have to earn it. The leader who does not see this writes commands. The leader who does see it writes invitations.

The reframe connects to [[reframe-boss-as-customer]], which works the other direction: the employee reframing the boss as a customer rather than as an authority. Both moves operate from the same underlying insight: the boss-employee relationship is a transaction between two parties who are both choosing to be in it, and the relationship is healthier when both sides treat it that way.

## What This Move Produces

When applied consistently:

- Internal communications get read more carefully
- The team experiences leadership as recognizing them as people, not as recipients of decisions
- The content of the message lands because the form invites engagement
- The leader develops a discipline that exposes their own assumptions about audience and authority

When skipped:

- Internal communications are skimmed at best, ignored at worst
- Each message reinforces the experience of leadership as detached from the team
- The team learns to wait out leadership initiatives because they sense that the form of the message reveals the leader doesn't actually mean it
- Politics expands to fill the gap that real communication would have filled

## What This Connects To

The move is one specific operational form of treating the internal organization with the same respect a market-disciplined company treats its customers. The broader pattern: anywhere the leader is asking for engagement, attention, alignment, or action from the team, the marketing discipline applies. The communication is itself part of the culture being created.

This is also a specific instance of [[spit-at-the-top-is-a-waterfall-at-the-bottom]] -- the leader's communication choices cascade. Bureaucratic communication from the top produces a more bureaucratic experience throughout the organization. Marketing-grade communication from the top permissions everyone to write more like that, both to each other and (importantly) to the leader.

## Tone

Joe describes this with the directness of someone who finds the anti-pattern almost embarrassing in hindsight. The implicit message: of course you would do this, why would you not? The fact that almost no one does it inside organizations is the operational gap to address.
