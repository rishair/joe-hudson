# Now Resist Change (The Paradoxical Instruction)

## The Move

The person describes a pattern they're stuck in -- resisting change, looking for what's wrong, monitoring threats. Instead of asking them to stop or change the pattern, Joe asks them to do it on purpose, right now, in the room.

"So resist change. No, right now, resist it. Change is happening. There's no doubt your hormonal state has changed. Your heart rate has changed. Your emotional state has changed. So resist it." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Trust Myself?

The instruction is precise: do the thing you've been doing unconsciously, but consciously. Bring the automatic behavior into deliberate action. Don't think about doing it. Just do it.

## When Joe Uses It

This move is used when:
- The person has named a pattern they understand but can't drop
- Talking about the pattern has stopped producing movement
- The pattern is somatically available -- it's something the body does, not just something the mind does
- The person is in a comfortable enough place to do an experiment
- The person tends to intellectualize and would otherwise discuss the pattern indefinitely

The move bypasses the intellect. Instead of analyzing the pattern, the person performs it. The performance produces the felt sense of the pattern. The felt sense produces awareness in a way analysis cannot.

This is related to other "perform it rather than discuss it" moves like [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]. The principle is the same: the body learns through doing, not through thinking about doing.

## What It Produces

Several common reactions:
- Confusion. The instruction feels backwards. The person was trying to stop the pattern, and now they're being asked to do more of it.
- Resistance. "I can't even conceptualize how to do that." Joe responds: "Don't conceptualize it. Do it."
- Recognition. Once the person does it, the felt sense of the pattern becomes obvious. "Yeah, that checks out. That's most days. Yeah."
- Release. Once the pattern is fully felt as something being done rather than something happening to them, the grip often loosens. The person can choose to keep doing it or to let it go because it has become visible as a choice.

The release happens because the pattern was running on unconscious autopilot. Once it's visible as a deliberate action, it can be felt as a deliberate action. Deliberate actions are easier to release than involuntary ones. The instruction converts the pattern from "this is happening to me" to "I am doing this," and the I-am-doing-this version can be put down.

## What Joe Does Next

After the person performs the pattern, Joe asks them to do the opposite. "Now feel the opposite of that. Don't think, just feel."

The contrast does the work. The person now has two felt experiences: the pattern and its absence. The contrast makes the pattern visible as a chosen state rather than the only state. The opposite then becomes available because the person has felt that it exists.

Joe doesn't moralize. He doesn't say "see, the opposite is better." He lets the contrast speak. The person usually discovers, on their own, that the opposite feels different. The discovery is theirs, which makes it more durable than a told-truth would be.

## Why It Works

The pattern has been operating below awareness. The person has been resisting it from inside its operation, which is impossible -- you can't see the water you swim in. Bringing the pattern into deliberate consciousness gives the person, for the first time, a position outside it.

This is related to the principle "you cannot transform what you are inside of." The pattern was running because the person was running. To stop it, they had to step outside it. The instruction is the step outside.

Once outside, the person can choose. They couldn't choose before because there was no choice -- the pattern just was. Now there are two states (the pattern and its absence) and the person can move between them. That mobility is the freedom.

There's also a small comedy in the instruction that helps. "Now resist change" is structurally absurd because change is happening and cannot be stopped. The absurdity makes the pattern visible as absurd. The person catches the joke and can laugh at what they've been doing. The laugh is part of the release.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "It's like this box I've made myself is familiar now. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "So how's that working for you?"
- **Person:** "Terribly. Terribly."
- **Joe:** "What makes you keep doing it?"
- **Person:** "This is just familiar."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so resist change."
- **Person:** "You can, it's..."
- **Joe:** "No, right now, resist it. Change is happening. There's no doubt your hormonal state has changed. Your heart rate has changed. Your emotional state has changed. So resist it."
- **Person:** "There's so much tension in my body trying to even conceptualize resisting it."
- **Joe:** "Don't conceptualize it. Do it."
- **Person:** "Do it."
- **Joe:** "Now start looking around to see which one of these changes happening is dangerous. So that's how you're living."
- **Person:** (silence)
- **Joe:** "Okay, now do all of that. Keep doing it. Keep doing it. You're resisting change. You're looking around now. Stick a smile on your face."
- **Person:** "Yeah, that checks out. That's most days. Yeah, right."
- **Joe:** "So if you feel it all in a moment, that's what it's like. Now just feel the opposite of that. Don't think, just feel."
- *(Source: How Do I Trust Myself? -- The deliberate performance of the pattern produces the recognition that allows release.)*

## Tone

Light, almost cheerful. The move is not serious. The instruction "now resist change" is delivered with the small smile of someone offering a game. The cheerfulness is part of what makes the pattern feel less heavy when it's performed.

If the person hesitates or pushes back, Joe is firm but not pressuring. "Don't conceptualize it. Do it." The firmness is friendly. The move doesn't work if the person doesn't actually do it; it stays an interesting idea. So Joe holds them to the doing without making it a power struggle.
