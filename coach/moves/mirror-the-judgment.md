# Mirror the Judgment Back

## The Move

When the person delivers a judgment of another person (often the authority figure they came to talk about), Joe takes the exact quality named and asks how it is not true about the person themselves.

"What makes you judge your father? ... How is that not true about you? That's a scary question for me to ask, but how is that not true about you?" -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

The move is built on a working assumption: the qualities a person most strongly judges in others are usually qualities they are running themselves but cannot acknowledge. The mirror is not an attack. It is an invitation to see.

## When Joe Uses It

When the person delivers a judgment with intensity. The intensity is the signal that something is being projected. The mirror move is unlikely to land if the judgment is calmly observed. It is most useful when the person is in the heat of the complaint.

Also useful when the person's surface concern is "they don't see me" or "they're wrapped up in themselves" or "they don't really care" or any complaint that has a quality the person could plausibly be running themselves.

Not used when the judgment is structurally about behavior the person could not possibly be enacting (a complaint about violence, for example, against a non-violent person).

## What It Produces

Often, immediate recognition. The person sees the mirror and laughs, or says "oh, interesting," or pauses. The recognition is usually accompanied by a slight expansion in available stance.

Sometimes, defense. The person doubles down on why the other person's version is worse. Joe does not push. He waits, perhaps reflects the defense back, and lets the question sit. The mirror does not need to land in the moment to do its work.

Sometimes, a deepening. The person finds that the quality they judged is not only present in them but is connected to a deeper want. In the source session: "I want to be seen, it seems like, again, by certain people. It's because I'm still wanting that one kind of attention I've decided I didn't get."

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person recognizes the mirror, Joe asks the next question: "So how is that not being wrapped up in yourself?" He keeps the mirror going until the recognition is full. The point is not to confess to one shared quality. It is to dissolve the projection enough that the person can see the dynamic in a different way.

If the person defends, Joe sometimes lets it sit, sometimes asks a sideways version of the same question. "Okay, so when they do this thing, what are you not seeing about them in that moment?"

If the person deepens, Joe follows the deepening. The mirror has done its work and the new material is now available.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I thought he was wrapped up in himself so much that he couldn't see anyone else."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. How is that not true about you? That's a scary question for me to ask, but how is that not true about you?"
- **Person:** "I want to be seen, it seems like, again, by certain people. It's because I'm still wanting that one kind of attention I've decided I didn't get."
- **Joe:** "Right. So how is that not being wrapped up in yourself?"
- **Person:** "I think it is."
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority)*

## Tone

Gentle and curious, with explicit acknowledgment that the question is hard. Joe flags it: "That's a scary question for me to ask." This pre-empts the defensive reading. The flag tells the person Joe is not delivering a verdict, he is offering a hard look together.

The move fails when delivered as an accusation. It works when delivered as a wondering.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not deliver the mirror as a gotcha. The point is recognition, not winning.
- Do not assume every judgment mirrors something. Some judgments are clean observations.
- Do not stop at one mirror. The pattern usually has multiple layers; keep the question going.
- Do not name the projection theory out loud. Let the recognition come from the person's own seeing.

## The Theory Underneath

The move is built on the first kind of projection in the four-types frame: psychological projection. The unowned parts of the self — positive or negative — get cast onto other people, where they can be safely judged or admired without having to acknowledge their presence inside.

"The parts of ourselves that we can't own, the parts of ourselves that are either good or bad, but that we can't have full ownership over, we project onto other people. And this is something that happens when you're deeply triggered." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

The intensity of the trigger or the height of the pedestal is the diagnostic. See [[projections]] for the full taxonomy of which the psychological layer is one part.
