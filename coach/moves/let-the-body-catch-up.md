# Let the Body Catch Up

## The Move

A reframe has just landed at the head level. The person says some version of "I think deep down I know that," signaling that the intellectual recognition has arrived but the body has not updated. The move is to deliberately pause and walk the insight through the body, asking the body to integrate the new information at the felt level.

Joe's specific instruction:

"Yeah, let the rest of your body catch up. Something in your body knows it. Now we're just letting all the rest of the body catch up. Let it like literally seep into every cell of your body for a moment." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

The instruction has three structural parts:

1. **Acknowledge what the body already knows** ("something in your body knows it")
2. **Frame the rest as catching up** (not "make your body believe this" but "let the body that already partially knows it finish updating")
3. **Specify the integration target** ("seep into every cell")

The follow-up question that completes the move:

"What's the truth of who you are now that it's updated for a minute?"

The question forces the body to produce a felt report from its newly updated state. The report itself anchors the update.

## When Joe Uses This

The move appears at the moment of a specific kind of response: the person has just received an insight that the head has accepted but the body has not yet metabolized. The signature signals:

- "I know that" delivered without somatic shift
- "Deep down I know that" or "intellectually I know"
- A nod or "yeah" without breath change
- The person agreeing with a reframe but their body still in the old state
- The recognition that arrives as a thought before it arrives as a sensation

The move is for the specific gap between intellectual recognition and somatic integration. It does not work if the insight has not yet arrived — there is nothing to integrate. It also does not work if the integration is already happening on its own — the move would interrupt it.

The move is calibrated for the in-between moment when the recognition is present but the body is still organized around the pre-recognition state.

## What It Produces

The body produces a different self-report than it could have produced a minute earlier. In the source session, the client's report immediately before the move was a description of his dissociation history and his parents' damage. His report immediately after the move was:

"I'm a big ball of love and wonder and just wanting and learning."

This is not a different opinion. It is a different felt-state. The body, given a minute to integrate the insight that "it wasn't personal," updated its self-organization. The new self-organization produced a different report. The report is what tells the coach the integration has landed.

The other characteristic output: a felt softening or expansion in the body. The breath deepens. The chest opens. The shoulders drop. The body's signature shifts from braced to released. These shifts are the somatic correlates of the integration.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the body's new self-report is significantly different from the pre-move report: stay there. Let the new state hold. The work of the move is the production of the new state; running ahead to the next question would interrupt the consolidation.

If the new report comes with emotion (tears, laughter, deep breath): let it move. Do not narrate it. The body is finishing what the insight started.

If the body's new self-report is similar to the pre-move report: the integration hasn't landed yet. Either the insight wasn't deep enough to require integration, or the body needs more time, or there is a layer of resistance that wasn't visible. The next move is usually to ask what is in the way: "What stopped that from landing?" or "What in your body is still holding the old version?"

In the source session, after the new self-report arrived, Joe extended with a calibration check: "It feels so natural, you said, or unnatural?" The client confirmed "unnatural, but it feels good." The unnaturalness was diagnostic — the new state was unfamiliar precisely because the body had been organized around the old state for a long time. The unfamiliarity is normal and is part of the integration.

The hands-together-but-opposite micro-demonstration Joe added: "Put your hands together like this for a second and then go like that, and now do it the opposite way. No different, but one definitely feels different, right? Yeah, that's what's happening." The demonstration externalizes the structural fact that the new state is structurally identical to the old state in terms of its underlying mechanics but feels different because the body is unfamiliar with this configuration. The naming reduces the destabilization of the unfamiliarity.

## Why This Move Matters

The standard failure mode of insight-based work: the person has a realization, agrees with it, and the realization fails to produce any change because the body never updated. The person leaves the session feeling smarter but not different. Two weeks later they are in the same pattern.

This move addresses the failure mode directly. The recognition is treated as the start of the work, not its conclusion. The body, which has been holding the pre-recognition state for years or decades, needs an explicit invitation to update. The invitation is the move.

The structural principle: insight without somatic integration does not produce change. The body runs the system. The body has to update or nothing updates. The move makes the update an explicit step rather than an assumed automatic consequence.

This is the operational form of the broader principle that change happens at the body level, not the cognitive level. See [[change-is-somatic-not-cognitive]] for the principle. This move is what the principle looks like in a live session at the moment of insight.

## The "Something in Your Body Already Knows It" Framing

A specific feature of the instruction worth naming: the move does not ask the body to learn something new. It asks the body to let the part that already knows finish telling the rest.

"Something in your body knows it. Now we're just letting all the rest of the body catch up."

This framing matters. If the move were framed as "make your body believe this," the body would resist — bodies don't update by being told what to believe. By framing the move as letting the partial knowing finish, the body's autonomy is preserved. The body is the agent of its own update.

The mechanism is real. The person's "I know that deep down" was accurate. Some part of the body had already registered the truth — usually a small organ or region, often the gut or the heart. The rest of the body — the trauma-holding tissue, the chronically braced muscles, the protective tension patterns — had not. The move gives the part that knows the time and space to communicate what it knows to the rest.

## The Seep-Into-Every-Cell Phrase

The "literally seep into every cell of your body" instruction is doing specific work. It directs the integration into the somatic layer at high resolution. The body responds to the instruction by allowing the recognition to permeate rather than localizing in one area.

This is also why the instruction is delivered slowly, with pauses. A fast delivery would invite cognitive processing. A slow delivery creates the time the body needs to actually do the permeation. The instruction's timing is part of its function.

## Tone

Slow, settling, almost meditative. The move is the moment in the session when the pace deliberately drops. The contrast with surrounding work matters — preceding moves may have been fast and pointed; this move is slow and spacious.

The voice is warm but not heavy. The instruction is a permission, not a demand. The body, given permission, will do the work on its own timeline. The coach's job is to hold the space and trust the integration.

The question that closes the move ("what's the truth of who you are now that it's updated for a minute?") is asked with curiosity. It is not a test. The coach genuinely wants to know what the body's new report is. The genuineness is part of what allows the body to produce a true report rather than a performed one.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not visualization. The body is not being asked to imagine a state. It is being asked to let an integration that has already started complete itself.
- It is not affirmation. The person is not being asked to tell themselves the new truth. They are being asked to let the truth that has already arrived land in the body.
- It is not meditation. The pause is short — a minute or less. The instruction is specific. The point is integration of a particular insight, not general somatic awareness.
- It is not a finishing move. The move opens the door to what comes next, which may include more integration work, a new question, or a closing of the session.

- *(Joe has asked what the client's parents were scared of and named that "all of their activity wasn't about you, it wasn't personal.")*
- **Client:** "I think deep down I know that."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, let the rest of your body catch up. Something in your body knows it. Now we're just letting all the rest of the body catch up. Let it like literally seep into every cell of your body for a moment. What's the truth of who you are now that it's updated for a minute?"
- **Client:** "I'm a big ball of love and wonder and just wanting and learning."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Client:** "Just feels so unnatural to feel things this way."
- **Joe:** "It feels so natural, you said, or unnatural?"
- **Client:** "Unnatural, but it feels good."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, it is. Yeah, put your hands together like this for a second and then go like that, and now do it the opposite way. No different, but one definitely feels different, right? Yeah, that's what's happening."

The hands-together demonstration at the end is the move's stabilization. The unfamiliarity of the new state is normalized by demonstrating that the unfamiliarity itself does not mean anything is wrong. The state feels strange because it is unfamiliar, not because it is incorrect.
