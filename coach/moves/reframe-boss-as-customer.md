# Reframe the Boss as Your Customer

## The Move

When a coachee is operating from "I have a boss who has authority over my life," the reframe is structural rather than emotional. The factual situation is not boss-and-employee in the parental-authority sense. The factual situation is: you have a customer, you happen to have only one customer, which is an undiversified business arrangement, but the structural relationship is customer-and-provider, not parent-and-child.

The reframe is delivered plainly, not as motivational reinterpretation.

"You don't have a boss. You have a client. You have a customer. Unfortunately, you're not diversified. You don't have lots of customers. You only have one. But you have a customer. And they're not your boss. They're not your authority figure. They're somebody who's a customer, and you can lose them, or you can get another customer. Or another client, whatever you, however you wanna look at it." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Through Family Dynamics

## When to Use This

The move applies when:
- A person is treating workplace dynamics with the emotional weight of family dynamics
- The phrase "my boss" carries authority-figure charge (not just "the person I report to")
- Decisions are being made to please rather than to serve
- The person experiences themselves as having less power than they actually have
- [[childhood-imprinting]] is recognizably transferring onto the boss

This is most useful for people who have not yet seen the projection. It surfaces the projection by contrasting the imagined structure (parent-child) with the actual structure (provider-customer).

## What the Reframe Does

The reframe is not motivational. It is a structural correction.

A boss who is treated as an authority figure controls your sense of survival, your worth, your safety. A customer who is treated as a customer is someone whose business you can lose, someone you can also choose to leave, someone whose relationship to you is contractual and bounded. The structural truth is closer to the customer frame. The authority-figure frame is the import from the family system. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

"Just even the perspective that you have a boss who has some sort of control over your life is a projection of a family dynamic, typically." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Through Family Dynamics

## What It Produces

People often resist the reframe at first, because the boss-as-authority frame is the very system through which they have organized their work life. Surfacing the substitution can produce relief, anxiety, or both.

- **Relief:** the sense of being trapped under someone's authority drops. The relationship becomes negotiable.
- **Anxiety:** the structure that organized "good employee / pleasing boss" is destabilized. New questions appear: what am I actually providing? Am I providing it well? What if I am not interested in providing it?
- **Visibility of projections:** the boss-projections (need to please, fear of disappointing, rebellion, expectation of disappointment) become observable as projections rather than as facts.

The cluster of behaviors that follows boss-as-authority is the cluster to watch for:
- "I need to please them"
- "They're never happy with me"
- "Their opinion matters more than mine"
- "I need to rebel against them"
- "I keep finding ways to disappoint them" (when the original parent was disappointed)

Each of these is a projection that the reframe makes available for examination.

## Boss-Side Projections Too

The reframe is also useful for the boss. Bosses carry the corresponding family-dynamic projections in the other direction.

"The boss also has projections back, like, I'm responsible for these people. No. That the boss is not responsible for, that everybody's responsible for themselves. Or these people, I can't depend on them. I have to do it all myself. Or I can't let them down. Or nobody can do it except me. Or I'm necessary." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Through Family Dynamics

The boss-as-parent and the employee-as-child are two sides of the same projection. Either side can use the reframe.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not say "you are empowered." That is a frame for the person to discover, not a label to apply.
- He does not promise that reframing will fix the workplace situation.
- He does not minimize that having a single client is genuinely undiversified and structurally precarious. The vulnerability of single-client work is real.
- He does not soften the move. It is delivered plainly.

## Tone

Direct, matter-of-fact, almost dry. There is no charge in the delivery. The flatness of the tone is part of the move: the very ordinariness of "you have a customer" pushes against the emotional weight the person has been carrying about "my boss."

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports a workplace dynamic with disproportionate emotional charge
- When [[i-freeze-around-authority]] is the concern and the workplace target is the boss
- When [[childhood-imprinting]] has been named and the workplace is the current expression
- When a person is making career decisions out of fear of disappointing the boss

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward identifying the specific family-dynamic projection running ("how is this person like dad / like mom?")
- Toward the parallel boss-side reframe if the person is a manager
- Toward [[empowered-vs-power]]: the reframe is one expression of the empowerment shift
- Toward [[structure-and-empowerment]]: organizational design that makes the customer-relationship structurally visible (teams that report to themselves, etc.)
- Toward [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]] when the projection is recognized but the felt sense remains
