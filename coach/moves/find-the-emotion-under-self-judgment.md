# Find the Emotion the Self-Judgment Is Protecting You From

## The Move

When self-judgment is present -- the voice rehearsing what you should have said, the post-event critique of how you came across, the perfectionist commentary on your own response -- the move is not to argue with the judgment. The move is to find the emotion the judgment is protecting you from feeling.

Self-judgment is almost always doing protective work. The judgment is loud enough, busy enough, and self-directed enough to keep the person from feeling something underneath. Aiming attention at the underneath-emotion is the move that releases the judgment, not arguing with the content of the judgment.

"When you notice that you judge yourself, see what the emotion is that it's protecting you from. Typically, when we're judging ourselves or somebody else, it's because we don't want to feel an emotion that's underneath it. And so if you fully allow that feeling -- whether it's ostracization or aloneness or the perfectionism or the rigidity -- if we fully allow the emotional experience underneath the judgment, then that'll allow you to have a more free expression. It's judgment that gets in the way of our expression typically." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

## What Reads Trigger This Move

- The person is exhausted from rehearsing what they should have said
- The person reports difficulty being themselves in conversations because they keep self-monitoring
- The person describes a constant background judgment of their own responses
- The person uses words like "stiff," "rigid," "stuck" about their own communication
- The person reports getting blocked in group situations and not knowing why
- [[perfectionism]] is producing paralysis in the moment, not just after the moment
- The person is in a recursive loop about their own behavior

These reads are particularly common in the [[my-inner-critic-wont-stop]] concern when the critic is mid-action rather than post-action.

## Why This Works (Where The Move Comes From)

Judgment is the cognitive form of avoided emotion. It is the system using analysis as a way to not feel. The pattern is structurally identical to [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]] applied to oneself: the judgment of another person points at an avoided emotion; the judgment of self does the same thing turned inward.

When the judgment is about your own performance in a social or group context, the avoided emotion is often one of:

- **Ostracization fear.** The dread of being excluded or rejected by the group.
- **Aloneness.** The felt experience of being separate from the others.
- **Perfectionism.** Specifically the feeling underneath perfectionism -- the dread of not being enough.
- **Rigidity.** The feeling of being locked in -- which when felt becomes loose.

The mechanism: by being busy with judgment, the person stays in the head and out of the feeling. The feeling is what is actually difficult. The judgment is a strategy to manage the unfeelable.

## How to Make the Move

In coaching:

1. **Notice the self-judgment.** "I hear you judging yourself a lot here." Often the person has not separated the judgment from themselves; naming creates the necessary distance.

2. **Pause the argument with the content.** Do not engage the question of whether the judgment is accurate. The accuracy is not the point. The presence of the judgment is the point.

3. **Ask what is underneath.** "If you let the judgment go for a moment, what would you be feeling?" Or: "What emotion is the judgment helping you not feel?"

4. **Stay with whatever surfaces.** Sometimes the answer is immediate. Sometimes it takes a few moments. The person often does not know consciously what the avoided emotion is, but the body knows. Wait.

5. **Allow the emotion fully.** Whatever surfaces -- ostracization fear, aloneness, the felt sense of inadequacy -- the work is to let it be felt rather than discussed. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

6. **Notice what happens to the judgment.** As the emotion moves, the judgment softens. The person reports that the post-event rehearsing is less compelling, that the self-monitoring loosens, that they can be more present.

## Self-Coaching Version

The same move applies to one's own practice. When you notice yourself in a loop of self-judgment about something you said or did:

- Stop trying to figure out if the judgment is right.
- Ask: what am I trying not to feel by being busy with this judgment?
- Let yourself feel that thing.
- The judgment will lose its grip.

The discipline is to redirect attention from the content of the judgment to the emotion the judgment is helping you avoid.

## Why It Frees Expression

Joe makes the operational claim directly: "It's judgment that gets in the way of our expression typically."

The mechanism: when self-judgment is running, the person is split. One part is performing or speaking; another part is monitoring and critiquing the performance. The split makes whole-system expression impossible. The expression that comes out is filtered through the monitor and arrives bracketed, careful, second-guessed.

When the underneath-emotion is allowed, the monitor relaxes because the monitor was the strategy for not feeling the emotion. With the emotion felt, the monitor has nothing to do. The expression that comes out then is undivided -- the person is just speaking, not speaking-while-monitoring-the-speech.

This is why the move applies particularly well to fast-paced group situations where there is no time to think between turns. The fast pace exposes the cost of the split. The work of feeling the underneath-emotion ahead of time, or right when judgment surfaces, removes the split before the next turn comes.

## The Pivot From "Should I Be" to "What Am I Feeling"

A specific structural pivot the move accomplishes: the person was asking "should I have done that differently?" The move converts the question to "what am I feeling that this judgment is helping me not feel?"

The first question is unanswerable in any useful way and produces more loops. The second question is answerable, leads somewhere, and dissolves the loop when answered.

The pivot is not a refusal to consider whether something could be done differently. It is a recognition that the rehearsing-of-it is not the path to learning. The path to learning is the feeling underneath. Once the feeling moves, what to do differently next time often becomes clear without effort.

## When to Use This Move

- The person is stuck in self-judgment after an event and cannot move on
- The person is in self-judgment during an event and finds it blocking
- [[perfectionism]] is producing paralysis around expression
- The voice in the head is loudest specifically around performance (talking, presenting, being in groups)
- A recursive loop of "I shouldn't have said that" or "I should have said this" is running
- The person is trying to fix their behavior in social settings and the fixing is not working

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not an excuse to avoid responsibility. The move is to feel the avoided emotion, not to dismiss what actually happened.
- It is not a way to make the self-judgment go away through analysis. The move requires actually feeling the underneath-emotion.
- It is not a substitute for the broader work on the [[voice-in-the-head]]. It is a specific move within that broader work.

## Tone

Curious and quiet. The move is not delivered as a confrontation with the person's self-judgment. It is delivered as a wondering: "What's the feeling that this is helping you not feel?" The tone matches the gentleness required for the underneath-emotion to surface.
