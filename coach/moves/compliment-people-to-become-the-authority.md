# Compliment People on Their Work to Become the Authority in the Room

## The Move

When an executive is in a peer power struggle, or when a leader is trying to find their footing in a room where the authority is contested, the move is to compliment people on their work.

The mechanism is structural. In any room, the person doing the complimenting is operating from a position the rest of the room is not. Most people in the room are wondering whether they are being seen, whether they are doing well, whether they will be approved of. The person who is acknowledging others is by definition not in that position. The room registers the asymmetry. The complimenter becomes the de facto authority.

Joe gives the move directly to executives in his coaching:

"It's interesting, like when I'm working with executives who are like, 'oh, how do I — like when the power struggle structures off between them and a peer' — it's like one of the things I'll say to them is, hey, just compliment people on their work. It makes you the authority in the room... It's crazy." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## Why It Works

The structural insight underneath: in most rooms, no one is complimenting the authority figure. The authority is the one in the room least likely to receive recognition for their work. Everyone is hoping the authority will give them recognition; no one is offering it upward. Conversely, the person who is offering recognition is functioning, in that moment, in the seat that the authority normally occupies.

"It's possible to walk into a meeting and somebody is like, 'hey, that's great work. That's fantastic. You did a great job there.' That's the authority figure in the room because no one's really doing that for the authority figure." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The move works because it inverts the gravitational pattern of the room. The authority orientation of every other person is upward, looking for approval. The complimenter is the only person whose orientation is outward, giving recognition. The room reorganizes around them.

## What This Is Not

This is not flattery. Flattery is approval-seeking dressed up as approval-giving — the complimenter is angling for something. The room reads this and the move fails.

This is also not a manipulation tactic. If the compliment is not actual recognition of actual good work, it has no power. The compliment has to be authentic — see [[empowered-vs-power]] for why empowerment requires actually being you, not performing a role.

This is also not a substitute for being the authority structurally. If you are the leader of an organization, you also have to provide structure, principles, and direction — see [[structure-and-empowerment]] and [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]]. The compliment move addresses the affective authority in the room; the structural authority of the role is a separate matter.

## When To Use It

- An executive in a peer-power-struggle with another executive
- A new leader trying to establish presence in a room of established players
- A consultant or coach walking into a client organization where they have no positional authority
- A facilitator beginning to hold a room of strangers
- Any context where the formal authority is unclear and the affective authority is contested

The move is especially powerful in rooms full of high-status people who are unconsciously waiting to be seen. The higher the status, the more starved the person tends to be for unconditional recognition (because nearly all the recognition they receive is conditional on their role).

## When Not To Use It

- When the compliment cannot be authentic. The fake version fails worse than no move at all.
- When the room reads the compliment as a status-claim. In some cultures and some groups, the complimenter is read as condescending — "who is this person to be giving me a compliment?" The move requires reading the room.
- When the authority is genuinely contested over substantive matters and the compliment would paper over the actual issue. The work has to be done in the right place.

## The Inversion: The Authority Who Compliments Sees More

A second-order benefit of the move: the executive who learns to compliment authentically also learns to see more. The capacity to acknowledge real work in others sharpens the capacity to see it in the first place. This compounds the authority of the complimenter — they are not only the person giving recognition, they are the person whose recognition is informed by actually seeing the work.

This connects to Joe's broader observation that the authority figure, in normal conditions, is the person least likely to be seen as a human. The compliment move, when done well, is the same move applied in the other direction: the complimenter is the person who is actually seeing the human in front of them. That capacity is itself the substrate of authority.

## What Tends to Happen

When the move lands cleanly:

- The recipient of the compliment visibly registers it. Often there is a slight surprise.
- The room's affective center shifts toward the complimenter without anyone naming the shift.
- The contested power dynamic dissolves, not by being won, but by being made obsolete — the complimenter is operating on a different axis.
- The recipient often returns the favor in some form. The complimenter has installed a reciprocity dynamic that the contested dynamic was preventing.

When the move does not land:

- The compliment is read as inauthentic. The room registers the calculation. The complimenter loses ground.
- The recipient deflects the compliment. The deflection is data — they are running approval-seeking and cannot let recognition land. See [[letting-it-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]] for the deflection pattern; the move can be paired with naming the deflection if the relationship allows.
- The compliment is heard as a status claim. The move fails when read as condescension.

## Tone

Direct. Specific. Brief. The compliment that works is the one that names something specific the person actually did. "Great work on the rollout" is fine; "the way you handled the cross-team coordination on the rollout was really good" is better. The specificity is the proof of authenticity.

The delivery is matter-of-fact. Not effusive. Not performed. The way you would name any other true thing you noticed. The unforced quality is what distinguishes it from flattery.
