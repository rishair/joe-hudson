# Name 'I'm In My Trauma Right Now' Out Loud to Your Partner

## The Move

Mid-fight, when the trauma is firing, the speaker names it directly to the partner: "I'm in my trauma right now. I can't be trusted in this fight right now."

The naming is short, undefended, and contains no demand on the partner. It is information. It says: what is happening between us is being driven by something older than us. Hold this loosely.

"I've said that dozens of times to Tara. She said it to me, 'Oh, I'm in my trauma right now.' And then the other one can say, 'Oh, right. This isn't you. This isn't actually...' How do I hold you? How do I love you? How do I be with you and your trauma so it doesn't retraumatize and you can actually see what's really going on?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This is the couple version of [[i-cant-trust-my-thoughts-right-now]]. The individual recognition is "my thoughts are not reliable right now." The couple version routes that recognition through the partner: I am telling you, and what I am telling you is that this is trauma, not us.

## When Joe Reaches For It

This move requires both partners to have an operative understanding of trauma firing. It is, in Joe's words, "an advanced move." It comes online after both people have spent time recognizing the body signature of their own trauma activation and have learned to trust the partner to receive the naming without using it against them.

The move shows up in the middle of an escalating fight, at the moment one of the partners notices the body signature of being triggered. The naming interrupts the trajectory of the fight. The fight does not have to end; the framing of the fight changes.

## What This Is Not

This is not an exit phrase. It does not mean "we are done talking." The partner who names their trauma does not get to disappear from the conversation; they have just renamed what is happening so it can be met differently.

This is not an excuse. "I'm in my trauma" is not "therefore I get to do anything." The partner who has used the naming is still responsible for what they do next. The naming is data, not absolution.

This is not avoidance. Naming the trauma and then immediately leaving the conversation is sometimes appropriate but is its own move (see [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for how to do that cleanly). The base form of this move is naming-and-staying.

"It doesn't have to be, oh my God, I'm starting to go into my trauma. I have to run away from this fight. That can be quite avoidant." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

## What the Other Partner Does With It

The receiving partner has a specific response available. The response converts the fight from "two people attacking each other" into "one person holding another while the trauma surfaces."

The receiver internally registers: "Oh, right. This isn't you. This isn't actually about us right now." The body re-classifies the partner from "opponent" to "person whose old wound is firing right now."

The receiver then offers something close to: "How do I hold you? How do I love you? How do I be with you and your trauma so it doesn't retraumatize and you can actually see what's really going on?"

The response is not in a specific script. The structure is the redirection of attention: from defending against the partner's words to making contact with the person underneath the words. The receiver becomes, in that moment, the loving witness that the partner could not access when the trauma was originally formed. This is the mechanism by which the fight becomes healing rather than retraumatizing. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] and [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]].

## The Reciprocity

The move requires both partners to be willing to use it about themselves. If only one partner names their trauma, the dynamic becomes "I am the one with the trauma; you are the one without." This is false (see [[your-trauma-is-equally-matched-with-your-partner]]) and over time generates resentment.

Joe describes the move as something Tara and he have done with each other "dozens of times" -- in both directions. The reciprocity is part of what makes it functional.

## The Avoidance Trap

The most common failure mode of this move is to weaponize it as avoidance. Once "I'm in my trauma" is in the relational vocabulary, it can become the universal exit from any difficult conversation. The moment the difficulty arrives, the trauma claim arrives, and the conversation never has to happen.

"You pointed to the possible avoidant way of saying, 'I'm in my trauma right now.' And, you know, this is, this can be a common pattern of when there's a difficult conversation to be had, I am just gonna state that I'm in my trauma and avoid it." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

The move is not avoiding the conversation. The move is reframing what the conversation is about. After the naming, both people are in a different relationship to the content. The content still gets met, but with the partner's love showing up for the trauma firing, not as combat.

If the naming consistently leads to the conversation ending and the topic not getting revisited, the move has been weaponized. If the naming leads to a different quality of contact and the topic returns when both are out of the trauma, the move is doing its work.

## The Fight Ends When Both Partners Hold the Frame

The cleanest outcome of this move, once both partners can hold the frame reliably, is that the fight stops needing to continue. The content of the fight loses its hold the moment both parties have shifted into the trauma-and-loving-witness configuration. The original purpose of the fight — to surface the unfelt emotion — is now being served by something other than continued conflict.

"That's an amazing thing. Like, where Tara and I got to a point in our fights where it was like, oh, I recognize I'm not here right now. I'm in my trauma. I need to feel my stuff. Amazing thing happened. The fight just ended. The fight was like, oh, this is why we're having the fight. The fight is for you to heal this trauma. Great, you're in it, you see it, I'm right here for you. And she would just come to me and just be with me while I felt it. And I would just come to her and I'd just be with her while she felt it. And it was just this, every one of our fights became a way to relieve the trauma that put us in the place to have the fight for the first time." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

The shape: naming the trauma exposes what the fight was for. Once exposed, the partner can come over, the trauma-firing partner can let themselves be held, the emotion can be felt with the loving witness present, and the loop completes. The fight does not need to be argued to a conclusion because the actual work of the fight (the emotion processing) is now in motion.

This is the late-stage version of the move. It requires both partners to have internalized [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]] not as concept but as lived experience. After it has happened a few times, the move starts to be reached for earlier, because both partners know what it produces.

## The Regulation Step That Precedes Naming

The naming move requires enough nervous system regulation to be able to speak. In the deepest activation, the speaking apparatus is partially offline. The move is to do the regulation work first, then name.

The sequence:

1. Notice the activation. The puppet feeling, the disproportionate reaction, the loss of presence.
2. Regulate the nervous system enough to come back into yourself. Use [[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]], [[hands-on-body-to-regulate-nervous-system]], or a brief pause. The regulation does not need to fully resolve the activation — only to bring enough of yourself back online that you can speak.
3. Name what is happening to the partner.
4. Allow the trauma to be met with the partner's loving witness.

"There's a whole bunch of very cool tricks on keeping your nervous system calm... The easiest one is, uh, just breathe. You can do box breath, you can look that up... You can put your hands on your body, feel your body, that can completely calm your nervous system. But the idea is when that's happening, that's your opportunity. The opportunity is calm your nervous system so you can be back in yourself. And then feel the thing that you didn't get to feel. And hopefully you've gone far enough with your partner where you can have that kind of agreement, like, oh, I'm in my trauma, I need to feel some shit." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

The regulation is the on-ramp. The naming is the move. The being-felt-while-witnessed is the work. Skipping the regulation step often means the naming comes out wrong — too aggressive, too pleading, too dissociated — and the partner cannot receive it as information. The brief regulation step gives the naming a clean delivery.

## How This Becomes Available

This is a late-stage move in the [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]]. It requires several prerequisites:

1. Both partners can recognize the body signature of being in their trauma. See [[i-cant-trust-my-thoughts-right-now]].
2. Both partners trust the other not to weaponize the naming as ammunition later.
3. Both partners have practiced staying close while the other is in trauma, rather than disengaging.
4. The relational vocabulary has been built up enough that "trauma" is a shared frame rather than an accusation.

A couple that has not built these prerequisites cannot use this move cleanly. Attempting it without the prerequisites usually produces one of two failures: the naming gets dismissed ("don't psychobabble me right now"), or the naming gets received but converted into a permanent label for the namer ("you're always in your trauma").

The move is built up over time, in small doses, in fights that are short enough to safely practice in.

## Dialogue Example

The structure as it tends to land cleanly:

- **Partner A:** [escalating, content-focused complaint that is bigger than the situation warrants]
- **Partner A:** [interrupts self] "Wait. I'm in my trauma right now. I can't trust what I'm saying right now."
- **Partner B:** [shift] "Okay. Thank you for naming that. What's happening in your body?"
- **Partner A:** [slows down, describes the sensation]
- **Partner B:** "I'm here. This isn't us right now. Tell me what's actually firing."

The move is not a script. The shape is consistent: name, slow, contact the body, partner receives the trauma rather than the content of the fight.

## Tone

The naming itself is delivered plainly, almost matter-of-fact. Not panicked. Not apologetic. Not as a power move. The plainness is what makes it land as information rather than as deflection.

The receiving partner's response is warm but not saccharine. It is the warmth of recognition: I see what's happening, I am with you, and we are going to be okay.
