# Compare Resisting vs. Not Resisting the Feeling

## The Move

The person is in contact with a difficult feeling (heartbreak, sadness, fear, anger). Instead of telling them what allowing feels like, have them run the experiment live. Have them feel the feeling, then deliberately resist it, then deliberately not resist it. Ask them to report the difference.

The teaching is the contrast. The person learns by feeling the change in their own body, not by being told.

"You feel the heartbreak. If you don't resist it, how does it change? You're gonna feel the heartbreak, then you're going to resist it, and then you're going to not resist it. And tell me how it changes."

Variations on the not-resist instruction:
- "Now allow it."
- "Embrace it."
- "Love it like a little kid."
- "Invite it."
- "Welcome it."

## When To Use It

The person has surfaced a difficult feeling and is reporting it as uncomfortable. They have not yet had a felt experience of the difference between resisting the feeling and allowing it. They are likely treating the discomfort of resistance as if it were the discomfort of the feeling itself.

Use the move when:
- The person says a feeling is "uncomfortable" or "I can't handle it."
- The person is conflating resistance to the feeling with the feeling itself.
- You need to install a felt understanding of the resist/allow distinction, not a conceptual one.
- The person has the capacity to stay in the body for the duration of the experiment.

Do not use the move when:
- The person is dysregulated and cannot track changes in their body.
- The feeling is overwhelming and resistance is currently the only thing keeping them functional.
- The person is dissociated and the experiment would be performed mentally rather than felt.

## What It Produces

The person usually reports that the resisted feeling is heavy, constricted, suffocating, or stuck. The not-resisted feeling is described as moving, lighter, releasing, or even pleasurable in a strange way. The contrast is immediate and visceral.

Common reports:
- "When I resist it, it feels like a weight. When I let it go, I feel like I can breathe again."
- "Resisting makes it sharper. Not resisting makes it move."
- "It's still uncomfortable, but it's different."
- "I feel more courage" (when allowing).

The experiment teaches what no amount of explanation can: that the suffering associated with feelings is mostly the suffering of resisting them, not the suffering of the feelings themselves.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** (after feeling fear) "It's a very uncomfortable feeling."
- **Joe:** "Oh wait, just to make sure, is the heartbreak uncomfortable, or is your resistance to the heartbreak uncomfortable? If you don't resist the heartbreak — okay, so you feel the heartbreak. If you don't resist it, how does it change? And then so we're gonna — you're gonna feel the heartbreak, then you're going to resist it, and then you're going to not resist it. And tell me how it changes."
- **Joe:** "Now resist that. Now allow it. Embrace it. Love it like a little kid. What's the difference?"
- **Person:** "I feel a little bit more courage."
- **Joe:** "Just as far as comfort — you said it's uncomfortable. Does the heartbreak become more comfortable when you resist it? When you don't resist it?"
- **Person:** "I feel a little something here, the weight that I feel when I'm afraid. When I let it go, when I let that feeling pass by, it feels better, feels like once that passes, I can breathe again."

Notice the move begins with the distinction-question — is the discomfort the feeling or the resistance to the feeling? Most people have never asked this. The question itself is part of the teaching. Then the experiment is run. Then Joe explicitly compares the two: more comfortable resisting or not resisting? The comparison forces the person to articulate what they just felt.

## What Joe Does With the Response

When the person reports the difference, Joe usually anchors it. He may name what was learned ("my experience when you embrace heartbreak all the way is that it increases your capacity to love") and let the felt experience confirm the teaching.

Joe does not push the person to allow harder or longer. The experiment is the teaching. Future allowing will be motivated by the recognition of what allowing feels like compared to resisting.

The move often leads into:
- A teaching about the feeling itself ("heartbreak is breaking open, not breaking apart" — see [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]])
- A deeper inquiry into what created the resistance in the first place
- A direct invitation to stay with the not-resisting state

## Tone

Curious, experimental, light. The energy is "let's run a small experiment and see what happens." Not "you should not resist your feelings." The instruction-tone of "should" would itself trigger more resistance.

The phrasing matters. "Now resist that. Now allow it. Embrace it. Love it like a little kid" — the cadence is playful, almost coaching a child through a game. The lightness is what allows the person to actually try it. A heavy instruction would be performed by the mind rather than felt by the body.

## Why This Works

People often live their entire lives without distinguishing between a feeling and their resistance to a feeling. They have one experience: "this is uncomfortable." They assume the discomfort is the feeling. So they keep resisting, which keeps the discomfort, which confirms the assumption.

The experiment breaks the conflation. By alternating between resisting and not resisting the same feeling, the person directly experiences that the two have different signatures in the body. The resistance is heavy, sticky, suffocating. The feeling itself, when allowed, is often moving, sometimes pleasurable, almost always lighter than the resistance.

Once a person has felt this distinction, they cannot un-feel it. The conceptual knowledge that "resistance creates suffering" was already available to most people who have read any spiritual literature. The felt knowledge — installed by the live experiment — is what makes the next allowing possible.

## The Setup Question

The setup for the experiment is the distinction-question Joe asks before running the experiment:

"Is the heartbreak uncomfortable, or is your resistance to the heartbreak uncomfortable?"

This question, asked alone, often produces a pause. The person realizes they have not thought about this. The pause is the opening. The experiment fills the opening with a felt answer.

In other framings:
- "Is the sadness hard, or is the not-wanting-to-feel-sad hard?"
- "Is the anger heavy, or is the holding-back-the-anger heavy?"
- "Is the fear unbearable, or is the trying-not-to-feel-it unbearable?"

The question itself is part of the move.
