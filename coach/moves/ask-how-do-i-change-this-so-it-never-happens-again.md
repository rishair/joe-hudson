# Ask: How Do I Change This So It Never Happens Again?

## The Move

When you notice frustration inside the organization -- a sloppy email, a missed handoff, a vague meeting, an incompetent action -- the move is to refuse the smaller question ("how do I fix this one?") and ask the larger one: "how do I change this so it never happens again in the company?"

The question is a deliberate redirect of attention. The frustration is real and the instance is in front of you. The smaller question would have you solve the instance. The larger question has you redesign the conditions so the same instance does not arise again. The frustration becomes the input to structural design rather than the input to direct intervention.

"So I was reading these emails and I noticed the following behavior, which was, we could kind of do this or this, I don't know. And somebody else going, oh, that's a cool idea, I guess... Anytime that I'm frustrated inside the company, I don't say, how do I change this? I say, how do I change this so it never happens again in the company? You know, if I see incompetency somewhere, I don't say, how do I help that person not be incompetent? I say, how do I make it so that incompetency doesn't happen inside the company generally?" -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

## When to Use It

The move is appropriate whenever:

- The frustration is about a category of behavior that has occurred before and will plausibly occur again (sloppy writing, vague decisions, late responses, defensive reactions, gossip, missed handoffs)
- The structural lever is available to you (you can change how things are done, not just intervene on one instance)
- The pattern is more important to address than the specific person

The move is not appropriate when:

- The frustration is about something genuinely one-off (an unusual external event, a single uncharacteristic moment)
- The right intervention is genuinely personal (a private conversation with a specific person about a specific dynamic)
- The structural lever would punish many for the failure of few

## How It Works in Practice

The move runs in three steps.

**Step 1: Notice the frustration.** This is the entry condition. Without frustration, the question does not get asked. Frustration is the signal that something in the system is not working.

**Step 2: Refuse the small question.** The reflex is "how do I deal with this email?" or "how do I get this person to stop?" The move is to set that question aside. It is not wrong, but it is not where the leverage is.

**Step 3: Ask the structural question.** "How do I change the conditions so this category of thing does not recur?" The answer might be a rule (every email ends with action needed), a norm (we name vagueness in the moment), a process (decisions get documented in a specific way), or a role design (the responsibility for X is owned explicitly by Y).

## What This Produces

The faffing rule. The action-needed-at-the-end-of-every-email convention. The "we don't do defensiveness here" norm. The making-the-team-report-to-itself structural design. Each of these came from a leader getting frustrated about something and asking the larger question.

When the leader keeps asking this question over time, the organization accumulates a library of structural answers that have removed entire categories of frustration. The leader's daily experience changes: less frequent encountering of the same problems, more frequent encountering of new ones. The new problems are the new questions to ask.

## Why This Connects to Leader Self-Awareness

Not every frustration leads to a structural change. Some frustrations reveal not a missing structure but a leader's pattern being imposed as culture. The leader who is frustrated that nobody does it the way the leader does has to ask first: is this a missing structure or am I trying to make my preference the rule? See [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]].

The structural-change question is most powerful when it is not "how do I make everyone act the way I want" but "how do I design this so the thing I want -- which the team also wants -- gets produced reliably." The first version is the leader's neurosis at scale. The second is genuine design.

## What This Produces in the Leader

The discipline of asking this question converts the leader's experience of frustration. Frustration stops being a signal of "things going wrong" and starts being a signal of "design opportunity available." The emotional charge of the frustration drops because the question gives the leader something productive to do with it.

Leaders who never ask this question often experience their own frustration as evidence of organizational incompetence. Leaders who do ask it experience their own frustration as data about what to design next. The difference compounds over years.

## What This Move Is Not

**Not avoidance of direct conversation.** Sometimes the right move is to talk to the specific person directly. The structural question does not replace that. It adds a follow-up: after the direct conversation, what could change so this conversation doesn't have to happen again?

**Not perfectionism.** The structural change does not have to eliminate the category entirely. The faffing rule reduced faffing by 90%, not 100%. The point is the leverage ratio, not the zero.

**Not control.** The structural change should empower people, not constrain them. See [[structure-and-empowerment]]. A structural answer that produces compliance through fear is the wrong answer. The right structural answer makes it easier to do the work well, not harder to do anything.

## What Joe Does With the Question

Joe uses this question as his standard operating procedure inside his own company. The frustration arises; the question is asked; the structural change is designed. He has been doing this long enough that the structural answers in his company are accumulated -- the action-needed rule, the anyone-can-assign-anyone practice, the design that any junior person can tell him what to do and when. Each of these started as a frustration that got converted into a structure.

The move is offered to other leaders not as a slogan but as a daily practice. The slogan ("structural change beats direct intervention") is easy to agree with. The daily practice (every frustration becomes a design question) is what produces the compounding effect.

## Tone

Joe describes this move plainly, the way someone describes a habit they have built. There is no exhortation, no inspiration. The question itself is undramatic. The drama is in what it produces over time.
