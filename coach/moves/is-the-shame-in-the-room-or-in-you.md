# Is the Shame in the Room, or Is It in You?

## The Move

When a client has acknowledged that shame is blocking them from doing visible work (marketing, asking, promoting, speaking up, taking the position), test whether the shame is actually relational or whether it lives only inside the client. Have them look at you and check: "Do you see that I'm ashamed of you?" Then extend to the broader audience: "If you look at all the people, do you see that they're ashamed of you?"

The form is a present-tense empirical check. The client has been operating as if shame were waiting in the room, in the audience, in the customers, in the social field. The check asks them to actually look and see if it is there.

## When Joe Uses It

- After [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] and [[binary-that-forces-the-avoidance-into-view]] have surfaced shame as the underlying emotion driving the avoidance.
- When the client believes that if they are visible, they will be shamed by others. The belief is usually unexamined — they have not tested it; they have assumed it.
- When the work needs to relocate the shame from "out there" to "in here." The shame is internal; the visibility is just the trigger.
- When a quick somatic check would short-circuit a long intellectual argument about whether the client should care what people think.

## What It Produces

**Recognition that the imagined audience is doing the work the actual audience isn't.** The client looks at Joe, looks at the people present, and notices that nobody is actually ashamed of them. The shame they expected to find in the room is not there. It is in their own body. They have been performing for a phantom audience the whole time.

**An immediate softening.** Once the shame is located internally rather than relationally, the avoidance loses some of its grip. The client has been protecting themselves from a threat that is not present. The protection can soften.

**Often a recognition of the self-judging voice.** The shame in the body, when surfaced, usually reveals an internal critic that has been broadcasting the shame from inside. See [[voice-in-the-head]]. The work then opens to that voice rather than to the imagined audience.

**A clean handoff to the next move.** With the shame located internally, the work can move to whatever the client needs to do with their own internal critic — apply VIEW to it, find the want underneath, take the contrary action.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Establish that shame is the operative emotion.** Through [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] and [[binary-that-forces-the-avoidance-into-view]].
2. **Make the empirical check at the immediate level.** "Look at me. Do you see that I'm ashamed of you?" The client looks. They almost always say no.
3. **Extend to the broader field.** "If you look at all the people, do you see that they're ashamed of you?" Same answer.
4. **Do not lecture about the implication.** Joe usually does not say "so the shame is in you." The client makes the connection themselves. The empirical check does the work.
5. **Move to the next layer.** Often [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] (what do you want to be known for?), or [[skill-transfer-redirect]] (you already know how to do this), or the internal critic work.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "Oh, Yeah, even now the shame is there. If you look at me, do you, do you see that I'm ashamed of you?"
- **Guest:** "No."
- **Joe:** "If you look at all the people, do you see that they're ashamed of you for [this]?"
- *(Source: How Do I Make Money Doing What I Love?)*

After the previous moves had surfaced that the client was making shame more important than money (and that this traced back to the dad's avoidance of shame), Joe notices the shame is still present in the client's body. He tests it against the room. The client confirms no one is ashamed of them. The shame is internal — and now it is visible as internal.

## Why It Works

The shame the client carries is built on a projection. The client has assumed the audience will shame them. The assumption is so persistent it operates as a fact. The empirical check breaks the fact by introducing actual data — the actual audience, in this actual room, is not ashamed of them.

The move also works because it is embodied. Joe does not ask the client to think about whether the shame is in the room. He asks them to look. The looking is somatic. The data the client gets is somatic. The recognition is somatic.

The two-stage form (Joe first, then the broader audience) is important. Looking at one person and not seeing shame is one data point. Looking at multiple people and not seeing shame multiplies the data point. By the second stage, the client cannot easily dismiss what they're not seeing as "well, Joe wouldn't be ashamed of me anyway."

## Tone

Direct, even, slightly curious. Joe is not making a rhetorical point. He is genuinely asking the client to look and report what they see. The tone has to be that of an empirical question — "what do you see?" — not a rhetorical question — "do you think they're really ashamed?"

The tone also has to permit the client to find that the shame IS in the room if it actually is. If the client looks at Joe and says "yes, I see you're ashamed of me," that's data — about the client's projection, or about something Joe is unconsciously doing, or about something else. The move is genuinely an inquiry, not a setup.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] has surfaced shame as the operative avoidance-driving emotion
- When [[binary-that-forces-the-avoidance-into-view]] has named avoidance due to shame
- When the client is visibly carrying shame in their body (slumped, looking down, avoiding eye contact, hedging)
- When the client is operating from an assumed-but-unexamined relational shame ("they'll think I'm pushy," "they'll think I'm a fraud," "they'll think I'm not good enough")
- When the work needs to relocate the shame from the imagined external source to the internal critic

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[skill-transfer-redirect]] — once the shame is internal and the avoidance is visible, name the skill the client already has
- Toward [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] — what they want to be known for / what they want to ask for / what they want to do
- Toward [[apply-view-to-the-voice]] — work on the internal critic that broadcasts the shame from inside
- Toward [[mirror-to-group-then-substitute-i-for-you]] — when there is a group present and the shame needs externalizing-then-internalizing
- Toward [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] — refuse the moral frame on whatever the client is supposedly ashamed of
- Toward [[chase-it-around-the-room]] — when the internal critic is the obstacle and needs meeting with love

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not deploy without the client having acknowledged shame as the issue.** If the client has not yet seen that shame is operating, asking them to test for it in the room is premature and confusing.
- **Do not script the answer.** The check has to be a real inquiry. If the client is reading the move as a setup, they will give the expected answer without the somatic recognition that makes it land.
- **Do not lecture after the answer.** "So the shame is in you" is the recognition the client should make themselves. Saying it for them robs the move of its weight.
- **Do not deploy when the room actually does carry the shame.** If the client is in a hostile audience, a judging family, a culture that genuinely shames the work they are doing — the empirical check will confirm the shame and the move will not help. Use only when the shame is genuinely projected.

## Variations

The same structural move runs in many forms:

- **For visibility shame:** "Do you see that I'm ashamed of you?" (the version above)
- **For fear of judgment:** "Do you see anyone in this room judging you for that?"
- **For imposter syndrome in a group:** "Look around the room. Do you see anyone here who thinks you don't belong?"
- **For shame about a specific trait:** "If I look at you and see that trait right now, what do you see in my face?"
- **For projected disgust:** "If you watch my face while you tell me, do you see disgust?"

In each case, the move asks the client to substitute actual observation for assumed projection. The relief that follows is the same relief: the threat the client has been protecting themselves from is not present in the room.
