# Senior People Speak Last, Junior People First

## The Move

In any meeting where a decision is being made or where intelligence needs to be mined from the room, the senior person speaks last. When a decision is up, the most junior person speaks first. The order is deliberate; it is not just a courtesy.

"If they're senior, they're the last person to speak in a meeting. If a decision gets made, the most junior person starts. Because they know their opinion is going to influence other folks. They're just really good listeners because they know that there's intelligence everywhere." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## What Reads Trigger It

- A meeting where the senior person speaks first and the rest of the room then nods or refines the senior person's framing.
- A pattern over time where the senior person notices that "everyone agreed" with their proposals and the agreement is suspiciously uniform.
- A team where the leader has noticed they keep being "the smart one" — and is starting to wonder why no one else is.
- A meeting where the same one or two voices speak and the rest are silent.
- A team where decisions are made and then later quietly undermined or unenthusiastically executed, suggesting the buy-in was not real.
- A leader who is asking "where is all the talent? Why doesn't anyone bring me ideas?" while reliably opening every meeting with their own.

## Why It Works

A senior voice in a room exerts gravity on everyone else's voice. Once the senior person has said what they think, the junior people in the room have two choices: agree, or contradict the boss. Both options narrow the conversation. Most people choose agreement and shape their contribution to fit the senior framing. The intelligence in the room is filtered through the first voice before it ever gets spoken.

Reversing the order changes the physics. When the most junior person speaks first, their contribution is uncontaminated by what the senior person thinks. When the next person speaks, they have heard the junior view and the senior view is still unknown. By the time the senior person speaks last, the room has already laid out its full thinking. The senior person now has actual material to work with rather than a hall of mirrors.

The deeper mechanism: this move is an applied form of [[listening]]. The senior person is treating the rest of the room as if they have intelligence worth hearing. They do, but only if the conditions allow it to surface. The speaking order creates those conditions.

## What It Produces

- Information the senior person did not have. The room knows things the senior person does not; that knowledge only surfaces when it is not pre-filtered.
- Buy-in that is real. When people have actually spoken, they own the decision. When they have only nodded, they do not.
- Junior voices that develop. People who get to speak first in real decisions grow faster than people who only get to react.
- A senior voice that gets sharper, not duller. The senior person who has heard the full range of the room has more to say, not less. Their voice — when it does come — carries more weight because it is built on what was just laid out.
- Less politics. Where wants and views can be expressed in the meeting, they do not need to be expressed in the hallway afterward. See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]].

## What It Is Not

The move is not silence from the senior person. The senior person speaks; they just speak last. Their voice is still present in the decision. The contribution is sequenced differently.

The move is not voting by committee. The decision authority still rests where it rests; the senior person may still make the call. What changes is what the call is informed by. See [[aristotle-project]] for the related principle that input is welcome from many while decision authority stays narrow.

The move is not always-appropriate. Some situations need the senior person's framing first — emergencies, situations where the senior person has unique information, situations where alignment on a constraint must be set before discussion can be useful. The move applies wherever the goal is to mine the room's intelligence. It does not apply wherever the goal is to set a direction the room then refines.

## How to Introduce It

If you are the senior person and want to install this:
- Name it explicitly the first time. "I want to hear from each of you first. I'll go last."
- Resist the temptation to react during the round. Just listen. Note what you want to come back to, but let each voice complete.
- When your turn comes, speak from what you have just heard, not from the position you walked in with. The walked-in position was prep; the new position is response.
- Do not preface your remarks with "I agree with X" or "I disagree with Y" — that signals approval and disapproval and re-introduces the gravity you were trying to remove. Speak to the substance.

If you are a junior person in a room where this is not the practice, you can still apply a version: speak earlier rather than later when you have a view; do not wait to hear which way the senior voice is leaning before forming your own.

## Why Joe Notices This About the Best Leaders

The leaders Joe describes who have both success and a certain peace are all good listeners. The speaking-order move is one of the visible behaviors of those leaders. It is not a technique they apply; it is a consequence of having genuinely internalized that there is intelligence everywhere and that their job is to surface it rather than to supply it.

A leader who has not internalized this will use the move as a technique and the technique will not work. The room will sense the performance of listening rather than the listening itself. See the warning against treating receiving as a manipulation tactic in [[listen-as-the-wisest-person]].

## Tone

Practical, almost procedural in the delivery. Joe describes this as something he has noticed about leaders he respects. The undertone is mild surprise that more leaders do not do it. There is no moralizing — the move is not "good leadership," it is just leverage that more leaders could be applying and are not.
