# The Forgive-This-Or-Stop-Blaming-Everything Choice

## The Move

When a person presents a specific event they want to forgive themselves for, and the chronicity of the self-blame is operating underneath, offer them an explicit binary: forgive this one thing, or stop blaming yourself for everything. Make them pick.

The form Joe uses:

"If I could give you a choice at the end of this call, you could realize how to forgive yourself for fucking up this relationship, or you could realize how to stop blaming yourself for everything. What would you choose?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?

The choice is offered as a real choice, not a rhetorical setup. Whichever option the person picks shapes the next part of the session.

## When Joe Uses This

- After the chronicity of the self-blame has been confirmed — the person has agreed they blame themselves "a lot," not just for this event.
- After [[how-is-it-not-your-fault-that-its-always-your-fault]] has been asked and produced the "I don't know" wedge.
- When the presenting concern is [[i-cant-forgive-myself-for-hurting-them]] and the session needs to be oriented toward the larger structure rather than the specific event.
- When the person is about to spend the session relitigating one specific event when the structural work is the always-my-fault pattern.
- When the person is at risk of getting closure on this event by self-forgiveness without addressing the pattern that will produce the next event.

## What Each Choice Reveals

The choice the person makes is diagnostic.

**If they choose "forgive myself for this":** They are still working at the level of the event. The session can address the specific event, but with the awareness that the pattern is going to produce the next one. The work after the forgiveness will need to point at the pattern. If the choice is delivered with relief — "yes, just help me forgive this" — the conscious mind is still on the event and the body is using the event as cover for the larger pattern.

**If they choose "stop blaming myself for everything":** They are ready for the structural work. The session goes directly to the pattern. The specific event becomes one instance of many, not the central case.

**If they cannot choose:** This is often the real diagnostic. The inability to choose means both feel out of reach, or one feels disloyal to the other. Sit with the inability. The not-being-able-to-choose is the data.

In the canonical session, the client chose "forgive myself." Joe took the choice and then immediately moved to the structural question anyway — because the choice is also a setup. Whichever option the person picks, the next move surfaces the pattern.

## What Joe Does After

After "forgive myself for this," Joe pivots to the payoff question: "What makes it important to you to make sure that you keep blaming yourself for shit?" The pivot keeps the chosen orientation (forgiveness for this) but moves to the structural question (what does the blame do for you). The two are linked. The forgiveness this person is asking for cannot land while the structural blame is being used as a tether — see [[self-blame-as-tether-to-the-lost]].

The sequence: chronicity check → inversion question → forced choice → payoff question. Each step builds on the previous one. The forced choice is the orienting move: it locates the person's conscious wish so the rest of the session can serve it while also opening the structural work.

## Why It Works

The forced choice does three things at once:

1. **It makes the larger pattern visible.** The person has been treating the specific event as the problem. Offering the choice between "fix this event" and "fix the pattern" surfaces the pattern as a separate object. Even if they choose the event, they have now registered that the pattern is also a thing.

2. **It surfaces the actual wish.** Most people, given the choice, want both. The realization that they have to pick one reveals the deeper preference. People who deeply want to be free of the self-blame in general will hesitate before choosing the specific. People who really want to atone for this one thing will pick it immediately. The hesitation or speed is data.

3. **It exposes the trade.** The choice implies that the two are different objects. Forgiving this one thing is one move; learning to stop blaming yourself for everything is another. The implication that these are different moves prevents the person from collapsing them and assuming that handling the specific event will solve the pattern.

The move is also a small intervention against the [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]] mid-stage. People stuck in stage two often present as wanting to forgive themselves for the specific thing. The forced choice exposes that the work is actually at the pattern level (stage three).

## What Not To Do

- **Do not offer the choice when the chronicity has not been established.** If the person is in acute genuine remorse about a single thing they really did, the forced choice is the wrong move. They are not in a self-blame pattern — they are in proportionate response to a specific event. Use [[upright-apology]] or [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]] instead.
- **Do not impose the "stop blaming yourself for everything" answer.** The choice has to be a real choice. The coach who forces the structural answer when the person wants the specific is overriding the body's pacing.
- **Do not skip the question about what they would choose and just move on.** The choice is the orientation device. Without the choice being made, the session loses its center.
- **Do not get lost in the specific event if they choose the specific.** Even if they pick "forgive myself for this," the next move is the structural payoff question. The choice oriented the session; the structure is still the work.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "If I could give you a choice at the end of this call, you could realize how to forgive yourself for fucking up this relationship, or you could realize how to stop blaming yourself for everything. What would you choose?"
- **Client:** "Forgive myself."
- **Joe:** "And what makes it important to you to make sure that you keep blaming yourself for shit?"
- *(Source: Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?)*

The exchange shows the move's full arc: the choice is offered, the person picks, Joe immediately pivots to the structural question anyway. The "forgive myself" answer was real — Joe took it seriously — and the pivot was the way to actually serve that wish. To forgive yourself for this requires loosening the structure that keeps making everything your fault.

## Tone

Direct and clean. The choice is offered as a real thing the person can answer. Joe does not soften it or apologize for the framing. The forced-choice quality is the move's strength — the person knows they have to pick.

There is no judgment in either direction. Both options are legitimate. The phrasing "you could realize how to" is generous — it puts both options as realizations the person can have, not as performances they must accomplish.
