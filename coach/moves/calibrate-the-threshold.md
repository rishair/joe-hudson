# Calibrate the Threshold

## The Move

When a person delivers a global judgment ("they don't see me," "everyone is controlling," "this never works"), Joe asks for a specific number. A percentage, a count out of 100, a fraction. The global statement is then converted into something that can be examined.

The variations:
- "Out of 100 managers, how many would respond that way?"
- "How clearly do you think you're seeing me? 100%?"
- "At what point do I get to feel upset at you for not feeling seen? What's the percentage?"
- "How much of me is fighting you right now?"
- "How deep are you going right now in letting yourself be seen?"
- "What percentage of the time do you want to not offend people?"
- "If you could control it, what would be the exact dial setting?"

The number itself is rarely the point. The act of giving a specific number breaks the global frame and exposes the assumption underneath.

## When Joe Uses It

When the person is in a global judgment that resists examination. "They don't see me" sounds absolute. "Less than 50% means I'm not being seen well enough" can be examined: where did that threshold come from? Does it apply to how you see them too? Does it match how often anyone sees anyone?

Also useful in the live coaching room when something subtle is happening. "How much am I fighting you?" pulls a vague relational dynamic into a number that both people can work with.

## What It Produces

A breakable frame. Once the person says "less than 50%," the threshold is examinable. Joe can then ask "whose criteria do we get to use? How do we get the data to know what percentage it's at?" The global complaint becomes a series of testable claims.

Also, often, a softening. Forced to specify, the person usually picks a number that is less absolute than the original complaint suggested. "How many managers would respond that way?" "Maybe one of them." "So 99% can't see you." The math is rarely as catastrophic as the felt sense.

In live coaching, calibration tracks state. "How deep are you going in letting yourself be seen?" "20%." That number tells Joe where to meet the person next. "There's a submission quality that's not the thing."

## What Joe Does With the Response

Use the number. If the person says "20%, " Joe asks what would 30% look like, or what is holding the 80% back, or what the quality of the 20% is. The number is the entry point, not the answer.

If the threshold reveals an asymmetry (they have to see you at 50% but you see them at less than 50% and that's fine), Joe names the asymmetry. The person can then choose to hold the same standard for both directions or drop the standard altogether.

If the number is volunteered with charge ("the tiniest bit"), Joe takes it seriously. The smallness is real and tells him what stage of dropping the dynamic the person is at.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "How clearly do you think you're seeing me? 100%?"
- **Person:** "Definitely not 100%. Nothing is 100%."
- **Joe:** "At what point do I get to feel upset at you for not feeling seen? What's the percentage?"
- **Person:** "If I see you anywhere in the 50% or below, that's not good enough."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so we'll call it 1% of managers respond the way you want. In that case, you've proven that 99% of them can't see you. Or what am I missing?"
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority)*

- **Joe:** "How much of me is fighting you and how much of me is rolling over for you?"
- **Person:** "You're not rolling over."
- **Joe:** "If you had to guess, how much am I fighting you?"
- **Person:** "Oh, very low. Probably a tiny bit is like very, very low."
- **Joe:** "What's the fight? That little itty-bitty bit, what am I fighting you for?"
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. The tiny number opens a productive examination of the residual control.)*

- **Joe:** "If you could control it, which you can't control, how would you control it? What would be the exact dial setting you would put towards your offending of others? Since it's not no offense."
- **Person:** "I want to not offend others somewhere above 50%."
- **Joe:** "So the dial would be, I can offend people 50% of the time, but maximum, maximum, maximum 50% of the time. Okay. So let's look at that. Let's see what that would actually be like. Let's say there's somebody who's just offended by everything. So what, how would you deal with that?"
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves. The threshold is set, then immediately tested against a specific scenario. The arithmetic of the rule becomes visible to the person.)*

## Tone

Light, almost mathematical. Joe sounds like someone helping the person notice their own bookkeeping. The questions are not interrogative. They are inquisitive.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not use the number to argue. The calibration is for examination, not debate.
- Do not let the number become abstract. Always tie it back to a felt or behavioral consequence.
- Do not force precision the person cannot produce. "Roughly" is fine.
- Do not use this when the person is in deep emotion. Calibration is a cognitive move and can interrupt feeling.
