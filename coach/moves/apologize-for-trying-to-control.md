# Apologize for Trying to Control

## The Move

When you catch yourself trying to control someone, either through a botched boundary, a demand, a manipulation, or any form of behavior modification, name it directly and apologize. The apology is short, specific, and free of shame.

"I noticed I was trying to control you and that's not what I wanna do. It's not the relationship I wanna be in with you." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The move is for the speaker, not for the listener's comfort. It is what you do when you realize, mid-conversation or after, that the boundary you stated was an ultimatum, the request was a demand, or the emotional move was an attempt to make the other person do something.

## When To Use It

- After delivering a boundary that you now recognize landed as an ultimatum.
- When you notice mid-conversation that you are trying to change the other person.
- When the body signal of constriction reveals that what you said was a control move.
- When you have been managing someone's behavior through guilt, sulking, or tone.
- When [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums]] just happened in your own mouth.
- When you are practicing drawing boundaries and not getting them right yet.

The move is part of the practice of learning to draw clean boundaries. The expectation is not perfection. The expectation is the willingness to notice and correct.

"We all are gonna mess up. We're all gonna get scared and we're gonna think we're drawing a boundary that we're actually trying to control somebody else. But it's amazing if you can say to somebody, 'I noticed I was trying to control you and that's not what I wanna do. It's not the relationship I wanna be in with you,' how much people wanna hear that and how much trust that builds." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## How to Do It

Three components:

1. **Name what you noticed.** "I noticed I was trying to control you." Specific, present, owned. Not "I'm sorry I'm such a controlling person" (that is shame, see below) and not "I'm sorry if you felt controlled" (that is non-apology). Just the noticed move.
2. **State that it is not what you want.** "That's not what I wanna do. It's not the relationship I wanna be in with you." This grounds the apology in your own preference rather than in moral self-flagellation.
3. **Stop.** Do not over-explain. Do not re-justify the boundary. Do not pivot to a different version of the demand. Let the apology be what it is.

The whole thing is often less than two sentences. The brevity matters; the move is fast and clean.

## The No-Shame Requirement

The apology only works if it is not shame-based. A shame-based apology is itself another form of control: it makes the other person responsible for absolving the apologizer. The shame becomes a new burden on the relationship.

"Let that apology be one without shame that's heartfelt." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The clean version: the apologizer notices the move, dislikes it as misaligned with how they want to be, names it, and lets it go. The other person is not asked to do anything with it. There is no implicit ask for reassurance, no fishing for "it's okay," no need to be told they are still a good person.

See [[sorry-from-shame-vs-sorry-as-solidarity]] for the broader distinction. The control-apology lives in the "solidarity" version: I notice I did the thing, I am sorry it happened, here we are.

## Why It Works for Behavior Change

A heartfelt apology is one of the most effective behavior modification tools available. The mechanism is not punishment of the self; it is alignment between stated values and actual behavior.

"If you really give a heartfelt, non-shame-based apology, it's a great way to modify behavior. So yeah, we all are gonna mess up." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

Each time the apology is made, the controlling move is named explicitly. The unconscious behavior becomes conscious. The conscious behavior becomes harder to repeat without the awareness firing. Over time, the controlling move arises less because the cost (naming it, apologizing) becomes more immediate than the perceived benefit (controlling the other).

This is the same mechanism by which Joe says boundaries themselves are "some of the most effective change agents for healing transformation" (see [[boundaries]]): both directly test how you see the world and produce learning through the testing.

## Why It Builds Trust

Counterintuitively, the willingness to apologize for a botched boundary builds more trust than a perfectly executed boundary would. The reason: the apology demonstrates that you are tracking the dynamic, that you care about the relationship, and that you are not invested in being right.

The implicit message to the listener: "I am not trying to dominate you. When I slip into trying, I will notice it and stop. You can trust that this relationship is not a power game."

The trust this builds is more durable than the trust built by never slipping. It is grounded in the reality that humans do slip and the relationship can hold it.

## What This Is Not

Not over-apologizing. The chronic apologizer who says "I'm sorry" for everything is performing a different move (often [[fear-triangle|savior]] or [[victim-stance-invites-abuse|victim]]). The clean apology is specific to the noticed control move, not a general apology for existing.

Not undoing the boundary. If the underlying boundary was legitimate but got delivered as an ultimatum, the apology is for the delivery, not the boundary. After apologizing, the boundary itself can still be redrawn cleanly. The apology does not require capitulation on the substance.

Not the same as fishing for reassurance. The apology is a statement, not a question. It does not need to be received warmly to be valid.

Not a substitute for the internal work. The apology is the surface correction. The underlying work is on the fear that produced the control move. See [[empowered-vs-power]] for the underlying stance.

## Tone

Direct. Warm. Slightly self-amused, in the right context. The move is most powerful when delivered without heaviness. Heavy apologies often function as another control move (eliciting reassurance). Light apologies land cleanly.

The voice should match the original moment. If you were sharp in the controlling move, the apology can be soft. If you were cold, the apology can have a little warmth in it. The contrast itself is part of the repair.
