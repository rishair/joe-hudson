# Turn the Blame Into Wants

## The Move

When the impulse to blame arises -- about the partner, about anyone, about a situation -- catch it before it lands and convert it into an owned want. The blame and the want are the same energy in two different costumes. The blame externalizes the missing thing. The want owns the missing thing.

"Instead of blaming somebody, every time you feel that urge to blame, just own what you want. Say, 'Oh, I would want this. I want that.'" -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

The move is a real-time translation. The blame impulse is the signal. The translation is the practice.

## When To Use This Move

- When the partner has done something that triggers the blame impulse
- When the inner monologue is running a list of what the partner is doing wrong
- When the person is presenting a relationship problem framed entirely as the partner's failures
- When the urge to blame has become a habit and is producing the [[shame-hot-potato]] dynamic
- When the relationship has reached the [[walking-on-eggshells]] pattern and the suppressed material is leaking out as blame
- When the person is in [[i-feel-trapped]] and locating the trap in the partner

The move is especially useful early in the work because it is concrete and immediately actionable. The person does not have to do deep emotional excavation to begin. They just have to catch one blame impulse a day and translate it.

## What It Produces

The first effect is empowerment. Blame is the position of being acted upon; wanting is the position of having agency.

"Often when you're blaming, you feel disempowered. You feel like I can't have the life that I want. Which means asking for what you want is a way to feel empowered again." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

The second effect is clarity in the relationship. The partner now knows where the speaker stands rather than absorbing low-grade resentment.

"Sometimes people don't do it because it's a little scary to ask for what they want, but at least your partner knows where you are, knows where you stand, instead of you just being resentful in the background and going, 'Nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang.' Like, who the hell wants to be with that? Who wants to be married to that?" -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

The third effect is structural: blame produces shame, and shame is the lock that holds bad habits in place. The move breaks the lock by removing the blame that would have produced the shame.

"Placing blame onto another person or onto yourself, it doesn't actually help you get better. It just creates shame and shame stagnates the emotions that we're having and stagnates the reality we are having. There's this great saying that I love and it says, Shame is the locks that hold the chains of bad habits in place. And when you're blaming, you're putting shame on each other. And when you do that, it's just holding those bad habits in place." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

See [[shame-drives-behavior]] for the mechanism.

## How The Translation Works

The translation is not a formula but it has a recognizable shape. The blame is always pointing at something the speaker wants. The translation locates the want.

| Blame | Want |
|------|------|
| "He never helps with the dishes." | "I want help with the dishes." Or: "I want to not be the only one carrying the household load." |
| "She's always on her phone when I'm trying to connect." | "I want your full attention when we are together." |
| "He doesn't listen to me." | "I want to feel heard when I share something." |
| "She doesn't care about my career." | "I want you to be interested in what I'm working on." |
| "He's so emotionally distant." | "I want you to be present with me emotionally." |

The translation is not always one-to-one. Sometimes the blame is masking multiple wants. Sometimes the surface want covers a deeper want. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]].

## Why Wants Land Where Blame Does Not

Blame triggers defense. The partner hears the blame, their nervous system reads it as attack, they defend. The interaction becomes a power struggle. Nothing moves. See [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]].

A clean want does not trigger the same defense. The partner can engage with the want -- can choose to meet it, decline to meet it, or negotiate with it. The conversation is about what the speaker actually wants, not about what the partner is doing wrong.

The want also surfaces the actual content. Blame is often vague ("she doesn't get me"). The want forces specificity ("I want you to ask me how my day was when I get home"). Specificity is workable. Vague blame is not.

## The Trap to Watch For

The move can be turned into a weapon. "I want you to stop being such an asshole" is a blame in want costume. The translation has to be clean -- a want for something, not a complaint dressed as a want.

The diagnostic: if the want still has the partner as the wrong one in the sentence, it is not a clean translation. The clean translation locates the want in the speaker's experience and lets the partner be a free actor responding to it.

## How It Relates To Adjacent Moves

This move is the relational application of [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]]. The should-version translates an internal command into a personal want. The blame-version translates an external complaint into a personal want. Same mechanism, different surface.

The move is also the in-the-moment companion to [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]]. The eggshells practice surfaces the withheld wants. The blame-to-wants practice catches the wants that try to come out as blame and corrects the form before they land.

The move pairs with [[ask-from-slowness-not-intellect]] for clients who want to do the move but are not yet in touch with what they actually want. The blame is the entry; slowness is what produces the actual want underneath.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not "stop having grievances." Grievances are real. The move is about the form of the expression, not about pretending the underlying complaint does not exist.

It is not "ask politely and you will get what you want." The partner may decline. The point of the move is the empowerment and the clarity, not the production of compliance.

It is not "always have a want ready." Sometimes the blame impulse is real and there is no immediate want underneath -- just the need to express anger. In that case the work is to discharge the anger elsewhere first ([[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]]) and then return to the want.

## The Body of the Practice

A simple daily form:

1. Notice each blame impulse for one day. Most people are shocked at the frequency.
2. For each one, ask: what is the want underneath this?
3. Once a day, say the want to the partner. Clean and direct. Not all of them. Just one.
4. Notice what changes in the body when the want is spoken instead of the blame.

The body shift is the diagnostic. Blame produces contraction. Owned want produces a small opening. If the translation is not producing the opening, the translation is probably not clean yet. Keep working.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The shock of seeing how much of the day is blame
- The discomfort of owning a want that has been hidden behind blame for years
- The fear that the partner will refuse the want
- The relief of saying something clean for the first time in a long time
- The first taste of the empowerment that has been displaced by chronic blame

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person presents a relationship problem framed as the partner's failures
- A person describes themselves as resentful in the background
- A person uses "always" and "never" language about the partner
- [[toilet-blame-story]] type pattern is operating -- automatic blame attached to anything
- A person is in the early stages of the [[4-keys-to-save-your-relationship]] framework

## Tone

Light, direct, slightly humorous in the public-teaching delivery. The "Nyang, nyang, nyang" mimicry of resentful muttering is part of the tone. The move is presented as obvious and accessible, not as a depth practice. The depth is downstream.

When delivering this move one-on-one, the tone shifts to gentler. The recognition that the blame has been running for years is often sobering for the client. The lightness in the teaching contrasts with the weight of the recognition in the room.
