# Be Intimate With the Feeling

## The Move

When a person has located a felt experience — longing, sadness, helplessness, love — and the next instinct is either to push it away or to be swept up by it, Joe offers a third option: be intimate with it.

"Great, so go right into the middle of the longing. Keep your eyes closed, go right into the middle of the longing. Don't disconnect from your longing, be intimate with your longing." — Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

The instruction has three parts:
- **Stay close to the feeling** — don't pull back, don't analyze from a distance
- **Don't disconnect** — the person was about to leave, language for the leaving is named ("disconnection," "leaving," "going into the head")
- **Be intimate with it** — the relationship offered is intimacy, not control and not surrender

The word "intimate" is doing specific work. It names a kind of contact that is close, sustained, and relational — but not merged. It is what one does with a beloved. The instruction is to bring that quality of contact to the feeling itself.

## When Joe Uses It

This move comes after the catastrophic image has been entered (see [[lets-go-there]] and [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]]) and the actual felt material has surfaced — usually a longing, a sadness, or a love the person has been keeping at bay.

The signals that the move is the right next step:
- The person has named a feeling and is now describing it from a distance ("it feels like a disconnection and a longing")
- The body is already in contact with the feeling but the head is starting to organize an exit
- The feeling is intense enough that the person's instinct is either to manage it or to surrender to it
- The work has moved past the abyss and into the actual material that was being avoided
- The person needs a third option between control and dissolution

The move is also used when a person is using their analytic mind to handle the feeling. The person describes the feeling articulately, accurately, even insightfully — but is no longer in contact with it. The instruction redirects from articulation to intimacy.

## What It Produces

The first thing is a deepening of contact. The person who was describing the feeling now feels it. The articulation falls away. The body changes.

The second thing, often, is the surfacing of how big the feeling actually is. "It's so big." When the person stops managing the size of the feeling, the actual size becomes apparent. Often it is much larger than the managed version suggested.

The third thing is often a specific fear: "It feels like if I connect to it, it might absorb me whole." The fear of being absorbed is the fear that intimate contact equals dissolution. Joe meets this fear with the precise distinction that the move encodes: "But you don't give into it. You just be intimate with it." See [[intimate-with-vs-giving-in-to-feeling]].

The fourth thing is usually the discovery that intimate contact is sustainable. The person can stay in the longing without being absorbed. The fear of dissolution was a story about what would happen, not a description of what does happen. Intimate contact can be held.

## What Joe Does Next

Once intimate contact is established, Joe permits calibration. The person can choose how long to stay in the contact today, and increase tomorrow.

"You can do that if you want. You can just like spend a little time with the longing. Tomorrow you can spend a little more time with the longing. The next day you can taper if you'd like."

The permission is structurally important. It distinguishes intimacy (which can be calibrated) from surrender (which cannot). The person retains agency over how much contact they make. The agency is what makes the move sustainable as a practice.

What is not within the calibration: the love itself. "You don't get to control love. That's not how it works. You just get to be heartbroken from time to time, increasing your capacity to love." See [[you-dont-control-love]] and [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]. The calibration is over the contact, not over what is being contacted.

## Why It Works

The move resolves a structural problem in working with intense feelings. The person has two felt options and both are bad: push the feeling away (which is what they have been doing) or surrender to the feeling (which is what they fear). Both options preserve a binary that keeps them stuck.

Intimacy is a third position the person did not know was available. It has the closeness of surrender and the agency of management without the costs of either. The closeness lets the feeling complete. The agency lets the person stay themselves.

The success of the move usually depends on the person discovering experientially that this third position exists. The instruction alone does not produce it. The person has to try it and notice that they have not been absorbed and have not disconnected. That noticing is what makes the position available again outside the session.

The move also enacts a particular truth about how feelings work. Feelings are not threats; they are visitors that want to be met. The instruction "be intimate with it" treats the feeling as something to know, not something to defeat or surrender to. The framing changes the felt quality of the encounter. The same longing approached as a threat is one thing; approached as something to be intimate with is another.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not surrender. The move explicitly distinguishes itself from surrender ("you don't give into it"). The agency is preserved.

This is not management. The person is not handling the feeling from outside it. The contact is close.

This is not the same as [[loving-the-anxiety-vs-being-with-it]] applied to feelings. That distinction targets the gap between detached observation and full welcoming. The intimacy move targets the gap between disconnection and absorption. They are adjacent distinctions but operate on different binaries.

This is also not catharsis. The move does not require dramatic release. The intimate contact can be quiet. The body knows what it needs.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "Feel the loneliness for a minute, like jump into the loneliness."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, no reason. Yeah, how is that not love?"
- **Person:** "It feels like a disconnection and a longing more than love."
- **Joe:** "Great, so go right into the middle of the longing. Keep your eyes closed, go right into the middle of the longing. Don't disconnect from your longing, be intimate with your longing."
- **Person:** "It's so big."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, love is really fucking big."
- **Person:** "It feels like if I connect to it, like, it might absorb me whole."
- **Joe:** "It will. That's what love does. It rips away our identity."
- **Person:** "It feels like if I give in to the longing, I might be just like bare, like a little baby, like totally helpless."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's how it works. But you don't give into it. You just be intimate with it."
- *(Source: I Am Scared Of The Unknown — the move's full arc from the redirect ("don't disconnect, be intimate") through the discovery of size and fear of absorption to the precise distinction Joe offers between intimacy and giving in)*

## Tone

Quiet, slow, warm. The voice has the quality of an invitation into a deep pool the speaker has been in many times. There is no urgency. The pacing widens.

The word "intimate" is delivered with the same weight one would use for "beloved" or "sacred." It is not casual. The person is being invited into a particular kind of contact and the tone has to carry the invitation.

When the fear of absorption arrives, the tone shifts slightly to validation: "Yeah, that's how it works." Joe is not arguing the fear away. He is naming what it is true about (intimate contact does, in some way, dissolve the self that has been managing) while preserving the third option (the dissolution is not the absorption the fear is anticipating).
