# Go to Separate Rooms and Yell, Then Come Back

## The Move

When the fight you are in (or the fight about to start) is a displaced discharge — one or both of you is looking for friction because something else needs to move — interrupt the dynamic and propose a shared separate-room release. Each person goes to their own room. Each person yells (or cries, or releases however the body wants). Then both come back together to have the actual conversation, if there is one.

"I highly recommend in a — like, if you see a fight where this is happening, say, hey, let's just go and yell in other rooms and then come back together so that you don't have to actually have this like false emotional outlet at somebody to discover what the real thing is happening for you. Instead, just go and have the emotional outlet without hurting each other. Get the yelling, get the crying, get whatever needs to happen that's not — nyeh nyeh nyeh nyeh — at each other. And then come back and then have the discussion, whatever that discussion is." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The move is the practical operationalization of the principle [[looking-for-a-fight-means-self-disconnection]]. The body wants the discharge. The discharge does not have to happen at the partner. The third option — release in private, then reconnect — is what the move installs.

## When This Move Is Available

This move presumes the read [[everything-i-say-they-contradict]] is firing. The fight is not (or not primarily) about content. The discharge-seeking is the signature. If the fight is actually about content — a broken agreement, a real grievance, real relational material — this move can land as deflection, and a different move is needed.

The cleanest version is when both partners have been introduced to the framework beforehand. One person can say "hey, looks like we want to fight" and the other recognizes the move and accepts it. Without the prior introduction, the move can be initiated by one person and the other may need to learn what is being offered. In a relationship where this has been agreed on outside a fight (see [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]]), the move is much easier to deploy.

## The Verbal Shape

The opening phrase is direct and shared, not adversarial:

- "Hey, it looks like we want to get in a fight. Let's just go yell and then we'll come back."
- "Looks like we're both wound up. Let's go discharge in separate rooms and then talk."
- "I'm picking a fight. I want to go yell for a few minutes and come back."

Joe's framing: "It's just a simple thing." The simplicity matters. Long explanations of why the fight should not happen tend to escalate the fight. The short proposal of an alternative tends to interrupt it.

## What Happens in Each Room

Each person uses the room as the [[get-angry-in-the-car]] practice. Yell. Move the body. Let the held charge discharge. No script. No prescribed format. Whatever the body wants.

"Get the yelling, get the crying, get whatever needs to happen." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The release does not have to be only anger. Sometimes what was wanting to come out was crying, or trembling, or laughing out the absurdity of the day. The body knows what it wants once it has been given the room.

The duration is whatever it takes. Ten minutes is often enough. Twenty minutes if the charge is bigger. The signal that the discharge has completed is the body settling — the wound-up state releasing, the breath deepening, the felt sense of "okay, that needed to come out."

## Coming Back Together

After the discharge, both partners reconvene. The conversation that follows is qualitatively different from the fight that was about to happen.

- If there was actual relational material underneath the discharge (a real complaint about the partner that was getting mixed in with the displacement), it can be named cleanly now. The clarity that comes after the discharge is described in [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]].
- If the fight was entirely displaced — the boss yelled, the day was hard — the partners can reconnect without any conversation being required. "I'm just so glad to see you. Today was hard." The discharge revealed that there was no relational material; there was just held emotion that needed somewhere to go.
- Often a mix: there is a small piece of actual relational material that becomes visible after the displaced material has discharged. The piece is much smaller than the fight would have been if it had run on the conflated content.

The reconnection move at the end often looks like a hug, a cuddle, a quiet moment of contact. The body that was reaching for friction has now had its release; what it actually needed (the connection) becomes accessible.

## Why This Works

The move solves the third-option-blindness problem named in [[looking-for-a-fight-means-self-disconnection]]. The mind in the heat of the moment defaults to two options: have the fight, or repress the anger. Neither works. The first damages the relationship; the second leaves the held charge in the body for the next round.

The third option — release elsewhere, then reconnect — gives the body what it wants without using the partner as the discharge surface. The partner is preserved from being the target. The discharge happens. The actual conversation, if there is one, gets to happen from clarity instead of from charge.

"It works wonders. It's such an easy thing to do." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The "easy thing to do" framing matters. Most couples have never heard this as an option. Once they have, the move is mechanically simple. The hard part is remembering it in the moment when the fight-seeking state is already running. The first few times require deliberate effort to interrupt the pattern. After a few successes, the move becomes more automatic.

## The Withdrawal-With-Timeline Connection

Going to a separate room without explanation lands as abandonment. The move depends on the proposal being mutual and explicit. "Let's both go" is fundamentally different from "I'm leaving." The shared proposal frames the separation as a shared move toward each other, not as one person retreating from the other.

If only one person is in the discharge-seeking state, the move can still work — but the framing has to include the timeline. "I'm wound up and I want to go yell for twenty minutes. Then I'll be back." See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for why the timeline distinguishes this from abandonment-by-leaving.

## What This Is Not

This is not the "go cool off" move that some couples have absorbed from popular psychology. "Cool off" suggests suppression — go calm yourself down, regulate, come back composed. The separate-rooms-and-yell move is the opposite: do not cool off. Heat up. Let the heat actually move through the body. Come back when the heat has moved, not when you have managed to suppress it back down. The distinction matters because suppressed heat returns; moved heat does not.

This is also not silent treatment dressed up. Silent treatment is a punishment-by-absence with no announced return and no acknowledgment of what is happening. The separate-rooms move announces the return, frames the absence as shared work, and ends in reconnection. The two have almost nothing in common despite the surface similarity of "we are in different rooms."

This is also not the in-the-relationship-doing-emotion-together move. Some couples do anger work together, with consent, both partners present and active. That is a different move with different prerequisites. See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] for the consent carve-out where in-the-relationship anger work can be appropriate. The separate-rooms move is for the case where the discharge is displaced and the relationship should not be the surface at all.

## When This Move Is Not Enough

The move is in-the-moment. It interrupts the discharge from landing on the partner. It does not address why the discharge keeps needing to happen. If the underlying conditions — chronic work stress, an unprocessed trauma, an authority figure who is regularly producing the loaded state — are not addressed, the discharge will keep arriving home, even if it no longer lands on the partner.

The longer-term resolution is upstream: a daily practice that keeps the charge from accumulating to the level where it needs the relationship as the discharge surface. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the daily solo version and [[daily-emotional-yoga]] for the broader practice. The in-the-moment move and the daily practice work together.

## When the Read Is Wrong

If you propose the move and the partner says "no, this is actually about you, this is real," take that seriously. The read [[everything-i-say-they-contradict]] is a hypothesis, not a verdict. Sometimes the fight is about content. The contradict-myself test (see [[the-contradict-myself-test]]) is the disambiguator. If the partner engages substantively with the reversed position, the discharge frame does not apply and a different move is needed.

The move should not be used as a way to deflect actual relational complaints by relabeling them as "you're just discharging." That is the anti-pattern of using the principle as a weapon. See the warning in [[everything-i-say-they-contradict]].

## Tone

The proposal of the move should be light. Not heavy, not therapeutic, not "we need to talk about our pattern." Light. "Hey, looks like we want to fight. Let's go yell and come back." The lightness signals that the structure is normal, the move is available, and neither person is being pathologized for the fight-seeking impulse. The lightness is also what makes the move repeatable; couples who treat each invocation as a major event will use it less often than couples who treat it as a simple available tool.

Joe's tone when describing the move: matter-of-fact, almost playful. "It works wonders. It's such an easy thing to do." The energy of the description signals the energy of the deployment.
