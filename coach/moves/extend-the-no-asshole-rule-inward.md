# Extend the No-Asshole Rule Inward (How About Your Internal Asshole?)

## The Move

When a person reports a strong external boundary against asshole behavior -- a "no asshole rule" they have lived by for years, a strict three-strikes policy with friends, a workplace they curated for non-toxicity -- Joe asks the parallel question about the inside.

"Yeah. How about your internal asshole?" -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Feel Financially Safe?

The move is short. One question. It surfaces a pattern that is common in people who have done significant external boundary work: they will not tolerate disrespect, contempt, or emotional discharge from anyone in their life -- except the voice in their own head, which is allowed to do all of the above without consequence.

## When Joe Uses It

The move appears when a few signals stack:

- The person has named an external no-asshole rule with some pride. The principle is solidly held, often for decades.
- The work has surfaced that some current pattern (a difficulty stopping a chase, an inability to enjoy what has been built, a continuous low-grade anxiety) is still operating despite the external rule being in place.
- The person has not yet looked at how the rule is applied internally.

The diagnostic for whether this move is the right one: the person has built external protection, has met what they thought was the threat, and is still operating as if the threat is present. The threat that has not been addressed is internal.

The move is also useful when the person describes themselves as "having dealt with" an internal critic, a temper, a self-destructive pattern. Phrases like "the demon's been slayed," "I dominated that part of myself," "I beat that out of me a long time ago" are signals that the internal asshole was met with violence rather than with a boundary, and the boundary is now the relevant move.

## What It Produces

A pause, then usually a recognition. The person who has carefully arranged the outside of their life to be free of mistreatment is being asked whether the same standard applies to how they treat themselves internally. The answer is almost always no.

Sometimes the recognition produces shame ("oh -- I'm doing the thing I won't let anyone else do to me"). Sometimes it produces clarity about why the external work has not produced internal peace. Sometimes it produces an immediate insight about a current pattern that had been mystifying the person.

In Doug's case, the move produced an immediate confirmation that the internal asshole existed, followed by the report that he had been at war with it for a long time. The follow-up question "Where's the protection from that?" then opened the work into what the rest of the session addressed.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Whatever the person reports, Joe stays with it. The most common patterns:

- **"He's been dominated."** The person reports that they fought the internal asshole and won. Joe notes the framing ("You said my asshole has been dominated. Was that the words?") -- the war language is the diagnostic that the wrong relationship is in place. The internal critic was met with violence and the violence won, but at the cost of the relationship. The move from here is usually toward [[chase-it-around-the-room]] -- the inverse relationship of pursuit-with-love rather than dominance.

- **"I have one and it's brutal."** The person reports active ongoing self-attack. The move from here is usually toward [[voice-as-care]] -- the reframe that the brutal voice is care with bad strategies, and toward [[find-the-emotion-under-self-judgment]] -- what the self-attack is protecting the person from feeling.

- **"I never thought about it that way."** The person had not yet seen the pattern. Joe does not press. The naming has done its work. The person will keep noticing now.

- **"I don't really have one anymore."** Less common, sometimes true, sometimes a defended report. Joe checks somatically -- is the body soft when the report is given, or is there a small clench? If the body confirms the report, the work moves on. If not, the report is held lightly and the work continues with the assumption that the internal asshole is still operating, just not yet visible.

## Dialogue Example

Doug had just described his external boundary in detail. His "no asshole rule" of 30 years. The three-strikes-and-out policy with friends. The no-exceptions framing.

- **Doug:** "I have a no asshole rule, and I've had that for 30 years."
- **Joe:** "Me too. I have that same rule."
- **Doug:** "My friends, my friends get 3 chances. The first time I tell them nicely not to be an asshole, that what they said hurt me. The second time I'm not as kind, and the third time I just say we're not hanging out anymore. And there's, there's no exceptions to that rule."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. How about your internal asshole?"
- **Doug:** "What, what, we all -- I, I have one, so what about --"
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's what I mean. Yeah, yeah, exactly. Where, where's the protection from that?"
- *(Source: How Do I Feel Financially Safe?)*

The move is two beats: the question naming the internal asshole, then the question naming the missing protection. The second question is what opens the work. Doug had spent decades protecting himself from external assholes and had built no equivalent protection against the internal one. That asymmetry was the place the rest of the session went.

## Tone

Light, almost casual. Joe matches the conversational tone the person used to describe the external rule. The move is not delivered as a challenge or as a "gotcha." It is delivered as a peer noticing a parallel: "Me too. I have that same rule. Yeah. How about your internal asshole?"

The lightness is functional. The move would land as an attack if delivered seriously. The person is being shown a parallel they had not noticed, not being accused of hypocrisy. The casual tone is what allows the recognition to happen without defensiveness.

There is also a kind of cheerful realism in the delivery. Everyone has one. The question is not "do you have one?" The question is "what protection do you have against it?" The presumption of universality reduces the shame that might otherwise attach to admitting the internal asshole exists.

## Boundary Conditions

The move requires that the person actually has a strong external no-asshole rule. Used on someone who tolerates abusive external behavior, the move would land as a comparison to something the person has not yet established, and would produce confusion rather than recognition.

The move also does not work if the internal asshole is actively producing crisis. A person in active self-harm or acute suicidal ideation requires a different and more clinical approach. The move is for the everyday internal critic that is operating below the threshold of crisis but is still doing significant damage.
