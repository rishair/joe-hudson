# Speak the Welcoming of the Feared State Aloud

## The Move

Once the person has discovered (often through the [[press-the-button-thought-experiment]]) that the state they are afraid of recurring is also something they value, have them say the welcoming out loud. The form is "I can't wait to be [feared state] again." The exact word their body has been bracing against ("depressed," "anxious," "heartbroken," "alone," "rejected") goes into the sentence.

The move is not an affirmation. The point is not to install a belief by repetition. The point is to give the body a stimulus -- the same word it has been treating as a threat -- delivered in a sentence that frames the word as welcome, and watch what happens.

The form Joe uses:

- **Joe:** "So yeah, let's do the experiment. I can't wait to be depressed again."
- **Guest:** "I can't wait to be depressed again."
- **Joe:** "What a pleasure."

Three turns. Joe models the sentence. The person speaks it. Joe meets what arises. The session ends there.

## When Joe Uses It

- After the [[press-the-button-thought-experiment]] has revealed that the person values the state they are afraid of (they would not push the button to erase it).
- After the principle [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] has been named for this specific case ("the more that you don't want it to happen again, the more likely it is to happen again").
- When the [[the-smile-of-recognition]] is already on the person's face -- the body has registered that the welcoming is true; the mouth has not yet said it.
- When the conceptual ground for [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]] has been laid and the felt instantiation needs a specific moment to land.
- At the end of a session, as the move that consolidates the shift the session has been working toward.

This move is not an opener. It is a closer. It works only after the cognitive frame has been built. Said without the prior work, the sentence is just words.

## What It Produces

- A smile, often the smile that was already flickering. See [[the-smile-of-recognition]]. The smile usually deepens when the sentence is spoken aloud.
- A small laugh, sometimes. The absurdity of the sentence is the recognition. The laugh is the body voting yes.
- A felt drop or release in the chest or face. The bracing pattern around the word releases when the word is delivered in a welcoming sentence by the person's own mouth.
- A pause, often with a soft "yeah" afterward. The pause is the integration. The "yeah" is the speaking self catching up to what the body has already done.
- Occasionally, tears -- when the person realizes how long they have been bracing against the very thing they were getting wisdom from.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says it cleanly with a smile or laugh:** Joe meets the sentence with affirmation that is not coaching. "What a pleasure." "Yeah." A small smile of his own. The session is at its endpoint; the move was the move. Pushing past the moment would dilute it. See [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]].
- **If the person hesitates or says it flatly:** Joe slows down and asks them to feel the sentence as they say it. The hesitation is data -- the body has not yet released the bracing the principle has cognitively loosened. The move can be rerun after a deeper feel of what was protecting the bracing.
- **If the person laughs and tries to walk it back ("but really I don't want to"):** Joe stays with the laugh. The laugh was the data. The walk-back is the verbal defense rearming. Joe does not argue. He can ask: "what did the laugh know?"
- **If the person resists the sentence entirely ("I can't say that"):** the resistance is itself the next layer of work. What is the bracing protecting? The move can be deferred until more of the bracing has been felt through. The premature attempt to speak the welcoming would manufacture a performance.

## Dialogue Example

The person has just recognized that the depression she was through was a gift she would not trade. Joe has just said: "the more that you don't want it to happen again, the more likely it is to happen again."

- **Joe:** "So what is it like to say, I can't wait to be depressed again? That is..." *(the guest starts to laugh)*
- **Guest:** "is..."
- **Joe:** "Look at that smile. Just say it out, try it."
- **Guest:** "Oh yeah."
- **Joe:** "So yeah, let's do the experiment. I can't wait to be depressed again."
- **Guest:** "I can't wait to be depressed again."
- **Joe:** "What a pleasure."

*(Source: I'm Afraid I'll Get Depressed Again)*

The sentence is delivered three times: by Joe modeling, by the guest speaking, and by Joe meeting ("what a pleasure"). The triplet is the move. The "what a pleasure" reframes the meaning of the sentence as it sits in the room. The sentence is no longer about depression. It is about the relationship to depression that has just shifted.

## Why It Works

The bracing against the feared state is held in part by an unspoken sentence in the person's body: "I don't ever want to be that again." That sentence runs underneath behavior, often without being noticed. Speaking the opposite sentence aloud -- in the person's own voice, in the same room as the held bracing -- forces the body to register a contradiction.

The body can either re-armor against the new sentence (which is what would happen if the move were attempted without prior work) or let the new sentence reorganize the bracing. When the cognitive frame has been built ([[press-the-button-thought-experiment]] has revealed the gift; [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] has named the recursion), the body usually chooses the second.

The mouth saying the sentence is the operative variable. Joe could deliver the welcoming on behalf of the person ("you can welcome the depression"). It would not land the same way. The person's own mouth, in their own voice, makes the welcoming a claim of the self rather than an instruction from outside.

This is the felt-instantiation form of [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]]. The distinction between being-with and loving is conceptual until the welcoming is spoken in the first person about the specific feared object. Then it becomes lived. The "I can't wait to feel it again" sentence is the operational form of love-rather-than-be-with for any specific emotion or state.

## Variations Across Feared States

The sentence form is constant; the object changes.

- For depression: "I can't wait to be depressed again." See the source session.
- For anxiety: "I can't wait to be anxious again." This is the form Joe uses explicitly in [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]].
- For helplessness: "I can't wait to feel helpless again." See [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]].
- For sadness, anger, fear, heartbreak: "I can't wait to feel sad / angry / scared / heartbroken again."
- For specific feared events: "I can't wait to be rejected again." "I can't wait to be left." "I can't wait to be misunderstood." This extends the move beyond emotional states to the events the person is afraid of.
- For the criticism-feeling underneath doubt: "I can't wait for somebody to criticize me." The form Joe uses when the doubt is rooted in the childhood criticism-sensitivity ([[doubt-requires-closed-heart]]).
- For the failure-feeling underneath doubt: "I can't wait to feel like a failure." The form Joe uses when the doubt is rooted in the projected fear of the failure-feeling.
- For the success-feeling underneath doubt: "I can't wait to feel that massive success." The form Joe uses when the doubt is rooted in the projected fear of the expansive feeling of winning (see [[fear-of-success-as-projected-failure]]).

The precision matters. The exact word the person has been bracing against goes into the sentence. A generic "I welcome difficult feelings" does not work; the body does not register the specific object. The specific word, in the specific sentence, in the person's mouth, is the move.

## Using the Sentence as the Diagnostic for Doubt's Source

The move has a specific application as the diagnostic for [[doubt-is-protection-from-a-feeling]]. When a person presents with chronic doubt about an ambition, the source of the doubt is one of several possible feelings the body is protecting against. The welcoming sentence, tried in several forms, locates which one.

"If you can say, oh, I can't wait for somebody to criticize me, the doubt goes away. If you say, I can't wait to feel like a failure, the doubt goes away. If you say, I can't wait to feel that massive success, the doubt goes away." -- Joe Hudson, Ambitious But Afraid

The diagnostic procedure: try the sentences with the most likely candidate underlying feelings. Watch which one drops the doubt. The one that drops it is the source.

- If "I can't wait to be criticized" drops the doubt → the source is the criticism-sensitivity, often rooted in childhood
- If "I can't wait to feel like a failure" drops the doubt → the source is the projected failure-feeling
- If "I can't wait to feel that massive success" drops the doubt → the source is the projected expansive feeling of winning

Often more than one sentence drops some of the doubt. The doubt was being held in place by multiple protections. The full release comes from welcoming all of them.

This diagnostic application makes the move usable earlier in a session than the depression-form version. In the depression-form, the move is a closing operationalization after substantial prior work. In the doubt-diagnostic form, the move is an early-session probe to locate the source of an undiagnosed protective doubt. The depth of prior work required differs because the goal differs: in the depression-form the goal is a felt-experience shift in the room; in the doubt-diagnostic form the goal is locating the source so subsequent work can be targeted.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not [[have-them-say-i-want-all-of-me-loved]]. That move uses a welcoming statement to surface the part of the self that cannot be loved. The probe is about loving self. This move is about welcoming a feared state -- typically external to the self, an emotion or event the person is afraid of recurring.

This is not [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]]. That move surfaces a forbidden want by speaking it; the bait is desire. This move surfaces a welcoming of what is feared; the bait is the inversion of the bracing.

This is not an affirmation practice. Affirmations are repeated to install belief through reinforcement. This move is spoken once, after substantial prior work, with the body's specific reaction being the data. The repetition would dilute the move; the once-said sentence is what makes it operative.

## Tone

Light, playful, almost mischievous. The sentence is absurd on its surface ("I can't wait to be depressed again"). Joe does not deny the absurdity. He uses it. The smile precedes the sentence; the laugh accompanies it; the "what a pleasure" lands as a small joke that is also serious.

The lightness matters because a gravely delivered version of the move would push the person to perform sincerity. The performance would be the next layer of defense. The playfulness lets the body do what it is already doing rather than producing a manufactured response.

The closing "what a pleasure" is delivered with the warmth of a small celebration. The person has just done something that took the whole session to make possible. Joe acknowledges the doing without explaining it.
