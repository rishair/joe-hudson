# Redirect From "How Do I Do This Right" to "How Do I Enjoy Doing This"

## The Move

When someone is stuck on a task, performing poorly, or producing less than they want to produce, the diagnostic question is often: are they asking themselves how to do it right, or are they asking themselves how to enjoy doing it? The two questions sound similar but produce opposite results.

The "right" question is a perfectionism question. It activates inspection, self-judgment, and the comparison of the in-progress work to an external standard. It collapses the relationship to the work into a series of evaluations. Production slows. Enjoyment vanishes. Quality often drops.

The "enjoy" question is an engagement question. It activates the present-moment relationship with the work itself. It produces motion, curiosity, and the sustained attention that good work actually requires. Production accelerates. Enjoyment arrives. Quality often rises.

The move: when you hear the "right" frame in someone's relationship with a task, redirect to the "enjoy" frame.

"Don't ask yourself when you have a task, don't ask yourself, how do I do this right? Please ask yourself, how do I enjoy doing the task? And then do the task the way that you enjoy doing the task." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3—Ambition, Narcissistic Parents, Addiction to Stress, Parenting Emotional

## The Employee Story

Joe's worked example: a recent hire was not happy and not doing the work that needed to get done. In conversation, it became clear that she was working hard to do things right. Joe gave her the redirect.

"For whatever reason, it clicked, and boom — like, 2 days later, everything that I would want to work with was there, just because of that flip of the switch. The ambition was wanting to be a part of the team and contribute and live this purpose that she gets to live by working with us. But the thing that was getting in the way was the trying to get something perfect. And as soon as it went to like, 'oh, how do I enjoy this?' it was like the productivity quadrupled." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The shift was not in skills, support, or workload. It was in the question she was asking herself about the work. The new question produced a different relationship to the work. The different relationship produced different output.

## Why "Right" Is the Wrong Question

The "right" question presupposes an external standard that judges the work. The work becomes a thing to be checked against the standard rather than a thing to be done. The standard is often unreachable (because perfectionism reaches for moving targets). The constant comparison-to-standard produces:

- Slowed motion (every action is evaluated before it happens).
- Self-judgment (every action that does not perfectly match is a failure).
- Avoidance (the easiest way to avoid being judged for doing it wrong is to not do it).
- Loss of contact with the work (the attention is on the meta-question, not the work itself).
- Loss of contact with the want underneath (the underlying impulse — to contribute, to make something, to express — gets covered by the should).

See [[perfectionism]] for the broader pattern. The "right" question is the verbal form perfectionism takes in the moment of starting a task.

## Why "Enjoy" Is the Right Question

The "enjoy" question presupposes that the work is something one is in relationship with, not something being inspected from outside. The work becomes the place attention lives. Multiple downstream effects follow naturally:

- Motion happens (engagement does not require pre-evaluation).
- Curiosity is engaged (what is the most enjoyable way to approach this? often surfaces creative solutions).
- The body comes online (enjoyment is a felt state; perfectionism is a head state).
- The actual want underneath the work re-emerges (the contribution, the making, the expression).
- Quality often rises (because attention is on the work, not on the evaluation of the work).

The mechanism is the same as [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]] applied to work tasks specifically. The general principle: enjoyment of what is happening produces better engagement with what is happening than evaluation of what is happening does.

## When to Use This Move

- A coachee reports being stuck on a task they intellectually want to do.
- A coachee is producing poor or slow work and attributes it to motivation problems.
- A coachee describes themselves as a perfectionist whose perfectionism is interfering with output.
- A new hire is struggling and the explicit or implicit motivation is "I want to do this right."
- Someone is overworking but feels they are not actually getting much done.
- The phrase "I just want to do it right" or "I want to make sure I'm doing this correctly" shows up.
- Someone is paralyzed at the start of a task and looking for the "right" approach.

## How to Deliver

The redirect is short and concrete. Do not philosophize. Name the wrong question; offer the right one.

- "I notice you're trying to do it right. Try asking 'how do I enjoy this?' instead and do it that way."
- "What if instead of doing it right, you did it the way you most enjoy doing it?"
- "What's the way you would enjoy doing this task?"

The instruction is operational. It is not "stop being a perfectionist" or "love yourself more." It is a different question to ask in the actual moment of doing the work.

## What the Response Reveals

The person's response to the redirect is itself diagnostic.

- **If the person engages and finds an enjoyable way to do the task:** the redirect lands. The work moves. See if the new approach holds the next day.
- **If the person says "I don't know how I would enjoy this":** the next move is [[whats-stopping-you-from-enjoying-x]]. There is usually a should or a fear underneath that needs to be felt.
- **If the person says "I don't enjoy any of this work":** the redirect surfaces a different question: should they be doing this work at all? Sometimes the "do it right" framing has been masking the absence of any actual desire to do the work. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].
- **If the person performs the redirect intellectually but the body does not engage:** the perfectionism has co-opted the new frame. They are now trying to "enjoy it right." Name this and slow down.

## What This Move Does Not Mean

This is not "only do work you enjoy." It is not "if it is not fun, do not do it." It is a redirect of the question being asked while doing the work. The same work can be done from the right frame or the enjoy frame. The latter tends to produce more and better.

This is also not "drop your standards." High standards are compatible with the enjoyment frame. The standards are not the issue. The relationship to the standards is the issue. From the right frame, the standards are judges. From the enjoy frame, the standards are part of the craft one is engaged with.

## Connection to Ambition

This move is the operational application of [[raw-ambition-vs-stories-on-ambition]] at the task level. The story-loaded ambition produces the "do it right" question. The raw ambition produces the "enjoy doing it" question. The redirect is therefore one of the more accessible ways to surface the difference for someone who has not yet seen the distinction in their own work.

## Tone

Direct, light, concrete. Joe delivers this as a small flip of a switch, not as a deep teaching. "Don't ask yourself how to do this right. Ask yourself how to enjoy doing it." The instruction is small enough that the person can try it that day. The change is large enough that the result often surprises them.
