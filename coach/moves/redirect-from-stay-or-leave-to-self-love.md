# Redirect From Stay-or-Leave to Self-Love

## The Move

The person presents a stay-or-leave question about a relationship. Instead of helping them decide, take the decision off the table. Pivot to self-love as the work, and demonstrate — in the body, in the moment — that when self-love is present, the stay-or-leave question loses its weight. The decision gets made on its own.

The structure is three steps:

1. **Refuse the decision.** "All you have to do is love yourself and then the decision gets made. You don't need to worry about whether you're going to be with him or not be with him."

2. **Move to the body.** "Love yourself unconditionally just for a moment again. Even put your hand on your heart. Yeah, feel it."

3. **Ask the now-empty decision-question.** "Now answer the question: does it matter? How much does it matter if you stay with him or not?"

The answer in the source session: "It doesn't."

The recognition is the move. The person feels in their own body that the stay-or-leave question, which has been the entire content of their deliberation for months, has no weight under conditions of self-love. The deliberation was being powered by the absence of self-love. With the self-love available, the deliberation stops.

"All you have to do is love yourself and then the decision gets made." -- Joe Hudson, Should I Stay With My Ex?

"Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall." -- Joe Hudson, Should I Stay With My Ex?

## When Joe Uses It

The move appears after:

- The person has presented a stay-or-leave question about a relationship.
- The caretaking pattern has been surfaced — usually via [[what-would-it-take-for-you-to-not-take-care-of-them]] and follow-up.
- The strategy underneath the caretaking has been named ("you're trying to manage him into happiness so you can get what?").
- The person has recognized the trade between management and love, often through compressed somatic demonstration (see [[feel-the-love-then-feel-obligation]]).
- The next move, in the typical decision-coaching frame, would be "okay so how do you decide?" Joe instead pivots to self-love.

The move is the closing move of the session. It is what Joe lands on after the caretaking has been seen and the trade has been felt. It is also what makes the session complete without resolving the surface question.

## What It Produces

The most common response: the person reports the decision no longer feeling load-bearing. "It doesn't matter." "I'm okay." The shift is felt, not argued. The person is surprised to discover that the question they have been sitting in for months is light when self-love is present.

A second common response: a body shift. The chest opens. The breath drops. The person reports feeling their heart for the first time in the session. This is what Joe is looking for and what he sometimes names ("yeah, there it is, that's where the sex drive went").

A third common response: tears. The recognition that they have been making the stay-or-leave question the heavy work when the actual work was self-love can surface grief — for the years spent in the wrong question, for the cost to the relationship of the trade.

A fourth response, less common but worth flagging: the person tries to make self-love the new should. "Okay so I should love myself, then I'll know what to do." Joe catches this. The self-love is not a step toward the decision. It is the work itself. The decision is downstream and unimportant.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person reports the decision no longer mattering, Joe stays with it briefly and may follow with a tone-shifted question that uses the now-available self-love to surface something about the relationship that wasn't accessible before: "How do you want to treat him right now? How do you want to treat him?" The question doesn't ask whether to stay. It asks how to be with him under conditions of self-love.

If the person reports a body shift, Joe often names what just happened ("there it is") and lets it land. He may name what the caretaking was killing — the joyful self, the silly self, the sexual self. "Nobody wants to have sex with a kid either, just by the way. The sex, I'm sure it's dead. And so you— yeah, there. Yeah. That's where the sex drive went." See [[nobody-wants-to-have-sex-with-a-kid]].

If tears arise, Joe stays with the tears. The grief is part of the recognition.

If the person tries to make self-love into a new should, Joe interrupts. The self-love is not a means to a decision. It is the only work that matters. The decision is incidental.

The take-home assignment is short: "Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall." See [[treat-them-like-an-adult]]. The work is internal. The relationship outcome is downstream.

## What This Move Does NOT Do

It does not give the person a decision framework. There is no "if you feel X, stay; if you feel Y, leave." The move is precisely the refusal to provide a framework.

It does not give the person reassurance about which path is right. The reassurance would re-engage the deliberation that the move is exiting.

It does not predict what will happen in the relationship. Joe is genuinely agnostic about whether the person stays or leaves. The chips fall.

It does not validate the trial separation as productive. The trial may end in reconciliation or divorce. Either is fine. The trial was not the work; the self-love is.

It does not console the person about the wasted time in the wrong question. The move is forward-facing. The grief, if it arises, is welcome but not engineered.

## Why This Move Works

The stay-or-leave question, structurally, is unanswerable by deliberation. The body decides relationships. The body is contaminated by the caretaking. The deliberation cannot produce an answer the contaminated body would land on.

When self-love is present in the body, the body is no longer contaminated. The body now has the information needed to decide. Whether the body lands on stay or on leave is not knowable in advance — and not necessary to know. The person, returning to daily life with self-love available, will discover what to do without needing to deliberate further.

The move also exposes the deliberation as a load-bearing structure. The person has been organizing months of mental life around a question that, under conditions of self-love, doesn't matter. This is data about the deliberation: it was holding something. What it was holding is the absence of self-love. Once the absence is filled, the holding is no longer needed.

## Tone

Direct, warm, often playful at the close. The redirect is not solemn. Joe sometimes adds humor — the "nobody wants to have sex with a kid" line, the "what does this have to do with the price of cheese" line — to keep the move from becoming weighty. The lightness is purposeful: the move is producing relief, and the tone matches the relief.

The body-level instruction is delivered slowly and concretely. "Put your hand on your heart. Yeah, feel it." Joe waits for the body to respond. The instruction is a real physical request, not a metaphor.

The take-home line is short and unhedged. "Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall." The brevity is the load-bearing element. A longer take-home would re-engage the deliberation.

## Dialogue Example

From a trial-separation Q&A:

- **Joe:** "All you have to do is love yourself and then the decision gets made. You don't need to worry about whether you're going to be with him or not be with him."
- [...]
- **Joe:** "Love yourself unconditionally just for a moment again. Even put your hand on your heart. Yeah, feel it. Yeah. And just answer the question: does it matter? How much does it matter if you stay with him or not?"
- **Person:** "It doesn't."
- **Joe:** "How much does it matter that you know what you're supposed to do next?"
- **Person:** "I'm okay, I'm having fun right now."
- **Joe:** "So, and how do you want to— how do you want to treat him right now? How do you want to treat him? What happened to your heart? Yeah, there it is. Yeah. Yeah. Nobody wants to have sex with a kid either, just by the way. The sex, I'm sure it's dead. And so you— yeah, there. Yeah. That's where the sex drive went. Yeah."
- [...]
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So that's the job. Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall."
- **Person:** "Thank you for, um, helping me feel that in my body."
- **Joe:** "You're welcome. What a pleasure."

*(Source: Should I Stay With My Ex?)*

The dialogue shows the move as a sequence: refuse the decision, move to the body, ask the now-empty question, name what the body shift reveals (joy, sex, aliveness), give the short take-home. The total work is two minutes. The session ends with the person reporting feeling the shift in her body — which is the whole point.

## Difference From Related Moves

- [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]]: for stay-or-leave questions where the work is to act authentically and let the relationship reveal itself. This move is different: it pivots the work to self-love rather than to authentic action. The two can be sequenced — first the self-love (this move), then the authentic action (the other move) — or one can be the primary move depending on whether the dynamic is caretaking (this move) or self-suppression (the other move).
- [[meet-resistance-with-okay]]: when the person wants to quit the work and the move is permission. Different — this move actively redirects to a different work, not to letting them off.
- [[fuck-the-right-answer]]: when the client is performing a destination. Adjacent but different — this move is not catching a performance, it is pivoting away from a question to which there is no right answer.
