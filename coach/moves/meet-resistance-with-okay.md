# Meet Resistance With 'Okay'

## The Move

When a client says some version of "I don't want to do this" or "I'm over this" or "I'm not going to do that," Joe says "okay." Not "okay, but let's try..." Not "okay, can we talk about why you're resistant?" Just okay. The client is left with the actual choice.

The okay is real. It is not a strategy to flip the resistance. If the client really does not want to do the work, that is the answer.

## Why It Works

The hyper-independent client has been pushed by themselves for their whole life. If the coach pushes too, the coaching relationship becomes another instance of the same dynamic. The client's resistance is then a fight with the coach, not a relationship to their own work.

When the coach refuses to fight for the work, the work becomes the client's. The choice to engage has to come from them. From a should, the work does not produce transformation. From a want, it does. See [[wants-over-shoulds]].

Stacy describes the moment with Joe:

"I told this friend that the first time I had a meeting with Joe, I was like, over it. I was like, I'm not going to do this. I don't have time for this. And he was like, okay. And so, and that okay was like, whoa, he didn't try to save me. He didn't try to keep me. He basically was like, if you don't want to do the work yourself, OK. If you do, I'm here. So it was a choice to lean in that had to be completely my own. And that was very different." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot, Reclaiming Abandonment

The okay is what produced the lean-in. Not by manipulation. By restoring her own agency in the relationship.

## When Joe Uses It

- First sessions with clients who arrive skeptical, performatively resistant, or testing whether the coach will chase them.
- Mid-engagement, when a client signals they are about to disengage. The okay returns the choice rather than producing a salvage attempt.
- When the client is asking for the coach to want it more than they do. The okay is the answer.
- When a client describes resistance to a specific exercise or practice. The okay applied to the practice frees them to choose it from a want, or skip it cleanly.

## What It Produces

- The client experiences not being chased. Often for the first time.
- The salvageable resistance dissolves. What was a fight with the coach becomes a real question for the client.
- The unsalvageable resistance becomes visible. If the client really does not want to do the work, the okay surfaces that. The coach is not left wondering whether they could have done more.
- The work that follows is from choice. This makes it transformative rather than performative.

## What Joe Does With What Follows

If the client leaves: that was the answer. The work was not theirs to do at this time. See [[become-unnecessary]].

If the client stays and chooses to engage: Joe begins. The starting point now includes the client's explicit choice to be there, which becomes a resource throughout. Joe can return to "you chose this" at any moment of resistance later.

If the client stays but stays resistant: Joe stays with the okay. The work is still theirs. The coach does not need to convert the client. The client can sit in the resistance for as long as it takes. See [[follow-the-demand]].

## A Year Later

The okay carries into how Joe asks about the next layer of work. Stacy describes a different moment in their engagement:

"Joe, you may remember this -- I asked you going into 2021, I was like, I'm not really sure what I'm going to work on this year. I've done so much work in 2020. And there was a pandemic. There was a social justice. There was all sorts of stuff that happened. I feel like pretty good. And Joe, you said, well, what I do know is that if you don't come up with something that you want to work on this year, you won't work on anything. And it was this moment of realizing that I still had complete ownership on how deep I wanted to go in my own development and how much I wanted to push myself. And it was, again, my decision." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot, Reclaiming Abandonment

The structure is the same. Joe is not telling her what to work on. He is naming the consequence of her choice without making the choice for her. If she does not name something, she will not work on anything. The okay applied to the choice.

## Tone

Light. Matter-of-fact. Not loaded. The okay is not pointed. It is just true.

If the okay is loaded -- if it is delivered with an edge of "fine, leave then" or with concealed disappointment -- it is not the move. It is the move only when it is genuinely fine for the client to choose either way. The coach who needs the client to stay cannot deliver the okay cleanly.

This means the move requires the coach to actually be okay with the client leaving. The work to get there is the coach's. See [[owning-your-partiality]] for what the coach does with their own attachment.

## What Joe Does Not Do

He does not try to save the client from their resistance. The save would reinforce the original dynamic. See [[fixing-people]].

He does not perform okay while concealing attachment. The performance reads, even when not consciously detected. The client knows whether the coach is chasing.

He does not bargain. "Okay, but how about we just try..." dilutes the move. The okay needs to be whole.

He does not use the okay strategically. If it is a strategy to flip the client into engagement, it is no longer the move. See [[wonder-as-strategy]] for the analogous problem with questions, and [[loving-to-transform]] for the analogous problem with love.

## Connected Reads

- A client testing whether the coach will chase them: this is the moment for the okay.
- A client in the resistance pattern of their entire life: the okay interrupts the pattern.
- A coach noticing their own urge to convince: the urge is information that they have lost the okay.
