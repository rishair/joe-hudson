# Question the Caveat

## The Move

When a person uses an unnecessary qualifier word, do not engage the content of the sentence. Engage the word. Ask why the modifier is there.

The shape:

- "What would make you just not say [the plain version]?"
- "What makes you give the caveat of [the qualifier]?"
- "What's the difference between [what they said] and [the plain version]?"

The move is not to argue that the qualifier is wrong. It is to surface the work the qualifier is doing.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the read [[linguistic-caveat]] is active: a modifier word is doing real work in protecting the speaker from their own statement.
- Early in a session, often within the first few exchanges, when the person is still mostly in their head.
- When the conversation has been at the abstract level and the person uses a careful word that signals the bracing.
- When other intellectual approaches would let the person stay above the material.

It is not used to play language police. The move is only worth making when the qualifier is at the precise spot where the speaker is avoiding feeling.

## What It Produces

Almost always: the speaker discovers a specific self-judgment they had not named. The qualifier was protecting against the criticism. Surfacing the qualifier surfaces the criticism.

In the Brett session, the question "what makes you give the caveat of attentionally?" produced: "I think I have a little bit of a self-judgment around my attention." The self-judgment was the door into the whole pattern. Without questioning the qualifier, the conversation would have stayed at "yeah, I disappear into things" — a description of the pattern rather than contact with the felt material underneath.

## Dialogue Example

- **Brett:** "Disappearing attentionally from other areas of my life."
- **Joe:** "What makes you say it disappearing intentionally?"
- **Brett:** "No, not intentionally, attentionally."
- **Joe:** "Right, what would make you just not say I was disappearing into it? What makes you give the caveat of attentionally?"
- **Brett:** "I think I have a little bit of a self-judgment around my attention."
- *(Source: Allowing Yourself to Change)*

The mishearing in turn two is incidental. The move was made by holding the question on the qualifier.

## Variations

- For "kind of" / "sort of": "What if you took out the 'kind of'?" or "What would it be if it were not kind of?"
- For "a part of me": "What if it's just you, not a part of you?"
- For "I guess": "What if you weren't guessing? What would you actually say?"
- For "just": "Just? Why just?"

The variation is always toward removing the qualifier and asking what is underneath the protection.

## Tone

Light, curious, almost casual. Not a gotcha. The energy is "huh, why that word?" rather than "caught you." The lightness is what allows the speaker to look at the word without defending.

## What Not to Do

Do not turn this into a vocabulary lesson. Do not catalog every qualifier the person uses. Pick one. Make the move once. Stay with what surfaces.

Do not use this when the person is already in deep feeling. The move pulls toward the head briefly. Used at the wrong moment, it interrupts something more important.
