# Name the Gap Between the Felt Story and the Lived Evidence

## The Move

The person reports a chronic felt-state (unsafety, scarcity, not-enough, can't-do-it, no-one-loves-me). The body believes the report. The life they have actually been living tells a different story. The move is to lay the two side by side and ask the person to locate the seam.

"Yeah, here's the weird thing, man. The weird thing is you freeze around money, but you always have enough of it. Yeah, you always feel unsafe, but yet somehow you always figure out how to be safe. You feel unsafe, but yet you're safe enough to come here and have this conversation with me in front of all these folks." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Money Blocked?

Then the question:

"So where's the story end and the truth begin in this? Like, how does that — what's the truth about your safety?"

Three observations, one question. The three observations come from the person's own life. The question hands the discrimination back to them.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a person's felt-state is chronic and absolute ("I always feel unsafe," "I never have enough," "nobody loves me") and their life history contains continuous counter-evidence.
- When the felt-story is operating as a fact rather than as a felt-experience the person can examine.
- When the person has been arguing with the felt-state as if the felt-state were the whole truth — and what they need is to see that the felt-state is one truth running alongside another.
- After the felt-state has been brought into the room (see [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]]) and the person has touched it. The contradiction lands harder once the felt-state has been admitted into the present moment.
- Before [[surface-the-rejection-then-demand-its-evidence]] when the projection is moral; after it when the projection is about felt-capacity.

## What It Produces

- **A small fracture in the absoluteness of the felt-state.** The person had been operating "I feel unsafe" as a complete report. The move makes audible that the report is partial: the unsafety is real, and so is the safety the person has been continuously producing.
- **A both-and that the body can hold.** The "and" is the operative word: "you freeze around money, *and* you always have enough of it." Neither is denied. The new piece of information is that they coexist.
- **Permission for the person to be the one who decides where the seam is.** Joe does not declare which part is "the truth." He asks. The person's answer is usually halting at first ("That feels like it's part of the problem. Feels fragmented") and the halting is itself the data — the person had not yet looked at their own life as a source of counter-evidence.
- **A pivot toward feeling the felt-state from inside the contradiction.** Once the both-and is named, the person can be invited to feel the unsafety while holding the truth of their actual safety. See [[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]] for the move that follows.

## What This Move Is NOT

- It is not [[i-dont-buy-your-story]]. That move flatly refuses the claim. This move accepts the claim and adds the counter-evidence. The person's experience is not being dismissed; it is being placed alongside what their life has been demonstrating.
- It is not [[surface-the-rejection-then-demand-its-evidence]]. That move works on moral projections ("money is dirty") by testing them against the people in the room. This move works on chronic felt-states ("I feel unsafe") by testing them against the person's own continuous life. Different mechanism, different evidence base.
- It is not reassurance. Joe is not saying "you are actually safe, so you don't need to worry." He is naming a structural contradiction in the report and asking the person to look at it. The unsafety stays real; what changes is the person's relationship to it as the only truth.
- It is not argumentation. The observations are offered, not pressed. The question that follows is genuine.

## The Sequence

1. **Three observations from the person's life.** Joe gives them in series, each one a piece of counter-evidence to the felt-story. The series matters because one observation can be dismissed; three in a row are harder to wave away.
   - The first observation: the chronic behavior ("you freeze around money").
   - The second observation: the outcome that contradicts the implied catastrophe ("but you always have enough of it").
   - The third observation: the live demonstration in the room ("you're safe enough to come here and have this conversation in front of all these folks").

2. **The discrimination question.** "Where's the story end and the truth begin in this?" The framing matters. Not "what's the truth?" (which sounds like Joe knows the answer). Not "isn't the story wrong?" (which would push the person to defend the story). The phrasing assumes that there is a seam and asks the person to find it.

3. **The follow-up specification.** "What's the truth about your safety?" The general discrimination is followed by the specific instance. The person now has both the structural prompt and the specific topic to apply it to.

4. **Wait.** The person's answer will often be partial or fragmented at first. The fragmentation is the work — the body is reorganizing how it holds the felt-state in light of the counter-evidence. Joe does not fill the silence.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says "it feels fragmented" or "I don't know":** Joe takes this as data, not as a stopping point. The fragmentation IS the felt-experience of the both-and being held for the first time. The next move is usually [[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]] — have the person re-answer from inside the felt unsafety, which lets the unsafety speak its truth without being denied by the lived-evidence.
- **If the person tries to defend the absoluteness of the felt-state:** Joe stays with the observations. He does not retract them. The person's defense is the system protecting the felt-story. Joe lets the defense be there and stays with what the life shows.
- **If the person has a flash of recognition:** Joe lets it land. He does not over-explain. The recognition is the person's own; it does not need to be reinforced from the outside.
- **If the person flips into self-attack ("oh, so I've been making it up this whole time"):** Joe redirects. The point was not that the felt-state is fake. The point is that there are two truths operating, and one of them has been doing more work than it deserves. The self-attack is the system trying to make the recognition into a new shame.

## Why It Works

Two mechanisms in parallel.

1. **The body cannot easily defend a felt-story against the body's own history.** The unsafety claim has been operating because nothing has been admitted into evidence against it. The body has been treating the felt-unsafety as the report and ignoring its own continuous safety as background. Naming the safety as foreground — as data — changes what the body has to do with the unsafety. The unsafety has to make room for the fact that the person has been safe enough to live a whole life. The room-making is the loosening.

2. **The person becomes their own witness.** Joe is not asserting that the person is safe. He is pointing at what the person already knows. The recognition lands as the person's own observation about their own life, which is more durable than a coach's reassurance. Coach-reassurance produces a temporary lift and then is forgotten. Self-witnessing produces a structural shift in how the felt-story is held.

The combination is what makes the move portable. The person can return to it outside the session whenever the felt-state arises: "the unsafety is here, and I am also still safe enough to be having this thought." The both-and becomes the structural form the felt-state is held in, rather than the felt-state being the only thing in the room.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person (about money):** "I... I would guess that I don't feel like I deserve it."
- **Joe:** *(after surfacing safety as the underneath of the deserving claim, after the person admits trying to hold it back)* "Yeah, here's the weird thing, man. The weird thing is you freeze around money, but you always have enough of it. Yeah, you always feel unsafe, but yet somehow you always figure out how to be safe. You feel unsafe, but yet you're safe enough to come here and have this conversation with me in front of all these folks."
- **Joe:** "So where's the story end and the truth begin in this? Like, how does that — what's the truth about your safety?"
- **Person:** "That feels like it's part of the problem. Feels fragmented. So, well, for sure, at the same time."
- **Joe:** "Beautiful. So answer that question, but this time answer it from while deeply in touch with that feeling of lack of safety. And let me just — while you're there, I'm just going to ask, what's the truth about your safety?"
- *(Source: Why Am I Money Blocked?)*

The move took roughly ninety seconds. The both-and is now operating. The person's next answer comes from inside the felt unsafety while holding the lived safety — see [[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]].

## Tone

Light, slightly amused, conspiratorial. "Here's the weird thing, man" is the operative framing. The observations are offered as something curious rather than as a charge. The person is invited into the curiosity rather than asked to defend.

The lightness is essential. If the observations are delivered with weight, the person reads them as attack and defends the felt-state. With lightness, the person reads them as noticing and joins the noticing. The same content with different tone produces opposite results.

The question "where's the story end and the truth begin?" carries genuine inquiry. Joe does not know exactly where the seam is in this person's life. The question is real. The genuineness is what allows the person to look.

## The Moment-to-Moment Variation

The move generalizes from "felt-story vs. life-history-evidence" to "identity-story vs. the moment-just-passed." A specific compressed version: the person identifies as unhappy/depressed/anxious as a fixed condition, and a moment of relief just passed without being registered. The gap to name is between the persistent identity claim and the single moment of felt-data the person just produced.

The canonical demonstration: an 18-year-old depressed person, after Joe's loving-instruction, was suddenly happy and smiling. Joe stopped and walked the loop:

- **Joe:** "Let's just look at the thing. What just happened is you hung out, you loved this aspect of yourself. Before, you weren't happy. Now you're happy."
- **Person:** "Uh, wow. Oh my goodness. You are right. You're absolutely right."
- **Joe:** "So there's also something happening. You have this story in your head about how you're unhappy that persists even if you're happy and on a moment-to-moment basis. So there's moments where you're happy, but you're like, no, I'm unhappy. I'm an unhappy person. This is what I do. I'm unhappy. But then you don't, you're not noticing the actual moments of happiness."
- *(Source: This is How To Love Yourself)*

The structural pattern is the same as the life-history variant — chronic felt-state on one side, contradicting evidence on the other, gap to be located. But the timescale is compressed: the evidence is not a continuous life-pattern; it is the single moment that just occurred and was overridden by the identity claim. The instruction "you're not noticing the actual moments of happiness" is the gap-naming applied to the moment-by-moment time scale.

This variation is particularly useful with [[im-depressed|depression-presenting clients]] who treat depression as a fixed identity and unconsciously override the felt-data of moments of relief. The move makes the identity-override visible. The person can then watch the override happen in real time and choose differently. See [[story-of-suffering-persists-over-moments-of-relief]] for the read this variation responds to.

## Variations

The move generalizes wherever a chronic felt-state coexists with a continuous life-evidence contradiction:

- **For "nobody loves me":** "You say nobody loves you, and you've been married for twenty years, and your sister calls you every week, and the friend in this room is here because they wanted to be with you tonight. Where's the story end and the truth begin?"
- **For "I'm not capable":** "You say you're not capable, and you raised three kids, and you ran the office for fifteen years, and you handled the move alone last year. Where's the story end and the truth begin?"
- **For "nothing ever works out":** "Nothing ever works out, and you have a job and a home and people who show up for you and a body that is here. Where's the story end and the truth begin?"
- **For "I can't be alone":** "You say you can't be alone, and you've been alone every night this month, and you're alone right now in this conversation in the sense that nobody else is in your room. Where's the story end and the truth begin?"

The structure is the same: list the chronic felt-claim, list multiple pieces of life-evidence that contradict it, ask the person to find the seam.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not deliver the observations as proof.** They are not proof. They are counter-evidence that has to be admitted into the person's awareness. If the move is delivered with the energy of "see, you're wrong," the person will defend. If it is delivered with the energy of "here are some other things that are also true," the person can take them in.
- **Do not skip the discrimination question.** Just naming the contradiction without inviting the person to look at the seam leaves the person at the contradiction. The question is what gives them the work to do.
- **Do not insist on a specific answer.** "Where's the story end and the truth begin?" is genuinely open. If the person says "I don't know," that is a real answer. The not-knowing is part of the loosening.
- **Do not use the move to dismiss the felt-state.** The unsafety is real. The not-deserving feeling is real. The not-loved feeling is real. The move adds a both-and, it does not subtract the original feeling. If the person experiences the move as dismissal, the work undoes itself.
- **Do not use the move when the lived evidence is not actually contradictory.** Sometimes the felt-state matches the life. The person who says "I'm broke" and is in fact broke does not need this move. This move is for the gap between the felt and the lived. If there is no gap, the move has nothing to do.

- A person reports a chronic felt-state as if it were a complete report on their life
- The person's life history contains continuous counter-evidence to the felt-state
- The felt-state has been operating as a fact rather than as a felt-experience
- The person is asking how to fix the felt-state at the level of the felt-state, rather than examining whether the felt-state is the whole story
- See [[how-do-i-create-wealth-and-financial-stability]] for the money-and-safety presentation; the move is one of Joe's first responses when the lived evidence and the felt-story are visibly in contradiction
- See [[i-feel-stuck]] and [[familiar-over-safe]] when the felt-stuckness is the identification; the move surfaces that the person is not as stuck as the identification claims

- Toward [[familiar-over-safe]] when the move surfaces that the felt-state is part of an identification that has been doing safety work
