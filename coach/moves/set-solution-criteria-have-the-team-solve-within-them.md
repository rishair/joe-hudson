# Set the Solution Criteria, Then Have the Team Solve Within Them

## The Move

When delegating, the leader identifies and owns the criteria the solution must meet. The team identifies and owns the solution itself, within those criteria. The two roles are separate and both real.

The leader's role: define and protect the criteria. Sales cycle has to be under ten months. We have to identify an ideal customer profile. The story has to be differentiated in the market. The team's role: come up with a solution that meets every criterion the leader has named, augment or debate the criteria when they have reason to, and execute on the resulting plan.

"The team's job is to solve a problem within your solution criteria and to augment, improve, or debate the solution criteria. But it's the team's job is not to determine, not to be the end-all be-all of what the solution criteria is." -- Joe Hudson

The move is the practical answer to the binary that traps most leaders: either I tell them exactly what to do (micromanagement) or I leave them alone and hope (no oversight). The third option is to be specific about the constraints while leaving the design space open. The leader is precise about the what and the why; the team is sovereign over the how.

## Why the Binary Fails

The default delegation pattern: leader either issues a complete plan or hands off the problem entirely. Both fail.

When the leader issues the plan:

"There's 3 problems with that. The first problem is they might not agree with you and then they're not gonna do it. The second thing is that the best way to get it done for you is not the best way to get it done for your team, meaning that they might have other strengths and weaknesses and their way of doing it for them will be better, or they believe in it more and therefore it's gonna have a better execution. And the third problem with it is that you are not taking into consideration all the details that they understand about that part of the work, right? So you're on high, you have one, viewpoint, they have another viewpoint. And if you're not taking both viewpoints into consideration, then it's probably not the best solution." -- Joe Hudson

Three failures stacked: agreement (they don't), alignment of method to executor (yours isn't theirs), and information (they know things you don't). The leader's plan is one viewpoint masquerading as the whole picture.

When the leader hands off entirely:

"A lot of problems when people delegate, when they say, okay, I need you to get this done. Well, they think these 3 things are important. You think these other 3 things are important. They go about it executing on these 3 things. You execute on these other 3 things. Nobody knows what's what. And then you go and you say, this isn't what I wanted, but nobody would have particularly known that." -- Joe Hudson

The hand-off without criteria produces work that does not match the leader's actual want. The team did exactly what they thought was being asked. The leader never made the criteria visible. The mismatch is the leader's failure, not the team's.

The solution-criteria move solves both failures. The team gets the agency to design (which produces buy-in, leverages their information, and matches the method to the executor). The leader gets the criteria respected (which ensures the result meets the actual want).

## The Three Components

**1. Name what the solution must meet.** The criteria are specific. "Replicable sales" is not enough. "A sales cycle of under ten months, an identified ideal customer profile, and a differentiated market story" is enough. Each criterion is something the leader can hold the solution against and say yes or no.

**2. Hand the design problem to the team.** Once the criteria are clear, the leader stops designing. The question moves from "here's how we'll do it" to "okay, how are we going to get it done?" The team takes the question and produces options.

**3. Reserve the right to decide on the criteria; leave the solution to the team.** The team can debate the criteria, propose new ones, push back on the existing ones. The leader engages those debates as the legitimate work of refining the constraint set. But the leader holds the final say on which criteria the solution has to meet. On the solution itself, the team holds the say. The roles do not cross.

"So I want — we need to solve the sales, replicable sales, replicable revenue, replicable revenue. We need to solve that. And what I know is that it has to sell. We need a sales cycle of under 10 months. What I know is, is that we have to identify an ICP, our ideal customer profile. What I know is, is that we, that we need to be differentiated in the market. The story needs to be differentiated in the market, whatever those things are that you know. And those are solution criteria. Now, you can debate the solution criteria, but at the end of the day, you get to choose what the solution criteria are." -- Joe Hudson

## Where the Buy-In Comes From

The move produces real buy-in because the team did the work. They wrestled with the criteria. They proposed options. They ran into the constraints and revised. The solution that comes out the other side is something they own, because they made it.

"The way that you create alignment isn't by convincing as much as it is by handling objections. You want the team's objections. You want those objections to improve the solution. And then that creates the alignment." -- Joe Hudson

The leader who issued a complete plan and then convinced the team got compliance at best. The leader who set the criteria and let the team solve gets commitment. The difference is who the solution belongs to.

"If they have to wrestle with it, if they have to say, okay, here's the solution criteria. No, this is the wrong solution criteria. No, I don't like this. I like this. And they debate it. And they've contributed to the solution, then they're bought in. If they've gotten to express all their doubts and had them listened to and reflected and solved, then they're far more going to buy into the solution and the execution of it." -- Joe Hudson

The wrestling is not a stylistic preference. It is the structural mechanism that converts compliance into ownership. Skip the wrestling, you get a team marching to a plan they will forget in five minutes.

## The Forgetting Test

A diagnostic. If the team has been told the plan and "agreed" without wrestling, they will forget the plan within minutes of leaving the meeting. The agreement was a social formality, not an internalization. The team will revert to what they actually believe, which may or may not align with the plan.

The wrestling makes the plan stick because the team's body has been through the design process. They know why each piece is the way it is. They have considered and discarded alternatives. The plan is now their plan, and they execute it as their plan.

"You might've even convinced them, but it wasn't alive in them. They hadn't done the work, right? They hadn't actually lived the experience. They hadn't gotten to the conclusion themselves." -- Joe Hudson

Alive-in-them is the standard. The wrestling is what produces aliveness.

## Why This Is Not Abdication

The leader who sets the criteria has done the harder strategic work, not the easier. Naming the criteria requires the leader to know what actually matters. Most leaders who default to issuing plans do so because issuing the plan is easier than naming the criteria. The plan can be vague-but-feel-specific. The criteria force the leader to articulate what the plan is actually for.

The leader also has to be willing to hold the criteria when the team pushes against them. Some pushes are legitimate (the criterion needs revision) and some are not (the team is trying to dodge a constraint that has to hold). The leader has to judge which is which and hold the line where the line needs to hold.

The leadership is in the criteria-setting and criteria-defending. The leader who said "go figure it out" without criteria abdicated. The leader who set criteria, defended them under push, and let the team solve inside them led.

## What This Pairs With

The move pairs with [[whats-the-case-against-it]]. After the team proposes a solution within the criteria, the leader can ask what speaks against it. The criteria-defining move and the speaks-against move together produce a solution that has been shaped by both the leader's view of the whole and the team's view of the details.

The move pairs with [[no-doctrine-leadership]]. The team's objections to the criteria, and to each other's proposed solutions, are the nos that refine the work. The leader's posture toward those nos determines whether the move produces real wrestling or performed agreement.

The move pairs with [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]]. The criteria are an articulation of the leader's want for the work. Naming them clearly is the leader's responsibility, not their imposition. The team needs to know what the leader is wanting in order to solve for it.

## When the Move Fits

The move fits when:

- The leader has clarity on what matters but does not have clarity on how to do it
- The team has expertise in the doing that the leader does not have
- The work is complex enough that no single viewpoint can design the whole solution
- The leader has the discipline to articulate criteria rather than issue plans
- The team has the maturity to engage criteria as work rather than as obstacles

The move does not fit when:

- The leader actually does have a complete plan and is using "let's set criteria" as a way to avoid owning it (the team will detect this)
- The team lacks the skill or experience to solve within the criteria and needs more direct guidance to develop the capacity
- The criteria themselves are not yet known to the leader (the move requires the leader to have done the upstream strategic work)
- The team has been trained for years to wait for instructions and the criteria-handoff produces paralysis rather than design

## Tone

Direct, structural, and operational. Joe presents this as a craft of leadership, not a leadership style. There is a right way to delegate that produces results, and a wrong way that produces friction. The move is the right way described in mechanical detail.

When the leader runs the move with the team, the tone is collaborative on the solution and clear on the criteria. "Here is what the solution has to do. Now let's figure out how." The first sentence holds the line; the second opens the field.
