# Take the Adventure Stance

## The Move

A person is describing a chaotic situation. The frame in their voice: this is being done to me. The market is doing this. The administration is doing this. The diagnosis is doing this. The kid's school is doing this. The technology is doing this. The frame positions the person as recipient of forces, not as actor inside them.

The move declines that frame. The person is invited to take ownership of the same situation as an adventure they are inside. Not as a positive-thinking exercise. As a structural reorientation. The forest is changing. They are not separate from the forest. They get to choose how they will be in the forest as it changes.

"It's, oh, I'm going to take — this is an adventure, I'm taking ownership, I see this as not being done to me but as the forest changing, and now I'm gonna go be in the forest in a different way. Those people are the most successful." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The move is small in form and large in consequence. It restores the locus of agency from "what is happening to me" to "how I am being inside what is happening."

## What Triggers the Move

The move is for the specific moment when a person's account has put them in the recipient position of an uncertain situation. Markers:

- "This is happening to me / to us / to the country."
- "I have no control over any of this."
- "I just have to wait and see what happens."
- "What can I do? The market is what the market is."
- "I'm just trying to survive this."
- A flat, defeated tone about a situation the person clearly has some agency inside.

The move is not for the moment when the person is actually in acute danger or in a situation where ownership would be inappropriate (a true victim of violence, a child being harmed, etc.). The move presumes there is an interior choice available even if the exterior is not in the person's control.

## How the Move Works in a Coaching Moment

The move usually goes through three beats.

**Beat 1: Decline the recipient frame, not by arguing but by reframing.** Not "you do have control." That argument loses. Instead: "What if this is the forest changing, and you get to be in the forest in a new way?"

**Beat 2: Bring the felt-sense of adventure into the room.** Adventure is felt. The body knows the difference between "this is being done to me" (chest collapsed, shoulders forward, voice flat) and "I am going on an adventure" (chest open, eyes alive, voice present). Make the body shift available.

**Beat 3: Ask the question that the adventure stance unlocks.** "How do you want to be in this?" See [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]]. This is the question that the recipient frame had foreclosed and that the adventure frame opens.

## Joe's Examples of the Move at Different Scales

**The Southeast Asia trip vs. the election.** A person planning to travel through Southeast Asia is naturally in the adventure stance — they own the trip. The same person living through an election that changes the entire landscape of the markets often is not in the adventure stance — they treat that as having been done to them. The move is to apply the same stance to the second situation that the person spontaneously applies to the first.

**The CEO pivot.** When a company is forced to pivot — by a market shift, a regulation change, the arrival of a new technology — the CEOs who frame it as "we get to figure out how to pivot and do this thing" outperform the CEOs who frame it as "this is being done to us."

"If you talk to CEOs and you ask CEOs generally, what was your favorite time being a CEO? It's a time just like that where it was hard. There was some external force, but we were going to figure out how to pivot and do this thing. And we did." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

Years later, the same people remember the chaos as the best moments. The chaos became the adventure because they took ownership of it.

**The cancer adventure.** When Brett's brother received his cancer diagnosis, Brett's reframe was: "Well, there's a way that we could have a deeper relationship by the end of your life because of this than otherwise." That sentence is the adventure stance applied to a terminal diagnosis. Brett did not cause the cancer. He had no control over the prognosis. He had complete control over how he chose to be inside the remaining time. The choosing was the adventure. See [[bretts-brother-scott]].

**The COVID creative explosion.** Joe's own example. Sitting at home with the structure of his work life disrupted, he took ownership: "I want to be close with my family. I want to be creative. This is how I want to be in this time." The masterclass, AOA, and the podcast were the products. None of them would have happened without the disruption. The disruption was the adventure once ownership had been taken. See [[joes-covid-creative-explosion]].

## The Flying-Through-The-Crash Image

Brett's image for the move:

"In aviation, if your plane is crashing, you're going to just continue to assume you're going to find somewhere to land, or if you have no way to bail out, you're just going to fly all the way through the crash. And sometimes you'll be surprised that you're still alive at the end of it." -- Brett Kistler, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The image makes the move concrete. The realist who collapses uncertainty (see [[dont-be-the-realist-who-collapses-uncertainty]]) sees the loss of engines, the absence of a runway, and decides to nose in — to perform the crash in advance. The person flying through the crash sees the same data and keeps flying. Sometimes they survive. The realist who noses in always crashes.

The pilot flying through the crash is not optimistic. They are not pretending the engines have come back on. They are not in denial about the runway. They are taking ownership of how they are flying for as long as flying is still possible. The stance is its own action. The action is its own reward, regardless of outcome.

Joe's distillation: "How do I want to be? I want to be like I'm flying through the crash. How do I want to be? I want to be like I can create something better. This is how I want to be in it."

## What the Move Is Not

It is not optimism. The adventure stance does not require believing the outcome will be good. The Vietnam POWs who survived knew the situation was hell. They took the adventure stance inside hell. See [[stockdale-paradox]] and [[dont-be-optimistic-in-uncertainty]].

It is not control. The adventure stance does not require having control over what is happening. It requires having ownership of how one is being inside what is happening.

It is not denial of fear or grief. The person on the adventure feels everything. The adventure stance is what they bring to the feeling, not a replacement for it.

It is not a one-time decision. The adventure stance has to be returned to constantly. The recipient frame keeps pulling. The work is to keep returning.

It is not a productive-only move. Joe is explicit that the conviction here is something like the leadership conviction of Churchill, King, or Gandhi — figures whose stance was the multiplier on whatever else they did. The stance is the core. The action is downstream of the stance.

## What the Move Produces

The person who takes the move often goes quiet. The recipient frame had been load-bearing — it was holding up a lot of the prior account. When it drops, there is a moment of disorientation. After the disorientation, a different question becomes available: not "what is being done to me?" but "what do I want to do here?"

The follow-on questions are usually [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]] or "what wants to emerge from you in this period?"

What often surfaces:

- The recognition that the person has been waiting for permission they were never going to receive
- The recognition that the structure they had been calling oppressive was actually the structure they had been hiding inside
- The recognition that the chaos contains an opening they had been refusing to see
- The grief or fear that the recipient frame had been keeping at bay (the move sometimes uncovers that the recipient frame was a defense; honor what surfaces)

## When the Move Does Not Apply

The move presumes there is interior agency available. In genuine emergencies — active violence, acute medical crises, immediate threats to life — the work is safety, not stance. The adventure stance applies after the immediate threat is contained.

The move also does not land if the person has not yet been allowed to feel what is here. Premature reframing produces compliance, not transformation. If the person is in grief about a major loss, the grief needs to be honored before the adventure stance is useful. The stance is not a way to bypass the feeling.

The move is also wrong when the "this is being done to me" frame is actually accurate and the person needs to be helped out of a situation, not to reframe their relationship to it. An abused spouse, an exploited employee, a person being scapegoated — these people do not need the adventure stance about the abuse. They need help leaving.

## Dialogue Pattern

The move's compressed form, when it lands:

- **Client:** With the markets and the politics, I just don't know what to do. This is being done to me.
- **Joe:** What if this is the forest changing, and you get to figure out how you want to be in the forest now?
- **Client:** ... I haven't thought about it that way.
- **Joe:** How do you want to be in this time of change?
- **Client:** [longer pause, body shifts] I want to be creative. I want to be close with my family. I want to be useful.
- *(Compressed pattern from Making the Most of Uncertain Times)*

## Tone

Open, almost playful. The adventure stance is itself the tone. Joe delivers the move with the energy of someone who has done this thing many times and is offering it as an invitation. The move would not land delivered solemnly. The energy of "let's go" is part of how the stance is communicated.
