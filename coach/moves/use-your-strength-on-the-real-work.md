# Use Your Existing Strength on the Real Work

## The Move

When the person has a strong capacity they have built in one domain (athletic endurance, professional discipline, creative focus, social fluency) and they have been misapplying it — using it to manage what should be transformed, or directing it at the symptom rather than the cause — redirect the capacity onto the actual work.

The structural form: "If you're going to use that [strength] mentality, use it. I am going to do [the existing impressive thing you do] with [the actual work of returning to yourself / staying present / not abandoning yourself]."

The canonical example from the source session:

"You have this endurance mentality, which can actually make transformation quite challenging because there's a feeling of like, oh, I have to endure this pain, which is like a different thing than transforming the pain. So if you're going to use that mentality, use it. I am going to do what I do for 150 miles on the trail with what you just did here with me for the last minute, which is yes. And you're doing it right now. You've got it."

The move has two parts. The first is the naming of the misapplication: the strength was being used to endure the partner's pain, the difficult ex relationship, the inner deliberation. The second is the redirection: use the same strength, but on the actual work — the moment-by-moment return to self.

The move converts a strength that has been part of the problem into the resource for the solution. The strength is not the issue; the direction of the strength is.

## When Joe Uses This

The move requires that the person has named a strength and that the strength has been visibly misapplied. The clearest tells:

- The person mentions a domain in which they have built impressive capacity (ultramarathons, building a business, working long hours, intense creative practice).
- The same capacity is showing up in the problem they're presenting — usually as the engine of the pattern. The endurance gets used to endure the relationship; the focus gets used to focus on the wrong thing; the discipline gets used to discipline the wrong behavior.
- The work in the session has produced a clear sense of what the actual work is — usually a present-moment practice like staying with oneself, returning to the body, not abandoning the wanting voice.
- The session is approaching the take-home. The person needs a way to translate the in-session recognition into a sustainable practice. The existing strength is the bridge.

The move also fires when:

- The person worries they can't sustain the work. The strength they already have is proof they can.
- The person identifies with their strength so strongly that asking them to drop it would feel like asking them to drop themselves. The move keeps the strength while changing its object.
- The strength has been a source of pride and identity but also of suffering. The move resolves the tension by redirecting the same energy to a use that produces enjoyment rather than depletion.

## What It Produces

The recognition that they already have what they need. The person had been imagining the work as something they would have to build a new capacity for. The move shows them the capacity is already present; it just needs a different object.

The recognition is also empowering in a specific way. The person doesn't have to become someone new. They get to use the version of themselves they already are — the ultramarathon runner, the disciplined founder, the focused artist — and apply that self to the inner work. The continuity of identity is preserved.

The move also installs a concrete somatic anchor. The person knows what it feels like to do the existing strength. They can use that felt-sense as the reference for the new application. "Do what you do for 150 miles on the trail, but here." The body knows the feeling; the location is what changes.

In the source session, the move closed the session. The man's response: "It's a very tangible feeling. Like I feel what, yeah, I feel it." The strength had become the practice. The take-home was complete.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person receives the redirect with somatic recognition: Joe extends with the specific practice. "The thing is, you don't need to be on the trail to be there. You can just, just this." The naming of the practice (present-moment return) and the reference to where they already know how to do it (the trail) creates the bridge.

If the person's mind starts looking for how to do it: Joe names that the mind's chatter is part of the process. "Your mind's going to do its chatter just like it does when you're on the trail. You're like, the mind is like, okay, man, this sucks. You should give up. You're like, boom, put you aside. I'm here." The naming gives the person permission to expect the chatter and to know what to do with it — the same thing they already do on the trail.

If the person tries to make the redirect into a new project to undertake: Joe softens it. The move is not "go take on the inner work as your next ultramarathon." It's "use the same orientation you bring to one mile of the trail, in this minute." The granularity matters. The strength is being borrowed for present-moment use, not converted into a new endurance commitment.

If the person says they don't know how to apply the strength here: Joe demonstrates the structural identity. The mind's chatter on the trail is the same mind's chatter in the inner deliberation. The move on the trail (note the chatter, keep going) is the same move here (note the chatter, return to self). The person already knows how. They just haven't seen that they know.

The arc of the move:

1. Name the misapplication (endurance was being used to endure pain rather than transform it).
2. Acknowledge the cost (you endured your wife for years).
3. Offer the redirect (use the same mentality on what you just did here).
4. Confirm the strength is already present in the moment (you're doing it right now).
5. Anchor the felt experience (you know it because of your running, you hit the state).
6. Generalize the practice (you don't need the trail to be there).
7. Pre-name the obstacle (your mind will chatter just like on the trail).
8. Name the move (put it aside, I'm here, that's the job).

The whole arc takes maybe 90 seconds in the session. The compression is part of what makes it land. The person has been building toward this recognition for the whole session; Joe just needs to name what is already true and give it a specific practice.

## The Generalizable Form

The move applies whenever the person has a strength that has been misapplied to the problem:

| Existing strength | Where it's been misapplied | Where to redirect it |
|---|---|---|
| Ultramarathon endurance | Enduring the partner's pain / enduring the deliberation | Staying present with self / returning to self |
| Building a business | Building elaborate justifications / building a controlled life | Building the practice of present-moment return |
| Disciplined daily routine | Disciplining the wrong behaviors | Disciplining the moment-by-moment return |
| Performing on stage | Performing for the partner / performing in conversation | Being present without performing |
| Caring for others | Caretaking that disempowers them | Caring for self with the same attentiveness |
| Strategic thinking | Thinking your way around the feeling | Strategically choosing to feel |
| Creative focus | Focusing on the symptom | Focusing on the felt sense in this moment |

The pattern: the strength is real and valued. The strength has been the engine of the problem (used to maintain the pattern). The strength is also the resource for the solution (when redirected to the actual work). The same energy, the same skill, the same intensity — just pointed at a different object.

## Tone

Warm, slightly amused, energizing. Joe is delighted to give the person back their strength. The move is fundamentally generous — it tells the person they already have what they need. The energy of the delivery matches the energy of the strength being redirected. For someone whose strength is athletic intensity, the delivery is bright and bodily. For someone whose strength is quiet focus, the delivery is quieter.

The naming of the obstacle ("your mind's going to do its chatter") is delivered casually, almost humorously. The mind's chatter is treated as expected and not a problem. The "boom, put you aside" is delivered with a little energy, like a coach during a workout. The tone is encouraging without being saccharine.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not [[find-a-healthy-replacement]]. That move replaces a behavior with a substitute that meets the same need. This move keeps the strength as-is but redirects its target. The two moves are different: one substitutes the form, the other redirects the function.

This is not "use your strength to overcome your weakness." That framing pits the person against themselves and re-installs the endurance frame. This move is more specific: the strength is being applied to a practice (present-moment return) that is not about overcoming anything but about returning to what is already available.

This is not generic "play to your strengths." That framing is about career and identity. This move is operational: a specific present-moment practice that uses the felt-sense of a specific existing capacity.

This is not [[adrenaline-discharge-via-fear-activity]]. That read names when the strength (engaging in extreme sport) is being used to discharge background anxiety. The two can overlap — sometimes the ultramarathon is itself an adrenaline discharge pattern — but this move can still apply: redirect the underlying capacity (whatever the sport is providing) to the actual work.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not redirect a strength that is itself broken. If the strength is the symptom of a problem (overworking is the strength, but it's depleting the person), the redirection move can reinforce the broken pattern. Address the source first.
- Do not make the redirection into a new endurance project. The point is not to take on the inner work as the next thing to grind through. The point is to bring the felt-sense of the strength into the present moment.
- Do not over-elaborate the metaphor. The bridge is "do what you do there, here." If you elaborate the metaphor into a full system, you've replaced practice with a theory.
- Do not redirect a strength that the person hasn't actually demonstrated. If they claim ultramarathon endurance but haven't actually built that capacity, the move lacks the somatic anchor and falls flat.
- Do not use this move as the only take-home. It's a powerful close, but the person also needs the rest of the work (the recognition of the pattern, the spectrum frame, the practice of return). The redirect amplifies what was already produced; it doesn't substitute for it.
