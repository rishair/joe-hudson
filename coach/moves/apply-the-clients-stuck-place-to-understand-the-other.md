# Neither Did Your Ex

## The Move

When a client has just named a difficulty they are facing in real time — a thing they do not know how to do, a state they cannot find their way to, a question they cannot answer — and the person they are processing about (an ex, a parent, an estranged friend) faced the same difficulty in their actual life, name the parallel directly.

The form Joe uses in the divorce session:

- **Joe:** How do you love somebody who's ashamed without becoming ashamed?
- **Client:** I don't know. I don't know the answer to that question.
- **Joe:** Neither did your ex.

Two words. The whole reframe is in them.

The move does not interpret the other person, does not defend the other person, does not condemn the other person. It names that the other person had the same impossibility the client has just admitted to. The wall the client cannot get over is the same wall the other person could not get over. Both were trying with the same broken tool.

## When Joe Uses It

The move arrives after the client has done real work on themselves — felt the love, moved the anger, named the shame — and has just bumped into a question they cannot answer. The question is usually about what to do, how to be, how to handle a relational impossibility. The client says "I don't know."

Joe waits for the moment when the question is fresh and the not-knowing is present in the body. The move depends on this freshness. If Joe waited until later in the session, after the client had moved on from the question, the move would not land the same way. The client's own felt sense of "I don't know how to do this" is what makes the "neither did your ex" make immediate sense.

The move is most useful when:
- The client has been carrying a hard story about the other person's actions ("she came home with a letter and that was it")
- The session has produced both the love and the anger the client had toward the other person
- The client has just named a difficulty in handling a relational dynamic
- The other person plausibly faced the same difficulty in their actual situation
- The client has done enough work that the move will land as recognition rather than as a defense of the other person

## What It Produces

The move shifts the client's relationship to the other person without requiring the client to forgive, excuse, or reconcile. The client does not have to do anything with the other person. The work is internal: the recognition that the other person was facing the same impossibility allows the client to hold the other person as a fellow human stuck in the same difficulty, rather than as a singular antagonist who knew better and chose harm.

This shift does several things in the body of the client:

- It reduces the heat of the story without erasing the validity of the hurt. The hurt is real. The harm is real. The anger is real. And: the other person was a person stuck in the same wall.
- It loosens the identity of "I am the wronged one and they are the wronger" that often locks the post-relationship work in place. The asymmetry that grants the client a special suffering role gets softened.
- It provides a kind of meta-compassion that does not require the client to make the other person look good. The other person can remain difficult, problematic, even abusive in their behavior. The shared impossibility is at a different level: both were trying to handle something neither of them knew how to handle.

In the divorce session, after Joe says "Neither did your ex," he does not stay on the move long. He immediately follows with: "Oh, I see you now. There's 100 people on this call who see a beautiful man, and there's one who sees something to be ashamed of." The "100 vs 1" reframe is also Joe redirecting the client back to the present, to the room, to the love already available. The "neither did your ex" lands quickly and gets folded into the larger work.

## Why the Move Works

The move works because it leverages a recognition the client has already made about themselves. Joe is not asking the client to imagine what the ex was experiencing. He is pointing out that the ex was experiencing the thing the client has just admitted to. The client cannot dispute their own current experience. So they cannot dispute that the ex faced the same.

This is different from the standard empathy move ("try to see things from her perspective") which often produces resistance because the client does not want to do that work, especially when the hurt is fresh. The standard empathy move requires the client to do imaginative labor on behalf of the person who hurt them. The "neither did your ex" move does not require imagination. It uses the client's own present-moment difficulty as the data.

The move also works because it does not relieve the client of their pain. The pain stays valid. The anger stays valid. The story of what happened stays the story of what happened. What changes is the implied indictment: the implied claim that "she should have known better and chose not to." If the client themselves does not know better in this moment, the implied claim collapses. She did not know better either. The behavior was bad and the wound is real and the other person was a person stuck in the same wall.

## What It Does Not Do

The move does not excuse the behavior. The ex still ended the marriage by reading a letter without allowing response. The hurt is unchanged. The anger is unchanged. The move says: she did this because she was facing an impossibility she did not have the tools for. It does not say: therefore the behavior was okay.

The move does not require forgiveness. The client does not have to feel anything kind toward the ex. The work is purely internal recognition: she was facing what I am facing. Whatever the client does with that recognition is their own business.

The move does not move into reconciliation. There is no implied call to repair the relationship. The relationship is over. The recognition is for the client's own integration, not for the relationship's restoration.

The move does not skip the anger. Joe deploys the move *after* the client has released significant anger at the ex. If he had deployed it before, it would have been [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]]. The order is anger first, recognition second. The recognition lands cleanly because the anger has already moved.

## Why the Move Came From the Specific Question

In the divorce session, the question that triggered the move was: "How do you love somebody who's ashamed without becoming ashamed?" This question is structurally significant. The client had been describing his own shame standing in the way of connection from the start of the session. The ex, in her last act in the marriage, had handled the marriage's difficulty by going to an external coach and then delivering a letter — a move that, viewed through the lens of the session, looks like its own version of shame management. The client and the ex were both, in different ways, unable to be in shame openly with each other.

Joe's question — "how do you love somebody who's ashamed without becoming ashamed?" — names the central impossibility of the marriage that ended. The client cannot answer. The ex could not answer either. The move makes this visible.

The specific question matters because it is the question that names the actual structural difficulty of the relationship that ended. Generic versions ("she had her own stuff") would not land the same way. The specific question — about loving someone in shame — is the specific impossibility the relationship was running against. Naming that both people faced it is more useful than naming that the other person was a person.

## Tone

Spare and steady. "Neither did your ex." Three words, no explanation, no softening. The move does not need elaboration; the client's own admission a moment earlier provides all the context required.

The tone is not gentle in the soft sense. It is direct, almost flat. The directness is what gives it weight. If Joe had said it warmly — "neither did your ex, and that's okay" — the move would have absorbed back into the comfort frame and lost the recognition. The flat delivery makes it land as a fact, not as a comfort.

The tone is also not accusatory. It is not "neither did your ex, so you should be more understanding." It is simply: this is true. The lack of moral framing keeps the move from triggering the defense the client would mount against being told to be more understanding.

## What Often Follows

After the move lands, Joe usually moves on quickly to something present-tense and somatic. In the divorce session, he immediately pivoted to: "Oh, I see you now. There's 100 people on this call who see a beautiful man, and there's one who sees something to be ashamed of." The pivot is back to the room, to the love available, to the body.

This pattern is important. The "neither did your ex" is not meant to dwell. It is a recognition that lands and gets folded. Dwelling on it would invite the client to spend cognitive effort either accepting or arguing with the recognition. Folding it into the next live moment lets it integrate without becoming a discussion.

The move's most lasting effect happens days or weeks after the session, when the client is in a quiet moment and the recognition surfaces. The integration takes time. The session plants the seed; life produces the integration. Trying to force the integration in-session by dwelling on the move would interrupt the body's own work.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not deploy before the anger has moved.** This is the most important constraint. If the client has not had the chance to release their anger at the other person, naming the shared difficulty becomes [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]]. The other person's matching difficulty has to be named after the anger, not as a way of getting around the anger.
- **Do not extend the move into an argument.** "Neither did your ex" is the whole move. If the client resists ("yeah but she still chose to do it that way"), do not argue. Let the client have the resistance. The recognition will surface in their own time.
- **Do not use the move to excuse abuse.** If the other person's behavior was abusive and the client is still processing the abuse, the move can land as minimizing. Use only when the work has already established that the harm was real and the anger has been validated.
- **Do not use it as a coaching trick.** The move depends on the actual symmetry of the impossibility. If the client has named a difficulty the other person did not actually face, the move does not work. The match has to be real.
- **Do not soften the delivery.** The flat directness is what makes the move land. Softening it makes it a comfort, which neutralizes the recognition.
