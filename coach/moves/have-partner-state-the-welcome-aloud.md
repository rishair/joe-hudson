# Have the Partner State the Welcome Aloud (in Couples Coaching)

## The Move

When one partner has been operating under a false belief about what the other partner needs from them ("I have to be the strong one," "I can't fall apart in front of them"), have the other partner state directly, in their own words, what they actually want.

The form: "Christopher, just to, in your own words, if it's true for you, can you say something to Sadie like, 'I never want you to deal with my emotions. I only want you to be with me when you, when you feel like you can.'" -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Hold It All Anymore

The conditions: it has to be true for the partner. It has to be in their own words. It has to be said directly to the other person, not described in third person.

What Christopher said: "Baby, I never want you to deal with my emotions for me. I just want you to be with me when you feel like you can. And I truly mean that... I love all of you, every part. There's nothing you can show me that I won't be able to love. Any emotion, anything. You can say anything to me, you can feel anything with me. I love you for everything."

## When Joe Uses It

This move applies when:
- The session has surfaced that one partner is operating under a belief about what the other needs that is constraining them.
- The constrained partner has been described as needing protection, needing the strong one, needing to be lifted out, needing managed emotion.
- The other partner is present and has the capacity to speak their own truth about what they actually want.
- The coaching has done the prior work of revealing the constraint so the welcome lands in the right place.

The move does not usually come early. It comes after the constrained partner has been able to feel and name the burden of their belief. Said too early, before the constraint is felt, the welcome bounces off. Said at the right moment, after the constraint has been touched, the welcome lands directly into the place that has been bracing.

## What Joe Watches For

After the partner speaks, the question is: did the other person let it in?

"You let that in. Usually when this happens, the person doesn't let it in, but I saw you fully let that in. You had like one little flash maybe where you didn't believe something. Is that, is that accurate, or did you -- is it -- was it all in?" -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Hold It All Anymore

The signals of letting it in: physical softening, eye contact maintained, body opens rather than closes, no rehearsed counter-narrative starts running in the head. The signals of not letting it in: small flinch, eyes drop, body braces, an internal "yes but" begins.

Most people, when offered this kind of welcome from a partner, do not fully let it in on the first pass. The years of operating under the false belief have built a structural resistance. The welcome runs into the architecture of the suppression and bounces. Naming the bounce ("I saw one little flash where you didn't believe something") is part of the move. It surfaces the resistance so it can be worked with, rather than letting it function as a quiet refusal.

## What This Replaces

This move replaces the coach speaking *about* what the partner wants. The coach can describe the welcome ("your partner probably doesn't need you to be the strong one") and the description lands intellectually. But the words from the actual partner, in their actual voice, addressed directly, are received differently by the body of the person hearing them.

It also replaces the constrained partner having to imagine or guess what the other wants. The imagining is filtered through the same constraint that produced the problem. Hearing the partner speak directly bypasses the filter.

## Why It Works

Three mechanisms.

**First, direct testimony from the source.** The person who has been doing the work of "being the strong one" has been doing it on the assumption that this is what their partner needs. The actual partner saying directly that this is not what they need contradicts the assumption at the source. The coach saying it secondhand does not have the same effect.

**Second, the embodied delivery.** When the partner speaks in their own voice, with their own breath and feeling, the recipient receives the felt truth of it, not just the proposition. "I love all of you, every part" said by the actual person, with the actual face and the actual love, carries weight that no description can.

**Third, it shifts the relational picture in real time.** The constrained partner has been holding a picture of the relationship in which their suppression is needed. Hearing the welcome in their partner's voice reorders the picture. The new picture does not require the suppression. The constraint that has been running for years can begin to relax because the foundational belief it was built on has just been visibly contradicted.

## What It Requires

The partner speaking has to actually mean it. If they are saying it because the coach asked, but underneath they do want the constrained partner to be the strong one, the move backfires. The receiver detects the gap.

The partner speaking also has to have done enough of their own work to be able to speak from a clean place. If the welcome is performed from a place of "I should be saying this," the receiver hears the performance, not the welcome.

Joe's instruction includes the qualifier "if it's true for you." This is essential. If the partner does not feel it as true, the move is not run. The coach asks the partner directly before asking them to speak.

## What It Is Not

This move is not:
- The coach scripting the partner. The partner finds their own words from the prompt.
- A guarantee that the recipient lets it in. Often a follow-up is needed.
- A solution by itself. The welcome is one moment in a longer process of unwinding the constraint.
- A substitute for the welcomed partner doing their own work. The welcome opens the door; the person has to walk through.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, after Sadie has surfaced the belief that she has to be the strong one because Christopher cannot be:

- **Joe:** "Christopher, just to, in your own words, if it's true for you, can you say something to Sadie like, 'I never want you to deal with my emotions. I only want you to be with me when you, when you feel like you can.'"
- **Christopher:** "Baby, I never want you to deal with my emotions for me. I just want you to be with me when you feel like you can. And I truly mean that. And I don't need you to deal with my emotions. And I love you more than anything right now when you're emotional. I love you so much, baby. You don't have to be strong all the time. Fuck that."
- **Joe:** "You let that in. Usually when this happens, the person doesn't let it in, but I saw you fully let that in. You had like one little flash maybe where you didn't believe something."
- **Sadie:** "No, maybe, but no, I, I think I took it in. Yeah, it was nice."

*(Source: I Can't Hold It All Anymore)*

What the move did: removed the foundational assumption that had been organizing Sadie's behavior for years. After this exchange, Joe could then push on the next layer (is the welcome enough to stop the voice in your head?) because the floor had shifted.

## When the Recipient Doesn't Let It In

If the recipient does not let it in fully, the move is not failed. The bounce is information. Joe followed up immediately with: "Is that enough for you to be able to just not believe the voice in your head anymore, at least with Christopher?" Sadie said no. The "no" was data: the constraint is not held only by the partner's belief; it is also held by the internal voice. The work then moves to that voice. See [[voice-in-the-head]].

The move surfaces what else is in the way. Whatever the welcome doesn't dissolve is the next thing to look at.

## When to Use This Move

Use when:
- Both partners are present in the coaching
- One partner has clearly been operating under a false belief about what the other wants
- The other partner has the capacity to speak their truth cleanly
- The session has surfaced the constraint enough that the welcome will land in the right place

Do not use when:
- The other partner cannot honestly offer the welcome
- The relationship is in a state where this kind of direct exchange would feel coerced
- The constrained partner has not yet felt the burden of the constraint and is still defending it
- Either partner is dysregulated to a degree that direct exchange is not possible

## Tone

The setup is matter-of-fact. The coach offers a sentence as a template ("something like 'I never want you to deal with my emotions'") and lets the partner find their own form. The coach does not coach the delivery. The partner brings whatever feeling they bring.

After the speaking, the coach's tone moves to noticing: "You let that in." "I saw one little flash." The noticing is precise and not interpretive. The work is observing what just happened in the room, not making meaning about it.
