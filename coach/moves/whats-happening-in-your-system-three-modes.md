# What's Happening in Your System? (The Three-Mode Diagnostic)

## The Move

When a person is producing somatic distress (dissociation, lightheadedness, anxiety, body symptoms) while attempting something uncomfortable, ask them which of three modes is operating: are you trying to overcome the discomfort, are you fully in the discomfort, or is the discomfort not happening and you're comfortable?

"What's happening in your system right now? Are you trying to overcome the discomfort? Are you fully in the discomfort? Are you comfortable and discomfort's not happening? What of those three things is going on?" -- Joe Hudson, What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?

The move structures a self-diagnostic around three discrete modes:

1. **Trying to overcome the discomfort.** The person has discomfort and is fighting it. The fight produces a managed-state in which the body is split between the discomfort itself and the effort to suppress or push through it.
2. **Fully in the discomfort.** The person has discomfort and is in it. Not fighting, not managing, just present in the felt state.
3. **Comfortable and discomfort's not happening.** No discomfort is operating. The state is comfort, not discomfort.

The move names the three modes as distinct possibilities and asks the person to identify which one is running. From inside the person, all three can feel like "discomfort" — but they have very different operating signatures, and the work that follows depends on which one is actually present.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person reports somatic distress (lightheaded, out-of-body, dissociated, shaky, hot, tight) while attempting an uncomfortable thing.
- When the person says "I'm uncomfortable" but the work needs to surface what kind of uncomfortable. The same word ("uncomfortable") can mean any of the three modes from the inside.
- When the read [[trying-to-be-comfortable-instead-of-being-uncomfortable]] is suspected and needs to be confirmed by the person's own body-knowledge.
- Before instructing the person to relocate the speaking ([[walk-into-the-discomfort-and-speak-from-there]]). The relocation only makes sense if the diagnostic has surfaced that mode one is the one currently operating.
- When a coaching session has been running on assumption ("the person is uncomfortable, so we should help them feel through it") and the assumption needs to be tested.
- When the person has been operating on a comfort-as-prerequisite frame and needs a vocabulary for distinguishing modes other than "I'm uncomfortable, I need to get to comfortable."

## What It Produces

- **A precise answer.** The body almost always knows. The person can usually answer within seconds. The body has been operating in one mode the whole time; it just hasn't had a vocabulary for it. Once the modes are named, the person can match the felt sense to the right mode.
- **An immediate clarification of what the work is.** If mode one is operating, the work is to relocate to mode two ([[walk-into-the-discomfort-and-speak-from-there]]). If mode two is operating, the work is to stay there and let it speak ([[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]]). If mode three is operating, the work is either to be curious about why the discomfort isn't here or to recognize that the work for the day is something different.
- **Permission to be in mode two.** Mode two ("fully in the discomfort") is named as a legitimate option, equal to the other two. For a person who has spent their life in mode one — fighting the discomfort whenever it arrives — mode two has often felt unavailable or wrong. Naming it as one of three valid possibilities makes it visible as a choice.
- **A vocabulary that travels.** Once the three-mode diagnostic has been run in a session, the person has a vocabulary they can use in their own life. They can ask themselves "which mode am I in right now?" outside the coaching context. The diagnostic becomes a personal tool, not just a coach's question.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says they're trying to overcome the discomfort:** the read [[trying-to-be-comfortable-instead-of-being-uncomfortable]] is confirmed. The next move is [[walk-into-the-discomfort-and-speak-from-there]]. The discomfort is the location they need to relocate into. Optionally, Joe asks the further question: "Is that what happens when you're uncomfortable or when you're trying to be comfortable?" This second question disambiguates which felt state is being produced by the trying versus by the discomfort itself.

- **If the person says they're fully in the discomfort:** the work is already happening. Stay with the discomfort, ask the truth-question from inside it ([[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]] or [[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]]), let the felt state speak.

- **If the person says they're comfortable and the discomfort isn't happening:** check whether this is real or a fourth mode (dissociated so successfully that the discomfort has been suppressed below conscious access). If it's real, the work for the day is probably different than what was presenting. If it's the suppressed version, the work is to access the discomfort that's been pushed down. The diagnostic question to check: "What would it be like to not be comfortable for a minute?" The body's response tells you which version is real.

- **If the person can't tell which mode is operating:** this itself is data. The person is operating without contact with their own felt state. The work might be smaller-scale presence work before the three-mode question becomes answerable. See [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] or [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]] for the access-restoring moves.

## Dialogue Example

The compressed exchange from the source session, after the woman had started telling her story and stopped:

- **Joe:** "What's happening in your system right now? Are you trying to overcome the discomfort? Are you fully in the discomfort? Are you comfortable and discomfort's not happening? What of those three things is going on?"
- **Person:** "I feel kind of like lightheaded, like sort of out-of-body experience."
- **Joe:** "And is that what happens when you're uncomfortable or when you're trying to be comfortable?"
- **Person:** "I think when I'm trying to be comfortable."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The diagnostic produces the body-symptom (lightheaded, out-of-body) without yet a mode-assignment. The follow-up question disambiguates: the symptom is from the trying, not from the underlying state. With both pieces of data, the next move (relocate the speaking into the discomfort) becomes the obvious instruction.

The two-question structure matters. The first question names the three modes as possibilities. The second question makes the person commit to a specific mapping. Without the second question, the person could answer the first with the body-symptom alone and the diagnostic wouldn't have produced an operating mode. With the second question, the diagnostic is complete and the next move is available.

## Why It Works

The move works because most people have been treating "uncomfortable" as an undifferentiated state. From the inside, the lightheaded floaty feeling of trying-to-not-feel-the-discomfort feels like discomfort, and so does the actual discomfort, and so does the post-comfort residue. The word "uncomfortable" covers all of them. The conflation prevents the person from doing the right work, because the right work depends on which mode is operating.

Naming the three modes as discrete possibilities forces the felt sense to map to one of them. The mapping is informative. Once the person identifies that they're trying to overcome the discomfort (rather than being in it), the entire framing of the work changes. They've been doing the wrong operation. The work is to switch operations, not to do more of the wrong one.

The three-mode structure also installs a frame that can be re-used. Anywhere a felt state is operating, the person can ask which of the three modes they're in. The diagnostic becomes a tool. The tool is portable.

## Tone

Calm, curious, evenly-weighted. Joe gives the three modes as equally valid possibilities. He's not signaling which one is the "right" answer. The evenness is part of what makes the diagnostic work — if the person can hear which answer Joe is hoping for, they'll produce that answer rather than the actual one.

The follow-up question ("is that what happens when you're uncomfortable or when you're trying to be comfortable?") is also asked with curiosity. Joe is asking the body, not the person's interpretation of the body. The person's first answer is the body's report; the second answer is the mapping.

When the person answers, Joe receives the answer without evaluation. The answer is the data. He doesn't say "yes, that's right" or "interesting" — he uses the answer to set up the next move. The lack of evaluation keeps the diagnostic clean.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not skip the second question.** The first question alone produces the body-symptom without a mode-mapping. The second question completes the diagnostic. Without the mapping, the next move is ambiguous.

- **Do not collapse the three modes into "are you fighting it or feeling it?" The third mode (no discomfort happening) is important. Sometimes the work for the day is not in the discomfort at all, and the diagnostic surfaces that. Removing the third mode loses this case.

- **Do not lead the person to mode one.** If Joe is sure the person is trying to overcome the discomfort, the temptation is to ask "you're trying to overcome it, aren't you?" The leading question prevents the body from answering. Even when the leading-question answer is right, the diagnostic value is lost because the person didn't locate it themselves.

- **Do not use the diagnostic as a quiz.** The three modes are not a test the person needs to pass. They're a vocabulary for what's happening. If the person says "I don't know which one," that's a real answer and points at different work.

- **Do not deliver the three modes too fast.** Each mode needs a beat to land. If they're delivered as a rushed list, the person can't actually match the felt sense to the options. The pacing is part of the move.

- **Do not assume the diagnostic transfers to non-discomfort states unchanged.** The three-mode structure works for discomfort because comfort/discomfort is a clear binary. For other felt states (sadness, anger, joy), the three modes need to be rephrased: are you trying to push the [feeling] away, are you fully in it, or is it not happening?

## Variations

The same structure adapts to other felt states:

- **For sadness:** "Are you pushing the sadness away? Are you in the sadness? Or is sadness not happening?"
- **For anger:** "Are you trying to manage the anger? Are you in it? Or is anger not happening?"
- **For anxiety:** "Are you trying to stop being anxious? Are you in the anxiety? Or is anxiety not actually present?"
- **For grief:** "Are you fighting the grief? Are you in the grief? Or is grief not happening right now?"

The three-mode structure generalizes because the structural insight is the same: the felt state has three possible relationships at any moment (managed, occupied, absent), and the work depends on which one is operating.
