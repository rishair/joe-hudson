# Ask the Criticizer What They Mean

## The Move

When someone criticizes you, judges you, or expresses disappointment in you, ask them what they mean. Engage them in genuine wonder about the criticism rather than defending against it. Most of the criticism will dissolve, not because you agreed with it, but because the criticizer felt heard.

The instruction in Joe's words:

"If somebody's upset, which means they're disappointed or criticizing you or judging you, it means that they feel unseen in themselves or they feel unseen in others. Like most fighting that we see happen, da da da da da da da, if you just stop and you say, hey, what do you mean by that? What's going on? What am I missing? You'll notice that they'll get calmer. They'll start talking. They'll feel heard. And it's not like they need you to agree. They don't need you to be smart. They just need you to feel, they just need to feel heard." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The operational questions Joe lists:

- "What do you mean by that?"
- "What's going on?"
- "What am I missing?"
- "What would you have me do?"

These are not strategic questions. They are real questions from someone who is genuinely trying to see the critic. The internal state is wonder, not defense.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is the same one named in [[being-heard]] and [[see-them-be-seen-find-alignment-instead-of-control]]: under most upset is "I want to be seen." The criticism is the surface form the unseenness is taking. The criticizer is using the move (criticizing) that they unconsciously hope will produce the result (being seen). It usually does not, because the receiver of the criticism gets defensive and the seeing does not happen.

When you respond with genuine questions instead of defense, you provide the seeing the criticizer was actually after. The substrate need is met. The surface need (the criticism itself) often dissolves because the engine driving it is no longer running.

"If you think about any fight that you've gotten into, anybody who's criticized you, if you're like, 'Oh, tell me, like, what's your criticism? What am I missing? What would you have me do?' What happens to that criticism? All of a sudden, they're engaged in you. They're in wonder with you. They're in relationship with you." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The critic does not just get heard. The critic shifts state. The defensive/critical posture cannot be sustained inside genuine inquiry from the recipient. The criticizer's own nervous system settles. They become available for actual contact.

## The Prerequisite: Being In Yourself

The move cannot be executed from a person who is not in themselves. A person who needs the criticism to not be true to feel okay will hear the questions as fake, and their fakeness will be visible. The questions become the new approval-seeking move, dressed as wonder.

"You can't do this if you're not in yourself, but if you're in yourself, it's really easy to do." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The prerequisite is the same one named in [[what-you-seek-from-approval-is-self-presence]] and [[see-them-be-seen-find-alignment-instead-of-control]]: you have to be okay with yourself first. Without that, the genuine wonder is not available — you are too busy managing your own threatened state to be curious about the other person.

This is why the move is paired in the source teaching with the present-moment self-okayness work. First the practitioner finds the internal good-with-self ([[what-do-you-need-to-be-good-with-yourself-right-now]]). From that ground, the move becomes available. Without that ground, the move is a technique that does not land.

## What the Move Looks Like

A coworker says: "I'm really disappointed in how you handled that meeting."

The defensive response (which fails): "I don't know what you mean, I handled it fine." Or: "Well, you weren't even there for the hard part." Or the apologetic-but-still-defensive: "I'm sorry, I'll try to do better."

The move:

- "What do you mean? What happened that disappointed you?"
- (Listen. Do not interrupt to defend.)
- "What was missing for you? What would have been different if I had done it the way you'd want?"
- (Listen. Receive what they say without rushing to respond.)
- "What am I missing about how that landed for you?"

The questions keep coming until the criticizer has been thoroughly heard. The internal state is genuine — you actually want to know. You are not gathering ammunition for a counter-argument. You are not waiting for them to finish so you can defend. You are seeing them.

By the third or fourth question, often: the criticism softens. The criticizer says something like "okay, I think I just needed to say that." Or: "actually, I don't even know if it was about you, I was already upset." Or: "thanks for asking, that helped." The criticism dissolves not by being argued with but by being received.

## The Caveat

Joe is explicit: most of the time the criticism dissolves, not all of the time.

"You just allow that person to feel seen, and all of a sudden, that disappointment, that judgment, that criticism, most of the time is gonna just go away. Not all the time, but most of the time it's just gonna go away." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The cases where it does not dissolve:

- The criticism is actually accurate and represents a real issue that the critic needs you to address. The seeing-them move makes the issue able to be worked on; it does not make the issue go away. This is fine — the work can now happen because the connection has been re-established.
- The criticizer has a structural issue that prevents the seeing from landing (entrenched contempt, a personality dynamic, a long-running resentment that does not collapse in a single conversation). The move still produces some softening, but the dissolution is not complete.
- The criticizer is using the criticism as a controlling move ([[dont-do-emotion-at-people]]) and does not actually want to be seen — they want compliance. The move surfaces this dynamic, which is itself useful information.

In all of these cases, the move is still the right one. It just does not always produce instantaneous dissolution. The "most of the time" qualifier is honest about the move's reliability without abandoning its effectiveness.

## What This Move Replaces

The defaults the move replaces:

- **Defense.** "I didn't do that, you're wrong about me." Produces escalation.
- **Apology without seeing.** "I'm sorry, I'm sorry, I'll do better." Produces continued criticism because the seeing has not happened and the substrate need is still unmet.
- **Counter-criticism.** "Well, you do the same thing." Produces a fight.
- **Distance.** "I don't want to talk about this." Produces accumulated unspoken criticism that eventually surfaces worse.
- **Withdrawal of love.** "If that's how you feel about me, then..." Produces relational damage on top of the original issue.

None of these address the substrate (the unseenness). The move addresses the substrate directly.

## Connection to the Larger Move

This is a specific operational form of [[see-them-be-seen-find-alignment-instead-of-control]]. The larger move says: in a fight, see them, understand where they are, allow yourself to be seen when they are ready, find alignment. This move says: when the fight takes the specific form of criticism directed at you, the operational starting move is to ask questions until the critic feels seen.

It is also a specific application of [[ask-open-ended-non-judgmental-questions-when-triggered]]. The trigger-response move and this move share the same mechanism: respond to the threat-stimulus with genuine wonder rather than defense, and the threat-dynamic dissolves. The two articles differ in what the trigger is — that one is for any in-the-moment trigger, this one is for the specific case of receiving criticism or judgment.

The narrower scope of this move is useful in its own right. People who hear "respond to triggers with questions" often do not generalize it to "respond to criticism with questions" because criticism feels different — it is content-laden, it is about them specifically, it carries a verdict. The move is the same. The teaching deserves its own naming because the application has to be specifically authorized for the criticism case to land.

## What Joe Says This Cures

The move is offered in the source teaching as one of the things that cures the chronic problem of caring about others' approval. The mechanism: when you can engage criticism with wonder rather than defense, the criticism stops being a thing you have to avoid. The avoidance was structuring much of the approval-seeking behavior. Once the criticism can be engaged, the avoidance is no longer necessary. The behavior reorganizes.

The deeper claim: most fights, most workplace conflicts, most family tensions are running on unmet seeing-needs that show up as criticism. The move dissolves a large class of these by giving people what they were actually after.

"What does this do? It allows that person to feel seen, and all of a sudden that disappointment, that judgment, that criticism, most of the time is gonna just go away." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

## The Connection Course Frame

In the Connection Course teaching, this is presented as one of the operational forms of [[view]] applied in a difficult moment. The criticism is the trigger. The questions are the wonder/empathy enactment. The internal state of being-in-yourself is the vulnerability/impartiality enactment. The whole move is VIEW deployed at the moment most people would deploy defense.

This is also why the move "starts in the body" — the prerequisite of being-in-yourself is somatic, not conceptual. A person who is intellectually convinced they should ask questions but is internally braced will produce questions that have no wonder in them, and the move will not land. The body has to be in the open state for the questions to be the real questions.

## What Tends to Happen When It Works

- The criticizer's voice softens within two or three questions
- The criticizer starts disclosing what is actually going on underneath the criticism (often unrelated to the surface issue)
- The criticism reframes itself in the criticizer's own words ("actually, I don't think you did that, I was just frustrated about...")
- The conversation moves from adversarial to collaborative — "what would you have me do?" often produces a real conversation about a real thing
- The relationship deepens because the criticism event has become a connection event
- The original surface issue may still be addressed, but now it is addressable

## What Tends to Happen When It Doesn't Work

- The questions feel performative to the criticizer, who continues escalating
- The asker is unable to maintain the open state and slips into defense mid-questions
- The criticism is being used as a control move and the asker discovers they are dealing with [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] or similar — the move is the right move, but the larger context is different
- The criticism is accurate and the issue needs actually addressing — the move clears the relational ground but the work remains

## Tone

The questions are asked with genuine wonder. The voice tends to slow. The body is relaxed. There is no edge in the questions, no implicit "and you better have a good answer." The asker actually wants to know.

The asker is not making themselves small to receive the criticism. They are standing upright in themselves while being interested in what the critic is bringing. This is the distinction that makes the move different from people-pleasing reception of criticism.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't perform the move.** "What do you mean by that?" asked with a defensive edge is not the move; it is defense disguised as wonder. The criticizer will feel the difference.
- **Don't use questions to evade.** The move is to genuinely receive what the criticizer says, not to bury the criticism in questions until the criticizer gives up.
- **Don't make it a therapy session.** The questions are about the criticism, not about the criticizer's childhood. Stay with what they are bringing right now.
- **Don't agree just to make it stop.** Agreement that is not true is its own dishonesty. The move is to see them, not to capitulate. You can see them and still hold your own truth.
- **Don't do this if you are not in yourself.** The prerequisite is real. If you are reactive, get yourself regulated first. The move requires the internal ground.
