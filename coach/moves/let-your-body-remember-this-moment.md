# Let Your Body Remember This Moment (Anchor the Felt Shift)

## The Move

When an insight lands and the body responds — release, tears, laughter, expansion, a wave of energy — invite the body to remember the moment. Do not move on. Do not explain. Let the body do what it is doing and explicitly point at it as something worth keeping.

The instruction is short and direct:

- "Please do. Just feel, just feel your body. Let your body remember this moment."
- "Let your body have that."
- "Feel that in your body now."

The move plants a somatic anchor for what just happened so the body has a reference for the felt shift, not just the mind.

## When Joe Uses This

- Right after an insight lands and the body produces a visible release.
- When the person is in the middle of a felt experience — tears, laughter, expansion, energy — and the mind is about to take over and start interpreting.
- When a recursion has just been seen ("the pattern of worrying is the thing I was doing") and the body is responding to the seeing.
- When the person spontaneously names the body experience ("my body feels like it's exploding with joy," "I feel like crying and laughing at the same time") and is asking permission to be in it.
- When the person says they feel silly or embarrassed at the size of the response — the embarrassment can pull them out of the body, and the instruction holds them in it.

This move is the partner of [[point-at-the-present-evidence]]. The first move surfaces evidence the person was missing. When the evidence lands, the body releases. This move keeps the person with the release rather than moving on to the next intellectual frame.

It is also distinct from [[move-the-body-to-integrate-insight]], which is a practice about not suppressing the body's natural impulse to stretch, yawn, breathe. This move adds a specific instruction: that the body remember the moment. It is anchoring, not just sequencing.

## Why It Works

Insight that is only mental fades. Insight anchored in the body is recallable. The body knows how to return to states it has been in before. Inviting the body to remember installs a felt reference.

The instruction names what is already happening as something valuable. Without the instruction, the person often interprets a big body response as too much, too dramatic, or evidence that they were silly to be so wound up. The interpretation pulls them out of the body and back into the head, which is where the gripping happens. The instruction prevents that exit.

It also extends the duration of the felt shift. A body response that is allowed to complete is different from one that is cut short by self-consciousness. The instruction gives permission to stay until the response completes itself.

## Dialogue Example

After Joe had just pointed at the recursion — that the worry about the future was the very pattern that produced the overworking — the person's body responded:

- **Person:** "A thousand percent. Oh, that just hit like a ton of bricks. Oh my God, I just felt like everything that was on me just splatted out. Wow. Oh my God, my body feels — it's just like exploding with joy and just ready. I can't contain it. Like, part of me, I'm like happy, and part of me like wants to cry a little bit at the same time."
- **Joe:** "Please do. Just feel, just feel your body. Let your body remember this moment."
- **Person:** "Yeah. Oh my gosh, wow. I just feel really silly now."

*(Source: Fear Of Overworking)*

Joe does not interpret. He does not summarize. He does not move to the next question. He invites the body to be with what is happening and to make a felt record of it. The instruction is permission ("please do"), invitation to stay ("just feel your body"), and explicit anchor ("let your body remember this moment").

The "I feel really silly now" that arrives is the mind trying to come back online. The instruction had already worked. The body had been with the moment long enough to remember it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person stays with the body:** stay quiet. Let the response complete. Do not narrate it. The silence is the work.

- **If the person tries to talk through it:** gently redirect to the body. "Just feel, just feel your body." The repetition is the move.

- **If the person says they feel silly:** receive it without joining. "You're so welcome." The silliness is the mind trying to discount the body experience. The instruction has already installed the anchor; the silliness comes after.

- **If the person says they feel both joy and tears:** invite both. "Please do." Both are part of what the body is processing. There is no need to sort them.

- **If the person resists ("I don't want to feel this much"):** the resistance itself is information. The work shifts to the resistance, but the body invitation stays available.

## Tone

Quiet, warm, very brief. Almost a whisper. There is no urgency. The instruction is a permission slip, not a directive. "Please do" is closer to a tender encouragement than a command.

The tone is also not solemn. The body is doing something beautiful and the moment is allowed to be light. Joe is not making it heavier than it is. He is just keeping the person in it.

## Variations

- "Please do. Just feel your body."
- "Let your body have that."
- "Let your body remember this."
- "Feel that. Let it move through you."
- "Stay with it for a moment."
- "Let your body know that this happened."

The wording is less important than the function: keep the person with the body experience and explicitly mark it as worth remembering.

## What This Is Not

- It is not a meditation instruction. The person is not being asked to observe the body neutrally. They are being asked to inhabit a specific moment.
- It is not a request to perform or extend the emotion. The body is already doing what it is doing. The instruction is to stay with it, not to amplify it.
- It is not a substitute for the insight itself. The move only works after a real felt shift. Used too early, it becomes a request to manufacture a body experience that isn't happening.
- It is not [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]. The body is producing the response on its own. The instruction is to be with what is already there.
