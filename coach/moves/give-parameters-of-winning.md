# Give the Parameters of Winning, Not the Method

## The Move

When delegating, state what winning looks like and the constraints inside which the work has to land. Stop there. Do not specify how the work gets done.

The car-cleaning gradient illustrates the move by showing where it sits on the spectrum:

1. **No instruction at all.** See if anyone cleans the car. With clear roles and the right people, sometimes this works. See [[valve-handbook-desks-on-wheels]].
2. **Bare minimum:** "I need the car cleaned."
3. **Full micromanagement:** "I need the car cleaned this way, detail this, polish that, use this product, in this order."
4. **The parameters of winning (the missing middle):** "I need the car cleaned, I need it to look like it looks when you get off of a new car lot, I need it to be done for less than $150, and I need you to enjoy yourself doing it."

The fourth option states what a good job is. It specifies the outcome (looks like off the lot), the constraint (under $150), and the experiential requirement (enjoy yourself). It does not specify the method.

"You give people the parameters of what a good job is, but you don't tell them how to do it. You just tell them how to win." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

## What the Move Replaces

Most leaders move directly from option 2 (too vague) to option 3 (kills autonomy). The intermediate move is the one they have not been taught.

Option 2 fails because the worker does not know what success looks like. They have to guess, and any guess can be wrong by the leader's unspecified standard. The worker is anxious because they cannot tell whether they are succeeding.

Option 3 fails because the worker is reduced to executing the leader's plan. They cannot bring their intelligence. They cannot find a better way. They cannot enjoy the work because the work is not theirs. The leader is doing the thinking; the worker is doing the moving.

Option 4 succeeds because the worker has a clear definition of success, knows what constraints to operate inside, and is given the entire space inside the constraints to figure out how to win. The intelligence of the worker is now applied to the problem. The result is often better than what the leader would have specified.

## The Three Components

A complete win-parameters specification includes:

**Outcome.** What does success look like in concrete terms? "Looks like off the lot" is concrete enough to evaluate. "Looks clean" is not.

**Constraint.** What boundaries does the solution have to respect? Time, money, scope. "Less than $150" gives the worker a real constraint that shapes the solution space.

**Experiential requirement.** How should the doing of the work feel? "Enjoy yourself doing it" is not decorative. It is a real specification. A worker who is suffering through the work is operating at low efficiency. See [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]].

Most leaders give one or two of these. Few give all three. The complete specification is the move.

## Why It Works

Most people want to know how to win. Once they know, they will find the path. Without the win condition, they cannot find the path even if they want to. With the win condition, they can self-organize.

The leader who specifies the method has implicitly said: "I do not trust your way of doing it." The message reaches the worker before the instructions do. The worker disengages. The work suffers.

The leader who specifies the win condition has said: "I trust you to figure out the path. Here is what counts as figuring it out." The trust is the activation. The figuring-out is the work.

This is [[inherent-goodness]] applied to delegation. If people are inherently capable and motivated, the leader's job is to specify the destination, not the route. The route belongs to the person walking it.

## The Determination Coexists

This move does not require softening on the outcome. The leader can hold the outcome firmly. The car has to look like it just came off the lot. The constraint is $150. The worker has to enjoy it. These are non-negotiable.

What is negotiable is the path. The leader does not hold opinions on the cleaning product, the tools used, the order of operations. The path can be whatever the worker chooses, as long as the path reaches the parameters.

"You have to keep all that determination, you have to keep all that fortitude, you have to keep all that utter unacceptance of a reality that you don't envision. And there is also a reading the river and letting the river flow and paying attention to that river and following it to get there." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

The same posture for an executive: I want a company that's super successful. I want a product that sells better than all the competition. Those are clear. The sales technique, the marketing approach, the product details -- those are the path. The path is the worker's domain. The outcome is the leader's.

## When to Use This Move

- When delegating a piece of work and noticing the urge to specify the method.
- When a team has been over-managed and is no longer bringing initiative.
- When the work is creative or judgment-driven and the method matters less than the outcome.
- When the worker is more expert than the leader at the specific work.
- When the leader is operating in [[enjoy-over-manage|management mode]] and looking for a structural alternative.

## When Not to Use This Move

- When the method is part of the outcome (compliance work, regulatory work, safety-critical work). State the method because the method is the win.
- When the team is new and lacks the structure to navigate ambiguity. The parameters without the structure can produce drama. See [[structure-and-empowerment]].
- When the worker has explicitly asked for more direction and the leader is using "give them space" as an excuse to avoid leading. If the worker wants more instruction, they need more instruction.

## Tone

Plain and complete. The parameters need to be stated fully, with the three components (outcome, constraint, experiential requirement). Hedging the parameters undoes the move. "Maybe try to make it look pretty good" is not a parameter. "Looks like it just came off the lot" is a parameter.

The plainness also includes not adding instructions afterward. The temptation, after stating the parameters, is to add "and you might want to try X" or "I'd probably do Y." Each addition is a recursion back into method-specification. The move ends with the parameters. Then silence. Then trust.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader is delegating and feels the urge to specify everything.
- A team has stalled because the leader has been over-specifying or under-specifying.
- A leader is operating between two failure modes (too vague, too prescriptive) and wants the structural alternative.
- A leader has just done [[tell-them-like-an-asshole]] and discovered what they actually want. Now they need the form to deliver it.
- A coaching conversation about [[enjoy-over-manage]] has landed and the leader is asking what to do Monday morning.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Increased autonomy and engagement in the team.
- Better outcomes because the team's intelligence is applied to the problem.
- Less management overhead for the leader, which frees attention for the next-leverage work.
- Toward [[structure-and-empowerment]] as the broader organizational frame this move operationalizes.
- Toward [[community-over-teacher]] applied to delegation.
- Toward the leader discovering that the team is more capable than the previous management mode was making visible.
