# Open the Heart to the Doubt

## The Move

When the client has named a specific recurring blocker emotion (doubt, anxiety, self-criticism, fear) and the work has surfaced what is underneath it (the need, the anger, the determination), there is a final in-session move that gives the client a portable practice they can take home. Direct them into the blocker emotion. Then have them open their heart to it.

Pair the instruction with a felt-state the client already knows from a different context. The known state is the target. The blocker is the material that gets welcomed using the known state as the body's reference.

The form Joe uses:

1. **Direct into the blocker.** "Go into the doubt for a moment. All the doubt. It's not going to be the best. You're going to fuck it up. No one's going to pay attention. No one's going to listen."
2. **Confirm the felt access.** "You feel the doubt?" Brief, somatic check.
3. **Instruct the heart-opening.** "And open your heart to it. Love the doubt."
4. **Anchor to a known felt state.** Find a feeling the client has already named as a place of flow, aliveness, or openness. "How close is that to the way you feel when you dance?" The client already has the somatic memory; the heart-opening borrows that template.
5. **Continue the loving as a sustained motion.** "Just keep loving the doubt." Not as content but as a body posture. The blocker is staying; the relationship to it is what changes.
6. **Prescribe the practice as homework.** "Every time you go into your head spin of doubt, move into opening your heart to the doubt." The in-session experience becomes the take-home practice.

In the canonical session:

- **Joe:** "So I just have, I have a hack for you and then this'll, this'll do it. So I want you to go into the doubt for a moment. All the doubt. It's not going to be the best. You're going to fuck it up. No one's going to pay attention. No one's going to listen. Yeah. You feel the doubt?"
- **Client:** "Yep."
- **Joe:** "And open your heart to it. Love the doubt."
- **Client:** "And there's some fear."
- **Joe:** "Just keep loving the doubt. How close is that to the way you feel when you dance?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, much closer."
- *(Source: Why Am I Stuck In Life When I Know My Patterns?)*

The dance reference was the client's own — she had reported that dance was the one place she got out of the head spin and felt the spark. Joe took that known felt state and used it as the anchor for the heart-opening toward the doubt. The client's "much closer" confirmed the somatic translation had landed.

## When Joe Uses It

- At the close of a session where a specific blocker emotion has been the recurring obstacle
- When the client has named the blocker explicitly (doubt, anxiety, fear, criticism) and can locate it somatically
- When the client has already had access to a contrasting felt state (flow, aliveness, openness) in a different context
- When the underlying material has already been worked (the anger has moved, the need has been named) and the remaining work is establishing a portable practice for the days when the blocker returns
- When the client needs a take-home practice that is short, embodied, and does not require finding the underlying emotion every time the blocker arises

The move presupposes the client has access to both ends of the somatic A/B: the blocker (which they know well) and the open-hearted state (which they have touched in some context). If either is unavailable, the move is premature — the prior work is locating one or both.

## What This Move Is and Is Not

It is not the same as [[feel-the-shame-then-feel-the-opposite]]. That move asks the body to produce the somatic opposite of the current emotion as antidote. This move asks the body to bring an opening posture toward the current emotion without trying to produce its opposite. The doubt stays present. The relationship to it changes.

It is closer to [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]] — the distinction between being with an emotion and loving it. This move is the in-session enactment of that distinction for a specific blocker, with an anchor to a known felt state to give the body something to reach for.

It is not the same as [[chase-it-around-the-room]]. That move pursues an avoidant feeling with playful loving energy. This move is gentler — the doubt is already present, not hiding. The work is welcoming what is already there.

It is the prescription analogue of the moves above: not just the in-session demonstration, but the homework that converts the in-session shift into a daily practice.

## Why It Works

The blocker emotion (doubt, anxiety, fear, criticism) is usually resisted. The resistance is what makes the blocker stick. The body sends mobilization, the mind labels it bad, the mind fights it, the mobilization compounds, the loop repeats. Most of what people call "anxiety" or "doubt" is the resistance-loop, not the original emotion.

Opening the heart to the blocker interrupts the resistance. The body is now in a posture of welcome rather than rejection. The mobilization no longer feeds the fight; it has nowhere to go and naturally dissipates.

The anchor to a known felt state is the move's specific contribution. Without the anchor, "open your heart to the doubt" is an abstract instruction the client may not be able to operationalize. With the anchor — "how close is that to the way you feel when you dance?" — the body has a concrete reference. The client is not trying to manufacture a new state; they are translating a state they already know into a new context.

The anchor also lets the coach test whether the move landed. The client's report — "much closer" — is the diagnostic. If the answer were "not at all," the heart-opening did not actually happen; some more work is needed first. The known felt state functions both as the body's target and as the verification scale.

## The "Hack" Framing

Joe's introduction of the move uses the word "hack." This is not casual. The framing tells the client that what follows is a specific shortcut, not the full work — and that the shortcut is offered because the full work has already been done in the session and the client needs something operational for the next time the blocker shows up.

"So I just have, I have a hack for you and then this'll, this'll do it."

The "this'll do it" is the closing signal. The session has done what it needed to do. The hack is what makes the session's discovery portable. Without the hack, the client leaves with a felt sense that may not be repeatable on their own. With the hack, they have a procedure.

The "hack" framing is part of the move. It signals: this is small, this is concrete, this is what you do.

## The Two Kinds of Closed Eyes During This Move

A critical distinction the move surfaces: when the client closes their eyes after being told to open their heart to the doubt, the closure may be one of two opposite kinds. Joe surfaces the distinction in the breakdown of the source session.

**Disconnective closure (escape):** the body composes, the face goes neutral, the breath flattens. The client has gone to an inner space where the room can no longer reach them. This is the same kind of closed-eyes as the [[closing-eyes-at-the-start]] read — the client has withdrawn rather than entered.

**Embodied closure (entry):** the body moves with the feeling. The head moves like a wave. The face shows the emotion arriving. The client has gone more deeply into the felt material the heart-opening invited.

The diagnostic is what is happening in the rest of the body during the closure. Composed-and-still is escape. Moving-with-feeling is embodiment.

In the source session, the client opened her eyes closed at the very start (escape) and again later when Joe directed her into the doubt with heart open (embodiment). Joe paused to show both, side by side, so the listener could distinguish them:

"When you say doubt, she's looking down. You can see the shame. You can see her tucking her belly a little bit. And now you're like, oh, the heart. Now watch what happens right here to her face... It's like that moment of like, she's, it literally feels like the emotion is moving her physically in the body. So it's not about how do I suppress this emotion? It's actually alive in her. You can see her, her head kind of move like a wave. And that's, it's an interesting thing to know the difference between, oh, I'm closing my eyes and I'm dissociative, I'm separating myself from myself, I'm closing my eyes and I'm stepping more deeply into myself." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The distinction matters for the coach assigning this move as homework. The client needs to be able to distinguish the two for themselves, because the practice can run in either direction. Closed-eyes that lets the doubt move through is the practice working. Closed-eyes that suppresses the doubt is the practice being co-opted by the same regulation reflex it was meant to bypass. The somatic distinction is what tells the practitioner which mode they are in.

## The Larger Frame: Welcoming Doubt Is the Authentic Confidence

The move's deepest teaching is articulated by Joe in the breakdown: the work is not getting rid of the doubt. The work is the relationship to the doubt. See [[confidence-is-running-from-doubt]] for the concept this move enacts.

"There's this perpetual, if you look through the tape, there's this perpetual running from the doubt. And this is her first time stepping fully into the doubt. I have to be confident is running from the doubt. We all doubt. We, none of us move with 100% confidence. The question is how much can you welcome the doubt is the difference, not if you have it or not." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The frame matters because it positions the move as not a fix for doubt but a different relationship to it. The doubt remains. The fight ends. The change is in the relationship, which is also where most of the suffering lived. See [[the-issue-isnt-the-issue-its-how-you-react]] for the underlying principle.

The move is therefore not in the same category as confidence-building exercises. It is in a different category entirely: doubt-welcoming practices. The category distinction is part of the move's correct application. A coach who assigns this move while still operating in the confidence-building paradigm will inadvertently corrupt the move into one more attempt to make the doubt go away, which is the structure the move was supposed to interrupt.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the client reports the dance-state (or whatever anchor) is much closer:** the move landed. Confirm and assign the practice. "So you've got two jobs. [The other practice]. And every time you go into your head spin of doubt, move into opening your heart to the doubt."

**If the client reports the anchor-state is not closer:** some interfering material is still present. Often it is more of the blocker than the heart-opening has yet contacted (fear underneath the doubt, anger underneath the fear). The reasonable next move is to acknowledge what surfaced and either work with it briefly or assign it as something to notice over the next week.

**If the client reports a new emotion arriving (as in the source session: "and there's some fear"):** treat the new emotion as part of what the heart is opening to. "Just keep loving the doubt." The instruction includes the fear that is part of the doubt-complex. The body does not need to discriminate between the layers; the heart-opening welcomes whatever is there.

**If the client tries to make the doubt go away with the heart-opening:** name that this is not the goal. The doubt stays. The relationship to it changes. The doubt-going-away may happen as a downstream effect, but it is not what the move is for.

## The Two-Job Closing

In the source session, this move was the second of two practices Joe prescribed at the end. The first was the anger-moving practice (move the dominating anger every day). The two together formed the take-home structure:

"So you've got two jobs. Move the anger every day. Move the anger. Not, not that kind of victimy anger, but that, that dominant anger. Don't do it at anybody... And every time you go into your head spin of doubt, move into opening your heart to the doubt." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Stuck In Life When I Know My Patterns?

The two-job structure is worth noticing as a pattern. One practice is for the underlying material (the anger, the need, the held energy). The other practice is for the surface symptom (the doubt, the anxiety, the inner critic). The first is the source-level work. The second is the in-the-moment work. Together they cover both the daily energetic maintenance and the in-the-moment relationship to the recurring blocker.

The pattern generalizes: when closing a session, consider both — what is the daily practice for the underlying material, and what is the in-the-moment practice for the surface symptom. The two-job framing makes both visible and actionable.

## Dialogue Example

The full closing sequence:

- **Joe:** "So I just have, I have a hack for you and then this'll, this'll do it. So I want you to go into the doubt for a moment. All the doubt. It's not going to be the best. You're going to fuck it up. No one's going to pay attention. No one's going to listen. Yeah. You feel the doubt?"
- **Client:** "Yep."
- **Joe:** "And open your heart to it. Love the doubt."
- **Client:** "And there's some fear."
- **Joe:** "Just keep loving the doubt. How close is that to the way you feel when you dance?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, much closer."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So you've got two jobs. Move the anger every day. Move the anger. Not, not that kind of victimy anger, but that, that dominant anger. Don't do it at anybody. I mean, you can do it at somebody, but just not with them in the room, right? You can like move that anger at people, but just not with them in the room or whatever. And every time you go into your head spin of doubt, move into opening your heart to the doubt."
- **Client:** "Mm-hmm. Yeah, I can feel that really grounded."
- *(Source: Why Am I Stuck In Life When I Know My Patterns?)*

The exchange shows the full move plus the two-job closing. The client's final report — "really grounded, belly energy" — is the somatic confirmation that the session has landed in a place that can be returned to.

## Tone

Quiet and direct in the entry into the doubt. The induction of the blocker uses the client's specific story material ("it's not going to be the best, you're going to fuck it up, no one's going to listen") so the body is contacting the actual blocker, not a generic one. Reading back the client's specific catastrophic content makes the entry land.

Warm and instructive in the heart-opening. "Open your heart to it. Love the doubt." Said as a simple instruction, not as a sweeping spiritual frame. The smallness of the instruction is part of what allows the body to actually do it.

Reaching for the anchor with genuine curiosity. "How close is that to the way you feel when you dance?" The question is asked as if the coach genuinely wants to know — because the answer carries diagnostic information about whether the move landed.

Clear and practical in the prescription. "You've got two jobs." The closing is operational, not ceremonial.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not use the move as the primary work.** The move closes a session; it does not open one. Asking the client to love the doubt before the underlying work has happened produces performance, not welcoming.
- **Do not skip the anchor.** "Open your heart to the doubt" without a known felt state to reach for tends to land as an abstract instruction. The anchor is what makes it operational.
- **Do not let the client make the loving an effort.** If the body has to strain to love the doubt, the loving is now another should. Back off, find a closer anchor, or assign the practice as something to attempt rather than something to perform now.
- **Do not promise the doubt will go away.** The mechanism is the changed relationship, not the elimination. Selling the move on doubt-elimination installs the management agenda that defeats the move.
