# Announce the Behavior Change as Meta-Context First

## The Move

Before changing how you show up with a group (a team, a partner, a family), announce the change as meta-context. Name what you have been doing, name what you are going to start doing, and ask them how they want to receive it. Then start the new behavior.

The move is the difference between "I am going to start asking for what I want" (just doing it and surprising people) and "I want to tell you that I am going to start asking for what I want, and I want to know how you want to hear them, and how important hearing them is to you."

"You could say things like, hey, how do you want to hear my wants? How important are my wants to hear for you? So you can actually get the feedback. They're like, yeah, we've been waiting for you. Please tell us what you want. And you can ask them how they want to receive it, how they want you to deliver it. All that stuff is all flexible and can be something that, that agreement makes the whole thing easier. The conscious, so the principle there is that you bring it forward in a conscious way in a meta-context first." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## Why This Works

A behavior change without announcement registers as inconsistency. The team or partner experiences the new behavior as out of character and starts adjusting around it without knowing it is the new normal. The change is fragile because no one has signed up for it.

A behavior change with meta-context becomes an explicit shift. People know what is coming, know it is intentional, and have been asked how they want to receive it. The change is now a shared project rather than a unilateral departure.

The meta-context also surfaces information the changer cannot get otherwise: how the group actually wants to receive the new behavior, what they have been waiting for, where they have concerns. The information shapes the implementation.

## The Two-Part Structure

**Part 1: Name the old and the new.**
"For years I have not been asking for what I want here. I have been trying to get the group intelligence to surface its wants without putting mine in. I am going to experiment with bringing my wants in more directly."

**Part 2: Ask them how they want to receive it.**
"How do you want to hear my wants? How important is it to you to hear them? What would make it easier to receive them? And I want to be able to trust you to say no when my want is not your want."

The second part is the move that distinguishes this from a unilateral announcement. It invites the group into the change rather than imposing it.

## Why Asking How They Want to Receive It Matters

Without the asking, the changer is still in unilateral mode. They have shifted from hiding the want to expressing the want, but they have not yet shifted from acting alone to acting in relationship.

The asking does several things:
- It gives the group permission to shape the change so it works for everyone
- It reveals that the group often wants the new behavior more than the changer expected
- It transfers responsibility for the change from the changer alone to the group
- It establishes a precedent for the group to give ongoing feedback on the new pattern

The move is therefore not just announcement. It is the construction of a new agreement.

## What It Produces

The team's relief at being asked is often immediate. The team has been navigating the absence of expressed wants for as long as it has existed. Being asked how they want to receive expressed wants is the first time they have been treated as participants in the want-economy of the group.

The common response: "Yeah, we have been waiting for you. Please tell us what you want." The team turns out to have been hoping for the shift the changer was afraid to make.

## What Not to Do With This Move

- Do not use the meta-context as a substitute for the actual behavior change. Announcing it and then not doing it is worse than doing it without announcing.
- Do not turn the asking into a performance of humility. The point is real information about how to land the new behavior, not the appearance of consultation.
- Do not let the group's preferences fully determine the new behavior. The change is yours. Their input shapes delivery, not whether to do it.
- Do not use the meta-context to pre-apologize for the change. The new behavior is not a transgression.

## When to Use This Move

- When a leader is shifting from hidden wants to expressed wants (see [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]])
- When a partner is changing a long-standing relational pattern
- When a parent is changing how they show up with their kids
- When a team member is starting to bring directness they have been withholding
- When the change is significant enough that people will notice and need to recalibrate

## The Tone

Conversational, exploratory, light. The move works when it is delivered as "I want to tell you about something I am going to try" rather than "I have made an important decision about how I will be from now on." The heavier delivery makes the change about the changer's identity. The lighter delivery makes it about the relationship.
