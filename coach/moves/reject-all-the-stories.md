# Reject All the Stories

## The Move

The client is producing stories — explanations for why the pattern is true, reasons the problem is hard, narratives that justify the status quo. The stories are the defense's preferred medium. Engaging with any of them invites more of them.

The move is to explicitly reject all the stories for a moment, in one sentence, before the conversation proceeds. Not to dismiss the stories permanently. Not to argue against any specific story. To set them aside temporarily so the actual structural work can happen.

The form Joe uses:

"Okay, so you've got — what I notice is that your brain has a lot of stories as to why it's true, but I want to, like, reject all those stories for just a second." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The sentence has three parts:
1. Acknowledge that the stories exist and are about something real ("your brain has a lot of stories as to why it's true").
2. Take ownership of the rejection rather than asking the client to do it ("I want to reject").
3. Bound the rejection temporally ("for just a second").

Each part does specific work. The acknowledgment prevents the rejection from landing as dismissal. The ownership keeps the client from having to defend the rejection — Joe is the one doing it. The temporal bound makes the rejection a procedural move, not a value judgment about the stories.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the client opens the session with multiple intertwined explanations for why the problem is hard
- When the client responds to each direct question with a story about why the question is harder than it sounds
- When the client uses the word "because" repeatedly — every "because" is a story
- When the brain is actively generating new stories in response to Joe's interventions
- When [[abstract-framing-as-defense]] has produced not just abstraction but a thicket of explanatory narratives
- When [[complicating-it-as-the-distraction]] is firing through narrative complication rather than analytical complication
- When the stories share a structure — they all explain why the same thing is true — and engaging with one would mean engaging with all of them

The move arrives early in the session, often right after the opening. The stories tend to be in place from the first minute. Letting them stand for many minutes builds the implicit contract that the session will be about understanding the stories. Rejecting them early establishes that the session will not work this way.

## Why It Works

The stories are not the work; they are what the work is being avoided through. Engaging with the stories — believing them, arguing with them, analyzing them — reinforces the implicit frame that the client is correctly understanding their situation and the route through is more understanding. The frame is wrong. The route through requires action that the stories have been making impossible to imagine.

Rejecting the stories does not require disproving them. The stories may be entirely accurate. Their accuracy is irrelevant to the move. The move is procedural: for the next few minutes of this conversation, we will not be in story-mode. We will be in something else. After we have done some other work, the stories can come back if they want.

The temporal bound is what makes the rejection tolerable. If the move were "your stories are wrong and we are dispensing with them," the client would defend the stories and the session would derail. With the bound — "for just a second" — the client can tolerate the temporary suspension. By the time the second is over, the work has usually moved enough that the stories no longer have the same grip.

The ownership matters. Joe says "I want to reject," not "you need to set aside." The phrasing places the rejection inside Joe's coaching method rather than as a demand on the client. The client does not have to defend their stories; they only have to allow Joe a moment to operate without them.

## What It Produces

Usually the client agrees, briefly, with a small "okay" or "and, and okay" or similar. The agreement is procedural; the client is not committing to anything beyond the temporary suspension.

The agreement opens a small window in which the structural work can happen. Joe typically uses the window to:
- Restate the situation in concrete simple terms ("you want to start your own business, you do some things, you get distracted")
- Confirm the simple statement ("is that generally what's going on?")
- Then ask the structural question that the stories had been preventing ("what's wrong with the question, how do I start my own business?")

Once the structural question is in the room, the stories tend to return — but now they have to compete with the structural framing. The competing frames often produce the recognition that the stories have been the avoidance. The client sees the loop from outside.

If the client refuses the rejection — if they insist on continuing to elaborate the stories — the refusal is data. The stories are the work the client wants to do. The work the client wants to do is the avoidance. The next move is to name this directly. See [[complicating-it-as-the-distraction]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client agrees to the bracketing and engages with the structural work that follows: the work proceeds. Joe will often return to specific stories later in the session when the felt material has been opened up enough that the stories no longer have charge. The stories then become useful data ("oh, so that story about not being ready was the contained excitement").

If the client agrees verbally but immediately produces a new story: Joe names the new story too. "Even that — that you have to figure out the underlying pattern first — is also a story." The naming continues until the client either sees the loop or refuses to. Either outcome is workable.

If the client resists the rejection: Joe usually does not insist. Instead, he names the resistance itself. "I notice you're holding on to needing the stories. What's the danger in setting them aside for a minute?" The naming converts the resistance into the new material to work with.

If the client agrees and the stories simply do not return: rare but it happens. Often when the client has been working with Joe before and recognizes the move. The session can proceed straight to the structural work.

## Dialogue Example

The full move and its immediate effect:

- **Client:** "Um, I want to, like, become my own business owner. Like, I have something that I've started, but I recognize, like, even through watching some of your videos, I'm having a hard time stepping into the identity of somebody who can provide for herself in, in that way."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so you've got — what I notice is that your brain has a lot of stories as to why it's true, but I want to, like, reject all those stories for just a second."
- **Client:** "And, and okay."
- **Joe:** "You want to start your own business. You do some things to start your own business, and then you get distracted, and then you go to what's comfortable. Is that generally what's going on?"
- **Client:** "Yes."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

The exchange shows the move's full arc. The client produces an explanation for why the problem is hard (the identity-stepping story). Joe acknowledges the explanation exists, takes ownership of rejecting it temporarily, and immediately restates the situation as a simple structural loop. The client confirms the simple loop ("yes"). The session has been converted from understanding-the-problem mode to working-the-structure mode in about fifteen seconds.

The "and, and okay" in the client's response is the procedural agreement. The client has not committed to anything more than allowing Joe a moment. The moment is enough.

## Tone

Direct, friendly, slightly impatient. The same tone as [[abstract-framing-as-defense]]'s interruption — the impatience signals that Joe is taking the client seriously enough to redirect them away from a route that would not help.

The acknowledgment ("your brain has a lot of stories") is delivered without judgment. The brain having stories is presented as a fact about how brains work, not as a failure of the client. The implicit message is: this is what brains do, this is what we're working with, here is how we move past it.

The temporal bound ("for just a second") can be said almost casually, as if the rejection were a small procedural matter. The casualness reduces the stakes. If the move were delivered as a major intervention, the client would brace. Delivered casually, the client tends to allow it.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not [[fuck-the-right-answer]]. That move refuses the intelligent-sounding answer in favor of the felt one. This move refuses the explanatory stories in favor of structural work. The two can co-occur but they target different defenses.

- It is not [[surrender-your-way-out-not-think-your-way-out]]. That move closes the cognitive exit entirely for the session. This move closes one specific cognitive exit (the stories) temporarily.

- It is not the same as ignoring the client. Joe acknowledges the stories exist before rejecting them. The acknowledgment matters; without it, the rejection would feel dismissive.

- It is not a permanent rejection. The stories can come back if they have something useful to add later. The move just clears space for one phase of the work.

- It is not a request for the client to reject their own stories. Joe does the rejecting. The client only has to tolerate the temporary absence of the stories. The placement of the rejection in the coach, not the client, is part of why the move works.
