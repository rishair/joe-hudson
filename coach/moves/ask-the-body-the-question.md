# Ask the Body the Question (Stop, Feel the Whole Body, Hear the Body's Answer)

## The Move

When a person has been working a question in the head and the head has not produced an answer that lands, redirect the question to the body. The move has three steps in sequence:

1. **Name what is happening.** "Our whole conversation, you've been up here thinking."
2. **Instruct the body shift.** "I want you to stop for a moment and just feel your whole body. Whole thing."
3. **Ask the body the original question.** "And if I'm asking your body, what's progress? What is your body's response? Not your head's."

The question that gets redirected is whatever question the head has been spinning on. In the source session, the head had been working the question "what counts as progress?" and finding only external answers (career achievement, things to show to others). When Joe asked the body the same question, the body gave a different answer: "It's movement."

This is distinct from [[what-does-your-heart-say]] (which is a redirect from head to heart, typically for relational or value-laden questions) and from [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]] (which is anchoring a felt shift after an insight). This move uses the body as an answering instrument for a specific abstract question that the head has been unable to answer well.

## When Joe Uses This

- After a person has been intellectualizing about a question for an extended part of the session and the intellectual answers are not landing.
- When the head's answers are all in one register (external, comparative, performance-oriented) and the body might have an answer in a different register.
- When the person reports being "stuck in their head" about a question.
- After [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]] has surfaced what is underneath a decision and the person is ready to hear what the body wants rather than what the head can defend.
- When the question is abstract enough that the head will produce only abstract answers ("what is progress?", "what is success?", "what is a good life?") and the body's answer will be specific and concrete.
- When the person has been performing the right answer rather than producing the actual answer. See [[fuck-the-right-answer]].
- At the end of a session, to give the body the chance to say what wants to be said, especially after the head has been working hard.

The move is especially powerful when the question being asked is one the person has been asking themselves for a long time. The head has long since exhausted its inventory of answers. The body has not been asked.

## What It Produces

The body's answer usually arrives quickly once the person has actually shifted attention. It is often a single word or a short phrase. It does not look like the head's answers. It is not a list. It is not qualified. It is not a comparison.

In the source session: "It's movement. Yeah, yeah." Two words. The head had been producing elaborate answers about external achievement, recognition, professional progress. The body, asked the same question, said "movement." The shift was immediate.

The body's answer often surprises the person. They may laugh. They may smile. They may report that the answer came from somewhere they did not know was working. The surprise is part of the diagnostic value: a body-answer that surprises is usually one the head had been ruling out or had no access to.

Sometimes the body's answer contradicts the head's answer. When this happens, the contradiction is the work. The person now has two reports: what the head has been defending, and what the body actually says. Joe will usually side with the body's answer without arguing the head's answer down — the body's answer makes its own case once it has been heard.

Sometimes the body produces no answer right away. This is also information. It may mean the body has not been asked anything for a long time and the channel is rusty. The follow-up is to stay with the body and wait, or to ask a simpler body-question first (what does my body feel like right now?) to re-open the channel.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the body's answer lands, Joe stays with it. He does not interpret. He does not add. He often mirrors what the body said and lets it sit. "Look at that smile" is what he said in the source session — pointing at the body's confirmation of its own answer (the smile was the body's check on its own report) without analyzing.

If the body's answer contradicts what the head had been saying, Joe makes the contradiction visible without resolving it. "You said it's been miserable for the last 6 months. You do not look miserable right now, my dear." The contradiction surfaces the question: which report is more reliable? The body's report usually wins because the body's report just produced the visible smile.

If the body's answer opens onto an insight, Joe extends with a related body-question. In the source session, after the body said "movement," Joe extended: "How much of this misery is just the being in your head looking for external instead of being in your body looking for the internal?" The head's pattern (looking for external) and the body's pattern (looking for the internal) become the two modes the person can now choose between. The body's answer is no longer just an answer to one question; it is a template.

If the head tries to take back over and re-evaluate the body's answer ("oh, but is that really progress?"), Joe stays with the body. He may repeat the instruction: "Go back to your body for a minute. Just be there with your body. Keep your sensation in your body." The repetition holds the channel open.

If the body's answer is short and the head wants to add to it, let the short answer be short. The body's answers are usually irreducible. Adding to them dilutes them.

## Dialogue Example

After a 10-minute conversation in which the person had been intellectualizing about progress as something external and visible to others, Joe gave the instruction:

- **Joe:** "Yeah, I think great. So do me this favor. Our whole conversation you've been up here thinking. I want you to stop for a moment and just feel your whole body, whole thing. Yeah. And if I'm asking your body what's progress, what is your body's response? Not your head's. Look at that smile."
- **Person:** "It's movement. Yeah, yeah."
- **Joe:** "You said it's been miserable for the last 6 months. You do not look miserable right now, my dear. How much of this misery is just the being in your head looking for external instead of being in your body looking for the internal? Oh, it can't be that easy. I just saw -- yeah, there you go. It can be that easy."

*(Source: How Do I Not Burn Out Again?)*

The setup ("our whole conversation you've been up here thinking") names the pattern without judgment. The instruction ("stop for a moment and just feel your whole body, whole thing") gives the body something specific to do — feel the whole thing, not a part. The redirect ("if I'm asking your body what's progress, what is your body's response? Not your head's") asks the same question that has been the conversation's whole thread, but addressed to a different organ.

The "look at that smile" before the person even speaks names the body's report through the face. The body answered before the person spoke; the smile was the report.

## Why This Works

The head and the body know different things. The head knows what has been argued, learned, defended. The body knows what is alive in the moment, what is wanted, what is true at the level of felt experience. When a question has been working the head and finding nothing, asking the body the same question is going to a different source.

The body also has access to data the head does not. The smile, the tightness, the lightness, the contraction — these are reports the head does not generate. The head can describe a state; the body can produce one. Asking the body the question is asking for both the report and the production at once.

The body's answer is also often pre-verbal. The smile precedes the words. The person who is shifting toward the body's answer is doing so before they articulate it. The articulation is the body's report on its own state, not the head's report on the body. This is why the answers are short and immediate: they are arriving from the place that is producing them.

## Tone

Warm, slow, intimate. The instruction is not a demand. It is an invitation. The voice drops in volume. The pacing widens. The body responds to the tone the question is asked in. A demanding tone produces a defensive body. An inviting tone produces an open body. The tone IS part of what makes the body willing to answer.

The "look at that smile" or the "yeah, there you go" that comes after the answer lands is delivered with a quiet delight. Joe is not surprised by the body's answer (he expected it could come); he is welcoming it. The welcome is what tells the person it was the right thing to listen to.

## Variations

The same move can be applied to many questions:

- "What is progress?" → ask the body
- "What do I want?" → ask the body
- "What is success?" → ask the body
- "What is the next step?" → ask the body
- "What matters?" → ask the body
- "What does my heart want with this person?" → ask the body
- "How do I feel about this?" → ask the body

The general form: any question the head has been working unsuccessfully can be redirected to the body. The body's answer may be different, and that difference is the data.

The wording variant matters. "What does your body say about X?" is one version. "What is your body's response to X?" is another. "If I asked your body X, what would it say?" is a third, which has the advantage of distancing the question from the head's authorship — the head is being asked to relay rather than to answer.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not [[what-does-your-heart-say]]. That move is heart-not-mind for relational questions. This move is body-not-mind for abstract questions that have been working the head.
- It is not [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]]. That move is anchoring a felt shift after insight. This move is asking the body for an answer to a specific question.
- It is not [[not-sometimes-right-now]]. That move pulls a person from abstract to present. This move uses the body specifically as an answering source.
- It is not a meditation instruction. The person is not being asked to observe the body neutrally. They are being asked to query the body as an oracle and receive its specific response.
- It is not [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]. The body is being asked a question; whatever answer comes is the answer. The instruction is not to produce a body-state; it is to listen for what the body is already reporting.
