# Apologize to Your Partner Without Shame for the Distance Pattern

## The Move

When a client surfaces that they have been the receiving end of a long-term [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic|chase configuration]] — the partner has been pursuing connection and the client has been pulling back for years — Joe invites a direct apology to the partner for the pattern itself.

The instruction is precise:
- Use the partner's name aloud
- No shame in the delivery
- Fully empowered
- For making them chase when all the client actually wanted was the connection

The setup as Joe gives it:

"Okay, so see what it's like without any shame to apologize to your husband for making him chase you when all you really want is that connection. Use his name, but no shame, just like fully empowered." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The apology is not "I'm sorry I'm a bad partner." It is "I'm sorry I've been making you do the work of getting to me when actually I want what you're reaching for." The structure inverts the usual defensive shape: it acknowledges the pattern, owns the part the client has been playing, and explicitly names the underlying want that the pattern has been hiding.

## When Joe Uses It

The move requires specific prerequisites:

- The client has named or admitted the pattern in this session ("100% accurate" when the chase configuration was described)
- The client is in a long-term relationship where the partner has been the pursuer for years (not a brief dynamic)
- The client has begun to feel the cost of the configuration on the partner, not just on themselves
- The client has acknowledged that what they actually want is the connection the partner has been reaching for
- The client is open to a live in-session exercise, not still in pure intellectual analysis

The move is not for partners who have been emotionally absent due to abuse, trauma, or genuine incompatibility. It is for the specific case of the avoidant-side of a chase configuration where the partner's pursuit has been steady and the client's distance has been the structural feature.

## The "No Shame" Instruction Matters

The single most important component of the setup is the no-shame clause. A shame-based apology for this pattern installs the deflated identity ("I am a bad partner") more deeply, which is the same structure that has been generating the distance in the first place. The pattern cannot be undone by adding more of the structure that creates it.

The upright, empowered apology (see [[upright-apology]]) does something structurally different. It owns the pattern from the position of agency. The body delivers "I have been doing this. I see it. I want differently." That delivery does not require the deflation of self-worth. It requires the lifting of the self into a position from which it can see what it has been doing.

The "fully empowered" cue is the body cue. Joe is asking the client to do this from an erect body, not a collapsed one. The apology has to come from someone who is here, not from someone who is making themselves smaller in penance. The smallness is the old move.

## Why the Apology to the Partner (and Not to the Self Yet)

The move runs the apology to the partner first because:

1. The partner-as-recipient gives the client something concrete to address. The "I've been making you chase me" has an external referent. The body knows how to apologize to someone else more readily than to itself.

2. The apology to the partner exposes the pattern as something the client has been doing to the partner. This is information the client has often been able to avoid by framing the pattern as something happening to the client. The apology, by addressing the partner, names the client as the actor in the configuration.

3. The apology to the partner usually surfaces grief about the years of the pattern. This grief is the material that needs to move. The grief moves more readily when directed at someone — even a stand-in or an imagined partner — than when held internally.

4. The apology to the partner is the prerequisite for the eventual apology to the self. The pattern's symmetric form ("I made my husband chase me; I made myself chase a feeling that wasn't here") becomes available only after the first apology has landed. See [[apologize-to-yourself-for-holding-back-from-feeling]] for the self-apology that often follows.

## What This Move Produces

Common responses:

- A flatter, less defended delivery than the client expected. The shame-free instruction removes the usual emotional armor.
- A body softening during the delivery. The pattern releases its grip slightly as it is named aloud.
- An emergence of the underlying want. After delivering "I'm sorry I made you chase me," the client often spontaneously says or feels what they actually want from the partner. The wanting was being hidden by the running.
- Grief about the years of the configuration. The "22 years" recognition often arrives here. The grief is appropriate and should be held.
- A felt opening to receive what the partner has been offering. The apology, by acknowledging the pattern, creates the conditions for the love to actually land afterward.

## Dialogue Example

After the chase-configuration has been named and the client has confirmed it ("100%"):

- **Joe:** "Okay, so see what it's like without any shame to apologize to your husband for making him chase you when all you really want is that connection. Use his name, but no shame, just like fully empowered."
- **Client:** "Yeah. Brad, I feel very sorry that you had to continually chase me to get that connection."
- **Joe:** "And what do you want?"
- **Client:** "I want the same thing. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "And what do you want him to do when he's chasing and you're pulling back? What do you deeply most want from him in that moment?"
- **Client:** "To keep coming at me, to save me."

*(Source: If You Can't Accept Compliments)*

The apology lands. The body softens. The next move ([[what-do-you-want]]) opens into the want that the pattern was hiding. The apology made the wanting accessible.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the apology is delivered with shame, Joe re-issues. The shame is the old pattern. He may say "without any shame" again, or have the client put their body in a more erect position before re-delivering.

If the apology produces tears, Joe stays with the tears. The tears are usually the grief about the years. The grief deserves time.

If the apology produces a sudden access to the underlying want, Joe pivots to [[what-do-you-want]] immediately. The wanting that has been hidden under the distance is the next layer of material.

If the apology surfaces guilt about the partner ("he must have suffered so much"), Joe redirects gently. The work is not to make the client feel worse on the partner's behalf. The work is to acknowledge the pattern and to begin to do something different.

If the client cannot bring themselves to do the apology, Joe does not push. The inability often points to upstream work needed on the [[inherent-goodness]] required to apologize from agency rather than collapse.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not a request to actually apologize to the partner in real life later.** That is a separate move requiring separate conditions. This is internal work using the partner as the addressee.
- **It is not shame.** The no-shame clause is structural. With shame, the move installs more of what it is supposed to dissolve.
- **It is not self-criticism in apology costume.** "I'm so sorry for being such a bad partner" is the deflated ego. The move is "I have been doing this thing, and I see it now, and I am sorry that you have been on the receiving end of it."
- **It is not the whole work.** The apology is one move in a sequence. Without the follow-on work on [[what-do-you-want]] and the receiving of the partner's actual love (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]), the apology is incomplete.
- **It is not always indicated.** If the partner has not actually been the pursuer, or if the dynamic is more complex than the simple chase configuration, the move does not fit.

## How This Differs From [[apologize-to-the-other-for-what-you-do-to-yourself]]

The reverse-direction apology has the client apologize to the partner for what the client does to *themselves* (e.g., the composure pattern that the client imposes on both). The distance-apology has the client apologize to the partner for what the client does to *the partner* (the chase configuration the client has been generating).

The two moves can both be appropriate in the same session if the client is running multiple patterns. The distance-apology addresses the relational structure directly; the reverse-direction apology addresses the pattern of self-imposition. Sequence them based on what is most live in the session.

## Tone

Warm, direct, slightly insistent on the no-shame cue. The "see what it's like without any shame" carries the energy of "let's try this differently." The "fully empowered" carries the energy of "I'm asking you to stand up while you say this, not to collapse." The tone does not soften the apology; it shapes its container.

After the apology lands, the tone moves to soft. The client has just done something hard. The acknowledgment of what they did is part of the move's completion.
