# Coach Defensive Behavior Out of the Meeting

## The Move

When defensiveness shows up in a meeting, name it and ask the person to step out until they can return non-defensively. The exit is not punishment; it is a pause that protects the meeting from the gum-up that defensive behavior produces, and gives the defensive person time to find their ground.

"You can try to coach them out of it or you can be really direct and say, 'Wow, we don't do defensiveness here. Like, can you please leave the meeting until you can come back in a non-defensive way and show up?' Lots of ways of handling defensiveness, but it's really critical to make it so that everybody feels safe and comfortable sharing whatever it is they need to share." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## Why the Move Is Necessary

Defensive behavior in a meeting shuts down communication. If the leader does not address it, people stop saying things to the defensive person, then stop saying things at all. The meeting becomes a careful negotiation around the defensive person rather than a working session.

"If a leader allows that to exist, that dynamic to exist, it'll definitely kill a team. Because what happens there is that if someone's getting defensive, then people on the team, if they're super functional, they'll be like, hey, you're defensive, what's happening here? But if they're not, then people start being scared to say things to that person and then communication gets all gummed up. And so allowing defensive behavior is just super toxic for a team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The cost of allowing defensiveness is paid by the team, not just the defensive person.

## The Tone That Makes the Move Land

The move can be done in a way that is itself defensive ("We don't allow that here, end of discussion") or in a way that is genuinely inviting ("Hey, I see you're being defensive, take a break, come on back, we'll continue.") The second lands; the first replicates the dynamic it is trying to stop.

"There's a huge difference between, we don't allow defensiveness here, which is in itself defensiveness, as compared to saying like, oh hey, like, I see that you're being defensive, you know, this isn't something that we do here, so take a break, you know, get back to yourself, come on back, and we'll continue the conversation." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The tone is matter-of-fact and warm. The defensive person is not being banished; they are being asked to take a breath.

## The Alternative: Coach Them Into Naming the Fear

A gentler form of the move is to help the defensive person convert the defensiveness into a direct statement of what is actually happening. The defensiveness is a way of saying "I am afraid right now" without saying so. Naming the fear directly is the functional alternative.

"You help them to say, 'I am afraid right now,' instead of being defensive. You know, 'This thing is bothering me,' or even ask the question is like, 'Oh, am I being told that I've done something wrong?' What's happening?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

This form treats the defensive behavior as a symptom and helps the person speak the underlying experience instead. It is closer to [[name-your-trigger-as-vulnerability]] but performed by the leader on behalf of someone else.

## What This Does Not Make Wrong

Brett raised the concern: "We don't do defensiveness here" can be heard as "You're not allowed to feel afraid right now." Joe agrees the wording matters. The move does not make the fear wrong; it interrupts the strategy the fear is using.

The defensive person's nervous system is detecting threat. That is real. What is being asked of them is not to stop feeling the threat but to handle it differently -- step out, regulate, come back. The fear is welcomed (see [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]]); the defensive strategy is not.

See [[safety-vs-defensiveness]] for the deeper distinction that makes this move workable. Feeling unsafe is not the same as being defensive. The move addresses the second without invalidating the first.

## When To Use This Move

- In a meeting where defensiveness has appeared and is starting to shut down others
- In a culture-setting moment when the team needs to know that defensive behavior is not the norm
- When the defensive person is operating on autopilot and is unlikely to notice the pattern on their own
- When the meeting has stakes that cannot accommodate the slowdown that working with the defense in-place would require

## When Not To Use This Move

- When the defensiveness is a rational response to actual unsafety in the room -- the room needs fixing, not the person
- When the person is too dysregulated to handle a public callout (then a private intervention)
- When the coach or leader making the move is themselves operating from defensiveness about the defensive person

## Tone

Direct and warm. Not harsh, not gentle in a way that softens the message into nothing. The closest analogue: a host asking a guest to step into the next room for a moment. The implication is that the meeting continues, the person is welcome back, and the issue is the behavior, not the person.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- Defensiveness has appeared in a meeting and is starting to gum up communication

- A coach or facilitator is in the room when defense shows up and a decision has to be made quickly
- The defensive behavior is a known pattern with a particular team member that the team has been working around for months
