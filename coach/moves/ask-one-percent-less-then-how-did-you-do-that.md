# Ask for One Percent Less Then "How Did You Do That?"

## The Move

Once a feeling has been scaled (see [[scale-the-feeling-1-to-100-with-anchors]]), ask if the person can shift it by one increment in the desired direction. If they say yes, ask how they did it. If they did it, ask how they did it again. The mechanism is in the doing. The person teaches themselves the move by being asked to name what they did.

The form Joe uses:

"You're 40% self-conscious right now. And can you make it 39?"

When the person says yes:

"How did you do that?"

When the person describes the mechanism, that mechanism becomes their tool. They have just discovered the actual move that reduces their self-consciousness, and they discovered it themselves through their own action. The coach did not teach them the move. The coach asked them to do the move and then asked them to name what they did.

## Why the Increment Is So Small

One percent is small enough to be unmistakably available. A person who would resist "make yourself feel better" cannot resist "make it 39 instead of 40." The smallness of the increment removes the implicit demand. The person discovers they can do the small thing without it feeling like an obligation.

The smallness also defeats the all-or-nothing trap that produces the original stuckness. The person had been operating as if the only acceptable outcome was the full elimination of the feeling. Asking for one percent reframes the practice as "make a small move," which is something a body can do without the resistance produced by a large demand.

The smallness also makes the move repeatable. After the first 39, the question becomes "can you make it 38?" The person can do it. After 38, 37. The increments compound. The person learns that they can keep shifting by one. Eventually the question "can you make it 0?" is not the right question; the right question is always just "can you make it one less?"

This connects to the larger principle of [[enjoy-this-just-a-little-bit-more]] and the 10% practice in [[ten-percent-more-connected-experiment]]. Small increments are not a compromise. They are the actual mechanism of transformation. The body learns through small shifts in the right direction.

## Why "How Did You Do That?"

This question is the second half of the move and is what makes the practice durable.

When the person shifts the feeling by one increment and is asked how, they have to look at what they actually did. They cannot answer in the abstract. They have to identify the specific shift they made. In doing so, they teach themselves their own mechanism.

The mechanism, once named, becomes a tool they can use later. The next time the feeling arises, they know exactly what to do to shift it by one — because they have already done it once and named it. The move stays with them. They do not need to remember a technique from a coach. They have their own technique, surfaced from their own action.

The question also locates agency in the person, not in the coach. The shift happened because of something *they* did. The naming of the mechanism reinforces that they are the one with the move, not the coach. This builds self-trust and reduces dependence on external coaching.

If the person says "I don't know how I did it," the question becomes "look at what just happened — what changed?" The mechanism is always discoverable when the person is invited to look at their own action. The "I don't know" is usually a reflex, not a final answer.

## Variations

The same structural move applies across many feelings:

- "You're at 40% anxious. Can you make it 39?"
- "You're at 70% angry. Can you make it 69?"
- "You're at 80% scared. Can you make it 79?"
- "You're at 20% in connection. Can you make it 21?"

The direction can also be reversed when the person has shifted in an unwanted direction. "You said the self-consciousness went up when you looked at yourself. How did you do that?" The mechanism that increased the feeling is also a useful tool to know about, because the person can stop doing it.

## What It Produces

- A small but real shift in the feeling, demonstrated in the body.
- The person's own naming of their own mechanism for producing that shift.
- A repeatable tool the person can use later without the coach.
- The recognition that the feeling is workable, not fixed.
- The recognition that the person is the one who shifted it, not the coach.
- The pattern can be iterated: 40 to 39, 39 to 38, and so on, with the mechanism surfaced each time.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "You're 40% self-conscious right now. And can you make it 39?"
- **Guest:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "How did you do that?"
- **Guest:** "Just looking at you on the Zoom instead of looking at me and you."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah. So how about just look at you for a minute on the Zoom?"
- **Guest:** [looks at self]
- **Joe:** "Did you become less or more self-conscious?"
- **Guest:** "Think more."
- **Joe:** "You said, I think. Yeah, you would know. So just look at yourself for a minute. See, see what— see how, how do you become just 1% less self-conscious looking at yourself? Oh, something just happened. What was that? That was good."
- **Guest:** "Just, I don't think— I just felt like taking up more space, um, like settling comfortably in the space."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Yeah. So connection with me made you less self-conscious. Connection with yourself just made you less self-conscious."

*(Source: How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?)*

Note the structure across the exchange. Joe asks for the 1% shift. The person names her mechanism (looking at Joe). Joe immediately tests the reverse mechanism (look at yourself) and asks what changed. The person reports the result. Joe asks again for a 1% shift in the new condition. The person discovers a different mechanism (taking up more space, settling into the body). Joe then names the larger pattern: both mechanisms are forms of connection — connection with the other, connection with self. The person has just discovered the operative principle by doing it twice in two different ways.

The closing observation by Joe — "connection with me made you less self-conscious; connection with yourself made you less self-conscious" — is the synthesis. The person now has a generalizable principle (connection cures self-consciousness) that emerged from her own discovered mechanisms, not from a coach's instruction.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not a goal-setting move. The goal is one increment. There is no end-state being targeted.
- This is not behavior change. The shift is in the felt state, not in behavior.
- This is not the coach giving a technique. The coach is asking for an action and asking for the name of the mechanism. The person produces both.
- This is not interpretation. The coach does not interpret how the person did it. The person names it themselves.
- This is not a manipulation to get the person to feel a particular way. The move is open to "I can't make it 39" as a real answer, in which case the work shifts to what is making the feeling stay at 40.

## Tone

Light, curious, slightly delighted. The "can you make it 39?" question carries a quality of "let's see what happens" rather than "you should be able to do this." The lightness is what makes the person willing to try.

The "how did you do that?" question is genuinely curious, not investigative. Joe is not testing the person. Joe wants to know. The wanting-to-know invites the person to look at their own action with the same curiosity.

When the shift happens, Joe acknowledges it specifically. "Oh, something just happened. What was that? That was good." The acknowledgment marks the moment so the person can identify it. The marking is part of the teaching.

## When to Combine With Other Moves

This move is almost always preceded by [[scale-the-feeling-1-to-100-with-anchors]]. The scale produces the number that this move asks to be shifted by one.

The move is often followed by [[wheres-you-go-naming-the-retreat-from-connection]] when the person, having shifted, retreats. The naming of the retreat is the same kind of present-moment work as the naming of the shift.

The move is also a building block for [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]] — once the presenting problem is in the room, the scaling + increment + mechanism sequence converts the live instance into a teaching moment.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not ask for big shifts.** "Can you make it 30?" defeats the move. The smallness of one increment is what makes it available.
- **Do not skip the "how did you do that?" question.** Without it, the person has shifted but does not own the mechanism. The mechanism is the durable artifact.
- **Do not interpret their mechanism for them.** "I think what you did was..." removes the agency. The person names their own mechanism. The coach reflects what they said, not what the coach thinks they did.
- **Do not chain too many iterations without breathing.** Three or four iterations of "and now 38, and now 37, and now 36" can become mechanical. Use the sequence to surface the underlying principle, then move on to apply that principle elsewhere.
- **Do not deploy when the person is not in the feeling.** The move requires the feeling to be live. Without the live feeling, "can you make it 39?" has no referent.
