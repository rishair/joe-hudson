# Get the Minimal Information You Need to Feel Safe

## The Move

A specific structural fix for leaders and parents exiting [[the-helper-trap]]. When the leader stops helping uninvited, the team's natural response is to go around them — do the thing without telling them, then show up either with "it's done" or with "it's not done." A leader cannot operate this way. The anxiety is too high. Without a structural alternative, the leader will revert to the helping.

The move is to define and agree to the minimal information channel that gives the leader enough to feel safe without recreating the intrusion. Not maximum information. Minimum. The criterion is the leader's anxiety baseline, not the leader's curiosity.

"You have to find a way that you can get the minimal amount of information you need so that you can feel safe about how it's going." -- Joe Hudson, When You Help Others, You're Asking Them to Hold Your Fear

## When Joe Uses It

The move comes up specifically in two situations:

1. **A leader is exiting [[the-helper-trap]]** and has agreed to stop helping uninvited (see [[the-helper-repair-conversation]]). The structural question becomes: how does the leader stay informed enough to fulfill their actual leadership responsibility without recreating the helping?

2. **A parent is recognizing the same dynamic with a child or teenager** and asking how to step back without abdicating the parental responsibility. "As a parent, you have a responsibility to make sure your child's happy and learning. As a leader, you have a responsibility to make sure that the organization is happy and healthy. And so you do need some information."

The move applies whenever there is a legitimate operational or developmental responsibility on the leader/parent's side AND a need to stop the constant intervention.

## What It Produces

A clean working agreement that resolves the leader's binary between micromanaging and abdicating. The third position is "ask explicitly for what you need to know, then leave the rest alone."

Predictable response from the team or child after the agreement is in place:

"Now, you might get some kickback on the information until they realize, oh, this means you're not going to be in my shit trying to help me all the time. Then they're going to be like, this is great. Yeah, I'll write the 2-minute report. Happy to do it." -- Joe Hudson, When You Help Others, You're Asking Them to Hold Your Fear

The initial kickback is information. The team has been reading the leader's requests for information as additional intrusion (because they have been). The agreement reframes the request: this is the channel that lets me leave you alone. Once the team experiences that the leader actually does leave them alone in exchange, the resistance dissolves. The 2-minute report becomes a small price for the absence of the leader being in their stuff.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The move presupposes a conversation, not a unilateral imposition. The leader explains what minimal information they actually need (be honest — not maximum information dressed as minimum), asks the team how to deliver it with the lowest overhead, and agrees on the channel.

The criterion for the leader's side is the leader's actual safety threshold. Not what the leader would prefer to know. What the leader needs to know to not be anxious enough to intervene.

"How do you get the minimal amount of information you need so that you know your child is good?" -- Joe Hudson, When You Help Others, You're Asking Them to Hold Your Fear

For most leaders, the actual minimum is much less than they imagine. The intuition that they need detailed status reports is the helper anxiety still talking. The structural test: what is the smallest amount of information that lets you sleep at night? Start there.

## Operational Forms

The form varies by context. The structural shape is the same: a low-overhead reporting mechanism agreed on with the receiver, used in exchange for the leader's discipline of not intervening.

**In an executive-team relationship.** A weekly written update covering specific status fields. A monthly 1-on-1. A 2-minute end-of-week voice note. The form is whatever both parties can sustain that delivers the leader's minimum.

**In a parent-teenager relationship.** A weekly check-in conversation. A shared family calendar with key items visible. An agreement that the teenager will say something if a specific kind of trouble shows up. The form is whatever the teenager is willing to deliver in exchange for the parent staying out.

**In a co-leadership relationship.** A specific recurring meeting with a defined agenda. A handoff protocol for cross-team decisions. The agreement is bilateral; both parties define their minimal-info needs.

The forms are not the move. The move is the upstream principle: ask for the minimum, deliver the leave-alone in exchange.

## Why This Is Not Yet Another Way to Help

A subtle point: the minimal-information channel could become a back door for the helping if the leader is not honest about their minimum. If the leader asks for "minimum" but the minimum is actually maximum-with-the-label-changed, the channel is the helper trap with a new uniform.

The honesty check: the information requested should be the kind that lets the leader confirm the work is on track, not the kind that lets the leader second-guess the team's decisions. Status of outcomes, not details of process. Did it ship; not how it was built. Are you happy at school; not what specific grade you got on what specific test.

If the leader notices their "minimal" request is creeping toward control, the helper anxiety has not been adequately addressed. The structural fix needs the third component of [[the-helper-repair-conversation]] in place — owning the fear privately rather than dissipating it through information-gathering.

## What It Avoids

This move avoids both poles of the leader's [[over-control-vs-hands-off-oscillation]]:

- **It avoids the over-control pole** because the leader has explicitly bounded what they will engage with. The minimal-info channel is the channel; everything else is left alone.
- **It avoids the hands-off pole** because the leader has not abdicated their legitimate responsibility. The minimum is real; they still know what they need to know.

The third position is engaged-but-bounded. The leader is fully a leader, with full information about what they actually need, and the team has full agency over everything else.

## Where the Conversation Sits

The minimal-information conversation is a structural component of [[the-helper-repair-conversation]] for leaders. It is not optional. Without it, the apology and the agreement will not hold because the leader's anxiety will spike when they no longer have access to the team's internal workings.

Order matters:
1. The apology (see [[the-helper-repair-conversation]] component 1).
2. The agreement to stop helping uninvited (component 2).
3. The agreement on the minimal-information channel (this move).
4. The internal commitment to own the fear privately (component 3 of the repair).

The minimal-information channel is the leader's structural support for component 3. The leader will still have anxiety; the channel gives the anxiety somewhere legitimate to go that does not undo the agreement.

## Tone

Joe delivers this move with operational directness. The texture is not therapeutic; it is practical. The leader has a real job. The team has real work. The agreement is a working agreement, not an emotional event. The minimal-information channel is built like a piece of infrastructure — minimal, sustainable, fit for purpose.

The lightness in the delivery: Joe is amused that the team will resist the request until they see it as the price of being left alone, then will deliver the 2-minute report cheerfully because the math is so favorable on their side.

## In Coaching

When a leader presents [[the-helper-trap]] concern and is asking how to step back without the team falling apart, this is the move. It is the practical answer to the practical question.

The coaching work is to help the leader find their actual minimum, not the minimum they would prefer to claim. The actual minimum is usually less than the leader thinks. The work is to surface what they really need (often: confirmation that nothing is on fire) and to design the lightest mechanism that delivers it.

A useful coaching question: "If you got one piece of information per week, what would it be?" The leader's first answer is often too detailed. The follow-up: "And if you could only get one thing every two weeks?" The answer continues to compress. Often the actual minimum turns out to be a single-line status, delivered on a regular cadence, that takes the team 2 minutes to produce.
