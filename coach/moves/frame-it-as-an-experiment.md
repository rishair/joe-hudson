# Frame It As an Experiment (For the Skeptic)

## The Move

When introducing a practice that an intellectually-oriented person is likely to resist as soft, woo, or unrigorous, the move is to frame the practice as a time-bounded experiment with clear conditions for stopping. The framing meets the skeptic on their own terms: they value evidence, they value rigor, they value the right to disagree with conclusions that the data does not support. The experiment frame honors all three.

The move is the entry point. Without it, the skeptic blocks the practice before it gets to demonstrate anything. With it, the skeptic has agreed to test it for a defined period and look at the results.

"For the next 3 weeks, we're gonna start our meetings this way and we're gonna note how our decision-making changes and if it's effective. If it's ineffective, then we won't do it." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

## The Structure

Four components, all required:

**Time bound.** "For the next 3 weeks." Specific. Not "let's try it for a while." The skeptic can hold their breath for three weeks; they cannot agree to indefinite practice.

**Specified observation.** "We're going to note how our decision-making changes and if it's effective." The thing being measured is named. The skeptic knows what to watch for and what counts as evidence.

**Conditions for stopping.** "If it's ineffective, then we won't do it." The skeptic retains veto power if the evidence does not support continuation. The practice is not a mandate; it is a test.

**Inclusion of the negative scan.** "Because we're being positive, we're not being Pollyannish. I want to hear everything that's going wrong. I just also want to hear everything that's going right." The framing makes clear that the new practice is additive, not substitutive. The skeptic's concern that the new practice will replace rigorous problem-analysis is preempted.

## Why It Works

The skeptic resists because they suspect the practice is unfalsifiable. The experiment frame makes the practice falsifiable. If it does not work, it will not be continued. The very act of agreeing to the experiment is the act of preserving the skeptic's intellectual standards rather than asking them to suspend those standards.

The frame also flips the social dynamic. Without the frame, the skeptic is positioned as the obstacle to the leader's desired practice. With the frame, the skeptic is positioned as the evaluator of an experiment the leader is running. The skeptic's role shifts from "block this" to "evaluate this." Most skeptics are happy to be evaluators.

## Who This Move Is For

The move is specifically calibrated for the intellectually-based person. "The people who are gonna do that particular thing are usually very intellectually based." -- Joe Hudson. The same person whose intellect is the resistance is the person whose intellect is the lever. Meet them with intellect.

Other people on the team may need different framings. The team member who is already enthusiastic does not need to be sold on the experiment. The team member who is anxious about disruption needs reassurance about the time bound. The framing should be designed for the highest-resistance member, because if they agree, the rest will follow.

## The Half-Ass Trap

The experiment frame creates a specific risk: the skeptic agrees to the experiment but participates without genuine engagement, producing data that is not real. The practice cannot be evaluated because the inputs were never authentic.

This is the half-ass failure mode. Joe names it directly:

"You're either running the experiment or you're not running the experiment. But don't half-ass it, because it's like, if the experiment isn't clean, you're never gonna learn anything." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

The leader who introduces the experiment frame has to also be willing to call out half-assing when it happens. See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]]. The skeptic who agreed to a clean experiment is now bound to run a clean experiment. The leader has the standing to enforce that.

For gratitude specifically: the half-ass version is the team member who lists "I'm grateful that I get a paycheck" without feeling it, while the genuine version would be "I'm grateful that this team is working together in such a way that allows us all to be paid and allows me to take care of my children" -- and actually felt. The first version produces no data. The second produces real data.

## Dialogue Example

The pattern of how Joe describes running it:

- **Leader (Joe):** For the next 3 weeks, we are going to start our meetings with gratitude. We are going to note how our decision-making changes and if it is effective. If it is ineffective, we will not continue.
- **Skeptic:** This sounds Pollyanna.
- **Leader:** It is not. I want to hear everything that is going wrong. I also want to hear everything that is going right. If you can convince me that hearing all the data is a bad idea, we can stop it right now.
- **Skeptic:** Fine, three weeks.
- **Leader:** Two weeks in, observing skeptic listing performative gratitudes. You are either running the experiment or you are not running the experiment. Do not half-ass it. If the experiment is not clean, we will not learn anything.

The third move (calling out half-assing) is what protects the experiment from being a false negative.

## When This Move Is Used

- Introducing gratitude practice to a skeptical team
- Introducing a new meeting structure
- Introducing any of Joe's practices to an intellectually-oriented client or team
- Coaching a leader who is rolling out a culture change
- Asking a skeptical individual to try a personal practice ([[love-relationship-with-anxiety]], [[dont-improve-for-two-weeks]], etc.)

The move generalizes well beyond gratitude. Any practice that would benefit from a defined trial period with clear evaluation criteria can be introduced this way.

## What It Produces

- The skeptic agrees to participate
- The team operates the practice with intellectual integrity
- The evaluation at the end of the time period is honest
- If the practice works, the team has actual evidence; if it does not, the team has actual evidence
- The leader's credibility is preserved either way, because the leader did not over-promise

## Tone

Calm, intellectual, almost clinical. The move is delivered with the energy of a researcher proposing a protocol, not a leader pushing a new value. The skeptic should feel met at their level of analysis, not asked to lower themselves to a less rigorous mode.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A team or person is presenting intellectual resistance to a practice
- A leader is rolling out a new practice and anticipates pushback
- A coach is introducing one of Joe's practices to a client whose default is skepticism
- The skeptic in the room is the highest-leverage person and their buy-in matters
