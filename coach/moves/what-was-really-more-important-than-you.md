# What Was Really More Important Than You?

## The Move

When a client names a childhood scene in which something was experienced as "more important than I was," do not stop at the first answer. The first answer is usually the surface object: the booze, the work, the affair, the other kid, the money. Ask what that surface object was actually doing for the parent. What did it represent? What was the parent really protecting themselves from?

The structure of the question:

- **Round 1:** "What was more important than you in that moment?" → Person names the surface object ("the booze," "his work," "money," "her depression").
- **Round 2:** "What did the X represent? What was that really about?" → Person finds the deeper layer ("avoiding his feelings," "avoiding his helplessness," "feeling worthwhile," "not feeling the shame").
- **Round 3 (optional):** "So [that deeper thing] was more important than you?" → Person confirms with new precision.

The move keeps drilling until the answer lands on an emotion the parent could not bear — usually shame, helplessness, fear, or grief. That emotion is the actual thing that was more important than the kid. The surface object was the management strategy.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a client describes a childhood scene as "X was more important than me" (a parent's drinking, work, affair, depression, success, other child).
- When the surface object is named cleanly but the client has not yet seen what the surface object was actually doing.
- When the work needs to land on the parent's avoided emotion rather than on the parent's choice. Without the deeper layer, the client stays angry at the surface object. With the deeper layer, the client sees the parent's wound, and the wiring becomes legible.
- When the current adult avoidance pattern needs to be connected to the childhood pattern. Drilling to the parent's avoided emotion makes the connection: "the parent avoided shame; you avoid shame; that's what's running."

## What It Produces

**A more honest answer about what the parent prioritized.** Most parents did not actually prioritize work or money or alcohol over their kids. They prioritized not feeling something they could not bear. Surfacing that does not absolve the parent, but it does make the dynamic accurate.

**A clean handoff to the present pattern.** Once the parent's avoided emotion is named (shame, helplessness, fear), the question "how much are you doing the same thing?" can land. The childhood imprint and the adult presenting problem become the same problem. See [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]].

**A reduction in the moral charge.** "My dad chose booze over me" carries blame. "My dad chose not to feel his shame over me" carries grief. The first keeps the client stuck. The second moves them.

**A specific direction for the work.** If the parent's avoided emotion was shame, the client's work is with shame. If it was helplessness, with helplessness. The drill surfaces the specific emotion that needs to be welcomed.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Get the surface object.** Let them name the obvious thing first ("the booze," "the work").
2. **Reflect it back with new framing.** "So the booze was more important than you?" This sometimes does the work by itself — naming the surface absurdity surfaces the deeper question.
3. **Ask what the surface object represented.** "What did the booze represent?" The word *represent* invites the client past the literal to the function.
4. **Stay until the emotion is named.** Not "responsibility," not "his job," not "his marriage." Stay until the answer is an emotion the parent could not bear. Shame, helplessness, fear, grief, rage, loneliness.
5. **Restate the trade.** "So avoiding his helplessness was more important than you." Spoken plainly. Let it land.
6. **Apply forward to the present.** "And what are you doing right now with [the current presenting problem]?" The client usually sees the parallel immediately.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "What was it really, if it wasn't money that was more important than you were?"
- **Guest:** "Booze."
- **Joe:** "And what did the booze represent?"
- **Guest:** "Avoiding, avoiding his feelings, avoiding his helplessness."
- **Joe:** "So avoiding his helplessness was more important than you?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah."
- *(Later, drilling further into a different scene from the same childhood)*
- **Joe:** "When he was fucking pissed because you were bragging about that $1,200, what was more important than you at that moment?"
- **Guest:** "He was ashamed."
- **Guest:** "The shame was more important. Not feeling the shame, avoiding the shame by getting angry. Yeah, raging."
- *(Source: How Do I Make Money Doing What I Love?)*

The drill lands on the parent's avoided emotion (shame). The next move applies the same pattern to the adult: "How much are you making shame more important than money? How much are you making avoidance more important than you?"

## Why It Works

The first answer (the surface object) is the answer the family system already gave. Everyone in the family knew the booze was the problem, the work was the problem, the affair was the problem. The first answer has been narrated for decades. It is rehearsed.

The second answer (the parent's avoided emotion) has usually never been named. The family system did not have language for what the booze was doing. Surfacing it gives the client access to data that has been sitting under the rehearsed story the whole time.

The third element — applying the same dynamic to the adult present — depends on having reached the emotion. Without it, the parallel is just "I avoid things like my dad did." With it, the parallel is specific: "I avoid shame in the exact way my dad avoided shame, and it's blocking the exact thing I want." The specificity is what makes the work possible.

## Tone

Curious, direct, unrushed. Joe does not push. He asks again. The questions are short and clean. The client does most of the work; the move is in the persistence past the first answer.

The questions are not asked with weight. The weight comes from the client's recognition of what they've been seeing all along but not naming.

- Toward [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] — the move that takes the surfaced emotion and applies it forward
- Toward [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]] — when the parent's avoided emotion was shame about not being able to provide, the kid wired wanting to unlovability
- Toward [[the-pattern-is-not-personal]] — the parent's avoidance was not about the kid; anyone in that parent's position would have done the same
- Toward [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] — what the avoidance kept the parent alive through
- Toward the specific emotional work on whatever emotion was named (usually shame, helplessness, or fear)

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not stop at the surface object.** "His work was more important than you" is the rehearsed story. Drilling past it is the whole move.
- **Do not name the parent's emotion for the client.** The client has to find it. If Joe says "it was shame," the client hears the word but not the recognition. The recognition only comes when the client says it.
- **Do not turn it into therapy about the parent.** The point is not to understand the parent. The point is to surface what got wired into the kid. Stay oriented toward the present pattern.
