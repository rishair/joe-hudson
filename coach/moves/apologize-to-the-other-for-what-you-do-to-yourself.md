# Apologize to the Other for What You Do to Yourself (Reverse-Direction Apology)

## The Move

Have the client apologize to a significant other (most often spouse) for all the ways they have pressured that person to compose themselves. The coach plays the other. The apology is delivered to the coach-as-stand-in.

The mechanism: the pattern the client is doing to themselves they are almost certainly also doing to the person they live with — often invisibly, in micro-expressions and silent expectations. The apology addresses both at once. The client cannot apologize to themselves cleanly because they are still inside the pattern; they can apologize to the other person, and the apology reaches the self through the projection.

The setup:
1. "What's your husband's name?" (or partner's, kid's — whoever the live-with person is)
2. "Alex. I want you to apologize to Alex. I'll be Alex. I'll pretend I'm Alex."
3. "I want you to apologize to me for all the ways you've told me to compose myself."

The client delivers the apology. The body usually softens during the delivery. Joe checks: "How's that feel?"

## When Joe Uses It

After the client has felt the absurdity of the composure pattern (often via [[visualize-the-kid-composing-themselves]] or [[tell-me-to-compose-myself]]) and after some opening has begun. The move requires:

- The client has acknowledged that they apply the composure pattern to themselves constantly.
- The client has a significant person in their life they could plausibly be applying the same pattern to (spouse, partner, child, parent).
- The client has begun to feel some grief or anger about the original imposition of the pattern.

The move does not require the spouse to actually be receiving these messages from the client — many spouses are not. But the move works regardless because the apology's true addressee is the self. The spouse-stand-in is the safer vehicle.

## What It Produces

The apology usually produces:

1. **A flatter, less defended delivery than expected.** The client cannot rev up the composure to defend against this apology because the apology is to someone else (the surrogate spouse), so the defense systems do not activate.
2. **A body softening during the delivery.** The act of saying the apology aloud changes the somatic state.
3. **A reported felt-shift afterward that is hard to name.** Common reports: "empty in a nice way." "Lighter." "I don't know." The shift is real but not easily articulated because the apology bypassed the usual narrative apparatus.
4. **A relocation of the work.** The client now has a small lived experience of what it would be like to not impose composure on others, which by extension makes it imaginable to not impose composure on themselves.

The "empty in a nice way" report is diagnostic. The composure had been filling the space with effort. The apology releases some of that effort. The space that remains is uncrowded — empty — but not in a depressed or void way. It is the kind of empty that has room in it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client gives a heady, intellectual apology, Joe gently redirects: "You're asking the wrong part of you. Ask your heart, how does that feel?" The redirection from analysis to bodied check makes the apology land instead of staying performative.

If the client softens during the apology, Joe reflects what happened ("there's that awareness, man. You're amazing") and uses the opening to introduce the next teaching — often the [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]] reframe.

If the client cannot bring themselves to do the apology, Joe does not push. Inability to do this move often points back to needing more work on the introject itself. He may return to [[tell-me-to-compose-myself]] or the visualization.

If the apology produces tears or strong emotion, Joe stays with it, does not redirect, and gives the body time. The grief is the grief of having pressured the person they love the way they were pressured. It deserves space.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "I want you to apologize to Alex. I'll be Alex. I want you to apologize to me for all the ways you've told me to compose myself."
- **Person:** "I'm sorry, Alex, that I asked you to compose yourself, be pulled together. There's not really any need for it."
- **Joe:** "Oh, how's that feel? You're asking the wrong part of you. Ask your heart, how does that feel?"
- **Person:** (felt check)
- **Joe:** "How did it feel to apologize to your husband?"
- **Person:** "Empty in a nice way."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, that's what I'm— there's that awareness, man. You're amazing."

The "empty in a nice way" is the response that confirms the move landed. The apology cleared something. The space remaining is the un-composed self — felt for a moment, not yet permanent.

## Tone

Quiet and serious during the apology itself. The client needs the coach to hold the role of the spouse with enough realness that the apology has a recipient — but not so much that the coach becomes a character the client is performing for.

After the apology, the tone returns to warm and appreciative ("you're amazing"). The appreciation is for the client having actually said it, not for the content of the apology.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not let the apology become a list of specific incidents. The general apology ("for the ways I've told you to compose yourself") works because it addresses the pattern as a whole; the specific apology pulls the analytic mind back in and breaks the felt move.
- Do not skip the felt check. If the apology lands intellectually but not in the body, ask the heart how it feels. The somatic confirmation is what makes the move complete.
- Do not require the client to deliver the apology to the actual spouse later. That is a separate move requiring separate conditions. This move is internal work using the spouse as the vehicle.
