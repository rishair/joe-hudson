# Go Down the Rabbit Hole in Your Kid's World

## The Move

A specific posture the parent takes with the kid: get genuinely interested in how the kid sees the world. Not as a parenting technique. As real curiosity. Follow them into whatever they are interested in, see what they see, ask about it from inside the rabbit hole rather than from outside it.

"Be in your kids' worlds for a while, right? And like see how they see the world." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The phrase "go down the rabbit hole" carries the felt sense. The parent does not skim the kid's interests from outside, evaluating them. The parent goes in. The parent learns what the kid finds compelling. The parent asks questions from the inside of the kid's world rather than from the outside.

## What This Replaces

The default parental posture with a teenager's interests:

- Treating the interest as a phase to be tolerated until they pick something better
- Evaluating the interest against what the parent thinks is valuable
- Asking surface questions out of obligation, then redirecting to the parent's preferred topics
- Showing interest in a way the kid registers as performance, not curiosity
- Treating the kid's worldview as immature and waiting for them to grow into a more reasonable one

The rabbit-hole move replaces all of these with: the kid's world is its own real thing, worth understanding for its own sake. The parent is curious about it the way a respected friend would be curious about a different friend's domain.

## The Coaching Session Joe Describes

Joe tells the story of being asked by a friend to talk to her teenage son who had been suspended. The mother brought the boy reluctantly. Joe sat down with him and did one thing — got genuinely interested in how the boy saw the world.

"In my talking to him, I was just really interested in how he saw the world." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

Joe asked him about his friend group. He proposed an interpretation: maybe there is some kind of top-dog dynamic. The boy corrected him: "no, we're like more like Tom Sawyer and Huck Finn. Like we just like to like get into big trouble together." Joe asked about the trouble. The boy described it with joy. Joe asked what was fun about that.

The result: the boy lit up. Joe describes it as visible — "what was clear is that there wasn't a lot of people who were interested in the way he saw the world, right?" The boy had a worldview. Almost no adult in his life had been curious about it. The simple act of being curious about it changed the boy's posture in the conversation.

The move is not a technique. The move is what happens when a parent (or any adult) actually wants to know how the kid sees things.

## Why This Works

**The kid registers being seen as themselves.** The structural message of the rabbit-hole move is: who you actually are, with your actual interests and your actual way of looking at things, is welcome here. This is the felt complement of [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]]. The kid registers liking when the parent is interested in their actual world, not the world the parent wishes they were in.

**The kid's worldview gets validated as a real thing.** "You're also sending the teenager the message, which is the way you look at the world is valid. Who you are is valid." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years. The validation is not performed through compliments. It is performed through the parent's actual investment of attention.

**It removes the resistance the kid would otherwise have.** Teens push back against being told what to do or what to think. A teen who is being asked about their world without an agenda has nothing to push against. The conversation opens up because there is no friction to resolve.

**It produces information the parent could not get any other way.** The parent who goes down the rabbit hole learns what the kid actually cares about, who their actual friends are, what actually scares them, what actually excites them. The parent who stays on the surface gets curated answers. The parent who goes in gets the kid.

## The Connection to NATO and VIEW

Joe makes the link explicit: the rabbit-hole move is the parenting application of the broader principle of [[no-attachment-to-the-outcome]] (NATO), which is itself a facet of [[view]].

"If I ask you questions from not a, from an, I just read this recently, NATO place, which is like no attachment to the outcome. Like if I ask you questions like that, no attachment to the outcome. Which is view basically, like, yeah, one of the aspects of view, like if I, if I ask you questions from view, you, if we have the space, you like, you love it. We talk, and then at the end of it, you feel deeply connected and you're really grateful for it." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The same posture that makes a good coaching question makes a good parenting question. The parent who asks from no-attachment-to-outcome is asking to know, not to steer. The kid feels the difference. The conversation becomes one the kid wants to have, not one they have to endure.

This is also why the move connects to [[wonder]] — the W in VIEW. Wonder is genuine curiosity about how things actually are, without needing them to be one way. The parent who brings wonder to their kid's world is offering the same thing a good coach offers a coachee.

## When to Reach for This Move

- After a stretch where the parent has been managing or correcting the kid and the relationship feels distant
- When the parent realizes they cannot describe their kid's current friend group, current interests, or current concerns in any depth
- When the kid mentions something the parent does not understand — instead of nodding, ask
- During unstructured time together (meals, drives, walks) when the move is easiest to do without it feeling staged
- During [[connect-trip-with-your-kid]] when there is dedicated time and minimal distraction

## How to Do It Without Faking It

The move fails when faked. The kid can tell the difference between genuine curiosity and a parent performing interest. Some practical conditions for the move to be real:

- Set down the phone. The kid registers attention through the parent's actual orientation, not the parent's words.
- Drop the agenda. If the parent is asking questions in order to get to a point, the conversation will dead-end at the point. If the parent is asking because they actually want to know, the conversation will keep opening.
- Ask the questions the kid's answers actually provoke, not the questions the parent had prepared. Follow the energy.
- Be willing to be surprised. The kid will say things the parent did not expect. If the parent meets those with "huh, tell me more" rather than "well, but you should consider," the rabbit hole opens further.
- Be willing not to teach. The parent's instinct will be to extract a moral or to redirect to a lesson. The move is to resist that instinct and stay in the kid's world.

## What This Move Produces Over Time

Joe describes the cumulative effect with his own daughters: he can ask either of them questions and the conversation goes wherever the daughter wants it to go. The daughter feels deeply connected at the end and is grateful for the time. Esme confirms: "It's never much of a struggle."

Over years, the move produces a relationship in which the kid trusts that the parent is actually interested in them. That trust is what makes the kid bring the parent the hard things later. A kid who has been received with curiosity for years brings their problems to the parent because the parent's track record is curiosity, not correction. A kid who has been managed for years does not bring the parent anything if they can help it.

## Tone

The parent's tone in the move is light, curious, undefended. Not formal. Not performative. The parent is allowed to be a little uncool, a little out of their depth, a little uncertain. The willingness to be a little lost in the kid's world is part of what makes the move work. The parent who tries to seem expert in the kid's interests has missed the point.
