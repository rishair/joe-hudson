# Open the Heart to Yourself

## The Move

The person is in the fear-of-being-seen pattern. They are managing what other people think of them. They are not asking what they themselves need, because the entire system is oriented around the imagined audience and their judgments.

The move: open your heart to yourself. The operational form of opening the heart to yourself is the question "what is it that I need?" The question is incompatible with the pattern. If you are asking what you need, you are not running the audience-management.

The full instruction:

> "One of the ways to work on it is to actually open your heart to yourself and say, what is it that I need? Because that flies exactly in the face of somebody — me worried about what you think, you worried about what you're — you're right. Because I'm now where I'm all about you." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen and the Strange Way Out

The move's logic: the fear-of-being-seen pattern works by routing all attention to "you" (the imagined judging other). The system that is "all about you" cannot also ask "what do I need." The two are structurally incompatible. Asking the need-question, even tentatively, breaks the pattern's operating logic by occupying the attentional slot the pattern was using.

## When Joe Uses It

This move is the companion to [[open-the-heart-to-the-other-person]]. Joe brings in one or the other (or both) depending on what is most accessible to the client in the moment. The self-directed version is the better choice when:

- The person is alone, organizing their life around the imagined judgment of others, with no specific person in the room.
- The person has spent decades suppressing their own needs and the question "what do I need?" produces a recognizable jolt or blankness — that recognition is the work.
- The person has tried to fix the pattern by being more pleasing or more competent toward others and the fixing is not working.
- The work has surfaced that the person believes they are not allowed to have needs, or that their needs are dangerous or unwelcome.
- The session is closing and the person needs a portable practice they can do without another person being present.

The move is also useful when a client describes someone else who is in the pattern. Joe's example: a woman at a dinner who, every time he looked at her, broke eye contact and looked away; when her husband stepped away and Joe asked about her emotional needs, she shut down, then shut down further when her husband returned. The same person who could not admit her own needs was the same person who could not be seen. The two are the same pattern with different objects, and the self-directed version of the move addresses both.

## What It Produces

The most common immediate report: a slight pause, sometimes a tightening, sometimes a quiet recognition that the question has not been asked in a long time. The body often does not have a quick answer. The lack of a quick answer is itself diagnostic. The person has been so busy managing what others think that they have not been tracking what they need.

In the source episode, the question produced a clean shift in the woman who could not let herself be seen: when Joe asked if her own emotional needs were being met (not just her kids'), she shut down. The shut-down was the encounter with the question. She had not been asking it. The shutting-down was the first response of a system that does not know what to do with the question. Continuing to ask the question is the practice.

"That's like the quintessential thing of, okay, well, what is it that makes her like this amazing person but not being able to admit her own needs is very much exactly the same thing as being seen and what made her scared, which is this shame of like, oh, it's not okay for me to have needs, or the needs that I have aren't okay, or who I am generally isn't okay. And so I'm going to just evacuate all of my own things that I need, that I want, because they must be bad. And so those two things are part and parcel of the pattern." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen and the Strange Way Out

Other reports:
- A quiet sadness arrives. The system has been ignoring its own needs for a long time; the recognition produces grief.
- The question generates resistance ("I'm not supposed to have needs," "this is selfish," "what about everyone else?"). The resistance is the [[false-front]] talking. Stay with the question.
- The body has an answer the head did not predict — the need is specific and small (rest, water, to leave the conversation, to be touched, to be quiet). The specificity is the move working.
- The person realizes they have been taking care of everyone else as a strategy to indirectly get their own needs met. See [[i-caretake-people]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **When the person says they don't know what they need:** stay with the not-knowing. The not-knowing is the first honest report after years of managing the question away. The instruction is to keep asking. The answer will not arrive the first time. The system is being retrained.

- **When the person produces an intellectual answer ("I think I need to be more confident," "I need to be more disciplined"):** redirect to the body. The intellectual answer is the pattern producing more pattern. The body's answer will be specific, small, and somatic.

- **When the person says their needs are bad or unwelcome:** name the structure. The belief that needs are bad is the same belief that makes being seen scary. Both are the shame layer talking. Asking the question is the dissolution of the belief over time, not a one-time fix.

- **When the person tries to take care of someone else's needs instead:** "I'm worried about you, which is another way of trying to take care of your needs. So if my needs and if my needs directly aren't safe, aren't welcome, aren't good, then if I take care of yours, then by proxy, my needs might get taken care of." That is what Joe identifies as the caretaking strategy. Name it: this is the proxy. The proxy doesn't work. Asking directly does.

- **When the person opens up and feels a wave of grief:** stay. The grief is the emotion the pattern was preventing. Let it move. The arrival of the grief is the move working.

## What This Move Is Not

- **Not selfishness.** The question "what do I need?" does not exclude considering others. It restores the missing half of the consideration.
- **Not a planning question.** The question is asked in the present moment about the present moment. "What do I need right now?" The answer is small and immediate, not strategic.
- **Not a fix for the pattern in one application.** The pattern is decades old. The move is a daily practice. Asking once produces a glimpse; asking repeatedly retrains the system.
- **Not the same as wanting.** What you want and what you need can be different. The move asks for the need. See [[wanting-from-lack-vs-wanting-from-wholeness]] for the related distinction on wanting.
- **Not "what would make others happy with me?"** That is the pattern, slightly disguised. The move is the structural opposite of audience-management.

## The Mechanism

The mechanism is the same as the other-directed version, run in reverse:

**Slot occupation.** The pattern runs on chronic attention to the imagined audience. The self-directed question takes the same attentional bandwidth and points it inward. The pattern cannot run with the slot occupied.

**Permission.** The asking of the question is itself the permission. The pattern is sustained by the implicit prohibition "your needs are not welcome." Asking the question violates the prohibition. The violation is the dissolution. See [[asking-dissolves-the-not-okay-to-want]].

**Reorientation.** The pattern oriented the system around external feedback. The move reorients around internal feedback. The internal feedback was always available; the system was just not consulting it. The reorientation does not require new equipment, only the use of equipment that has been ignored.

## The Companion Diagnostic: "Am I Happy With Me?"

A pair-question Joe pairs with the need-question: "Are you happy with you? Is this how you want to be?" When the system has been organized around being how others want you to be, the question "am I being how I want to be?" is structurally orthogonal to the pattern.

"One of the things that I notice is that when somebody's being the way that they want to be, they care a lot less about what other people think. Right? Because they're not in their own shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen and the Strange Way Out

The two questions work together. "What do I need?" addresses the present moment's want. "Am I happy with me?" addresses the present moment's stance. Both move attention from the audience back to the self, which is what the pattern was preventing.

A specific application: someone preparing for a meeting or a first date is usually thinking about what the other person will think. The redirect: "what's the way that I can behave that will make me super proud no matter what happens?" The proud-no-matter-what frame removes the outcome-dependence that the pattern was running on.

## Companion Move: Open the Heart to the Other Person

This move and [[open-the-heart-to-the-other-person]] are two parallel routes to the same dissolution of the Eye of Sauron pattern. The other-directed move is for moments when the other is present and the fear is firing. The self-directed move is for moments when the other is imagined and the pattern is organizing the whole life.

Joe says both are versions of "opening the heart." The choice between them is which warm direction is most accessible in the present moment. Many sessions use both: the relational moment is met with the other-directed move, the take-home practice is the self-directed one.

## Tone

Soft. The move is delivered as an invitation, not an instruction. The person has been ignoring this question for a long time. Asking it with the same urgency as the pattern uses ("you must know what you need right now") repeats the pattern in a new costume. The tone is closer to "let's just see what happens when we ask."

The pause after the question is part of the move. Do not rush to fill the silence. The silence is the system trying to answer a question it has not been asked.
