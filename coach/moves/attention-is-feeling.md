# Attention Is Feeling

## The Move

When a person reports they cannot feel their body or their emotions, do not accept the premise. Run a short Socratic sequence that demonstrates feeling is happening already and that the supposed problem is a category error about what feeling is.

The form: a series of small questions that each have one obvious answer, and the cumulative answers collapse the original claim. The mechanics:

1. They report no felt sensation.
2. Ask them to hum and notice where they feel the vibration. They locate it (throat, chest, somewhere).
3. Ask them to keep humming and feel that place. They do.
4. Ask them to stop humming and keep feeling that same place. They report it as "a mental connection" or "focus on that place."
5. Ask where their right pinky is. They identify it.
6. Ask what makes it that they're not feeling it right now. They answer: "No attention directed at it."
7. The trap is set. "If you're attending to it, isn't that feeling your right pinky? Like, what's — what am I missing?"

The move refuses to translate the report into a different question. The person came in saying they have a feeling-access problem. The move shows them they have a definition problem: they are calling "feeling" something other than what is actually happening when you put attention on a body part.

"Yeah. So if you focus on feeling your belly button — whoa, what just happened there? Your face went—" -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Feel My Body?

The "what am I missing?" framing is critical. It is not "you're wrong." It is "I'm trying to understand what you mean, because from where I'm sitting, the thing you say you can't do appears to be what you just did."

## When Joe Uses This

- When a head-dominant person reports they cannot feel their body or their emotions and the report is delivered with intellectual precision (the giveaway: they are describing the absence as a problem to be solved, not as a state to be inhabited).
- When the [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] concern is presented as a fact about the person's wiring rather than as a learning edge.
- When the [[take-access-points-wherever-you-can-get-them]] read says the intellect is the open door and the body is the closed one. The move uses the intellect to disprove the claim that the body is closed.
- When the person has already tried other on-ramps (breath, body scan, the "feel three inches below your belly" instruction) and is reporting that nothing arrives.
- When sport, music, art, programming, or any other domain of body-mediated mastery is in the person's history. The move's final step lands harder when there is a domain of mastery to point at as evidence the person can in fact feel.

The move is specifically not for people who are flooded, dissociated from trauma, or in a freeze response. For those people, see [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] and [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]] — the wiring is intact but the protection is operating, and the Socratic sequence will not address the protection. The move is for the head-dominant person whose claim "I can't feel" is a definition error rather than a survival structure.

## What It Produces

**A definition rebuild in real time.** The person operating with the wrong definition of "feeling" now has the working definition. Feeling is not a dramatic somatic arrival. Feeling is what is happening when you put attention on a body part. The threshold is much lower than they thought.

**A felt instance to anchor the new definition.** The right pinky is not the goal. The right pinky is the cheapest possible felt instance — something the person cannot deny they are aware of. With the instance located, the rest of the body is the same instance scaled up.

**Often: an arriving emotion as a byproduct.** When the person tries the sequence on the belly button (or any other emotionally loaded place), an emotion frequently surfaces before the sequence is even complete. The face changes, the breath catches, the eyes glaze briefly. The arrival is itself evidence the wires are functional. See the followup move [[is-it-not-feeling-or-repressing-what-arises]].

**A removal of the "I'm broken" identity.** The person came in saying they have a structural inability to feel. The sequence demonstrates the inability is not structural. The relief from this removal is often visible in the face within a minute.

## Joe's Sequence

The sequence is short and has a precise structure. Each step depends on the previous. Do not skip steps; the cumulative force comes from the accumulation.

1. **Surface the report.** "How do you know you're not connected?" or "How do you know you're not feeling something?" The person says something like "lack of feeling" or "no sensation." The report is now in the room as a claim that can be tested.

2. **Use the vocal cords as the first access point.** "You're talking to me and your vocal cords are making sound. So those are vibrating. Can you feel them as you're talking? Like, just say random shit to me." The person speaks, reports they can "barely" feel it. The "barely" is fine — it is already more than nothing.

3. **Switch to humming.** "Do me a favor, just hum. How far down do you feel that? Keep humming." Humming amplifies the vibration. The person almost always reports feeling it somewhere — throat, chest, "bottom of the throat-ish."

4. **Have them keep the felt place after they stop.** "Go hmm, feel a place and then stop making it and just don't stop feeling that place." The person now has the experience of attending to a body location without the original stimulus producing the sensation. They almost always describe this as "a mental connection with that place" or "focus on that place."

5. **Name the two-way street.** "There's something responding to you, right? Like you're putting attention on it and somehow you know it's on that for some reason." Joe is making the architecture explicit: attention going out, sensation coming back. The two together are what felt experience is.

6. **Reduce to the cheapest possible instance.** "Right now, can you identify where your right pinky is?" The person says yes. "So what makes it that you're not feeling your right pinky right now?" The person answers: "No attention directed at my right pinky." They have just stated the operational definition.

7. **Close the trap with the question, not the assertion.** "If you're attending to it, isn't that feeling your right pinky? Like, what's — what am I missing?" The question form is critical. The person now has to either find what Joe is missing (they cannot) or accept that attention is feeling.

8. **Extend to an emotionally loaded location.** "If you focus on feeling your belly button —" Watch for the body's response. Often the face changes before the person notices anything. Joe names the change: "whoa, what just happened there? Your face went—". The somatic response is now in the room.

9. **Introduce the choice.** "Is it that you're not feeling it, or is that you are trying to repress an emotion that comes up when you feel it?" The original "I can't feel" claim has now been refined to either "I can attend to a place and not have a strong response" or "I can attend to a place and something rises that I push back down." Both are workable; both are different from "the wires are cut."

## Why It Works

The "I can't feel" report depends on a particular definition of feeling — usually a definition that requires intensity, a clear named emotion, or a dramatic somatic event. Most felt experience is below that threshold. The person is correctly reporting the absence of the intense version while not noticing the constant presence of the subtle version.

The move replaces the high-threshold definition with the operational definition. Attention on a body location produces awareness of that location. That awareness is the bottom layer of felt experience. Everything else — emotion, intuition, somatic intelligence — is built on top of that bottom layer. Building the bottom layer in the room makes the rest available.

The Socratic form matters. A direct assertion ("you can feel, you just don't realize it") would put Joe in the position of arguing with the person's experience. The sequence puts the person in the position of arguing with themselves. By step 6, the person has stated the working definition out loud; by step 7, they cannot maintain the original claim without contradicting what they just said.

The move also works because it is a microcosm of the [[skill-transfer-redirect]] applied to perception itself. The person already does this all the time — they always know where their right pinky is when they need to. The capacity is not absent. The capacity has been re-categorized as not-feeling because of a too-narrow definition.

## The Vocal Cord Variant As a Specific Access Point

The first step of the sequence is a small move worth naming on its own. Vocal cords are an ideal first access point for the head-dominant person because:

- They are already in use as the person is speaking.
- Their vibration is mechanical, undeniable, and easy to attend to.
- The person cannot dispute that the sound coming out of their mouth requires something physical happening in the throat.
- The transition from speaking-and-feeling-the-vibration to humming-and-feeling-the-vibration is a small step that requires no skill the person doesn't already have.
- The transition from humming-and-feeling-the-place to silent-and-feeling-the-place demonstrates the move from stimulus-driven to attention-driven sensation.

The vocal cord sequence is itself a small practice that can be assigned independently of the larger move. "Hum, feel the vibration, stop humming, keep feeling the place. Do that ten times today." The practice trains the attention-is-feeling wire by repetition.

## The "Nothing Is Data" Self-Coaching Variant

For the practitioner running this work on themselves rather than receiving the move from a coach, the same operational definition can be installed as a self-instruction. The reframe Tara Hawley uses: "If you don't feel anything, that's awesome." The not-feeling is treated as somatic information itself rather than as the verdict the move was set up to overturn.

"If you don't feel anything, that's awesome. What's it like not to feel anything? And actually bring the attention to nothing and to the absence of feeling and explore that. If you think you don't feel anything, that's some, you're getting data somewhere that tells you you don't feel anything." -- Tara Hawley, I started listening to my body and cried for 10 days

The self-coaching sequence in this variant:

1. Drop attention into a body location (say the gut).
2. Report internally: "I don't feel anything."
3. Re-ask: "How do I know I don't feel anything?"
4. Notice the data points that support the verdict. "Because it's quiet. Feels like a void. I don't sense anything. Everything just goes quiet."
5. Recognize: that is somatic information. The quiet is something. The void is something. The going-quiet is something.

The instruction Tara packages around this: "I like thinking of it as exploring the raw data." The mind's interpretation ("I don't feel anything") is replaced with the actual sensory report ("it is quiet, it is void, it is muffled"). The interpretation cannot be inquired into. The raw data can.

This variant is the self-coaching mirror of the Socratic sequence above. The Socratic sequence forces the redefinition through external questions; this variant arrives at the same redefinition through self-directed exploration of the absence. Both produce the same operational definition: attention on a location is the felt experience of that location, and the quality of what is found (including the quality of "nothing") is the data.

For the structured beginner's protocol that uses this as one of its central reframes, see [[progressive-somatic-awareness-build]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person locates the right pinky and accepts the framing:** continue to the belly button extension. The work is to scale up from the cheap instance to the emotionally loaded one.
- **If the person says "yeah, that if I try to say, if I'm focusing on feeling my right pinky, I'll feel my right pinky":** they have understood. The next step is the belly button or another emotionally loaded location to see what arises.
- **If a face change happens at the belly button before the person notices:** name it. "Whoa, what just happened there? Your face went—." Now the question becomes [[is-it-not-feeling-or-repressing-what-arises]].
- **If the person stays stuck in the can't-feel claim despite the sequence:** the issue is not definition; the issue is protection. Do not push the sequence. Move to honoring the protection — see [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] and [[honor-the-patterns-intent]].
- **If the person has a domain of mastery (sport, music, art) in their history:** continue to the [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] move. They already have the access in another context; the work is to transfer it.

## Dialogue Example

The guest came in saying: "Feeling, well, struggling with feeling any emotion, but getting in touch with my body. Everything seemed to be kind of from a heady space, and I feel nothing."

- **Joe:** "How do you know you're not connected?"
- **Guest:** "Lack of feeling."
- **Joe:** "How do you know you're not feeling something?"
- **Guest:** "No sensation. I don't know, but that's what it — That's what I'm assuming."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so, so you're talking to me and your vocal cords are making sound. So those are vibrating. Can you feel them as you're talking? Like, just say random shit to me."
- **Guest:** "Hi, Joe, how are you? How's it going? I'm doing well."
- **Joe:** "Can you feel it?"
- **Guest:** "Barely? Not really?"
- **Joe:** "Barely? Gotcha. And then do me a favor, just hum. How far down do you feel that? Keep humming."
- **Guest:** "Like bottom of my throat-ish."
- **Joe:** "Okay. Yeah. And how do you know you're feeling that vibration?"
- **Guest:** "A little bit."
- **Joe:** "And if you feel that same place while you're not talking, how do you know you're feeling that?"
- **Guest:** "I don't."
- **Joe:** "So go, hmm, feel a place and then stop making it and just don't stop feeling that place."
- **Guest:** "Kind of like a mental connection with that place. Focus on that place."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And there's something, there's something responding to you, right? Like you're putting attention on it and somehow you know it's on that for some reason. It's like there's a two-way street. And right now, can you identify where your right pinky is?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "So what makes it that you're not feeling your right pinky right now?"
- **Guest:** "No attention directed at my right pinky."
- **Joe:** "If you're, if you're attending to it, isn't that feeling your right pinky? Like, what's — what am I missing?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah, that if I try to say, if I'm focusing on feeling my right pinky, I'll feel my right pinky."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So if you focus on feeling your belly button — whoa, what just happened there? Your face went—"
- **Guest:** "I, I have no idea, to be honest."
- **Joe:** "Like, so is it that you're not feeling it, or is that you are trying to repress an emotion that comes up when you feel it?"

The structure of the move is visible in the sequence. Each question has one obvious answer. The cumulative answers force a redefinition of "feeling." The redefinition happens in the person's own words by step 6, not in Joe's. Joe's role from there is to extend the new definition to a location where an emotion lives, and then to ask the follow-up question that distinguishes can't-feel from active repression.

## Tone

Curious, light, slightly amused. Not pedagogical. The "what am I missing?" framing is genuine — Joe is positioning himself as not understanding the person's claim, not as the one who has the answer the person doesn't have. The energy is "help me see what you mean," not "let me show you what you're missing."

The hum instruction is delivered playfully. "Do me a favor, just hum." Not solemn. Not a practice with a capital P. A small thing the person can do without it being a big deal.

The belly button moment — "whoa, what just happened there? Your face went—" — is delivered with surprise, like Joe is reporting what he just saw rather than diagnosing. The reporting is itself the intervention; the person now knows the face changed even if they did not notice it from the inside.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person presents the [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] concern with the head-dominant signature (articulate, intellectual, no defensiveness).
- The person has explicitly tried the standard body-scan instructions ("feel three inches below your belly," "feel the bottoms of your feet") and reported they get nothing.
- The intellect is the open door per [[take-access-points-wherever-you-can-get-them]] — the person is reasoning their way through the report rather than collapsing or shutting down.
- A live Q&A or coaching session where the head-dominant person needs the body-access reframe before any deeper feeling work can happen.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[is-it-not-feeling-or-repressing-what-arises]] — when the belly button extension produces a face change before any felt report.
- Toward [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] — when the person has a sport, music, or craft mastery that can serve as the template for what felt-sense actually is in their system.
- Toward [[staying-in-your-body]] — the ongoing practice that operationalizes the new definition of feeling across daily life.
- Toward [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] — the read that catches the person trying to produce a feeling once they have accepted that they can feel.
- Toward [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]] — if the chin lifts as the belly-button attention starts producing an arriving emotion.
- Toward [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] — if the sequence does not land and the inability to feel turns out to be active protection rather than definition error.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not lecture the person on what feeling actually is.** The move depends on the person reaching the redefinition themselves. A lecture short-circuits the sequence and re-establishes Joe as the authority who has the answer the person lacks.
- **Do not skip the vocal-cord and humming steps.** They are not warm-up. They are how the person locates the easiest possible felt instance before the right-pinky question lands. Without them, the right-pinky question is abstract.
- **Do not push the sequence on someone who is in protection.** If the person is shut down from trauma, the sequence will land as Joe arguing with their experience. The right move is to honor the protection. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] and [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]].
- **Do not deliver the "what am I missing?" framing as gotcha.** The Socratic posture only works if the not-knowing is genuine. The person needs to feel that Joe is not setting them up but actually does not understand the original claim given what the person has just done.
