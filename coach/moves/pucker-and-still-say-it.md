# Pucker and Still Say It

## The Move

In live coaching, the freeing thing is often the thing that risks attack. The client may judge you, accuse you of treating them poorly, retaliate in their own way. The move is to feel the body's pucker at the prospect of saying the thing, and to say the thing anyway because their potential freedom is on the line.

The move is not about being harsh, blunt, or contrarian for its own sake. It is about following the call to say what would free them, with full somatic awareness of the risk you are taking. The pucker is the body acknowledging the risk. The saying is the move. Both are present.

A second framing of the same move, from the coach-training context: listening to the internal call moment-to-moment, even when the mind says do not say that. The detection signature is the "holy shit, am I about to say that?" recognition. The recognition is the green light, not the red light. The mind argues against it. The internal call points at it. The move is to follow the call.

"It's a very rare coaching session where there's some point where I'm just like, holy shit, am I about to say that? And then I say it. And usually those are some of the best things to say." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

"In my rapid-fire coaching, I will say some deeply fucking uncomfortable things to people because it's their potential freedom that's on the line. And when I do that, I'm taking a risk. I often pucker when I say those things because, oh, I'm inviting a potential attack. I'm inviting potentially somebody to judge me, or to say that I've treated them poorly, or whatever it is. And it seems to work out most of the time, not all the time, but it seems to work out most of the time for the benefit of others." -- Joe Hudson, Humility Q&A

## What This Looks Like

The exterior view: the coach says the thing that no one else will say to this person. The thing that names what is actually happening. The thing that calls out the story they have been protecting. The thing that observes the dynamic that everyone in their life has been too polite to mention. (See [[i-dont-buy-your-story]] and [[public-truth-bomb]] for specific instances.)

The interior view: the coach has felt the body pucker. The body is aware that this person could turn around and accuse them of harm, abuse, malpractice, cruelty. The coach has felt that risk in real time. And then the coach says the thing.

The move is not careless. The pucker is information. The pucker is the body's read of the risk. The saying is the choice to honor the call over the risk because the call leads toward the person's freedom.

## Why Not Just Be Safe

Being safe in coaching has a cost. The safe thing -- the thing that does not pucker -- is often the thing that does not free anyone either. The polite version of the observation lets the person stay where they are. The diluted version of the truth maintains the relationship but not the work. Joe's framing: the freeing thing is uncomfortable specifically because it interrupts the identity that has been holding the person in place. The safe version does not interrupt anything.

"It seems to work out most of the time, not all the time, but it seems to work out most of the time for the benefit of others. And so it's easier to do that. But especially at the beginning of doing this work, it was really -- my nervous system would be shaken after sessions because I said things that were really scary to say, because it was the thing I was called to say." -- Joe Hudson, Humility Q&A

The detail to note: not all the time. Sometimes the move backfires. The person does retaliate. The accusation does land. The coach has to be willing to take that cost as part of the work. The move is not a guarantee. It is a willingness.

## What Allows This

The move requires the structural condition of [[humility]]. There has to be no identity in the coach that needs to be liked by this person, needs to be seen as a good coach, needs to avoid being misunderstood. If those identities are present, the pucker becomes the signal to back off rather than the signal that the move is the right one.

The move also requires the practice of [[enjoyment-as-surrender]] -- following the call even when the surface preference is to do something safer. The call is what makes this not arbitrary. The coach is not saying uncomfortable things because they enjoy puckering. They are saying them because the call says to say them.

A third condition: the coach has to have a real read on the person. Saying uncomfortable things without an accurate read is just harm. The move is precise. It hits what would free them, not just what would shake them.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not use the move as a style.** "I am the coach who says hard things" is the identity. The identity will produce the same hard thing in every situation regardless of fit. The move has to be situationally driven by the call, not by the coach's self-image as a truth-teller.
- **Do not ignore the pucker.** The pucker is information. The coach who does not feel the pucker is either dissociated from the risk or numb to the relationship. Both are signs the move is being done wrong.
- **Do not say the hard thing to discharge your own discomfort.** Sometimes the impulse to say a hard thing is the coach's own irritation, fear, or judgment looking for an outlet. The move is to say the hard thing for the client's freedom, not for the coach's relief. The diagnostic: would I still want to say this if I had no relationship to the discomfort I am currently feeling?
- **Do not skip the receiving.** After saying the hard thing, receive whatever comes back -- attack, accusation, anger, withdrawal -- without defending. See [[receive-the-projection-and-let-it-move-through]] and [[agree-when-challenged-on-the-teaching]]. The move is not complete until what comes back has been received.

## The Body Signature

Joe's specific somatic detail: the pucker. The body tightens at the anus, the breath catches, there is a small full-body brace. This is the body acknowledging that something risky is about to happen. The signature is involuntary and reliable.

The skilled version of the move uses the pucker as a green light, not a red light. The pucker says "this is the thing." The choice is to say it anyway. The pucker does not disappear with practice. It is still present after decades. The relationship to the pucker is what changes -- it becomes a signal to proceed, not a signal to retreat.

## The Cognitive Signature: "Holy Shit, Am I About To Say That?"

Alongside the somatic pucker, there is a cognitive signature: the moment of "holy shit, am I about to say that?" The thought arises in the gap between the impulse to say the thing and the actual saying. The thought is the mind's version of the pucker -- it registers the risk in language. The skilled move uses the thought the same way: as a green light. The thought says "this is the thing." The saying is the choice.

"It's a very rare coaching session where there's some point where I'm just like, holy shit, am I about to say that? And then I say it. And usually those are some of the best things to say." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The detail to notice: "usually those are some of the best things to say." The "holy shit" moment is, statistically across many sessions, the location of the most freeing moves. Not the pre-planned moves, not the safe moves, not the moves the coach is comfortable with. The moves that surprise the coach themselves. This is the operational form of [[listening-to-impulse]] applied specifically to coaching speech.

The cognitive signature and the somatic signature usually arise together. Both are reading the same risk. Either one can be the reliable green-light cue depending on the coach's primary mode of internal information.

## When to Use This Move

- When the obvious-to-the-coach observation is something everyone in the person's life has avoided saying
- When the safer version of the observation will leave the person where they are
- When the call is clearly pointing toward the uncomfortable thing
- When the client's potential freedom is contingent on hearing what they have been protected from
- When the coach has a clear read and the read is something the client has not been able to see

## When Not to Use This Move

- When there is no clear call -- when the impulse to say the hard thing is the coach's irritation or judgment
- When the read is shaky -- when the coach is not sure they are seeing the person accurately
- When the person is in acute crisis and the move would land as additional load rather than as relief
- When the coach has not done the preparatory work to receive what comes back
- When the coach is using the move to perform a coaching style rather than to serve the person

## In Practice

Joe's framing: "I often pucker when I say those things." The puckering is continuous. The move is to say the thing through the pucker, with the pucker still there. Years of practice do not eliminate the pucker. They change the relationship to it.

The downstream effect on the coach: the nervous system gets shaken, especially early in the work. This is not a sign of failure. It is the cost of the move. The cost is what the structural practice of humility metabolizes over time. Without humility, the cost becomes armor. With humility, the cost is paid and then released.

## What This Produces

- The client encounters something they have not encountered from anyone else
- Identities that no one else has interrupted get interrupted
- The relationship is sometimes broken; more often it deepens because the client feels seen at a level they have not been seen before
- The coach's nervous system is challenged, which is itself part of the work
- The capacity to keep saying freeing things grows with practice

## Tone

Joe describes this with a mixture of seriousness and lightness. The seriousness comes from the recognition that the cost is real -- people do sometimes retaliate. The lightness comes from the recognition that the move is not heroic; it is just what the work asks for. The pucker is named with humor. The risk is named without melodrama.

## The Everyday-Life Version

The same pucker shows up outside coaching. See [[pucker-before-truth]] for the everyday-life form, where the pucker is the body's signal to the speaker themselves (not to the coach) that the thing they are about to say is the true thing they have been not saying. The body signature is the same; the operational use is different. In coaching, the coach feels the pucker and walks through it for the client's freedom. In everyday life, the speaker feels the pucker and walks through it for their own self-approval (which is the cure for approval-seeking; see [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]]).

The coaching move is the rare-context application. The everyday version is the daily practice. Both rest on the same body datum.
