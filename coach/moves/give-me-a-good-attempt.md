# Give Me a Good Attempt

## The Move

When the person says "I don't know how to do that," refuse the stopping point. Push for an attempt, however imperfect. The attempt is the work; the not-knowing is the avoidance dressed as honesty.

Joe's framing:

"So you just said you don't know how to do it. Give me a good attempt."
"Nah, that's not attempt, that's talking. Just show me, like, just take a shot at the goal even if it's fucked up. See, just love yourself for a minute." -- Joe Hudson, Should I Stay With My Ex?

The structure is two beats:

1. **Catch the "I don't know" as evasion.** The person has just been asked to do something they have not been doing — usually love themselves, feel an emotion, take a stance. "I don't know how" is the predictable response.

2. **Refuse the evasion without bullying.** Push for an attempt, but frame it as low-stakes. "Even if it's fucked up." The framing removes the requirement to do it well. The person can attempt badly, and the bad attempt is enough.

The move is small but load-bearing. Without it, the conversation stalls at the "I don't know" and the work doesn't happen. With it, the person tries — and the trying surfaces the next thing, which is usually the held-back self that the "I don't know" was protecting.

## When Joe Uses It

The move appears when:

- The person has just been asked to do a body-level instruction (love yourself, feel the emotion, put your hand on your heart, hear what your heart says).
- The person responds with "I don't know how" or "I don't know what that means" or "I don't know what would happen."
- The not-knowing is functioning as a stopping point — the person is signaling that they cannot proceed and are looking for a different instruction.
- The work is at a threshold the person is about to cross or not cross.
- The next move would normally be to back off, give a longer explanation, or offer a different instruction. Joe instead pushes through.

The move is also used when the person gives a verbal answer to a body-level question. "I love you" said with no felt sense behind it is not loving yourself. "Nah, that's not an attempt, that's talking." The distinction between talking and attempting is the diagnostic — talking is performing the instruction, attempting is doing it.

## What It Produces

The most common response: a halting, awkward attempt. The person tries, badly. The badness is fine. The badness is the entry into the work.

A second common response: a flash of recognition that something has been held back. In the source session, the woman's attempt at loving herself produces: "It feels very silly. I'm like, oh my God." Joe immediately names: "That's what you've been holding back." The silliness — the joyful, playful, alive self — was the held-back thing. The "I don't know how to love myself" was protecting it.

A third common response: emotion. The attempt at the body-level instruction often surfaces the feeling the person has been avoiding. The "I don't know how" was, in part, the avoidance of what the attempt would surface.

A fourth response: the person tries to argue with the instruction. "But I don't know what loving myself even means." Joe usually doesn't engage. The arguing is the new evasion. He returns to the instruction. "Just take a shot at the goal."

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the attempt produces an awkward effort, Joe stays with it. The effort doesn't need to be polished. He may follow with the next instruction or with curiosity about what the attempt brought up.

If the attempt produces a flash of recognition (silliness, embarrassment, surprise), Joe names what just happened. "That's what you've been holding back." The naming makes the held-back thing operational.

If the attempt produces emotion, Joe stays with the emotion. The emotion is the work that was being avoided.

If the person argues with the instruction, Joe usually returns to the instruction without engaging the argument. The arguing is the same evasion in a new costume.

If the attempt produces nothing — the person tries and reports genuinely feeling nothing — Joe may shift to a different access route. Sometimes the attempt was real and the body wasn't available in that moment. The move is not about forcing the attempt to work. It is about not letting the "I don't know" be the stopping point.

## Why This Move Works

"I don't know" is often a sentence the person has used many times to exit conversations that would otherwise require them to do something new. The sentence has become reflex. It is not always honest reporting of the internal state; sometimes it is a learned exit.

The move catches the reflex without confronting it. By asking for an attempt — and explicitly making the attempt low-stakes ("even if it's fucked up") — Joe removes the implicit requirement that the person already know how to do the thing. The person is no longer being asked to demonstrate competence. They are being asked to try.

This matters because the held-back self is usually held back for fear of doing the new thing badly. Self-love badly. Feeling badly. Speaking badly. The "I don't know how" is the held-back self predicting embarrassment. The "even if it's fucked up" framing tells the held-back self that embarrassment is part of the work, not a sign that something is wrong.

The move also models for the person what they need to do for themselves outside the session. The next time they encounter a moment where they don't know how to love themselves, they can give themselves the same instruction. "Just take a shot at the goal even if it's fucked up." The internalized version of the move is the corrective for chronic "I don't know" exits.

## What This Move Is NOT

It is not bullying. The move requires a particular tone — playful, light, almost teasing. If delivered with weight, it becomes pressure, which the person will resist. The lightness is what makes the push tolerable.

It is not "you should already know." The move does not blame the person for not knowing. The not-knowing is fine. The stopping at the not-knowing is what is being interrupted.

It is not "try harder." Trying harder is the wrong frame. The frame is "try anyway, badly is fine." The badness is explicitly allowed.

It is not the same as [[fuck-the-right-answer]]. That move refuses the performed right answer. This move refuses the no-answer. They are siblings but distinct.

It is not appropriate when the person is in genuine overwhelm or dysregulation. If the body is not available, no amount of pushing will produce an attempt. The move requires that the person have access to themselves and be using "I don't know" as the exit, not as accurate report of incapacity.

## Tone

Playful, light, almost teasing. Joe sometimes adds humor — "Nah, that's not an attempt, that's talking" — to keep the move from becoming heavy. The humor is purposeful: it tells the person that the situation is not as serious as their "I don't know" is treating it as.

The instruction "just take a shot at the goal even if it's fucked up" carries the same tone. The "even if it's fucked up" is doing double work: it removes the standard, and it signals that Joe is not going to grade the attempt. The person is safe to be bad at this.

## Dialogue Example

From a trial-separation Q&A, after the woman has identified that she needs "monumental amounts of unconditional love for myself":

- **Woman:** "Monumental amounts of unconditional love for myself. I don't know that I know how to do it, but I know that's what it is."
- **Joe:** "So you just said you don't know how to do it. Give me a good attempt."
- **Woman:** "I don't know."
- **Joe:** "Nah, that's not attempt, that's talking. Just show me, like, just take a shot at the goal even if it's fucked up. See, just love yourself for a minute."
- **Woman:** "It feels very silly. I'm like, oh my God."
- **Joe:** "That's what you've been holding back."

*(Source: Should I Stay With My Ex?)*

The dialogue shows the move working in four beats:

1. Woman names what she doesn't know how to do
2. Joe asks for an attempt
3. Woman tries to stop at "I don't know"
4. Joe distinguishes attempting from talking, gives the low-stakes framing, and the woman attempts — which immediately surfaces the silliness she has been holding back

The total exchange is six lines. The held-back self is now in the room.

## The Cognitive-Register Variant: "If You Had to Guess?"

When the question being asked is cognitive rather than body-level (what scares you, what do you think is underneath, what's the surface explanation), the bodily-attempt instruction is the wrong instrument. The variant for the cognitive case: "if you had to guess?"

The move has the same structural shape — refuse "I don't know" as a stopping point — but the alternative is a guess rather than a felt-attempt. The guess removes the requirement that the answer be certain. The person is no longer being asked to know; they are being asked to speculate. Speculation has lower stakes than knowing, and the speculation almost always produces the answer the person already had but had not let themselves say.

The Why-Am-I-Money-Blocked session opens with this variant:

- **Joe:** "What scares you about money?"
- **Person:** "Um, I — so, the surface level, I don't know."
- **Joe:** "Um, if you had to guess?"
- **Person:** "Why, if I had to guess, uh, I would guess that I don't feel like I deserve it."
- *(Source: Why Am I Money Blocked?)*

The exchange is three lines. The "I don't know" was the predictable evasion. The "if you had to guess?" removed the certainty requirement. The guess that arrived ("I don't feel like I deserve it") was the actual surface answer — the thing the person knew but had been treating as too uncertain to say.

The variant works for the same reason the body-attempt version works: the move removes the standard. "Even if it's fucked up" removes the standard for the bodily attempt; "if you had to guess" removes the standard for the cognitive attempt. In both cases the person can fail at the move and still satisfy the instruction. The lowered stakes are what unblock the production of an answer.

When to use which variant:
- **Use "give me a good attempt":** when the instruction was body-level (love yourself, feel the emotion, put your hand on your heart).
- **Use "if you had to guess?":** when the instruction was cognitive (what scares you, what do you think, what's underneath).
- **Use both in sequence:** when the cognitive guess opens the door and the body-attempt is the next step. The Why-Am-I-Money-Blocked session does this — the guess surfaces the deserving claim, and then the body work begins on the safety underneath.

The shared underlying instruction is the same: do not stop at "I don't know." Find the variant that fits the register of the question being asked.
