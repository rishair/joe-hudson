# I Love Your Frustration

## The Move

When the leader of an organization is having an emotion — frustration, fear, sadness, irritation — and the team is in the room, the move is for someone on the team to welcome the emotion directly. Not to manage it. Not to try to fix what produced it. Not to caretake. To name that the emotion is welcome and that the leader's full humanness is welcome.

The canonical form Joe describes is a single sentence from his colleague Sarah:

"I love your frustration." -- Sarah, recounted in Authority Issues

See [[sarah-i-love-your-frustration]] for the full story of the specific moment this move comes from. This article extracts the move from the story for use as a coaching instrument.

What it does: dissolves the leader-team separation in a single move. The us/them dynamic that had been forming in Joe's experience disappeared. The leader was no longer the leader in that moment; they were a human having an emotion, and the human had been met as a human.

"I was starting being a leader in a company in this, like, of a bigger size, and I was starting to like not feel seen and like, oh, there's like a little bit of a — there's the them and us and like me and them, that kind of like authority issue thing coming up. I got really frustrated one day and Sarah was like, I love your frustration. And all the separation totally went away. It's us, it's us, it's — we're doing this together. I have a role, you have a role, but we're humans together. And that's what happens if you don't have an authority issue, right?" -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## What Makes This Move Possible

The move is structurally simple — a single sentence — but it requires several conditions to deliver cleanly:

**1. The person delivering it does not have an [[authority-issues]] activation toward the leader.** If the deliverer is in Position 2 (approval-seeking), the welcome lands as flattery. If the deliverer is in Position 1 (rebellion), the welcome lands as sarcasm. The move only works from a position of meeting the leader as a person rather than as the authority slot.

**2. The welcome is for the emotion, not for the surface trigger.** Sarah did not say "I love that you're frustrated with X." She said "I love your frustration." The emotion itself, not the content, is what is being welcomed. The move depends on this distinction. The surface content can be addressed later. The emotion is what most needs the welcome.

**3. The leader has to be capable of receiving it.** The move can fail when the leader cannot let the welcome land. A leader who has built their identity around being the strong one may flinch away from the welcome. The move is still worth making — repeated landings shift the pattern — but the first welcome may bounce off.

**4. The welcomer has to be in their own undefended state.** A welcomer who is internally bracing for the leader's frustration cannot offer a real welcome. The move requires the welcomer to actually be okay with the leader's emotion, not to perform okayness while internally managing.

## Why It Dissolves the Us/Them

The us/them dynamic in a leader-team relationship is held in place by the leader being treated as the role rather than as a person. The role can be frustrated, but the team manages around it. The role can be tired, but the team adjusts. The role's emotions are conditions to optimize against rather than experiences to meet.

When someone on the team welcomes the emotion directly, the role is briefly dropped. The leader is met as the person having the emotion. The person-meeting-person frame replaces the role-meeting-role frame. The us/them is a function of the role-meeting-role frame. Once the person-meeting-person frame is operating, the us/them has no purchase.

This is also why the move is more powerful than any structural change the leader could announce. The structural changes operate inside the role frame. This move steps outside the role frame entirely. The dissolution happens because the frame itself has shifted, not because the contents inside the frame have improved.

## The Inverse: What Most Teams Do With Leader Emotion

The default team responses to a leader's emotion:

- **Manage around it.** Lower voices, exit the room, take the call later. The emotion is real; the team's response treats it as bad weather.
- **Try to fix the trigger.** Identify what produced the emotion and address it as fast as possible. The emotion is treated as a problem to be solved.
- **Caretake.** Offer reassurance, soften the situation, take on additional work to reduce the leader's load. The emotion is treated as a vulnerability to be patched.
- **Distance themselves.** Withdraw from the leader to avoid being splashed by the emotion. The emotion is treated as contagion.
- **Mirror the emotion.** Get frustrated back, get scared back. The emotion is treated as an attack.

Each response confirms the role frame. The leader is being responded-to as the role having the emotion, not as the person having it. The leader, registering the role-response, becomes more held in the role and less met as a person. The loneliness Joe describes in [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] is partly produced by these responses, repeated over years.

## What the Welcomer Has to Have Internally

The move is not a technique. It is the natural expression of a particular internal stance:

- **The leader is a person to me, not a role.** I see the human in the role.
- **The emotion is welcome here.** Including frustration. Including fear. Including the messy ones.
- **My job is not to fix what they are feeling.** My job is to be present to them having the feeling.
- **The relationship is reciprocal.** I am not in a relationship with the boss. I am in a relationship with another person who happens to hold the boss role.

A welcomer who has all four can make this move naturally. A welcomer who lacks any of them may say the same words and not produce the same effect. The internal stance is the move; the words are just one carrier.

## When the Move Comes From the Leader's Side

The move has an inverse form that the leader can initiate. When the leader is in an emotion, the leader can name it openly and welcome being seen in it, rather than performing composure. This is the [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] application of the same principle. The leader's openness invites the team's welcome. The welcome is more likely to arrive when the leader has signaled that it would be received.

Joe's own version: he names what is happening in him with his team, which signals that emotional welcomes are part of how the team operates. The Sarah-said-I-love-your-frustration moment was possible because the culture Joe had built made it possible.

"I'm sure that happened because I've made the culture a safe place to be able to do that." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The leader who wants to receive this move has to create the conditions under which it can be given. The conditions: a culture where emotion is welcome from any direction, including upward; a leader who has demonstrated that they will receive without retaliating; team members who have been welcomed enough to develop the capacity to welcome back.

## What This Move Looks Like Across Contexts

The Sarah example is in a startup. The move applies in any leader-team context:

- A direct report to a C-level executive: "I love that you're nervous about this. It tells me you care about it."
- A junior employee to a senior leader in a meeting: "I appreciate that you're angry about this. The anger is helping us see what matters."
- A facilitator to a CEO who is frustrated in a workshop: "Your frustration is welcome here. We can work with it."
- A spouse to a partner who runs a company and comes home tired: "I love that you let me see how exhausted you are. Welcome to being a human again."

The form rotates. The structure is the same: name the emotion, welcome it, indicate the person is welcome as the person having the emotion.

## What This Move Is Not

**Not flattery.** "I love your frustration" delivered with the goal of getting in the leader's good graces is the opposite of this move. The flattery version reinforces the role frame; the move dissolves it.

**Not therapy.** The welcomer is not pretending to be a clinician processing the leader's emotion. They are being a person meeting another person.

**Not enabling.** Welcoming the emotion does not mean accepting whatever behavior the emotion is producing. If the frustration is being weaponized — yelling at people, throwing things — the move is different. See [[name-the-authoritys-fear]] for the move when the emotion is being acted out destructively.

**Not a substitute for boundaries.** A welcomer who lacks the capacity to also hold boundaries with the leader cannot make this move cleanly. The capacity to welcome requires the capacity to also say no when the emotion becomes a demand. Both have to be present.

## What Tends to Happen

When the move lands cleanly:

- The leader visibly softens. Often the body registers the welcome before the conscious mind does.
- The us/them dynamic dissolves in the moment. The relational field shifts.
- The leader becomes more able to do what they could not do while in the role frame: ask for help, name what they actually need, drop the performance.
- The welcomer earns a particular kind of trust. The leader will return to that person for the unwelcome conversations later, because the person has demonstrated the capacity to meet the leader as a person.

When the move does not land:

- The leader does not register the welcome. The role frame is too well-defended. The move was still worth making and may need repetition.
- The leader interprets the welcome as flattery or boundary violation. The internal stance of the welcomer was unclear or compromised.
- The welcomer's own discomfort with the leader's emotion leaked through. The leader sensed it and could not receive the welcome.

## Tone

The move is delivered with warmth, lightness, and zero subtext. The tone matters as much as the words. Said with affection, it lands. Said with performance, it bounces. Said with sarcasm, it harms. The welcomer's body has to be open and undefended for the words to carry the welcome.

The brevity matters. "I love your frustration" — five words — is more powerful than a paragraph of welcome. The compression is part of the move. The leader does not need to hear an explanation of why their emotion is welcome. They need to feel it as welcome.
