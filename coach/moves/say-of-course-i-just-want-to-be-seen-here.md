# Say "Of Course I Just Want to Be Seen Here" (The Phrase That Softens the Fight)

## The Move

When the person is mid-fight, mid-cycle, mid-rumination about not being seen by a partner, parent, boss, or friend, have them say (out loud or silently): "Of course I just want to be seen here."

The move is small. The phrasing is precise. The recognition that the want is natural -- "of course" -- is the active ingredient. The internal fight against having the want is what was producing most of the suffering, and the "of course" softens the fight.

"I love the phrase 'of course' cuz there's something in my nervous system and most people I work with, the minute it's like, 'of course I just wanna be seen here.' That it just like makes it not a problem. And the fight stops, the internal fight stops, and there's like just this softening of, 'oh, of course I just wanna be seen here,' and hanging out with that." -- Tara Howley, The Desire To Be Seen

The move is doing two things at once:

1. **Naming the actual want** (to be seen) instead of running it sideways through frustration or withdrawal
2. **Validating the want as natural** ("of course") instead of treating it as a defect to be argued away

The second part is what most people skip. They have learned to either suppress the want for being seen or to demand it from others. Neither move recognizes that the want is the same biological need they had as an infant tracking the mother's face. The "of course" delivers the recognition.

## Why "Of Course" and Not Other Phrasings

The specific phrase matters. Some alternatives that look similar but do something different:

- "I want to be seen" -- names the want but does not validate it. The shame layer is still operating.
- "It's okay that I want to be seen" -- adds an "okay" that implies the want was previously not okay. This subtly reinforces the shame structure rather than dissolving it.
- "I deserve to be seen" -- invites a counter-argument about deservingness. The system can argue with deserving.
- "Of course I want to be seen here" -- treats the want as obviously natural. No deservingness to argue. No permission to grant. The validation is structural, not conditional.

The "of course" is doing the same work that other "of course" moves do in Joe's coaching, where the recognition that a difficult feeling makes complete sense is what allows the system to relax around it. See [[heartbreak-is-the-love-underneath-the-blockers]] for another "of course" framing ("of course you want them to be well") working the same mechanism on a different feeling.

## What the Move Produces

The fight stops. Specifically the internal fight -- the part of the person that was trying to not want what they want, or trying to make the want be met by extracting it from someone who is not currently giving it, or making the want wrong.

When the internal fight stops, several things become available:

- The person can actually feel the want, instead of fighting it
- The seeing self begins: saying "of course I want to be seen" is already a form of seeing oneself
- The somatic state shifts -- usually a softening in the chest, the jaw, the shoulders
- The next move (which might be asking for what is wanted, or noticing where it is already available, or grieving where it was not met) becomes possible

The "of course" is not the destination. It is the move that unlocks the rest of the work.

## When to Use This Move

- The person is in a fight with a partner about not feeling seen and the fight is escalating
- The person is ruminating about a boss, parent, or colleague who does not acknowledge them
- The person has just noticed they are in [[in-the-story-im-not-being-seen]] and the read needs an in-the-moment intervention
- The person is mid-session and the desire to be seen has surfaced and is unowned
- The person is doing the [[see-yourself-and-acknowledge-others]] practice and needs the phrase that turns it from a checklist into a felt recognition
- Anywhere the phrase "I just want to be seen" arrives in the room and needs to be received rather than fixed

## How to Deliver the Move

Slowly. The phrase has weight only if it lands. Saying it fast collapses it into a checkbox.

In live coaching:
- Slow the pace. Let the previous statement settle.
- Offer the phrase as an experiment: "Try saying this, out loud or in your head: 'of course I just want to be seen here.'"
- Let them say it.
- Pause. Let them feel what happens.
- Ask what they notice. Often: softening, tears, a small smile, a release in the chest.
- If the phrase did not land, try saying it together. Or try a slight variation calibrated to the specific moment ("of course I want my mom to see me").

In self-practice (a person doing this for themselves):
- Notice the moment you are caught in the fight about not being seen
- Pause
- Say it to yourself: "Of course I just want to be seen here"
- Let it land. The landing is the practice.

## The Companion Recognition

The phrase often produces an immediate side-effect: by saying "of course I want to be seen here," the person is, in that act, beginning to see themselves. The recognition that the want is natural is itself a form of acknowledging the self that is wanting. The move starts the bidirectional seeing of [[see-yourself-and-acknowledge-others]] in the same instant it names the want.

"In doing so, that usually is the beginning of -- when you say 'of course' to yourself, you're starting to see yourself, right? That's honoring a part of yourself." -- Tara Howley, The Desire To Be Seen

This is why the move is structurally complete on its own, even before any external seeing arrives. The seeing has begun internally the moment the recognition lands.

## What Not to Do

- Do not turn it into a should ("I should be okay with wanting to be seen"). The shoulds reinforce the original shame structure.
- Do not use it to bypass the actual feeling. The move is not "say this magic phrase and then move on." The move is "say this phrase and stay with what arises."
- Do not deliver it as an analysis ("see, you have a natural human need to be seen, this is normal"). The intellectual frame does not land somatically.
- Do not use it as a tool to manage your partner ("I'm just going to tell myself it's okay so I don't ask you for what I want"). The move is for you, not as a strategy for managing someone else.

## Tone

Soft. Slow. Permissive. The "of course" carries the tone. It is not the tone of a teacher granting permission, which would imply that permission was needed. It is the tone of pointing out something obvious that the person had been laboring not to see.

In Tara's delivery on the podcast, the phrase carries the same weight as a sigh of recognition -- "oh, *yes*, of course." The move is to deliver it with that quality.
