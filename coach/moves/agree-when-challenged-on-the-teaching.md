# Agree When Challenged on the Teaching

## The Move

When someone challenges the teaching itself -- says it is wrong, says the coach is full of shit, says the perspective does not apply -- the move is to agree rather than defend. Not to perform humility. Not to capitulate. To genuinely refuse to claim the teaching as absolute truth.

The teaching is offered as a helpful perspective for certain people at certain times. The coach holds it that way. When someone says it does not work for them, that report is taken at face value, not as resistance to be overcome.

"You see me do this. You've been in rooms with me where someone's like, 'You're full of shit.' And I'll be like, 'Yeah, yes, yeah.' I can't say that what I'm saying is the absolute truth. I'm not gonna say that that's true. I'm gonna say that this is a helpful perspective, and it might not be helpful to you." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## What It Sounds Like

- "Yeah, yes, yeah."
- "I can't say that what I'm saying is the absolute truth."
- "This is a helpful perspective, and it might not be helpful to you."
- "It might be only helpful at a certain time in a certain place for certain people. And if the perspective gives you freedom, fantastic. And if it doesn't, fantastic."

The structure is the same each time: agree first, then add the qualifier. The qualifier is not a defense; it is a description of how the teaching is actually being held. Helpful for some, not for others. Right for some times, not for others.

## When Joe Uses It

When a student, audience member, or person in the room challenges the perspective directly. The challenge can come as criticism ("you're full of shit"), as disagreement ("I don't think that's true"), or as a request for absolute claims ("but isn't this the right way?").

The move is also useful when someone is trying to position the teaching as a new ideology to fight or defend. By refusing to claim the teaching is absolute, the coach refuses the fight before it starts.

## Why It Works

It demonstrates the very thing being taught. The teaching is about not thinking you have the whole story (see [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs]], the third kind). A teacher who claims their teaching is the whole story is contradicting the teaching with their stance. A teacher who actually holds the teaching as partial is teaching it twice: once with the words, once with the stance.

It also bypasses the architecture of being-right. The challenge usually expects defense. Defense escalates. Agreement deflates the dynamic. The person is left without anyone to argue against. They are now alone with their own experience, which is where the actual investigation can happen.

It also models the principle of [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]]. If the teaching does not produce freedom for this person, then for this person it is not the right teaching. The coach does not own the test. The person's experience is the test.

## The Internal Move

The external move is "yeah, yes, yeah." The internal move is harder: actually not needing the teaching to be true. The coach who agrees externally while internally believing the person just does not understand has not made the move; they have only performed it.

The actual move requires holding the teaching as one's best current articulation, useful in many cases, certainly not universal, and not the coach's identity. The coach is not the teaching. The coach's worth does not depend on the teaching being right. When that internal stance is real, the external agreement is honest rather than strategic.

This is also why the move is hard. The coach has built a life and a body of work around these perspectives. To genuinely hold them as not-absolutely-true requires the coach to have done the same work on themselves that the work asks of clients. The coach who has not done this work cannot make this move; they can only fake it.

## What It Produces

Usually relief. The person who came in to fight finds nothing to fight. The energy that was set up for opposition has nowhere to discharge. Often what comes next is curiosity: "okay, so what is this then?" That curiosity is the actual opening.

Sometimes confusion. The person expected the teacher to defend. The non-defense leaves them disoriented. The disorientation is also an opening; it interrupts the script they were running.

Occasionally the person leaves angry that they did not get the fight they came for. That is also data. The teaching was not the issue; the fight was. The move surfaces this cleanly.

## How It Connects to the Third Kind of Limiting Belief

The third kind of limiting belief is thinking you know the whole story. See [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs]] and [[covey-subway-story]]. A teacher who refuses to admit their teaching is partial has installed the third kind of limiting belief about their own teaching.

"It's about being able to integrate new knowledge. And if you can't integrate new knowledge because you think you have the whole story, then you're limited, period." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

The teacher applies this to themselves first. The teaching includes the possibility that the teaching itself is wrong, partial, or unhelpful for some people. This is not a hedge. It is a structural commitment.

## The Distinction From False Humility

The move is not "I might be wrong about everything." That is its own performance and produces a different kind of trap. The move is closer to "I have a perspective that has been useful for me and useful for many. It might not be useful for you. Test it. If it produces freedom, fantastic. If not, fantastic."

The position is undefended without being weak. The coach has spent decades developing the perspective; the perspective is not nothing. But the perspective is also not absolute. Both can be true. The move holds both.

"Otherwise, I'd be like, 'Oh no, this is the right way.' And I would be right. It is the right way for certain people at a certain time. You know what I mean?" -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## The Without-Dodging-Your-Truth Clause

Joe adds an important qualifier when Brett asks about integrating multiple perspectives: agreement with the challenger does not mean abandoning your own truth. If a perspective still does not feel right to you, do not adopt it just because someone is pushing it. The move is about not claiming your truth as absolute. It is not about losing your truth.

"Without dodging your truth. Meaning if it still doesn't feel right for you, you don't go, 'Okay, yes, because there's another side.' You still have to be in integrity with yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

This is the difference between this move and capitulation. The coach can agree that their teaching is partial without abandoning the part that is true for them. The integrity of the stance is in the both/and: I hold this and I hold it as partial.

## Tone

Light, undefended, slightly amused. Not solemn. Not performatively humble. Just genuinely not needing to be right. The slight amusement is at one's own previous attachment to being the right one. The lightness comes from being unhooked from the outcome.

The "yeah, yes, yeah" carries a particular casualness. It is not "you make an excellent point." It is more like "okay, sure." The casualness is part of what disarms the dynamic.

## Anti-Pattern Form

The move degenerates into capitulation when the coach actually believes the challenger and only afterward rationalizes it as humility. If a challenge produces actual reconsideration of the teaching, that is fine -- iterate the teaching. But the move proper is not about agreeing because the other person is right; it is about refusing to claim absolute status while still standing in the teaching.

It also degenerates when used to avoid difficult conversations. "Yeah, yes, yeah" can become a way to slide out of an actual exchange. The point is to refuse the absolute claim, not to refuse the engagement. The coach still has the perspective. The perspective is still being offered. Only the absoluteness is dropped.

It also fails when the coach is using it to look impressive (look how non-defended I am). That is itself a position. The move only works when the coach genuinely does not need to be right.
