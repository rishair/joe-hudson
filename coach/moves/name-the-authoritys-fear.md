# Name the Authority Figure's Fear (From Below)

## The Move

When a more powerful person -- a boss, an executive, a senior leader -- is dysregulated and aiming the dysregulation at you (yelling, micromanaging, criticizing in front of others), name what is underneath the behavior. Specifically, name the fear. Then state what you need.

The form Jaime Waydo used with a senior leader who had been yelling at her for six months:

"I can tell you're scared. I just want you to know, I don't respond to yelling. I actually become less creative and less able to help you solve the problem." -- Jaime Waydo, Taking Care of Yourself First

She then sat there. She did not try to fix him. She did not try to make it okay. The leader looked at her, smiled, leaned forward, and said: "Great, let's start solving the problem." The relationship shifted 180 degrees in one move.

## What Has to Be True for This to Work

The naming has to be accurate. Yelling is often anger displayed over fear of failure, fear of not being successful, fear of losing control. If the read is right, the naming lands. If the read is wrong, the naming feels like an attack. See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]].

The naming has to come without contempt. The tone has to be observational and clean. The same words delivered with judgment ("you're just scared") instead of compassion ("I can tell you're scared") produce opposite results. The first feels like a counter-attack. The second feels like being seen.

The person making the move has to be willing to be fired in the next ten seconds. If the move is held back by fear of consequence, the move is already compromised. The clean version requires having pre-felt the consequence. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]]. Jaime's preparation: "in all cases, by the way, at work, the worst thing that can happen is you get fired. And it's like, okay, well, I have 3 recruiters calling me right now."

The naming has to be followed by what you actually need, stated plainly. Not "please stop yelling." Not a request that the leader manage themselves. A clear statement of what you need to be able to do your work: "I don't respond to yelling. I become less able to help you." This converts the moment from accusation to collaboration.

## The Relationship to the Public Truth-Bomb

This move is the subordinate version of [[public-truth-bomb]]. Both name what everyone already knows. Both work because they break the polite fiction that the dynamic is invisible. The difference:

- The public truth-bomb is delivered by a facilitator or coach with explicit consent from the leader.
- This move is delivered by a subordinate without consent and without permission. The risk is greater. The required clarity in the deliverer is greater.

What makes the move possible from below is the same thing that makes it possible from a coach: the deliverer is unattached to the outcome. They have already grieved the worst case. They are speaking from clarity, not from the hope that the leader will respond well.

## What This Replaces

The default options when an authority figure is yelling:

- **Submit:** absorb the yelling, try to fix the surface complaint, hope it ends. The yelling continues because the underlying fear has not been addressed.
- **Rebel:** yell back, escalate, get reported or fired. The dynamic worsens. See [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]].
- **Flee:** quit, transfer, avoid. The pattern remains for the next person.
- **Caretake:** anticipate the leader's anxiety, manage everything to prevent triggering it. Exhausting and reinforces the dynamic. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

This move is none of these. It names the actual emotion and asserts what is needed. It treats the leader as a whole human capable of receiving the truth, not as a force of nature to be managed.

## What Tends to Happen

When the move lands cleanly, the leader often visibly relaxes. The fear they have been hiding has been named with compassion. They no longer have to perform the cover-anger. The hidden thing has been seen, and the world did not end.

Jaime's experience: the leader smiled and leaned forward. The behavior reorganized in the same conversation. This is consistent with what happens after a public truth-bomb -- the body, presented with truth, often releases instead of defending. See [[public-truth-bomb]] for the same dynamic at the facilitator level.

When the move does not land cleanly, several things can go wrong:
- The leader denies the fear and escalates the anger.
- The leader fires the person or escalates them out.
- The leader receives it as insubordination.

The risk is real. The decision to make the move depends on whether the deliverer has pre-felt the consequence and is acting from clarity rather than from hope.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The way you would name any obvious thing in the room. Not therapeutic. Not condescending. Not soft. Not loud. The tone of someone reporting what they see, exactly as Jaime described it: "It was just so clear to me. It came out of me. I wasn't there. The filter was gone."

The filterlessness matters. The move requires the [[rehearsing-conversations]] machinery to be turned off. If you are running 4D chess about how the leader will respond, you cannot make this move cleanly. The move comes from the first thing that arrives in your head when you stop calculating.

## What It Models for the Team

When a subordinate names an authority figure's fear in a clean way, the entire team learns something. They learn that the truth can be spoken upward without destroying the speaker. They learn that the authority figure can be approached as a person, not just as a role. They learn that the unspoken thing can be named.

This is how cultures shift -- not from policy but from one person doing the thing that nobody thought was possible and not being destroyed for it.
