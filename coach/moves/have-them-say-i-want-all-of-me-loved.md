# Have Them Say "I Want All of Me Loved"

## The Move

Have the person sit grounded (both feet on the floor, attention down in the body, eyes open). Then have them say aloud: "I want all of me loved."

The move is not an affirmation. The point is not the words. The point is to set up a stimulus that the body will respond to — most often with a small retreat or refusal somewhere — so that the retreat can be located and met.

The full sequence:
1. Have them ground their body (both feet on floor, attention down, eyes open).
2. Have them say: "I want all of me loved."
3. (Optionally Joe says back: "I want all of you loved too, by the way." This is not just kindness — it is delivering the welcoming externally as well as internally, so the body has two angles to react from.)
4. Then: "Take a second, scan your body, see what part of you can't be loved in your mind, in your heart. What is there? Any part of you?"
5. Watch the body.
6. When the body retreats, name it. See [[the-pulled-back-from-part-is-the-unloveable-part]] and [[name-the-body-defense-in-real-time]].

The statement is bait. The body's refusal is the data.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a person presents a self-judgment they have moralized into a fact ("I'm fake," "I'm corrupt," "I'm too much")
- After [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] has loosened the moral collapse but the actual hidden part has not yet surfaced
- When the person cannot calibrate the self-accusation ("I can't understand how fake I am") — see [[any-amount-answer-as-total-installation]]
- When the work needs to drop from the head into the body
- Before a body-scan move ([[scan-body-for-what-cant-be-loved]]) to give the scan something to react to

## What It Produces

- The body produces a specific retreat at a specific moment, often within seconds of saying the statement
- The person sometimes laughs or makes a small sound ("Oops!" in one transcript) when they notice the retreat
- The person sometimes notices a thought arising ("It was a doubt") that points at the part that cannot be loved
- The person sometimes goes blank, which is itself the data — the dissociation IS the refusal
- The eyes often move (down, away, up) during the moment of refusal

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the body retreats: name it in real time. "Look what just happened. Where'd you go?" See [[name-the-body-defense-in-real-time]]. Then send them back to what they retreated from. See [[the-pulled-back-from-part-is-the-unloveable-part]].
- If the body does not retreat (rare): the work may already be done, or the person may be in the head and not connected to the body. Joe will then have them re-ground or re-state more slowly.
- If the person says the statement performatively without contact: Joe slows them down. The point is not to perform the welcoming. The point is to attempt it sincerely and watch what blocks.
- If the person says it and then immediately offers a story to explain why parts of them can't be loved: Joe interrupts and returns to the body. The story is the next layer of defense. The body is the access.

## Dialogue Examples

From "Am I A Fake?":

- **Joe:** "Will you do me a favor and sit squarely with both feet on the ground. Bring your attention not just like up here, but I want you to bring it all the way down. Eyes open. There you go. Okay, there it is. I'd love to hear you just say: I want all of me loved."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, I want all of me loved."
- **Joe:** "I want all of you loved too, by the way."
- **Guest:** "Thank you."
- **Joe:** "You're welcome. And just take a second for — scan your body and see what part of you can't be loved in your, in your mind, in your heart. Like, what is there? Any part of you? Oh, look what just happened. That was amazing. Where did you retreat into? Where'd you go?"
- *(Source: Am I A Fake?)*

Within seconds of the welcoming statement, the body produces the retreat that becomes the entire rest of the session.

## Tone

Gentle, slow, almost ceremonial. Joe sets up the body first ("both feet on the ground, attention all the way down, eyes open"). The grounding is part of the move — without it, the statement bounces off the head and produces no body signal.

The "I want all of you loved too, by the way" is delivered as a small intimate gift, not as a coaching technique. This matters: the move only works when the welcoming is sincere. Performed welcoming produces performed retreat (or no retreat at all).

## Variations

- **For people in inner-critic struggles:** Have them say "I love you" to themselves in a mirror or to the camera. See [[tell-yourself-you-love-yourself-in-a-mirror]]. Same logic — bait the system, watch the refusal.
- **For people in shame about wanting:** Have them say "I want a shit ton of X" — see [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]]. Same logic at a more specific layer.
- **For people unable to receive a partner's love:** Have the partner state the welcoming aloud. See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]]. The partner becomes the external source the body has to refuse or receive.

## Anti-Pattern Risk

If used as an affirmation, the move backfires. Affirmations install the words on top of the existing wiring without producing change. The whole point of this move is to use the statement as a probe to surface the wiring. If the coach (or AI coach) treats the saying-out-loud as the goal, the work stops where it should start.

The other risk: forgetting to ground the body first. Without grounding, the statement is processed in the head and produces nothing useful.
