# Name the Appreciation Arriving in the Moment

## The Move

The person has just had a recognition. They are asking how to keep it — how to turn it into a practice they can repeat. Joe has already named the trap of turning the insight into a discipline (see [[when-experiments-become-management]]). The question then becomes: if not discipline, what?

Then, in the room, the person looks at Joe with appreciation. The look is small, often almost involuntary — a brief softening, a glance, a smile. Joe catches it and names it.

"And there you are, look at there. Like, yeah, there — what's that? What do you have to do to —"
"Like, what was that? Yeah, that was appreciation. That was me looking at you with appreciation."
"That is your answer."
-- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Money Blocked?

The appreciation that just arose in the room is the answer to the practice question. Not "appreciate me more" — but "this thing that just happened, this looking-with-appreciation, this connection that just arose between us — this is the form. Appreciate that thing. Connect with that thing. Appreciate yourself in the doing of that."

## When Joe Uses It

- Immediately after [[when-experiments-become-management]] has been named — when the person has just been warned against turning the work into a discipline and now needs an alternative.
- When a live appreciative gesture has just happened in the room (a look, a softening, a smile, a thank you, a small acknowledgment) and the gesture itself contains the answer the person was reaching for.
- When the person is asking "what should I do?" and the answer is "look at what you just did." The doing has already happened; the move is to make it conscious.
- When connection has just been produced in the session and the person is about to leave the connection in search of a method to produce more of it.
- When the substitution for the felt-suppression has appeared spontaneously and the person needs to be shown that it has.

The move is opportunistic. It requires that the appreciation actually arose. It cannot be manufactured. The presence of the appreciation in the moment is the precondition; the move is what makes the precondition load-bearing.

## What It Produces

- **A conversion from abstract to concrete.** The person had been asking "how do I do X as a practice?" — an abstraction. The move converts the question into "look at what you just did, naturally, with no practice." The concrete instance replaces the abstract method.
- **An internal reframe of what counts as the work.** Before the move, the work felt like something to be done in dedicated practice time. After the move, the work is what is happening in the small moments — the look of appreciation, the small connection. The work is everywhere; the practice is recognizing.
- **A felt confirmation that the alternative is available.** The person had been worried that without the discipline framework, nothing would happen. The move demonstrates that something has already happened, without any discipline. The proof is in the room.
- **Permission to operate from connection rather than from method.** The "appreciate that thing, connect with that thing, appreciate yourself in the doing of that" instruction is a stance instruction, not a technique. The person now knows they can stay in the stance rather than reach for a tool.

## What This Move Is NOT

- It is not gratitude as a practice. The person is not being told to count their blessings. The move is the naming of a specific appreciation that has just happened in the room, and the conversion of that specific event into the form of the work.
- It is not "be appreciative more often." The instruction is not to produce more appreciation. The instruction is to recognize the appreciation when it arises and let it be the location of the practice.
- It is not [[gratitude-as-a-strategy-for-an-outcome]]. That anti-pattern is gratitude deployed to manifest something. This move is gratitude that has already arisen being recognized as the answer to a question about how to live.
- It is not flattery for the coach. Joe is not asking the person to appreciate him. He is using the appreciation that happened to arise toward him as an example of the form the person was reaching for. Any moment of appreciation, toward anyone, would have served the same function.
- It is not [[surface-the-spectrum-of-presence-in-real-time]]. That move names the live shift in presence ("I see you here differently now"). This move names the live arrival of a specific felt-state (appreciation) and treats that felt-state as the answer to a structural question.

## The Sequence

1. **The trap has been named.** The person has been warned against turning the recognition into a discipline. The cascade Joe names ("you'll tell yourself to do it, wonder if you'll do it, fail to do it, beat yourself up, feel unsafe") is in the room.

2. **The person is at a loss.** Without the discipline framework, what is the alternative? The question is genuine; the person does not have an answer.

3. **The appreciation arrives spontaneously.** In the source session, the person looked at Joe with appreciation — a small live moment of connection. Joe sees it.

4. **The catch-and-name.** "What's that? What was that?" Joe asks. The person, prompted, names it: "That was appreciation. That was me looking at you with appreciation."

5. **The conversion.** "That is your answer." Joe makes the specific live event the structural answer to the question. The appreciation that just happened is the form of the work.

6. **The stance instruction.** "Appreciate that thing, connect with that thing. Appreciate yourself in the doing of that." The instruction is not "do more appreciation." It is "stay with the appreciation that arises, including the appreciation of yourself in the doing."

7. **The closing.** "I'm with my lack of safety right now and appreciate that." The instruction collapses the felt-difficulty and the appreciation into a single move: be with what is here, and appreciate that you are being with it. The work has shape without being a discipline.

## Dialogue Example

After Joe has named the trap of turning the insight into a practice ("that's a pretty good way to make it into a lack of safety / you'll tell yourself to do that, wonder if you're going to, fail, beat yourself up, feel not safe"):

- **Person:** "Fair enough."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And there you are, look at there. Like, yeah, there — what's that? What do you have to do to —"
- **Person:** "like, what was that? Yeah, that was appreciation. That was me looking at you with appreciation."
- **Joe:** "That is your answer."
- **Person:** "That, that appreciation connection."
- **Joe:** "Appreciate that thing, connect with that thing. Appreciate yourself in the doing of that."
- **Person:** "Oh, look, right?"
- **Joe:** "Yeah, look at that. I'm with, I'm with my lack of safety right now and appreciate that."
- *(Source: Why Am I Money Blocked?)*

The exchange is roughly thirty seconds. The work converts from a methodology question to a felt-orientation question. The person leaves the session not with a practice to do but with a stance to occupy.

## Why It Works

The structural problem with "how do I make this a practice?" is that the question already presupposes the answer it is looking for — a method, a routine, a discipline. Any answer in that frame will produce the cascade Joe just warned against. The question itself is the trap.

The move bypasses the question by pointing at the answer that does not fit the question's frame. The appreciation that arose in the room cannot be turned into a method, because it was not produced by a method. It arose. The instruction "stay with what arises" is a stance, not a method.

This works because the person can verify the stance is real. They just experienced it. The appreciation was here. They did not produce it. They did not have to. The instruction "appreciate that thing, connect with that thing" is asking them to stay with the same kind of arising they have already had. It is a known territory, not a future practice.

The third mechanism: the move locates the work in the present moment rather than in a future scheduled time. The person who was about to leave the room with a practice to do is shown that the work is what is happening right now, in this conversation. There is nowhere else to go to do it. The location of the practice is wherever the person is.

## Tone

Light, present, specific. The catch happens fast — "what's that? what was that?" — before the appreciation can fade. The naming is small and direct. The conversion ("that is your answer") is delivered as a small declaration, not a teaching pronouncement. The stance instruction that follows ("appreciate that thing, connect with that thing") is offered as a description of what just happened more than as a future instruction.

If the move is delivered with too much weight, it becomes another teaching the person has to remember and apply. The lightness keeps it in the felt-territory it was just operating in. The appreciation was light; the catching of the appreciation has to be light too.

## Variations

The move generalizes wherever a specific felt-state has just arisen in the room and the person is asking a structural question about how to live:

- **For connection:** A person asking "how do I feel connected?" — and connection is what is happening between them and the coach right now. "What's that? That's the thing. You don't have to make it; it's here."
- **For self-love:** A person asking "how do I love myself?" — and a small kindness has just arisen toward themselves in the room. Catch it, name it, make it the answer.
- **For trust:** A person asking "how do I trust?" — and they have just trusted Joe with a vulnerable disclosure. "What did you just do? You trusted me with that. That's the form."
- **For presence:** A person asking "how do I be present?" — and they have just been present for a full minute of silence. "Look at where you just were. That's it."

The structure is the same: the structural question is in the room, the answer is also in the room, and the move is to point at the answer that has already arrived.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not manufacture the appreciation.** The move requires that the appreciation actually arose. If the coach asks for appreciation or solicits it, the move loses its structural shape. The person experiences it as flattery being requested rather than as a live event being noticed.
- **Do not over-explain after the catch.** The "that is your answer" is the conversion. Anything after it that is not the stance instruction tends to dilute the move. The work is in the recognition, not in the elaboration.
- **Do not turn the move into a new method.** Saying "from now on, watch for appreciation and let it be your answer" reinstalls the discipline trap. The move is for this specific moment in which appreciation arose, and the stance the person can occupy when other appreciations arise. It is not a watchfulness instruction.
- **Do not skip the stance instruction.** Just naming the appreciation without the "appreciate that thing, connect with that thing, appreciate yourself in the doing of that" leaves the person with a noticed event but no operational form. The stance instruction is what converts the event into a way of being.
