# Don't Chase Your Teen, but Always Be Available

## The Move

The standard parental instinct, especially when the teen starts pulling away, is to chase. Ask how their day was. Probe what is going on. Show interest by initiating contact constantly. Try to fix what feels like distance by adding more attempts at connection.

The move Joe describes: do the opposite. Do not chase. But always be available. The two halves are equally important and they work together.

"Emotionally, I find you never want to chase a teenager. But you always want to be available." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The move is structural. The parent removes the pursuit pressure entirely. The parent maintains a steady availability such that when the teen does turn toward the parent, the parent is present and the connection is uncomplicated.

## Why Chasing Backfires

Esme describes the felt experience of being chased, using a puppy metaphor:

"It's like the puppy running away and you're like, come back, come back, come back. And the puppy's like, oh my God, they're chasing me." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The chasing makes the teen want to run further. The pursuit creates the distance it is trying to close. This is the parenting version of [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]]: the chaser intensifies the avoider's avoidance. The structural dynamic does not change because the people involved are a parent and a child instead of romantic partners.

The teen experiences the chase as:
- Pressure they have to manage
- A sign the parent is making them responsible for the parent's emotional state
- An invasion of the space they need for their own developmental work
- A reason to retreat further to get back the room they need

The parent experiences themselves as showing love. The teen experiences pressure and retreats. The parent reads the retreat as rejection and intensifies the pursuit. The cycle accelerates.

## What "Available" Looks Like Without Chasing

The available-but-not-chasing stance has specific behavioral markers.

**When the kid enters the room:** the parent looks up, makes eye contact, becomes present. Not "what do you need?" or "how was your day?" Just present. Available.

"When you come in the room, I — if you're doing homework or something, but generally when you come into the room, I try to make myself available to you. Sometimes I'm really good at it, sometimes I'm not quite as good at it. So I make myself available, but I really try not to chase." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

**When the kid starts talking:** the parent listens. Without an agenda. Without trying to steer the conversation toward what the parent thinks the kid should be discussing. The parent's attention is given freely.

**When the parent has not heard from the kid in a while:** the parent can offer connection without pursuing. The form Esme describes: "Hey, I haven't seen you and talked to you in a while. Like, can we get lunch or can we get dinner?" The form is a genuine offer of presence, not an interrogation about why they have been distant. The difference is structural — the parent is offering themselves, not extracting information.

**When the parent has something to say:** they say it directly. Not through softening or hedging or "have you really thought about" insertions. The directness is part of the availability. The parent is not making the kid do the work of decoding hints.

"When you say something, be direct about it instead of, oh, I'm not sure. I see a lot of, I want to say this thing, but I don't feel like I can say this thing. So I'm going to kind of skirt around it a little bit. Have you really thought about — instead of, that doesn't feel good. You know, instead of like, that's a very direct thing." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

## The Distinction: Chasing vs. Offering Connection

The move is not "never reach out." There is a difference between chasing and offering connection.

**Chasing** has the felt quality of needing something from the teen. It comes from the parent's worry, loneliness, or fear that the relationship is slipping. It puts the kid in the position of having to manage the parent's state. It usually arrives in the form of probing questions, repeated attempts at contact, or surveillance ("how was your day, who were you with, what did you do").

**Offering connection** has the felt quality of giving something to the teen. It comes from the parent's recognition that the relationship is one they value and they want to make space for it. It does not require anything from the kid. It usually arrives in the form of an invitation that the kid can decline without consequence, like "want to get dinner together?" or "I'd love to spend time with you, not talking about anything in particular."

The teen can tell which is which. The chase produces retreat. The offer produces the choice to come closer or not, without pressure either way.

## What "Available" Means Internally for the Parent

For the parent to actually be available rather than chasing, something internal has to be true. The parent has to not be running their internal connection meter on the kid's behavior. The parent who tracks "how connected do I feel to my kid today" and adjusts their pursuit accordingly is functionally chasing even if the surface behavior looks calm.

The internal condition: the parent's wellbeing does not depend on the kid being available right now. The parent is okay if the kid spends the afternoon in their room. The parent is okay if dinner is short. The parent is okay if the kid is moody for a few hours. The parent's regulation does not require the kid's regulation.

When this internal condition is in place, the available stance is sustainable. When it is not, the parent will start chasing again as soon as the kid pulls back, regardless of intention.

This is why the upstream work — [[take-care-of-yourself-then-marriage-then-kids]] and the parent's own self-relationship — is required for this move. A parent who has not taken care of themselves cannot sustainably be available without chasing. The chasing is the leak.

## The Complementary Moves

The available-but-not-chasing stance pairs with several other moves:

- [[bring-the-problem-to-your-teen]] — when the parent does have something to address, they bring it as a shared problem rather than a directive
- [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]] — when the teen does offer something of their world, the parent receives it with curiosity
- [[connect-trip-with-your-kid]] — the parent creates structured time for connection that the teen can show up to without it being a constant chase

Together these moves produce a relationship where the teen knows the parent is there without feeling pursued. The teen comes toward the parent because the parent is present and undefended, not because the parent is demanding contact.

## What Esme Reports This Produces

Esme experiences the stance directly:

"It's never much of a struggle either." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The connection happens without effort. The conversations happen without negotiation. The teen comes to the parent because there is nothing in the way of coming. This is the inverse of the standard pursued-teen-and-pursuing-parent dynamic where every interaction involves the parent extracting and the kid resisting.

## When This Move Does Not Land

The move is designed for parents whose default has been to chase. If a parent is on the opposite end — checked out, unavailable, distant — telling them to "not chase" is the wrong instruction. They need to learn availability before they need to learn restraint.

The move also does not apply when the kid is in actual crisis. Active mental health crisis, safety risk, or substance abuse situations require the parent to actively engage, even at the cost of being read as chasing. The move is for the day-to-day parenting baseline, not for emergencies.

## Connected Concerns

- A parent realizing their teen has gone quiet and not knowing how to reach them
- A parent who keeps asking "how was your day" and getting nowhere
- A parent who feels they are losing the relationship and is intensifying contact in response
- A parent recognizing they are in the chase configuration with their teen
- A parent whose own parents chased them and who wants to do it differently

## Tone

The parent's internal tone is settled, not anxious. Not "I am being available." Just present. The work is not to add a parenting technique; the work is to drop the pursuing energy. The available stance emerges when the pursuit stops, not as a new performance.
