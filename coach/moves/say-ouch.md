# Say "Ouch"

## The Move

When someone is being passive-aggressive at you — criticizing, needling, guilt-tripping — respond with one word: "ouch." Or with a short sentence that names the hurt: "ouch, that hurt." Say it each time. Do not argue with whether it was meant as criticism. Do not try to change them. Just say ouch.

The move owns your own experience without trying to control theirs. It reports what landed. It is a small, repeated naming of the impact.

"Just try this one out for a minute. Just every time she criticizes, say ouch." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## What It Does

Several things at once:

1. **It makes the impact visible.** The passive-aggressive form depends on the impact being deniable. "Ouch" names that there was an impact, without arguing about intent.
2. **It takes the victim role away from the speaker.** The passive aggressor is often operating from the role of "I'm the one being hurt here." When you say ouch, you are naming that you are being hurt. The role no longer fits both of you.
3. **It refuses to argue about content.** You are not engaging with what they said. You are reporting your experience of being on the receiving end. This sidesteps the entire frame of "am I or am I not entitled to say that."
4. **It is repeatable.** Unlike a longer boundary statement, "ouch" can be said again and again without escalation. Each repetition makes the pattern more visible.

## Where the Move Came From

Joe used it with a client whose European mother had come to help with their child. The mother criticized constantly while framing herself as the one being oppressed. The client tried to address it directly; she made his every request itself into an oppression ("Am I not allowed to share my opinion?").

"He was very much actively trying to say, hey, it really hurts when you criticize the way we're taking care of the kid. 'Oh, am I not allowed to say my piece? Am I not allowed to like share my opinion? Fine. If I'm not allowed to share my opinion, I want to share my opinion here,' right? Like it's like that kind of like whatever thing he asked for, she wasn't aggressive. Like, don't tell me how to behave. She was basically making everything an oppression, right? So that she could maintain this thing." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The "ouch" move was the way out. Every criticism, just say ouch.

What happened:

"At first it was like, hey, uh, I didn't hurt you. And he would say, yeah, but it hurt, so I'm saying ouch. And eventually, like, she couldn't be in the position of passive aggression anymore because every time he said ouch. So it became really clear who, who was being hurt in that moment." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## What to Expect From the Receiver

The first response is usually denial: "I didn't hurt you." This is predictable. The passive aggressor cannot maintain their role if they accept that they are hurting someone — so the first instinct is to refuse the report.

The correct response to the denial is the same response: "yeah, but it hurt, so I'm saying ouch." You do not need them to accept your report for your report to be true.

Over repetitions, the position becomes unsustainable. They cannot keep being the one being hurt while their behavior keeps producing the "ouch" reports.

"But she really fought for a while, like, no, you can't take — I'm the hurt one. I'm like, you can't take that from me. You can't take the hurt one from me. That's me." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The fight to keep the victim role is real and worth being prepared for. See [[cant-take-the-victim-role-away]] for the read that explains this resistance.

## How to Say It (Upright vs. Passive-Aggressive Ouch)

The same word can be delivered uprightly or passive-aggressively. The form matters.

**Passive-aggressive ouch:** "Oww, you hurt me." Plaintive, victim-voiced, with the implication that the speaker is now being made to feel bad. This version repeats the dynamic in reverse.

**Upright ouch:** "Ouch." Clear, undefended, reporting what is. The speaker is not collapsing into being the hurt one. They are simply naming what landed.

"So the difference there is like, are you empowered? Do you feel empowered? Are you clear that you're making a choice and that you're not stuck?" -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The body cue is similar to the upright apology (see [[upright-apology]]). Back straight, voice steady, the report given from a place of being okay rather than from a place of collapse. The word "ouch" delivered uprightly is closer to a research observation than to a complaint.

## What It Replaces

The unhelpful alternatives:
- **Arguing about whether it was criticism.** "That wasn't very nice." → "I wasn't being mean, I was just saying..." → endless loop.
- **Getting angry back.** Matches their aggression with aggression, locking the dynamic. See [[dont-meet-aggression-with-aggression]].
- **Walking on eggshells.** Becoming hypervigilant about not provoking, which signals to them that they are in charge of the room.
- **Withdrawing love.** A passive-aggressive response to their passive aggression. See [[withdrawal-of-love-as-passive-aggression]].
- **Trying to change them.** "You shouldn't be passive-aggressive." Direct demand. See [[dont-weaponize-the-make-it-active-move]].

"Ouch" replaces all of these with a single small naming move that does not enter the dynamic.

## When To Use It

- A family member is making subtle digs that the rest of the family is letting slide
- A partner is being needling or critical under cover of "helping"
- A coworker is making sharp side comments in front of others
- Anywhere passive aggression is happening and you can speak

When not to use it:
- When the situation is overtly abusive (this move is for passive aggression specifically, not for direct attack)
- When you cannot stay grounded while saying it (it slides into passive-aggressive ouch)
- When you are using it as a control move yourself

## What It Requires of You

You have to actually be in touch with the hurt. "Ouch" performed without feeling becomes a passive-aggressive performance of being hurt. The word has to match what is happening in your body.

You also have to be able to keep saying it without resentment building up. If the resentment is loading, you are storing rather than releasing, and the move starts contaminating. You may need to release the underlying anger somewhere else (see [[get-angry-in-the-car]]) so that "ouch" stays clean each time.

## Tone

Light, direct, almost flat. Not plaintive. Not aggressive. The closest analogue is the way a child says "ouch" when something hurts — matter-of-fact, immediate, no story attached. The lack of story is what makes the move work.
