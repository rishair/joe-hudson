# Move the Anger, Then Draw the Boundary

## The Move

If you catch yourself being passive-aggressive — discovering that you have resentment, that you've been guilt-tripping, that you've been making digs without owning them — the move has two steps in this order:

1. **Move the anger in private.** Not at the other person. Not as a venting attack. Let the held anger fully discharge so the energy is no longer in your body driving the indirect form.
2. **Then draw the boundary.** Once the anger is no longer running you, name what you will and will not do going forward.

The order matters. A boundary drawn while the anger is still loaded comes out as a passive-aggressive boundary — accusatory, controlling, designed to punish or change them. A boundary drawn after the anger has moved comes out clean: a statement of your behavior, delivered from a place of being okay.

"If you are the one that realizes you're being passive-aggressive, like you have resentment, then the thing to do is to move that anger, to like let the anger go, to express it, not at the person, not in an abusive way, but to just fully express that anger and then draw the boundary that you need to draw instead of feeling like you can't draw the boundary and you can't move the anger." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression: How To Identify & Respond

## Why the Order Matters

The passive aggression is the kink that forms when both halves are blocked: you cannot move the anger (because it doesn't feel safe to be openly angry) and you cannot draw the boundary (because it doesn't feel safe to say no directly). The energy that should have done either job leaks sideways through guilting, criticism, withholding, lateness.

If you only do step two — set the boundary without first releasing the anger — the boundary comes loaded with the unfelt anger. It becomes another vehicle for the passive aggression. The other person feels it.

If you only do step one — release the anger — and skip the boundary, the anger keeps coming back, because the situation that produced it has not changed. You will need to release again, and again, and again, on a schedule set by their behavior.

Both halves are required. The anger release frees you from the load. The boundary changes the structure.

## What "Move the Anger" Means

Not at the person. Not as a confrontation. Privately, in a way that lets the held energy actually discharge.

The available practices:
- [[get-angry-in-the-car]] — yell, scream, growl in private
- [[free-write-the-anger]] — write everything you would say if there were no consequences
- [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] — perform the anger to start the engine if it is stuck
- [[how-to-release-anger-out-loud]] — practical instruction on the release

The point of all of these: get the energy out of your body. The anger is real, the boundary needs to be drawn, but the anger does not need to travel through the person you are setting the boundary with.

## What "Draw the Boundary" Means After the Anger Moves

The boundary is about your behavior, not theirs. See [[boundaries]] for the broader teaching. Once the anger has moved, the boundary often becomes obvious. The shape of the situation is clearer because you are no longer trapped inside the unfelt charge.

Examples from this transcript and elsewhere:
- "I will not come over to your house while you criticize me." (See [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]].)
- "I will not stay in a room where someone is yelling at me; I'll come back in 30 minutes."
- "I'm not going to spend Thanksgiving with you if you're going to make snide comments about my partner."

The boundary names what you will do under what condition. It does not demand that they change. It opens a door to the relationship continuing on a different basis.

## What This Replaces

The default move when you discover your own passive aggression is one of three:

**1. Spiral into shame about it.** "I'm so passive-aggressive, I'm a terrible person, why can't I just be direct." The shame does not release the anger, does not draw the boundary, and adds a new emotion to the pile.

**2. Decide to just be more direct from now on.** "I'm going to stop being passive-aggressive." Without releasing the anger and changing the structure, the resolution will not hold. The next time the situation repeats, the same kink will form.

**3. Confront the other person about your own passive aggression.** "I've been resenting you, I need to tell you about it." This is the form most likely to deteriorate fastest: the confrontation becomes the new vehicle for the passive aggression. The conversation feels like progress and produces another round of damage.

The two-step move replaces all of these. You take responsibility for the energy by moving it. You take responsibility for the structure by drawing the boundary. You do not require the other person to do either job for you.

## The Underlying Diagnostic

The reason passive aggression formed in the first place is that the person did not feel empowered. They did not feel they could be openly angry. They did not feel they could say no. The kinked form was the system finding a way to discharge the anger and exert some control without taking the risks of the direct form.

"That lack of empowerment, that lack of sovereignty, that lack of agency is the thing that makes somebody passive-aggressive. They don't feel like they can actually get what they want, and so they have to do it in a backhanded way." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression: How To Identify & Respond

The two-step move is the structural fix for the lack-of-empowerment underneath. Moving the anger demonstrates to your own nervous system that you can be openly angry without the world ending. Drawing the boundary demonstrates that you can say no without the relationship ending. Both demonstrations build the empowerment that was missing.

This is why the move is not just behavioral hygiene. It is the path out of the conditions that produced the passive aggression in the first place.

## When To Use It

- You notice you've been guilting someone and want to stop
- You realize you've been making digs about your partner to friends, or about your team to peers
- You catch yourself being chronically late for someone you would never be late for at work
- You feel ongoing resentment toward a person and notice it is leaking out sideways
- A friend or coach has pointed out a pattern of passive aggression and you can see it
- You can feel the held anger but you have not been letting yourself express it or draw the limit
- You are the cared-for in a caretaking dynamic and you have low-grade resentment about a decision that was made for you (the fridge case — see [[joe-and-tara-the-fridge-resentment]])
- The resentment has been showing up as criticism of one specific thing for weeks or months and the criticism is not landing or resolving

## When Not To Use It

- When the situation is acute and the boundary cannot wait for the anger release (then draw a temporary boundary first, do the release after, then refine)
- When the anger is the surface and there is grief or fear underneath that is the real material (the anger release will only do partial work; the deeper feeling needs to be met too)
- When you are confusing this move with a license to be aggressive at someone (the release is not at them)
- When the other person is not actually doing anything that warrants a boundary, and the resentment is yours to work with internally (see [[is-this-mine]])

## The Self-Diagnostic

Before you draw the boundary, check: has the anger actually moved, or am I about to draw the boundary as the new vehicle for the anger?

The indicators that the anger has moved:
- You can think about the person without your jaw tightening
- You can describe the boundary without your voice changing pitch
- The boundary statement can be delivered with care for them, not against them
- You are not rehearsing how to deliver it for maximum impact
- You can imagine them refusing the boundary and you are still okay

If any of these check out as no, the anger has not fully moved. More release work first.

## What the Boundary Sounds Like When It's Clean

The clean form starts from love and names what you will do. It does not start from grievance and demand that they change.

Joe's example with his parents: "Hey, I love you. I like coming home. But every time I come home, you criticize me. ... I love you. I want to come over to your house, but I'm not going to do that until you can agree to stop criticizing me when I come over." (See [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]] for the full story and what happened.)

The structure: love named, the behavior named, what you will do named, the door to a different future named. The anger has been released elsewhere. The boundary is clean.

## Variant: The Cared-For In A Caretaking Dynamic

The move also applies to a slightly different presenting case: not someone who has been actively passive-aggressive, but someone who has been the receiver of caretaking and has developed low-grade resentment about it.

The structure is the same. The anger is held (because being openly angry at someone who was "trying to help" feels ungrateful). The boundary is undrawn (because the action that needed the boundary has already been taken). The resentment is doing both jobs sideways — most commonly as criticism of the specific decision or thing the caretaker did, returning weekly for months at a time.

The two-step move is the path. Move the anger that has been held about the disempowerment. Then draw the boundary about how the cared-for wants to interact going forward: "Don't buy the fridge until we talk." "Don't make medical decisions for me without checking with me." "Don't sign me up for something without asking first."

The fridge story is the canonical instance. Tara had been criticizing the loud fridge weekly for six months. The criticism never resolved anything because it was not actually about the fridge — it was about the anger she had not expressed and the boundary she had not drawn. Once Joe and Tara could see the structure, the path became visible: move the anger, draw the boundary that says "this is how I want to interact with you around purchases that affect my sleep." See [[joe-and-tara-the-fridge-resentment]] for the full structure and [[resentment-as-caretaking-diagnostic]] for the dynamic the move addresses.

## In Coaching

When someone presents as the receiver of passive aggression, the moves are [[say-ouch]] (own your experience) and the principle [[dont-meet-aggression-with-aggression]] (do not try to change them).

When someone presents as the passive-aggressor — having recognized the pattern in themselves — this move is the path. The two-step nature is important to name explicitly because the cultural default is to skip the anger release and try to fix the behavior directly. The behavior cannot be fixed while the underlying energy is still loaded.

A common stuck point: the person believes they need to confront the other party as part of the work. The move is the reverse — do the work privately first, then act from a place where the work has already been done.

## Tone

Joe describes this move matter-of-factly, as the structural pair to the moves for the receiver. The tone is not corrective ("you should have been doing this all along") but mechanical ("this is the path that works for this case"). The passive-aggressor is not being shamed for being passive-aggressive — the form is the kink that develops when the conditions for the direct form are not available. The work is to make the direct form available, in stages.
