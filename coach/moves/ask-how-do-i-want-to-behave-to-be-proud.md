# Ask "How Do I Want to Behave That Will Make Me Proud?"

## The Move

When the person is preparing for a moment they have been dreading — a meeting, a first date, a hard conversation, a public moment where the fear of being seen is anticipated — the move is to redirect the planning from outcomes to behavior. The question that does the redirect is:

*What's the way I can behave that will make me super proud — no matter what happens?*

The "no matter what happens" clause is doing critical work. It severs the answer from outcome management. Whatever the meeting produces, whatever the date partner says, whatever the boss decides — these are not the metric. The metric is whether the person showed up in a way they themselves would be proud of.

"Oftentimes, if someone's preparing for a meeting or preparing for a first date, they're thinking about what the other person's gonna think about them and they're trying to manage that situation. But if they go in and say: what's the way that I can behave that will make me super proud no matter what happens? Everything starts changing." — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen*

## When Joe Uses It

The trigger is the moment of pre-meeting / pre-date / pre-conversation rehearsal. The person is in their head, running scenarios, planning what to say, predicting what the other will think. The signals are familiar:

- The person says things like "I'm trying to figure out how to handle the meeting"
- The person describes the other person's likely reactions in detail
- The person is mentally rehearsing language to manage the impression they make
- The pre-event anxiety is rising with each rehearsal pass
- The person reports they can't stop thinking about the upcoming moment

This is also the right move when the person has just come out of a meeting/conversation and is in the post-mortem loop ("I should have said this, why didn't I say that"). The move applied retrospectively becomes: were you proud of how you behaved? The answer redirects attention from the unchangeable outcome back to the choosable behavior.

The move pairs with [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]] — both are versions of the same redirect, from external management to internal compass. The "need" version operates in the heat of the conflict-seeking state; the "pride" version operates in the anticipatory planning state.

## The Mechanism

The fear-of-being-seen pattern runs on outcome-tracking: monitoring the other person's reaction, predicting it, adjusting to it, taking it personally when it doesn't go as predicted. The outcome-tracking is the activity that keeps the person out of themselves (see [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]]). The outcome-tracking is also the activity that generates the anxiety — because the outcome is not in the person's control, the tracking produces a futile loop of trying to manage an uncontrollable variable.

Pride about how you behaved is a fundamentally different metric:

- It is internal — the person decides
- It is in the present — the person can know in the moment whether they are behaving in a way they would be proud of
- It is in the person's control — they choose the behavior
- It is decoupled from outcome — the proud behavior remains proud regardless of what the other person does

The shift in metric is the shift out of the fear-mode. The fear was sustained by tracking a variable the person could not control. The new metric tracks a variable the person can. The system relaxes because the impossible task has been replaced with a possible one.

A second mechanism: when the person is acting in a way they are proud of, they care a lot less about what other people think. The shame layer that normally produced the fear of being seen is reduced because the person is currently behaving in a way that is shame-free. The shame and the fear of being seen are the same layer; reducing one reduces the other.

"When somebody's being the way that they want to be, they care a lot less about what other people think — because they're not in their own shame." — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen*

The move is also a version of [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]] applied to behavior-planning: "what is the way I want to behave" is structurally close to "what do I want / need." Both reorient from external to internal. The pride question adds the criterion of self-respect to the want, which makes it especially useful in moments where the person might otherwise act on the wanting-to-be-liked impulse and regret it.

## What It Produces

The question reliably produces several immediate shifts:

- The person stops rehearsing what they will say to manage the other person
- The person becomes able to name actual values for the moment ("I want to be honest," "I want to actually say what I think," "I want to listen instead of perform")
- The pre-event anxiety drops several notches because the impossible task has been swapped for a possible one
- The person often surfaces a value they had been hiding from themselves (e.g., "I really want to actually tell my partner I don't want this thing they want")
- The conversation/meeting/date often goes better, but the improvement is downstream of the shift, not the point of it

The point is not that the move produces better outcomes — though it often does. The point is that the question reorients the person from outcome-management to behavior-pride. The reorientation is the result. The outcomes are a side effect.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person can name a behavior they would be proud of: the work is to support them in actually doing that behavior in the moment. Sometimes the person names the behavior easily and then immediately reports they could not do it; the next-layer work is to look at what is in the way of the behavior (often a shame layer that says the behavior is not allowed). See [[two-layers-of-shame-about-being-seen]] when the wanting-shame is the block.

If the person can't name a behavior they would be proud of: the inability is the data. Sometimes the person has been so absorbed in outcome-management that they cannot find their own values for the situation. The work shifts to slower questions about what matters to them, what they care about, what they want their life to be about. See [[what-do-you-want]] for the broader question.

If the person names a behavior and then beats themselves up for not doing it last time: the work is to refuse the should-frame entirely. Pride is not a club to hit yourself with. See [[dont-should-yourself-into-being-seen]]. Pride is forward-looking, generative, freeing. Pride-as-criticism is the [[the-eye-of-sauron]] in disguise.

## Connection to Other Moves

This move is the planning-phase companion to [[open-the-heart-to-the-other-person]] (the in-the-moment companion) and [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]] (the in-the-heat-of-the-conflict companion). All three operate by the same principle: redirect from the external (managing the other) to the internal (knowing your own need / pride / connection). The three are deployed at different temporal points around the same configuration of fear-of-being-seen.

This move is also a specific instance of the broader principle of [[orientation-to-internal-feedback-instead-of-outcomes]]. The pride-question is the specific operational form of that principle when applied to interpersonal moments.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I have this big meeting tomorrow. I'm trying to figure out how to handle it. I want them to actually take me seriously."
- **Joe:** "What's a way you could behave in that meeting that would make you super proud of yourself — no matter what they do or say or think?"
- **Person:** [pause] "I'd be proud of myself if I actually said the thing I think, even if they don't agree with it."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Person:** [some quiet recognition]
- *(Source: Fear of Being Seen, paraphrased structure)*

The exchange compresses the work: the person arrived focused on outcome management, the move surfaced a value they already had, and the value gave them a metric they could actually use in the meeting.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly bright. The question is delivered as a real invitation, not as a leading prompt. The "no matter what happens" clause is dropped in casually but its importance is structural — the coach can land it lightly while still ensuring the redirect lands.

The tone is also not solemn or weighty. Pride in this context is small-p pride — the satisfaction of acting in accordance with what you actually value. Not the heavy moral self-assessment a person might bring. The lightness of the tone is part of what allows the question to function.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not a request to be a hero. The behaviors that would make the person proud are usually small and human — "I'd be proud if I actually said what I think," "I'd be proud if I listened instead of performed," "I'd be proud if I admitted I don't know." Not large-scale heroism; ordinary alignment with one's own values.

This is also not a setup for self-criticism if the person fails to behave in the proud way in the moment. The work after the moment is to look at what got in the way, not to punish the person for failing. See [[regret-dissolved-by-pride-in-how-you-behaved]] for the related principle that pride about behavior is the working antidote to retrospective regret.

This is also not goal-setting. Pride about behavior is the metric of internal alignment in the moment. Goal-setting is forward-looking outcome-tracking, which is the exact mode the move is designed to interrupt.
