# Go to Your Heart, Not Your Brain

## The Move

When the person is trying to answer a question with their mind and the mind is failing — particularly questions about gifts, contributions, nature, or purpose — interrupt the mental search and redirect attention literally into the heart. Tell them to put their attention in their heart and ask the heart for its answer. When the mind tries to answer, name that it was the mind and redirect again.

"Do me a favor, just go to your heart instead of your brain. Like, just literally put your attention in your heart. What's the heart's answer? Now that was the brain. Get out of the brain. Heart. It's going to take its time. Don't force anything." -- Joe Hudson, What Do I Offer The World?

The instruction is small. Three parts: locate attention in heart, ask the heart, let it take its time. No interpretation, no analysis, no problem-solving. Just attention in heart, and listen.

## When Joe Uses This

The move comes out specifically when:

- The person is in the brain trying to answer a question that the brain cannot answer
- The person has just said "I'm having a hard time grasping at it" or "I can't quite get to it"
- The question is about nature, gifts, contribution, purpose, or what one wants — questions that require felt-sense access
- The mental search is producing the very stuckness it is trying to resolve
- The person has just answered with the mind and the answer felt thin or by-the-book

The move is not for every coaching moment. It is specifically for moments when intellectual grasping is the obstruction and felt-sense receiving is the answer. See [[opposite-of-trying]] for the broader family.

## How It Works

The brain is set up to search, analyze, and produce. For questions where the answer is already present in the body — what is my nature, what do I value, what am I drawn to — the brain's search-operation runs forever because there is nothing to find. The thing is already here, not somewhere to be located.

The heart-attention move bypasses the search. Attention in the heart is structurally different from attention in the head. The heart does not search. It registers what is already happening. The move shifts the operation from finding to receiving.

The literalness matters. Not "feel into your heart" (which can be done abstractly with the mind still running). Not "let your heart speak" (which sounds like permission rather than relocation). The instruction is: put your attention in your heart. The location of attention is what changes. The question stays the same. The answering organ changes.

## What Tends to Happen

The heart often takes a few seconds. The person may say nothing at first. The mind, restless, will often produce an answer — an analytical, by-the-book, clean answer. Name that this was the brain. Redirect.

When the heart actually answers, the answer is short, specific, and often surprising in its plainness:
- "It's very warm."
- "It's also chill."
- "It's loving."
- "It's spacious."

These are not analyses. These are reports from a different instrument. The person is often slightly surprised that this is the answer, because it does not match the framework-shaped answer they were trying to produce.

The body-state usually shifts during the move. The person slows down. The shoulders drop. The voice changes. The heart-attention is not just a mental relocation; it is a body-state shift.

## Variations

**For questions about gifts/contributions/uniqueness:** Use exactly as above. "What's the heart's answer?" The heart produces qualities (warm, loving, chill, motivating). These are the gifts.

**For questions about what one wants:** "Go to your heart. What does the heart want?" The mind's wants are often inherited; the heart's wants are usually more specific and more felt.

**For questions about whether to do something:** "Drop into your heart. Does the heart want this?" The mind can argue for and against forever; the heart often produces a clear felt-yes or felt-no.

**When the mind keeps interrupting:** Each interruption gets the same response. "That was the brain. Get out of the brain. Heart." No moralizing about the brain interrupting. The brain interrupts because that is what brains do. The redirect is the work.

## What Joe Does With the Answer

Whatever the heart produces gets received as the answer. No analysis of whether it is correct, comprehensive, or business-friendly. The heart said "warm, loving, chill" — those are the gifts. The next move is to confirm: "Yeah, that seems like at least two of your gifts."

The confirmation matters. The mind, hearing the heart's answer, will often dismiss it as too simple, too obvious, too unprofessional. The coach's job is to refuse the dismissal. The heart's answer is the answer. The dismissal is the old pattern reasserting.

From the confirmation, the conversation can move to:
- The structural problem of looking in the wrong place (the brain) for an answer that lives in another place (the heart)
- The gift-as-nature reframe: "what you're calling 'the gift,' I would just call his nature." See [[your-gift-is-your-nature-not-your-skill]].
- The grief or relief that comes with the recognition that the answer was always available
- The apology to self for the years of searching in the wrong place. See [[apologize-for-contorting-into-frameworks]].

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** I'm having a hard time grasping at it. And it—
- **Joe:** Does that mean you're having a hard time putting it into words?
- **Guest:** Yes. And I think something—
- **Joe:** Do me a favor, just go to your heart. Instead of your, your brain. Like, just literally put your attention in your heart. What's the heart's answer? Now that was the brain. Get out of the brain. Heart. It's going to take its time. Don't force anything.
- **Guest:** It's very warm. Yeah, it's very warm and loving.
- **Joe:** Yeah, that seems like at least two of your gifts.
- **Guest:** It's also chill.
- **Joe:** Yeah, yeah. So how many people do you think just in the greater Austin area need warm, loving, chill?
- *(Source: What Do I Offer The World?)*

## Tone

Direct, gentle, slightly playful. The instruction is delivered without weight — "do me a favor" makes it a small request, not a heavy intervention. The redirect when the mind interrupts is matter-of-fact, not corrective. "Now that was the brain. Get out of the brain. Heart." No criticism, no irritation. Just relocation.

The patience after the instruction is important. "It's going to take its time. Don't force anything." The coach has to hold the silence without filling it. The heart's answer arrives in its own time. The mind, trying to be helpful, will fill the silence with a brain-answer. The coach has to wait through that.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not let the move become a technique to produce a specific answer. The mind, hearing "the heart will say warm and loving," can produce those words without the body-shift. The body-shift is the work; the words are downstream.
- Do not analyze what the heart said. "What does 'warm' mean to you?" sends the person back to the mind. The heart's answer is received as-is.
- Do not skip the silence. The "it's going to take its time" is not filler. The mind needs the silence to lose interest enough that the heart can speak.
- Do not use this when the question genuinely requires analytical thinking. The move is for felt-sense questions, not for "should I take this job offer based on these specific terms."
