# Just Be Yourself and See What Happens (For Stay-or-Leave Questions)

## The Move

When a person is in the deciding-whether-to-leave moment -- a relationship, a job, any long-term arrangement -- the move is not to help them decide. The move is to redirect from "decide" to "be authentic and see what happens."

"My response to that is almost always the same, which is, 'Why don't you just be fully who you are? And if it ends, it ends. And if it doesn't, it doesn't.'" -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The move surfaces three things at once: the inauthenticity the person has been holding (which is what made the situation feel intolerable), the agency they actually have (which is to stop holding the inauthenticity), and the data they have been missing (which is what the situation looks like with them fully present in it).

## What Triggers the Move

The move is for the specific moment of stay-or-leave ambivalence about a long-term arrangement:

- "Should I break up with him or should I stay?"
- "Should I quit my job or should I stay?"
- "Should I leave this community or should I stay?"

The hallmark is the looping. The person has been turning the question over and cannot resolve it. Pro-and-con lists, advice from friends, therapy, none of it moves the needle. The looping is the symptom.

The looping is the symptom because the question itself is malformed. The conscious chooser cannot answer it from inside the configuration that produced it. The way out is to change the input by changing the person's authenticity in the situation, then read the new output.

## The Logic of the Move

Joe's reasoning:

"There's all these ways that you have stopped being yourself in the relationship. Why don't you just be yourself in the relationship? And if it becomes a dumpster fire, it's a dumpster fire. You're done anyways. And if it doesn't become a dumpster fire, and it's like some like 70, 80% of the time, the relationship heals just from that, just from people showing up and saying, this is how I want to be, you know, love it or leave it, but I'm not gonna defend myself. I'm just gonna be me. And I hope you stay, or I hope you don't, or whatever it is. Like, it doesn't matter. It's just like, my job is just being me here and not being defensive about it." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

Two outcomes, both useful:

**1. The arrangement cannot survive your authenticity.** Then the question of whether to leave is answered. You were already done -- you just had not given the relationship the chance to confirm it. The leaving happens cleanly, with the configuration that produced the stuckness clearly visible.

**2. The arrangement heals.** Joe estimates this at 70-80% in romantic relationships. The relationship was being held in dysfunction by your absence from it. Your return changes the dynamic. The partner, given a real person to engage with, often shows up differently.

In either case, the data has been gathered. The question that was unanswerable from inside the looping has been answered by the experiment.

## Why the Person Was Not Already Being Themselves

The person has usually been in some form of compromise -- the kind Joe warns against in [[dont-compromise-find-both-needs-met]]. The compromise produced the friction that produced the stay-or-leave question. The compromise was not chosen consciously; it was the cost of keeping the arrangement in its current shape.

The compromise is also often holding the agreement that the other person is responsible for the person's happiness (see [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]]). The compromise is what the person is doing in lieu of doing the work themselves.

The move asks the person to stop compromising and to stop demanding that the other person fix it. The person becomes responsible for their own authenticity, which is the only thing they can actually be responsible for.

## What "Be Yourself" Means in This Move

It does not mean: be reactive, be cruel, dump your accumulated grievances, perform a different version of yourself. The "be yourself" is the same authenticity that [[vulnerability]] points at: speak your truth, name what you want, do not defend, do not require the other person to change.

It includes:
- Stating what you want without dressing it up
- Stating what you don't want without dressing it up
- Doing the things you have been not doing because they would offend the configuration
- Stopping the things you have been doing because they were the configuration's price of admission
- Not requiring anyone else to like it
- Not requiring anyone else to change because of it

It does not include:
- Picking fights
- Performing a new identity
- Using "being yourself" as a weapon
- Refusing to listen to the other person
- Hardening into a stance

## The Companion Frame: Act As If You've Already Lost It

A related framing Joe uses: act as if you have already quit the job, already left the relationship. The act-as-if frees you from the calculus of preservation, which was the source of the inauthenticity. With nothing to preserve, you can be present. See [[what-if-you-acted-exactly-how-you-want]].

The two frames work together. "Act as if you've already lost it" addresses the fear that keeps the person inauthentic. "Just be yourself and see what happens" addresses what to do once that fear is no longer running the show.

## What the Move Is Not

It is not "stop trying to make your relationship work." It is the opposite -- it is the most honest attempt to make it work, by giving the relationship the chance to respond to the actual person.

It is not "be selfish." See [[am-i-being-selfish]]. The move usually produces more care, not less, because the person is no longer running on resentment.

It is not "just decide already." The move declines the framing that the decision can be made from inside the looping. The move is to step out of the looping by changing the input.

## When the Move Lands

The person often goes quiet. The relief is the relief of being told they do not have to decide -- they have to be present. The decision will make itself. Sometimes the person laughs. The terror of the decision was itself produced by the inauthenticity that the move asks them to drop.

When the person resists -- "but what if it doesn't work?" -- the resistance often points at the original fear that produced the inauthenticity in the first place. That fear is the next thing to work, often using [[fear-is-about-feeling]] or [[visualize-the-felt-aftermath]].

## When the Move Does Not Apply

Some stay-or-leave questions do not get answered this way. Abuse situations. Situations where the person's safety is at stake. Situations where the person genuinely does not know what authentic means yet because they have lost contact with themselves.

In these cases, the work is earlier. The move presumes that the person has access to their authentic self and has been suppressing it. If the person has not yet recovered access, the work is recovery first, experiment later.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The shock of realizing how much they have been holding back
- The relief of being told the decision is not theirs to make right now
- The fear of what will happen if they actually show up as themselves
- The unexpected ease, weeks later, of either the relationship reforming or the leaving being clean
- The discovery that the question that felt impossible was being held in place by the avoidance of being authentic

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person presents as stuck between staying and leaving
- A person is making lists of pros and cons that do not resolve
- A person has been compromising for years and is at the friction point
- A person asks "how do I know if this is the right relationship?"
- A person describes walking on eggshells
- A person presents [[parts-work-made-things-worse]] applied to a major life decision

## Tone

Plain, direct, slightly amused at the way the question dissolves. Joe is not gentle here. The move is delivered as the obvious thing to do, which usually surprises the person because the question had not seemed answerable. The matter-of-factness is what gives the move its power.
