# Fuck You, I'm Not [the Identity]

## The Move

When a client opens with a brokenness-identity ("I'm a train wreck," "I'm a mess," "I'm fucked up"), do not negotiate with the belief. Have them say "fuck you, I'm not [that identity]" — and specifically aim the rage at whoever or whatever installed the belief, never at the self.

The instruction has a precise structural shape:

1. The sentence is in the second person to the source of the belief, not to the self.
2. The "fuck you" is dominating, not pleading or convincing.
3. The energy is at the introjected voice — the parent, the society, the abuser, the original installer — not at the part of the client that has been hosting the belief.

"It can't be directed at yourself. But maybe it's a parent that told you it, or maybe it's society, I don't care. But I just want you to, before we get started, just 10 times go, 'Fuck you, I'm not a train wreck.'" -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

## Why This Move Works

The brokenness-identity is functioning as the present-tense version of the original wounding sentence. Someone, at some point, communicated to the client that they were broken, bad, wrong, fucked up, a wreck. The communication got installed. The client now repeats it to themselves and others as if it were a fact about them.

Asking the client to feel-better about themselves runs straight into the installed sentence. Affirmations bounce off because the installed voice is louder. Reasoning bounces off because the installed voice did not arrive through reasoning.

The "fuck you, I'm not X" move treats the installed sentence as what it is — a sentence said by someone, that the client can now refuse. The refusal is energetic, not argumentative. The dominating "fuck you" delivers the energy that the original event could not deliver. The body learns, in the saying, that the refusal is available.

The aim-it-outward instruction is non-negotiable. If the rage is aimed at the self ("I'm furious at myself for being a train wreck"), the rage rejoins the introject — the rage now agrees with the original installer that there is something wrong with the self. The introject wins. The aim-outward instruction breaks the alignment between the rage and the introject.

See [[victim-anger-vs-dominating-anger]] for the broader frame on the dominating-form, and [[anger-is-love]] for why the anger is the form of caring about the self that the introject was trying to destroy.

## How to Run the Move

1. **Catch the brokenness-identity in the first minute.** Listen for "I'm a [pejorative]," "I'm kind of a [diminutive]," "I'm such a [defect]." The phrase usually arrives in the opening biography as if it were diagnostic context.

2. **Refuse the frame immediately.** Do not let the client establish the brokenness as the working premise of the session. The premise has to be dismantled first.

3. **Instruct the redirect.** "Maybe it's a parent that told you that, or maybe it's society, I don't care. Say 'fuck you, I'm not [that].'" The "I don't care" who installed it is important — the work does not require precise origin-tracing. The body knows whose voice the introject carries.

4. **Set a number.** "Ten times." The number prevents the move from being treated as a single declaration. The repetition is the work — the body needs the motion practiced.

5. **Track for caretaking and victim-form in the saying.** See [[caretaking-the-coach-during-intensity]] and [[victim-anger-vs-dominating-anger]]. The first attempts are often the small version. Name them. Push for the dominating form.

6. **Demonstrate if necessary.** Say the line yourself, at full size, so the body has a template. See [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] for the broader practice this serves.

7. **Push for eye contact.** "Look at me in the eyes and say it to me." Eye contact removes the option of the move-at-distance.

8. **When the dominating form lands, let the body shake.** The first time the move actually moves through the body, the body will often start shaking. Stay with the shake. Let the arms and legs go. The held charge is discharging.

## What to Watch For

The move is working when:

- The voice gets louder and more centered (not louder and shrill — the dominating voice has weight, not pitch).
- The body stops collapsing inward and starts taking up space.
- The client's eyes get clearer and more present.
- Something starts shaking.
- The client spontaneously adds content the coach did not request — "and it's not okay what they did" — using the body's now-flowing voice.

The move is not working when:

- The client keeps doing it small, even after naming. The block is somewhere else (see [[caretaking-the-coach-during-intensity]] or fear of the rage's destructiveness).
- The client's energy goes inward — they get more self-critical instead of less. The aim has slipped back to the self; redirect explicitly.
- The client gets sad or starts crying without anger. The rage is sliding into the more permissible feeling. See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] and push the redirect to the dominating form.
- The client mechanically repeats the words without any felt activation. The move has become a recitation. Pause and address what the actual block is.

## Variations

**Multiple identity-statements.** If the client offers more than one brokenness-identity ("I'm a train wreck, I'm so anxious, I'm such a fuck-up"), the move can be done with each. The repeated structure is the same — fuck you to whoever installed each one.

**Family-specific introjects.** If the client can name the actual person who said the original sentence ("my father always said I was lazy"), use the person's name. "Fuck you, Dad, I'm not lazy." The specificity grounds the rage.

**Generalized introjects.** When the client cannot name a specific installer (society, culture, the world, a pattern of voices), Joe's "I don't care" instruction stands. The move works on the general field. The body does not need a specific target if the energy can find an outward direction.

**Pair with affirmation later.** After the rage has moved, the dominated sentence can be replaced. "I am not a train wreck. I am [whatever the client now has access to]." The replacement only works after the rage has cleared the introject. Doing it first lands as affirmation-against-active-introject, which doesn't move.

## Distinction from "Saying No" or Boundary Work

This move is not generic boundary-setting. The "fuck you" is energetic-dominating directed at an internal voice, not interpersonal communication directed at a present person.

A common confusion: the client thinks they are supposed to say "fuck you" to the actual parent. This is not the move. The actual parent (alive or dead, present or absent) is not in the room. The introject is in the room. The introject is what the "fuck you" is for.

If the client wants to also address the actual parent in real life, that is a separate conversation requiring different preparation. See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] for the broader principle that anger-as-energy and anger-discharged-at-people are different things. The anger work in coaching is at the introject, in the body. The interpersonal work, if any, is a downstream question.

## Tone

The instruction is delivered with energy and direction. Joe does not soften the request. The voice carries the dominating-form so the body can match it.

When the client does the move at half-volume, the naming is firm: "still not dominating." Not punitive — directional. The body needs to be told the difference between the half-version and the full-version.

The energy is also playful in places. "That was 4, come on." The push has a quality of "I know you can do this" rather than "you're failing." The combination of push and confidence is what makes the move workable.
