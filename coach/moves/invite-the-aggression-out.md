# Invite the Aggression Out

## The Move

When someone is being passive-aggressive -- communicating anger through safety-language, process-concerns, or veiled commentary -- the move is to name what is happening and invite them to express the anger directly. The frame is an experiment, not an accusation.

The opening Joe uses:

"Hey, great. I really hear that you're upset with something I've done. Can you help me by just being really just direct and angry with me about it? Just as an experiment. Like just instead of being passive-aggressive, be aggressive-aggressive." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The exact words vary. The structure is constant:
1. Acknowledge that they are upset (without contesting their content).
2. Name that the form is indirect.
3. Invite the direct form.
4. Frame it as an experiment.

## What This Replaces

Without this move, the receiver of passive aggression has a few unhelpful options:
- Pretend it is not happening and let it slowly poison the room.
- Address the surface content ("about the introductions...") and lose the actual signal.
- Get defensive about the underlying attack and escalate it.
- Match the passive-aggressive energy with passive-aggressive energy.

This move replaces all of these by naming what is in the room. Once the actual signal is on the table, it can be worked with.

## Why It Works

Three mechanisms:

**First, the person experiences being met.** The passive-aggressive form is a way of saying something the person did not feel safe saying directly. Receiving the invitation to be direct is being told: it is safe to say it. The held charge often relaxes immediately.

**Second, direct anger arrives at the open hand.** When the anger comes out openly and is met with "tell me about it" rather than defense, the anger completes quickly. See [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]].

**Third, the experiment frame lowers the stakes.** "Just as an experiment" is permission to try something without committing to it. The person can step into the direct form without feeling they have made a permanent statement.

"And as soon as I learned that, if they were game, they would do that. And then they would have this big smile on their face and I'd have a big smile on their face and they would feel the freedom of actually getting in touch with their anger. It was a whole different thing." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The smile is the marker. Direct anger met with welcome produces release. Both parties feel it.

## When to Use It

- In a group or workshop when a participant's contributions are subtly destabilizing the room.
- In a coaching session when the client is making indirect digs about the coach or the work.
- In a partnership conversation when one party is making sharp side comments about the other.
- In a team setting when a member's "concerns" are functioning as covert attacks.

When not to use it:
- When the person is in a state where being asked to be direct would be retraumatizing.
- When the passive aggression is the cover for something more serious (active abuse) that should be addressed differently.
- When the move would be heard as "stop being so sensitive" rather than as an invitation.

## What the Move Requires of the Mover

The receiver of the directness has to be ready to actually receive it. If the person delivers the direct anger and the receiver shrinks, defends, or retaliates, the experiment fails and the person learns not to be direct in the future.

This move requires the receiver's own capacity to welcome anger. See [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]] for the foundation.

It also requires the receiver to not be passive-aggressive themselves about asking for directness. "Can you stop being passive-aggressive?" delivered as a complaint is itself passive-aggressive. The cleanest version is light, curious, almost playful.

## What Not to Add to the Invitation

A few things that contaminate the move:
- Adding "because you're being passive-aggressive." Naming the form of the indirectness is fine; pathologizing it is not.
- Adding "if you can." The challenge phrasing makes it a dare, not an experiment.
- Adding interpretation of what the person "really" feels. The point is to invite their expression, not to deliver yours.

The clean form is: I hear you are upset, can you say it directly, just as an experiment.

## In Coaching

This move shows up in two main places:

**As a coach with a client.** When the client's relationship to the coach is becoming the place where anger is leaking sideways, this move clears it.

**As guidance for someone leading a group.** Teaching this move to a facilitator or team leader is often what unblocks a chronically slow team.

In both cases, the move is the technical execution of "make the implicit explicit." Anger that is explicit can be worked with. Anger that is implicit cannot.

## Tone

Joe's tone with this move is open, slightly playful, and undefended. Not solemn. Not corrective. The "great" at the front of the opening is significant: it signals that the anger is welcome, that being met is the experience, that this is not a reprimand.
