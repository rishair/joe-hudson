# Push Past the Protective Frame (When a Parent Underestimates Their Children)

## The Move

A parent describes their children as still needing what the parent has learned to give. The framing positions the parent as the developmental teacher and the children as the recipients of installation. The framing is often loving. It is also often inaccurate -- the children may already have what the parent thinks they are missing.

The move is to push, gently but firmly, past the protective framing. Not by contradicting the parent. By asking whether the children have already arrived at the orientation the parent is trying to instill.

The structure:

1. Hear the parent's description of their children's situation.
2. Notice the framing that positions the parent as the source of the development the children need.
3. Offer the inversion: what if they are already there in the relevant way?
4. When the parent deflects ("they're really driven," "they don't get it yet"), push once more.
5. Name the push explicitly: "I'm going to push here just a little bit."
6. Surface the possibility that the parent's project of teaching may be misplaced.

## Where This Move Lives in the Transcript

Joe makes this move with Aaron Taylor about Aaron's water polo-playing sons. Aaron has been describing his shift from the "making" orientation of his first half of life to the "allowing" orientation of his second half (see [[making-vs-allowing]]). The implicit project: teach this shift to his sons so they get there sooner.

Joe's move, in three beats:

**Beat 1 -- the inversion offered:**

"My guess is if I was to meet your sons they're already far more towards the way you're looking at life on the back nine than you were even like 6, 7 years ago. You know what I'm saying? I'm saying like, to some degree, your kids are already allowing it to come to them. They're doing the work, but their attitude, their perspective isn't like, I have to prove myself as much as it is, Oh yeah, I'm letting this come to me." -- Joe Hudson, Feel Your Way to Freedom

**Beat 2 -- the deflection met:**

Aaron deflects with "Some. I guess we could probably measure it by the amount of flicker when their eyes roll [...] They're pretty driven right now."

**Beat 3 -- the push:**

- **Joe:** Is that not true for you though? Because I don't see an unambitious, undriven man.
- **Aaron:** No.
- **Joe:** When I heard your approach on the back nine, I didn't hear that you lost your ambition.
- **Aaron:** It's diff -- it's, you know, you got to let go to take control. [...]
- **Joe:** That's my question. I'm going to push here just a little bit.
- **Aaron:** Yeah.
- **Joe:** Good. Isn't that what your kids already kind of get?

The third beat is the move. The pushback is explicit ("I'm going to push here just a little bit"), is named with care ("Good"), and arrives at the question that surfaces what the parent's protective framing was hiding: maybe the kids already have it.

## Why This Move Matters

The protective frame is a specific shape of love. The parent has hard-won wisdom and wants their children to have it without the suffering it cost the parent. The frame organizes around: I have something they need, and I need to install it before they suffer like I did. The motivation is real love.

The frame is often inaccurate. The children, raised by a parent who has done the work, often have the orientation by osmosis. They have not had to construct it through suffering because the environment has already taught it. The parent's framing -- "they don't yet get it" -- has more to do with the parent's identity as teacher than with the children's actual development.

The cost of leaving the protective frame intact is significant. The parent continues to install lessons the children do not need, which the children experience as not being seen. The parent's actual gold (which their *being* models without instruction) is obscured by the constant teaching. See [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] for the underlying principle.

The move surfaces all of this without requiring the parent to feel called out. It is held as a question: maybe they already have it? The parent gets to discover the answer rather than be told it.

## The Tone Required

This move requires specific tone calibration:

- Warm. The parent's protective framing is loving. The move does not contradict that.
- Curious. The push is delivered as wondering, not as correcting.
- Patient through deflection. The first deflection is normal. The protective frame is not given up easily.
- Explicit when escalating. Naming the push ("I'm going to push here just a little bit") is itself the warmth -- it removes the experience of being argued with.
- Light. The move can land harder if the parent does not feel the coach is invested in being right.

When the tone slips toward correcting ("you don't see your kids accurately"), the move fails. The parent defends harder. The frame becomes more rigid. The move requires the coach to be in [[wonder]] about whether the inversion is true, not certain that it is.

## What This Move Produces

When the move lands, several things often surface:

- The parent recognizes that they have been treating their children as the version of themselves at that age, not as the actual children.
- The protective frame loosens. The parent can see the children more accurately, often with surprise at what they already have.
- The parent's parenting project shifts from installation to witness. See [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]].
- The parent's own sense of value to their children reorganizes. They are valuable for being, not for teaching.
- Sometimes grief surfaces: the parent realizes how much energy they have spent installing what the children already had.

When the move does not land, what surfaces is also useful information: the protective frame is doing more work than first appeared, often defending against some grief or fear the parent has not been able to feel directly.

## The Generalized Form

The move generalizes beyond parenting. The same structure applies whenever someone is positioning themselves as the developmental source for someone who may already have what they are trying to install:

- A leader and their team.
- A coach and their client (this one is recursive -- the coach can be doing this with the client they are working with).
- A therapist and their patient.
- A mentor and their mentee.
- A spouse and their partner.

In each case, the move is the same: offer the inversion, push gently past the first deflection, name the push, and let the person discover whether the recipient already has what they were trying to give.

## When Not to Make This Move

- When the children (or recipient) genuinely do not have what the parent is describing. Pushing the inversion here is dishonest and damaging.
- When the parent is in acute crisis and the protective frame is providing stability they need.
- When the coaching relationship has not yet established the trust required for this kind of pushback.
- When the parent has named that they are worried about their children's actual safety, not just their development. The move addresses a developmental framing, not a safety concern.

The honest check: am I offering this inversion because I sense it is true, or because it is a coaching move I have? If the latter, hold it. If the former, the move is honest.

## What Joe Goes To After

After this move lands, Joe typically goes to one or more of:

- The parent's own developmental story at that age. (In Aaron's case: the mother's letting-him-decide moment at 15.)
- The principle of [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] in some specific form for this parent's situation.
- The parent's grief about how their own parent did or did not parent them, which is often what the protective frame was defending against.
- The shift from installation parenting to witness parenting, often with practical implications for the next conversation with the children.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A parent describes worrying that their children lack qualities the parent has worked hard to develop.
- A parent describes the frustration that their children are not listening to their wisdom, lessons, or TED Talks.
- A parent describes their children's "drivenness" or "obliviousness" in language that mirrors the parent's earlier self.
- A parent is in a developmental phase themselves (see [[making-vs-allowing]]) and is trying to teach the children the same arc.
- A parent is reaching for [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] in their own development but cannot extend it to their children.
