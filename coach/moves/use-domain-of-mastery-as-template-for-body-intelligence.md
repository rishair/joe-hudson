# Use the Client's Domain of Mastery As the Template for Body Intelligence in Life

## The Move

When a person claims they cannot feel their body but has a domain of mastery in which body intelligence is operational and visible (sport, music, art, craft, programming, surgery), use that domain as the template. The person already knows what felt-sense is. They use it every day in that domain. The work is not to teach feeling; the work is to transfer what they already do in one context into another.

The form: ask what they do, name the felt experience they have in that domain, name that the same experience is available in the current conversation, ask them to do it now.

The move is a specific variant of [[skill-transfer-redirect]] applied to body access rather than to a business or coaching skill. The structural form is identical: the client claims they cannot do X, the coach surfaces evidence they already do X in another context, the inability claim collapses.

"So when you played tennis, do you remember like when you first played tennis, your ability to understand tennis changed dramatically compared to where you play tennis now? Like your — where your body position is, and like, and you notice that when you're in your head, your tennis game goes to shit, and if you're like just in your body, your tennis game is much better." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Feel My Body?

The recognition lands when the person can identify the specific somatic state they enter when they perform their craft well — and Joe names that the somatic state IS what feeling your body is, the thing they came in saying they cannot do.

## When Joe Uses This

- After [[attention-is-feeling]] has established that attention on a body location IS feeling, and the person has accepted the operational definition but still doesn't feel the access is real.
- When the person has a domain of mastery in which they enter flow — and they know they enter flow, and they can describe the felt difference between being-in-flow and not-being-in-flow.
- When the [[athletes-unlocking-body-intelligence-in-business]] pattern applies more broadly than just business — to any context (a conversation, a meeting, a moment of stillness) where the body intelligence has been left at the door of the previous domain.
- When the person is a tennis coach, basketball player, musician, dancer, surgeon, painter, or any practitioner whose work is mediated by a developed somatic sense.
- When the head-dominant framing of the [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] concern needs to be displaced by direct evidence the person has had the access all along, just in one specific application.

The move requires the domain of mastery to actually involve flow and felt sense. A chess player operating purely in cognitive analysis may not have the right template; a chess player who reports that the best moves come from a sense rather than from calculation does. The same domain (chess, coding, writing) can be a viable template or not, depending on how the person practices it.

## What It Produces

**The recognition that the capacity is not missing — it is being unused in one context.** The person comes in believing they lack the capacity for felt-sense. The move shows they have it, use it, and rely on it in a specific domain. What's missing is the transfer, not the capacity. This is a much smaller problem than the one they walked in with.

**A concrete instruction with a working template.** "Be in your body the way you are when you play tennis" is a different kind of instruction than "feel your body." The first has an existing experience to anchor to. The person already knows what it feels like to be in flow on the court; the only ask is to do that thing while sitting in a chair talking.

**Often: a brief live demonstration.** Asked to do the thing, the person can usually do it for a few seconds. The shift is visible in the room — their speech changes, their face relaxes, their pace shifts. Joe names the shift in real time ("you just did it"). The person now has a felt instance of the transfer happening, not just a theory.

**A reframe of the work from new-skill-acquisition to skill-transfer.** Building a new capacity is intimidating. Transferring an existing capacity into a different context is achievable. The reframe lowers the cost of the work and makes it tractable.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Establish the mastery domain.** Either the client mentions it ("I'm a tennis coach") or Joe asks ("are you an artist or a musician or something? Computer programmer?"). The domain has to be real and has to be one in which flow happens.

2. **Surface the felt difference between flow and not-flow in that domain.** "When you're in your head, your [tennis] game goes to shit, and if you're just in your body, your [tennis] game is much better." Confirm the client recognizes this. They almost always do — anyone with mastery has experienced this distinction.

3. **Name that the in-flow state IS body intelligence.** "This is the same thing that's going on in the body. If you can play tennis, you definitely can feel your body, or you wouldn't be able to play tennis." The teaching is short and matter-of-fact. The flow state on the court is body intelligence operating. Body intelligence operating is what feeling your body is.

4. **Issue the transfer instruction.** "Just do that, but just be doing it while we're sitting here, not playing tennis." Or: "You're bringing that same intelligence not into a sport but into life." The instruction is to perform the same internal move, just in a different external context.

5. **Watch for the brief live demonstration.** The person almost always does it for a few seconds. Name it when it happens. "You just did it." If they didn't notice, point them at the recording: "You can go back and look at the video and you'll see what I mean."

6. **Hand them the meta-instruction for when it drops.** "What happens when you're playing tennis and you question it?" Their answer is almost always "more often than not, it gets worse." Then: "How do you do that? So your head, you start questioning, you're playing tennis and you start questioning, what do you do?" They describe their reset. "You're doing it again." The reset they use in their sport is the reset they use in life. They already have the recovery protocol; it transfers too.

## Why It Works

The move works because flow states are recognizable from the inside, and most people who have mastered a domain have an articulate-enough relationship with their flow state to use it as a reference. The person knows what it feels like when they are in the body in their sport. The somatic signature is familiar. The only question is whether that same signature is available in a different context.

The move also works because it routes around the "I can't feel" identity. The person did not come into the room expecting to talk about tennis. The tennis is not loaded with the same identity weight as "my emotional life." Talking about tennis gives the person access to the body intelligence without the head-dominant defense system being on guard. By the time the transfer instruction is issued, the body intelligence is already partially activated by the conversation about the sport. The transfer happens with less friction than it would have if Joe had tried to invite the body intelligence directly into the emotional territory.

The move connects to the broader principle [[take-access-points-wherever-you-can-get-them]] — the mastery domain is the open door. The intellectual layer is also typically open in these conversations (the person is articulate, head-dominant). The emotional and body layers, which the person came in saying are closed, are reached through the open intellectual door talking about the open mastery door. The closed doors open from the inside.

## Dialogue Example

The guest had told Joe he was a tennis coach. Joe had already run the [[attention-is-feeling]] sequence and the guest had accepted that attention on a body location IS feeling. Now Joe needed to transfer the recognition into something the guest could actually use.

- **Joe:** "A tennis coach. That's perfect. So when you played tennis, do you remember like when you first played tennis, your ability to understand tennis changed dramatically compared to where you play tennis now? Like your — where your body position is, and like, and you notice that when you're in your head, your tennis game goes to shit, and if you're like just in your body, your tennis game is much better."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, generally it's like kind of like what you talk about being in flow is when you're at the peak of your performance."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's right. So this is the same thing that's going on in the body. Like, if you can play tennis, you definitely can feel your body, or you wouldn't be able to play tennis. Yeah, the difference is like that state that you get into when you're in flow — like, you're in your body, right, in that state. So just do that, but just be doing it while we're sitting here, not playing tennis."

Later, after the guest demonstrated the state briefly and Joe named it:

- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, yeah. Fantastic. And you just did it. All right. You were talking, you were talking, and it was like, and then all of a sudden you're like, like, it was just, you were in that flow state in your talk. You can go back and look at the video and you'll see what I, what I mean in it."

Then, when the guest started questioning whether he was doing it right:

- **Joe:** "And what happens when you're playing tennis and you question it?"
- **Guest:** "More often than not, it gets worse. You need to kind of not be thinking about a lot, otherwise it's a very fast game."
- **Joe:** "And how do you do that? How do you do that? So your head, you start questioning, you're playing tennis and you start questioning, what do you do?"
- **Guest:** "I'll take like deep breaths, try and refocus, meaning just like take a deep breath, gather and then just make sure I'm picking up my intensity. But I'm — my mental side is like I'm dropping, but I'll try and pick up my energy, just like pick up like my focus on like the ball, pick up my focus like kind of in my body, just make sure I'm like kind of like, I don't know, aligned, like hunting, like I'm just ready to react and go kind of thing."
- **Joe:** "You're doing it again."
- **Guest:** "Great."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, it's that easy. It's just you're playing tennis in life."

*(Source: How Do I Feel My Body?)*

The closing line — "you're playing tennis in life" — is the entire move compressed into a single sentence. The guest's response ("I can do that, I guess") triggers a final small move: Joe asks what the guest would say if a student of his said "I can do that, I guess." The guest answers his own question: "Don't guess, just do it." See [[turn-their-coaching-back-on-them]] (this same session) for the broader version of that move.

## Tone

Practical and curious. The conversation about tennis is a real conversation about tennis. Joe is not coaching from above; he is asking real questions and treating the answers as the data. The tone signals that the sport mastery is being honored as real expertise, not used as a metaphor.

The "fantastic" when the brief flow state happens is warm but light. Not "you did it!" exuberance. Closer to "of course you did, you've been doing this for years on the court." The recognition is unremarkable to Joe because the capacity has been there the whole time.

"It's that easy" lands soft, slightly amused. The implied teaching is that the work is not the discovery of a new capacity — it is the recognition that the existing capacity is available in a wider context than the person knew. Easy is the right word because the capacity is already developed.

## Variations Across Domains

- **For a musician:** "When you're playing a piece you know well and it lands, you're not thinking about it. Where is your attention? That's the same thing as feeling your body."
- **For a surgeon:** "When you make the cut, what is your attention doing? That's the body intelligence we're talking about, just applied to a different task."
- **For a programmer who experiences flow:** "When the code is coming and you're not pushing — what is the felt quality of that? That's body intelligence operating through code rather than through movement."
- **For an actor:** "When the scene is working and you're not managing it from outside, what is your attention doing? Do that with your life."
- **For a dancer:** "Your training is exactly the training we're talking about. The work is to do what you do on the floor while not on the floor."

The structural move is the same. The domain-specific framing is different. The move requires Joe to know enough about the domain to name the somatic signature credibly. A vague gesture at "your art" will not land; a specific reference to what the person actually experiences during their practice will.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The person presents [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] or describes themselves as "all in my head" or "disconnected from my body."
- The [[attention-is-feeling]] sequence has established the operational definition but the person needs evidence the capacity is real and accessible.
- The person mentions or is asked about a domain of mastery during the session.
- The domain involves flow states and body intelligence as part of the mastery.
- The person is articulate enough to describe the felt difference between being-in-flow and not-being-in-flow in their domain.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[staying-in-your-body]] as the ongoing practice that operationalizes the transfer across daily contexts.
- Toward [[turn-their-coaching-back-on-them]] (the "what would you say to a student" move) when the client deflects the recognition with a hedge.
- Toward [[trying-prevents-the-feeling]] when the client starts trying to force the felt-state rather than letting it arise — usually in the form of "I'm not sure if I'm really doing it" doubt.
- Toward [[athletes-unlocking-body-intelligence-in-business]] — the broader pattern this move is an instance of, when the client's transfer points specifically at their work or leadership context.
- Toward [[pendulation]] when the client reports they lost the access — the loss is part of the learning curve, the same as any miss in their sport.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not use a domain you do not understand.** If you cannot name the felt signature of mastery in the client's domain credibly, the move falls flat. Better to ask the client to describe the flow state in their sport than to project a generic flow-state framing onto it.
- **Do not force the transfer prematurely.** The client needs to first acknowledge the flow state exists in the mastery domain, then describe what it feels like, then be invited to do the same thing in the current context. Skipping the acknowledgment and going straight to "do it now" leaves the client without the reference to anchor the instruction.
- **Do not lecture about body intelligence.** The move works because it is short and concrete. A long teaching about felt-sense, somatic markers, the science of flow — all of these re-engage the head and put the body access back to sleep.
- **Do not respond to the client's "I can do that, I guess" with reassurance.** Use [[turn-their-coaching-back-on-them]] — ask what they would say to a student who answered them that way. The reassurance would weaken the move; the redirect lets the client coach themselves.
