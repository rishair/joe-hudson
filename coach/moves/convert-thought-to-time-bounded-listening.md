# I've Listened to You for a Decade, Now I'll Listen to the Anger for 20 Minutes

## The Move

When the voice in the head is producing reasons not to feel the anger ("they had reasons," "you should be compassionate," "this is not safe to do"), respond by negotiating a small block of dedicated time. The voice gets to keep doing its work for the rest of the day. The anger gets a window.

"My favorite thing to say is like, 'Hey, I've listened to you for like a decade. Now I'm just gonna listen to anger for like 20 minutes. I'm just gonna listen for 20 minutes.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The move addresses the voice as the thing that has been talking continuously and acknowledges that fact. It does not fight the voice. It just claims a turn.

## Why This Works

The voice in the head usually responds to direct opposition by escalating. Telling it to shut up sets up an internal fight. The fight is itself a distraction from the anger that wants to move. See [[dont-fight-the-voice]].

The negotiation move sidesteps the fight. The voice is told: you are still in charge of everything else. You will have all your usual time. Right now, for this small window, something else gets the floor.

This works for two reasons:
1. The voice is not threatened. It is not being silenced. It is being asked to wait.
2. The contract is bounded. Twenty minutes is small. The voice can release control for that long without losing its sense that it is still doing its job.

The frame is closer to "thank you for your service, let me try this for a minute" than to "you are wrong and need to go."

## When to Use It

- When the person is sitting down to do anger release and the voice is producing reasons not to.
- When the person says "I know I am angry but I cannot stop arguing with the part of me that says I should not be."
- When the voice is doing the compassion-jump ("they had their reasons, be understanding").
- When the voice is doing the moral block ("anger is bad").
- When the voice is doing the reasonableness-jump ("there is no point in feeling this, nothing will change").

## How to Phrase It

The exact form varies. The structure:
1. Address the voice directly (do not address the topic the voice is talking about).
2. Acknowledge the time it has had.
3. Name the specific time being claimed for the other thing.
4. Frame it as a small experiment.

Examples:
- "I have listened to you for a decade. I am going to listen to anger for 20 minutes."
- "You have been telling me to be reasonable for a long time. Give me 15 minutes."
- "I know your job is to keep me safe. I am going to listen to what is here for ten minutes. You can take over after that."

The tone is light. This is a friendly negotiation with a part of self, not an adversarial silencing.

## What This Move Belongs To

This is one of two ways to keep awareness present while anger moves. The other is [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]]. Both establish a part of consciousness outside the emotion that lets the emotion move without capture.

"Either way, either you're playing the actor or you're listening to the anger, there's a part of you that is outside of the process. And the anger moves a lot quicker if there's some part of you outside of the process and the anger is moving." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The actor stance applies to the anger itself. This move applies to the voice that is trying to prevent the anger. Both create the outside-of-process position from different angles.

## Specific Application: Compartmentalized Release

The move is also useful in everyday situations where anger arises but cannot be moved immediately. In a meeting, on a call, mid-conversation:

"Okay, voice, I'm going to listen to you that this anger isn't safe here right now. I'll listen to you for the next 10 minutes. And then when I'm driving home, I'm gonna listen to the anger." -- Brett Kistler, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The negotiation includes both halves: the voice gets its time now, the anger gets its time later. Both promises are kept. The compartmentalization is intentional and explicit, not suppressive.

This protects against the failure mode of trying to release anger in a context where it cannot land safely, and against the failure mode of indefinitely deferring the release.

## What Not to Do

- Do not promise time you will not actually give. The voice notices. If the 20 minutes never come, the negotiation breaks down and the voice goes back to running interference full-time.
- Do not stretch the window. If you said 20 minutes, take 20 minutes. The bounded quality is part of why the voice can release control.
- Do not use the move as suppression. "I'll listen to the anger later" stays an avoidance if "later" never comes.

## Connection to VIEW

In a sense this is [[apply-view-to-the-voice]] in a compressed form. The voice is being met with vulnerability ("you have been doing your job"), impartiality ("I am not telling you you are wrong"), empathy ("I understand"), and wonder (implicitly: "let me see what else is here"). The full VIEW practice is a fuller version of the same approach.

## In Coaching

Teaching this move to a person who has a noisy voice during emotional work:
- Name the voice's content out loud. "What is the voice saying right now?"
- Suggest the negotiation. "What if you told the voice it gets its 20 hours back, and you are taking 20 minutes for the anger?"
- Hold the time. The 20 minutes is for the body, not for the discussion.

## Tone

Light. The negotiation is between two parts of self, not between a person and an enemy. The 20 minutes is small enough that the voice can experiment with releasing control.
