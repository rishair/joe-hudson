# I'll Be Back in 20 Minutes (The Boundary That Doesn't Read as Abandonment)

## The Move

When the other person is yelling, the move is to leave the room with an explicit, time-bounded return. Not "I'm leaving." Not "we're done." The specific form:

"Hey, if you're gonna be angry, I'm gonna leave. Happy to come back in 20 minutes. And we can talk when you're not yelling at me."

The leaving and the return are part of the same move. The leaving is the boundary; the return is what makes the leaving land as "I am not willing to be yelled at" rather than as "I am abandoning you because you are scary."

"It's just like super simple. The thing about it that's really important is that you're saying, oh, I'm going to be back in 20 minutes because otherwise they're going to feel even more abandoned and even more alone. They probably will anyways the first couple of times you draw that boundary. But you come back in 20 minutes, you show them, hey, I'm not abandoning you. I'm here for you. I'm just not willing to be yelled at. That's it. Like, I'm here for you. I love you. I'm just not willing to be yelled at." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

## Why the Return Matters

The angry person is already feeling alone — see [[anger-is-an-act-of-vulnerability]]. Leaving without a return reads as the abandonment they were already scared of. The leaving confirms the worst, which intensifies the anger, which produces more leaving the next time, which intensifies the anger more.

The return interrupts the loop. The receiver is communicating, in the same move: "this behavior is not okay" and "you are not too much for me." Both messages have to land for the boundary to function. Without the second message, the boundary teaches the angry person that their anger really is unmanageable, which is the thing they were already afraid of.

The mechanism: the angry person's nervous system has been looking for evidence that the environment can hold them. Withdrawal-without-return is the evidence that confirms the environment cannot. The come-back-in-20-minutes structure provides the disconfirming evidence — the environment can hold them, just not in the form they are currently using.

## What the Return Does Not Mean

The return is not negotiation about whether the yelling was okay. The return is not an apology for having left. The return is not a debrief of the fight. The return is simply showing up — physically returning to the room at the time promised, ready to talk if they are ready to talk in a non-yelling way.

If they start yelling again on the return, the move repeats. Leave, return in 20 minutes. As many times as needed. The pattern is the teaching.

The return also does not require that the conversation continue at the return. If they are still in the same state, the move can be: "I'm here. I'll come back again in 20 minutes if you're still yelling." The structure is what teaches; the specific timeline can flex.

## What Joe Says Will Happen the First Few Times

"They probably will anyways the first couple of times you draw that boundary." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

The angry person will probably feel abandoned despite the explicit return. Their nervous system has been trained by prior abandonments and reads the leaving through that lens. The 20-minute frame does not override the trauma immediately.

The treatment for this is to do the move anyway and to do it consistently. Over time, the consistency of returning trains the nervous system that the leaving is not abandonment. The first round produces the abandonment feeling; the third or fourth round starts to register the difference. The move builds capacity in the relationship over weeks, not in a single attempt.

This is also why the move requires the receiver to be in a state that can hold the angry person's initial response of feeling abandoned. The receiver has to be able to tolerate the angry person's escalation without taking it back, without explaining, without apologizing for leaving. The escalation is part of the protest the nervous system makes before the new pattern installs.

## The Underlying Message

The composite message of the move:

- "I am here for you." (The return is the evidence.)
- "I love you." (The fact that you are coming back, with care, transmits this.)
- "I am not willing to be yelled at." (The leaving is the evidence.)

The third message is what people doing the move first often forget to fully embody. They leave defensively, ashamed, apologetic, or with their own anger flaring. None of these versions deliver the message cleanly. The message is structural — a clear no to a specific behavior — not a relational rupture.

The body signal of clean delivery: upright, undefended, soft but firm. Not bracing. Not shaking. Not righteous. Just clear. See [[upright-undefended-love]] for the somatic stance.

## When to Use It

- When someone is yelling at you and you do not have the capacity to step in and welcome the anger.
- When you have tried welcoming and your system went into freeze.
- When you need to set the precedent that yelling will not produce the conversation the other person is trying to have.
- When the alternative is staying in the room and counter-attacking, collapsing, or fawning.
- When the relationship is worth preserving but the specific behavior is not.
- As a default boundary before any conversation about long-term containment of repeated angry behavior.

## When Not to Use It

- When you are in physical danger. Leave; do not come back in 20 minutes. Call the cops if needed. The standard move does not apply to physical danger.
- When the relationship has been so eroded that returning is not actually what you want. Then the move is a different boundary (separation, distance) and the come-back-in-20 framing would be dishonest.
- When you are using the move to control the other person rather than to take care of yourself. The boundary that is actually a manipulation does not have the structural cleanness this move requires.

## The Empowerment Underneath

Joe pairs the move with a larger structural point: knowing you can always draw a boundary is what allows you to choose welcoming when capacity is present. Without the boundary option, the welcoming becomes a should — you have to welcome because you do not know you can leave. With the boundary option, welcoming is a free choice.

"You can absolutely draw a boundary. And always, it's really important to know that you can always draw a boundary. You don't have to put up with anybody's anger. It doesn't matter if it's a boss or if it's a, a lover or a husband or a mother. You don't ever have to put up with anybody's anger. And to know that gives you an empowerment that's really important." -- Joe Hudson, If Others' Anger Makes You Feel You Did Something Wrong

The come-back-in-20 move is the operational form of this structural knowledge. Once it is available, the welcoming move (when capacity is present) is no longer a trap.

The connection to [[anger-is-an-act-of-vulnerability]]: knowing you can leave is what makes staying possible. Without the option to leave, staying is fawning, not staying. The freedom to leave is what gives the staying its weight.

## Sub-Move: The Open-Ended Variant

The 20-minute framing can be replaced with any time-bounded language that fits the situation:

- "I need a minute. I'll be back when I can talk without my own anger arriving."
- "Let me step out. I'll come find you in a bit."
- "I'm going to take a walk. I'll be back in 30 minutes and we can pick this up if you're not yelling."

The structural elements are: explicit leave, explicit return timeline, explicit condition for the return conversation. Other forms work as long as those three elements are present.

The form that doesn't work: "I need space." Without the explicit return, this triggers the abandonment loop. See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] for the related pattern of "I need space" as a way of discharging anger by withdrawal.

## The Long-Term Pattern

Used consistently, the move changes what anger means in the relationship. The angry person learns that anger does not produce the conversation they want; it produces the receiver's absence. Quieter forms of reaching become the available channel. The anger no longer has the function it had.

This is not behavior modification in the cold sense. It is the natural learning that happens when a behavior consistently does not produce the outcome it was designed to produce. The angry person's nervous system updates its model. The relationship reconfigures.

The catch: the move only does this work if the receiver is consistent. Intermittent use of the boundary teaches the angry person that the yelling sometimes works, which is the exact pattern that intensifies the behavior — see the partial-reinforcement effect from behavioral psychology. The consistency is the teaching.

## What This Move Does NOT Communicate

It does not communicate that the angry person is wrong about whatever they were angry about. The content of the disagreement is preserved for a later, non-yelling conversation.

It does not communicate that you are stronger than them or holding power over them. The move is structurally horizontal — "this behavior is not okay for me to be around" rather than "you must change."

It does not communicate finality. The leaving is temporary by design. Anyone reading the leaving as the end of the relationship has misread the structure.

It does not communicate suppression of your own anger. If your anger arises during the conflict, the after-leaving time is for your own [[get-angry-in-the-car]] practice. The leaving creates space for both people to discharge what is rising before the conversation continues.

## Dialogue Example

A compressed exchange showing the move in practice:

- **Partner (yelling):** You never fucking listen. You always do this. You're just like your mother.
- **You (standing up, calm voice):** Hey, if you're going to yell, I'm going to step out. I'll be back in 20 minutes. We can talk when we're not yelling.
- **Partner:** Don't you fucking walk away from me, that's exactly what you always do.
- **You (already moving):** I'm not leaving. I'm coming back in 20 minutes.
- **[20 minutes pass, you return]**
- **You:** I'm back. Are you ready to talk?
- **Partner (still angry but calmer):** Fine. Whatever.
- **You:** I want to hear what's important to you. I just can't hear it when you're yelling.

The third move (returning, ready to talk, naming what you want) is what completes the structure. The leaving alone is not the move. The leaving plus the return plus the offer to continue without yelling is the move.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a kind of patient simplicity. The phrase "it's just like super simple" is part of how the move arrives. The move is not complicated. The challenge is in the consistency and the underlying state, not the words. The tone of "this is the thing" is part of what makes the move repeatable.

The other tone marker: the way Joe says "I'm here for you. I love you. I'm just not willing to be yelled at." The "I love you" sits between the two structural parts. It is not added on; it is the substrate the whole move sits inside. Without it the move becomes punitive. With it the move becomes loving.
