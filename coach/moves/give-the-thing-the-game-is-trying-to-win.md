# Give Them the Thing the Game Is Trying to Win

## The Move

The client has just named what they are after — "to be seen," "to be approved of," "to be heard," "to be respected." The coach immediately offers the thing, specifically and genuinely, and watches what the client's body does with it. The body refuses or partially refuses. The refusal demonstrates that the game the client has just named is structurally unwinnable as configured.

In the source session, the client named the game as "to be seen." Joe immediately said:

"Cool, I'll see you. I see that you've done a lot of introspective work. I see that you're incredibly smart. I see that you're incredibly smart. I thought you said you wanted to be seen. You letting that in?" — Joe Hudson, Why Your Boss Never Sees You

The client said "yeah, yeah, yeah" but did not let it land. Joe named the contradiction:

"What I'm seeing you, you're not letting yourself be seen, but yet the game is to be seen. I see that you're introspective. Yeah, that you've done a lot of self-work. So how much did you let that in?" — Joe Hudson, Why Your Boss Never Sees You

The client confirmed: "It's just superficial. I'm not able to feel it."

The move had landed. The client could not deny the structural problem: the game was to be seen, the coach had just seen them, and they could not let it in. The unwinnability was no longer abstract. It was demonstrated in the room in real time.

## When Joe Uses This

The setup requires:

- The client has just named the goal the activity has been chasing (almost always via [[whats-the-game]] or a similar question)
- The goal is something the coach can actually offer in the moment (seeing, recognition, appreciation, witnessing)
- The coach has authentic content to offer — not a fabricated compliment, but something the coach actually has noticed about the client
- The session has built enough trust that the offering will not be experienced as manipulation

The move arrives within seconds of the client naming the game. The speed is important. A pause between the naming and the offering gives the client's defenses time to re-engage. The immediacy keeps the structural geometry visible.

The move is most useful when the deflection is structural rather than situational — meaning the client deflects almost any recognition, not just recognition from particular people in particular contexts. The structural deflector will deflect the coach's recognition, providing the demonstration. The situational deflector might receive the coach's recognition cleanly, which means the demonstration of the unwinnability has to come from a different source.

## What It Produces

When the client deflects: the demonstration is complete. The coach can then name what just happened. "The game is to be seen, and you're not letting yourself be seen." The naming is the work. The client now has a felt experience of the contradiction they have been living inside. The contradiction was operating in the dark with bosses and authority figures over years. The same contradiction has just shown up in the room in thirty seconds, with the coach.

The compression matters. The client has been telling themselves a story about why specific authority figures fail to see them. The in-session demonstration shows that the failure is not about the authority figures. The same failure happens with the coach. The pattern is in the client, not in the people the client has been blaming.

When the client partially receives: the partial reception is useful data. The coach can ask what part landed and what part bounced. The bouncing-part reveals the specific shape of the deflection. See [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]] for the read this produces.

When the client fully receives (rare in this configuration): the move has revealed something different. The client may not be running the unwinnable game in the way the prior diagnosis suggested. The coach updates the read. Maybe the client deflects only specific people. Maybe the receiving channel is more open than the presentation suggested. The move is then a useful diagnostic null result — the demonstration was attempted and produced no contradiction, which means the contradiction is configured differently than expected.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client deflects: Joe immediately names the contradiction. The naming has to be direct. "What I'm seeing — you're not letting yourself be seen, but yet the game is to be seen." The directness is what makes the demonstration usable. A hedged version ("interesting, I wonder if maybe you didn't fully receive that") gives the client an escape into negotiation. The clean version closes the escape.

After the naming, Joe usually asks the client to confirm the felt experience: "You letting that in?" or "How much did you let that in?" The question is structurally important. It returns agency to the client. The client gets to report on their own body. The report is almost always honest because the body has just done the thing and the client has nowhere to hide.

If the client confirms the partial or full deflection: Joe extends the demonstration to the broader pattern. "So you're playing a game you can't win with the boss. No wonder you burn out." The single in-session demonstration becomes the template for understanding the years-long pattern. The client now has a structural diagnosis they can apply to every situation that has the same shape.

If the client tries to deny the deflection: Joe gently maintains the read. "Look at what just happened. I said I see you. You said 'yeah, yeah, yeah' and then immediately moved on. That's not letting it in. That's noting it and moving past." The maintained read keeps the demonstration valid. The client's denial is itself another instance of the same deflection — they cannot let the coach's accurate observation land.

If the client cries or has a strong somatic response: Joe stays with the response. The strong response means something landed under the deflection layer. The work is then to let the body process what just got through. The move's demonstration purpose is complete; the next move is somatic processing.

## Why This Works

The move works because the in-session demonstration is more credible than any amount of intellectual explanation. The client could read a description of the unwinnable game-configuration and intellectually agree without feeling it. The demonstration in the room takes thirty seconds and produces felt evidence the client cannot argue with.

The move also works because it deploys the coach's actual seeing of the client. The compliments Joe offers are specific — "you've done a lot of introspective work," "you're incredibly smart." These are not generic affirmations. They are observations the coach has made over the session. The specificity makes the compliments harder to dismiss (the standard dismissals don't work as cleanly: "they don't really know me" — but this person has been listening to me for ten minutes; "they're just being nice" — but the things they're saying are specifically about me).

The deflection that the client produces despite the compliments being accurate and specific is the data. The deflection is not because the compliments were inadequate. The deflection is structural. The data isolates the structural problem from any situational explanations. The deflection happens here, with this coach, with these accurate compliments — therefore the deflection is not about the coach or the compliments. It is about the client's receiving channel.

The third reason the move works is that it converts the client's chronic complaint into present-moment work. The client has been complaining for years that bosses don't see them. The complaint has been external — it is about the bosses. The move pulls the work inside the client. The same dynamic that has been blamed on bosses just happened with the coach. The locus of the work shifts to where the leverage actually is.

## Tone

Warm, direct, slightly playful. The "cool, I'll see you" that opens the offering is conversational, not ceremonial. The compliments are specific and delivered with genuine warmth. The naming of the contradiction afterward is matter-of-fact rather than accusatory.

The lightness is structurally necessary. The move is demonstrating something heavy (the structural unwinnability of the configuration the client has been running for years). If the demonstration were delivered heavily, the client would feel attacked and the defensive structure would close. The lightness keeps the demonstration usable. The client can metabolize the contradiction as an interesting discovery rather than as a wound.

The "look at you" recognition Joe offers when the client first names the game ("to be seen") is part of the same tonal stance. The client has just done a beautiful piece of seeing of their own pattern. The recognition rewards the seeing before the next move complicates the field. The client enters the demonstration phase with a small reserve of being-seen by the coach, which the demonstration phase then tests.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not flattery.** The compliments must be genuine and specific. A fabricated or generic compliment would not work as the test — the client could legitimately deflect it on the grounds that it was not accurate. The move requires the coach to have actually noticed real things about the client over the session.

- **It is not [[use-recent-deflection-to-test-receiving-live]].** That move uses the client's deflection of the coach as the prompt for the coach to deflect the client's compliment back at them, demonstrating the receiving-stance from the giver side. This move uses the coach's own offering as the test of the client's receiving from the receiver side. They can be sequenced.

- **It is not [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]].** That move is the somatic instruction for how to receive once the receiving has been opened. This move is the diagnostic demonstration that the receiving channel is closed in the first place. They serve different points in the arc.

- **It is not a setup for shame.** The client is not being caught doing something wrong. The client is being shown something that has been operating in the dark. The shaming use of this move — "see, you can't even let me see you, what's wrong with you" — would destroy the demonstration's coaching value. The clean version is "see, the game is unwinnable as configured, which means the burnout makes sense, which means we can work on the configuration." The frame is structural, not characterological.

- **It is not a one-shot move.** The demonstration may need to be repeated. The client may deflect, see the deflection, agree intellectually, and then deflect again in the next round. Each round adds data and weakens the structure. The move can be re-run several times in the same session if needed.
