# Apologize to Exit a Fight

## The Move

When you are in a fight, actively scan for anything you can honestly apologize for in an upright way. Find it, name it, deliver it. The fight often dissolves because you have stopped being in the power struggle on your end of it.

"If I'm in a fight with somebody, I will stop and look for any way that I can apologize that's in integrity, that's empowered. I'll be like, okay, what can I, I'm in the fight, it means I'm responsible somehow. Okay, what is it that I can apologize for that feels empowered? And I will apologize for it. So if I have that thought, man, immediately I'll do it, and it feels great, and it often just changes the entire conversation." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## The Searching Stance

The starting premise is structural: if you are in the fight, you are responsible somehow. This is not a confession that you started it or that you are wrong. It is a recognition that fights are co-created. See [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]].

From that stance, scan for something specific you can own. Not the abstract "I'm sorry we're fighting" but the concrete:
- A raised voice
- A particular sentence you wish you hadn't said
- A move into a power struggle
- A tone you wouldn't use with someone you respected
- A controlling impulse you acted on
- A moment you stopped listening

The standard is integrity. If you cannot find something to honestly apologize for, do not invent one. But the scan almost always finds something, because two-person conflicts almost always involve mutual contribution.

## Example Phrasings

"I'm sorry. I don't want to be talking to you in this way."

"I'm sorry for being in a power struggle with you. That's not how I want to be with you."

"I'm sorry for raising my voice. That's not how I want to be with you. And I know you deserve better than that." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

Each phrasing follows the [[upright-apology]] form: name what you did, state that it is not how you want to be, and stop. No "you" required. See [[dont-use-you-in-an-apology]].

## Why It Changes the Fight

The fight depends on both people being in defensive postures. When one person steps out of defense and into responsibility, the structure of the fight collapses. The other person can either continue alone, which feels strange, or step out with you.

"It often just changes the entire conversation." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The mechanism is also informational. Most fights are surrogates for not feeling seen. See [[apologies-as-power-struggle-surrogate]]. An upright apology gives the other person a small experience of being seen. This often dissolves the underlying drive of the fight.

## When To Use It

- Mid-fight, when you notice the dynamic and want out.
- Just after a fight has ended, before the resentment hardens.
- When you notice your own controlling, raised voice, or sharp tone.
- When the fight is recurring and you want to break the loop.
- When the other person has made themselves more defensive and you want to soften the field.

## When Not To Use It

- When the apology would not be honest. If you cannot find something you actually want to apologize for, do not perform one. The other person will feel the strategic quality and the fight will deepen.
- When the apology is being used to short-circuit a real grievance you have. The move is not a way to skip past what you also need to say. After the apology, you can still raise what you need to raise.
- When you are still flooded. The apology delivered while still in fight-flight often comes out with edge. Take a breath first. Sometimes the apology should be delivered later, not now. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]].

## What Joe Looks For To Know It Landed

- The other person's body softens.
- The conversation moves to a different topic or a different layer of the topic.
- Your own body relaxes. The apology that lands clears the apologizer too.
- New information becomes available. Things both people were holding back become sayable.

## Tone

Steady, warm, slightly relieved when you find what you can apologize for. Not desperate. Not self-flagellating. The body cue is the [[upright-apology]] body: back upright, eyes up. If the apology is coming out hunched, it has become shame-apology and will not change the fight. See [[sorry-from-shame-vs-sorry-as-solidarity]].
