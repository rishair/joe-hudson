# Strip Away the Externals (The Dark Cave / 30-Year Safety Variations)

## What This Move Is

A staged thought experiment that removes external referents one at a time so the person can see what is actually being scored, defended, or chased underneath. It comes in several variations Joe uses interchangeably:

- **The dark cave:** "Imagine you're in a cave forever. You have a mask on. You can't see anything. The weather, the temperature is the same. There's no outside interaction. All your needs are met. You're in a 5-star hotel comfortable bed, but no interaction. What's the scorecard?"
- **The 30-year safety guarantee:** "Let's just assume that for 30 years, you're physically safe. We're all going to die, but for 30 years, you're guaranteed safe. What's left to be scared of?"
- **The complete absence of the scorecard:** "Let's say you actually find out with 100% certainty that there is no scorecard. There's no way of describing your worth accurately."

Each variation removes a different external referent (social field, survival threat, measurement system) to surface what the person was using the referent to manage.

## When Joe Uses It

After [[what-if-there-was-no-scorecard]] has been asked and the person has tried to escape into intellect or into a new scorecard. The move closes the escape route by making the absence concrete.

The triggering signals:
- The person has said "I'd be lost without the scorecard" but is still in their head, treating it as an abstract question.
- The person has agreed the scorecard isn't working but immediately offered "well, I just need a better one."
- The person says "I'm worried about losing the money / status / recognition" and Joe wants to surface what specifically the body thinks it's losing.
- The person is at the edge of the felt-absence but still has a way to pretend it isn't real.

The move is also useful as the first major destabilization in a session where the person has organized their life around a scorecard structure and needs to feel the absence before any work on the substrate can happen.

## What It Produces

The body usually answers something the intellect would not have. With K, the sequence ran:

- Cave variation produces: "Survival. What's lurking behind the corner."
- Joe then closes that escape: "Now you have all your needs met, 5-star hotel, what's the scorecard?" producing: "What's around the corner. What's lurking."
- Joe then asks specifically what is trying to survive, producing: "My physical body."
- Joe then closes that escape with the 30-year safety guarantee: "Assume you're physically safe for 30 years. What's left to be scared of?" producing: "Loneliness."

The sequence is the move. Each variation strips an external. The person tries to put a new external in the empty space. Joe closes that escape. Eventually the person arrives at the felt thing that the scorecard was actually managing.

That felt thing is usually:
- Loneliness
- Worthlessness without external proof
- The sense of not existing without external recognition
- The fear of being seen as nothing
- The dissolution of identity

Once the felt thing is named, the work can move to it directly. The scorecard was a workaround for this thing. The thing itself is the material.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person names a new scorecard inside the thought experiment:** Joe closes the escape. He adds a new constraint that makes the new scorecard irrelevant. "Okay, you have everything you need. No one can see you. There's no one to compare yourself to. What's the scorecard?" The constraint-adding continues until the scorecard runs out of substrate.

**If the person says "but the cave isn't realistic":** Joe agrees and uses the constraint anyway. "Right, this is just hypothetical. What's the scorecard?" The unrealism is the feature. The thought experiment is doing the work of revealing what is operating, not of describing a real situation.

**If the person finds the actual fear (loneliness, dissolution, worthlessness):** Joe shifts modes. The destabilization is done. Now the move is to be with the felt fear. He may use [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]] or pivot to [[whats-the-scorecard-that-cant-be-taken-away]] to invite an internal substrate.

**If the person tries to solve the loneliness or fear from inside the scorecard system:** "If I had enough money / likes / recognition, would I feel less alone?" Joe usually asks what the connection is between the scorecard and the actual thing. "If you're enough, if you're validated, if you have enough checkmarks or likes, dollars in the bank account, women, friends, whatever, how does that help you deal with what's around the corner?" The question makes visible that the scorecard never could have helped with the actual fear.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the K session, showing the staged strip:

- **Joe:** "So I think there's this thing that they do now called like a darkness thing. You basically go into — you put a mask on and then you get put into a cave and you're just in the cave. It's pretty luxurious, but like you're with a mask in a dark cave. You can't see anything for like days on end. And I'm just going to ask you to imagine that that is your life forever."
- **K:** "And, and you can't see, right?"
- **Joe:** "You can't see. It's all dark. The weather, the temperature is the same. There's no outside interaction. All that's happening is what's happening inside of you. You have all your needs met. What's the scorecard?"
- **K:** "Survival."
- **Joe:** "Now you got food, comfortable bed, 5-star hotel. It's just, it's no interaction."
- **K:** "Like, uh, like there's, you know, like literally what's around the corner, because what's lurking behind the corner."
- **Joe:** "All right, that's great. So if you're enough, what does that do to change your capacity to what's lurking behind the corner? If you're enough, if you're validated, if you have enough checkmarks or likes, dollars in the bank account, women, friends, whatever, how does that help you deal with what's around the corner?"
- **K:** "It doesn't seem like it matters. Whatever threat is lurking around the corner, no one cares about your SAT score, or your business card. So it doesn't matter."
- **Joe:** "What specifically is trying to survive?"
- **K:** "I think my physical body."
- **Joe:** "If you knew you were physically being safe, what would then — if you had 100% transparency and clarity that you were going to be physically safe for the next 30 years..."
- **K:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "What's left to be scared of? In this dark room?"
- **K:** "Uh, loneliness."
- *(Source: What's Your Scorecard? — the full strip sequence from cave to safety guarantee to loneliness.)*

The work the move did: K started in "money is my scorecard," went through "survival is the scorecard," landed at "loneliness." The scorecard had been managing the loneliness. The recognition is the entry point for the rest of the session.

## Tone

Light, slightly playful, almost like Joe is proposing a fun game. The thought experiment is not delivered as heavy or destabilizing. The lightness is what makes the body let in the constraints. If the move were delivered with weight, the person would brace; with lightness, the person lets the constraints actually operate.

When the person tries to escape, Joe is friendly but not rescuing. The escape gets closed with another constraint, not with a lecture. "Right, you've got food too." "No, but assume you're safe." The constraints accumulate without making the person feel cornered.

When the actual fear arrives (loneliness, dissolution), the tone shifts to steady. The play is over; the felt thing is here. Joe meets it with presence, not with the next move.
