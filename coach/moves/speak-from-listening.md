# Speak From Listening

## The Move

Continue listening — to yourself, to the other person, to the silence around the words — while you speak. The speech arises from inside the listening rather than from planning. The speaker discovers what they are saying as they say it. Listening and speaking are not alternated; they happen at the same time, in the same body, from the same receive.

"When I'm listening and talking, when I'm in that deep form of listening and I'm talking at the same time, I'm very curious about what the fuck's going to come out of my mouth. It's like I'm experiencing it for the first time. It's an unfolding that delights me." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The move is the operational form of [[state-not-framework]] in speech. The framework-based speaker has the words planned and is executing them. The state-based speaker is in the listening and letting the words arise. The result is different in kind, not just in polish.

## How To Do It

1. **Establish the listening first.** Use [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] or [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]] to bring the attention into the body before speaking.
2. **Start speaking without knowing the full sentence.** Let the first word arrive. Let the next word arrive after it. Do not pre-formulate the sentence.
3. **Keep listening as the words come out.** Hear your own speech the way the listener hears it. Notice the response of your body and the listener's body as the speech unfolds.
4. **Let the speech surprise you.** When the next sentence is not what you expected, follow it. The surprise is the signal that the speech is arising from the listening rather than from planning.
5. **Trust the butler.** What needs to arrive will arrive. See [[the-butler-metaphor]] and [[faithing-faith-as-verb]].

The pause is part of the move. If the next word is not yet present, wait for it. Most people cannot pause; the pause exposes the very things the speech was being used to avoid. See [[listening]] for the development of pause capacity.

## Why It Works

The planned-speech speaker is split across two activities — generating the speech and monitoring its reception. Neither half is fully in the present. The body is bracing for the audience response while the mouth is delivering a memorized sequence. The result is speech that the listener registers as managed, performed, or pressured.

The speak-from-listening speaker is doing one activity: receiving. Speech is part of what is being received and offered back. The body is in the present. The next word arrives from the present, not from the script. The listener registers this as alive, responsive, in-contact.

The mechanism is the same as the mechanism behind [[cant-remember-what-i-said-in-flow]]. When the speech arises from the listening, the speaker is not constructing it from memory and is not storing it in memory. The speech happens and then is gone. The speaker can remember the relationship more than the content. This is the signature of channeled speech, in any context.

## What This Replaces

Most speakers default to one of two modes: either *speak from the script* (memorized, rehearsed, executed) or *speak from anxiety* (improvising under pressure, scrambling to fill the air, monitoring the audience for signs of failure). Neither is speaking from listening. Both produce the signature unpleasantness most people associate with speaking: the bracing, the depletion, the post-conversation collapse.

Speaking from listening replaces both. The speech is not memorized. The speech is also not anxious. It is the unfolding of the listening into spoken form. The speaker enjoys the speaking. The audience receives the aliveness. Energy goes up rather than down.

This is what [[opposite-of-trying]] looks like when there is sound coming out of the speaker's mouth. The trying has dropped. The receiving has taken over. The speaking is happening anyway, but it is no longer the effortful production it was.

## What You Will Notice

- The next word arrives without construction. There is no sense of having to find it.
- The pace varies naturally. Sometimes the words come quickly; sometimes a pause stretches out. The variation is not stylistic — it is the body responding to what is actually here.
- The speech includes things the speaker did not know they were going to say. Insights arise mid-sentence. Connections appear that were not pre-planned.
- The body softens during the speech rather than tightening. The breath remains available.
- After the speech, the details are hard to recall. The relationship with the listener feels closer than the content would predict.
- The audience or listener responds differently. They are more attentive, more present, more willing to engage. The reciprocity of the listening shows up in their listening back.

## When To Use It

- In any high-stakes speech, conversation, or coaching session where the planned version is not landing.
- During a difficult conversation when the rehearsed approach has failed.
- In coaching, when the impulse to deliver a teaching is pulling the coach out of the room.
- When the speaker notices themselves leaving their body to monitor reception.
- In facilitation, when the next move needs to arise from what is actually happening in the room rather than from the agenda.
- In public speaking, after sufficient preparation has been done — the preparation builds the substrate; the speaking arises from the substrate.

The move is not appropriate for genuinely scripted contexts (reading a prepared statement, delivering exact legal language). It is appropriate everywhere else.

## What This Is Not

This is not unprepared speaking. The speaker who can speak from listening has usually done extensive preparation. The essence is held. The bookmarks are known. The preparation is in the body. From the body, the listening can do the rest. See [[chain-vs-tree-thinking-for-content]] for the preparation form.

This is not free-association. The speech is not random. It is responsive — to the listener, to the room, to what the body knows is wanted. The constraint is the listening itself. The listening keeps the speech on the relevant signal even though the words are not pre-determined.

This is not a performance technique. The speaker who is trying to *look like* they are speaking from listening is still planning, just at a more sophisticated level. The move requires actually being in the receive. The audience can feel the difference.

This is not bypass for poor speakers. The development takes time. Most speakers need months or years of practice to sustain listening while speaking for any length of time. The reward is worth it; the practice is not optional.

## How To Develop It

The capacity builds gradually:

- **Start with listening alone.** Develop [[listening]] as a practice. Spend time in conversations where you are mainly listening. Get used to the felt sense of receiving.
- **Add the 10% return while speaking.** Use [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] in short sentences first. Notice the shift.
- **Increase the listening as the speaking continues.** Most speakers can sustain the listening for one or two sentences before the planner reasserts. Practice extending the duration.
- **Use the pause as the anchor.** When the listening collapses, pause. Re-establish the listening. Resume. The pause is not failure — it is the move that lets the listening return.
- **Welcome the surprise.** When the next word is not what you expected, do not snap back to the planned version. Follow the surprise. The surprise is the signal that the receive is operating.

## Tone

The move is delivered with curiosity, not with effort. The speaker is interested in what is going to arrive. The interest is what keeps the listening open. The opposite tone — determination, performance, drive — collapses the receive.

When Joe coaches from this state, the tone is responsive moment-to-moment rather than consistent across a session. The room shapes the voice. The voice arises from what is happening, not from what was decided beforehand. The coachee can feel the difference; they are being met, not delivered to.
