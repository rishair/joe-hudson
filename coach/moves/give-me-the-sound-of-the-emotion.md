# Give Me the Sound of the Emotion

## The Move

The person cannot name what they are feeling. The request to name an emotion produces nothing — sometimes a freeze, sometimes a blank, sometimes a sequence of words that describes the situation rather than the felt experience. Naming has hit the wall.

Joe abandons the naming and asks for the sound instead.

"What's the sound? Any sound is fine. It just — whatever you — when you think about your life, what's the sound you want to make?" — Joe Hudson, I Can't Feel Anything

The request is structurally different from the naming request. A name requires the cognitive layer to identify and label a felt state. A sound requires only that the body produce the noise that wants to come out. The sound does not have to be a word. It does not have to mean anything. It just has to be the sound that wants out.

The move bypasses the part of the system that has been trained not to access emotions. The sound goes around the cognitive checkpoint that has been blocking the access.

## When Joe Uses It

This move comes when:

- The person has reported they cannot feel or cannot name what they are feeling
- The cognitive request ("name it," "what is it," "what's the emotion") has produced freeze, blank, or word-salad
- There is a felt signal in the body (Joe can see it: a smile, a tension, water in the eyes) that the person cannot consciously access
- The person is in [[freeze-response]] or has gone [[in-the-blank-place]] and naming would require them to come out before they have the resource to do so
- A history of compartmentalization (childhood loss, emotional shutdown, alexithymia) means the naming wires are not yet built

The move is especially useful with people whose access to emotions was severed in early childhood. The cognitive labeling apparatus was never built or has been offline for decades. The sound apparatus is older — it is the channel a preverbal child uses. Asking for the sound is asking for the system that was the original channel, before the cognitive overlay went in.

It is distinct from [[whats-the-felt-sense-in-the-body]] which asks for sensation location. The sound move asks for expression. The felt-sense question is intake; the sound move is output. For a person stuck at intake — who can locate pressure in the chest but not name what it is and not let it move — the sound is the bridge from sensation to release.

## What It Produces

Several characteristic responses:

1. **More blank.** The person still cannot access the sound. This is data — the system has gone deep into freeze and the sound is also offline. The next move is to amplify the blank itself (see [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]] applied to the blank state).

2. **A description that isn't a sound.** The person reports "I think it's a lot" or "it feels like I just kept myself as hidden as possible." The cognitive layer has translated the sound request back into description. Joe pulls them back: "Ah, like, just what's the sound?"

3. **The sound arrives.** Sometimes a sigh, a small whimper, a guttural noise. When it arrives, Joe stays with it. The sound is the emotion beginning to move through the body.

4. **The recognition arrives via a different channel.** A common pattern: the person cannot make the sound but as they try, they suddenly become able to name what they would express. "I feel like I probably want to cry a lot right now." The sound request lowered the bar enough that the actual answer surfaced — but in words. The bypass freed the wire.

The fourth pattern is particularly important. The move does not require the person to actually make a sound for the move to work. The act of being asked for something more primitive than a name often allows the name to arrive. The cognitive system relaxes its checkpoint when it is not the part being asked.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session, where Joe asks repeatedly for the sound across an extended exchange:

- **Joe:** "I'm not going to ask you to feel it, I'm just going to ask you to name it. What's the — Let's try something different. Instead of naming it, give me the sound of the emotion that you aren't allowed to feel around the history of your life and mom's death and the father situation and all of that. What's the sound?"
- **Person:** "I think it's a lot. I think it's everything almost, like, um. It feels like I just kept myself as hidden as possible."
- **Joe:** "Ah, like, just what's the sound? Any sound is fine. It just — whatever you — when you think about your life, what's the sound you want to make?"
- *(Person freezes; reports feet and hands getting cold, body blank)*
- *(Joe shifts to amplify the blank: "go even more blank, super blank")*
- *(Later, after the body work)*
- **Joe:** "So what's the sound that you want, that you would make, just a little sound that you would make from the — if you were looking at your life?"
- **Person:** "I feel like I probably want to cry a lot right now."

*(Source: I Can't Feel Anything (Coaching Session With Master Coach))*

The fourth attempt at asking for the sound produced the recognition that the emotion is grief in the form of crying. The sound was never produced directly. The repeated asking dropped the cognitive defenses far enough that the underlying answer surfaced as a sentence.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the sound arrives: Joe stays with it. Lets the body make more of it. Does not interpret it. The sound is the emotion moving; words would interrupt.

If the description arrives instead: Joe pulls back to the sound request, gently. The person's first impulse will be to describe; the work is to keep asking for the sound without making the person feel they got it wrong.

If the answer arrives as a word ("I want to cry"): Joe takes the word. The bypass worked. The next move is to ask what the fear is around the word ("what would make you not cry? what does your mind tell you?"). See the source for Joe's follow-up: "And what's the fear that if you cry a lot, what would be the fear that — what would make you — what does your mind tell you as the reason not to do that?"

If the answer is still blank: Joe stays with the blank as the felt state. See [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] for the read and [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]] for the move that addresses the blank itself.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not asking the person to vocalize an emotion they don't feel. The move is not theatrical. Joe is not asking them to perform sadness or anger. He is asking what sound would come out if there were no cognitive interference. The sound is whatever is actually there in the body, no more.

It is also not the same as the [[four-tactical-ways-to-feel-your-emotions]] expression step. That step assumes the person knows what they are feeling and the move is to let it move through the body. This move is for the earlier stage: the person does not know what they are feeling and even the naming has failed.

It is also not catharsis-seeking. Joe is not pushing for a release. He is offering a different door. The person may make a small sound, may make a big sound, may not make a sound at all. The diagnostic is whether the channel opens, not whether the sound is loud.

## Tone

Warm, casual, almost throwaway. "Let's try something different." The move is presented as an experiment, not as a serious clinical intervention. The lightness is structural — if the person feels they are about to be asked to perform their grief, the asked-for sound will not come. The casualness lowers the stakes so the body can answer.

When the person describes instead of sounds, Joe's correction is gentle: "Ah, like, just what's the sound?" No frustration, no insistence. Another invitation in the same key.

When the answer finally arrives — even if it is in words — Joe takes it without commentary. The move is not the point. The opening was the point.
