# Ask the Witnesses

## The Move

When the person is making a claim about themselves that is hard to evaluate from inside their own perspective, ask what the people who have actually watched them would say. The witnesses are not in the room. They are summoned through imagination.

Variations:

- "If we were to ask any [partner / friend / colleague / family member] in your life over the last 20 years, what would they say your relationship to [X] was?"
- "If you had to guess, maybe they even said something to you at some point."
- "What's the data from the people who have actually been around you?"

The move surfaces evidence the person already has but has been ignoring because it contradicts the self-story.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the person makes a self-assessment that does not match their actions or how others have actually responded to them.
- When the self-story is dominant ("I'm a flake," "I'm not committed," "I can't be relied on") and is overriding observable history.
- When the person describes an internal experience but cannot see the external evidence.
- Particularly useful when the person already has a small admission they have not fully absorbed (e.g., "people have actually said I'm a deeply committed partner").

It is not used to argue the person out of their feeling. It is used to surface data the person can then sit with.

## Why It Works

People often have a self-story that is decades out of date. The witness question makes the actual data available. The data is not usually news — the person has heard it before — but the story has been louder than the data. Asking for the witness version puts the data back in the room.

It is also a form of [[mirror-the-judgment]] applied to the person's judgment of themselves. The person who believes "I am unreliable" has often been told by many people that they are reliable. Surfacing the contradiction lets the person notice they have been selecting against their own evidence.

The move works best when the question is specific. "What would your colleagues say?" is too general. "If we asked your last three partners how committed you were, what would they say?" produces actual remembered words. The specificity invites memory rather than abstraction.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "If we were to ask, let's say, any woman in your life over the last 20 years, what would they say your relationship to commitment was?"
- **Brett:** "That's a really good question. You'd have to ask some of them."
- **Joe:** "If you had to guess, maybe they even said something to you at some point."
- **Brett:** "Yeah, I mean, they say various things. I would say that they say that I am a deeply committed partner. And also that I change."
- *(Source: Allowing Yourself to Change)*

The person's self-story was that he was uncommitted. The witnesses he summoned actually said he was a deeply committed partner. The story and the data did not match. The discrepancy became material for the rest of the session.

## Variations

- For pattern detection: "Has anyone in your life ever said something to you about this?"
- For testing the harshness of self-judgment: "Would your closest friend describe you this way?"
- For surfacing strengths the person discounts: "If I asked the people who work with you what they appreciate, what would they say?"

## What Comes Next

Once the witness data is in the room, do not move on too quickly. Let the person sit with the contradiction. Often the next move is to ask: "so what do you make of the gap?" or "if that's what they actually say, what does that tell you about the story you've been telling yourself?"

The story does not collapse all at once. The data weakens it. Subsequent moves complete the work.

## Tone

Warm, curious, slightly playful. Not cross-examining. The energy is "I'm just curious what they'd actually say" rather than "you have no evidence for your claim."
