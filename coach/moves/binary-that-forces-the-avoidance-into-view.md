# The Binary That Forces the Avoidance Into View

## The Move

When a client describes a problem in their domain ("I can't get clients," "my book isn't selling," "I'm not making the income I want"), present the situation as a binary: "Either [you're bad at the work itself], or [you're not doing the thing required to get the work]. I'm going to assume it's the second."

The move works because the first half of the binary is almost always not true — the client is not actually bad at the work — and the second half is what they've been avoiding naming. The binary forces them to either defend the wrong claim ("yes I'm bad at the work") or accept the actual diagnosis ("I'm avoiding the marketing / asking / promoting").

The form has two parts:
1. **State the binary.** "Either A, or B."
2. **Rule out A.** "I'm going to assume it's the second." Or in some cases, just let the client rule it out by being unable to claim A.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a client has presented a business or visibility problem and is framing it as external (the market, the field, the technology, the timing).
- When the client clearly has the skill at the work itself, so "incompetence" is not a credible explanation.
- After the childhood pattern has been surfaced and the parent's avoided emotion has been named. The binary becomes the move that connects the avoidance pattern to the current behavior.
- When the client needs the avoidance to be named directly because they will not name it themselves.

## What It Produces

**Quick agreement.** The client almost always agrees instantly. "Yeah." They have known the avoidance was the issue. They needed someone to say it for them.

**Removal of the external-blame frame.** Once the binary has been deployed and the avoidance is named, the market / field / timing story collapses. The client cannot return to it without re-defending a position that has already been let go.

**A specific direction.** The binary surfaces what the avoidance is — marketing, asking, promoting, ending the thing, starting the thing. The work now has a clear target.

**Often a follow-up of "so that would be avoidance due to [the underlying emotion]."** Joe usually adds the diagnostic of why — shame, fear, scarcity. This finishes the move by connecting the avoidance to the emotion driving it.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Get the surface frame.** Let the client describe the problem as external. ("Cash flow. Clients. Nobody's public speaking anymore.")
2. **Apply the childhood pattern.** Use [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] first if the work has surfaced one. The binary lands harder when the avoidance has been pre-named.
3. **State the binary.** "Either [you're bad at the work], or [you're not doing the visibility/asking/promoting]."
4. **Rule out incompetence.** "I'm going to assume it's the second." Joe usually does this himself rather than asking the client to defend their competence.
5. **Name the underlying emotion.** "So that would be avoidance due to [shame / fear / scarcity]." This tags the avoidance with its source.
6. **Hand off to the next move.** Usually [[skill-transfer-redirect]] (if they have the relevant skill in another form) or [[is-the-shame-in-the-room-or-in-you]] (if the emotion needs testing).

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "What is it that, like, what's hard about making money here in coaching public speaking?"
- **Guest:** "Just cash flow, clients. Getting enough clients."
- **Joe:** "How much are you — how much are you making shame more important than —"
- **Guest:** "Yeah, much more."
- **Joe:** "And how much are you making avoidance more important than you, slash money. Either you're really a crappy coach, or you are not marketing yourself. I'm going to assume it's the second. And so that would be avoidance due to shame."
- *(Source: How Do I Make Money Doing What I Love?)*

The client framed the problem as cash flow and clients (external). Joe deployed the binary: either crappy coach (which neither of them believes) or not marketing yourself (the actual issue). Then named the underlying emotion: avoidance due to shame. Three sentences. The avoidance is in the room and the source of the avoidance is in the room.

## Why It Works

The binary is technically a false binary — there are other possible explanations (the field really is contracting; the client really is in a bad geographic market; the client's positioning is wrong). But the false-binary is doing the work it is meant to do: collapsing the external-blame frame by making the client choose between two specific positions, one of which is obviously false. The other positions are not in the binary, so the client cannot retreat to them without going back through the binary itself.

The "I'm going to assume it's the second" is the key. Joe does not ask the client to admit the avoidance. He just states the assumption. The client's choices become: (a) defend the incompetence claim, which they will not do, or (b) protest the binary, which would require them to introduce a third option they have not made credible, or (c) accept the diagnosis. Almost always they accept.

The move also depends on a specific kind of trust. The client has to trust that Joe is not going to use the avoidance against them. The diagnosis lands because Joe's tone is "this is the situation," not "this is your fault." The avoidance is a structural feature of the wiring, not a moral failing. See [[nothing-is-wrong-with-it-its-just-painful]] for the related principle.

## Tone

Flat, direct, slightly amused. The binary works when delivered without weight. Said with intensity, it feels like an attack. Said flatly, it feels like an obvious observation.

The "I'm going to assume it's the second" is the lightness of the move. Joe is not arguing the case. He is just noting which way the assumption falls. The client receives the noting as a relief — finally, someone is saying it.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the client has framed a problem in their domain as external (market, field, timing, competition)
- When [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] has surfaced the underlying avoidance pattern
- When the client clearly has competence in the domain, so the first half of the binary is genuinely implausible
- When the work needs the avoidance to be named explicitly to move forward
- When a client says things like "I just need to figure out the marketing," "I don't know how to sell," or "I'm not good at the business side"

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[skill-transfer-redirect]] — when the client already has the skill in another domain
- Toward [[is-the-shame-in-the-room-or-in-you]] — to test whether the shame the client carries actually corresponds to anything in the present
- Toward [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] — what they actually want in the blocked domain

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not use when the client genuinely might be bad at the work.** The move only works when "incompetence" is implausible. If the client really might be in over their head, the binary becomes an evasion. Use a different move.
- **Do not soften the binary.** "Maybe you're a little less effective than you could be, or maybe you're not marketing as much as you might want to" has no diagnostic power. The cleanness is the work.
- **Do not deploy without the underlying emotion having been surfaced.** "You're avoiding marketing" lands as personal criticism if there's no context for why. "Avoidance due to shame" with the childhood context lands as recognition.
- **Do not argue the binary if the client protests.** If they introduce a third option, take it seriously. The move forces a choice; it does not require the client to make the choice you predicted. Sometimes the third option is real (genuine market shift, real positioning problem). Stay curious.

## Variations

The binary structure adapts to many domains:

- **For a coach who can't get clients:** "Either you're a crappy coach, or you're not marketing yourself."
- **For a writer who can't sell their book:** "Either the book is bad, or you're not promoting it."
- **For a leader whose team is failing:** "Either your team is bad, or you're not giving them what they need."
- **For an entrepreneur whose business is stalled:** "Either the offering doesn't work, or you're not asking for the sale."
- **For a partner who feels disconnected:** "Either they don't want connection, or you're not actually asking for it."

In each case, the first option is the easy external explanation the client has been hiding behind. The second option is what they have been avoiding. Joe's assumption ("I'm going to assume it's the second") collapses the hiding place.
