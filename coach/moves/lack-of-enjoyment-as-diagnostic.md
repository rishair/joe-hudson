# Use Lack of Enjoyment as Diagnostic

## The Move

When someone reports they cannot enjoy a particular piece of work — a task, a process, a campaign, a meeting structure — do not treat the un-enjoyment as a personality issue or a motivation problem. Treat it as data about the work itself. Then ask: "what would it require to enjoy this?"

The answer to that question will name the structural problem with the work. The same answer is the structural fix.

The move runs in two beats:
1. The person says: "I can't enjoy this." (The work seems to be the work; the inability to enjoy seems to be the person's fault.)
2. The move replies: "What would it take to enjoy this?" (The work becomes the question; the conditions for enjoyment become the conditions for the work to function.)

The answer almost always describes the way the work would have to be restructured to be both enjoyable and effective. The two restructurings are the same restructuring.

## The Worked Example: The Marketing Plan

A business owner had a marketing plan that was not working. Joe asked what it would require for the owner to enjoy doing the marketing. The owner said: "I can't enjoy it." Joe pressed: "Okay, let's go from scratch. What would we have to do to enjoy this marketing plan?"

The owner answered: the team would have to be involved differently. The marketing would have to be authentic to the brand. Each person doing it would have to feel they were saying something meaningful, not something they had to say to get customers.

"And so of course I can't market and enjoy it." -- the business owner, quoted by Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work

Joe's response: "Exactly that. That's exactly what you need to do to solve your marketing problem. So let me guess that your marketing isn't working."

The owner: "Yeah, the marketing — how did you know?"

Joe: "Because you're not enjoying it. It's not authentic to you. And so everybody can read and feel that."

The marketing problem and the enjoyment problem were the same problem. The conditions the owner named for enjoying the work were the conditions the work needed to function. Within a quarter, the marketing started working — because the owner restructured it around the answer the question had produced.

"He started thinking of ways that the marketing department could come together and really work off of something that made them feel good and connected. Both with each other and with their audience. And after just a short period of time, I think it was less than a quarter, their marketing started working." -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work

## Why This Move Works

The move rests on a structural claim: the same compartmentalization that makes work un-enjoyable for the person doing it is the same compartmentalization that makes the work fail at its job. The two are not coincidentally co-occurring. They are the same phenomenon viewed from different sides.

The mechanism is the one named in [[consciousness-is-the-product]]: the state of the producer transmits into the product. Un-enjoyed work transmits its un-enjoyment to the customer, the user, the audience. They can read it. They reciprocate. The work does not land. See also [[authenticity-in-business]].

The same logic operates at the team level. A marketing campaign no one on the team is enjoying is a campaign no one on the team is fully invested in. The output reflects the lack of investment. Customers receive a campaign that has the texture of obligation rather than expression. The texture is read; the response is corresponding.

This is why the question "what would it require to enjoy this?" is not a soft question. It is the highest-leverage operational question available in the situation. It maps directly to "what would make this work?"

## How to Use the Move

1. **Notice the un-enjoyment.** The person describes not enjoying a work activity. The reflex is to coach them on motivation or to push through. Resist that reflex.

2. **Ask the diagnostic question.** "What would it take to enjoy this?" or "What would have to be true for you to enjoy doing this?" or "Let's go from scratch — what would we have to do to enjoy this?"

3. **Listen for structural conditions, not personal conditions.** The good answer is structural ("the team would need to be involved differently"; "this would need to be authentic to who we are"; "we'd need to be allowed to express what we actually think"). A personal answer ("I'd need to be in a better mood"; "I'd need more discipline") is a deflection. If the person goes there, redirect to the work itself.

4. **Name the equivalence.** "That's also what you need to do to solve the actual problem." Make the connection explicit. The person typically does not see it until it is named, because they have been treating the enjoyment-problem and the work-problem as two separate problems.

5. **Move toward the restructuring.** The answer is operational. It is not a wish list. The conditions for enjoyment are the conditions the work needs. Begin treating them as the work plan.

## What This Move Is Not

- **Not [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]].** That move is about the question the person asks themselves while doing a task. This move is about using un-enjoyment as evidence of structural problems with the work itself. The redirect is a per-task psychological intervention. The diagnostic is an organizational intervention.

- **Not "do whatever you enjoy."** The move is not permission to abandon hard work. It is a diagnostic for finding where the work is misstructured. Hard work in the right structure is enjoyable; pleasant work in the wrong structure is not.

- **Not validation of complaint.** When someone complains "I hate doing this," the move is not "yes, that is awful." The move is "good — what would it take to enjoy it? what would have to change about this for it to be enjoyable?" The complaint becomes the entry point to the structural fix.

- **Not [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]] for difficult emotions.** That move applies enjoyment to feelings that are present (shame, sadness, stress). This move applies the question of enjoyability to work structures. The two share a parent principle ([[enjoyment-as-path]]) but operate at different scales.

## Boundary Conditions

Sometimes the answer to "what would it take to enjoy this?" is "this work should not be done at all." That is also useful data. The unenjoyed work that has no restructured version that would be enjoyable is sometimes work that no longer needs to exist. See [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]] for the broader principle.

Sometimes the answer is "I am the wrong person to do this." Also useful data. The work might be enjoyable for someone else with different drives. The misfit can sometimes be fixed by redistribution rather than restructuring.

Sometimes the answer reveals a personal pattern rather than a structural one — for example, the person cannot enjoy any work because they have a [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] structure or because they are [[i-make-money-only-when-i-hate-what-im-doing]]. In those cases, the diagnostic move surfaces the personal pattern, and the work moves to that level. The diagnostic does not have to produce a structural answer to be useful.

## When to Use the Move

- A leader is asking why a campaign, product, or process is not landing with customers/users.
- A team member reports being unable to enjoy their work and the leader is reaching for performance coaching.
- A founder is grinding through marketing, fundraising, or operations and seeing low returns on effort.
- An organization has a function nobody enjoys (compliance, reporting, internal meetings) and the function is dysfunctional.
- A person is asking why their work feels inauthentic and is also asking why it is not working — without realizing those are the same question.

## How Joe Delivers It

The delivery is curious, almost playful. Joe does not approach the diagnostic with a serious "let's solve your problem" frame. He approaches it as if he is genuinely curious what would have to be true.

The "from scratch" framing matters. "Okay, let's go from scratch. What would we have to do to enjoy this?" The "from scratch" gives permission to reimagine the work rather than to optimize the existing version. The existing version is the unenjoyed version; optimizing it produces a more optimized unenjoyed version. The reimagining is where the structural answer comes from.

The reveal moment — "that's exactly what you need to do to solve your actual problem" — is delivered as a small surprise rather than as a triumphant insight. The lightness is part of why the person can receive it. A heavier delivery would cue defensiveness.
