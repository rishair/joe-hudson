# Receive the Apology Permeably (Don't Buy the Story)

## The Move

Let the apology move all the way through your body without bracing against it, without deflecting it, and without buying any story embedded in it. Permeable, not armored. Feel it, not analyze it. The receiving is its own practice.

"What I notice is that people who can receive them, which means that you're letting yourself feel it all the way through and bring up any emotion that it might come up. And when I say all the way through, it literally feels like I have armor up and I'm protecting myself, or I'm more like permeable and the thing can just move right through me." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## The Two Halves

The move has two halves, and missing either one breaks it.

**Feel it all the way through.** The apology should move through your system the way water moves through a strainer. Not bouncing off your skin. Not getting stuck in your chest. Through you. Any emotion that comes up with it (relief, sadness, anger, tenderness) is part of the move, not a problem to manage.

**Do not buy the story.** Apologies often contain implicit stories. The shame apology contains the story "I am bad and you are the wronged party." The passive-aggressive apology contains the story "you owe me an apology back." The guilt-relief apology contains the story "this should make you feel better." None of these stories are yours to buy. You can feel the apology fully without absorbing the story underneath it.

"I would describe it as if you feel the apology all the way through and let it permeate you, but don't buy the story." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## Permeable Is Not Bowled Over

Permeable receiving is sometimes mistaken for losing yourself in the apology. The two are distinct. Permeable means the apology moves through you. Bowled over means the apology overrides your own ground.

The clearest example: someone delivers a shame-based apology and you take on the shame. You start apologizing back ("no, I'm sorry, it was actually my fault"). The apology has not moved through you. It has installed itself in you and reorganized your sense of what happened.

"If somebody delivers to you an apology with shame and you take on that shame and you feel that shame, and then that makes you feel like you've actually done something wrong, then you find yourself in a 'no I'm sorry, no I'm sorry' kind of situation." -- Brett Kistler, The Upright Apology

The distinction Joe offers: feel the apology all the way through, but do not buy the story. The feeling is the permeable part. The not-buying-the-story is what protects your ground. Both halves are required; missing either produces a different problem.

## The Subtle Constriction Signal

The receiver's body knows when permeability has failed even if the receiver does not. The signal is small.

"You can detect that somewhere in your body you're closing down and you're constricting. And then it's like, what is, what is that constriction? What do I feel like I'm defending right now?" -- Brett Kistler, The Upright Apology

The closing-down can happen even when the receiver says all the right words. The words can be conciliatory while the body is hardening. Notice the body. The body is the diagnostic that the mind tends to miss.

## The Hungry Ghost

People who cannot receive apologies often chronically want them. They believe more apologies will fix what is missing. The bottleneck is not the supply of apologies but the receiving capacity.

"Some people are really good at receiving apologies. It's like receiving compliments. A lot of people want apologies, but then they don't receive them, so they're constantly a hungry ghost." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The hungry ghost pattern indicates that incoming apologies are bouncing off armor. Until the receiver becomes permeable, no apology will be enough. This is parallel to [[letting-judgment-in]] and [[being-heard]]: the issue is on the receiving side.

## What Permeable Receiving Sounds Like

There is often nothing to say. Permeable receiving is mostly silent. The person receiving lets the apology arrive, feels what arrives with it, and may have tears, may have a softening, may simply have a long exhale.

If a verbal response comes, it tends to be simple: "thank you" or "I hear you" or just naming what the receiver is feeling. The response is not "it's okay" (which often dismisses the apology) and not "yeah but" (which deflects it).

## When the Apology Is Passive-Aggressive

When the apology is dysfunctional, permeable receiving plus not-buying-the-story produces a different response. The receiver can feel the apology, decline the implicit story, and name what is happening clearly.

"If somebody does a passive-aggressive apology to me and they're like, 'Yeah, I'm really sorry that I couldn't read your mind,' if I feel that all the way through, if I allow that feeling all the way in my system and I don't take it personally, if I don't buy the story, then I easily can say something like, 'Oh wow, it feels like you still want me to be upset at something I've done. What are you feeling that I need to take responsibility for that I'm not taking responsibility for?'" -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The permeability is what makes the clarifying response possible without defensiveness. Without permeability, the response would be sharper. See [[hardening-means-personal]].

## The Constriction Diagnostic

The body tells you whether the receiving has gone well. If you notice yourself constricting, defending, or going quiet in a closed way, you have armored up. The move did not happen.

"When you're constricting and defending, then that's when you know that something's not. When my constriction comes that I'm just like, 'okay, yeah, sure, yeah, thanks for that apology,' you know, or whatever response that I would have." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

A constricted "thanks for that apology" is the diagnostic. The form is reception. The substance is rejection. Notice the constriction; investigate it as data. See [[defense-vs-love]].

## When To Use It

- Any time someone apologizes to you, especially if the apology is awkward, partial, or imperfect.
- When you notice yourself wanting more apologies than you receive.
- When you have just delivered an upright apology and the other person apologizes back.
- When the apology comes in a form you don't immediately like (passive-aggressive, shame-based, performative). Permeable receiving plus declining the story is the move that handles all of these.

## What Joe Looks For

- The person's body posture shifts from armored to open.
- Breath returns to the chest and belly.
- Tears or laughter or any clean emotional movement on the receiver.
- The verbal response is not "it's okay" (caretaking the apologizer's feelings) and not "you better mean it" (still in fight).

## Tone

The receiving is quiet. Not performative. The receiver does not need to demonstrate that they have received well. The demonstration is the felt sense in the room, not anything the receiver says.
