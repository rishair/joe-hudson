# Scale the Feeling 1 to 100 With Anchors

## The Move

When a feeling is operating live in the room — self-consciousness, anxiety, fear, anger, sadness — ask the person to rate it on a scale of 1 to 100. Anchor the scale explicitly. 100 is the most they have ever felt it. 0 is the opposite-state condition (hanging out with your best friend in a moment of Zen, the most relaxed they have ever been).

The form Joe uses:

"How self-conscious are you on a scale to like 1 to 100, the most self-conscious you've ever been?"

Then anchor:

"100 is the most self-conscious you've ever been. Zero is like hanging out with your best friend in that like moment of Zen."

The person produces a number. The number is the diagnostic. The scale is also the platform for the next move: asking if they can shift the number by one increment. See [[ask-one-percent-less-then-how-did-you-do-that]].

## When Joe Uses It

The scale gets deployed when:

- A feeling is firing live and the person is being vague about its intensity.
- The work needs a measurable baseline so that shifts can be detected.
- The person is in a state they think is fixed; the scale converts the state from binary to continuous.
- A subsequent move depends on being able to ask for an incremental shift. The scale is the prerequisite.
- The person is denying the feeling is there ("I'm not that anxious"); the scale exposes the actual intensity.
- The person is amplifying the feeling ("I'm completely terrified"); the scale exposes that there is room beneath the ceiling.

The qualifying condition: the feeling has to be present in the room. The scale is not a useful question about an abstract feeling. It is a tool for measuring an active one.

## What the Anchors Do

The anchors are load-bearing. Without anchors, the scale produces a number that is calibrated to nothing. With anchors, the number is calibrated to the person's own felt experience of the extremes.

**The 100 anchor: the most they have ever felt it.** This anchor is specific to the person, not to an external standard. The person who has been to 100 in a panic attack and the person whose 100 is being-spoken-to-in-line-at-the-store both produce useful numbers, because the anchor is their own ceiling, not an abstract ceiling.

**The 0 anchor: the opposite state, lived.** Not "no feeling" in the abstract. A specific moment of the opposite condition. "Hanging out with your best friend in that moment of Zen." This anchor matters because most people cannot imagine zero abstractly — they can only point at it through a memory of having been in it. The anchor gives them the memory to point at.

The anchors together make the scale a translation from the person's own life experience into a number. The number is then meaningful in a way a generic 1-10 rating is not.

## Why the Move Works

A feeling that has been treated as a fixed state ("I am self-conscious") cannot be worked. A feeling that has a number can be worked. The scale converts identity-language into state-language.

The move also defeats the all-or-nothing thinking that often accompanies social anxiety, self-consciousness, and fear. The person reports the feeling as if it were either fully on or fully off. The scale reveals that there is gradient. The gradient is what makes shifting possible.

The number also exposes the person's actual capacity. Most people, asked to scale a feeling they thought was overwhelming, report a 40 or 50. The 40 is itself the news: the person had been operating as if the feeling were a 90. The recognition that there is a lot of room above the current level is its own intervention — the person realizes they are not at the ceiling and have not been at the ceiling.

The scale also creates the conditions for [[ask-one-percent-less-then-how-did-you-do-that]]. Without the scale, the question "can you make it less?" has no referent. With the scale, "can you make it 39?" is a specific, answerable question.

## What It Produces

- A number, usually lower than expected.
- A felt shift in the person's relationship to the feeling — from fixed to variable.
- An opening for the next move: ask for a one-increment shift.
- Sometimes, the number drops just from being asked. The act of measuring the feeling is itself a step out of being inside it.
- Sometimes, the person resists giving a number. The resistance is data. It usually means the feeling is operating as identity, not as state. The work then is to gently insist on the number until the person can produce one.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "Okay, I don't care. Is it so right now you're self-conscious?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "How self-conscious are you on a scale to like 1 to 100, the most self-conscious you've ever been?"
- **Guest:** "Oh, right now?"
- **Joe:** "Um, yeah, 100 is the most self-conscious you've ever been. Zero is like hanging out with your best friend in that like moment of Zen."
- **Guest:** "Oh, just with a lot of practice, I've— I'm probably like 40%."
- **Joe:** "You're 40% self-conscious right now. And can you make it 39?"

*(Source: How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?)*

Note the structure. Joe asks the scale question. The person clarifies the temporal reference ("right now?"). Joe confirms and re-anchors. The person produces 40. Joe immediately uses the number as the platform for the next move ("can you make it 39?"). The scale was not the end. It was the bridge to the increment work.

The "40" itself is also useful information. The person had been talking about her on-stage self-consciousness as if it were an unmanageable condition. The 40 reveals that even her in-the-room self-consciousness has 60 points of room above it. The on-stage version is higher, but the room exists.

## Tone

Curious, almost technical. The scale is delivered with light, matter-of-fact energy. The implicit message: "I'm not asking you a deep question. I'm asking you a measurement question. Let's see what number comes out."

The anchors are delivered conversationally, not as instructions. "Hanging out with your best friend in that moment of Zen" is a warmer phrasing than "the lowest possible self-consciousness." The warmth keeps the scale from feeling clinical.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not asking how the person feels in general. It is asking for a number about a specific feeling that is operating right now.
- This is not therapy-style emotional check-in. It is a diagnostic for working with the live state.
- This is not the introduction to a long conversation about the feeling. It is the first move in a sequence that aims at producing a shift, not at understanding the feeling more deeply.
- This is not for use when the feeling is not present. A scale of an absent feeling is meaningless. The scale requires the live state.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not skip the anchors.** A naked 1-10 or 1-100 scale produces a number calibrated to nothing. The shifts you measure afterward will not be reliable. The anchors are not optional.
- **Do not use the number to interpret the feeling's severity.** A 40 is not "moderate" self-consciousness. A 40 is "40% of this person's lifetime peak." The number does not transfer across people.
- **Do not skip the next move.** The scale is a setup. If you ask for a number and then move to a different topic, you have wasted the move. Always have the increment question ready to follow.
- **Do not let the person hedge.** "Probably somewhere between 30 and 50" is not a useful answer. Push for a single number. The single number is what makes the next move possible.
- **Do not deploy the scale to talk the person out of the feeling.** The implicit message must not be "see, it's not that bad." The scale is for working with the feeling, not for minimizing it.
