# Ask for Specific Help Changing the Behavior (The Tennis Ball, the "I See How Much You Care")

## The Move

After announcing the behavior change and apologizing uprightly, ask the people you announced to for specific help noticing and interrupting the old pattern when it shows up. The ask must be specific: not "remind me," but the exact signal that would actually work for you.

This is the third step of the [[four-as-of-behavior-change|4 A's]] and is distinct from the broader [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]] move (which is about the lonely CEO asking for delegation help). Here the ask is for reflection and accountability tied to a specific behavioral pattern the person has just announced they're changing.

## Three Functions of the Ask

**1. Accountability with love (reflection that accelerates learning).**

"It's like you're basically asking for accountability in one way or another. So which is not accountability the way most people think about accountability, but accountability, like, with love, right? So you're asking for people to remind you, to see you, to show you, to help you. And so that's a really important thing because we learn better when we have reflection... I actually have somebody there who's like, oh, hey, Joe, you're doing that thing. And that reflection is super useful." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The outside view sees what the inside view can't yet see. When someone names the old pattern back to you in real time, the body files the moment as data. The reflection compresses the learning curve.

The defensive-response variant: if you get defensive when reminded, that's diagnostic data too. The defensiveness reveals how much shame is still attached to the pattern. You can work the shame directly.

"It's also useful in the fact that if I get defensive on that reflection, it's a world of beautiful stuff for me to sort through to undo the pattern. If I can't just be, oh, you're right, I'm totally taking care of you in this moment, I apologize for that... If I can't do that, if I'm like, boy, why are you reminding me? That means I still have a bunch of shame to work through for the thing." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**2. The receptivity shift the ask itself creates.**

The ask changes the psychology on both sides. The person asking has invited the reflection. The person reminding has been invited to do so. Both sides are now in a different relationship to the moment of correction than they would be without the explicit ask.

"That moment shifts too, because first, the person has been invited to remind you. And secondly, you have asked for it. So that changes your own psychology. So when somebody does remind you, you're receptive to it. There's a way that simply by having asked, you will be more receptive." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The reverse case shows the mechanism: when someone reminds you of something you didn't ask them to remind you of, the same words feel intrusive. When you've asked, the same words feel like care.

**3. The "you're not alone" reveal.**

Patterns hold partly because we think we're alone in them. The ask reveals that other people are willing to help — which dissolves the imagined-alone-ness underneath the pattern.

"It reminds you that you're not alone in it. And oftentimes these patterns hold in place because we think we're alone in it. And so if you can see, oh, there's other people who wanna support you... that immediately allows me to see that my reality might be different than everybody else's. And people do wanna help me and they see the same thing. And so I'm not alone in it, which is really, really useful for changing behavior." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The presumption of judgment — that everyone has been silently noting your pattern and judging you for it — breaks when people respond with active support. The pattern was held partly by the imagined cost of dropping it; the ask reveals that the imagined cost was higher than the real cost.

## The Specificity Requirement

Vague asks ("just call me out") don't work. Specific asks tied to the antidote do. The work of finding the specific antidote is itself part of the change.

**The "I see how much you care" example.**

Joe's team developed this phrase for him when he rushed. The antidote was so specific that hearing it would instantly resolve the felt-engine of the rushing.

"This was when we were doing Groundbreakers in the early days, I would get into this rush. And I wanted to be reminded of the rush... And we sat around like, what is the antidote? What's the specific antidote to the rush? And Janine was like, proposed, I see how much you care. And immediately, anytime anybody said that, my whole system would just, okay, I'm good. Because for me, rushing was — I was told as a kid, I didn't care if I was late or if I wasn't on time. So it just like immediately dissolved that." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The general principle: the specific antidote is the phrase or signal that addresses the original belief the pattern was protecting. To find it, you have to understand what the pattern was actually protecting — which is itself work that produces change.

"The more specific you can be, like, in these moments, at these times, this is the kind of thing to say that supports me. It makes you really understand the pattern and what it is that neutralizes that pattern. And just that work, just the work of knowing it and then getting it is unbelievably useful." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**The tennis ball example.**

When the team was being told to flag Joe's use of "you guys" instead of "folks," the standard reminder format (verbal correction) was loaded with potential awkwardness. The tennis ball game changed the energetic register entirely.

"I literally gave the person a tennis ball and I was just like, just throw it at me every time I say guys so that I remember it's not about hurting me, but just throw it at me so that I can remember because it's just so not in my consciousness at all." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The play form did several things:
- Removed the shame from the correction (you can't shame someone by throwing a tennis ball).
- Removed the shame from the corrector (no scolding required).
- Created a game-like feeling that made the correction welcome rather than annoying.
- Gave the body a clear signal (the physical impact of the ball) that bypassed the head's defenses.

"There's not shame in that too. If there was shame in it, you wouldn't be like, 'Yeah, throw a tennis ball at me.' There's like, 'Hey, yeah, this is personal. I haven't been tracking or aware of literally everything. And there are certain things that I could be more aware of. And I want my message to be heard. And if this helps you hear it, then I wanna do that.' So let's play a game, remind me." -- Joe Hudson and Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## How to Develop the Specific Antidote

The specific antidote often emerges from collaborative work with the people you've announced to. It's not something you can usually design alone. The conversation goes:

1. Name what the pattern is and what you think it's protecting.
2. Ask the others what they think the antidote would be.
3. Try a few. Notice which ones produce the felt shift in your body.
4. Settle on the one that works.

The collaborative discovery is itself part of the relationship change. The team is now genuinely engaged in your transformation rather than just being told to enforce it.

The diagnostic for a good antidote: when delivered, does your system actually settle? Does the body shift? If the antidote is right, the answer is yes, and reliably. If the antidote is just words, the system doesn't shift, and you need a different antidote.

## The Bidirectional Effect

Asking for help in this specific way doesn't just change your behavior. It changes the people you're asking too.

"In inviting people to interact with you in a certain way, you're inviting them to change their behavior by taking a different action. If the action might have been hold back, resent you, and then blow up at you later, the invited action is tell me, throw a tennis ball at me. By inviting them into a different action, it actually changes their side of the dynamic with you. It doesn't change it, it invites them into an action that can result in a better dynamic." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The relational dynamic had two sides. By giving the other side a clean, specific action to take when the pattern shows up, you stop them from having to discharge the dynamic in less functional ways. They're no longer the victim of your pattern; they're an active participant in changing it.

"It empowers everybody else around your pattern. So they don't have to feel a victim to it." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## What This Is Not

- Not asking for permission. The change is yours; the help is operational.
- Not asking for emotional support. The ask is for the specific behavioral interruption.
- Not asking for tolerance. You're not asking people to put up with the old behavior while you change. You're asking them to actively interrupt it.
- Not asking from shame. See [[asking-with-shame-invites-refusal]]. The shame-loaded ask produces a different and worse response than the clean ask.

## What Tends to Happen

- People respond with active willingness. Often more enthusiastically than you expected.
- The first time the antidote gets delivered, the body shifts in a noticeable way. The pattern's grip loosens immediately.
- The collaborative discovery of the antidote often produces unexpected information about the pattern.
- The relational field with the people who agreed to help becomes warmer and more honest.

The frustrating variant: 10 people remind you on the same day. This is data that the pattern is everywhere and that you've successfully restructured the field. It's annoying in the moment but a sign the move is working.

"I have done it in a way where it's like all of a sudden 10 people are reminding me on the same day. And that can be frustrating. It's like, I'm trying, I'm trying, come on. You know, but typically it works really, really well." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## When to Recommend This Move

- After the wide announcement and upright apology are complete.
- When the client is ready to accept active interruption from others.
- When the pattern is one the client cannot reliably catch in themselves.
- When the client has a clear enough sense of the pattern's engine to articulate (with the team's help) what the antidote would be.
