# Put Your Attention On Someone Else On Stage

## The Move

When self-consciousness is firing in the middle of a performance, redirect the attention off the self and onto something or someone else. Specifically:

- A person in the front row -- a best friend, a co-founder, a teammate, anyone whose face the performer can read with warmth.
- The mission of what is being said -- the cause, the message, what the audience needs to hear.
- The "heroes behind you" -- a felt-visualization of people who have done this kind of work before, supporting from behind.

The structural principle: stage fright is a solipsistic loop. The person is monitoring themselves -- "how am I doing, how do I look, what are they thinking, am I about to mess up." The monitoring is the self-consciousness. The monitoring is what produces the freeze. Redirecting the attention breaks the loop.

"Put all your attention on something else, like all your attention on your mission, or put all your attention on someone else, your best friend in the front row, or your co-founder right across from you at the table when you speak, or your teammate who's right on board with you. But putting your attention on something else, not on yourself." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

## What This Move Treats

The specific read: the performer is in a self-consciousness loop -- monitoring their own performance in real time, watching themselves from outside, thinking about how they look. The diagnostic signs:

- The performer reports "what if I, what if I, what if I" -- the looping question about themselves.
- The performer cannot find the next thing to say because they are watching themselves looking for the next thing to say.
- The voice goes high, the pace quickens, the palms sweat -- the body is in a feedback loop with the monitoring.
- The performer reports "I went into performance mode" or "I left myself up there."

When the loop is firing, the in-the-moment moves that work on the body alone -- breath, slowing down -- have a hard time landing because the monitoring will incorporate the body work into the loop ("am I breathing slowly enough? am I being present enough?"). The attention-redirect bypasses the monitoring by giving it something else to do.

## How To Do It

Several specific framings, each useful in different moments:

**Pick one person.** Find a specific face in the audience -- a friend you brought, a colleague you know is rooting for you, a member of the team you are pitching to whose face is warm. Talk to that person. The talk becomes more natural because it is talk-to-a-person rather than talk-to-an-amorphous-audience. The face provides a reference point that resists the abstraction that the self-consciousness feeds on.

**Pick the mission.** Put the attention on what needs to be said and why. The mission is bigger than the performer's self-image. The cause, the message, the thing the audience needs to walk away with. When the attention is on the mission, the performer becomes the conduit rather than the object. The judgment loops about "how am I doing" cannot easily land on a person who is focused on "does this need to be said this way to land for them."

**Pick the heroes behind you.** Tara's visualization: "Picture yourself at the front, like the prow of a boat, and you have everyone behind you at your back rooting for you. And you put everyone you want back there, like all of your heroes and great public speakers and orators or people who've done great fundraising in the past. Like just put them all in the back and feel their support."

The visualization works on the felt-presence layer. The performer who feels alone on stage is more vulnerable to the self-consciousness loop than the performer who feels backed. The actual physical presence of supporters is not required; the felt-presence is what matters. The performer who has internalized this practice walks on stage already in the felt-company of every speaker they admire.

## When To Use It

- When the self-consciousness loop is firing mid-performance.
- When the performer reports going blank, freezing, or losing the thread because they are watching themselves.
- As a deliberate pre-performance setup: pick the person, name the mission, install the heroes before walking out.
- When the performer is so focused on what the audience thinks of them that they cannot deliver what they came to deliver.

It is less effective when the underlying issue is the suppression-style self-consciousness that comes from the [[dont-suppress-emotion-to-perform]] anti-pattern. There the issue is not "attention on self" but "instruction to perform without self" -- attention redirection does not solve the contradiction. The deeper work in [[i-want-to-be-authentic-on-stage]] applies.

## What This Move Produces

The break in the loop. The performer's attention has somewhere to go that is not the self-monitoring. The voice drops; the pace slows; the body remembers it can breathe. The next sentence arrives because the person looking for the next sentence is no longer the same person who was watching the previous sentence happen.

Joe's read on this in the conversation: "Oh, when I step on stage, this is what I can do. I can actually put my attention on the people who are really on my team or index on, I know that people have my back." The move opens what the freeze had closed: the connection to the room rather than the isolation in the self-monitoring.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not a permanent fix. The self-consciousness loop will fire again. The move is a recovery tool for when it fires, not a solution to the underlying pattern.
- It is not avoidance. The attention-redirect is not about pretending the self is not there. It is about routing the attention through something other than the self-monitoring channel.
- It is not "fake it till you make it." The friend in the front row is real. The mission is real. The heroes are real (even if felt rather than present). The redirect is to actually-present things, not to imagined confidence.
- It is not the same as [[cutting-off-your-own-authenticity-in-real-time]]. Cutting off authenticity is the suppression of spontaneous emotion. The attention-redirect keeps the performer in their emotion but routes the attention through something other than self-judgment.

## Connected To The Larger Stage Fright Arc

This move fits inside the broader concern of [[i-want-to-be-authentic-on-stage]] and the multi-phase practice of [[nervous-system-prep-before-high-stakes]]. The position in the arc:

- Pre-performance: body discharge, internal permissioning, content prep as tree not chain.
- Pre-stage moment: pick the person, name the mission, install the heroes.
- In-the-moment when self-consciousness fires: the redirect.
- In-the-moment when activation spikes: [[say-im-excited-ten-times]].
- In-the-moment when something goes wrong: [[mistakes-on-stage-are-gifts]].
- Post-performance: reregulation and celebration.

The attention-redirect is the specific in-the-moment intervention for the self-consciousness flavor of stage difficulty. Other in-the-moment moves apply when the body floods (the I'm-excited practice) or when something goes wrong (the mistake reframe).

## Tone

Use a matter-of-fact, instructive tone when teaching this. The move is concrete and operational. The person trying to use it for the first time benefits from specificity ("look at this person; don't look at the audience as a whole") rather than abstraction ("just be in the moment"). The simpler the instruction, the more likely it is to land in a body that is already activated.
