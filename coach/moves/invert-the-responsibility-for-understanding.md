# Invert the Responsibility for Understanding

## The Move

When a coachee is reporting that they keep being misunderstood, ask them: "What makes it your job to be understood? What makes it not my job to try to understand you?" Then, after they have answered, take the move into the body: "Make it my responsibility to understand you. How would you do that? Look me in the eyes. Think to yourself, *this dude is a fucking professional coach, he should absolutely understand me, and if he can't, that's his fucking problem.* Now, what do you say?"

The intellectual frame is the question. The body frame is the live experiment. Both matter.

## When Joe Uses It

When the coachee is fused with the position that the speaker is responsible for being understood. The fusion usually shows up in the first few exchanges of a session — the person uses phrases like "it's my job to make sure I'm expressing myself clearly," "I'm trying every effort," "I have to communicate well." The frame is morally neutral to them. The move surfaces it as a position they did not know they were holding.

Use it especially when:

- The coachee is asking for communication tools or technique advice.
- The coachee is treating the listener as a fixed variable and themselves as the only variable.
- The coachee is exhausted by the work of being understood.
- The pattern [[chasing-understanding-as-control]] is active and a reframe is needed before the live experiment can land.

## How To Run It

1. **Ask the intellectual version first.** "What makes it your job to be understood? What makes it not my job to try to understand you?" Let them answer. The first answer is usually a defense of the position — "it's my responsibility because..." That answer is data; do not argue with it.
2. **Press gently on the answer.** Ask whether they actually have control over the listener's comprehension. They will admit they do not. The admission is necessary before the next step.
3. **Ask them to do the inversion in the body.** "Let's try it with none. You're totally putting the responsibility of understanding on me. Look at me in the eyes. Think to yourself — *this dude is a professional coach, he should absolutely understand me, and if he can't, that's his fucking problem.* And then what do you say?"
4. **Notice what comes out.** The first attempt often still has defense in it. Or chasing. Name what you got. "That's almost defensive. Without defense, just like, this is your responsibility. How do you do that?"
5. **Push until they hit the wall.** Most people, asked to fully transfer responsibility to the listener, will say "I can't." The "I can't" is the work. It surfaces the belief that has been running the whole pattern.
6. **Move on to the next reframe.** Once the "I can't" is in the room, the coachee is ready for the deeper teaching: "So they're not receiving it as you don't understand me. They're receiving it as I don't want to believe you. ... The more you try to control, the more they feel controlled." From here, the path opens toward [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] and self-listening.

## What It Produces

The coachee discovers, in the room, that the position they have been carrying (it's my job to make sure you understand me) is not a moral fact but a learned posture. They also discover that they cannot easily drop the position even when asked to. Both discoveries are useful.

The "I can't" itself is the gold. It surfaces a belief that has been operating below conscious access: that the speaker is responsible for the listener's comprehension. With the belief in the room, the work of unwinding it can begin.

A secondary effect: the move dissolves any expectation that Joe is about to give communication advice. The coachee was prepared to receive tips. Instead they got a reframe of the whole situation. The reframe creates the space for the actual work to happen.

## Dialogue Example

The intellectual version of the move:

- **Joe:** "What makes it your job to be understood? What makes it not my job to try to understand you?"
- **Savannah:** "Well, I feel like it's my job to make sure that I'm expressing myself in a way that's clear."
- **Joe:** "Clear to who? ... I might communicate something in a way that's clear to 20% of the people, unclear to 80% of the people. ... How much control do you actually have over people's capacity to understand you?"
- **Savannah:** "I guess I have no control over other people's capacity for anything, let alone to understand me."

The intellectual ground is laid. Then the body experiment:

- **Joe:** "Okay, so let's just assume I had no idea what you just said. I don't fucking understand at all the thing that you just said. I want you to make it my responsibility to understand you. How would you do that?"
- **Savannah:** "Okay, so if you are in a situation and you are trying to express something and the other person doesn't seem to be picking up on that, how would that make you feel?"
- **Joe:** "Okay, how much of that was you taking responsibility for me understanding, and how much of that was you taking responsibility for my understanding?"
- **Savannah:** "I guess probably all of it, because I was trying to be like a guide to get you to get to where we want to be."
- *(Source: 2024-04-12 How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?)*

Joe pushes further:

- **Joe:** "Now let's try it with none. You're totally putting the responsibility of understanding on me. ... Look me in the eyes. Think to yourself, *this dude is a fucking professional coach, he should absolutely fucking understand me, and if he can't, that's his fucking problem.* And then what do you say?"
- **Savannah:** "I'm communicating with you in a way that is clear even and with very specific words. I'm hoping that as an intelligent human being, you are listening with open ears and can at least grok some of my meaning."
- **Joe:** "That's almost defensive. ... Without defense, just like, this is your responsibility. How do you do that?"
- **Savannah:** "I can't. That's why I'm here."

The "I can't" arrives. The work the session is about to do is now visible.

## What This Is Not

This is not an attempt to install a new rule ("from now on, it's the listener's job to understand"). The rule would be just as constraining as the old one. The move is a temporary inversion designed to surface the belief, not to replace it.

This is not advice to be rude or to demand comprehension from others. The actual teaching that follows is the opposite — let go of the chasing entirely and bring the attention back to self-listening. The inversion is a stepping stone, not the destination.

This is not a move to be used when the coachee is in a fragile state and the inversion would feel like attack. Read the room. If the person is already collapsed under the weight of feeling misunderstood, the inversion may need to wait until some warmth has been established.

## Tone

Direct, slightly playful. The mock-instruction "think to yourself, *this dude is a professional coach, he should absolutely fucking understand me*" is delivered with comic emphasis. The comedy is part of the medicine — it lets the coachee try on a stance they would never adopt in real life, in the safety of obvious performance. They can drop into the experiment because nothing about it is sincere demand; it is sincere experiment.

When the "I can't" arrives, the tone shifts immediately to acknowledgment. The coachee has just surfaced the belief. The next words from the coach are quiet and present, not pressing.
