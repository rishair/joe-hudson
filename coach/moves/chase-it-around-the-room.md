# Chase It Around the Room

## The Move

When a feeling — often shame — keeps slipping away from the person's attempts to give it loving attention, Joe instructs them to chase it. Not catch it. Not subdue it. Pursue it playfully with the promise of love.

The instruction in his words: "chase it around the room trying to give it love. Like, I'm going to love you, you can't get away from me, I'm going to love you."

The move treats the avoidant feeling as a small animal hiding under the couch. The right relationship is not to corner it. It is to play the game it is playing — and to keep loving it while playing.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the person reports that the feeling keeps slipping away from their attention. "Like the attention misses the shame in some way. The shame manages to wiggle out from under the attention."
- When loving attention has been instructed and the loving attention is not landing.
- When shame is the specific emotion (this is most common, though it works for any avoidant feeling).
- When the person is trying to do the loving correctly and the trying is part of why it is not landing.

## Why It Works

The instruction reframes the difficulty. The person was treating the slipperiness as evidence that they were doing it wrong. The chase reframes the slipperiness as the relationship the feeling is in: it hides, you pursue. There is nothing to fix. The hiding is what the feeling does.

It also breaks the seriousness. Most attempts to give loving attention to shame are reverent, careful, almost solemn. The reverence is part of why the shame stays hidden — it feels like another version of "this is bad and must be addressed correctly." Play loosens the grip. The shame is not in danger from a chase. It is in danger from solemn extraction.

The move borrows from how love actually works with children in shame. When a child feels shame and the caregiver moves to give them love, the child often resists. Hides. Kicks. The instruction reverses the assumption that the child should accept love passively. The love can be playful, persistent, and unembarrassed by the hiding.

"It's what kids do, by the way. It's totally what kids do. If kids feel shame and you go to give them love, they just fucking hate it. No, no, no. Like little ones, they'll fight you tooth and nail over it." -- Joe Hudson, Allowing Yourself to Change

## Dialogue Example

- **Brett:** "It feels like when I try to give my shame loving attention, it somehow misses. Like the attention misses the shame in some way. The shame manages to wiggle out from under the attention."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, chase it around the room. I know that sounds weird, but chase it around the room trying to give it love. Like, I'm going to love you, you can't get away from me, I'm going to love you."
- **Brett:** "I can feel it there subtly."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, it's what kids do, by the way. It's totally what kids do."
- **Brett:** "Yeah, it's like hiding under the couch and peeking out through the skirt."
- **Joe:** "Yes, that's right. That's right, exactly."
- *(Source: Allowing Yourself to Change)*

The person's image ("hiding under the couch and peeking out through the skirt") is the sign the move landed. The feeling has been re-perceived as small, alive, and shy rather than as evasive and resistant.

## What It Produces

The shame becomes contactable. Not by being caught, but by entering into a different relationship with it. The pursuit is the contact.

It also dissolves the self-criticism about not being able to feel the feeling correctly. The slipperiness is no longer evidence of failure. It is what the feeling does. There is nothing to do better.

Often, after the chase, the feeling actually slows. It was running from solemn correction. Once the correction is gone, the running is also unnecessary.

## Tone

Playful, warm, light. There is humor in the instruction. The line "I know that sounds weird" acknowledges the strangeness and gives permission to do it anyway. The energy is the energy of an adult playing tag with a child, not the energy of a therapist explaining technique.
