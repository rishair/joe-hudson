# Apologize to Your Team for Not Seeing the Help They Have Been Giving

## The Move

The leader who feels alone at the top is operating from a childhood-installed abandonment imprint that is no longer accurate to their current reality. The team is actually trying. The coaching move has three parts, delivered in sequence:

1. **Name the old truth and the new one in the same breath.** "You had to be resourceful and self-reliant. You were alone as a kid. You are not alone now."
2. **Point them to the evidence.** Their team is actively trying to make them successful. Right now. Today. Probably nobody on the team is failing to try.
3. **Have them apologize to the team for not seeing it.** This is the vulnerable act. Not a confession of being a bad leader. A recognition that the team has been showing up and the leader has not been letting it land.

"I would say, 'I know you had to be resourceful and you had to be self-reliant and you were alone as a kid, but you're not alone now.' And if you're looking for evidence, look around at all the people who are trying to make you successful, who -- they might not be able to live up to every one of your expectations, but it's probably impossible to find anybody who's not trying to live up to your expectations, who's not trying to make it work for you and for them. And so take a look at that and then apologize to them for not recognizing it. And that would be the vulnerable act. And then see how much more inspired they are to be there with you and to show up with you because they see your humanness instead of are scared of you." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Over Perfection

## When To Use It

The signal is the felt loneliness of the leader who is also surrounded by people. The position is full of people, and the person inside it reports feeling alone. The contradiction is the diagnostic.

Specific triggering presentations:

- "Nobody around me really gets it. It's lonely at the top."
- "I have to hold all of this myself."
- "My team is fine, but they don't really show up."
- "I keep waiting for someone to step up and they don't."
- Any CEO or senior leader who describes their team's contributions in terms of what they did not do, almost never what they did.

The deeper marker: the leader is using the loneliness as evidence of how exceptional their burden is, rather than as data about a perception that needs updating.

## What This Replaces

The default leader response to felt loneliness is to either:

- Push harder, prove more, take on more (extending the same self-reliance pattern), or
- Withdraw further (treating the loneliness as confirmation that no one can be trusted).

Both responses reinforce the imprint. The move replaces both with a vulnerable, undefended apology that breaks the loop in a single act.

## The Two-Move Structure: First the Internal Reframe, Then the External Act

The internal piece is the reframe: "You are not alone now." This is not motivational; it is corrective. The leader has been carrying an outdated reality. The reframe corrects the present-tense map.

The external piece is the apology to the team. Not a private internal recognition. An actual conversation in which the leader says something like: "I have been treating this as if I were alone in it. I have not been seeing how much you have been showing up. I am sorry I did not see it." The apology is what converts the internal reframe into a relational change.

The apology does not require listing all the ways the team has shown up. The witnessing is in the act of apologizing for the not-seeing. The team will fill in the rest.

## Why It Works

Two mechanisms.

**The team experiences being seen.** People who have been showing up for a leader who did not register it have been working without acknowledgment. The apology is acknowledgment in compressed form. The shift in the team's energy is often immediate.

**The leader experiences their own humanness landing.** The team has been afraid of the leader because the leader was performing invulnerability. The leader has been afraid of being seen because being seen would expose the loneliness as a perception rather than a fact. The apology lets both fears drop in the same move.

"See how much more inspired they are to be there with you and to show up with you because they see your humanness instead of are scared of you." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Over Perfection

The team's inspiration is not the apology's purpose. It is its byproduct. The purpose is the dissolution of the imprint that has been running the loneliness.

## What the Apology Sounds Like

Some workable framings, in the leader's own voice:

- "I have been treating this like I am alone in it. I have not been letting myself see how much you have been here."
- "I owe you an apology. You have been trying to support me and I have not been recognizing it."
- "I have been operating as if I had to do all of this by myself. That is not actually what is happening. I am sorry I have not been seeing what you bring."

What the apology does not contain: a long list of grievances against the team that the leader is now retracting, a performance of vulnerability designed to elicit reassurance, or a sweeping apology for being a bad leader. See [[upright-apology]] and [[apologize-for-trying-to-control]] for the shape of clean apology.

## Tone

Steady, undefended, slightly slowed-down. The move only works if the leader is actually feeling the recognition. A performed version of this apology will land as another performance. The leader's body has to be in the apology.

The cleanest version is often quiet. The leader who is loud in the apology is often still managing the moment. The leader who is quiet has dropped underneath the management.

## What This Is Not

**Not a confession of incompetence.** The leader's self-reliance produced real results. The apology is not for the results. It is for the failure to see the others who were part of producing them.

**Not a reversal of the leader's authority.** The leader is still the leader after the apology. The team does not interpret the apology as a power vacuum. They interpret it as an invitation to bring more of themselves. The structural authority is intact; what changes is the affective dynamic around it.

**Not a one-time event.** The apology may need to be made more than once. The imprint is old. The pattern of not-seeing will re-arise. Each apology weakens the pattern.

**Not a substitute for the underlying work.** The imprint that produced the loneliness usually traces to childhood. See [[reclaiming-abandonment]] and [[trauma-mapping]]. The move releases the present-tense expression. The deeper work addresses the source.

## Boundary Conditions

The apology only lands if the team has, in fact, been trying. If the team genuinely is checked out, neglecting the work, or actively undermining the leader, the move is the wrong move and lands as denial. The diagnostic question for the coach: is the leader's loneliness story accurate to the current team, or is it the old story playing on the new tape?

Most of the time -- in Joe's framing, "probably impossible to find anybody who's not trying" -- the loneliness story is the old tape. The team is trying. The leader is not letting it count. The move releases the not-letting.

When the team genuinely is failing the leader, the work is different: it is about hiring, performance management, and the cultural conditions that produced the disengagement. The lonely-CEO apology is not that work.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a leader presents [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] in a form that includes felt loneliness in the role.
- When a CEO describes the position as inherently isolating and treats the isolation as a feature of leadership rather than as a perception.
- When [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]] has surfaced the original imprint and the present-tense expression needs a specific intervention.
- When [[trauma-mapping]] or [[reclaiming-abandonment]] has identified the childhood source and the leader is ready to update the current reality.
- When [[vulnerability-in-the-workplace]] is being introduced and the leader needs a concrete first move that is contained and learnable.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[vulnerability-in-the-workplace]] as the broader practice the move is part of.
- Toward [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] when the loneliness was being maintained by suppressing fear.
- Toward [[connection-doesnt-require-the-other]] when the leader needs to understand that connection was always available, the perception of isolation was the block.
- Toward [[reclaiming-abandonment]] when the imprint needs to be addressed at the source.
- Toward [[fear-as-signal-of-care]] when the loneliness reveals what the leader actually wants from the team.
