# Count the Years of Showing Up

## The Move

When working with a client who has been the receiving end of a long-term [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic|chase configuration]] — the partner has been pursuing connection for years and the client has been unable to let it land — ask the client to quantify the duration of the partner's pursuit. Then use the number as the receiving stimulus.

The sequence:

1. "How many years has he been chasing you for connection?"
2. Client gives the number.
3. "[Number] years he's fucking shown up for you."
4. "Let it all the way down. You've got it like at your heart, but like all the — let it all the way down."
5. "That's the biggest fucking compliment anybody could give you."

The instruction in Joe's words:

"22 years he's fucking shown up for you. Yeah, let it all the way down. You've got it like at your heart, but like all the — let it all the way down. Yeah, that's the biggest fucking compliment anybody could give you." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The move works because duration is undeflectable. A specific compliment can be doubted ("you're just saying that"). A specific moment of love can be discounted ("but he was angry the next day"). The duration of consistent showing-up across years and decades cannot be argued with the inner critic's usual tricks. The number is the number. The years are the years.

## When Joe Uses It

The move requires:

- The client is in a long-term relationship where the partner has been the consistent pursuer
- The client can name the duration in years
- The client has not yet been able to let the partner's love fully land in the body
- Other compliment-receiving moves have been tried and have produced partial reception at best
- The client is in a state where they can do somatic work, not just intellectual analysis

The move is most powerful after the client has already done some receiving work in the session. The receiving channel needs to be at least partly open before the duration-evidence can land. Delivered to a fully closed channel, the move bounces.

## The "Let It All the Way Down" Cue

A specific component of the move is the somatic instruction to let the recognition land deeper than the heart-level. Joe's observation that the client has "got it like at your heart, but like all the" — the cue tracks the client's body. The recognition has lodged at one level (heart) and needs to settle further (whole body, gut, lower body).

This is the precise somatic work the move is doing. The client has accepted the recognition partially. The partial acceptance is a stopping-point that needs to be moved past. The "let it all the way down" instruction asks the client to drop the recognition into the part of the body that has been the longest holdout.

The cue is read-specific. The coach has to be tracking where the recognition has lodged in the client's body. The instruction "let it all the way down" only works if there is a "down" to go to — that is, if the recognition has lodged somewhere shallow and the body has more depth available.

## Why Duration Works Where Intensity Doesn't

A high-intensity love-act (a grand gesture, a passionate declaration) can be deflected. The receiver can find a reason it was performative, or out of character, or a mood. The deflection-impulse has the high-intensity moment as a target it can argue against.

Duration does not give the deflection-impulse a target. "He has been showing up every day for 22 years" cannot be argued against in the same way. The deflection would have to assert that 22 years of consistent showing-up is not really showing-up, which is absurd. The deflection-impulse runs out of moves.

This is why the move sometimes lands when nothing else has. The cognitive-deflection apparatus has been doing its job for years, and the duration-evidence simply outflanks it. The client is left with the fact of 22 years and no way to make it mean less than it means.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The client softens visibly
- Often tears, or the felt sense of being overwhelmed
- The client may say "that's overwhelming" or similar — the expansiveness is the love that has been being deflected starting to land
- The body shifts to receive the recognition that has been blocked
- The client is now positioned to receive what the partner has been offering in real time

The "that's overwhelming" response is the diagnostic. The overwhelming is the love arriving at a quantity the system has not been sized for. The work is to keep the channel open without backing off, while pacing the inflow. See [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]] for the pacing principle.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client reports being overwhelmed: Joe stays. The overwhelming is the work, not a problem. He may name it: "Oh, it's fucking amazing, but it's overwhelming." The acknowledgment that the overwhelming is appropriate (rather than wrong) is what lets it continue.

If the client deflects with "but he wasn't always there" or similar: Joe acknowledges and reframes. See [[theres-always-a-way-to-find-the-lack-of-truth-in-a-compliment]]. "Even if there was a moment, you've also stuck it out for 20-something years."

If the client cries: Joe stays with the tears and gives the body time. The grief is for the years the client could not let it in.

If the client lights up with the felt sense of being loved across years: Joe uses this as the bridge to the next layer — often [[redirect-from-being-seen-by-them-to-seeing-yourself]] when the client says "he's the only one who's truly seen me." The receiving has now happened. The next work is to bring the seeing back to the self.

## Dialogue Example

After working through the compliment-deflection and the apology to the husband for the chase configuration:

- **Joe:** "What happens if you fully allow — instead of letting the compliment in, what happens if you fully allow the fact that your husband has always been there for you? Even if he's fucked up, he shows up the next day there for you. Like, yeah. How many years has he been chasing you for connection?"
- **Client:** "22."
- **Joe:** "22 years he's fucking shown up for you. Yeah, let it all the way down. You've got it like at your heart, but like all the — let it all the way down. Yeah, that's the biggest fucking compliment anybody could give you."
- **Client:** "That's overwhelming. Yeah, yeah, yeah."
- **Joe:** "It also means he sees something in you that you don't see in yourself."

*(Source: If You Can't Accept Compliments)*

The exchange shows the move's full arc: ask for the number, name the duration, instruct the body to let it land deeper, name the magnitude, watch the response. The "that's overwhelming" is the move landing. The follow-up pivot to "he sees something in you that you don't see in yourself" opens the next layer.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not a guilt trip.** The move is not "look how much he's done for you, you better appreciate him." That framing installs shame, which is the structure that has been doing the deflection. The move is about the client receiving what is there, not about the client owing something.
- **It is not a substitute for working on the actual relationship.** The receiving in the session is one piece. The actual relationship work — the live receiving when the partner offers love, the practices that change the dynamic — still has to happen.
- **It is not always indicated.** If the partner has not actually been consistently showing up, the move does not fit. The duration evidence has to actually be there.
- **It is not for fresh relationships.** The move works because of accumulated duration. In a relationship of two years, the move may still apply but with less force. In a relationship of months, it is not the right move.
- **It is not the same as compliment-deflection work in general.** This move uses the specific lever of duration. The general compliment work uses many other levers. Both can be in play in the same session.

## Tone

Direct, slightly emphatic, with weight on the duration. Joe's "22 years he's *fucking* shown up for you" carries conviction — the swear word is doing work, marking the magnitude. The tone is not soft; it is grounded. The client needs to feel that the coach is naming something significant, not just performing recognition.

The "let it all the way down" is delivered more quietly. The instruction is somatic. The voice has to slow down to match the work being asked of the body.

The "that's the biggest fucking compliment anybody could give you" is again emphatic. The duration is being weighted as the most consequential compliment the client could ever receive. The framing is intentional: the move is also a compliment-receiving move, with the duration-evidence as the compliment.
