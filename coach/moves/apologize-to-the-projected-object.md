# Apologize to the Thing You Have Projected Onto (Money, Body, Food, the Work)

## The Move

Once a person has seen what they have been projecting onto a thing (money, their body, food, their work, a relationship), have them apologize to the thing directly, in their own words, for the projection they put onto it.

The form: "I just want you to apologize to [the thing] for the projection you projected, in whatever words that you -- that are most aligned in you. Like, how would you apologize to [the thing] for the projections that you put onto it?"

The apology is not metaphorical. It is spoken to the thing as if to a being. The thing is given the dignity of having been distorted by the projection, and the person takes the responsibility of having been the one doing the distorting.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a person has seen, in the moment, that the thing they have been struggling with is not what they thought it was. Money is not the problem; the projection onto money is the problem. The body is not the problem; what was projected onto the body is the problem. See [[money-as-surrogate]], [[falling-in-love-with-projections]].
- After the surrogate has been surfaced (often via [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]] or [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]]). The apology lands the recognition into a relationship rather than leaving it as an insight.
- When the person has been at war with the thing for years and the war is what needs to end. The apology is the formal ending of the hostilities.
- When the person has made the thing carry the weight of an original wound (often a parent's love or absence), and the thing has had no way to be what it actually is because of what was loaded onto it.

## What It Produces

**A felt softening toward the thing.** Once the apology is spoken, the person's relationship with the thing changes. Money stops being a moral problem and becomes just money. The body stops being the enemy and becomes the body.

**Recognition of what the thing has been teaching.** When the projection is removed, the thing reveals what it has been doing all along. "What a teacher money has been. I mean, there's no way money could have taught you so much if it wasn't seeing you on some level or reacting to some truth in you." (Joe Hudson, What's Bad About Money?) The thing has been showing the person their own pattern. The apology lets that teaching be received.

**Often a deeper surfacing.** The apology often surfaces what is actually the most important thing in the person's life that they had pushed away. Joe's follow-up question after the apology to money was: "What's really the most important thing in your life?" The person named family, kids, the people she loves. The apology cleared the projection so that the actual priorities could speak.

**An end to the cycle.** The person has been treating the thing as adversary, projection screen, salvation, or curse. The apology stops the cycle. The relationship can now be something else.

## Joe's Sequence Around the Apology

1. **Surface the projection first.** The apology only works after the projection has been seen. Asking someone to apologize to money before they have seen what they have been doing to money makes no sense to them.
2. **Invite the apology in their own words.** "In whatever words that are most aligned in you." Joe does not script the apology. The person finds the language. The language is part of the data.
3. **Let it land.** Do not rush to interpret. The apology is its own event.
4. **Name what the thing has been teaching.** "What a teacher money has been." The reframe completes the move: the thing was not the problem; the projection was. Now the thing can be seen for what it actually was.
5. **Move to what is actually most important.** Often, when the projection is off the thing, the actual most important thing in the person's life surfaces. Joe asks for it and lets the person name it.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "I just want you to apologize to money for the projection you projected, in whatever words that you -- that are most aligned in you. Like, how would you apologize to money for the projections that you put onto it?"
- **Guest:** "Sorry for making you the most important thing in my life."
- **Joe:** "And because that being seen is painful, whatever the most important thing in your life is something that you make something that you have to earn and therefore push it away."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, very much so."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. What's really the most important thing in your life?"
- **Guest:** "My kids. My family, the people I love."
- *(Source: What's Bad About Money? Coaching Session)*

The apology surfaced the actual pattern: she had made money the most important thing as a way to avoid making the actual most important things the most important things. The projection had been doing the work of pushing away what she most cared about, because being seen by what she cared about was painful.

## Why It Works

Three mechanisms run together:

1. **The apology takes responsibility without shame.** See [[upright-apology]]. The person is not flagellating; they are acknowledging their part in the distortion. The thing receives the acknowledgment as repair.

2. **Direct address ends the proxy war.** As long as the person was fighting "the system" or "rich people" or "what money does to you," they were not facing what they were doing. Speaking to money directly collapses the proxy and puts them in relationship with the thing.

3. **Apologies open the heart.** The body posture of apology is open, not defended. The person cannot apologize from a closed heart without the apology feeling false. The opening that the apology requires is also the opening that allows the new relationship.

This is the relational form of [[falling-in-love-with-projections]]. Instead of fighting the projection, the person addresses what they have done to the thing they projected onto. The fighting was the projection-maintenance machinery. The apology takes it down.

## Tone

Gentle, dignified. Joe asks for the apology as if asking someone to do a tender thing. Not as a technique. The thing has been carrying the projection for a long time. The apology is a meeting between the person and what they have been doing.

The follow-up tone, after the apology lands, is often slightly amazed. "What a teacher money has been." Joe is not flattering the person; he is recognizing what the relationship has actually been. The thing taught the person about themselves by reflecting their pattern. The recognition is the natural close of the move.

## Variations

The move is portable to any projected object:

- **To money:** "I'm sorry for making you the bad guy / the savior / the most important thing / the source of my safety."
- **To the body:** "I'm sorry for treating you like the enemy / for hating you / for not listening to you."
- **To food:** "I'm sorry for using you to comfort me / punish me / fill what isn't food."
- **To the work:** "I'm sorry for making you the measure of my worth / the reason I can't be present / the excuse to not feel."
- **To a person who is gone:** "I'm sorry for making you carry what wasn't yours / for blaming you for my parents / for using you to avoid myself."

The structure is the same: the person addresses the thing they have been projecting onto, takes responsibility for the projection, and finds whatever words feel most aligned.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not write the apology for them.** "I'm sorry for projecting my abandonment trauma onto you" is the coach's language, not the client's. The apology has to come in the person's own words to land. The coach offers the prompt; the person finds the language.
- **Do not allow the apology from a slumped, shame-filled body.** This makes it self-flagellation in disguise, which reinforces the cycle. See [[upright-apology]]. The body has to be upright for the apology to be repair rather than re-injury.
- **Do not use the apology as a technique to make the projection go away.** The apology is a meeting, not a maneuver. If the person is apologizing in order to be done with the thing, the projection is still operating; only its surface has changed.
- **Do not skip what the thing was teaching.** Stopping at the apology without naming what the thing has been showing the person leaves the relationship incomplete. The full move is: apology, then recognition of the teaching, then the new relationship.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person has just seen the surrogate they were placing on a thing. See [[money-as-surrogate]].
- A person has been at war with something (their body, money, food) for years and the war has not produced freedom.
- A person has just done [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]] and the substitution has revealed what was actually being wanted.
- A person describes a thing in highly charged terms (savior, curse, enemy, source of all problems).

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward naming what the thing has actually been teaching the person about themselves.
- Toward the question "what's actually most important to you?" -- often the apology to the surrogate clears the way for the actual priorities to be spoken.
- Toward [[money-is-impersonal]] or the impersonal version of the relationship in whatever domain.
- Toward [[falling-in-love-with-projections]] as the broader principle the move operationalizes.
- Toward the deeper pain that the projection was protecting from. Often the projection was a way of not feeling something underneath; once the projection is addressed, that something becomes available.
