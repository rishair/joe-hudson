# Double the Freeze

## The Move

The person is in freeze. They are trying to get out of the freeze. The trying-to-get-out is making the freeze worse. The move is to instruct them to do the opposite -- deliberately double the freeze, then triple it, then quadruple it, until the freeze exhausts itself.

"The other kind of hack move -- hack as in it's a hack, like it can speed it up -- is to double your freeze, triple your freeze, quadruple your freeze. Because what that is doing is it's not resisting." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The move targets the nervous-system layer of stuckness specifically. It is not the same as the felt-emotional amplification of [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]]. That move amplifies a feeling. This move amplifies the physical freeze state itself -- the body's stillness, the held breath, the bracing, the not-moving.

## When Joe Uses It

This move comes when:

- The person is in physical freeze (held breath, bracing, immobile, can't move)
- The person has been trying to get out of the freeze and the trying is making it worse
- The freeze is at the nervous-system layer, not the emotional or cognitive layer
- There is a felt physical quality of holding-still that can be deliberately amplified
- The person has enough trust to follow the paradoxical instruction
- Standard "relax" or "let go" instructions have not worked

The diagnostic test for whether this is the right move: ask the person to try to relax. If the trying-to-relax is itself the activity that is holding the freeze in place, the paradoxical move is the right one. If they can actually relax when invited to, no need for the doubling.

## What It Produces

A series of stages similar to the felt amplification, but with the freeze state itself as the material:

1. The person is uncertain. The instruction feels wrong. "I'm trying not to freeze and you want me to freeze more?"
2. The person attempts the doubling. The body braces more, holds more, becomes more still.
3. The bracing has a ceiling. The body cannot sustain more freeze than it is already producing.
4. The freeze exhausts itself. Like a muscle held in tension too long, it releases when the holding can no longer be sustained.
5. The body moves on its own. Breath returns. Movement returns. The frozen state has run its course.

What is most often produced: a small somatic release -- a sigh, a tremor, a settling. The freeze has done its full motion and the body is free of it for now. The mechanism is the same as physical exhaustion of any held position; the body cannot hold what it was just instructed to hold more of.

## Why It Works

The freeze is being maintained by the activity of resisting it. The system is spending energy holding the freeze just-far-enough-away to be a "problem" rather than allowing the freeze to complete. As long as the resistance is operating, the freeze persists.

"The thing about the person who was stuck and feeling trapped is typically any kind of resistance makes it more and more and more stuck." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The doubling instruction removes the resistance. The person is now doing the freeze instead of resisting it. The freeze converts from chronic-and-held into deliberate-and-completable. The completion produces the release.

This is structurally identical to the psoas release: tire the muscles around the psoas through exertion, and once they are exhausted, the psoas itself releases the tension it has been holding. The freeze, exhausted through deliberate amplification, releases the held charge.

"Sort of like the psoas release exercises where you lean up against the wall in certain ways and it tires out all the muscles around your psoas muscle, which is the biggest muscle in your body that holds a lot of tension and trauma. And once you've finally exhausted all the muscles around it, you can lay down and put your knees up in a certain way that your psoas just shakes and releases a lot of held tension." -- Brett Kistler, How To Get Unstuck

"Or there's all sorts of modalities where you push up against something for a while and then you have the bigger stretch from it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

## The Alternative First Move: "I Wonder When I'll Thaw"

Before the doubling, the simpler version of the same principle: drop the resistance and accept the freeze as a temporary state.

"So you have one option. You can go, oh, look, I'm frozen. I wonder when I'll thaw. I can just relax and check this out, and you'll thaw quicker for sure." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The "I wonder when I'll thaw" stance is the relaxation form of non-resistance. It works for many freezes. When it doesn't work -- when the person genuinely cannot relax into the freeze because the relaxation itself becomes effort -- the doubling-hack is available.

The two moves are siblings. The first is the long-form, the second is the hack. The first works through allowing; the second works through exhaustion. Both are non-resistance routes.

## The Resistance-Adds-Pressure Frame

Joe and Brett surface the deeper structural reason this works:

"You could even say that the stuckness is putting pressure on yourself." -- Brett Kistler

"Yes." -- Joe Hudson

"And if you are trying to resist the stuckness, you're adding more pressure." -- Brett Kistler

"Exactly." -- Joe Hudson

"So you're just fighting the part of you that's fighting yourself harder." -- Brett Kistler

The structural reframe: stuckness is self-applied pressure. Resisting it is more self-applied pressure. The route out is not more pressure (effort to get unstuck) but less pressure (allowing or amplifying the existing pressure until it releases itself). The doubling move makes this concrete -- by amplifying the pressure deliberately, the system surfaces the pressure as a known quantity, which it can then put down.

## What Joe Does Next

Once the freeze releases, the system has access to whatever was being held underneath. Often this is one of:

- An emotion that the freeze had been protecting from being felt (most commonly anger, see [[anger-first-then-fear-out-of-stuck]])
- A movement impulse that the freeze had been blocking
- A clear sense of the next action
- Sometimes simply the body's normal animation returning

Joe stays present with whatever surfaces. If it is an emotion, the work moves to letting that emotion complete. If it is a movement, the work moves to honoring the movement. The doubling was the door; what's on the other side is the work.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not catastrophizing or worsening. It is not asking the person to make their situation worse. The amplification is purely somatic -- freeze more in the body that is already frozen -- not narrative.

It is not the same as the felt-amplification of [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]]. That move targets a feeling that is being held just-below-tolerance. This move targets a physical freeze state. The two can be deployed in the same session at different layers.

It is not appropriate for every frozen-feeling state. A person in acute panic-freeze (sympathetic shutdown after high activation) needs regulation first. The move is for the chronic, held, stuck-style freeze rather than the acute trauma freeze. The diagnostic: is the freeze a held position the body has been maintaining (use this move), or is it a crash from overload (use regulation moves instead)?

It is not a substitute for the emotional and intellectual work that may follow. The doubling addresses the nervous-system layer. The emotional layer (anger and fear that need to move) and the intellectual layer (recognizing the self-oppression) are separate work that may be needed too. See [[anger-first-then-fear-out-of-stuck]] and [[feeling-stuck-is-self-oppression]].

## The Three-Layer Frame for Stuckness

In Joe's framing in the source, stuckness gets worked at three layers, each with its own moves:

1. **Nervous system layer:** double the freeze (this move) or "I wonder when I'll thaw"
2. **Emotional layer:** move the anger, then the fear; see [[anger-first-then-fear-out-of-stuck]]
3. **Intellectual layer:** see that the self-oppression is happening and is being done by the self; see [[feeling-stuck-is-self-oppression]]

The three layers are not sequential or hierarchical. A coach picks the layer that has the most charge for this person in this moment. The doubling move is the nervous-system layer's primary intervention.

## Dialogue Examples

The transcript does not include a live deployment of the move; Joe describes it abstractly as part of the teaching. The shape of the deployment, based on Joe's description:

- **Joe:** "I notice you're frozen right now. I want to invite you to try something. Instead of trying to get out of the freeze, I want you to deliberately freeze more. Freeze twice as hard."
- **Person:** *(braces, holds more still)*
- **Joe:** "Good. Now freeze three times as hard. Make every muscle as still as you can."
- **Person:** *(longer holds, breath catches)*
- **Joe:** "Now freeze four times as hard. As hard as you possibly can. Don't move."
- *(After a few moments, the body cannot sustain the bracing. A release happens spontaneously -- a sigh, a small shake, the breath returning.)*
- **Joe:** "There. The freeze did what it needed to do. What's here now?"

## Tone

Light, almost playful, with a sense of "let me show you a hack." Joe is not solemn about this move. He explicitly names it as a hack -- a shortcut, a speed-up. The lightness keeps the absurdity of the instruction ("freeze more") workable. If the person is laughing slightly while doing it, that's a good sign; the comic energy is part of what loosens the held state.

Once the instruction has been given, the tone steadies. The amplification phase is delivered with calm confidence. "Good. Now more. Now more." Steady voice, no hurry. The system needs to know that the coach is with it through the amplification.

When the release happens, the tone shifts to spacious quiet. The coach gets out of the way and lets the body do what it is doing. Words at this moment would interrupt the completion.
