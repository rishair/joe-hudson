# Become the Authority Figure

## The Move

In a coaching session, when the person has identified the kind of authority figure that triggers them and has just located a new stance (often [[upright-undefended-love]] or empowerment), Joe steps into the authority role and lets the person practice the new stance live.

"I'll be the authority figure. You see what happens. You tell me, speak from that place and see what happens." -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

He does not act out a specific person. He delivers the kind of communication the person described as triggering. In the source session, it was the patronizing "good job" from the boss. Joe delivered: "Thank you for that. That really lets me know that you're ready for leadership soon."

## When Joe Uses It

After the person has done several things:
1. Identified the pattern (often with an authority figure who reminds them of a parent)
2. Located the new stance somatically and named it
3. Indicated they want to test it but the dynamic feels too abstract to address only in talk

The move is not used early. It is used when the person is ready to embody a new response. Done too early, it becomes performance. Done at the right moment, it produces the felt experience of the new pattern arriving in the old situation.

## What It Produces

Almost always, an immediate test of the new stance. The person sometimes finds it and sometimes loses it. Both outcomes are useful.

When the person finds it, they often describe the response as scary and good simultaneously. "I have been treating you like a person in my head instead of the person who's here. I haven't given you a chance to get to know me."

When the person loses it, they typically slip back into one of the original two stances: fight ("but you're going to misinterpret that") or roll-over (a small collapse). Joe then names the slip and lets them try again.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Whatever the person says in the new stance, Joe responds in character. He delivers the patronizing response, the dismissive response, whatever the original trigger was. Then he checks: how did that land from this new stance?

Critical: Joe does not soften the trigger. The whole point of the role-play is to give the person the actual experience of holding the new stance under the original pressure. If the role-play is too kind, the practice is not real.

"And it did hurt. Yeah. And it is absolutely fine that you don't see the way it hurt. It's okay." -- (person responding from the new stance, Father Figures and Authority)

When Joe hears that response, he stops. "That was perfect." The empowerment is in the capacity to feel the hurt while still being upright, not in avoiding the hurt.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "I'll be the authority figure. You see what happens. Speak from that place."
- **Person:** "I have been treating you like a person in my head instead of the person who's here. I haven't given you a chance to get to know me."
- **Joe (as authority):** "Thank you for that. That really lets me know that you're ready for leadership soon."
- **Person:** "Ah, ouch."
- **Joe:** "How much of a relief, how much of joy is there in that pain?"
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. Joe delivers the trigger, then asks the person to stay in the new stance with the pain.)*

## Tone

Joe takes the role-play seriously but lightly. He flags the transition ("I'll be the authority figure"), delivers the role-play line cleanly, then returns to his own voice. He does not perform a character. He uses just enough to give the person the felt sense.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not use this early in a session. The person needs the new stance located first.
- Do not soften the trigger. A too-kind authority figure fails to produce the practice condition.
- Do not stay in role too long. The role-play is a moment, not the rest of the session.
- Do not analyze the role-play immediately. Let the felt experience land before processing.
