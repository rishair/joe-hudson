# Hire by Asking 'What's Your Dream Job?'

## The Move

When hiring someone for a role, ask: "What's your dream job?" If their answer is not close to the job you have, do not hire them.

The move is the inverse of selling the role to the candidate. The leader is not trying to convince anyone to take the job. They are filtering for the candidate whose own desire already matches the work.

"I basically say, what's your dream job? And if they're not really close to the job that I have in mind, it's not a good fit. I'd rather have somebody whose dream job it is to do the job that I have in mind than to convince someone to do something, because eventually I'm going to have to manage them." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

## Why It Works

The logic is the [[management-required-to-launch-predicts-management-required-forever|investing principle]] applied to hiring. If you have to convince someone to take the job, you will have to convince them to do the job every day after that. The convincing does not stop at hiring. It becomes the ongoing relationship.

If their dream job already matches the role, no convincing is needed. The hire happens because both sides see the fit. The work happens because the worker actually wants it. The management overhead drops to near zero.

This is the operational version of [[inherent-goodness]] applied to hiring. People who want to do the work will do the work. The leader's job is to find them, not to motivate them.

## What the Move Filters Out

The move filters out three common bad-fit categories:

**The candidate who wants a different job but is willing to take this one.** Often a financially-motivated hire. The candidate is competent and the role is acceptable, but their actual ambition is somewhere else. Predictable outcome: they leave when something closer to their dream appears, or they stay and underperform because their attention is elsewhere.

**The candidate who is convinced into wanting it during the interview.** The convincing is a red flag. If they did not want it before the interview, they will not want it after the honeymoon. The leader's energy in the interview cannot be sustained as a daily management practice.

**The candidate who does not know what they want.** "I'm open to anything" usually means there is no internal pull toward the work. The candidate may be competent and the job may be acceptable, but the engagement that comes from wanting the work will not be there. The leader will be the engine.

## What the Move Selects For

The candidate who lights up when describing the role. Whose dream-job answer maps cleanly onto what is being offered. Whose own ambition is the engine the leader does not have to provide.

The signal is not "this person has the right credentials." It is "this person already wants what I am offering." Credentials can be wrong even when right. Want is rarely wrong about itself.

## The Investing Parallel

Joe describes the same principle in his investing work: the amount of management it took to make a deal happen was the amount of management it would take to keep the deal working. Deals that closed with flow and ease continued with flow and ease. Deals that required heavy push to close required heavy push forever.

"It's something that I learned in making investments. The amount of management that it took to make a deal happen was the same amount of management that I would have to consistently provide to make the deal work." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

The same logic applies to hiring. The hire that requires convincing will require ongoing convincing. The hire that closes itself will run itself.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not asking for the candidate's "ten-year vision." That question invites performance answers. The dream-job question, asked directly and casually, invites a more honest answer.

This is not screening for ambition or aspiration in general. The question is specifically about the work being offered. A candidate who wants to be CEO is not necessarily a good fit for a non-CEO role, even though their ambition is admirable.

This is not refusing to negotiate compensation or to make the role attractive. The leader can still negotiate. But the fundamental fit question is answered before the negotiation. A negotiation cannot create fit that was not there.

## Tone

Direct and curious. Asked early in the interview, before the leader has pitched the role. The order matters. If the leader pitches first, the candidate's answer is partly informed by the pitch. If the candidate answers first, the answer is closer to the truth.

The leader's response to a poor match should be matter-of-fact. "It sounds like this isn't quite what you're looking for. Let's not waste more of each other's time." The plainness is part of the move. There is no need to apologize for the non-fit. The non-fit is information, not a verdict on the candidate.

## When to Use This Move

- For any role where the work requires sustained engagement (most knowledge work).
- For roles where the worker will have meaningful autonomy and the leader cannot watch every action.
- For roles where the cost of a bad hire is high.
- For roles where the leader is choosing between several competent candidates and needs a deeper filter than competence.

## When Not to Use This Move

- For purely transactional, well-specified work where the worker's engagement matters less than their compliance with the spec.
- For roles where the leader has no choice and any competent person will have to do.
- For roles where the work itself is unattractive enough that no candidate's dream job would include it. In these cases, the move correctly tells the leader that the role design is the problem.

## The CEO Sibling: Empower the Hiring Manager Rather Than Veto

A related move at the CEO or senior leader level: interview every candidate, but rarely veto. The senior leader's job in interviews is to offer perspective and observations to the hiring manager, not to make the call. The decision belongs to the person who will manage the new hire.

"Even now we're hiring a bunch of people. We're an early-stage startup. And I interview everybody that's coming on the team. But very rarely do I veto. Like, I've done it twice in all of the people that we've interviewed, because I'm much more here to empower you as the hiring manager to make your own decisions. And I'm just going to give you perspective on here are some things I think are going to be important as they onboard. Here's some challenges you may have with them as you're managing them, whatever the kind of insights are that I see." -- Jaime Waydo, Putting My Needs First

The move is initially disorienting for the hiring manager. They expect the CEO to decide. "They would look at me and they'd be like, 'well, should we hire this person or not? You interviewed them. You're the boss.' And I'm like, 'I'm not the hiring manager. I'm not deciding. Here's what I see.'"

Holding that line is the move. The CEO who keeps deciding teaches the team that the CEO is the decision point. The CEO who gives observations and refuses to decide teaches the team that they are the decision point. The second team develops capacity the first team cannot.

This is [[dropping-roles-makes-room]] applied to hiring authority. The CEO who occupies the hiring-veto role prevents the hiring manager from developing the muscle of making the call. The CEO who drops the role transfers the muscle.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader has been making hires that look right on paper but require constant management.
- A leader is in [[enjoy-over-manage|management mode]] with their team and is wondering whether the team or the management is the problem.
- A coaching conversation has surfaced that the leader is convincing people into roles and then frustrated when the people need ongoing convincing.
- A leader is about to make a hire and is feeling the urge to sell the role hard.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- A team composed of people who actually want their roles.
- Lower management overhead as the natural fit replaces the need for ongoing motivation.
- Toward [[enjoy-over-manage]]: this move is one of the structural decisions that makes flow possible.
- Toward [[management-required-to-launch-predicts-management-required-forever]]: the principle this move operationalizes.
- Toward [[inherent-goodness]] applied to team-building: trust that the right people exist; find them rather than convince others to be them.
