# I Don't Buy Your Story

## The Move

When a person presents a defining feature of themselves -- their identity, their problem, their core limit -- as a fixed truth, the move is to refuse the frame directly. Not to argue with the content. Not to soften it. Just to say, in some form, "I don't buy that."

The dismissal is calibrated. It is not about the person. It is about the story they are telling about the person. The frame they have brought is being declined.

"What I said to them apparently, in the way that I can be a dick sometimes, is that I basically said, 'I don't buy your story.' Just like I dismissed what for them was like a defining feature of them apparently. And I was just like, I just don't buy it. I don't buy that story. Or in my world, it would've been limiting belief. Like I just didn't buy the limiting belief." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## When Joe Uses It

When someone presents a limiting belief as obvious fact and is asking how to navigate inside the belief rather than questioning it. Brett describes this dynamic well:

"That's really one of the common things that I see when you're doing sessions with people, it's, it's challenging those beliefs. And often I see people coming to you with sort of a, how do I approach this challenge in my life, but within my belief system, and then you're just like, eh, eh, like batting them aside and people are like, no, no, no, no, no."

The signal is the person bringing a question that presupposes the belief. They are asking "given that X is true about me, how do I do Y?" The belief X is the thing the move targets. If the person were genuinely uncertain about X, the move would not be needed. The certainty is what makes the move appropriate.

## What It Produces

Almost always: anger or upset first. The person has built their self-concept around the belief. Refusing it feels like an attack on them. The anger is the system defending the architecture.

"It really, I think, pissed her off. It's hard to describe, but I wasn't there for her experience of it. But I think she was definitely challenged by it. I think definitely angry." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

Then, often, an unfolding. The challenge to the belief opens the possibility that the belief might not be the whole story. The opening can be substantial.

"And then it led to like this huge unfolding for her as challenging any limiting belief does." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## Why It Works

The move bypasses the inquiry process. Inquiry is slower and more participatory: the coach helps the person question their own belief. The "I don't buy it" move skips the help. It puts a single point of refusal directly across from the belief.

This is sometimes the only thing that produces movement, because the inquiry process can itself be absorbed into the belief structure. A person can do hours of inquiry and end up more sophisticated about the belief without dissolving it. The direct refusal does not engage the belief on its terms.

It also works because it is honest. Joe actually does not buy the story. The move is not a technique; it is a report. The honesty is part of what challenges the person -- they are encountering a real reaction, not a coaching script.

## How Joe Calibrates It

The move is not appropriate for every limiting belief. It works when:
- The belief is presented as a defining feature of the person.
- The person is articulate and engaged enough to hear it.
- The relationship has enough ground to absorb the challenge.
- The coach actually does not buy the story (not a performed dismissal).

It is inappropriate when:
- The person is fragile and the belief is holding necessary protection in place.
- The belief is connected to a recent loss or trauma that needs grief, not challenge.
- The coach has not earned the standing to do this move with this person.
- The coach is using the move to discharge their own irritation with the person.

## What It Looks Like in Practice

The move is short. Often a single sentence.
- "I don't buy that."
- "I don't think that's true about you."
- "That's a story you're telling, not a fact."
- "I don't buy your story."

The brevity is essential. Long explanations turn the move into an argument. The argument can be won by the person defending the belief. The single sentence cannot be argued with on its own terms; it can only be sat with.

The follow-up is silence, or a question that opens what the person might actually be. "What would it mean if that weren't true?" "Who would you be without that story?"

## Anti-Pattern Form

The move can degenerate into cruelty if it becomes a coach's signature. "I'm the coach who calls out everyone's bullshit" is itself a limiting belief structure dressed in coaching language. The move works when it is used sparingly, in response to the actual situation in front of the coach, not as a coaching identity.

See [[fixing-people]]: the move is not about fixing the person, even though it can look like that from outside. It is about declining a particular frame the person has brought.

## Tone

Direct, blunt, occasionally dickish (Joe's own description). Not cruel. The directness is delivered cleanly, without preamble or apology. The willingness to be perceived as harsh is part of what makes it work.

"In the way that I can be a dick sometimes, is that I basically said, 'I don't buy your story.'" -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

The "in the way I can be a dick sometimes" is important. Joe is not performing kindness around this move. The directness has an edge to it. The edge is acknowledged rather than smoothed over.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents a limiting belief as a defining feature of themselves.
- When someone is asking for help inside their belief rather than questioning it.
- When inquiry-based approaches keep getting absorbed into the belief structure.
- When the person's certainty about the belief is the central thing holding it in place.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward the person's anger or pushback, which the coach must stay with.
- Toward an unfolding once the anger settles, often substantial.
- Toward [[inquiry-on-limiting-beliefs]] for the slower work that follows the initial challenge.
- Toward [[deconstruct-the-victim-to-release-anger]] when the belief is structured as victimhood.
- Toward [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] when the belief is structured as obligation.
