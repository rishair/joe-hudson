# Prove to All of Us Why It's So Important

## The Move

When someone has loaded a task or goal with extreme importance, and the importance is what is making the work impossible, the move is to make them defend the importance out loud. Not to argue against it. To force them to articulate it.

The articulation does the work. Said aloud, the load that has been silent and assumed becomes audible, and its actual weight becomes available for inspection. The person tends to hear the disproportion before the coach has to name it.

Joe's setup framing matters: "Now I'm going to make you really uncomfortable." The discomfort is announced because it is intentional. The move is going to expose something the person has been protecting.

> "Great. Now I'm going to make you really uncomfortable. Prove to all of us that it's important, your website."
>
> "That it's important."
>
> "Yeah, it's so fucking important that it's not fun and you don't do it. So prove to us why it's so fucking important." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The phrasing is specific. Not "tell me why it's important" but "prove to us." The "prove" framing positions the person as having to argue a case. The "us" framing imports a witnessing audience. Both move the person from internal feeling to public articulation. The shift is what makes the load audible.

## When to Use It

- The person has been defending the importance of a task as the reason they need to do it
- The importance is producing the felt experience that is causing the procrastination, paralysis, or perfectionism
- [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] has surfaced as the mechanism but the person is still inside the importance
- The person has been protecting the importance from inspection ("but it really is important")
- The bounded-procrastination diagnostic has revealed that the high-stakes work is the only work the person cannot do
- The intervention needs to land in the body, not just be heard intellectually

The move is intentionally uncomfortable. Use it when softer approaches have not produced the recognition. The discomfort is the lever — the person needs to feel the weight of what they have been loading onto the task.

## What It Produces

The most common response is a list of high-stakes reasons that, said aloud, sound disproportionate to the actual task. In the website case:

> "Because if I don't do it, then I will be working for somebody else and that I don't wanna do that anymore. And I'll, my income will be limited. And I won't get to where I want to be, and I won't be able to give my kids the lifestyle that I want them to have and be the dad that I want to be. I'll be stuck." -- (Source: Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes)

The list is real. Each item is real. But the cumulative weight, placed on the act of building one niche website, is structurally absurd. No single piece of work can carry that much — and the body knows, which is why the body refuses to begin.

The person often hears the disproportion as they speak. They do not always need it pointed out. The articulation itself produces the recognition.

## Joe's Follow-Up: Name the Cost

Once the person has articulated the load, Joe compresses the result into the principle that exposes the structural cost:

> "You are stuck."
>
> "I know."
>
> "It's so important that it makes you stuck." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The compression — "it's so important that it makes you stuck" — is the moment the move lands. The person had been holding the importance as the engine of action. The compression reveals that the importance is the engine of paralysis. The exact thing they were preserving (by defending the importance) is the cause of the problem they brought in.

This compression is the verbal form of [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] applied to importance-loading. The voice that demands the importance is producing the opposite of what it demands.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person produces a list of disproportionate stakes:** Joe lets them finish, then compresses. "It's so important that it makes you stuck." The compression names the structural cost without arguing with any specific item on the list.

- **If the person defends each item ("but it really does matter"):** Joe does not argue with whether the items matter. He stays at the structural level. Yes, the outcomes may matter. Loading them onto this specific act of work has produced the stuckness, not the outcomes. The importance is the mechanism preventing the outcomes, not delivering them.

- **If the person collapses into shame or self-attack ("I know, I'm so bad at this"):** Joe does not validate the shame frame. The shame is downstream of the same importance-loading. He returns to the structural read.

- **If the person tries to take on the new program "I just have to remove the importance":** Joe catches the recursive should. See [[dont-make-stop-judging-into-a-should]]. "Removing the importance" said as a new task is itself loading. The corrective is the redirect to enjoyment, not the installation of a new program.

- **If the recognition lands:** Joe moves immediately to the redirect ([[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]]). The exposure has done its work; the next move offers the structural alternative.

## What This Move Does NOT Do

It does not invalidate the person's care about the outcome. The outcomes the person named — financial freedom, providing for the kids, working for themselves — are legitimate. The move does not argue against any of them. It only exposes that loading them onto this act of work is the mechanism producing the inability to do the work.

It also does not work as a one-shot fix. The person who recognizes the importance-loading still has to do the work of redirecting their relationship to the task. The exposure is the start of the work, not the end of it. See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] and [[be-a-good-boss-to-yourself]] for what follows.

The move is not appropriate for someone in active overwhelm or shame. It is a confrontation that requires the person to have enough nervous-system bandwidth to hear the exposure without collapsing. Used at the wrong moment, the discomfort it produces becomes the new problem instead of becoming the lever.

## Variants of the Move

The "prove to us" framing is one variant. Other forms of the same move:

- "Tell me everything this work is supposed to deliver."
- "Make the case for me. Why does this matter so much?"
- "If you didn't do this, what specifically would not happen?"
- "What does this work have to be carrying for you to procrastinate on it like this?"

Each form invites articulation. The "prove" framing is most confrontational; the "tell me what it has to carry" framing is softest. Calibrate to the person's nervous system and the room.

The related question [[what-was-x-going-to-get-you]] is a softer cousin of this move. It asks the surrogate-endpoint question — what was the loaded thing supposed to deliver. The "prove why it's important" move is more direct: it skips the surrogate analysis and demands the articulation in real time.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "Now I'm going to make you really uncomfortable."
- **Person:** "Okay."
- **Joe:** "Prove to all of us that it's important, your website."
- **Person:** "That it's important."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, it's so fucking important that it's not fun and you don't do it. So prove to us why it's so fucking important."
- **Person:** "Because if I don't do it, then I will be working for somebody else and that I don't wanna do that anymore. And I'll, my income will be limited. And I won't get to where I want to be, and I won't be able to give my kids the lifestyle that I want them to have and be the dad that I want to be. I'll be stuck."
- **Joe:** "You are stuck."
- **Person:** "I know."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, for a long time."
- **Person:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "It's so important that it makes you stuck."
- **Person:** "Yeah."
- *(Source: Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes)*

The whole exchange takes roughly thirty seconds. The articulation, the compression, the recognition. The move is short because its mechanism — making the unspeakable weight audible — does not require length. It requires precise timing and the willingness to introduce the discomfort.

## Tone

Direct, slightly provocative, but warm underneath. The "now I'm going to make you really uncomfortable" framing is honest about what is happening. Joe is not pretending the move is friendly. The provocative language ("so fucking important") signals that the move is going to be vivid; the person needs to be able to hear it. The warmth comes through in the implicit message: I am doing this because you are stuck and the move will help.

The "all of us" framing — bringing a witnessing audience into the move — adds a small theatrical dimension. The person is being asked to defend their case to a public. This is part of what makes the articulation produce the recognition: in front of witnesses, the disproportion becomes harder to defend than it would be in solitary thought.
