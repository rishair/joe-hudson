# Welcome the Pattern by Naming Its Brilliance

## The Move

When the person identifies a behavior pattern they want to get rid of (the comparative mind, the self-criticism, the defensiveness, the perfectionism), don't help them get rid of it. Model the welcome out loud, with the brilliance of the pattern named.

The template:

> "I welcome my [pattern]. It helps me [function it serves]. There's a brilliance in it."

In the source session, the pattern was the comparative mind. The person identified it as the problem. Joe modeled the welcome:

> "I welcome my comparative mind. It helps me be good in work. There's a brilliance in it."

Three parts:
1. **"I welcome my [X]."** Spoken in the first person, modeled by the coach. The person hears the language they can use.
2. **"It helps me [function]."** The specific service the pattern provides, named concretely. Not "it's part of me" but "it does this useful thing."
3. **"There's a brilliance in it."** The acknowledgment that the pattern is intelligent, not defective. The pattern earned its place.

The person is then invited to repeat the welcome, or simply to receive it and notice what changes.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person identifies a pattern they think is the obstacle to self-love or to the work, and asks how to remove it
- When the person is in self-attack about a part of themselves (the inner critic, the comparative mind, the controlling part)
- After [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] has surfaced a specific pattern that keeps pulling the person out of welcoming
- When the more general "love the part" or "honor the pattern" framings need to become a concrete spoken move the person can run themselves

## What It Produces

- Immediate softening of the relationship to the pattern. The pattern stops being the enemy.
- The person often laughs or visibly relaxes. The "brilliance" phrasing in particular tends to land — the person had been treating the pattern as a defect, not a brilliance.
- The pattern itself doesn't disappear. The relationship to it changes, which is what unfreezes it. See [[love-is-the-best-change-agent]].
- The person gains a usable form of the welcoming move they can run on other patterns in other moments.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person can receive the welcome and laughs or softens:** stay there. Let the receiving happen. Don't immediately move on.
- **If the person can repeat the welcome with the body softening:** great. Notice the body change with them. Anchor it.
- **If the person says the words but the body stays braced:** the welcome is being performed. Slow down. Have them say it again with attention to the body. Or: drop the words and just notice what the pattern is afraid would happen if it were welcomed.
- **If the person can't believe the brilliance is there:** name the function more specifically. The comparative mind helps them be good in work. The self-criticism kept them safe in a critical household. The perfectionism produced the achievements that earned them love. The function is always real. The brilliance is in noticing that the pattern was solving a real problem.
- **If the person wants to attack the pattern again after the welcome:** ask what need wouldn't get met if the welcoming replaced the pattern. See [[whats-the-need-that-wouldnt-get-met]].

## Dialogue Example

The compressed sequence from the source session:

- **Guest:** "I think about what you've said about being in a comparative mindset, and I think about how often I do that."
- **Joe:** "So can you welcome that?"
- **Guest:** "I can't, right?"
- **Joe:** "I welcome, I welcome my comparative mindset. It helps me be good in work. There's a brilliance in it."
- **Guest:** "That's great. Wow."
- *(Source: How Do I Love Myself?)*

Two things to notice. First, when the guest says "I can't, right?" Joe doesn't argue. He models the move. The model is the answer to the question. Second, Joe's modeling includes the specific function ("it helps me be good in work") that ties to the person's actual life, not a generic "it serves you."

## Tone

Warm and matter-of-fact. The "I welcome my comparative mind. It helps me be good in work. There's a brilliance in it." is delivered the way one might describe a useful tool. There is no special reverence around the move. The simplicity is the teaching.

The "wow" or "that's great" response from the person is not the point. The point is the modeling. The person hears a language for the welcoming that they did not have. They can take that language and use it on other patterns later.

## Variations

- **For the inner critic:** "I welcome my inner critic. It's trying to keep me safe from the criticism it expects from others. There's a brilliance in it." See [[my-inner-critic-wont-stop]] for the concern that often surfaces this.
- **For perfectionism:** "I welcome my perfectionism. It's helping me produce work I'm proud of. There's a brilliance in it." 
- **For caretaking:** "I welcome my caretaking part. It's trying to keep the people I love safe. There's a brilliance in it."
- **For people-pleasing:** "I welcome the part of me that pleases people. It's keeping me connected, keeping me in relationship. There's a brilliance in it."

The pattern: identify the function, name it concretely, name the brilliance.

## Anti-Pattern Risk

The move can become rote — "say these words about your pattern" — without the actual receiving. The somatic check is required: does the body soften when the welcome is spoken? If not, the words are bouncing off. Slow down, drop the words, or find a more specific function the pattern is actually serving.

The other risk: naming a brilliance the pattern doesn't actually have. The comparative mind genuinely helps the person be good in work. If the coach (or AI coach) invents a brilliance ("your defensiveness is helping you set boundaries") that doesn't fit, the person knows immediately and the move collapses. The brilliance has to be real and visible. If it isn't visible, ask what the pattern is actually doing for the person before deploying the welcome.

The third risk: using the move to bypass the work of meeting the pattern in its actual shape. The welcome is not "I love every part of me equally and it's all fine." It is meeting this specific pattern at this specific moment with the recognition of this specific function. Generic welcoming doesn't reach the specific shape of the wound.
