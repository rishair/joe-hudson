# Visualize the Reality of Being a Leader

## The Move

In a group of leaders, walk them through a visualization. Have them sit into their normal meetings. Then name the social realities of being a leader that they would prefer not to look at, and ask them to feel each one in their body.

The specific realities to name:

- People are more concerned with you than with each other.
- People want to please you.
- People are rebelling against you.
- People want to make sure you are happy.
- People are passive-aggressive to you in ways they are not to other teammates.
- More conversations happen about you than about any other person on the team.

Ask the leader to allow each of these into their system as a felt experience. Not to think about whether each one is true. To feel it as if it is.

## What This Is For

The point is not to make leaders comfortable with the dynamic, or to validate the dynamic, or to teach them to manage it. The point is to surface the actual social reality of being a leader so that the leader can move from power (where they are unconsciously enjoying the deference or unconsciously suffering it) into empowerment (where they can lead from their own truth).

Most leaders do not feel into this reality. They either ignore it, become defended against it, or get unconsciously fed by it. None of these stances produces empowered leadership. Empowered leadership starts with feeling the dynamic clearly and then choosing one's stance from there.

See [[empowered-vs-power]] for the distinction this move is operating in service of.

## What It Produces

The move tends to produce a specific kind of recognition: the leader sees that they have been operating with a misread of what their people need. Often the misread is in the direction of trying to make people happy. The realization that arrives is that the people need to know what makes the leader happy, not the other way around.

Joe gives a specific case from a recent session:

"There was one person in the group had this radical realization. She was like, 'Oh, I was doing all this stuff to make people happy, and now I actually see that they need me to tell them what makes me happy for it to work.' And her whole business changed. It was crazy how quickly her business changed. It literally went like, she got rid of 2 people and then changed the way that she was leading and told an investor that they needed to settle down a little bit and started being more directive but still listening to everybody's feedback and just changed everything really quickly." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The behavioral signatures of the shift in this case: directness about wants, willingness to fire, willingness to set boundaries with investors, while still keeping the listening posture intact. The move out of power into empowerment did not produce harshness. It produced a leader who could state their actual position and stay present to the people on the team.

This connects to [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]] and [[state-the-want-not-the-action]]. The leader who has not felt the asymmetry of their position over-corrects toward managing others' feelings; the leader who has felt it can state their wants cleanly and lead from there.

## When to Use It

- A leader who is exhausted from managing people's emotions and cannot see why.
- A leader whose team is afraid of them or excessively deferent and who has not noticed.
- A leader who is operating with the assumption that they are one of the team and is unable to read why their team is not responding as peers.
- A CEO whose company is not moving because the leader is hiding from the asymmetry of their role.
- A founder transitioning from individual contributor to leader who is still operating from the old social position.
- Any leader in whom the [[empowered-vs-power]] distinction would produce immediate operational change.

## How to Hold the Move

The tone matters. The visualization is not designed to shame the leader for not seeing the dynamic. It is designed to let them feel it for the first time, often in their body, so that the response that follows is informed by the reality rather than by the leader's preferred image of the relationship.

Slow pacing. Let each named reality land before naming the next. Watch for body signals: a shift in breath, a softening, a tightening, a flinch. These are the signals that the visualization is doing its work. If a leader laughs it off or moves quickly through, slow them down and stay with the one that produced the reaction.

The shift that the move is working toward is not intellectual. It is the felt recognition of being held in a particular social position that the leader has been pretending was something else.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not a complaint about the dynamic. The dynamic of being a leader is structural. People do orient around the leader. Conversations do happen about the leader more than about others. The asymmetry is real and not avoidable by pretending it is not there.

This is also not the only place this move appears. The [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] makes the same observation about teachers and students. The structural feature — that being in a position of authority produces a specific social field around you — is not unique to business leadership. It is a feature of authority of any kind. The move adapts naturally for teachers, founders, board chairs, and anyone in a position where others orient around them.

## Tone

Direct. Slightly knowing. Joe walks leaders into this with the awareness that they will not have wanted to see it and that seeing it is what unlocks the next phase of their work. There is no apology in the delivery. The reality is the reality. The move is to let them feel it.
