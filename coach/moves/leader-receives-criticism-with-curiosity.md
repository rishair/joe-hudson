# Leader Receives Criticism With Curiosity

## The Move

When a leader is publicly criticized — in a Q&A, in a town hall, in a press interview, in a team meeting — the move is to receive the criticism with curiosity rather than defense. The form Joe gives:

"Oh wow, that's some really cool criticism. Can you tell me more? Like how would you improve the product? What do you see here?" -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The structure: name the criticism as welcome, invite more, ask what the critic would do differently. The response shifts the entire dynamic of the encounter from leader-defending-position to leader-and-critic-collaborating-on-the-thing-being-criticized.

## Why This Works

Three mechanisms operate at once:

**1. The leader stops being a target.** The defended leader provides the surface that criticism lands on. The undefended leader has no surface; the criticism has nowhere to discharge. The room shifts from "watching the leader get attacked" to "watching the leader engage with the critic." See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for the general principle.

**2. The critic is brought into collaboration.** The question "how would you improve it?" converts the critic from outside-attacker into inside-co-thinker. The critic is now offering, not attacking. The critic's posture often softens because the energy that was set up for opposition has been redirected into contribution.

**3. The room registers the leader's openness.** Everyone watching the exchange sees a leader who can hold criticism without flinching. This is more reassuring than any performance of certainty. The leader who can be challenged and stay open is the leader the room can actually trust with the next hard thing.

"They would be like very confident and very open and everybody would be like, oh, that's great." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The confidence is not in being right. The confidence is in being able to hold the question of being right without collapsing or attacking. The room reads this as a higher-order confidence than the performed kind.

## What This Replaces

The default leader responses to public criticism, all of which fail:

**Defense.** "Actually, here's why you're wrong about that." The leader doubles down on the position. The room watches a leader who cannot hear. Trust erodes.

**Collapse.** "Oh shit, my product fucking sucks, dude, do you know, like, I put fucking years of my time into this and then you don't even like it." The leader's fear is acted out. The room watches a leader who cannot hold weight. Trust erodes faster than with defense.

"Oh shit, my product fucking sucks, dude, do you know, like, I put fucking years of my time into this and then you don't even like it, you know, like, or whatever, a thousand other fear responses, they would have lost the room even further." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**Bully back.** "Who are you to ask that?" The leader attacks the critic. The room watches a leader who uses position to silence dissent. Trust evaporates.

**Compartmentalize the fear and respond flatly.** The leader manages their fear away and delivers a controlled, deflecting answer. The room senses the management and reads it as inauthentic. Trust slowly drops.

Any fear response — collapse, bully, freeze — costs the leader the room. The room can feel the leader managing fear, and the management itself is what loses the room. The only response that does not cost the room is the response that does not require fear management — receiving the criticism with genuine curiosity.

This is why the move is structural, not tactical. A leader who is trying to perform curiosity while internally defending will be detected. The move requires the leader to have done the internal work that lets curiosity actually arise in the face of criticism.

## What Has to Be True for the Move to Work

The move is not a script. The leader has to actually be curious about the criticism for it to land. Several internal conditions support this:

- **The leader does not identify with the product/position being criticized.** The criticism is about the thing, not about the person. The leader can hold the thing as not-themselves.
- **The leader is genuinely interested in improving the thing.** The criticism is potential information. The leader who is interested in the information is naturally curious about the source.
- **The leader has done enough internal work to receive disconfirmation without collapse.** See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for what this requires.
- **The leader trusts that being open will not produce annihilation.** This trust is often the result of having survived earlier moments of openness.

A leader who lacks these conditions can still attempt the move, but the attempt will likely register as performance. The move is a place to grow toward, not just a technique to deploy.

## The Variant Form: Agreement

A specific variant of the move is to fully agree with the criticism. This works particularly well when the criticism is about absolute claims or about the limits of the leader's knowledge:

"I made stuff up. I'm making this up right now." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

When a CEO challenges Joe with "you make shit up," Joe's response is full agreement, then a reframe that does not defend but recontextualizes: making stuff up is how humans create new things; the criticism is accurate and not damaging. See [[agree-when-challenged-on-the-teaching]] for the broader treatment of this variant.

The agreement variant works when the criticism contains a real observation that the leader can simply confirm. The curiosity variant works when the criticism contains an evaluation that the leader can investigate. Both are non-defense responses; the choice depends on what the criticism actually is.

## What Joe Notices About Why Leaders Struggle With This

The reason most leaders cannot do this move: they have unspoken pressure to appear certain. They believe (often correctly) that showing fear or uncertainty will cost them authority. So they manage the fear, manage the uncertainty, and respond from the managed position. The managed position has no curiosity; curiosity requires the willingness to actually not know.

"There is a very specific way in which, like if that authority figure would've said, as an example, oh wow, that's some really cool criticism. Can you tell me more? Like how would you improve the product? What do you see here? Like, you know, then they would be like very confident and very open and everybody would be like, oh, that's great. But if that authority figure went, oh shit, my product fucking sucks, dude, do you know, like, I put fucking years of my time into this and then you don't even like it, you know, like, or whatever, a thousand other fear responses, they would have lost the room even further." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The catch: any fear response loses the room. So leaders are trained that they cannot show fear. So they compartmentalize. So they cannot be curious. So the criticism lands as defense and the room registers the defense and trust erodes anyway. The move requires breaking this cycle by inhabiting a different relationship to the fear itself — not managing it but actually feeling it and responding from clarity instead. See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] for the underlying capacity.

## The Tone of the Curiosity

The curiosity has to be real. The exact words can vary:

- "Oh, that's interesting. Can you tell me more?"
- "What would you do differently?"
- "What are you seeing that I'm not seeing?"
- "Help me understand what's behind the concern."

The form is less important than the body. The leader's body has to actually be turning toward the critic with interest. A leader who delivers the words with a defended body is detected. The move works through the body first; the words follow.

The pacing matters too. Slow down. The criticism deserves a moment of registration before the response. Rushing the response signals that the response is rehearsed. Slowing the response signals that the leader is actually considering what was said.

## What This Move Produces

When the move lands cleanly:

- The critic's posture softens. They are no longer in attack mode; they are in contribution mode.
- The conversation deepens. The criticism produces an actual exchange about the thing being criticized, sometimes producing improvements the leader had not seen.
- The room's trust in the leader rises. The leader has demonstrated capacity that abstract talk about openness cannot demonstrate.
- The leader learns something. The criticism, even if partial, often contains information the leader needed to hear.
- Other people in the room become more willing to surface concerns later. The move signals that the leader is safe to bring concerns to.

When the move does not land:

- The curiosity is performed. The critic senses the performance and the move backfires.
- The leader receives the criticism but cannot follow up meaningfully because they have not actually engaged with it. The move was technique without substance.
- The critic was looking for a fight and finds none, and leaves dissatisfied rather than collaborated-with. This is fine; the move was still the right move.
- The leader uses the curiosity to deflect from making a decision. The criticism gets explored forever and never addressed. The move has slid into avoidance.

## When to Use It

- A leader in a Q&A or town hall who is being asked hard questions
- A founder pitching to investors who are pushing back on the product
- A CEO in a press interview who is being criticized for a recent decision
- A team meeting where a junior person is raising a concern about leadership
- Any context where the leader has the option to defend, collapse, or attack — and the room is watching how they respond

The move is especially important in moments where the room is watching for evidence of how the leader handles disagreement. The handling becomes the data the room uses to calibrate trust in the leader for everything after.
