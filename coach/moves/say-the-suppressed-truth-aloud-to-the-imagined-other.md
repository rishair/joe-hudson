# Say the Suppressed Truth Aloud to the Imagined Other

## The Move

When the person has been suppressing emotion that is specifically about another person — most commonly a parent suppressing anger or love toward their child — have the person say the suppressed content out loud, in the actual emotional register, repeated ten times, while imagining the person they are directing it at, and using the person's real name.

The form has two halves that are usually run in sequence:

**Half one (the suppressed negative direction):**
- "Say ten times in a row in an angry voice, 'You're not the fucking boss of me.' Think of your child. Use her name."
- "May, you're not the boss of me. May, you're not the boss of me. You're not the boss of me, May." — repeated ten times.

**Half two (the suppressed positive direction):**
- "Now go the other way. Say ten times, 'I would give up everything for you, May.'"
- "May, I would give up everything for you. I would give up everything for you, May." — repeated ten times.

The two halves together surface both directions of what has been being held back: the anger that was being managed because "I shouldn't be angry at a 2-year-old" and the heartbreak-as-love that was being managed because the depth of it is unbearable to feel cleanly.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a parent is suppressing anger toward their child because the cultural script says "good parents don't get angry at their kids." The suppressed anger is doing the work of preventing the deeper heartbreak from arriving.
- When a person has been managing both directions of an emotion toward a specific other (anger and love toward a child; anger and grief toward a dying parent; anger and tenderness toward a partner).
- After [[parenting-as-deep-tissue-massage]] has been named or is operating implicitly — when the person needs to feel the actual content of the practice rather than describe it.
- After a triage has confirmed the partnership/external relationships are not the active constraint, and the work is on the internal suppression strategy.
- When the person reports they want to "develop fluidity" — the move bypasses the developmental framing by getting them to do the fluidity in the room.
- When the live demonstration of fluidity has happened in the body (a felt shift, an energy spreading) and the next move is to give the fluidity a specific destination so it can be practiced in the relationship that produced the suppression.

## What It Produces

**Surface-level relief from the suppressed anger.** The ten repetitions usually move the held anger to the point of light embarrassment or a smile. The body has done the motion it was not allowing itself to do. The tension drops.

**A break in the "I shouldn't be angry" story.** Once the person has said it ten times to the imagined child, the abstract prohibition loses force. The person discovers that saying it does not destroy the child, does not make them a bad parent, does not even feel particularly serious. The proportion of the suppressed material to the actual consequences becomes visible.

**Light, often involuntary smile.** The contrast between the seriousness of the suppression and the silliness of the practice produces a laugh or smile. The person realizes they had been bracing against something that was much smaller than the bracing was treating it as. See [[the-smile-of-recognition]] for the related read.

**Heartbreak arriving in the second half.** The "I would give up everything for you" repetitions usually produce tears or a felt heartbreak. The person discovers they have been managing this side too, and that what was being managed is actually the love. The depth of what they are willing to give is what they have been protecting themselves from feeling.

**A felt sense of "I want that."** After the second-half repetitions, the person often reports something like "I want that" or "yes" or "whatever that is." The future they are pointing at — being the parent who has fully allowed both the anger AND the depth of love — becomes available as a felt destination, not an abstract goal.

**Both directions of the fluidity in the room.** The person who came in asking "how do I develop fluidity" has, in two sets of ten repetitions, done full anger and full love. The framework for "fluidity is something I need to develop" collapses. The next move ([[you-know-this-place-this-is-not-your-first-time-here]]) lands clean.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person smiles during the angry repetitions:** acknowledge it. The smile is the system recognizing the absurdity of the prohibition it had been holding. "What's happening in your face? I see a lot of smile." See [[the-smile-of-recognition]].

- **If the person reports feeling silly:** stay with the silliness. The silliness is the data. "I feel silly, like I'm tense for no reason around her." The work has done its job — the suppression has been revealed as disproportionate to the actual situation.

- **If tears arrive during the second half:** stay with them. Do not redirect. The tears are the heartbreak completing what the anger started. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] and [[letting-judgment-in]].

- **If the person tries to intellectualize after either half:** redirect gently to the body. The mind moving in to analyze is the same suppression mechanism re-asserting. The body has just done the work; the mind is trying to take credit or evaluate it. "Just let it land for a second."

- **If the person says "I almost don't know if it matters where I'd be in 18 years":** stay with that. The collapse of the future-orientation is the indicator the person has dropped into the present-tense felt-sense of what they want. The work has landed. See [[that-makes-sense-the-spaceless-yes-as-evidence-of-arrival]] (or similar) — the loss of future-tense as evidence of present-tense arrival.

- **If the person resists doing the practice at all:** the resistance is the data. "What just happened? What's the resistance?" The person has just shown the live form of the suppression. Work with that, not the practice itself.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, a father had reported he was clamping down on his negative emotions since his daughter was born, and asked how to develop emotional fluidity. After a brief somatic exploration, Joe ran the practice:

- **Joe:** "Do me a favor and just say 10 times in a row in an angry voice. And I want you to think of your 2.5-year-old. It's going to be fucking silly, but I want you to think of your 2.5-year-old. I want you to say, 'You're not the fucking boss of me.' And I want you to do that 10 times."
- **Father:** [conjuring her up] "What's her name?"
- **Joe:** "What's her name?"
- **Father:** "May."
- **Joe:** "May, great. 'May, you're not the boss of me.' Just say her name."
- **Father:** "May, you're not the boss of me. May, you're not the boss of me. You're not the boss of me, May." [continues ten times]
- **Joe:** "Great. What's happening in your — I just noticed a lot of smile thinking about doing — a lot of smile right afterwards."
- **Father:** "There's a little bit of, I'm in touch with the silliness because she's such a joy to be around. I feel silly, like I'm tense for no reason around her."
- **Joe:** "Great. I'm gonna do the other one now. We're gonna go the other way. 'I would give up everything for you, May,' 10 times."
- **Father:** "May, I would give up everything for you. I'd give up everything for you. May, I give up everything for you." [continues]
- **Joe:** "Let it break your heart. Let it break your heart. You don't have to be strong around this. Who would you be if you gave up everything for me — for May? Your identity, all the space you think you need, out of a deep want to, not because you had to, but because that's just the kind of father you are. Who would you be when she's 18?"
- **Father:** "I want that."

*(Source: He Discovers His Negative Emotions Aren't the Problem)*

The structure is precise. The first half surfaces the anger; the silliness shows up; the disproportion gets seen. The second half is set up by Joe with a longer instruction ("let it break your heart, you don't have to be strong, who would you be") that turns the second set from a parallel repetition into a deepening. The "I want that" is the destination Joe is reading for — the future self the person actually wants, made available as a felt sense, not an abstract goal.

## Tone

Direct, light, slightly mischievous in setup. The "it's going to be fucking silly" preface is doing real work — it gives the person permission to do the thing without bracing for it being serious. The instructions are short. The repetition count is specific (ten, not "a bunch"). The use of the child's name is non-negotiable; the name is what makes the abstract suppression land on a specific target.

In the second half, the tone shifts. The instructions become longer, slower, more invitational. "Let it break your heart" is delivered with weight. The pace of the language slows. The space around the repetitions widens. The first half is technical; the second half is sacred.

When the felt shift arrives — the smile, the tears, the "I want that" — Joe acknowledges it briefly and moves on. The acknowledgment lets the recognition land. The over-marking would re-engage the mind that just dropped out.

## Why This Move Works

The suppression is being held by an abstract prohibition ("I shouldn't be angry at my child," "I shouldn't be that consumed by love"). The prohibition operates by keeping the felt content away from the specific person it is about. The moment the felt content is directed at the specific person, by name, ten times, the prohibition has to either dissolve or become visibly absurd. Most prohibitions dissolve.

The two-direction structure (anger then love) does two things at once:
- It demonstrates that both directions have been being managed by the same mechanism. The person who can say "I would give up everything for you" ten times after saying "you're not the boss of me" ten times has touched the full emotional range in two minutes.
- It surfaces the heartbreak the anger was protecting against. The anger was the easier direction to feel because it has a defensive function. The love is the more vulnerable direction because there is no defense in it. Running the anger first creates the somatic permission for the love to come next.

The mechanism is structurally similar to [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]: the body needs the motion before the feeling arrives, and the performance of the motion makes the channel available. The difference is the direction of address. The fake-the-anger practice is generic; this move is to a specific person, by name. The specificity is what makes the suppression visible as the held-against-this-specific-other thing it actually is.

## Variations and Considerations

- **The child variant.** The form documented above. Use the child's actual name. The "you're not the fucking boss of me" line works because it surfaces the developmental indignity of being run by a small person whose needs are unrelenting — exactly the felt experience the parent has been suppressing.

- **The dying parent variant.** "I'm furious that you're leaving" and "I would give anything to keep you." Same structure, different content. Surfaces both the anger at the impending loss and the love that is producing the anger.

- **The estranged family variant.** "I'm so angry at what you did" and "I love you, mom" (or appropriate). Surfaces both the unfelt anger and the love that has been hidden underneath the anger. This variant requires more care — see [[dont-skip-wonder-and-jump-to-love-when-oppressed]] for when the love-direction is premature.

- **The partner variant.** Less common but available. The partnership context usually has more direct channels, but in cases where the suppression has been operating chronically and the person cannot say either thing in real life, the practice can clear the channel.

## When NOT to Use This Move

- When the suppressed material is trauma that needs slower titration. The ten-repetition direct address can be too much, too fast. See [[compartmentalizing-can-be-adaptive]] for the principle.
- When the person is in a freeze-state and cannot actually do the motion. The instruction lands on a body that cannot execute it. The fake-it practice ([[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]) may be the prior step needed.
- When the imagined other is someone the person is actively dissociated from and the practice would re-engage the dissociation rather than the felt content. See [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] for the relevant read.
- When the person is using the practice as a way to feel they have done the work, without actually being in the body. The performance without the body is its own version of [[manufacturing-the-feeling]].
