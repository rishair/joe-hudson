# So They Attacked You. How Do You Want to Be?

## The Move

A person presents the complaint that their partner is attacking them. The natural move is to validate the attack, explore how it feels, work the partner's behavior. Joe's move is to do none of that. He acknowledges the attack flatly and immediately pivots: "Yeah, so who gives a shit? How do you want to be in the face of this attack?"

The move bypasses the entire frame in which the question "they are attacking me" is supposed to lead anywhere productive. The frame is itself the problem. As long as the person's authenticity is contingent on the partner's behavior, the partner has already won. The move returns the agency to the only place it ever lived: in how the person chooses to be.

## What Triggers the Move

The move is for the specific moment when a person presents a long-standing complaint about being attacked, criticized, or wronged by a partner -- and the complaint has been on a loop. They have been telling themselves and others about the attacks for months or years. The complaint is no longer producing movement. It is producing more complaint.

Joe's session with the client who felt attacked all the time by her husband:

"Working with a client, and the client was saying how she felt like she was being attacked all the time by her husband. And I was like, yeah, so who gives a shit? And she -- I remember her looking at me like, what?" -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The look of "what?" is the marker that the move has landed. The person has been operating on the assumption that the work was to address the attacks. The move declines that work and points at the work that is actually available.

## Joe's Reasoning

"I'm like, yeah, but like, so he's attacking you. If you are different than your authentic self because of an attack, they've won. He's won. You're like, the only thing that you have to focus on is being authentic in the face of that attack. 'Cause otherwise, like, you're already out of yourself. You've already, you know, like, lost, so to speak. So how do you wanna be in the face of this attack?" -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The logic:
1. The person's authenticity is the only thing they have full agency over.
2. The attack is the partner's behavior, not the person's.
3. If the person modifies themselves -- becomes smaller, harder, defended, performative -- because of the attack, the person has lost their authenticity.
4. The loss of authenticity is the actual harm. The attack itself is not the harm.
5. Therefore the work is not to stop the attack. The work is to stay authentic regardless of whether the attack continues.

The "who gives a shit" is not callousness about the partner's behavior. It is the dismantling of the conditional clause -- "I can only be myself if they stop attacking" -- that is keeping the person small.

## What Joe Adds About the Apparent Attack

Joe also notes that the "attack" is usually not an attack at all. It is the partner's defense, which the person is reading as an attack. See [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]].

"It's most likely the way it kinda works in relationships is someone thinks they have something to defend, the other person sees that defense as an attack. They think they have something to defend, and then the first person sees that defense as an attack. And both people thinking they're defending themselves and both people feeling attacked. That's typically how it works." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The reframe is not delivered first because the person is not ready to hear it. The "who gives a shit" arrives first and clears the way for the structural reframe to be useful. If the structural reframe were delivered first, the person would defend the rightness of feeling attacked and the move would not land.

## What the Move Is Not

It is not dismissing the person's experience. The "who gives a shit" is about the consequence of the attack, not about whether the attack happened. The implicit acknowledgment: yes, they attacked you. The redirect: that does not determine you.

It is not "rise above it" or "take the high road." The move is not about moral superiority. It is about recovering agency.

It is not a denial of the option to leave. The move opens the question of how to be, not the question of whether to stay. If, in being authentic, the person discovers the relationship cannot hold them, the answer about leaving will become clear. See [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]].

It is not gendered or sided. The move applies regardless of who is presenting. The "attacker" is also doing this dance from inside their own scared head.

## What the Move Produces

The person often goes quiet. The framing they had been carrying -- "I am the victim of this attack" -- has been declined without being argued against. The decline opens the space for a new question: not "what did they do to me?" but "what am I doing right now?"

What often surfaces next:
- The recognition that they have been holding back significant parts of themselves for fear of the partner's reaction
- The recognition that the partner's behavior would be much harder to maintain against a fully present person
- The terror of actually being themselves in the face of the partner's reactivity
- The data that the "attack" has been keeping them in a configuration that was already producing the suffering they were attributing to the attack

The next move is usually [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]] or [[what-if-you-acted-exactly-how-you-want]].

## When the Move Does Not Apply

The move presumes the person has some capacity to be authentic and is not in actual danger. In situations of literal abuse, the move is wrong. The work there is safety first.

The move also presumes the person is in a long-running configuration of complaint. For a fresh wound -- a partner just betrayed them, the attack is new and shocking -- the move would land as dismissive. Different work applies there.

## Dialogue Pattern

The move's compressed form, when it lands:

- **Client:** My husband is attacking me all the time.
- **Joe:** Yeah, so who gives a shit?
- **Client:** What?
- **Joe:** He's attacking you. If you're different than your authentic self because of the attack, he's won. The only thing you have to focus on is being authentic in the face of the attack.
- *(Source: How Relationships Reveal Us)*

The "what?" is doing critical work. It is the moment the assumed frame breaks. The teaching is what follows.

## Tone

Blunt. Almost rude on the surface. The bluntness is the technology. A softer version of the move -- "well, but his behavior is his behavior" -- would not break the frame. The "who gives a shit" is the precise dose of impertinence required to dislodge the assumption that the attack is the determinative fact.

Joe delivers the move from real care. The bluntness is not contempt for the client. It is contempt for the configuration the client has been trapped in. The care arrives implicitly through the recovery of agency that the move produces.

## Connected Concerns and Reads

- A person whose entire account of the relationship is the partner's wrongs
- A person locked into the [[fear-triangle]] victim role with the partner as bully
- A person whose authenticity has gone underground because the partner's reactivity made it costly
