# Keep Asking 'What's the Problem With That?'

## The Move

The person describes a feared outcome. Instead of solving the outcome, ask: "What's the problem with that?" When they answer, ask again. And again. Keep asking until the answer is no longer a story or a strategic concern but a felt fear, sadness, or heartbreak.

The move is recursive. The same question, repeated, drills down through layers of explanation until it reaches the core feeling.

"What if you never utilized your potential? What would be the problem with that?"
"What makes you feel bad to not utilize your potential?"
"What's the problem with not doing what you could have done?"
"What's the problem with your talent getting wasted?"
"What's the problem with not living a fuller life?"
"I know it sounds like we're going nowhere, but we're going somewhere. Like, go to the basic core of: you didn't do anything. Nothing happened. You didn't fulfill anything."

## When To Use It

The person has presented a fear or concern in narrative form. The narrative has layers — a feared outcome, the consequence of the outcome, the meaning of the consequence. The person is in the story. The actual fear underneath is not yet named.

Use the move when:
- The person presents a concern as a strategic problem ("how do I figure out X").
- The person describes the consequences of an outcome without naming what feels bad about it.
- The answer to "what's the problem" produces another story rather than a feeling.
- The person is articulate and able to keep generating reasons — the articulateness is the avoidance.

Do not use the move when:
- The person is already in a felt emotion. Recursive questions interrupt the feeling.
- The person is in trauma or dysregulation. The drilling can overwhelm.
- The person has already named the core fear. The descent has already happened.

## What It Produces

After three to six repetitions, the person hits the basic core. The story collapses. What remains is a single felt emotion — usually fear, sadness, heartbreak, or shame. The voice often drops. The body often shows the shift before the words do.

The answers move through a predictable arc:
1. **Outcome.** "I wouldn't excel at things I could do well."
2. **Meaning.** "I would not live a fuller life."
3. **Self-judgment.** "I would have wasted my talent."
4. **Felt feeling.** "I fear" / "I feel sad" / "Heartbreak."

The last answer is the actual material. Everything before it was the story protecting the speaker from the feeling.

## What Joe Does With the Response

When the felt feeling finally arrives, the move stops. The next move is to be with the feeling — to invite it, to feel the body's response, to notice what wants to happen. The drilling was to reach the feeling. Once reached, the feeling is the work.

Watch for: the person trying to escape the feeling back into more story. The recursive question can be returned to if needed, but usually by this point the descent has done its work and the person is willing to stay with what surfaced.

## Warning the Coachee

Joe sometimes flags the recursive nature of the question to head off the felt sense that the conversation is going nowhere. The acknowledgment lands as orientation, not apology.

"I know it sounds like we're going nowhere, but we're going somewhere. Like, go to the basic core of you didn't do anything. Nothing happened. You didn't fulfill anything."

The naming gives the coachee permission to keep descending. Without the naming, the person can interpret the repetition as the coach not understanding, and try to explain the concern in a new way. The naming says: "I do understand. Stay with the descent."

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "How do I utilize my potential to the fullest? Because I don't know that myself."
- **Joe:** "What if you never utilized your potential? What would be the problem with that?"
- **Person:** "I feel very bad."
- **Joe:** "What makes you feel bad to not utilize your potential?"
- **Person:** "I feel like I would not live up to what I could have done in this life."
- **Joe:** "What's the problem? What's the problem with not doing what you could have done?"
- **Person:** "I think I feel like my talents would get wasted."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, what's the problem with your talent getting wasted?"
- **Person:** "It means that I didn't live a fuller life."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, what's the problem with not living a fuller life? I know it sounds like we're going nowhere, but we're going somewhere. Like, go to the basic core."
- **Person:** "I would feel like I did not excel at the things that I could do well."
- *(Source: How Do I Live Up To My Potential?)*

In this exchange, six repetitions of the question moved the person from "how do I utilize my potential" (a strategy question) to the edge of the felt fear. The next move was [[catch-i-feel-like-as-not-a-feeling]], which produced the word "fear."

## The Compressed Two-Turn Form (For Approval-Seeking Variants)

The drilling move also has a compressed form that runs in just two turns. It applies when the presenting concern is built around a "just" — "I'm just trying to be heard," "I just want to be liked," "I just want to belong." The "just" is doing the work of the prohibition; the drilling exposes that the underlying behavior is not actually wrong, and that the prohibition has no defensible basis.

- **Guest:** "How do I stop questioning if I'm showing up just to have my voice be heard, or if I truly have the thing to say?"
- **Joe:** "Oh, cool. What's the problem with showing up just to be heard?"
- **Guest:** "Maybe I feel like I don't have valuable input. It's not valuable if it's just me trying to get my voice heard or to be acknowledged."
- **Joe:** "What's wrong with just wanting to fucking be seen and being not valuable? What's the problem?"
- **Guest:** "I don't know why I think it's a problem."
- *(Source: How Do I Stop Caring What Others Think?)*

Two iterations of the move dissolved the prohibition. The person who came in asking how to verify their worthiness exited that frame entirely — "I don't know why I think it's a problem" is the structural sign that the drilling has reached the bottom of the unexamined prohibition. From there the session moves to [[the-importance-itself-is-the-selfishness]] and [[simultaneous-reception-is-uncontrollable]] rather than continuing to drill.

The compressed form works because the prohibition was thin to begin with. When the presenting concern is built around a "just" or a moralized "selfishness," the structure is already half-collapsed. The drilling only has to show that the prohibition has no defended base. Two turns are enough.

The longer-form descent (six repetitions toward the felt feeling) is for narratively-defended fears where the person has built a logical structure on top of the feeling. The compressed form is for moralistically-defended wants where the person has only built a shame on top of the want. The same move, calibrated to the depth of the defense.

## Tone

Patient, level, persistent. The same question, said the same way each time, builds the drilling rhythm. The energy is "I am with you, we are going somewhere, keep going."

The repetition is not aggressive. It is not interrogation. It is more like a coach holding the door open as the person walks through it, level after level.

## Why This Works

Most concerns are presented several layers above the felt fear. The person has already done the work of explaining the concern to themselves in terms that feel rational — "I should be doing more," "my talents would be wasted," "I'd regret it." Each of these is a story standing on top of the actual feeling.

Trying to address the top-level story leaves the underlying feeling untouched. The strategies that solve "you should do more with your life" do not resolve the fear that produced the question. They produce another version of the same loop.

The recursive question forces a descent the person cannot make alone. Left to themselves, they keep producing explanations at the same level. The question is "what's the problem with that" not "why" — because "why" invites another explanation. "What's the problem" demands the consequence, and consequences eventually terminate at a felt experience.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't accept the explanation as the bottom.** When the person says "I'd regret it" or "I'd be unhappy," that is not the bottom. The bottom is the felt emotion, not the felt outcome.
- **Don't add your own framing.** "It sounds like you're afraid of disappointing your parents" closes the descent prematurely with a coach-generated story. The descent has to be produced by the coachee, not interpreted by the coach.
- **Don't avoid the move because it feels repetitive.** The repetition is the work. Avoiding it because it feels uncomfortable for the coach abandons the coachee mid-descent.
