# Take Five Counter-Actions Immediately (The Act Step of the 4 A's)

## The Move

After announcing, apologizing, and asking for help, take five different contrary actions to the old pattern, as close to immediately as possible. Five matters. Immediate matters. Cleanness emerges through the reps.

This is the fourth step of the [[four-as-of-behavior-change|4 A's]] and the one where the recognition gets installed in the body via concrete action.

## Why Five (Not One)

The default move is to take one big counter-action, usually with the most loaded person, in the most consequential context. This sets up failure.

"What people will do is they'll take like one contrary action, they'll be very scared about it. They'll take that action and they'll do it in the exact place where they are the least likely to get the positive result. You know, so, okay, I'm gonna be honest with somebody, so it's gonna be my mom, you know, and it's gonna be around the thing... 'And I'm gonna tell her she looks fat in those jeans.'" -- Joe and Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The single action carries the entire weight of the change. If it goes badly, the whole project feels invalidated.

Five does three things one cannot:

**1. Five gives variety in difficulty.** Some are easy. Some are hard. You can start with the easiest, which lets you find the cleanness of the new behavior in a low-stakes context before you take it to the loaded one. "You can start with the easiest ones because those are the ones you're going to be most clean on. Typically, Mom, you're fat and ugly is probably like the hardest one." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**2. Five forces a real survey of the pattern.** The mind wants to claim the pattern is only in one or two places. If you commit to finding five, you have to actually look at how pervasive the pattern is. The looking is itself diagnostic work.

"A lot of times what your brain will do is, I can only find 1 or 2. And it's like, there's no way. If you're dealing with a pattern, you're going to see it everywhere. And so part of finding the five is you have to actually dig through the gunk to actually see the pattern more clearly." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**3. Five is reps.** The new behavior gets installed in the body through repetition. One rep is not enough for the system to file the new pattern as available. Five reps in close succession start to feel like "this is what we do now."

"Taking contrary behavior on 5 of them quickly is like doing reps. It's like, oh, I know how to do this now. I, I, I can do it." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

This is the operational form of [[change-behavior-to-change-consciousness]] applied specifically to interpersonal patterns. The 5-of-the-4-As is doing the same work as the 10-times experiment of that principle, scaled to the specific structure of post-recognition interpersonal work.

## Why Immediately

The recognition is fuel. It fades. Done in a day (or close), the reps consolidate the change before the recognition can dissipate. Spread across weeks, the recognition cools and the reps lose their punch.

"I just like went through and I looked... and I lined up 5 things that I had taken responsibility for and wrote single emails to each person... I did all of those emails basically within the same day." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The immediacy is also part of why the front-loading of [[announce-widely-the-behavior-change]] and [[upright-apology]] matters. By the time you reach the act step, the social field is prepared. You can act into it now without the field still pushing back.

## Doing Them Simultaneously

Joe's preference is to do all five (or as many as possible) at once or in close succession, so you can compare across cases what happened.

"Typically I like to do them all as simultaneously as possible so I can actually see what's the response. Oh, 4 out of 5 worked out really well, or none of these worked out. What is it that I have to do differently?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The cross-case comparison is its own data. If four landed cleanly and one didn't, you can look at what was different about the one. If none landed cleanly, you can look at what you were doing across all of them. The simultaneity gives you the diagnostic.

## What "Clean" Means

Cleanness is the quality of the action. Open-hearted, not defensive, not pre-armored. Just stating or doing the thing the pattern had been preventing, without the prior pattern's compensations baked in.

"When I say cleaner, what I mean is I'm not saying it in such a way that I'm looking for an attack or trying to avoid an attack or making a bunch of excuses or hedging a bunch or being really strong and hardcore about it so that nobody's gonna hurt me in it. Just, oh, I'm open-heartedly saying the thing that is the contrary action." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The first rep is rarely clean. You will overcorrect, or hedge, or come on too strong. The second rep is cleaner because you felt what the first one was missing. By the fifth, you usually have something pretty clean. The reps teach you cleanness — you can't get there by planning.

## The Feeling-Through Inside Each Action

Each counter-action requires feeling through the fear the pattern was avoiding. Not just intellectually acknowledging the fear; actually feeling it as you take the action.

"There is a lot of emotion that goes through doing that stuff because you are now facing the fear. You're facing the thing like, oh, this is where I'm gonna potentially get rejected. Oh, this is where I'm gonna potentially get attacked. You have these patterns because you're trying to avoid an emotional experience. And every time you take the contrary action, you have to live through the fantasy that that negative emotional experience is gonna happen and maybe even feel the negative experience once or twice." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The negative experience landing once or twice is information, not failure. It teaches you what wasn't clean. The cleaner versions reduce (but don't eliminate) the negative experience.

"When you feel the negative experience, it teaches you how to do it cleaner so that if you do it cleaner, then it's less likely that you get the negative experience." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The sequence inside each action: imagine the action, feel everything that comes up, grieve what needs to be grieved, then take the action. Then the next one.

"Imagine doing it, feel all the shit. Grieve everything you gotta grieve. Imagine doing the second one, feel everything you've gotta feel, grieve everything you've gotta grieve. And then do them." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## The Messiness Allowance

Because you asked for grace in step 3 ([[ask-for-help-with-specifics]]), the actions are allowed to be messy. The first attempts will overshoot or undershoot. You will overcorrect. You will mistake one form of the new behavior for another.

The recovery move when an action lands wrong: notice the overcorrection ("you know what, I've maybe overcorrected and this isn't the way I wanna be either"), name what you actually want, and then take the next five actions, getting cleaner. The cleanness compounds across batches.

"Then you just have to take the next 5 actions and you're just like getting cleaner and cleaner at it. They're never gonna be perfectly clean, but the cleaner they're gonna be." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The window-of-tolerance framing matters: because the wide announcement and upright apology and specific ask have already created a field that expects messiness, the messy first reps are absorbed rather than catastrophized. The field has been prepared.

## What This Is Not

- Not a single bold act intended to prove the change. The single act is the failure mode. Five.
- Not a checklist to complete. Each action requires feeling the fear underneath it. Rote completion of the five is not the move.
- Not white-knuckled willpower. The recognition is the fuel; the actions ride on that fuel. If you find yourself forcing the actions, the recognition probably wasn't there in the first place. See [[dont-run-the-four-as-from-a-should]].
- Not "do five hard things to prove your commitment." Start easy. Move up. The hardest one usually goes best after you've gotten clean on three or four easier ones.

## When to Recommend This Move

- After the first three A's are complete (announce widely, upright apology, specific ask).
- When a client says "okay, I see it, but I don't know how to actually change" and the recognition is fresh.
- When the consciousness-side work has produced the insight but the body hasn't caught up.
- When the pattern is interpersonal and the field has been (or is being) prepared by announcement.

## What This Move Produces

Often produces a felt experience of "oh, I can actually do this." The body files the new behavior as available. The pattern starts to feel less compulsory.

Often also produces unexpected emotion as the avoided feelings finally meet the air: grief about how long the pattern was in place, fear that turns out to be smaller than the imagined version, sometimes joy at the cleanness of the new behavior.

The downstream behavior shift is often visible within days when the move is executed cleanly. Not always permanent on the first round — most patterns require multiple rounds of five — but the change becomes self-evidently possible after the first batch, which dissolves the "I can't change this" story that was holding the pattern in place.
