# Coach Demonstrates the Stance via Reversed Role-Play

## The Move

In a coaching session, when the person has just named a new stance they want to take in a relationship but cannot picture it being possible, Joe inverts the usual role-play. He asks the client to play the other person (the parent, the partner, the boss). Joe plays the client in the new stance. The client gets to feel the new response land on them — from outside — while also feeling what it is like to deliver the trigger from the other side.

"So I want you to be your mom. You're going to guilt me. We're going to try something out and we're going to see how it works in your system. Okay?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

The mechanism is double: the client experiences what the new stance feels like to receive (which is often the first time they have ever seen it directed at the parent's behavior), and the client embodies the parent enough to recognize what was actually happening underneath the trigger.

## When Joe Uses It

The move fits a specific moment:

- The person has named the new stance in the abstract ("open-hearted, high-boundaried") but cannot picture it in the actual situation.
- The person says some version of "I don't know how to do that" about the stance.
- The original trigger is a familiar, repeatable behavior (a parent guilting, a partner shutting down, a boss criticizing) that the client can easily reproduce.
- The session has enough trust for the client to drop into character without performing carefully.
- Talking about the new stance has hit its limit. The next step is felt.

The move is **not** used early in the session. The client needs to have located the new stance first. The role-play is the test, not the discovery.

## How It Differs From the Other Role-Play Variants

There are three role-play moves in the toolkit. They look similar from outside; they do different things.

**[[become-the-authority-figure]]** — Joe plays the trigger (the boss, the parent), the client practices the new stance. The work is the client's embodiment of the new stance under the actual pressure.

**Parent-stand-in variant** (also in [[become-the-authority-figure]]) — Joe plays the parent so the client can say what they never could say. The work is the client speaking the unspoken truth.

**Coach-demonstrates variant (this move)** — Client plays the trigger (the guilting parent), Joe plays the new self. The work is twofold: the client sees the new stance land, and the client embodies the trigger enough to recognize what is underneath it.

The choice of which variant to use depends on what is needed. If the client knows the new stance but cannot embody it: become-the-authority-figure. If the client needs to express the unsaid: parent-stand-in. If the client cannot picture the new stance being possible: this move.

## What It Produces

Two parallel productions:

**On the receive side:** The client experiences the new stance landing on the guilt or the anger. Often the response stops the trigger in its tracks — not because the trigger has been suppressed, but because it has been met with care rather than defense. The client gets the felt experience of what their own future move could land like.

**On the delivery side:** The client embodies the parent's emotional position. This is where the recognition usually happens. Speaking from inside the parent's pattern, the client often spontaneously names what was underneath it. In the source session, the client played her mom guilting Joe, and within a few exchanges had the recognition: "My dad was the angry one, so she couldn't be angry. So I was like, oh, that's underneath that is the anger that she's not expressing." See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] and [[guilt-as-passive-fear]].

The double production is the move's value. Other role-plays produce one of these effects. This one produces both at the same time because the client is occupying both positions.

## What Joe Does

Joe sets up the move briefly. He flags the role-play ("I want you to be your mom"), states the trigger to deliver ("you're going to guilt me"), and frames it as an experiment ("we're going to try something out and see how it works in your system"). The experiment framing is critical. It removes performance pressure and invites genuine play.

Then Joe receives the trigger and delivers the new stance in role. The delivery is the demonstration. Joe does not lecture the client about what the new stance would look like. He shows it.

"Mom, I see that you're really scared, and I just want you to know I can be right here with you in that. I'm right here." -- Joe Hudson, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

The client (still in the parent's role) then escalates — as the parent would. Joe meets the escalation in the same stance.

"Yeah, I also see that you're really angry, and I can be right here with that too. I don't need you to be any different." -- Joe Hudson, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

After the demonstration, Joe checks in with the client. Not about the role-play technique, about what happened in the client's body. "What happened in you?" The recognition arrives from the body, often as a structural insight about the parent.

## Dialogue Example

The full move in compressed form, from the source session:

- **Joe:** So I want you to be your mom. You're going to guilt me. We're going to try something out and we're going to see how it works in your system.
- **Client (as Mom):** Why can't you be different? Why are you so sensitive? I can't say anything anymore. Uh, Hey, Mom.
- **Joe (as Client):** Mom, I see that you're really scared, and I just want you to know I can be right here with you in that. I'm right here.
- **Client (as Mom, escalating):** What do you mean really scared? What kind of awesome is that? This deep bullshit. You just don't want to be here and talk to me. You don't want to visit me.
- **Joe (as Client):** Yeah, I also see that you're really angry, and I can be right here with that too. I don't need you to be any different. What happened in you? Just to witness that for a second, what happened in you?
- **Client (out of role):** I just had a thought. My dad was the angry one, so she couldn't be angry. So I was like, oh, that's underneath that is the anger that she's not expressing.
- *(Source: How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner)*

The check-in question "what happened in you?" is delivered while the client is still partly in role. The recognition arrives from inside the parent's position. The client has just embodied the mom's escalation and immediately understands its structural origin.

## Tone

The setup is light. "We're going to try something out" — invitation, not command. The role-play itself is delivered in earnest but not in caricature. Joe does not act-out a character. He delivers the new stance with the same warmth and directness he would use in a real moment.

The check-in afterward is curious. "What happened in you?" — not "did that work?" or "did you see how I did it?" The question keeps the attention on the client's experience, not on Joe's performance.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not use this if the new stance has not been located.** The demonstration has nothing to model if the client has not yet articulated what they want to be different.
- **Do not lecture during the role-play.** The whole point is to show, not tell. If Joe steps out to explain, the move collapses into instruction.
- **Do not over-perform the parent role.** If the client is asked to play the parent and slips into caricature, the embodiment does not happen and the recognition does not arrive. Joe sometimes has to gently bring the client back into the actual parent's voice.
- **Do not skip the check-in.** The body-level recognition often only arrives when asked for. Without the check-in, the role-play stays an interesting exercise rather than a structural insight.
- **Do not deliver the new stance in a way the client could not realistically deliver.** If Joe's demonstration is so polished it feels impossible, the modeling fails. Joe demonstrates a version the client could actually reach. Often this means the demonstration is plain, not eloquent.

## Why It Works When It Works

The mechanism: the client has been imagining the new stance as something they would have to perform alone, against the parent's pattern, in some future moment. The role-play makes it concrete in the present. The client sees the stance directed at exactly the behavior they have been bracing against, and sees that it is not a fight but a meeting.

Simultaneously, the client occupies the parent's position long enough to discover that the parent's behavior is not the impenetrable wall the client has been treating it as. It is a behavior. A behavior with a structure. A structure made of fear or anger or unfelt material. From inside the position, the structure becomes visible.

The future move with the actual parent is then no longer a performance against an opaque force. It is a meeting between two people whose patterns are now visible.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The client has named a new stance and does not believe they can take it.
- The trigger is a familiar pattern the client could easily reproduce.
- Talk has hit its limit and the next step is felt experience.
- A concern like [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]] or [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] is being worked.
- The session has trust and the client is willing to play.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward the body-level recognition that follows the role-play. The "what happened in you?" check-in usually produces the next move.
- Toward [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] when the recognition is about what the parent was actually doing.
- Toward [[guilt-as-passive-fear]] when the recognition is about the structure of the parent's guilt.
- Toward [[welcome-the-hurt-the-way-you-wanted-as-a-kid]] when the role-play surfaces the child-hurt that was never met.
