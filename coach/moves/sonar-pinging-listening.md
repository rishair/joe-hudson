# Sonar Pinging

## The Move

In live coaching, Joe sends out probes -- statements, questions, gentle challenges, even sharp ones -- and reads what comes back. The probe is not the move. The read of the return signal is the move. Joe describes it as sonar.

"I'm definitely -- pattern recognition is happening. But the other thing that's happening is I'm really letting them lead because somehow or another what I've noticed is, and it might not be the most efficient path, but it is a more efficient path than the other ones I've run across, which is to -- that person knows where they need to go next better than I do." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

"But it's -- I'm pinging against them and like listening to the sonar that comes back. To say, oh, this is where they're ready to move." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

## Why This Looks Aggressive

A specific note in the way Joe describes it: from outside, the pinging often looks aggressive. Joe makes pointed statements, asks pointed questions, sometimes provokes. The watcher who is reading the surface of the move sees a coach being intense. The watcher who is reading the structure of the move sees something different: the apparent intensity is a probe being calibrated to elicit a clear return signal. A gentle probe might not produce enough return for Joe to read where the person actually is. A sharper probe surfaces the real material.

"It can be very aggressive looking from the outside. But it's -- I'm pinging against them and like listening to the sonar that comes back." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

The aggressiveness is in service of the listening. It is not the opposite of listening. The signal it produces is what the listening needs to operate on.

## How the Return Signal Functions

The return is not just verbal content. It is also somatic, tonal, affective, energetic. When Joe pings, what comes back includes: how the person's body responded, what tone they used, what they did not say, what their face did, what micro-movement they made, what got brighter or duller in their presence.

This is what makes the move structurally different from "asking the right question." A "right question" model treats the question as the work and the answer as the data. The sonar model treats the question (or statement, or challenge) as a probe, and the *whole field of response* as the data. The verbal answer is one element among many.

The capacity to read the full field of response is the capacity that is downstream of long inner work (see [[sensitivity-as-byproduct]] and [[dont-chase-spiritual-powers]] for why this capacity arises). The pinging move is what uses the capacity in real time.

## Why It Works Better Than Knowing the Path In Advance

Joe contrasts the move with a different approach: knowing in advance which protocol or which path to deploy based on a diagnostic profile. His sense is that the protocol-deployment approach can be effective, but the sonar-pinging approach is more efficient in his hands. The reason: the person being coached knows better than the coach where they need to go next. The job is not to determine the path. The job is to make space for the path to reveal itself by probing in different directions and reading which probe produces movement.

"That person knows where they need to go next better than I do." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

This is also why the move is paired with [[migratory-path]] in Joe's broader frame. The person has an internal migratory knowing about where they need to go. The pinging is how the coach gives that knowing space to express itself rather than overriding it with the coach's own theory of the case.

## The Distinction From Performance

A trap the move invites: the pinging starts looking like the coach being interesting or provocative or impressive. This is the form-one [[two-forms-of-charisma|charisma]] version of the move. The aggressiveness becomes about the coach's display. The listening collapses.

The actual move requires that the coach be empty enough (see [[two-cups-of-milk-and-water]]) that the pinging is purely instrumental. The probes are calibrated to produce a return signal that helps the coach find where the client is. The probes are not calibrated to produce admiration in the client.

A diagnostic for the coach watching themselves: after the probe, are you waiting for what comes back, or are you waiting for the next thing you are going to say? The first is sonar listening. The second is performance with intensity dressing.

## The Connection to Letting Them Lead

The most counterintuitive aspect of the move: the apparent intensity is in service of the client leading the session. The pinging makes space for the client's actual direction to surface. The coach is not driving where the session goes. The coach is reading where the session wants to go and then sending probes that confirm or disconfirm the read.

"What I've noticed is, and it might not be the most efficient path, but it is a more efficient path than the other ones I've run across, which is to -- that person knows where they need to go next better than I do." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

This is structurally the opposite of the apparent contour. The coach who looks like they are pushing hard is actually following. The coach who looks like they are following calmly is sometimes actually leading the client to where the coach has decided they need to go. The surface is reversed from the structure.

## What This Pairs With

The sonar move is most powerful when the coach has also done the inner work that allows them to read the return signal accurately. Without that work, the pinging produces signal the coach cannot interpret, and the apparent intensity is intensity without intelligence. The capacity is built over years of inner work. See [[sensitivity-as-byproduct]] for the capacity-arises-from-inner-work principle that is the precondition.

The move also pairs with [[opposite-of-trying]] -- the deeper orientation of receiving rather than producing, of which the sonar listening is a specific operational form. And with [[have-them-receive-your-listening]] for the move that makes the listening felt by the client.

## When to Reach for This in a Session

The move is useful when:

- The coach is uncertain where the session should go and wants the client's own direction to surface.
- A more gentle approach has not produced enough material to work with.
- The client is in their head and a sharper probe might bypass the verbal-management layer.
- The coach senses something the client is not naming and wants to test the read by surfacing it.
- The standard moves of mirroring and validation are producing comfort without movement.

The move is *not* useful when:

- The coach is using intensity to compensate for not knowing what to do.
- The client is in a state that needs softness rather than probing.
- The coach has not done the inner work to read what the probes return.
- The intensity would be experienced as attack rather than as engagement.

## Tone

The tone of the move varies because the probes vary. Sometimes the ping is a soft question. Sometimes it is a sharp statement. Sometimes it is a contradiction of what the client just said. Sometimes it is a long silence that probes by absence rather than presence. The constant across all of these is the orientation: send something, read what comes back, calibrate the next move based on the read.
