# Speak Your Own Elephant to Surface Theirs

## The Move

When you need to name a hard truth about a system you are inside — a family, a team, a marriage — open by naming your own elephant rather than the other person's. Name your role in the dynamic. Name the want underneath your contribution. Name the false shame you have been carrying. The conversation then has somewhere to go that does not require the other person to defend.

The move inverts the natural impulse, which is to name what the other person is doing wrong. The natural impulse produces defensiveness because the other person has something to argue with — their own behavior, their own character, their own contribution. The inverted move offers them nothing to argue with, because you are naming what is true *about you*.

"It's actually a great way to bring up the elephant in the room is to own the thing about what's going on with your elephant in the room. So I wasn't able to do this as a kid, but one of the things I could have done is say, oh, I'm, I'm, I noticed that I keep on playing the role of the person yelling something's wrong and I don't want that role anymore. Could have been a great way to talk about the situation with my dad, or this is what I really want is a family that where we can love each other and not fight at the table every day." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The two example openings from Joe's reflection on his own family:

- "I noticed I keep playing the role of the person yelling something's wrong, and I don't want that role anymore."
- "What I really want is a family where we can love each other and not fight at the table every day."

Neither says "Dad, you are an alcoholic." Both, however, open a conversation in which the alcoholism can be discussed. The first names a role the speaker has been carrying; the second names a want the speaker has. Both are undeniably about the speaker. Both are also about the entire dynamic the alcoholism is producing.

## The Underlying Principle: You Cannot Be Argued With About Your Want

The move is the application of [[cant-argue-with-your-want]], a teaching Joe attributes to Kasey DeBrawn:

"Somebody can argue with the fact that they're a drunk or not a drunk. Somebody can argue with the fact that you look like shit or you don't look like shit, or that they look like shit or they don't look like shit, but they can't really argue with the fact that you want to be in a family where people love each other because it's what you want. They can't argue with what you want. And so there's less of a place for them to be defensive." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

Your wants are not propositions about external reality. They cannot be contested with evidence. They are not accusations. They are not claims about who is wrong. They are simply what you want. There is no defensive move the other person can make against your statement of what you want. They can refuse to give it to you. They cannot tell you that you do not want it.

The same is true of your own role in the dynamic. "I have been playing the role of X, and I do not want to anymore" is not contestable. The other person was not in your head when you played the role. They cannot tell you what role you were playing. You can name your own role with no defensive move available to them.

The result: the conversation that follows is structurally different from the accusation-and-defense conversation. There is no shame to ping-pong. There is no who-is-right to fight about. There is just your truth, offered, with no demand that the other person agree, disagree, or change anything.

## Why This Works When Direct Naming Fails

Direct naming of the other person's elephant invites the shame hot potato. See [[shame-hot-potato]]. The other person feels shame, refuses to hold it, throws it back as defense or counter-attack. The naming becomes a fight about whose shame this is. The actual content of the elephant gets buried under the fight.

Naming your own elephant skips the hot potato. There is no shame to throw because you are holding your own. You are not making the other person hold yours. There is nothing for them to defend against because you have not attacked.

The structural shift: a direct elephant-naming is a transaction in which the speaker tries to install something in the listener. The self-elephant-naming is an offering in which the speaker reveals something about themselves. The transactional frame produces defense. The offering frame produces openness, if anything.

## What This Move Does Not Replace

The move is not a substitute for ever directly naming the other person's contribution. Some elephants do require eventually naming the other person's role. The move is the *opener*. It creates the conditions under which the harder direct naming can happen later, when the dynamic has softened enough to hold it.

A sequence:

1. Open with your own elephant. Name your role. Name your want. Name your false shame.
2. Let the conversation breathe. The other person, finding nothing to defend, may begin to name their own.
3. If the dynamic has softened, the direct naming of the other person's contribution can happen with much less defensive response.

Without the opener, the direct naming arrives cold, into a defended field, and produces the predictable defense. With the opener, the field has shifted. The direct naming becomes a continuation of an honest conversation rather than the start of a fight.

## What This Move Cannot Do With Narcissists

Joe is explicit about the boundary condition:

"Won't work with narcissists or people with personality disorders, but it'll work with most humans." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The move depends on the other person being capable of being moved by your vulnerability. A narcissist will read your self-naming as weakness to exploit, not as an opening. A person with certain personality disorders will not be able to hold the field the move creates. With these people, the move is wasted and may be actively counterproductive — you have revealed your vulnerability to someone who cannot meet it.

The diagnostic: have you, in this relationship, ever experienced the other person being moved by your honesty? Have they, in the past, met your vulnerability with their own? If yes, the move has a chance. If no, the move may not be the right tool. See [[how-do-i-deal-with-narcissistic-parent]] for the alternative case.

## Why The Move Requires Internal Work First

For the move to land, the self-naming has to be actually true. You cannot perform "I have been playing the role of X" as a manipulation. The other person will read the performance and the move will collapse into a more sophisticated version of the accusation it was supposed to replace.

This means the move requires that you have actually looked at your own contribution to the dynamic. What role have you been playing? What want of yours has been driving your part of the pattern? What false shame have you been carrying that has shaped your responses? The looking is the prerequisite. The naming is the result.

This is also why the move connects to [[the-elephant-in-the-room]]'s teaching that there is an internal elephant for each person:

"If the elephant is shame, is a false sense of shame, and everybody's feeling like they're doing something wrong, then you've got that false sense of shame. In you. And that's what you can bring up. That's how you can start the conversation." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

Your false shame is the entry. You have one. Bringing it up is the opener. The conversation starts there.

## What Reads Trigger This Move

The move is useful when:

- The relationship has a chronic elephant that direct naming has not resolved
- You have noticed that you have been playing a role in the dynamic, not just receiving it
- The other person is defensive and the defensiveness has prevented direct naming from landing
- You want the conversation to be about the dynamic, not about who is right
- You have already done some internal work on your own contribution and can name it honestly
- The other person is capable of meeting vulnerability with vulnerability

## What the Move Produces

In the best case, the move opens space. The other person, finding nothing to defend against, begins to soften. They may name their own role. They may ask questions. The conversation becomes about the shared dynamic rather than about who is the villain.

In the second-best case, the other person does not soften but also cannot mount a defense. The move has at least cleared the air on your side. You have named what you needed to name. You have not given the other person ammunition.

In the worst case, the other person treats the self-naming as a confession of weakness. This is the narcissist case Joe describes. You have learned something important about the relationship.

In all three cases, you have done the move from open heart, named what was true, and stayed in your own truth. The move's value is not contingent on the response.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You are about to bring up a hard topic with your partner and rehearsing what they did wrong, and notice you have not asked what you have been doing in the dynamic
- You realize you have been the family member who points at the problem for years and the pointing has produced no change
- A team conflict has stalled because each person is defending and you have not opened by naming your own contribution
- You have a hard truth to share with a friend and find yourself afraid to share it directly, and realize you have not first named the want underneath your wanting to share it

## Anti-Patterns

- **Performing the move when you have not actually looked at your role.** Becomes manipulation; the other person reads it.
- **Using the move as a Trojan horse to deliver the accusation.** "I notice I keep playing the role of pointing at how YOU are an alcoholic" is not the move. The accusation has been pre-loaded; the listener will hear it.
- **Using the move with someone who has shown they will exploit your vulnerability.** Joe's boundary condition: not with narcissists, not with personality disorders. The move requires a counterparty capable of meeting it.
- **Stopping after the self-naming if direct naming is still needed.** The move is the opener. Some dynamics require the direct naming eventually, after the opener has done its work.

## Tone

The move is delivered with calm and self-possession. You are naming something true about yourself; there is no urgency in the naming, no apology for it, no demand on the listener. The tone is closer to revelation than to confession. You are not asking for absolution. You are stating a fact about your inner experience and what you want.
