# Don't dress up narcissism in spiritual language

## The Anti-Pattern

Using spiritual language to justify pursuing ego needs. Framing the desire for specialness, attention, importance, or unchecked self-indulgence as following one's truth, listening to inner guidance, or pursuing the authentic path. The behavior is self-aggrandizement. The justification is spiritual.

This is distinct from genuine following of inner guidance. The difference is that genuine following accepts uncomfortable truths, includes other people's reality, costs the person something, and produces alignment that can be felt. Spiritual narcissism produces certainty, exemption from feedback, and a perpetual sense of being the misunderstood prophet of one's own path.

"This idea that I'm going to make myself special or big or, I'm gonna get all of my id needs met through the activities I'm doing in the world, whether it be business or art or whatever. And I'm gonna justify it with a whole bunch of spiritual talk and things like, I'm gonna listen to my truth, et cetera, et cetera. I think that's a real risk." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

## Why It Fails

The behavior is doing what unexamined ego always does: extraction, accumulation, performance, control. The spiritual framing prevents the corrective feedback that would otherwise interrupt the pattern. Friends, partners, colleagues, and customers either accept the framing (and get exploited) or reject it (and get cast as not understanding the work).

The cost is the person's own development. Spiritual narcissism is misery dressed up as freedom. The id needs being met do not actually feed the person, but the spiritual cover prevents the person from seeing the lack of nourishment. The hunger persists, the framing persists, the cycle continues.

## How To Detect It (In Yourself)

The diagnostic is misery. Spiritual narcissism produces a chronic background of friction, of being not-quite-aligned, of needing to defend the choices against people who don't understand. Genuine following of inner guidance produces alignment that can be felt in the body, even when the choices are difficult.

"The lucky part is that it's misery. So I can find those parts pretty easily if I'm just paying attention to where I'm not aligned, where I'm in friction with myself." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

Other signals to watch for:
- Decisions framed as "my truth" that consistently benefit you and consistently cost others
- A sense of being the misunderstood teacher, prophet, or visionary
- Spiritual language used to deflect critique ("you're judging me," "you're not in wonder")
- A pattern of leaving relationships, jobs, or commitments framed as following inner guidance, where the cost is borne by others
- Receiving rewards (money, attention, status, sexual access) and framing the receipt as service
- Resistance to ordinary humility, ordinary apology, ordinary correction

## The Subtle Versions

The obvious version is the cult leader who exploits followers. The subtle versions are more dangerous because they are harder to detect.

"It's not to pretend it's not there. It's not even like the risk is that I need to, see if it's there, the risk is that I need to find the more and more subtle versions of that in play in myself." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

The subtle versions in someone doing the work:
- Charging high prices and framing it as "honoring the work" when the truth is wanting the money
- Refusing to engage with critique and framing it as "not making myself wrong"
- Avoiding ordinary responsibilities and framing it as "following the call"
- Pursuing recognition while claiming to be doing it for service
- Letting close people down and framing the disappointment as their attachment

The work is to find these in oneself before they ossify. The principle of [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]] applies here: undeveloped selfishness is the problem, not selfishness itself. The corrective is to find the deeper want underneath the surface narcissistic want, which is usually about being loved, being safe, being enough -- and to address that want directly rather than through performance of spirituality.

## How Joe Holds This in His Own Work

Joe does not claim to be free of this. He claims to be looking for the subtle versions in himself. The acknowledgment that ego is at play, that he likes making money, that he enjoys the recognition -- these are the antidote to the dressed-up version.

"There's no way that you can completely annihilate an ego. I think if you think you've completely annihilated it, that is ego in itself." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

Direct admission of ordinary wants ("I want your money. I like making money") is more aligned than the spiritualized version ("I am here to serve, and the energy exchange supports the work"). The first owns the want. The second uses spiritual language to disown the want while still getting it met.

## What To Do Instead

Name the ordinary want directly. "I want this because I enjoy it." "I am charging this because I want the money." "I am taking this opportunity because I want the recognition." These statements do not require apology and do not require dressing up. Owned, they become available for evaluation. Hidden, they become engines of distortion.

If the want is unacceptable to admit, that is the work. The unacceptability of the want is what produces the need to dress it up. The acceptability of the ordinary want, including the want for money, status, attention, and pleasure, is what releases the need for spiritual cover.

The deeper move: investigate what the surface want is actually about. The wanting of recognition is often a wanting of love. The wanting of money is often a wanting of safety. The wanting of specialness is often a wanting of mattering. Addressing the underneath dissolves the urgency of the surface, which dissolves the need for the spiritual dressing.

## Boundary With Genuine Spiritual Practice

This anti-pattern is not a critique of spiritual practice. It is a warning about a specific corruption of spiritual practice. The corrective is more honest practice, not less practice.

The distinguishing question is direction. Genuine spiritual practice tends to surface uncomfortable truths about oneself, increase the capacity to receive critique, soften the need to be special, and produce ordinary humility. Spiritual narcissism tends to insulate the practitioner from critique, increase the sense of being special, and amplify the need to be seen as advanced.

If the practice is producing the first set of results, it is working. If the practice is producing the second set, the practice has been hijacked by ego, and the work is to notice that and bring the practice back to honest ground.
