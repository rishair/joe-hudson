# Don't Institutionalize Appreciation as the Employee-of-the-Month Plaque

## The Anti-Pattern

The leader hears that appreciation matters for team performance, and decides to "institutionalize appreciation" in a specific bad way: a formal slot in meetings, an employee-of-the-month plaque, a designated three minutes of appreciation, a section in the employee handbook. The form replaces the substance. Appreciation that the team can actually feel becomes appreciation that the team has to perform.

The Severance show, which depicts office workers crunching numbers all day and then being rewarded with cheesecake, is a clean visual reference for the trap. The cheesecake is supposed to be appreciation. It is actually a small reward delivered through the structure of a dehumanizing system. The form of appreciation, separated from the substance, becomes its own kind of cruelty.

"There's appreciation and then there's the like cloying employee of the month plaque. ... Right, right. I'm thinking of the show Severance. People would just, crunch numbers all day or press numbers and then finally get like a cheesecake." -- the dialogue

## Why It Fails

Institutionalized appreciation in this bad form fails for three reasons:

**The form signals its own emptiness.** When appreciation has a designated slot, the team understands that the appreciation is on a schedule rather than arising from a real feeling. The scheduled appreciation does not transmit care; it transmits the leader's compliance with a process. The team registers the difference immediately.

**It manufactures the absence of what it claims to deliver.** Appreciation that comes from a real noticing produces connection. Appreciation that comes from a scheduled slot produces awkwardness. The team learns to be embarrassed during the appreciation segment of the meeting, which is the opposite of what the slot was supposed to create.

**It substitutes for the real thing.** The leader who has institutionalized appreciation often stops doing the actual appreciation. The scheduled slot becomes the leader's evidence that appreciation is being handled. The texture of regular, real, unscheduled appreciation falls away because the leader has outsourced the function to the structure.

## What Joe Recommends Instead

The alternative is real appreciation: the leader notices what they are grateful for, on a regular basis, and tells the person.

"Yeah, so I mean, you've seen how we've done it. We just like, we just have moments of celebration and I make it a point, just like I make it a point to have the hard conversation, I make it a point to think about on a regular basis, who am I appreciative of honestly that day or that week? And I call people and tell them like, I'm really, or I send a text or I, do something where I'm like, hey, I'm really grateful for this thing." -- Joe Hudson

The form is unscheduled. The trigger is the leader's own noticing. The delivery is direct — a call, a text, a quick mention. The appreciation is for a specific thing the person actually did. The texture is unmistakably real because the structure that would have signaled performance is absent.

The discipline is the same as the hard-conversations discipline. The leader makes a point of looking for what they're grateful for, the way they make a point of looking for what hard conversations they're avoiding. Both are deliberate practices; neither is institutionalized into a slot.

## The Love-Bombing Variation

A related failure mode: love-bombing as appreciation. The leader praises the team member intensely and inauthentically as a way to get more work out of them or to manipulate their loyalty. The team member can usually feel the manipulation, even if they cannot articulate it.

"That's different from love bombing in a, like, trying to, like, oh, I'm gonna appreciate you in such a way that you do more work for me." -- the dialogue

The diagnostic for whether appreciation is genuine: would the leader be saying this if they were not trying to get something out of the person? If yes, the appreciation is real. If no, the appreciation is manipulation dressed in nice language.

The genuine appreciation lands even if the person does not let it in. Something registers somatically. There is a safety the genuine appreciation creates that the manipulated version does not.

"For me, it's like, oh no, what's the genuine appreciation that I have for somebody? And even if they don't let it in, something registers in them where they're like, oh, okay, I there's a safety that comes with that." -- Joe Hudson

## Why Real Appreciation Matters

The anti-pattern matters because appreciation, done correctly, is one of the two highest-leverage motivators (alongside autonomy).

"There's a tremendous amount of studies that show that the two things that motivate people more than money, more than a lot of things, is autonomy and appreciation." -- Joe Hudson

The stakes are high. Real appreciation, regularly given, produces a team that takes bigger risks, presents themselves with more confidence, and does better work because they have the underlying safety of knowing they are valued. The institutionalized form delivers none of this.

The pickleball/golf metaphor Joe uses: when you make a few bad shots, the self-criticism tends to make the next shots worse. When the surrounding context conveys "you're doing well," the next shots tend to improve. The same operates on teams. The team member who knows the leader's appreciation is real takes the next risk more freely.

## What Real Appreciation Looks Like in a Team

The institutionalized form is not the only structural form. Real appreciation can be a structural feature of the culture without being a slot in the meeting. The shape:

- Leaders and teammates regularly notice what they appreciate about each other
- The noticing gets communicated, in the moment, in a normal channel (call, text, mention in conversation)
- The appreciation is specific to what the person actually did
- The appreciation does not come with an immediate ask attached (which would mark it as manipulation)
- The team's natural rhythm includes celebration of wins as a normal feature, not as a ritualized event

The difference between this and the institutionalized form is that the structure is the leader's own habit of noticing and communicating, not a slot in the calendar. The structure is internalized rather than ritualized.

"What I hear you to mean is like really make it a part of the culture of the company where everybody feels it and is a source of it. ... Not bake it into the employee handbook." -- the dialogue

## What This Is Not Saying

**Not that recognition programs are always bad.** Some recognition programs work because the underlying culture is real and the programs are operating inside that culture. The anti-pattern is the substitution of the program for the underlying culture, not the program itself.

**Not that celebration shouldn't happen.** Celebration of wins is a real and important thing. The anti-pattern is the ritualized cheesecake version, not the natural celebration that happens when a team genuinely accomplishes something.

**Not that the leader should never say "thank you" out loud.** The leader should say thank you constantly. The anti-pattern is the formal-slot version, not the verbal version that arises in the moment.
