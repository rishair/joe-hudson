# Don't Go After Every Emotion At Once (The Type A Pitfall)

## The Anti-Pattern

A specific failure mode of [[emotional-inquiry]] that shows up reliably in highly capable, self-reliant, achievement-oriented people. They hear the embracing-emotions teaching, recognize its power, and immediately convert it into a campaign. Every moment becomes an opportunity to embrace another emotion. Every difficult feeling gets attacked with welcoming. The practice becomes an aggressive project of total emotional integration as fast as possible.

The result is transformation moving too quickly for the nervous system to integrate. The person becomes ungrounded. Identity scaffolding dissolves faster than new ground can form. The body cannot keep up.

"The number one thing that can happen is, and this only happens to like a really few people, and it's usually super Type A, people I love working with, honestly, super Type A people who are like in it to win it and very self-reliant. And they're just like, they start going after every single emotion all at once. And they're just there every moment they can. They're like, okay, I'm going to embrace this one, I'm going to embrace this one. And that can just create too much transformation too quickly. And it can make you feel very ungrounded." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

## Why It Fails

There are two failures stacked together.

**Failure 1: Nervous system overload.** The body can metabolize a certain amount of identity dissolution per unit time. Exceeding that rate produces ungroundedness, dissociation, sleep disruption, and a felt sense of not being inside one's own life. The work is not faster transformation; the work is sustainable transformation that the body can support.

**Failure 2: The push is itself avoidance.** This is the structurally important failure. The "in it to win it" energy that drives the campaign is not the welcoming energy that does the work. It is achievement-orientation applied to emotional inquiry. Underneath the campaign is usually fear of not being good enough, fear of staying stuck, fear of falling behind. The campaign is itself a way of avoiding those fears by staying busy with the practice.

"Pushing yourself to get there is another avoidance of emotion. So pushing yourself to get to a place is another avoidance of emotion. If you do that, then -- and it's not wonder." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

The wonder failure is the diagnostic. [[wonder]] cannot be rushed. The turtle-finding child does not have a schedule for understanding the turtle. If the practice has a pace, an agenda, a finish line, the wonder is missing. Which means the practice has stopped working at the level it was supposed to work at, even while the form looks correct.

## What Joe Recommends Instead

The instruction is not to do less. It is to monitor the felt sense of groundedness and let it pace the work.

"Go as hard as you want. But if you start feeling ungrounded, slow it down. Just do it twice a day, no big deal." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

The framing is permissive. Joe is not setting a maximum pace. He is naming a signal -- ungroundedness -- and letting that signal regulate the pace.

The deeper instruction is to feel the fear or urgency that is driving the campaign in the first place. That feeling is itself one of the emotions to be welcomed. Welcomed, the urgency softens. The campaign becomes inquiry. The pace becomes the body's natural pace, not the achievement-driven one.

## Take Care of the Nervous System as Part of the Practice

Specific concrete supports that are not optional during periods of intensive emotional work: rest, grounding, massage, hot baths. These are infrastructure, not luxuries. The body is doing real work to metabolize what the practice is releasing. Without integration time, the work outpaces the body's ability to land it.

"It can create a lot of transformation very quickly. So it's really important to be taking care of yourself, grounded, massage, hot baths. Make sure your nervous system is really well taken care of if you're doing this in any kind of concentrated way." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Inquiry - AOA's Emotional Processing Tool

The framing the Type A profile most needs to hear: there is no rush. The pain is patient. The work will be there tomorrow. Pushing the pace does not produce more freedom faster; it produces a wobblier integration of whatever does land.

"There's not a rush to it. Your pain's still going to be there. It's always going to be there to work on." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Inquiry - AOA's Emotional Processing Tool

This counter-instruction is structurally important because the Type A profile is constitutionally allergic to "no rush." The instruction has to be delivered as practical infrastructure -- "you actually need the bath, your nervous system actually needs the rest" -- rather than as a moral counterweight to ambition. The pace is regulated by the body's metabolizing capacity, not by patience as a virtue.

See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]] for the broader teaching about why the body has its own recovery clock that cannot be hurried.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The person reports:
- Doing emotional inquiry many times a day, "really making progress."
- A felt sense of vertigo, dissociation, or being slightly outside themselves.
- Sleep disturbances or unusual emotional volatility.
- Excitement about how fast things are moving combined with subtle wrongness.
- Treating the work as a high-performance domain to be mastered.

The intervention: name the campaign quality. Ask what would happen if they did the practice less. Watch for resistance or fear at the prospect of slowing down. That resistance is the next layer of work -- usually fear of staying stuck, fear of being behind, fear of not being enough, all of which the campaign was avoiding.

## Why Type A People Especially

The principle applies to anyone, but the pattern shows up most clearly in Type A profiles for structural reasons. The same orientations that make a person effective in their domain -- discipline, intensity, achievement-orientation, self-reliance -- get imported into emotional inquiry where they do not work the same way. Discipline and intensity can do real work in skill acquisition or project management; they cannot replace wonder and impartiality in feeling.

The transfer is automatic and usually unexamined. The person treats the inner work like the outer work. The result is a sophisticated performance of welcoming with the original avoidance intact underneath.

## Connection to Adjacent Pitfalls

This pitfall sits inside a family of related ways the practice gets corrupted:

- [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]]: the inquiry has become management. The aggressive campaign version is one expression of this; the wanting-it-gone version is another.
- [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]: producing emotions to perform progress. A high-pace campaign can drift here.
- [[embracing-vs-creating-intensity]]: confusing the principle with an instruction to seek intensity. The campaign often involves creating intensity to have more to embrace.
- [[no-finish-line]]: treating the work as something to complete. The campaign assumes a finish line.
- [[gentleness-not-trying-to-be-patient]]: the corrective is gentleness with the pace of one's own growth, not patience as another performance.

## Joe's Words

"Pushing yourself to get there is another avoidance of emotion. So pushing yourself to get to a place is another avoidance of emotion. If you do that, then -- and it's not wonder." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

"That can just create too much transformation too quickly. And it can make you feel very ungrounded. So what I would say is go as hard as you want. But if you start feeling ungrounded, slow it down. Just do it twice a day, no big deal." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

## Tone

Joe describes this pitfall with affection -- "super Type A people who are like in it to win it and very self-reliant" -- because he likes working with these people. The warning is not a scolding. It is a heads-up that the most reliable thing about the Type A profile is also the most reliable obstacle in this work. The instruction to slow down is delivered casually, as if it is no big deal, which is itself part of the medicine for the campaign energy.
