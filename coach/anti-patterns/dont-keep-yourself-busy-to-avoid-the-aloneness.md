# Don't Keep Yourself Constantly Busy to Avoid the Aloneness

## The Anti-Pattern

The person organizes their life so that they are never in direct contact with themselves. The calendar is full. Every moment is occupied — by work, by people, by media, by scrolling, by errands, by the next thing on the list. The result is that the felt sense of "I'm not enough" or "I'm alone" is never met. It is also never resolved. It runs the system from underneath.

The pattern is invisible from inside because it looks like an active, productive, well-managed life. The person can describe their schedule as full and demanding and would not call it avoidance. They would call it being responsible, being productive, being needed. The protective function of the busy-ness is what makes it hard to see as protective.

## Why It Fails

The busy-ness does not eliminate the avoided feeling. It only prevents the feeling from being met. The feeling continues to operate — every action shaped by its avoidance, every decision filtered through the question "will this prevent me from feeling that?" The chronic motion required is exhausting. The system that maintains the avoidance is doing full-time work the person did not consent to.

"What happens when somebody has this feeling is like being alone in a great relationship with themselves is something that's really scary. So they keep themselves incredibly busy, constantly occupied. And because it's constantly occupied, they don't actually get to see what that feeling is." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The cost-benefit math is structurally bad. The avoidance requires lifelong constant effort. The contact would require a few hours, repeated a few times. The chronic state the busy-ness is producing is much more expensive than the acute state the contact would produce.

"It's a whole lot easier than avoiding it, because that takes an entire life of energy to avoid it." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The busy-ness also has a specific failure mode in the alone-in-room configuration. Because the person never has direct contact with themselves, they cannot tell what they actually want, what they actually approve of, what would actually feel like the life they want to live. They are running on the externalized question "do they approve?" because the internal question "do I approve?" has no available answer — there has been no contact with the internal answerer.

This is why the busy-ness produces the felt aloneness it is trying to escape. The avoidance prevents the inner contact that would make external connection possible. The person is in motion all the time, in rooms full of people all the time, and alone all the time.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is direct: stop the motion deliberately, for a few hours, two or three times, and meet what's there. See [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]] for the practice form. See [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]] for the principle that justifies the contact.

The stopping is the work. Not the meditation technique or the breathwork or the journal prompt — those can be deployed in service of the avoidance if the person is sophisticated. The work is the actual deliberate stopping, with no replacement activity, until the busy-ness defenses exhaust and the avoided material surfaces.

Joe also names the practical entry point that makes the deeper stopping easier: [[daily-thank-you-notes-to-3-to-5-people]]. The daily practice of recognizing support builds the base that makes the deeper alone-with-self work possible without collapse.

The full corrective sequence:

1. Notice the busy-ness as the protective structure it is. The naming is the start.
2. Start [[daily-thank-you-notes-to-3-to-5-people]] to build the base of recognized support.
3. After a few weeks, deliberately set aside 2-3 hours, 2-3 times, for [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]].
4. Verify the principle that the contact is dramatically easier than the avoidance and that the avoided thing is not the catastrophe the avoidance has been preparing for.

## The Modern Variants

The anti-pattern shows up in specific forms in the current cultural moment that intensify its grip:

- **Constant scrolling.** Phones provide always-available distraction. Every moment of potential self-contact gets filled with the feed. The person becomes literally unable to be alone with themselves because the device is always present.
- **Productivity culture.** The cultural valorization of busy-ness provides moral cover for the avoidance. The person is not avoiding their inner life; they are being responsible, ambitious, accomplished. The busy-ness reads as virtue.
- **Stacking growth practices.** A particular sophisticated form: the person is doing meditation, breathwork, journaling, therapy, coaching — and using the stack to manage the avoided feeling rather than meet it. The practices are deployed in the service of the avoidance. See [[breath-as-fertile-soil-vs-breath-as-control]] and [[breath-to-self-regulate-instead-of-feel]] for the related read.
- **Achievement-as-self-completion.** The person is busy producing the achievement that they believe will produce the felt sense of being enough. The achievement never produces the felt sense because the felt sense requires the contact the busy-ness prevents. See [[overachieving-is-survival-mode]] and [[the-joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]].

The variants share the structure: motion as protection from contact. The specific motion varies; the underlying anti-pattern is the same.

## What This Anti-Pattern Is Not

- **Not "don't be productive."** The anti-pattern is not productivity as such; it is productivity-as-avoidance. A productive life that includes deliberate contact with the inner material is not the anti-pattern. The diagnostic is not the volume of activity; it is whether direct contact with the avoided material ever happens.
- **Not "don't have a full schedule."** Full schedules are not by themselves the anti-pattern. The anti-pattern is when the fullness is structured specifically to prevent contact, and any window for contact gets immediately filled.
- **Not "all distraction is avoidance."** Some forms of rest, play, and pleasure are not avoidance. The diagnostic is whether the activity is in service of presence or in service of preventing presence. The same activity can be either depending on the relationship.
- **Not "you have to do retreats."** The corrective is not necessarily multi-day retreats. The 2-3 hours, 2-3 times prescription is intentionally accessible. The anti-pattern is not the absence of retreats; it is the absence of any direct contact, in any form.

## The Coaching Move When You See This

When a coachee presents [[i-feel-alone-in-rooms-despite-success]] and is also reporting the kind of schedule that prevents inner contact, the coaching move is:

1. Reflect the structure back. "It sounds like there are no windows in your life where you are alone with yourself for any length of time. Is that accurate?"
2. Ask what would happen if there were such a window. The answer usually surfaces the avoided material directly. "If you sat for two hours with nothing to do, what would you feel?"
3. Name the function of the busy-ness without making it bad. "The busy-ness is doing protective work. It is preventing a contact that has felt unsafe to make. The work is not to be less busy. The work is to make the contact, briefly, with intention, so the busy-ness can stop doing that job."
4. Offer [[daily-thank-you-notes-to-3-to-5-people]] as the entry and [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]] as the deeper move, in that order.

The move does not require the coachee to immediately stop being busy. It surfaces the function. Once the function is visible, the coachee can begin to make choices about it.

## Tone

The naming of this anti-pattern is best done with neither judgment nor heaviness. The busy-ness is reasonable. The person who built it built it for reasons. The work is not to shame them out of it. The work is to make visible that it is doing more harm than the contact would, and that the contact is verifiable in 2-3 sessions.

When teaching the anti-pattern, lead with the structural account rather than the personal critique. "This is what busy-ness does. This is the cost of the busy-ness over time. This is what becomes available when the busy-ness pauses long enough for the contact to happen." The teaching is about the structure, not about the person's failure to see it.
