# Don't Try to Change Your Partner

## The Anti-Pattern

The position in a long-term relationship of trying to get your partner to be different. The partner is treated as a project, a problem to fix, a resistance to overcome. The framing is usually wrapped in care -- "I just want them to be happy" or "I just want them to grow" -- but the underlying move is "you need to be other than you are for me to be okay."

This includes:
- Trying to get them to share your path of growth or therapy or practice
- Trying to get them to stop doing the thing they always do that bothers you
- Trying to get them to want the things you want
- Trying to get them to feel different
- Strategic conversations whose purpose is to produce a change in them
- Indirect maneuvering -- gifts, articles sent, friends recruited -- whose purpose is to produce a change in them

The anti-pattern is so common that it often goes unrecognized as a pattern at all. It is the default operating mode in many partnerships.

## Why It Fails

It treats the partner as an instrument of your happiness rather than as a person doing their own work. It violates the principle that you are responsible for your own happiness (see [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]]). It puts the locus of your state in their behavior, which means you can never be okay until they comply.

"If you think that your partner is going to do anything that makes you happy, like if you have the fantasy that, oh, if they become this and this and that, they'll make me happy. That's like an absolute illusion. It's not how it works." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

It also fails because it produces resistance in the partner. Pressure to change activates the part of them that defends their integrity against being defined by you. The pressure produces the opposite of the change you are seeking. The harder you push, the less they move, the more frustrated you become, the more you push. The cycle is self-sustaining.

Joe is direct about what this position is:

"Yeah, that's, that's hell. That's absolute hell. Yeah, Tara and I definitely went through that phase. It was brutal. Yeah, and full of arrogance and hubris and distraction from your own shit." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The phrase "distraction from your own shit" is the key. Trying to change the partner is one of the most efficient ways to avoid doing your own work. The energy that would otherwise go to your own material gets redirected into managing theirs.

## The Most Common Version: The Convert-Your-Partner-to-Your-Path Trap

A specific instance Joe warns about: one partner discovers a transformation path (this work, psychedelics, therapy, meditation, anything) and starts trying to get the other on board.

"I see that all the time where people start discovering something in themselves, and, and I would say out of a deep love and care, they want their partner to join them, but also out of a desire to be happy. Like, oh, if they change, I'll be happy. That's the thing." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The motivation is mixed. Real care is present. So is the disguised wish that the partner's change will make you happy. The disguised wish is the part that converts the care into pressure.

Joe and Tara's own example: Tara went a separate path for a decade. Joe could not control that and could not make her join his path. The repeated attempts to get her on board were themselves the problem.

The best approach in that situation is to learn what the partner's path actually is. They may have a path that does not look like yours. They may have a path that operates on a different time scale. They may not be on a path at all, in which case the conversation is different -- not "be on my path" but "I notice you are not doing the work we agreed to do."

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement is twofold:

**1. Drop the project of changing them.** Treat their current behavior as theirs. Take them as they are, today, not as you would have them be. Stop running the calculation of how to get them to be different. See [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]].

**2. Turn the attention to what about them is hard for you to be with.** Whatever you are trying to change in them is something you cannot be with in yourself. See [[what-you-cant-love-in-partner-is-what-you-cant-love-in-self]]. The work is on you.

If, after doing the work on yourself, you discover that you genuinely cannot be in the relationship with the partner as they are, that is data. The data is about the configuration, not about whose fault it is. The response is honest -- "I can't be in this relationship with you as you are" -- rather than continued pressure for them to change.

## The Honest Carve-Out

The principle does not preclude honest naming of how their behavior affects you. Stating "when you do X, I feel Y" is not trying to change them. Asking for what you want is not trying to change them. Drawing a boundary is not trying to change them.

The line: are you stating your truth and leaving them free to respond, or are you stating something whose purpose is to produce a specific change in their behavior? The former is honesty. The latter is the anti-pattern.

The test: if they hear you fully and decide to do nothing different, are you okay? If yes, you were stating your truth. If no, you were trying to change them.

## What This Anti-Pattern Often Hides

The desire to change the partner usually hides:

- A wound the partner is being asked to heal by being different
- A demand for happiness that is being addressed to the partner because the person cannot generate it themselves
- A trauma configuration the partner is fitting precisely (see [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]])
- A part of the self that is being projected onto the partner (see [[projections]] and [[what-you-cant-love-in-partner-is-what-you-cant-love-in-self]])
- An avoidance of doing your own work

The hiding is what makes the anti-pattern so durable. The energy of trying to change the partner feels like care, feels like investment in the relationship, feels like doing the work. It is often the substitute for doing the actual work.
