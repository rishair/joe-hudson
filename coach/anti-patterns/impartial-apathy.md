# Don't confuse apathy with impartiality

## The Anti-Pattern

Two versions of this failure:

**Version 1: "I don't care."** Saying "I don't care" is almost always a sign of caring. It is a strategy to avoid the pain of wanting. The person disowns the want because the want hurts. Joe's daughter at age eight or nine figured this out: "Dad, I think I know what 'I don't care' means. It means I care."

"When people are saying I don't care, they're saying I do care, but it's hurting so much that I don't want to care." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3

This is not [[impartiality]]. It is avoidance. The want is still driving behavior; it is just hidden. True impartiality does not require denying wants. It requires owning them. See [[wants-vs-partiality]].

**Version 2: Spiritual bypassing.** Trying to be so impartial that wants and humanity are transcended. "All is bliss." This leads to dissociation. If the practice of impartiality saps joy from life, it has gone wrong.

"If you're trying to be so impartial that you don't have any wants and you're above your humanity, you start disassociating. All is bliss and that kind of thing, which is true, it is all bliss. But if you're denying your own wants and your own humanity, it'll sap all the joy out of your life." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3

## Why It Fails

Both versions deny wants rather than owning them. Denied wants do not disappear. They drive behavior from underground. The person becomes either passive-aggressive ("whatever you want") or disconnected from their own life.

Impartiality without wants is not impartiality. It is numbness.

## What To Do Instead

Own wants openly and vulnerably. [[impartiality]] means holding wants lightly while being transparent about them, not pretending they do not exist.

A person who is genuinely impartial about a conversation can say: "This isn't holding any juice for me. How is it for you?" That is not apathy. It is honest engagement.
