# Don't Skip the Consultation Because It Would Evoke Uncomfortable Emotions

## The Anti-Pattern

A leader is about to make a decision that affects a team they are not part of. The right move is to talk to the team first -- hear their objections, hear the situation, then decide. The leader skips the conversation. They make the decision without consulting. They tell themselves they don't have time, or they already know enough, or the decision is theirs to make.

The actual reason: the consultation would surface emotions that are uncomfortable to be in. Anger from the team. Grief over what they're losing. Pleading to reconsider. Difficult information that would force a harder decision. The leader avoids the consultation not because the consultation lacks value but because being in the room with the team's response is hard.

The avoidance is dressed as efficiency. It registers internally as a reasonable executive judgment about time. The pattern is hidden from the leader by the very thing the pattern is protecting them from.

## Why It Fails

The short-term ease produces a long-term mess. The conversation that gets skipped today produces years of distrust, disengagement, and politics. Each unconsulted decision adds another layer of "they don't see us, they don't include us, decisions just happen to us." The cumulative effect of many skipped conversations is a team that no longer believes consultation is real even when it happens.

The metaphor Joe reaches for: it's easier in the way it's easier not to potty train a dog.

"It's easier in the way it's easier not to potty train a dog... You know, like, I mean, like that day it's much easier than training the dog, but the rest of your life you're gonna have like poop on your rug." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The image is exact. One uncomfortable training session prevents an indefinite stream of unpleasantness. One uncomfortable consultation prevents an indefinite stream of damaged trust. The avoidance is not avoiding the discomfort; it is just postponing and multiplying it.

## What Joe Says Instead

The move is [[listen-to-objections-before-deciding]] -- have the conversation, hear the team, then decide, even if the decision is the same as the one you would have made without listening. The consultation is not optional; it is the structural thing that makes the decision land.

The underlying capacity required: being able to be in the room with the emotions the consultation evokes. This is a developable capacity. It is also a non-negotiable leadership skill. A leader who cannot tolerate the emotional weight of consulting the people they are deciding about is operating with a hard ceiling on how good their culture can become.

See also: the related principle that the same person who avoids hard emotions at home is going to avoid them at work. The pattern is the leader's, not the situation's. See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]].

## The Specific Form This Takes With Layoffs

The layoff version of the pattern: a decision is made about who to cut without consulting the manager whose people are being cut. The avoidance is structural -- "we don't want HR leaks" or "we have to move fast" -- but the operative reason is often that consulting the manager would mean being in the room with the manager's anger or grief, or hearing information that would make the decision harder.

The consequence: the manager finds out about the decision when the team does. The manager's trust in leadership is damaged in a way that does not heal. The next time a decision is made above the manager, the manager is operating from "they don't tell me anything." The cycle compounds.

The structural cost is not just the manager. The team observes that even the manager was not consulted, and concludes that no one is ever really consulted at this organization. The unconsulted layoff produces years of organizational caution because the implicit lesson is: decisions can land on you without warning, so optimize for self-protection.

## How the Pattern Hides

The pattern hides through a chain of plausible reasons:

- "I don't have time for the conversation right now"
- "I already know what they'd say"
- "It's my decision to make, not a committee decision"
- "Bringing them in would slow everything down"
- "They wouldn't have the strategic context anyway"
- "If I consult them, I'm signaling weakness or indecision"

Each reason is true in some abstract sense and operationally serving avoidance in the specific situation. The pattern is not "the leader has bad reasons." The pattern is "the leader has reasons that happen to be the reasons that let them skip the part they don't want to do."

The diagnostic for the leader: when about to skip a consultation, ask -- if the consultation would be enjoyable, would I still be skipping it? If the answer is no, the skip is being driven by the emotional cost, not by the operational reason being cited.

## What Makes the Capacity Develop

The leader who has done their own emotional work -- who can be present with grief, anger, disappointment, fear, both in themselves and in others -- can run the consultation without the avoidance reflex. The capacity is not "be unaffected by the team's reaction." It is "be present with the reaction without needing to fix, dismiss, or escape it."

This is one of the specific places where [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]] shows up as a concrete constraint. The leader who cannot sit with their own difficult emotions cannot sit with the team's. The skip-the-consultation pattern is the operational expression of that limit. Expanding the inner capacity is what makes the outer move available.

## Joe's Words

"It's easier in the way it's easier not to potty train a dog... that day it's much easier than training the dog, but the rest of your life you're gonna have like poop on your rug." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The image is offered as a corrective without scorn. It is not "shame on you for not having the conversation." It is "look at what you are choosing -- one uncomfortable moment vs. years of mess. Are you sure you are making the trade you think you're making?"

## When the Pattern Is Most Common

- Around layoffs and reorgs
- Around budget decisions that will affect specific teams
- Around hires or fires the leader hasn't yet announced
- Around strategic pivots that change what a team is working on
- Around any decision where the leader anticipates emotional pushback

The pattern is also visible in personal life: the parent who decides about the teenager without talking to the teenager, the partner who decides about the household without consulting the partner. The form transfers across domains because the underlying capacity is the same.

## What This Connects To

This anti-pattern is the specific operational form of [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]] applied to the decision-making process. The leader who is conflict-avoidant skips the consultation; the conflict-avoidant culture accumulates around them. The decisions get worse, the relationships get worse, the avoidance escalates. The cycle is the same as in any conflict-avoidant system.

It is also a specific instance of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]: the leader is avoiding the hard conversation, and the avoidance produces a culture in which they are constantly having hard conversations about decisions that did not get the consultation they needed. The thing being avoided is being created at scale by the avoidance itself.

## Tone

Joe's tone here is dry and slightly amused. The dog metaphor lands with the comedic precision of someone who has seen the same pattern many times. There is no moralizing. The pattern is just inefficient by its own logic. Naming it that way makes the change easier than treating it as a character flaw.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader has skipped consulting the team about a decision and is being asked why
- A leader is rationalizing the skip with operational reasons that the consultant senses are covering for avoidance
- A team is exhibiting the downstream pattern (distrust, disengagement, "they don't tell us anything") and the upstream cause needs to be named
- A coachee is asking why their decisions land badly even when they are good decisions
- A consultant is being asked how to interrupt a leader's repeated pattern of unconsulted decisions
