# Don't Answer "How Do I Stop?" as If It Were a Real Question

## The Anti-Pattern

When a person has been working with a compulsive pattern — fixing, judging, pleasing, controlling — and asks "how do I stop?", the temptation is to answer the question. The answer would be an instruction or a technique. Giving the instruction is the anti-pattern.

The question is not a real question. It is the pattern re-firing in question-form. The person who is compulsively fixing is now fixing the fixing. The "how do I stop" is a fix-request. Giving the fix reinstalls the pattern at a higher level. The person now has a new thing to fix-themselves-into-doing, which is the same operation that produced the problem.

"It's, um, the question— that's the question that leads to failure. And so it lets you stay in the loop." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The structural fact: any "how do I stop X" question, where X is itself a compulsive doing-pattern, will reproduce X by answering the question. The how-do-I-stop frame requires a doing as the answer. The pattern was a doing. More doing cannot undo a doing-pattern.

## Why It Fails

The doing the person was caught in was the response to an underneath-feeling they could not be with. The doing was the substitute for being-with. When a "how do I stop the doing" question is answered with a new doing, the underneath-feeling is still unaddressed. The new doing has to be sustained continuously, which is exactly the same dynamic the original doing was caught in.

The instruction also installs a new measurement layer. The person now has a metric — "am I doing the new technique?" — which becomes a new field for self-judgment. They will fail at the new technique sometimes; the failure produces shame; the shame drives more fixing. The instruction has multiplied the loop by one.

The instruction also confirms the frame that what is needed is a technique. The frame is the problem. The work is to drop the technique-seeking entirely. Giving a technique, even a good one, signals that technique is the appropriate response, which keeps the person in the seeking-mode that is the pattern.

## The Right Question

The right question is the inversion. Not "how do I stop?" but "how do I be?" Not "what do I do?" but "what do I undo?"

"The question is, how do you be with people? And it's the exact opposite of a doing. It is an undoing, as you're learning right now. So to ask, how do you do it, is counterproductive." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The inverted question shifts the operation from action to perception. From production to release. From the technique-seeking mode to the noticing mode. The answer is no longer a thing to do; it is a thing to stop doing.

See [[undoing-not-doing]] for the broader principle that runs through this. The doing/undoing distinction is the structural feature that makes the original question wrong.

## The "How Do I Undo It?" Variant

When the coach has named the undoing as the work, some clients will helpfully rephrase: "how do I undo it?" This is the same trap with vocabulary updated. The how-do-I question structure is the pattern; replacing "do" with "undo" inside the structure does not exit the structure.

The source session handles this directly:

- **Guest:** "Is that the same by asking, how do I undo it?"
- **Joe:** "Let's find out. How do you be with me? The answer: undo it."

The exchange is a small loop, intentionally. Joe takes the guest's "how do I undo it?" and converts it back into the actual question ("how do you be with me?"). The answer to that question is "undo it." The undo-it is not a technique; it is the dropping of the doing-attempt. The guest has to discover this by trying to apply the question and feeling what happens.

What Joe is teaching here: the answer cannot be received as content. It has to be discovered as practice. Any phrasing that lets the answer be received as content is the trap. The phrasings that work are the ones that put the person back into the live attempt.

## What Joe Does Instead

When the "how do I stop" question arrives, Joe names the trap and refuses to answer.

"It's the question that leads to failure. And so it lets you stay in the loop." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The naming is the move. The person now sees that their question is the pattern firing. Once the question is seen, the person can drop it. The dropping is the work. The work cannot be done for them by an answer.

After the naming, Joe redirects to the inverted question. "How do you be with me?" The redirected question is then worked in the room, not answered abstractly. The work is the live experience of being-with, which is what undoing actually feels like. See [[make-the-session-itself-the-demonstration]].

## When This Anti-Pattern Is Most Tempting

The anti-pattern is most tempting when:

- The client has done good work in the session and asks the closing-question that seems like it wants a take-home.
- The client is sincere and the question is delivered earnestly.
- The coach has a known technique that addresses the pattern.
- The client is paying for the session and the coach feels an obligation to send them home with something actionable.
- The frame "every coaching session should produce an actionable next step" is operating in the background.

The temptation to answer is high precisely when the answer would do the most damage. The clearer the question and the better the available answer, the more important it is to refuse the question and name the trap.

## What "Naming the Trap" Sounds Like

The naming does not have to be elaborate. The lightest form is best.

- "That's the question that leads to failure. So it lets you stay in the loop."
- "That's the same pattern in question form."
- "Asking how to stop is the doing-pattern reapplied to the doing-pattern."
- "I notice the question is itself the thing you're asking about."

The naming is delivered without judgment. The person is not being criticized for asking the question; the question is just being seen. The seeing is enough. The client, once they see, will usually drop the question on their own. If they don't, the coach can re-extend the live invitation: "let's just be with each other, no fixing, what happens?"

## When the Client Resists the Naming

Some clients will not accept the naming and will press for an answer. "But I really need to know what to do." The pressing is the pattern intensifying. The right response is not to relent and answer; it is to receive the intensity as part of what the person is sitting with.

"The need to know what to do is what the pattern feels like. Let's stay here for a minute and feel it."

The pressing usually softens once the pressing itself is met with presence rather than with the answer it is demanding. The body, registering that the answer is not coming, settles. What was a demand for technique becomes available as the actual material of the session.

## Anti-Patterns of the Anti-Pattern

A coach who has learned this principle can over-apply it. The over-application is its own failure mode.

- **Refusing every how-to question.** Some how-to questions are genuine and deserve genuine answers. "How do I have this conversation with my mom?" may be a real planning question, not a meta-instance of a pattern. The discrimination is whether the question is about a discrete situation or about a chronic doing-pattern. The chronic doing-pattern is the case for the anti-pattern principle.
- **Naming the trap as a coaching flourish.** The naming has to land in service of the client's recognition. If the naming becomes a coach-move the coach is performing, the client will read the performance and disengage.
- **Refusing all instruction.** There are moments when a small concrete instruction is appropriate. The principle is not that no instruction can ever be given; the principle is that the "how do I stop my chronic compulsive doing" question specifically cannot be answered with an instruction without reproducing the pattern.
