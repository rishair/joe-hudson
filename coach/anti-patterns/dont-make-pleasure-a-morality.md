# Don't Make Pleasure a Morality

## The Anti-Pattern

The person hears the pleasure teaching and converts it into a rule: "I am supposed to feel pleasure all the time." Or: "There must be pleasure in every moment and if I am not finding it I am failing the practice." Or: "A nuclear bomb goes off and I should be finding the pleasure in it." The teaching has been turned into a have-to. The have-to enacts the exact configuration the teaching was trying to undo.

"I don't want pleasure to become a morality, or a lot of people when they listen to this, they might take things into an extreme and say, oh, I have to feel pleasure all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The structural reason this fails: pleasure operates in get-to mode, not have-to mode. The have-to mode is the chase. The chase is the avoidance of pleasure. So treating the pleasure teaching as a moral rule installs the very apparatus that prevents the pleasure from arriving. The practitioner doing the discipline of finding pleasure everywhere is, by structure, the practitioner who cannot find pleasure anywhere.

## Why It Fails

The receive-state and the produce-state are incompatible body configurations. The teaching points toward the receive-state: pleasure is what is here when attention can land on the sensation that is here. The receive-state is permissive, slow, unhurried, attentive. The produce-state is the opposite: directed, urgent, oriented toward an outcome that is not yet present.

The have-to converts the practice into the produce-state. "I have to feel pleasure" is the body trying to make the pleasure happen. The making is itself the obstruction.

"Pleasure can't become a goal or something you're supposed to do or have to do. It's more of a noticing or receiving or getting to do, because it's just a lot less pleasurable, if pleasurable at all, if it becomes a have-to or a should or a trying or anything like that." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The reframing matters. Pleasure is a get-to. The system that grasps this can rest into it. The system that converts it back to a have-to has not grasped it and is doing a different practice that produces different results.

## The Extremism Failure Mode

A specific variant: the person takes the teaching to its absurd limit and uses the limit to invalidate the teaching. "But what about a nuclear bomb? Are you saying I should find pleasure in that?" The extremism is itself the morality trap operating — the person is testing the rule for universal coverage and using the failure of universal coverage as evidence that the rule is wrong.

Joe heads this off directly:

"A nuclear bomb goes off, there's no pleasure in that. So I would just say to anybody listening, let go of the extremes and just see how deep the rabbit hole goes. Take some moments here and there and see, oh, can I find pleasure in this? Can I find pleasure in this?" -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The reframe: the teaching is not "find pleasure in every moment universally." The teaching is "find pleasure in many more moments than the system has been allowing." The horizon of the practice is not coverage; it is discovery. The discovery extends as far as it extends. The places it does not extend are not failures of the practice; they are the boundary conditions of the practice. Honoring the boundary is part of the practice.

The extremism failure mode also tends to produce moral self-recrimination when the rule is not met — "I am not enlightened because I cannot find pleasure in this terrible thing." The self-recrimination is the morality trap doing its damage in plain sight. The corrective is to drop the universal-rule framing entirely.

## What Joe Says Instead

The instruction in its operational form:

"Just take some moments here and there and see, oh, can I find pleasure in this? Can I find pleasure in this? By noticing the sensations moving." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The phrasing is exploratory, not commanding. "Can I find pleasure in this?" is a question the practitioner asks, not a standard the practitioner is being measured against. The question can be answered yes or no honestly. The practice is the asking. The practice is not the achieving of a particular ratio of yes-answers.

Joe also frames the corrective by reference to the get-to/have-to distinction:

"It's more of a noticing or receiving or getting to do." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The receiving framing is critical. Receiving is something one does in a body that is open and available. The receiving cannot be done in a body that is in producing mode. The reorientation from "I have to" to "I get to" is not a cognitive shift; it is a body shift. The body has to find the configuration in which receiving is possible. Once that configuration is found, the get-to framing becomes felt rather than recited.

## Joe's Words

"I just note that because I think it's really important that it doesn't become like, I am supposed to feel pleasure, because by the way, that'll chase all the pleasure right out of the situation that you're in." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The "chase all the pleasure right out" framing is the structural mechanism stated as observable consequence. The have-to does not merely fail to produce pleasure; it actively chases away whatever pleasure would otherwise have been available. The morality is more destructive than the absence of practice, because the morality installs the apparatus that prevents what the practice was supposed to deliver.

## In Coaching

How to spot the pattern in a session:

- The person reports "I have to find pleasure in this" or "I should be feeling pleasure right now"
- The person uses the pleasure teaching to judge themselves for not feeling pleasure
- The person tests the teaching with extreme counterexamples ("but what about X terrible thing?")
- The person reports doing the practice diligently and getting nothing from it (the diligence is the morality)
- The person treats the pleasure capacity as something they are failing at rather than something they are exploring

The intervention:

1. Name the morality directly. "You're treating this as a should. The teaching is the opposite of that."
2. Reframe to get-to. "Can you ask the question 'can I find pleasure here?' instead of stating 'I have to find pleasure here'?"
3. Honor the boundary. "There are moments where you cannot find pleasure. That's fine. The practice is not coverage; it is exploration."
4. Watch for the morality reasserting itself in a new form. The pattern is sticky. It may come back disguised as "I should be exploring more."

## Tone

Joe delivers this warning lightly, almost in passing. The lightness is part of the medicine. Heavy emphasis on "don't do this" would itself be a morality. The note is offered as information about how the practice works, with the expectation that the practitioner will calibrate from there.
