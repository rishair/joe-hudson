# Don't Coach From the Presumption That the Person Is Broken (Even When They Present a 'Problem')

## The Anti-Pattern

When a person presents a problem in a coaching session, the default coaching response is to try to help with the problem. The trying-to-help often carries an implicit premise: there is something wrong with the person that needs to be fixed. The premise is rarely stated; it operates underneath the words.

The premise is the anti-pattern. Even when delivered with warmth and skill, coaching from the brokenness premise communicates "you are broken" to the person. The communication contradicts whatever else is being said.

A person who presents a problem with the disclaimer "I don't think this can be fixed" is often pre-warning the coach. They have been treated as broken before. They have felt the pressure to demonstrate fixing. They are testing whether this coach will collapse into fix-mode or whether something else is possible.

## Joe's Counter-Move

Joe declines the fix-frame explicitly and early, often in the first or second turn of the session. He does not argue with the disclaimer ("of course it can be fixed, let me help"). He does not reassure ("we'll figure it out together"). He drops the frame:

"I have absolutely no interest in fixing you. It assumes you're broken." -- Joe Hudson, Am I Being Authentic Or Fake?

The move is two-part. First, the declaration of intent: I am not here to fix you. Second, the naming of the premise: fixing assumes you are broken. The second part is what does the work. The person hears not just that this coach will not fix them, but why fixing would be the wrong move — it would treat them as broken, which they are not.

The declaration changes the frame of the entire session. From that point forward, neither person is operating from "there is something wrong here that needs to be made right." Both are operating from "there is something happening here, let's see what it is."

## Why It Fails

When a coach operates from the brokenness premise:

1. **The person feels broken.** The premise communicates regardless of the words. The person leaves the session more identified with being broken than they came in.

2. **The "fix" doesn't last.** Even if a fix appears in the session, the underlying premise that produced the need-for-fix remains. The problem recurs in a different form.

3. **The person performs being fixed.** The pressure to demonstrate improvement converts the session into theater. The person learns to perform progress rather than actually move.

4. **The coach becomes a recurring necessity.** If the brokenness is the premise, the fixer is always needed. The coach becomes part of the structure that requires the brokenness.

5. **The deepest move becomes unavailable.** What the person actually needs is often to be met as whole. That meeting is impossible from the brokenness premise. The premise blocks the move that would do the work.

Joe is explicit that the brokenness premise is itself the problem in cases like depression: "There's a way to think of it as 'there's something wrong with me' is the depression. And so any fight to make yourself better is part of the self-fulfilling prophecy of depression." -- Joe Hudson, Exploring Depression in the Head, Heart, and Gut. See [[symptoms-vs-root-causes-of-depression]]. The same logic applies more broadly: the "fix me" premise is the engine of much of what shows up as problem.

## When This Anti-Pattern Is Most Tempting

The anti-pattern is most tempting when:

- The person presents a problem clearly and asks for help
- The coach has a known technique that fits the presented problem
- The person is in pain and the coach feels the discomfort of the pain
- The coach is being paid and feels an obligation to deliver value
- The person has had previous coaches who delivered fixes and expects the same
- The coach's identity is tied up in being a helpful problem-solver. See [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]].

The temptation to fix is not bad. It is usually well-intentioned. The anti-pattern is to act on the temptation in a way that confirms the brokenness premise.

## What Joe Does Instead

The alternative is to coach from the presumption of wholeness. The person presenting a problem is whole. The problem is something happening in the wholeness, not a defect of the wholeness. The coaching is investigation of what is happening, not repair of the person.

This is the operational form of [[send-the-message-that-youre-whole]] applied to the coaching session itself, not just to supporting someone who is depressed. The supporting-the-depressed-friend version is the broader case; the coaching-session version is a specific application.

Practically, the move looks like:

1. Decline fix-mode explicitly when the person opens with a fix-request
2. Stay curious about what is happening without trying to make it different
3. Receive the person's experience as accurate perception of real territory
4. Refuse to position the coach as the holder of the answer
5. Let the work happen without forcing arrival at a resolution

See [[loving-presence-as-defense]] for the failure mode this anti-pattern can drift into if "no fixing" is used as a defense against actually engaging.

## What Joe Says Instead

"I have absolutely no interest in fixing you. It assumes you're broken." -- Joe Hudson, Am I Being Authentic Or Fake?

The line itself can be useful as a frame-setting move at the start of a session, especially when the person opens with a fix-request or a "this can't be fixed" disclaimer. The line declines the frame both ways: not as fixer, not as collaborator in the brokenness premise.

A related move, used when a person is collapsed into self-improvement: "Self-improvement starts with the assumption that something is wrong with you. Self-awareness starts with nothing wrong." See [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]]. The improvement orientation installs the brokenness premise even when the person is doing the work on themselves.

## The Three-Turn Refusal: "What's Wrong With You?" / "Nothing" / "So What's to Fix?"

A second canonical form of the refusal operates as a three-turn opening sequence rather than as a direct declaration. The form is most useful when the person opens the session by stating that they are trying to fix themselves.

The sequence:

1. Coach: "What's wrong with you?"
2. Person: "Nothing's wrong with me."
3. Coach: "So what's to fix?"

The third turn does the refusal. The person has just stated, in their own voice, that nothing is wrong. The coach surfaces the contradiction between that statement and the fix-attempt. The person hears their own non-endorsement of the brokenness-premise echoed back. The frame wobbles in the first 30 seconds.

This variant works because the person rarely actually believes they are broken in the way the fix-attempt presupposes. The fix-pattern operates on an inherited or absorbed premise, not on a considered judgment. The simple act of asking "what's wrong with you?" and receiving "nothing" gets the person to state the position they have been acting against. The coach does not have to argue with the brokenness-frame; the person has just declined to defend it.

See [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]] for the move in its detailed form, including the fix-premise variant section that documents this exact sequence.

The variant is the canonical opening for the [[why-am-i-always-trying-to-fix]] presenting concern. The session needs the broken-frame refused before the actual work can begin. The three-turn sequence is the most economical version of the refusal.

The variant works alongside the declarative form ("I have absolutely no interest in fixing you, it assumes you're broken"). The declarative form names the coach's stance directly. The three-turn form gets the person to discover the same thing from their own statement. Both refuse the same frame; the right choice depends on whether the person needs to hear the coach name it or needs to hear themselves name it.

## The Tone of the Refusal

The decline is delivered warmly, not defensively. The "no interest in fixing you" is a statement of stance, not a refusal to engage. It is the equivalent of a doctor who says "I'm not going to treat you as a problem to solve, I'm going to be with you and see what's actually happening here." The patient feels the difference between being met as a person and being processed as a case.

The warmth is what allows the refusal to land as care rather than as withholding. The coach is not refusing to help. The coach is refusing to participate in the brokenness premise that has been making the person's actual help less available.
