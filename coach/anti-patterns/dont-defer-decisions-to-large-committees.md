# Don't Defer Decisions to Large Committees

## The Anti-Pattern

Routing decisions through committees of more than 5-6 people, or having more than 5-8 direct reports report to one leader, structurally prevents decisions from being made well. The size of the decision-making group is not a small detail; past a threshold, the group cannot decide regardless of the goodwill or intelligence in the room.

The pattern often looks like distributed authority, consultation, or care. It produces the opposite. The OSS literally recommended this pattern as a sabotage technique. See [[cia-organization-sabotage-manual]].

## Why It Fails

Past a certain group size:

- Every decision requires reading too many faces, modeling too many positions, predicting too many objections
- Responsibility diffuses to the point that no one is actually responsible for the decision
- The slowest member sets the floor for the entire group's pace
- The decision-maker's authority is replaced by a politics of consensus
- The person who knows the work gets overruled by the group dynamics

"If they have over 5, it's a red flag. If they have over 8, I just know there's no functionality happening, or not a great deal of functionality happening in that organization. To have that many direct reports is just, you can't make a decision, or incredibly hard to make a decision." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The same logic applies to standing committees and decision-making bodies. Above six members, the body becomes a venue for performance rather than a venue for decision.

## Why It Looks Like Good Practice

The pattern survives because it has the visual signature of inclusion, care, and risk reduction. "We consulted everyone." "The committee reviewed it." "We did not act unilaterally." Each of these phrases reads as virtue while describing what is functionally sabotage.

The OSS knew this. The 1944 manual instructs operatives to advocate for exactly these moves under exactly these framings, knowing the framings would make the moves hard to oppose.

## What Joe Says Instead

- Keep decision-making bodies small (5 or fewer)
- Keep direct-report spans of control small (5 or fewer for high-functioning leaders)
- Identify the single person responsible for each decision and trust them to consult as needed
- Distinguish between decision-making meetings, discovery meetings, and execution meetings, and right-size each
- When a committee has grown past the threshold, split it or break out the deciders

The Gore-Tex split-at-150 model is a related practice: when any part of the organization exceeds 150 people, split it into two units of 75. The principle is the same -- size produces structural dysfunction past the threshold, and the response is structural.
