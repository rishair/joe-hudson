# Don't Add 'Anyway' to 'I Love You'

## The Anti-Pattern

Adding "anyway" (or "even though," "in spite of," "despite") to a statement of love or acceptance directed at a person's pain, shame, illness, or other difficult experience. The form looks supportive. The structure underneath communicates the opposite of what it intends.

Examples of the failure mode:
- "I love you anyway, even though you're in pain."
- "It's okay that you're in pain. I love you."
- "Despite everything you're going through, I still love you."
- "Even with this, you're still loved."
- "I love you in spite of your illness."

What every one of these sentences says, under the surface: the thing being mentioned is something the love is overcoming. The pain is a bad thing. The illness is a bad thing. The shame is a bad thing. The love is heroic for showing up despite this bad thing.

The opposite is the actual move. The love is for the person AND for the pain. The pain is not a thing being loved around. It is part of what is being loved.

## Why It Fails

The person being loved is, in their own experience, the person-with-the-pain. The pain is not separable from them in this moment. When the speaker says "I love you anyway," the structural message is "I love the person you are minus the pain. The pain is the obstacle my love has to get past."

This re-installs the very dynamic the person has been suffering under. The person has spent their life suspecting that their pain (or shame, or need, or illness) makes them less lovable, more inconvenient, harder to be around. They have been pulling the brakes on expressing the pain to remain lovable. See [[pulling-the-brakes]].

The "anyway" sentence confirms the brake's logic. It says: yes, the pain is the bad part. Yes, it requires being loved-past. Yes, you are right to want to hide it.

The work the person actually needs is for the pain to be loved, not loved-around. The pain is part of who they are right now. Loving the person without loving the pain leaves the pain still unloved. The hiding continues. The brake continues. The shame about the pain continues.

## What Joe Says Instead

The clean move is to love the thing itself. Not the person despite the thing. The thing.

> "It's not 'I love you anyway.' No, that still makes pain the bad guy."

And then, in the same session, what loving the thing actually sounds like:

> "You got to look at my face. You got to see that I actually do love your pain and anguish. And for me, it's not even holding space. It's a privilege and an honor to be with it."

The pain itself is the object of the love. Not a problem the love is overcoming. Not a fact about the person the love is willing to tolerate. The pain itself is loved. The anguish itself is loved. The framing change is precise and small and structural. It changes everything about what the person receives.

## The Specific Mistake This Pattern Catches

Many compassionate people, when faced with someone in chronic pain or chronic illness, default to "love you anyway." The intention is reassurance. The structure is rejection of the part causing pain. The person receiving the sentence is reassured at the surface level and rejected at the structural level.

This is a quiet anti-pattern. The speaker is not being unkind. They are using the language they were taught. But the language carries an implicit verdict on the difficult thing, and the receiver hears the verdict more clearly than they hear the love.

The repair is verbal. Drop the "anyway." Drop the "even though." Drop the "despite." Let the love include the thing.

- Instead of: "I love you anyway, even though you're in pain."
- Try: "I love you. I love your pain. It fucking sucks."

The "it fucking sucks" is not in tension with the love. It is part of the love. The love is meeting the pain in the pain, not above it.

## When the Person Catches the Coach Doing It

In the session this comes from, the coach actually did not say "I love you anyway." The client, scripting what he wanted to hear, included the phrase: "it's okay that I'm in pain, I love you anyway, it sucks." Joe corrects the script in real time:

> "It's not 'I love you anyway.' No, that still makes pain the bad guy."

The correction is offered without heat. Joe is not shaming the client for using the language. He is making the structural distinction visible so the client can ask for what he actually wants.

This is the second-order use of the anti-pattern: catching it when the person *asks* to be talked to this way, and renegotiating the request so what they receive will actually land. People sometimes ask for the wrong sentence because the wrong sentence is the only one their childhood modeled. Letting them ask for the right sentence is part of the work. See [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]].

## A Note on "It's Okay to"

The cousin of "I love you anyway" is "it's okay to feel [the feeling]." The same structural problem: the okay-ness has to be granted because there is an implicit not-okay-ness being overruled. The person is being permitted to feel the feeling rather than being met in the feeling.

Better: just be in the feeling with them. "Yeah. Ah, fuck. I hate it for you." This says nothing about whether it is okay. It is just present with the thing. The presence is what does the work. The permission framing keeps the permitting voice in charge and never quite gets to presence.
