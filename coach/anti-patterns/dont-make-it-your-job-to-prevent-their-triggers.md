# Don't Make It Your Job to Prevent Your Partner's Triggers

## The Anti-Pattern

A common framing in long-term partnership: "It is my job to prevent my partner from being triggered." The person organizes their behavior, their speech, their pace, and their disclosures around managing the partner's emotional state. They believe they are doing the loving thing.

The framing is the wrong job description. It produces the relationship that is technically still functioning while both people are quietly miserable.

"I working with a couple recently where they've been married for 16 years, have kids and everything, and they, they thought their job was to make sure the other person wasn't triggered. And now they're just pissed all the time, all the time at each other." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The 16-year marriage in Joe's example is not exceptional. It is the typical end-state of this framing.

## Why It Fails

**1. You cannot actually prevent your partner's triggers.** Triggers come from your partner's history, not from your behavior. The behavior is just the latest stimulus. You can avoid the topics they have already shown you are landmines, but new topics will produce new triggers. There is no behavior you can perform that makes them un-triggerable.

**2. The attempt to prevent triggers requires self-suppression.** The only way to walk carefully enough to prevent their reactions is to stop being yourself. This is [[walking-on-eggshells]]. It corrodes both you and the relationship.

**3. It takes away their developmental work.** A trigger is the partner's opportunity to find what they hold. If you successfully prevent the trigger, you also prevent the opportunity. You are "protecting" them from their own growth. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].

**4. The shoulding doubles the trigger.** Once a person believes it is their job to prevent the other's triggers, every trigger that occurs becomes evidence of their failure. The trigger is now the trigger plus the shame about not having prevented it. See Alexa's observation in the transcript: "I would even go so far as to say anytime you're shoulding yourself, that's also trigger."

## What Joe Says Instead

The job is not preventing your partner's triggers. The job is:

- Being yourself, including the parts the partner is sometimes triggered by
- Saying the things you have to say, kindly but actually saying them ([[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]])
- Being present with your own trigger when their trigger lands on you
- Letting them have their triggers as their own material to work
- Drawing boundaries when their emotional state is being directed at you ([[boundaries]])
- Welcoming their difficult emotions when you have capacity, not when you have shoulded yourself into doing so ([[dont-should-yourself-to-welcome-anger]])

"The work is say what you want, be yourself, don't worry about the consequences, don't get angry at each other." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The reframe: their triggers are theirs. Your withholding to prevent their triggers is yours. The first half is their work; the second half is yours.

## The Shame Variant

A common variant: "I am a bad partner because I keep triggering them." The person has organized their self-image around being the cause of the partner's pain.

This is shame doing trigger-prevention's work. Both partners have triggers. The person carrying the shame is one of two people; the shame is treating the configuration as if they alone produced it. See [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]] for the structural correction.

"You're well, you're creating a shame cycle either way. Like, if it's a should, it's a shame cycle. There's I think there's a healthy way to say, oh, this isn't the partner I want to be, and I want to do this. But if you're in the should, then you're in a shame spiral with it." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## The "But I Should Be There For Their Trigger" Variant

Another variant Joe and Brett both name: "I should be able to be there for my partner's trigger, so I'm going to suppress my own trigger so that their trigger can be held."

This is the trigger-prevention pattern with extra layers. The person is now (1) preventing themselves from being triggered, (2) suppressing the trigger that comes anyway, and (3) performing capacity to receive the partner's trigger while running on empty.

The correction Joe gives is boundaries. Sometimes the right move is "I'm not going to be here with you crying at me or being angry at me. I'm happy to come back to the conversation when that's over."

"Self-care is absolutely the priority in all of this work, to take care of yourself, to love yourself, to treat yourself with love and respect. You can't, you can't treat others without that." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

See [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]] for the underlying principle.

## How To Tell If You Are Doing This

- You have a mental catalog of topics you do not bring up with your partner
- You scan your partner's face before speaking to gauge their state
- You feel responsible when your partner is upset
- You modify your tone, your timing, or your facial expression around your partner constantly
- The thought of saying what you actually think produces fear
- You feel resentful about how much you have to hold back
- You believe you are protecting them, and you feel slightly proud of how much work this takes
- You experience yourself as a "good partner" specifically because of this work
