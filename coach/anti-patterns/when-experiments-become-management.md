# Don't Use the Experiment to Manage Yourself

## The Anti-Pattern

A person runs an experiment or a practice. It works. They start running it again because it worked. After a few rounds, the practice stops working. They run it harder, try variations, get frustrated. It still does not work the way it used to.

What happened: the practice was alive when the person did not know what would happen. Once the person started running it to produce a specific result (to calm down, to feel better, to be more present, to be more loving), the practice became a self-management tool. As a self-management tool, the same practice does not produce the same effect. The wonder is gone. The expected-outcome has replaced it.

"There was this time when I was doing these experiments that were helping me for a while, and then they would not help me, and then they would help me, and then they would not help me. And then so I do this experiment to great effect, and then it would stop working. And then I do this experiment to great effect and it would stop working. And I realized what was happening was if I did a practice and I didn't know what was going to happen, it worked really well. But as soon as I was using the practice to manage myself, it stopped fucking working." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## Why It Fails

The structural problem: the practice was producing its effect because of the state the person was in while doing it. The state was wonder, openness, willingness to find out. The outcome (the felt shift) was a downstream effect of the state.

When the person starts running the practice for the outcome, the state changes. They are no longer in wonder; they are in management. The state was the active ingredient. The action without the state does not work.

"It's very similar to, to, you know, if I'm, if I'm loving an emotion to, to change it, to, to get rid of it, I'm not actually loving it. If I'm, if I am running the experiment to manage myself, I'm no longer running the experiment. It doesn't work." -- Joe Hudson

The parallel Joe draws to "loving an emotion to get rid of it" is exact. The structure of the failure is the same: a process whose effectiveness depends on a particular stance, applied with the opposite stance, produces nothing. Love as a state transforms emotion. Love as a technique to remove emotion is not love. Wonder as a state transforms what is seen. Wonder as a technique to produce a result is not wonder.

## How the Slippage Happens

The slippage is subtle and rarely intentional. The typical sequence:

1. The person runs the experiment in genuine curiosity about what will happen.
2. The experiment produces a felt shift -- they feel better, more grounded, more open.
3. The person notices: "this works."
4. The person runs the experiment again, now with the implicit goal of producing the same felt shift.
5. The shift is smaller this time. The person tries harder.
6. The shift continues to diminish or disappears.
7. The person concludes the practice has stopped working.

The diagnostic moment is step 3. The recognition "this works" is the seed of the slippage. The recognition is true (the practice does work) but it converts the practice from inquiry into instrument. The instrument stops working because the practice was never an instrument; it was a state, and the state cannot be summoned by the same person who is now trying to use it as an instrument.

## The Larger Teaching

Joe names what this realization taught him: it was not one experiment. The pattern showed up in six or seven of his experiments before the principle became visible. The visible principle: trying to manage yourself usually does not go well.

"That didn't take, that wasn't one experiment. I learned that because I saw that pattern in like 6 or 7 experiments, which was like this huge teaching for me. That I had never read in any book, which is, oh, if you're trying to manage yourself, it's usually not going to go well for you." -- Joe Hudson

The teaching extends beyond experiments. Any time the person is using a tool to manage themselves -- a meditation practice, a journaling habit, a breathwork routine, an emotional-inquiry method -- the same dynamic can install itself. The tool worked because of the state of curiosity in which it was first used. The tool stops working when it becomes the way the person tries to be a certain way.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is to stay in wonder, impartiality, and view while running experiments. These are the active ingredients. The activities of the experiment are downstream of the states.

"If you stay in a place of wonder and impartiality and view, which is really the way you're approaching these experiments, that, wow, it works really well." -- Joe Hudson

This connects directly to [[dont-use-view-as-technique]]. VIEW is a state. The same logic applies. Wonder used to produce an outcome is not wonder. Impartiality used to engineer detachment is not impartiality. The states are what produce the results, and the states cannot be activated by the goal of producing the results.

## The Practical Recognition

When a practice you have been doing stops working, the diagnostic question is not "what is wrong with the practice?" It is "what stance did I bring to the practice this time, and what stance did I bring the first time?" The first-time stance was almost certainly wonder. The current stance is almost certainly management. The fix is not a new practice. It is the restoration of the original stance.

The restoration is not technique. It cannot be achieved by deciding to be in wonder. The most reliable move is to drop the practice for a while, let the management goal recede, and pick the practice up again only when there is genuine curiosity about it.

A second move: run a different experiment, one for which you have no prior expectation of outcome. The novelty itself is the structural protection against the management slippage. New experiments are easier to do in wonder than old ones.

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- A meditation practice that worked for months and now produces nothing
- An emotional-inquiry method that produced shifts and now produces flatness
- A journaling habit that revealed things and now produces familiar repeated content
- A breathwork practice that altered consciousness and now feels like exercise
- A coaching practice that produced movement and now produces predictable performances
- The person who has been doing the work and is now noticing themselves "doing the work" with a slight performance quality
- The person who is "loving" an emotion in order to make it go away

## The In-The-Moment Catch: The Felt-Insight About to Become a Discipline

The slippage is most catchable at the moment it is about to be installed. Immediately after a person has a felt-recognition in a session, the predictable next move is to ask how to turn the recognition into a practice. The question contains the trap.

The Why-Am-I-Money-Blocked session demonstrates the in-the-moment catch and articulates a specific five-step cascade for how the discipline conversion produces unsafety:

> Person: "So I turn that into a practice. I, I just try and do that over and over again until it has no sting. Like, what's—"
> Joe: "That's a pretty good way to make it into a lack of safety."
> Person: "Tell me more. What, what do you mean when you say that?"
> Joe: "Well, you're going to tell yourself to do that, and then the next step is you're going to wonder if you're going to do it, and then the next step is you're going to fail to do it at some point, and then you're going to beat yourself up for not doing it, and then you're going to feel not safe because you told yourself to do something that you're not doing. Like, typically that's how that moves."
> *(Source: Why Am I Money Blocked?)*

The cascade is five steps:

1. **You tell yourself to do it.** The instruction is installed as a should.
2. **You wonder if you're going to do it.** The doubt installs alongside the instruction. The doubt is part of the structure.
3. **You fail to do it at some point.** Inevitable. Any chronic instruction will be unmet at some point.
4. **You beat yourself up for not doing it.** The failure is converted into self-attack.
5. **You feel unsafe because you told yourself to do something that you're not doing.** The original work — which was about felt-safety — is now producing the opposite of itself. The discipline structure has reproduced the original problem.

The cascade is structural, not personal. Joe describes it as "typically that's how that moves" — it is what happens to almost everyone who turns a felt-recognition into a discipline. Knowing the cascade is what makes it possible to catch it before it installs.

The diagnostic phrasing to watch for in real time:

- "So I'll just do this over and over again..."
- "I'll turn this into a practice and..."
- "Until it has no sting / doesn't hurt anymore / becomes automatic..."
- "How do I make this a habit / a daily thing / a routine?"
- "I'll commit to doing this every day until..."

Each of these is the system reaching for the discipline form. The catch is to interrupt before the form installs, name the cascade, and pivot to a non-disciplinary form. See [[name-the-appreciation-arriving-in-the-moment]] for the specific pivot Joe used in the same session: when the discipline question is in the room and an alternative appreciative gesture is also in the room, the gesture itself becomes the answer.

The cascade is also visible after-the-fact. A person who reports "I started doing the practice and it worked great, then I noticed I was beating myself up about it, and now I feel worse than when I started" has run the full five steps. The recovery is the same: notice the cascade has run, drop the discipline frame, return to the original felt-territory the practice was pointing at.

## The Connection to the Aliveness-Surrogate

When the felt-insight is about safety specifically — when the person has just discovered that feeling the felt-unsafety directly is the route to empowerment rather than dodging it — the discipline conversion is particularly costly. The discipline reinstalls the unsafety it was meant to dissolve, but through the side door of self-attack rather than through the front door of avoidance.

The structural symmetry is important. Both the avoidance pattern (don't look at the bank account) and the discipline pattern (force yourself to do the practice) produce the same outcome (felt-unsafety) through opposite mechanisms. The body that has organized itself around unsafety as an aliveness vehicle (see [[unsafety-as-aliveness-vehicle]]) will tend to find new vehicles for the same felt-state. The discipline cascade is one of those new vehicles.

The corrective: stay with the felt-insight as it arose, in the form it arose in. The recognition was a present-moment event. The practice it points at is also a present-moment event, not a future repeated event. See [[name-the-appreciation-arriving-in-the-moment]] for the in-the-moment form that replaces the discipline form.

## The Connection to Authenticity

The deeper version of this anti-pattern: using any practice to make yourself a particular way is a form of self-improvement, and self-improvement is shame-inducing. See [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]. The shift from experiment-as-inquiry to experiment-as-management is the shift from authenticity to improvement. The same slippage Joe is naming in experiments is the slippage at the heart of why self-improvement fails.

The corrective is the same: experiment in service of self-discovery, not self-improvement. The experiment is the way you find out what is already true, not the way you make yourself into someone different. When the experiment becomes the way you make yourself different, it is no longer producing the discovery, and the alteration it was previously producing as a side effect of discovery is no longer arriving either.

## Tone

Joe shares this with the energy of a hard-won discovery -- something he had to learn through running experiments over years and watching them fail in a pattern. The teaching is delivered without judgment. The slippage is human and common. The naming of it is the gift; the person can now watch for it.
