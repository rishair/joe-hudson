# Don't Shame Kids to Change Behavior

## The Anti-Pattern

The standard parenting frame: shame the behavior, the child learns not to do it. Shame the late-coming-home, the messy room, the bad grade, the rude tone, the lying. The shame is supposed to function as the corrective signal. The expectation is that the corrected behavior will then stop.

The behavior does not stop. This is the anti-pattern. The shaming does not change the behavior; it locks it in. Years later, the child is 16 and still doing the same behavior. The shaming has not worked, despite the parent's continued investment in it.

## Why It Fails

Joe is direct about the mechanism:

"Notice that everything you shame your kid for hasn't changed. Yeah, it doesn't fucking change. You shame your kid for something, I guarantee they'll be 16 years old still doing it." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The "I guarantee" is doing a specific kind of work. It is an empirical claim, not an opinion. The parent can run the test in their own household. Pick a behavior that has been shamed for years. Is the behavior gone? Almost universally, no. The behavior has continued, often in the same form, sometimes in a more elaborated form.

The mechanism underneath is the same one as [[shame-drives-behavior]]: shame does not eliminate behavior; it produces behavior. The shame becomes a felt state that the child carries about themselves and around the behavior. The state itself recreates the behavior. The shame the parent is using as the cure is one of the active ingredients of the disease.

## The Test the Parent Can Run

The strongest move for a parent reading this anti-pattern is to test the claim against their own household. The test:

1. **List the behaviors you have shamed your child for over the past several years.** The eating, the messiness, the language, the screen time, the homework patterns.
2. **For each, ask: has the behavior actually stopped?** Honestly. Not "they're better than they were." Has the actual behavior — the thing you were trying to get them to stop doing — stopped?
3. **Notice what has happened instead.** Usually one of: the behavior continues openly, the behavior continues secretly, the behavior has shifted to a related form that the parent is also shaming, or the child has added a layer of shame about themselves that has not changed the behavior.

The test is not a rhetorical move. It is a real empirical check. If the parent finds that years of shaming have not changed the behavior, the parent has the data to consider that the shaming is not the variable doing the work. Something else is needed.

## What the Parent Was Trying to Do

The shaming impulse usually comes from a real concern. The parent does not want the child to be hurt by the behavior in the long run. The parent wants the child to be capable, regulated, kind, healthy. The shaming is a misapplied tool toward a real goal.

The corrective is not to drop the concern. The corrective is to drop the tool that does not work and find tools that do. The principle [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]] articulates the alternative: the child develops a moral compass on their own, around age 5-6, and self-shames when they violate their own values. The parent's job is to support that natural compass, not to install an external one through imposed shame.

The other available tools, in the absence of shame: connection, boundaries delivered cleanly, modeling the alternative behavior, witnessing the child's own self-correction without amplifying it. See [[stay-close-and-insist]], [[draw-boundaries-with-kids-to-let-anger-arise]], [[model-emotional-movement-for-your-kids]], and [[welcome-the-shame-without-extracting]].

## How the Parent Feels About Shaming

A related diagnostic: notice how the parent feels in the moment of shaming a child. The parent rarely feels good about it.

"I've never met somebody who's shaming their kid and then they're like, I feel great about that, you know? ... Oh my God, did you see me shame that kid? That was awesome. I'm such a good parent for shaming that kid. People feel like shit when they do that kind of thing." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The bad feeling is the parent's own inherent goodness registering that the move does not match the intention. The parent is doing something that does not feel right because they have been told this is what good parents do. The bad feeling is data: the parent's body knows the move does not produce what the parent wants.

This is one of the rare places where the parent's discomfort is an accurate reading of the situation. Many forms of parental guilt are misapplied. The discomfort while shaming a kid is correctly pointing at the fact that shaming is not the right tool. Following the discomfort leads the parent toward dropping the tool. Overriding the discomfort to stay "consistent with discipline" produces years of unsuccessful shaming.

## What Joe Says Instead

The principle that replaces this anti-pattern: do not impose shame. Trust the child's inherent goodness and natural compass. Provide connection. Draw clean boundaries. Model what you want to see. Witness the child's self-correction when it happens. See [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]] for the full positive frame, and [[inherent-goodness]] for the underlying belief that supports the practice.

The frame change is not "be permissive." The frame change is "use tools that actually change behavior." The boundaries are still drawn. The expectations are still communicated. The shame is what is dropped, because the shame is the part that does not work.

## What Replaces Shame as the Behavior-Change Mechanism

If shame is not the mechanism, what is? Multiple things, working together:

- **Connection.** The young child's behavior is largely a function of how connected they feel. See [[connection-is-what-young-kids-actually-need]]. Restoring connection often resolves the behavior without any direct address.
- **Modeling.** The parent's actual behavior teaches the child what is acceptable, regardless of what the parent says. See [[if-you-do-x-youre-telling-them-its-okay-to-do-x]]. The parent who wants the child to handle anger differently has to handle their own anger differently.
- **Boundaries delivered cleanly.** "Hey, we are not going to do X. If you do X, this is what I'm going to do." Without shame, without escalation. See [[stay-close-and-insist]] and the parenting application of [[boundaries]].
- **The natural compass.** The child develops their own moral compass that produces self-correction. The parent's job is to not override this with imposed shame. See [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]].
- **Witnessing self-correction.** When the child does something wrong and feels their own response, the parent witnesses without amplifying. See [[welcome-the-shame-without-extracting]].

These tools work. The shame tool does not. The empirical case is the parent's own household, where the shaming has been running for years without producing the change.
