# Don't Lean Into Conflict From Defendedness

## The Anti-Pattern

The teaching to lean into conflict is correct. The pattern people fall into when they first hear it is to lean in while still defended -- to walk into the room armored, claim they are not being conflict-avoidant, and pick a fight. This is not the teaching working. It is the teaching being used as cover for what they were already doing.

The signature is the opening line. "Why did you do that?" "I'm not avoiding conflict, I just need to bring this up." "I just have to be honest with you." All true on the surface. All starting from a place that is already in opposition. The conflict that follows is not the conflict the teaching points toward.

"The other version of that is, 'I'm gonna step into conflict, but I am, um, I'm not able to do it in a non-personal or non-defended way.' And I'm not able to announce that and own that. So to come in and say, 'Wow, this is bothering me and I'm taking it really personally and I don't want to be taking it personally,' that's one thing. And I think a great thing. But to be like, 'Why did you do this? I'm not avoiding conflict.' And like, so you're starting a fight because you're defended and because you're shut down and you're armored up." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## The Related Version: "I'll Never Get What I Want"

A different version of the same anti-pattern is leaning in from a stance of expecting to lose. The body is constricted around the assumption that the outcome will not go your way, and that constriction shapes the language and the energy of the lean-in.

"Walking into conflict like I'll never get what I want also isn't a great way to walk into the conflict either... And one would be like, oh, like, why can't -- why do you keep doing that to me? Why are you doing that to me? Which is also going to not get you what you want." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The internal posture of "I'll never get what I want" is itself a conflict the person is carrying internally (see [[avoided-conflict-goes-internal]]). When the external conversation begins from that internal posture, the internal conflict imports itself into the external situation. The other person feels it, often does not know what they have walked into, and responds defensively.

## Why It Fails

The mechanism is precise. The lean-in that comes from defendedness imports the defendedness into the room. The other person registers an attack and defends back. The conflict that emerges is the kind of conflict the teaching is trying to avoid: two defended people, both feeling attacked, neither moving toward the better solution that the friction was supposed to reveal.

The teaching of [[conflict-as-evolutionary-tension]] requires the person leaning in to be open to being changed by what they encounter. Defendedness is the explicit refusal to be changed. You can technically be "leaning in" while structurally not leaning in -- showing up to the conflict while making it impossible for the conflict to do its work.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is not "do not lean in until you are perfectly undefended." That standard would itself become an excuse for not leaning in. The corrective is to bring the defendedness consciously into the room: announce it, name it, take responsibility for it, and proceed from there.

"To come in and say, 'Wow, this is bothering me and I'm taking it really personally and I don't want to be taking it personally,' that's one thing. And I think a great thing." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The move is the same opening words plus the awareness of how you are arriving. "This is bothering me. I'm taking it personally and I don't want to be. Here's what's happening for me." That opening converts the defendedness from a hidden driver into a named element of the conversation. The other person can meet you in the actual situation rather than reacting to the attack they thought was coming.

This connects to [[vulnerability]]: the vulnerability is the naming of the defendedness, not the absence of it.

## The Acceptable-But-Not-Ideal Form

Joe is explicit that leaning in from defendedness is still better than not leaning in at all.

"And to be honest with you, I think that's actually a better way to go than to be completely conflict avoidant. However, it doesn't teach you the main thing, which is that the conflict is the grist for the evolutionary mill." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The ranking, from worst to best:

1. Total conflict avoidance (worst -- nothing moves, internal conflict accumulates)
2. Leaning in from defendedness (better -- something moves, but the conflict re-creates conflict)
3. Leaning in with the defendedness named (good -- the situation can shift)
4. Leaning in undefended, in view, oriented to mutual seeing (best -- the conflict produces the evolution it was meant to)

The anti-pattern is being on level 2 and claiming to be on level 3 or 4. The honesty about being on level 2 is what makes the move to level 3 possible.

## The Diagnostic

The honest diagnostic for which version is happening:

- Are you trying to get your way?
- Would you rather win?
- Do you think there is a winner and a loser?
- Are you primarily oriented to seeing and being seen, or to securing an outcome?

If the first three are yes or the last has not been considered, the lean-in is the defended version. See [[loving-conflict-vs-wanting-conflict]] for the broader frame.

"If you're trying to get your way, or if you rather win, or if you think there's a winner or a loser, all of those are pretty much good signs that you are either creating conflict or avoiding conflict." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## Joe's Words

"You're starting a fight because you're defended and because you're shut down and you're armored up. You can do that. And to be honest with you, I think that's actually a better way to go than to be completely conflict avoidant. However, it doesn't teach you the main thing, which is that the conflict is the grist for the evolutionary mill. It is the exact place that we need to lean in and to learn to resolve it." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance
