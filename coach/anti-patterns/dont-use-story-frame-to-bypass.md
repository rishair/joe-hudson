# Don't Use "It's Just a Story" to Bypass the Feeling Work

## The Anti-Pattern

The person learns that every thought is a [[story]]. They learn they can see through their stories. They start using the seeing-through as a way to never have to feel anything. When an emotion comes up attached to a story, they dismiss the story ("oh, that's just a story") and skip past the emotion. The dismissal looks like wisdom. It functions as suppression.

"Yeah, it sounds like there's a sort of a trap or a kind of a dodge that can occur where you see a story that you're in and then you see through that story, but then you see past feeling the emotion and you're just like, oh yeah, nothing is real. What is free will?" -- Brett Kistler, When the Story Falls Apart

Joe confirms: this happens, and it does not work.

"Yes, yes, yes, yes. Yeah. That's what I'm talking about. And you can do that and you will still be bothered the next time somebody is, by the next time that somebody is having that superficial conversation, or the next time that somebody cuts you off or whatever it is. You can overcome it in the moment. You can mentally kind of sidestep it, but it doesn't get you out of the pattern. If you want to get out of the pattern, you got to feel the emotion all the way through." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

## Why It Fails

The story is held in place by an emotion. Seeing through the story intellectually does not release the emotion. The emotion is still there, in the body, ready to reload the same story (or a different one) next time the trigger fires.

The mental sidestep works once: in the immediate moment, the person can intellectually un-believe the story enough to avoid acting on it. But the next time the trigger arrives, the emotion fires again, and the work has to be redone. The pattern continues because the underlying mechanism was never touched.

This is the negative-space version of [[feeling-changes-story]]. That principle says: feel the emotion, the story changes. The corollary: cognitively change the story without feeling the emotion, and the emotion remains, ready to reload another story tomorrow.

## The Disguise

This anti-pattern is sophisticated. It uses the language of awakening, non-duality, story-deconstruction. It sounds advanced. "What is real?" "Nothing is permanent." "These are all just thoughts." "Free will is an illusion." Each statement may have philosophical merit. The use of those statements to avoid feeling is the problem.

A person can spend years in spiritual traditions developing exactly this skill. They become very good at not believing their stories. They are also, often, very bad at actually feeling. The mismatch produces a kind of glassy calm with no underlying movement -- a peaceful surface over a frozen interior. See [[zen-sickness]] for one form this can take.

## The Diagnostic

A clean test: does the same trigger keep producing the same reaction? If a person is in the cognitive-bypass pattern, they will report:
- "I keep seeing through this story but it keeps coming back"
- "I know intellectually that this doesn't matter, but I keep getting bothered by it"
- "I've worked on this belief but it still controls me"

These are reports of work happening only at the cognitive layer. The emotional layer has not been touched. The trigger will keep triggering until the underlying emotion is felt.

## What Joe Says Instead

Feel the emotion all the way through. Not metaphorically. Actually move it through the body. The pattern dissolves when the emotion does its full motion, not when the story is intellectually dismissed.

"You can overcome it in the moment. You can mentally kind of sidestep it, but it doesn't get you out of the pattern. If you want to get out of the pattern, you got to feel the emotion all the way through." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The pairing matters. Stories can be loosened cognitively, and that is useful. But the cognitive work needs to be joined with feeling the emotion the story was holding. The two channels work together. Either alone leaves work undone. See [[emotions-and-stories-dance]].

## How an AI Coach Spots This

- The person uses sophisticated language about stories and beliefs while reporting no recent emotional release
- The same trigger keeps producing the same response despite repeated cognitive work on it
- The person says variations of "but it's just a story" when asked to feel something
- The person describes their progress in terms of what they no longer believe rather than what they feel
- Spiritual or non-dual language appears when emotions get close
- The person's body is consistently quiet and contained while they discuss emotionally heavy material
- "I've already worked on this" said in a flat tone about a recurring pattern

The move when this pattern shows up: redirect from "what do you think" to "what do you feel in your body right now." Stop letting the cognitive layer carry the work.
