# Don't Micromanage Your Kid

## The Anti-Pattern

The parent who corrects every small thing the kid does — how they wash the dishes, how they fold the laundry, how they pack a bag, how they speak to a sibling. The pattern shows up as constant low-grade correction in the moments when correction is not actually needed. The kid is doing the dishes; the parent says no, not like that, like this. The kid is folding clothes; the parent stops them to demonstrate the right way.

Esme describes this through a friend whose father micromanages constantly:

"They'll be washing the dishes and the dad will come over and, you know, you're not doing the dishes right. Like, this is how you actually wash the pan. Or like, no, no, no, like, not like that. It's actually like this. And I do that voice cuz it's just so absurd to me. You're telling your 15-year-old or your 18-year-old how to wash a pan." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The micromanaging is not occasional course-correction. It is the parent's default mode of presence with the kid. The kid cannot do a basic task without the parent's intervention. The parent does not register this as excessive because the corrections feel reasonable in the moment.

## Why It Fails

The micromanaging fails on multiple dimensions:

**It disempowers the kid.** Esme names this directly: "I think in the long term it just disempowers the teenager or the kid. In any situation, whether it be a boss or a parent, of being micromanaged all the time, it doesn't help you get better at things or feel confident doing things by yourself. It just does the complete opposite."

The kid learns that they cannot do things on their own. Every task gets interrupted before they can develop their own competence. They develop the felt sense that they need someone else to validate or correct their attempts. As adults they will reproduce this — either by becoming unable to act without external approval, or by reacting against it through compulsive non-asking.

**It transmits "you are not okay as you are."** The structural message under the constant correction is: the way you do things is wrong, the way you naturally are is inadequate, you need to be adjusted constantly. This is the same not-okay signal that produces the cold teenage years. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]]. The micromanaging is one of the most consistent transmission channels for that signal.

**It blocks the transmission of like.** A parent who is constantly correcting cannot also be transmitting "I like who you are." The two are incompatible. See [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]]. The micromanaging parent often loves the kid deeply, but the constant correction prevents the kid from registering being liked.

**It models micromanaging as a relational style.** The kid learns that micromanaging is what people do in close relationships. They will micromanage their own future kids, their partners, their employees. The pattern transmits. See [[if-you-do-x-youre-telling-them-its-okay-to-do-x]].

## What Joe Names As the Actual Cause

Joe identifies the structural cause of micromanaging:

"When I notice I wanna micromanage something, it's because I'm feeling a little bit outta control typically." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

Esme confirms: "You're trying to find control in one part of your life cuz you feel outta control in another part."

The micromanaging is not really about the kid's dish-washing. It is about the parent's own dysregulation. The parent has something unprocessed somewhere — work stress, marital conflict, unfelt emotion, generalized anxiety — and the parent is reaching for control in the available domain (the kid) to compensate for the felt lack of control elsewhere.

The kid is not the cause. The kid is the available target. The micromanaging is the parent's attempt to feel okay by exercising control over something they can control, which happens to be the kid because the kid is there.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is not to add a parenting technique on top of the dysregulated parent. The corrective is to address the upstream condition. If the parent is micromanaging because they are out of control elsewhere, the work is to be with what is actually out of control. What is the parent not feeling? What is being avoided? What is the actual source of the dysregulation?

Once the parent addresses the actual source, the impulse to micromanage the kid dissolves. The parent can let the kid wash the dishes their way because the parent no longer needs the kid's dish-washing to be a site of regulation.

The diagnostic question Joe offers in the moment of noticing the impulse to micromanage: "What am I out of control about right now? What am I trying to regulate by reaching for the kid?" The question redirects the parent's attention back to the actual issue, where the actual work is.

## The Specific Pattern with Teenagers

Micromanaging a teenager is particularly costly because the teenager has the developmental task of building competence and autonomy. The micromanaging directly blocks that task. Every interrupted attempt at a basic task is one less repetition of "I can do this on my own." Over years, the absence of those repetitions produces a teen who genuinely cannot do basic things — at which point the parent treats the deficit as evidence that the kid needed the micromanaging in the first place.

The reverse direction also runs: a teen who is micromanaged constantly often pushes back hard, refusing to do anything the parent suggests in the right way. The pushing back gets read as "the teen is rebelling" and produces more micromanaging in response. The cycle accelerates.

The exit from the cycle is the parent's recognition of what is actually happening: the micromanaging is the parent's dysregulation, the pushback is the teen's correct response to being disempowered, neither is about the surface issue.

## The Distinction From Real Teaching

The anti-pattern is not "never instruct your kid in how to do things." Teaching how to wash a pan to a 6-year-old who has never washed a pan is legitimate. Showing a 12-year-old a different technique once and letting them try it is legitimate. The anti-pattern is the chronic correction of basic tasks the kid is already competent at, or the correction of every detail of a task that the kid is in the middle of learning.

The test: is the correction adding information the kid did not have, in a moment when the kid can use it? Or is the correction interrupting a kid who was managing fine, because the parent could not tolerate the kid doing it their own way?

## When This Is Useful

- A parent presents a constant low-grade conflict with their kid over small things
- A parent describes their kid as defiant or resistant to instruction in basic tasks
- A parent is bewildered why their kid does not respect their guidance
- A parent recognizes they are correcting constantly and is asking why
- A teen presents that their parent will not let them do anything their own way
- A coachee remembers being micromanaged as a kid and is processing the cost
