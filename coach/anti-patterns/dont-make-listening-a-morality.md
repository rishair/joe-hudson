# Don't Make Listening a Rule, a Morality, or a To-Do

## The Anti-Pattern

After hearing the teaching on deep listening, the mind immediately converts it into a discipline. "Now I'm going to be a better listener. I should listen this way. From now on I'll do it right." The very installation of the rule is the move that kills the listening.

Joe issues this warning directly:

"If you're listening to this, somebody might be like making this into a rule or morality or something. And I would say watch out for that. Like, listen, listen, listen. If you listen to your body, if you listen to awareness, if you listen to listening, notice how it thinks about making a rule or making it a morality or making it something that you have to do. It almost feels like when you have to do it, it's the opposite of doing it." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## Why It Fails

Listening is a receiving move. See [[opposite-of-trying]] and [[listening]]. Receiving cannot be produced by trying. The instruction "you must receive" recruits exactly the trying that prevents the receiving. The body of the person who has decided to listen-better is now loaded with effort and self-monitoring. There is no spaciousness left for the listening to occur in.

The same structure that makes the listening teaching valuable — that it is non-management, non-trying, non-performance — also makes it especially vulnerable to being turned into the opposite. The morality-making move feels like virtue (I am taking the teaching seriously, I am being disciplined about it) but is functionally the inverse of the practice.

This is also why measuring your listening (am I listening well enough? did I just listen right? am I doing this correctly?) breaks it. The measurement requires you to step out of the listening to evaluate it. The stepping out is the leaving.

"When you have to do it, it's the opposite of doing it." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## The Wider Pattern

This anti-pattern is a specific case of a broader one: any teaching about receiving, allowing, or non-management can be turned into a rule, and the rule is always a contradiction in the body. See:

- [[view-as-technique]] — using VIEW as a strategy to get a result, which dissolves the state VIEW points at.
- [[should-as-threat]] — the four layers of should, all of which run the same internal mechanic.
- [[good-and-corruption-cycle]] — anything of significance gets weaponized; the listening teaching will become a new morality if it is allowed to.
- [[nothing-is-wrong-with-it-its-just-painful]] — the non-moralizing posture that protects against this conversion.

The teachings in this family all share a vulnerability: they describe a state that cannot be reached by trying. When the mind insists on having a to-do, the to-do gets installed and the state recedes.

## What Joe Says Instead

The right relationship to the listening teaching is delight, not duty.

"It's much more of a, like, oh, what a joy. Yeah. What a joy it is to listen like this." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The recovery move is to drop the discipline and notice what is enjoyable. Listening done well feels good. The spaciousness feels good. The conversations feel better. The body softens. You evolve faster. The reward is intrinsic and immediate. When the relationship to the practice is to the enjoyment of it rather than to the duty of it, the practice runs itself.

If you notice you have turned listening into a should — "I should listen better, I should listen more, I'm a bad listener, I need to fix this" — the recovery is not to listen *harder*. It is to notice the should and let it go. Then, without the should, see what listening is available right now.

## The Detection Signal

If your relationship to listening contains any of these phrases, the morality has been installed:

- "I should listen better."
- "I need to be a good listener."
- "I'm not listening right."
- "I have to remember to listen."
- "I have to make myself listen."
- "I'll listen properly this time."

Each of these has the same internal mechanic: there is a meta-position evaluating the listening, a should driving the listening, a performance hidden in the listening. The listening that results will be the performance, not the receiving.

The presence of these phrases is data, not a problem. Notice them. Let them be there. Then drop the should and see what listening is available without it.

## Tone

Joe issues this warning in the middle of the teaching, almost as an aside, but with clear emphasis. The bluntness — "watch out for that" — is the tone marker. He does not soften it; the softening would let the listener miss it. The follow-through, on the other hand, is warm: the alternative is the joy of the listening itself.
