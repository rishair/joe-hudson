# Don't use VIEW as a technique

## The Anti-Pattern

Using VIEW components as communication tactics while the internal state remains agenda-driven. Asking open questions while wanting a specific answer. Performing empathy while trying to change someone's mind. Going through the motions of vulnerability while keeping the real truth hidden.

This is the same failure mode as "weaponized" nonviolent communication or any communication training that gets applied as technique without the corresponding shift in state of mind.

## Why It Fails

The body does not lie. Microexpressions, intonation, body language, and mirror neuron signals all transmit the speaker's actual internal state. These signals cannot be faked. A person on the receiving end registers the incongruence even if they cannot articulate it. They feel the agenda underneath the open question. They feel the manipulation underneath the empathic nod.

"If you are using it as a technique, it can't work because the actual state of mind is going to shine through in these other non-intellectual ways." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

"If you see people, they learn these great techniques that are out there, like nonviolent communication or something like that, and all of a sudden they've been weaponized. You just see them being weaponized. It's like you see all this communication training go south." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

**The unwilling attention example:** A partner asks for undivided attention. You give it, but resentfully, because you would rather watch TV. You are doing the right behavior (paying attention) with the wrong state (frustrated, unwilling). Inevitably, the partner says "you don't really want to be here." The conversation escalates. You feel wronged: "I stopped what I was doing to give you what you asked for, and now that's not good enough?" The behavior was correct. The state of mind was visible in everything besides the words. This is exactly how VIEW-as-technique fails: the behavior matches but the other person reads the state.

"If the state of mind isn't, oh, I want to pay attention to you, it will never work. If the state of mind in the communication is I'm trying to get something out of you, which is the antithesis of VIEW, then it'll never work no matter what technique you use." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course)

Duplicitous sales techniques work roughly 1 in 8 times, fishing for the person who wants that particular bait. A study of car salesmen found that top performers (roughly 3x the average) were not the most skilled manipulators. They were the ones who genuinely cared about their customers, saw sales as a relationship, and focused on putting people in the right vehicle. Technique without care produces marginal results. Authentic care produces outsized results.

"You can learn really duplicitous sales techniques and they work, you know, 1 out of 8 times, 'cause you're just basically fishing for the fish who wants that bait." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course)

## When Inauthenticity Goes Undetected

Two situations allow technique-without-state to appear to work:

1. **The listener wants to believe.** People believe a lie when it rhymes with the truth they already want. Infomercials, mass media, and sales pitches succeed when the audience's desire to believe overrides their ability to read the speaker's state. "We'll believe a lie if it rhymes with the truth that we want to believe." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW (Connection Course)

2. **Trauma activation.** When someone is triggered by a speaker who resembles a past source of trauma, they are no longer reading the speaker. They are in relationship with the old figure. They are not in the same time and space. See [[trauma-mapping]], [[familiar-over-safe]]. In these moments, the person cannot detect inauthenticity because they are not fully present to the actual interaction.

Neither case contradicts the principle. In both, the technique-without-state is not producing genuine connection. It is exploiting a gap in the listener's awareness. It works only on the subset of people who are unable to read you clearly, and even then, only temporarily.

**Duplicity has a shelf life.** Intentional duplicity almost always collapses, usually within five years. People who do morally questionable things but are straightforward about it can sustain for much longer. The concealment, not the behavior, is what fails. "You can see people who are maybe morally questionable but they're straightforward about it, and they can actually do it for a long time. But when they're not being straightforward about it, it almost always blows up." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3

The [[impartiality]] version is particularly subtle. Wanting to be *seen as* impartial is itself partiality. "If you want them to see you as impartial, then you're definitely being partial. And it's going to be seen." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3

## The 'New Age Fight' Form: Tools Weaponized in Couples Conflict

A specific shape this anti-pattern takes in couples is what Joe calls "the new age fight." The couple has learned relational tools -- nonviolent communication, VIEW, "I" statements, sharing feelings, naming needs -- and they are using the tools as ammunition rather than as the practice they were meant to be.

"I cannot tell you how many times I have seen like what I call the new age fight. Do you know what the new age fight, right? They've learned some, even it could be VIEW, it could be any nonviolent communication. It could be anything. And they've, they're fighting using it such as nonviolent communication, where it's really important to state your needs. Somebody might be saying something like, 'I really need you to stop right now.' That's not nonviolent communication. So you can take any series of protocol and you can turn it into ways of fighting that are not productive." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The structural diagnostic: the form of the language matches the recommended practice; the underlying intent is control, demand, or victory. The "I really need you to stop right now" example has the syntactic shape of a needs-statement and the felt-sense shape of an order. The receiver registers the order, not the needs-statement. The conversation goes the way an order goes, not the way a needs-statement goes.

This is the same failure mode as the broader VIEW-as-technique pattern, applied specifically to the practice of fighting well. The couple that has read the books and listened to the podcasts is uniquely vulnerable to this because they have a richer vocabulary for dressing up the same old fight in a new costume. The intermediate-stage couple who knows the tools but has not yet recognized the underlying trauma is the most common case. See [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]] for where this stage sits in the developmental arc.

The exit from this stage is recognizing that the tools cannot do the work the state has to do. The tools amplify the state that is present. They cannot manufacture a state that is absent. Couples that get past this stage usually do so by recognizing that the work is the trauma underneath the fight, not the technique on top of it. See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] for the in-the-moment move that becomes available after the technique stage.

## What To Do Instead

Practice VIEW as a state of mind, not a set of behaviors. The communication is a natural outpouring of the state. The four components ([[vulnerability]], [[impartiality]], [[empathy]], [[wonder]]) are doorways into the state, not boxes to check.

If the state is not there, the most useful thing is to notice that and be honest about it. That honesty is itself vulnerability, which begins to shift the state.

Wonder-specific version: see [[wonder-as-strategy]] for the pattern where questions are used to avoid intimacy or to manipulate, which Joe addresses specifically in the Wonder episode.

**Empathy-specific version:** Techniques like mirroring body language, nodding yes when people speak, repeating the last three words, and using someone's name at the start of sentences can all be done with or without genuine empathy. With empathy, they are appealing. Without it, they come off as false and creepy. The techniques sometimes work because they hack the mind into genuine empathy. But when the intent is to disarm the other person as a trick rather than to disarm yourself, it fails. "You can do all that stuff, but if you're not being empathy, it comes off as false, fake, and gross." -- Joe Hudson, Empathy. See also [[losing-yourself-in-others]].

**Vulnerability-specific version:** Using vulnerability to control, guilt, or keep score. Performing weakness to get caretaking. Saying "I don't feel safe" to require others to change. Tracking whether someone was "vulnerable back." All of these use vulnerability language without the vulnerability state. The test: guilt indicates control, not vulnerability. See [[vulnerability-as-control]] for the full pattern.

**Narcissist-specific version:** Using VIEW with someone far out on the narcissistic spectrum (see [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]]) converts the practice into technique in a particularly visible way. The narcissistic system is specifically tuned to detect openings created by vulnerability and to exploit them. The moment a person uses VIEW to "get somewhere" with the narcissist, they have stepped out of VIEW (because VIEW cannot be used instrumentally) and the deployed vulnerability becomes a lever the narcissist will use against them. The principle: VIEW directed at a narcissist as strategy is no longer VIEW; the appropriate tool is containment via boundaries. See [[contain-the-narcissist]].

"When people are using VIEW, they drop, like using VIEW in itself is dropping, is becoming partial. So if you're using VIEW as a way to get somewhere with the parent, then you're not actually in VIEW because VIEW can't be used particularly." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers
