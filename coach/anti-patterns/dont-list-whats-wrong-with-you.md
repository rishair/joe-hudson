# Don't Write a List of What's Wrong With You

## The Anti-Pattern

A specific kind of self-improvement practice: writing down everything that is wrong with you, in detail, with specificity. The list might include things like "I'm depressed because my mom hated me because I have a buck tooth," or "I have a really big forehead," or "I'm bad at relationships," or "I procrastinate." The practice is often framed as honesty, as self-awareness, as facing the truth, as the necessary first step toward change.

It is not. It is the most reliable way to install and reinforce the shame structure described in [[shame-drives-behavior]]. The specificity is what makes it especially toxic. A vague "I'm not good enough" is bad; a list of fifty specific reasons why is much worse.

The anti-pattern is delivered in Joe's satirical doomscrolling video as one of the "professional moves" for becoming an elite doomscroller:

"Write down all the things that are wrong with you. So if you write on the list and you're like, oh, I'm depressed, that's great, but you want it specific. So it's like, I'm depressed because my mom hated me because I had a buck tooth. And I still do. Or like, I have a really big forehead. That's a good — that I use that one personally. That's a great one to use. So you want something that's specific to how you are broken in at least 50 times." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

The instruction is satirical. The point is that this is exactly what not to do — and the reason it is so harmful is precisely because the specificity makes it effective at installing the shame the listener is trying to escape.

See [[satirical-anti-advice-as-teaching]] for the format and [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] for the underlying principle.

## Why It Fails

The brokenness-catalog fails for several interlocking reasons:

1. **Specificity locks shame in.** Generic shame is harder to hold than specific shame. "I'm not good enough" is vague enough that the body can sometimes let it pass. "I'm not good enough because of my forehead, my buck tooth, my procrastination, my temper, my relationship history, etc." gives the shame a structure to attach to. The body now has specific objects to feel bad about, and each one becomes its own little shame-generator.

2. **The list reinforces the unique-defectiveness premise.** The format of "here are MY specific defects" implicitly carries "these are uniquely mine." Other people's defects do not appear on the list. The list, by its structure, isolates the writer from the universal human pattern. See [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]] on the unique-to-me distortion as the engine of false shame.

3. **The list is treated as discovery when it is actually invention.** The person believes they are seeing what is already true. They are actually constructing a self-portrait organized around defects. The portrait then becomes the operating identity. Months later the person is operating from an identity that has fifty documented things wrong with it, and the identity does what identities do — it produces behavior consistent with itself.

4. **The list trains the attentional bias.** Whatever you focus on, the brain learns to find more of. A daily list of defects trains the brain to find defects. The brain becomes faster and more efficient at noticing defects. The list grows. The bias deepens.

5. **The shame produces the very behaviors the list is trying to address.** The "I procrastinate" entry on the list installs shame about procrastinating, which produces more procrastination (because the shame is the engine of the procrastination, per [[shame-drives-behavior]]). The list creates the conditions for the items on it to multiply.

6. **It collapses the distinction between awareness and self-attack.** Awareness sees what is here. Self-attack lists what is wrong with what is here. The two look identical from outside — both are descriptions of behavior or trait. The difference is the felt stance. Awareness is curious; self-attack is verdict. The list, by virtue of being a list of what is "wrong," is structurally on the self-attack side regardless of how dispassionately it is written.

## The Close Cousin That Works: The Brutal-Honesty List

There is a real practice that looks similar from outside but works on the opposite mechanism. See [[joes-26-year-old-list]]. At 26 Joe wrote down everything about himself he didn't want to admit. Six months later most of the items had changed. He attributed the change to awareness — the recognition itself was the work.

The distinction between the brutal-honesty list and the brokenness catalog is structural, not cosmetic:

| Brutal-Honesty List (works) | Brokenness Catalog (doesn't work) |
|---|---|
| Items framed as what is here ("I avoid hard conversations") | Items framed as what is wrong ("I'm a coward about hard conversations") |
| List is data | List is verdict |
| List is set aside and returned to later | List is reread daily as reminder of defects |
| Stance is curiosity | Stance is self-attack |
| Items are observable behavior | Items are character traits |
| Done once or occasionally | Done as ongoing practice |
| Privately, briefly | Often shared, displayed, referenced |
| Aim is to see clearly | Aim is to feel bad enough to change |

The two practices look formally similar — both involve writing a list about yourself — and produce opposite outcomes. The difference is whether the list is data or indictment, awareness or self-attack.

## Why People Reach For This Practice

The brokenness-catalog is often delivered (or self-prescribed) with apparent wisdom. It looks like:

- Honesty about your flaws
- Taking responsibility
- Facing the truth
- The "first step" of any change program
- Stripping away delusions and ego protections
- Being your own toughest critic
- Hard-won maturity

Each of these framings has some truth in it, which is what makes the anti-pattern hard to see. Honesty is good; honesty about flaws is good in the right context; facing the truth is good. The trap is that the brokenness-catalog hijacks each of these legitimate moves and converts them into shame-installation.

The format of "if I just write down what's wrong with me, I'll have to face it and change" rests on the assumption that shame produces change. The assumption is structurally wrong. Shame stagnates. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] and [[shame-stagnates-emotion]].

## What Joe Says Instead

The work that actually produces change is not a defect catalog. It is some combination of:

- **Awareness without indictment.** Seeing what is happening, with curiosity, without the "wrong" frame. See [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]] for the self-awareness alternative to self-improvement.
- **Feeling what is being avoided.** The behaviors and patterns the person is trying to catalog as defects are usually avoidances of unfelt emotions. The work is on the unfelt emotion, not on the avoidance behavior. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].
- **Self-forgiveness in spoken form.** "I forgive myself for [specific thing]" — done in the body, with the eyes closed, in private. See [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]]. This is what specific naming is good for: not indictment, but forgiveness.
- **The universalization.** Whatever the person was about to add to their defect list is happening to hundreds of millions of others at the same moment. The list-as-defect-catalog assumes uniqueness; the universalization dissolves the assumption. See [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] and [[its-a-human-thing-redirect]].

The specificity that the brokenness-catalog gets wrong has a right use: specificity in self-forgiveness, not in self-attack. The same specific item — "I procrastinated for six hours yesterday" — produces opposite outcomes depending on whether it is followed by "and I'm pathetic" or by "and I forgive myself."

## Joe's Words

"You want it specific. So it's like, I'm depressed because my mom hated me because I had a buck tooth. And I still do. Or like, I have a really big forehead. That's a good — that I use that one personally. That's a great one to use. So you want something that's specific to how you are broken in at least 50 times. And I think that will help you find the way to really see yourself as just not good enough in an essential level rather than just a, you know, human level." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

The satirical framing makes the mechanism explicit: specificity-times-quantity is what installs shame at an essential level. The actual teaching, by inversion: do not let your list-making practice cross from human-level honesty into essential-level indictment. If the list is producing felt sense that you are essentially defective rather than that you are human and noticing what is here, the practice has become the anti-pattern.

## When This Anti-Pattern Is Most Tempting

The anti-pattern is most tempting when:

- A person has just had a breakthrough and feels the need to "really face" what is wrong with them
- A therapy or coaching modality has framed "facing your shadow" as the work
- The person has been doing positive-thinking practices and is reacting against the bypass with "real honesty"
- The person is in a depressive episode and the depression feels like clarity about their flaws
- Self-help reading has framed self-critique as the necessary first step
- The person is performing self-awareness for an audience (a partner, a therapist, a social-media following) and the performance rewards visible flaw-cataloging

In all these cases, the trap is the same: the cataloging feels like work but is installing what it is supposedly addressing.

## How To Catch It In Yourself or a Coachee

Diagnostic questions:

- After writing the list, do you feel relieved or heavier?
- Are you re-reading the list as motivation? (If yes, that is shame as motivation, which doesn't work.)
- Is the list a snapshot you set aside, or an ongoing project?
- Are the items observable behavior or character verdicts?
- If you imagine someone you love writing this list, does it look like honest seeing or like self-attack?

If the list is heavy, re-read often, ongoing, character-verdict-shaped, and would look like self-attack from a loving outside view — it is the brokenness catalog, not the awareness practice. Stop. Burn the list. Start with one item, in self-forgiveness form, in the body.
