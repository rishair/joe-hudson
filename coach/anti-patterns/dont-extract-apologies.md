# Don't Extract Apologies

## The Anti-Pattern

Forcing or demanding an apology from someone, especially a child. "Say you're sorry." The extracted apology installs shame in the person being extracted from, ruins their capacity to use apology as a transformation tool later in life, and teaches them that apologies are smallness rather than power.

The pattern shows up in:
- Parents requiring children to say sorry to siblings or to adults.
- Partners demanding "I want an apology" as a precondition for moving on.
- Leaders or teachers requiring public apologies.
- Group dynamics where social pressure forces an apology to restore status.
- Internal self-extraction: forcing yourself to apologize before you have actually arrived at upright responsibility.

## Why It Fails

The extracted apology is a shame apology. The mechanism of shame is the lock that holds bad behavior in place. The very thing the extracter is trying to correct gets reinforced by the act of extracting the apology. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

The longitudinal failure is worse than the immediate one. The person from whom apologies are extracted learns that apologizing makes them small. They become an adult who either cannot apologize or who apologizes constantly in fear. Both forms are dysfunctional. See [[forms-of-dysfunctional-apology]] and [[i-cant-apologize]].

"Whenever I see somebody who has a hard time saying I'm sorry, it's always because somebody extracted an apology from them when they were younger. And it was a way to make the person small, to make the person feel bad, to exact control over them." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## The Alternative

Apologies arise spontaneously and uprightly from people who have felt the cleanness of an upright apology. The condition for spontaneous apology is having experienced the [[upright-apology]] form. The condition is not pressure.

Joe's parenting example demonstrates this:

"With our kids, we've never asked them to say, 'I'm sorry.' It's just never happened. And yet, oddly, our children say they're sorry all the time and they say it in a very empowered way." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The mechanism: children who experience adults around them giving upright apologies internalize that apology is a clean, empowered move. They do it because it feels good, not because they were made to.

## What To Do Instead

- Model the upright apology yourself. Apologize uprightly to your child when you snap at them, to your partner when you control them, to your subordinate when you mismanage them. The modeling is the curriculum.
- Make space for the apology to arise. Do not fill the space with a demand. Often the child will apologize within minutes when not forced. They were processing what happened.
- If an apology does not arise, do not force one. The behavior change you want is not produced by the extracted apology. It is produced by the underlying repair, which may take other forms.
- Allow the child or person to make the choice. The clean apology has to be chosen freely or it is not the upright apology. See [[upright-apology]].
- Address the underlying behavior, not the apology cadence. If a child keeps hitting their sibling, the work is on what is happening for the child, not on whether they say sorry afterward.

## Joe's Words

"You're teaching your children or you're teaching yourself that the way that I can navigate danger in the world is by making myself small and by kowtowing to authority, instead of being in myself and being in my truth." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

"The thing that you shame your kid about is something you're going to shame your kid about for 5 or 10 years because they're going to keep on repeating that pattern. It just doesn't work." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## Boundary Conditions

Acknowledging what happened is not the same as extracting an apology. A parent can say "you hit your sister" without demanding "say you're sorry." Naming the behavior, holding the impact, and letting the child arrive at their own response (which often includes spontaneous repair) is different from extraction.

In adult relationships, asking for an apology is also different from extracting one. "I would like an apology for X" is a request. "I am not moving on until you apologize" is extraction. The first allows the upright form to arise. The second forces the shame form.
