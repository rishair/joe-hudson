# Don't step on the signal

## The Anti-Pattern

When fear arrives -- in self or in others -- the impulse is to override it. Stay positive. Be bold. Don't let it stop you. Reassure the team that everything is fine. Push through. Conquer it.

The override is called "courage" or "leadership" or "discipline" or "resilience." What it actually is, is the suppression of the fear before its information has been received.

The fear has content. The override discards the content.

"Like stepping on the signal and not listening to it." -- Brett Kistler, describing the pattern, Welcoming Fear

## How It Looks in the Self

- "I'm scared but I'm going to do it anyway" said with clenched-jaw determination rather than with attention to what the fear is signaling.
- Treating anxiety as a character flaw to be defeated rather than as information to be read.
- Powering through a project despite repeated body signals that something is off.
- Doing one more rep, one more jump, one more deal because backing off would feel like weakness.
- Identifying with being the brave one or the unflappable one. See [[im-not-an-anxious-person]].
- Reframing every fear-signal as "just excitement" or "just nerves" without checking the body. The reframe is not always wrong (see [[fear-vs-excitement]]), but used as a default it becomes suppression.

## How It Looks in Leadership

- A team member raises a concern. The leader says "we got this" and moves on.
- A junior surfaces a fear about timeline, scope, or resourcing. The leader treats it as morale damage to manage rather than as data to investigate.
- The leader's own fear about a business situation is hidden from the team. The team senses something is off and starts inventing worse stories than the actual one.
- The leader rallies the team with "stay positive" or "don't let fear win" while the underlying conditions that are producing the fear go unaddressed.

"Oftentimes those concerns were very, very valid. And if they weren't valid, the feeling that somebody had around them was also very valid. And if it wasn't addressed, led to them feeling disconnected from the project or from the team." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

## Why It Fails

The fear was usually right about something. Even when the threat assessment was inaccurate, the fear was pointing at something real: an unaddressed concern, a misaligned plan, an exhausted team member, a misjudged risk. Stepping on the signal does not eliminate the underlying issue. It just removes the system's only mechanism for surfacing it.

The cost compounds:

1. **The unaddressed issue produces the bad outcome anyway.** The project goes off the rails. The relationship erodes. The accident happens. The person looks back and recognizes that the fear was telling them exactly what was about to occur.

2. **The override teaches the system to escalate.** When small fear signals are ignored, the body learns to make them bigger. Eventually the signal arrives as panic, breakdown, or physical symptom rather than as the small initial nudge it was at first.

3. **In leadership, others learn to stop voicing fear.** A team that watches the leader override their own fear and others' fear learns to suppress its own. Concerns stop being raised. The information loop closes. The leader is then surprised when problems surface that "no one saw coming."

4. **The suppressed fear comes out sideways.** See [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]]. The fear becomes irritability, distraction, judgment, control. The override does not eliminate the fear. It just routes it.

## The Subtle Form: Identifying with the Override

The most subtle version is when the person has built an identity around overriding fear. The extreme athlete, the founder, the surgeon, the soldier. The work itself is treated as proof of fearlessness. Each accomplishment reinforces the identity.

The identity is not false. The person really does the difficult thing. What is false is the absence of fear. The fear is present and has been present all along. The performance was happening despite the suppressed fear, not because there was no fear to suppress.

When the identity cracks (often through a near-miss, a business collapse, a relationship rupture, a sustained breakdown of focus), the person discovers that the fear has been running underneath everything. The discovery is disorienting because so much of the self-concept depended on the fearless story. See [[im-not-an-anxious-person]].

## What Joe Says Instead

Listen to the signal. Feel the fear. Let it differentiate from anxiety into specific fears. See [[fear-vs-anxiety]]. Ask what it is pointing at. See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]]. Address what the signal is showing.

In leadership: welcome the team's fear. Treat raised concerns as information rather than as morale problems. Bring your own fear into the conversation so the team learns the practice. See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]].

The reframe Joe holds: there is a real distinction between fear that constricts you into doing something inauthentic and fear that opens you into the information you need to do the next thing well. The first kind passes when felt. The second kind never goes away because it is the body's intelligence at work.

"You're never gonna get the intelligence of the emotion while trying to control it." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection (the principle that grounds this anti-pattern)

## When the Override Is Appropriate

Not every fear should change the decision. Sometimes the fear is felt, heard, and the person still chooses to act despite it. This is [[vulnerability]]: doing the scary thing because it is true. The key is that the fear has been felt and addressed, not bypassed. The action is informed by the fear, not in spite of it.

The diagnostic: did the fear get heard? If yes, the action that follows is not stepping on the signal. If no, it is.

## Joe's Words

"Stepping on the signal" is the language Brett uses in this episode and Joe affirms. The phrase captures the somatic violence of the override: the foot coming down on the very thing that is trying to communicate.

"What I'm hearing is that what they said would bring up a fear in you that you didn't wanna feel and therefore you, I think you said it as step on the signal." -- Joe Hudson, Welcoming Fear
