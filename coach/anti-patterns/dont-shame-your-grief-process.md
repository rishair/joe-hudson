# Don't Shame Yourself for the Form Your Grief Takes

## The Anti-Pattern

Every grief process invites the same set of judgments from the person doing it. "I'm not crying enough." "I'm crying too much." "This happened too quickly." "This shouldn't have taken so long." "I can't be angry, but I can be sad." "I can be angry, but I can't be sad." "I feel relief and that's wrong." "I'm not feeling enough relief."

Whatever shape the grief takes, the person finds a reason to judge it. The judgment then shuts down the grief. The grief recycles in place because it cannot move past the shame attached to whichever form it is taking.

"There's nobody who goes through the grief process -- very few people who go through the grief process without shame, and they'll shame themselves for the exact opposite. I'm not crying enough. I'm crying too much. That happened too quickly. This should happen. This shouldn't have taken so long. Oh, I can't be angry, but I can be sad. Oh, I can be angry, but I can't be sad. Everybody seems to, on some level, question their grief process." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

## Why It Fails

Shame is the universal solvent for emotional movement (see [[shame-stagnates-emotion]]). Whichever emotion gets shamed in the grief process becomes the bottleneck. The grief stops moving through that emotion. And because grief is multi-emotional (see [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]]), shutting down one of the emotions tends to shut down the others as well.

The mechanism is precise. Joe describes a woman who lost her husband after 53 years and let herself notice she felt relief. "I don't want to admit it, but I feel relief." The moment she let the relief through, she started to tear up. The moment she started to tear up, she shut it down. Both the relief and the sadness got blocked because she could not allow the relief.

"What's interesting is we tell ourselves that one of the emotional states that we feel is not okay in the process, but what that does is it shuts down all the other ones too. Or it makes them spin. It makes it like, oh, and I'm still scared and I'm still scared, or I'm still sad and I'm still sad when we tell ourselves that we can't have one of the emotions." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The recycling -- the "I'm still scared, I'm still scared" loop -- is the diagnostic signature of this shutdown. When grief loops on one emotion for an unusually long time, somewhere in the system is a judgment that one of the other emotions is not allowed.

## The Common Shames

The shame attaches to whatever emotion is least permitted in the person's framework. Some common ones:

- **Anger at the dead person.** "How can I be angry at someone who died?" The mind cannot find a justification, so it shuts the anger down. The body has the anger anyway.
- **Relief that something is over.** Especially when the relationship was complicated, long-illness, or chronically painful. The relief is real and gets coded as wrong.
- **Fear about your own future.** Coded as selfishness in the middle of grief about someone else.
- **Laughter, lightness, ordinary functioning.** Coded as not grieving enough, or grieving "wrong."
- **Crying about the wrong things.** A small detail unleashes tears while the big things stay dry. Coded as proof you do not really feel it.
- **The duration.** Too fast means you did not care. Too slow means you are stuck.
- **The timing.** Tears at inappropriate moments. Dryness at appropriate moments.

The specific shame varies by person. The pattern -- that some shame attaches to some part of the grief -- is universal.

## What Joe Says Instead

The instruction is to make all the emotions allowable, especially the ones that feel disallowable.

"If you're not doing them at anybody, they're all appropriate, they're all great, they all offer relief." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The qualifier matters: not doing them at anybody. The freedom is to feel the emotion, not to express it as an attack on someone else. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]]. With that boundary in place, every emotion in the grief is welcome.

The deeper instruction: when you catch yourself judging your grief, the judgment is the work. Whichever emotion you are telling yourself you cannot feel is the next door. Notice the shame, notice what it is shaming, and invite that emotion deliberately.

## The Mind's Voice in Grief

Joe describes the kind of voice the mind produces during grief:

"My brain will say things for each emotional state. Oh, there's no reason to be angry. I mean, why would you be angry at someone who just died? Or hasn't this gone on long enough? Or shouldn't you have grieved more by now? Or there's so many things that the brain says. And my relationship with that is, oh, so cool, I've heard from you a lot. I've lived with that. I'm going to just listen to my body. I'm just going to listen to the grief right now." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The voice does not stop. The relationship to the voice is what changes. The instruction is not to argue with the judgments but to notice them and continue listening to the body. The mind's relationship to emotion is genuinely not equipped to handle grief well. The mind thinks its job is to manage. The grief moves when the management stops.

This is the same anti-pattern as [[dont-block-emotion-waiting-for-reason]] in the specific domain of grief.

## Joe's Words

"The mind's relationship with emotion, with the emotions, is usually pretty wonky for most people unless they really have had a lot of work with the emotions. The mind just doesn't really know. I love when someone says something like, 'There's no justification for that emotion.' You know, there's no, it's not logical to be sad about this. It's like, yeah, the emotions are definitely never -- it's not logical to be happy or joyful. It's like that. Yes, correct. Like that. So to me, it's about listening to the body, and allowing the emotions without commenting on them, not having some sort of head trip around it. I love the mind, but it's not equipped to handle emotions very well. It just thinks its job is to manage them, right? Doesn't work." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- When someone in grief is reporting their grief alongside a critique of it.
- When grief has been recycling on one emotion for an unusually long time.
- When the person describes their grief process as "wrong" in some way.
- When someone is monitoring their grief to see if it is the right shape.
- When a person reports an emotion in their grief that they are unwilling to feel.
