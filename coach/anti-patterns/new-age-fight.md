# The New Age Fight

## The Anti-Pattern

A couple has learned a communication protocol -- nonviolent communication, VIEW, Imago dialogue, internal family systems, attachment theory language, any of the relational frameworks. They start fighting using the protocol. The form is the form. The content is still the same fight.

"I really need you to stop right now" gets delivered as if it is a need-statement from NVC. The recipient is supposed to receive it as a clean need. Inside, it is a demand. The protocol has been weaponized to make the demand harder to refuse.

"I cannot tell you how many times I have seen what I call the new age fight. They've learned some— even it could be VIEW, it could be any nonviolent communication, it could be anything. And they've, they're fighting using it. Such as nonviolent communication, where it's really important to state your needs. Somebody might be saying something like, I really need you to stop right now. It's like, that's not nonviolent communication. So you can take any series of protocol and you can turn it into ways of fighting that are not productive." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The anti-pattern is the use of relational protocols as fight gear. The form is calm, articulate, framework-aware. The function is the same old control move, repackaged.

## Why It Fails

The protocols work by addressing the underlying state. NVC asks for genuine connection between the speaker's need and the listener's care. VIEW asks for a state of vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, and wonder. Imago asks for genuine mirroring. None of these work as scripts when the underlying state is missing.

A "need" expressed from demand is not what NVC means by need. A "feeling reflected back" with no impartiality is not what mirroring means. A "boundary stated" as a power move is not what boundary means. The protocols name states; deploying the scripts without the states produces the same fight in fancy clothes.

The receiving partner often detects this faster than the deploying partner. The receiving partner hears the protocol-language and feels the same old pressure underneath it. The result is usually one of two failures:

1. The receiving partner pushes back, and the deploying partner accuses them of "not being able to receive my honest sharing" or "refusing to honor my needs."
2. The receiving partner submits to the surface form and complies, which seems to work in the short term but loads the relationship with resentment because the form was being used to coerce, not to connect.

Either failure mode is worse than an honest fight without the framework, because the framework provides cover that prevents the actual material from being addressed.

## The Specific Markers

The deploying partner can check for the markers:

- Are you using the protocol's language in a moment where you would not normally choose to speak that way?
- Is there a "I just learned this, let me try it on you" quality to the move?
- Is the protocol being applied in one direction (yours to them) and not the other (theirs to you)?
- Are you ending statements with "right?" to elicit agreement to the framework?
- Are you explicit about which framework you are using ("I'm trying to do NVC here") in a way that makes it harder for the partner to push back?
- Does the protocol-language land for your partner as more pressure, not less?
- If they refused to engage with you in the protocol, would you experience it as them not being willing to do the work?

If yes, the protocol is being weaponized. The framework is functioning as a fight move, not as a connection move.

## Why Couples Slide Into This

The anti-pattern is not malicious. It is the predictable result of learning a tool and applying it to the highest-stakes context (the relationship's hardest fights) before the tool has been integrated.

The integration of a relational protocol takes time. The state the protocol points at has to be available in the body. The protocol's purpose has to be felt, not just understood. The application has to wait for situations where the state is genuinely accessible. Couples who learn a protocol and immediately try to deploy it in their hardest fights have skipped the integration step. The protocol becomes a tool the body uses to fight more sophisticatedly, not a tool that produces the state the protocol was designed to support.

This is one of the developmental stages of the [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]]. A couple learns the techniques and "they're using the tools, but they're starting to co-opt the tools." The stage is normal. The work is to notice it as a stage and to do the underlying state work that lets the tools become genuine.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is not to abandon the tools. It is to remember that the tools are pointers to states, and to do the state work the tools point at.

The relevant teachings:

- [[view]] is a state, not a script. When VIEW is being deployed without the underlying state, it is not VIEW.
- [[speak-your-truth-with-open-heart]] is the truth-state with the open-heart state. The combination is the move. The truth-language without the open-heart state is a different move.
- [[vulnerability]] is felt vulnerability, not stated vulnerability. Stating you are being vulnerable is not the same as being vulnerable.

The honest version of the protocol: do the inner work that produces the state, then let the protocol-language arise naturally from the state. The protocol's role is to mark the territory of the state, not to substitute for it.

If the state is not available right now, the honest move is to name that. "I don't have it in me to do this cleanly right now. Can we come back to this in an hour?" is more aligned with the protocols than a forced deployment of the protocol-language from a state that contradicts the protocol's purpose.

## The Particularly Brutal Variant: I'm Trying to Use the Tools Right and You're Not

A specific failure mode: the deploying partner becomes meta about the protocol use. "I'm trying to do this cleanly and you keep getting defensive." The meta-move makes the deploying partner the good-faith party and the receiving partner the obstacle. The framework has been weaponized at the second order: not just used as a fight move but used as evidence of the deployer's relative virtue.

The clean form: notice that the protocol is not landing. Take that as data about your own state. The other partner's resistance is often a clean read of the deployer's actual state being inconsistent with the deployer's form. Test that read in yourself before insisting the partner is wrong to resist.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You catch yourself reaching for framework-language in a moment where you are activated
- You notice your partner getting more annoyed when you "use the tools"
- A new framework has just been learned and is being deployed everywhere
- A previously open fight has become a fight where one partner is "doing it right" and the other is "not getting it"
- The protocol-language has become a vocabulary that you reach for to avoid the harder thing under it
- You hear yourself saying "I feel ____ when you ___" with the felt energy of accusation, not vulnerability
- You name boundaries that are actually demands disguised as boundaries
