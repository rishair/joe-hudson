# Don't Make "Stop Judging" Into Another Should (You're Just Judging Yourself for Judging)

## The Anti-Pattern

The person notices they are judgmental. They decide they should stop being judgmental. They start telling themselves "stop judging" every time they catch themselves judging. The result is that they have added a new judgment — the judgment of themselves for judging — on top of the original pattern. The system is now running two judgments where it was running one. The loop intensifies rather than resolving.

The form is recursive: judging is bad, I judge, therefore I am bad for judging, therefore I should stop, when I catch myself I judge myself for failing to stop, the self-judgment is itself a judgment, so now I am bad again, and so on.

"Don't make it into a should because that's just another judgment. Don't tell yourself you shouldn't judge. That's another judgment. Then you're judging yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Judgmental

## Why It Fails

The mechanism that produces judgment in the first place is emotional avoidance — judgment is doing protective work, keeping a feeling from being felt. See [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]]. The "I shouldn't judge" instruction does not address the protective function. It just adds a new layer of avoidance on top.

The new layer is structurally identical to the original. The person is now avoiding the feeling underneath the judgment AND the feeling of being a person who judges. Both feelings are being managed through the same mechanism: judgment. The system has not changed; it has just acquired a more sophisticated version of itself.

A second mechanism: shoulds activate the part of the system that resists being told what to do. The person who tells themselves "stop judging" produces an internal pushback that often takes the form of more judgment. The teaching about [[should-as-threat]] applies directly — a should is a threat the system reacts to with rebellion or submission, neither of which produces sustainable change.

A third mechanism: the person is now spending attention on monitoring whether they are judging, which is the same vigilant-monitoring posture that judgment requires in the first place. The body cannot relax. The cycle of monitoring-judging-judging-the-judging keeps the constriction continuous. See [[judgment-hurts-the-judger]] for why this is costly.

## What Joe Says Instead

The alternative is to notice rather than to should.

"All you have to do is just notice the pain, just notice what you want, and allow yourself to go in that direction. It's as easy as that." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Judgmental

The structural difference is:

- **Should:** "I shouldn't judge. I have to stop. I'm going to catch myself and correct."
- **Notice:** "Oh, I'm judging. That hurts in my body. That is not the kind of life I want. Let me feel what is underneath."

The should produces a fight against the judgment. The notice produces a relationship with the judgment that already includes seeing it, feeling its cost, and knowing what is wanted instead. The relationship to the pattern shifts even without any direct attempt to stop the pattern. The pattern dissolves through being felt rather than through being fought.

The operational substitute moves:

- [[notice-what-judgment-costs-you-and-what-you-actually-want]] — use the felt cost as the lever instead of the should.
- [[find-the-emotion-under-self-judgment]] — find the emotion the judgment is protecting and feel it.
- [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]] — understand the function so the cessation comes from completion rather than from suppression.
- [[stick-a-fucking-shit-in-front-of-the-should]] — interrupt the new should when it shows up.

## The Specific Form This Anti-Pattern Takes in Coaching

The person says: "I know I shouldn't judge, I'm working on it." The coaching mistake is to agree — to validate the meta-frame that they shouldn't be judging. This locks the loop in place by making the coach a collaborator in the new should.

The corrective move is to gently decline the meta-frame: "Forget should for a second. What does judging actually cost you? What do you actually want?" The decline does not argue with the person's intention to change. It just refuses to operate inside the should frame, which moves the conversation into the only frame where change is possible.

The same anti-pattern shows up in self-coaching when the person reads about judgment, decides to stop, and constructs a new program of judgment-suppression. The program will fail in the same way and for the same reasons. The alternative is to notice the pattern when it happens, feel the cost, locate the underneath-emotion, and let the system update without an enforcement campaign.

## The "I've Gotta Take the Pressure Off Myself" Instance

A clean live example of this anti-pattern from a procrastination coaching session. The man had just recognized that his side-project perfectionism was making the work impossible. He moved to:

> "Yeah, I got to take the pressure off myself."

Joe stopped him immediately:

> "That, that is a way to put pressure on yourself. 'I've got to take the pressure off myself.'"
>
> "Yeah, yeah, that's pressure, isn't it?"
>
> "That's more pressure."

The recursion is the same as the judgment loop. The insight was real — he had been putting pressure on himself, and the pressure was producing the procrastination. But the conversion of the insight into "I gotta take the pressure off" added a new pressure (the pressure to take pressure off) on top of the original. The system now has two pressures where it had one.

This is the exact same failure mode as "I shouldn't judge" — converting a felt recognition into a self-imposed program. The recognition wants to be lived from; the program wants to enforce. The enforcement is the pattern firing again in disguise.

The corrective is structural, not motivational. The person does not need to be reminded that pressure produces pressure. They need to be redirected from the should frame ("I gotta do X") to the felt frame ("X is happening, let me notice and stay with what is here"). In the website case, Joe did not say "stop pressuring yourself." He said, two breaths later: "How do you enjoy this moment 10% more?" The redirect to the felt enjoy-frame bypasses the should loop entirely. See [[enjoy-this-just-a-little-bit-more]].

## The "Can't Make It a Should" Closing Beat

A specific live instance of this anti-pattern surfaces as a closing beat at the end of a session where a daily emotional-discharge practice has just been prescribed. The client has been given an assignment ("move anger every day for a month"). The risk is that the assignment immediately becomes another inhabitant of the should-cage the practice was meant to free them from. Joe's closing reminder catches this in advance:

"Move anger. Pleasure. Oh, can't make it a should. Yeah." -- Joe Hudson, "I Should Be More Productive" Is Killing You

The structure of the closing beat:

1. **Restate the assignment** ("Move anger.") — make it concrete.
2. **Reframe the orientation** ("Pleasure.") — the practice is for pleasure, not for compliance.
3. **Name the anti-pattern in advance** ("Oh, can't make it a should.") — surface the failure mode before it happens.
4. **Get the client's recognition** ("Yeah.") — let the client confirm they see it.

The "pleasure" reframe matters. The client cannot just be instructed "do not make this a should." That instruction is itself a should. The reframe to "pleasure" gives the client a different orientation toward the practice — the question becomes "how do I enjoy this?" rather than "did I do my anger today?" The orientation-change is what prevents the should-conversion.

The closing beat is especially important when the assignment is being given to a client whose presenting concern was the should-cage itself. For these clients, every new practice immediately gets the should-form applied. The session would otherwise close having given the client one more thing to fail at. The reminder gives the client the explicit instruction to keep the practice in the wants-frame.

See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the practice this most often closes; the month-long-bet form of that practice often pairs with the "can't make it a should" closing beat.

## A Related Anti-Pattern: Making Any Inner Work Into a Should

This anti-pattern is one instance of a broader anti-pattern: making any inner work into a should. The same dynamic shows up with listening (see [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]]), with feeling emotions, with vulnerability, with VIEW. Any practice that is good for you becomes counterproductive when imposed via should.

The general principle: the route to sustainable change is felt-cost plus felt-want, not should. Whatever the practice, the question is not "should I do this more?" but "what is the current state actually producing, and what do I actually want?" The shift from should to notice-and-want is one of the most general moves in the methodology.

## Tone Note

The anti-pattern is not delivered as a scolding. The coach who catches themselves should-ing the person to stop judging would themselves be running the very pattern they are warning against. The teaching has to be applied to its own delivery: notice when the should is showing up in your own coaching, feel the cost (the conversation goes nowhere), and redirect to the wants frame.
