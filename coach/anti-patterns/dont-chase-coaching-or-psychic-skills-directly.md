# Don't Chase Coaching or Psychic Skills Directly

## The Anti-Pattern

Going directly after the skills — reading people, empathic attunement, intuitive knowing, the ability to sense what is going on in someone's body, the ability to coach someone into transformation in five minutes — is destructive. The skills are real. They emerge in people who have done a great deal of self-understanding. But pursuing them as goals creates a more solid form of ego, not the loosening that produces the actual skill. The chase produces the opposite of what it is reaching for.

"My experience is that if you go after trying to figure those skills out, it can be incredibly destructive. It can be like chasing money, it can be like chasing sex. It can really destroy somebody. It can create a much more solid form of ego." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The skill the person is reaching for arises as a side effect of a different project (knowing themselves). Trying to get the side effect directly bypasses the project that produces it, and substitutes a project (acquiring the skill) that is itself contaminated by the ego the skill is supposed to be free of.

## Why This Fails Structurally

The deep coaching ability is the ability to be present with another person without an agenda, to sense what is happening in them without projecting, and to respond from a place that has no investment in the outcome. The capacity for this comes from having done the same work on oneself: knowing oneself deeply enough that there is no defended self in the way, having moved through enough emotional terrain that one recognizes the same terrain in others, having loosened the ego enough that the attention can be free.

The chase of the skill installs the opposite. The person trying to acquire the ability to read people has, as their motivating frame, "I will be more competent / powerful / valuable when I can do this." That frame is itself ego. It is the same frame that produces form-one charisma (see [[two-forms-of-charisma]]). The frame is structurally incompatible with the loosening that would actually produce the skill.

Worse: the chase often does produce *something*. The person who studies body language, reads psychology books, watches Joe's coaching videos repeatedly, and trains themselves to look for cues can build a kind of pattern-matching ability that approximates the real skill. This approximation works in many surface situations and convinces the person they are getting there. What they have built is a more sophisticated version of the same construction the deep work would have dissolved. They have become a more skilled imposter at the work, and the better they get at it the more they reinforce the construction.

See [[the-pattern-seer-using-pattern-knowledge-to-stay-stuck]] for the version of this trap that catches therapists and coaches who learn the language of patterns and use the language to stay where they are.

## The Zen Story Joe Cites

"There's a Zen story about a young and an old monk walking down the path and the young monk says, 'oh, I can start to like know what people are thinking.' He's like, 'ah, what does that have to do with awakening?' 'Oh, I can read people's thoughts.' 'What does that have to do with awakening?' Like, as he walks through, he's going, having all these different things." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The story compresses the principle into one image. The young monk is excited about the powers because they are dramatic and feel like progress. The old monk keeps returning to the question: does it have to do with awakening? The implied answer: no. The powers are a side effect. The chase of them is a detour. The actual work is something else.

This is the same teaching Dr. K cites from Patanjali in the same conversation: the third chapter of Patanjali's Sutras is the description of psychic powers (siddhis) that emerge from the practices. Patanjali says, by the way, these things happen — but the third chapter is a side note. The work is elsewhere. Many traditions warn explicitly that getting attached to the powers is one of the most reliable ways to derail the practice.

## What Actually Produces the Skill

The longer route is the only route. The skill emerges in people who have done sustained self-work and have, over years, dissolved enough ego that the empathic and intuitive capacities become available. The capacities were always available; the ego was blocking them.

Joe's description of how he developed his ability to read what emotion someone is holding in their body:

"So I did this form of breathwork for like 7 years or so... And when I was 2 or 3 years into it, I could look at somebody's body and know what emotions they were holding and like how they were raised. Not because anybody said this arc means critical parent and this means like I'm not playing the failable mother. It was because I actually just went through all that stuff in myself." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The mechanism: by working through his own emotional terrain, Joe became familiar with the somatic signatures of those emotions in himself. When he later looked at someone else's body, the same signatures were legible to him because he had felt them in his own body. The ability to read others was a side effect of having known himself.

The contrast: someone who tries to learn the somatic signatures from a textbook — "this arc means critical parent" — is doing the chase version. They might get a few correct readings. They will not develop the deep ability, because the deep ability requires the felt familiarity that only comes from having moved through the terrain personally.

The same is true of empathic attunement, intuitive coaching ability, and the ability to sense the energetic state of a group. Each of these emerges from the same source: extensive self-work. None of them can be acquired as skills. All of them can be cultivated by working on the underlying capacity, which is one's relationship to oneself.

## Why It Is So Tempting

The chase is tempting because the skills are real and dramatic. Joe and Dr. K can both, in fact, sense things about people that are not visible to most others. They can hold space in a way that produces transformation in minutes. The visible effect is impressive. People who watch Joe coach or watch Dr. K work with patients can see the result and want the result.

The temptation has two specific shapes:

**The shortcut hope.** The person watches the coaching, identifies the moves, and thinks: if I learn the moves, I can do this. The shortcut hope is the chase. It assumes the work is in the moves. The work is in the underlying state. The moves are an expression of the state, not the source of it. See [[dont-treat-coaching-videos-as-how-to-coach]] for the related anti-pattern.

**The power hope.** The person wants to have the ability for the same reasons they want money or sex — because having it would change their relationship to the world. Joe is explicit that the chase of these powers can destroy people in the same way the chase of money or sex can destroy them. The contaminant is not the power; it is the chase. The power, arrived at by its proper route, does not destroy anyone. The chase of it, by any route, destroys what it touches.

## The Anti-Pattern's Operational Forms

Specific forms the chase takes:

- Reading every book on body language and trying to apply the lessons in conversations.
- Watching coaching videos and trying to copy the moves.
- Asking what specific question to ask in this specific situation, looking for the technique.
- Treating empathy as a skill to be acquired through training.
- Going to many workshops, retreats, certifications, looking for the one that confers the ability.
- Trying to be more present, more attuned, more intuitive, as a project.
- Studying psychology to read people more accurately.
- Trying to "build the empathic muscle" as a discipline.

None of these are wrong in isolation. The corruption is in the orientation: doing them in order to acquire the skill rather than as part of the deeper self-knowing project. The same activity can be either: a person who reads psychology because they are curious about how minds work, including their own, is doing something different from a person who reads psychology to be able to manipulate others or impress others with insight.

The diagnostic is the question: what am I doing this for? If the answer is "to acquire the skill," the chase is on. If the answer is "to know myself better, and the side effects will be what they are," the actual work is happening.

## What Joe Recommends Instead

"That's how I would recommend the path. I would recommend is to understand yourself more and more and more and more. And the more you understand yourself, this stuff naturally occurs." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The path is the path. Understand yourself. Keep going. The skills will arrive on their own timetable when they arrive at all. Some people develop strong empathic abilities through this work; some do not. The work is not contingent on the skills arriving. The work is its own reward.

The instruction is also the freedom from the chase. Once the person understands that the skills will arrive when they arrive, they stop trying to engineer the arrival. They can do the self-work for its own sake. The work goes faster when it is not being instrumentalized.

See [[migratory-path]] for the structural frame: the development unfolds according to its own internal sequence. Each stage of self-understanding produces what it produces, when it produces it. Trying to skip stages or grab side effects out of order does not work.

## The Wife-Mocks-the-Skill Variation

There is a corollary in the same session: people whose ego attaches to the skill they have developed are often the ones whose closest relationships destroy the ego attached to it. Dr. K describes how his wife relentlessly mocks his spiritual seriousness. Joe describes the same with his own wife. The mockery is destruction of the ego that had attached to the developed ability. The destruction is, in retrospect, what made the ability usable rather than performative. See [[anything-that-destroys-you-reveals-what-cannot-be-destroyed]].

The implication: even when the skill has been honestly developed through self-work, the ego can attach to having the skill. The chase can reassert itself one layer up: "now I am the person who can do this." The mockery, the rupture, the destruction of that ego is part of the developmental sequence. The skill that survives the destruction is the skill that is actually free of the ego that would have corrupted it.

## Joe's Words

"My experience is that if you go after trying to figure those skills out, it can be incredibly destructive. It can be like chasing money, it can be like chasing sex. It can really destroy somebody. It can create a much more solid form of ego. Whereas if you allow that stuff to unfold naturally because of your own self-awareness — so that's how I would recommend the path. I would recommend is to understand yourself more and more and more and more. And the more you understand yourself, this stuff naturally occurs." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

"So that's the way I recommend doing it because that's the thing that actually gets you to the freedom that you're really looking for beyond the power of being able to do it." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The last clause is the structural reveal: the freedom is what the person is really after. The power feels like the goal but is not. The freedom that produces the power is the actual goal. Chasing the power, even if it works, produces the power without the freedom — which is not what the person wanted, even if they thought it was.
