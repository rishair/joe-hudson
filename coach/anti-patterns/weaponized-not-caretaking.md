# Don't weaponize 'not caretaking'

## The Anti-Pattern

A person learns that caretaking is suboptimal. They decide to stop. The stopping takes one of several distorted forms that are not actually clean care, but are not caretaking either. The new stance looks like growth from inside, but functions as a new wall.

Specific manifestations:

**The "not my job" wall.** "I don't care about your feelings. I'm not responsible for them. It's not my job to make you feel better." Said in a tone that pushes the other person away rather than honors them. The words are technically correct. The energy is rejection. The new stance has the same effect as caretaking (managing the other through behavior), just in the inverse direction.

**Judging other caretakers.** Once the pattern becomes visible in the self, it becomes irritatingly visible in others. The new convert points out caretaking everywhere they see it, often with contempt. The judgment is the inner war with the self's own caretaker, projected outward.

**The "I'm done with caretaking" pedestal.** "I've gotten past this. Everyone else needs to process their own emotions." The new framing positions the person as advanced and the people around them as primitive. The distance is dressed as development.

**Cutting off as the new default.** Whatever situation might activate caretaking gets avoided. The person stops having emotional conversations, stops being available for hard moments, stops engaging with the messy. They call it boundaries. It is withdrawal.

## Why It Fails

The mechanism is the same in all forms: the person has not actually dropped the management of the other. They have changed the direction of the management. Where caretaking tried to keep the other comfortable, weaponized non-caretaking tries to keep the other at a distance. Both are forms of using the relationship to avoid feeling something in the self.

The body knows. The somatic signature is identical to defense.

"Your body will start constricting, whether you're being codependent or whether you decide to be defensive in a more traditional manner or cut yourself off from them. You'll start feeling your body somatically start to tense up, and that's like the indicator." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The body tension is the universal diagnostic. The form of the avoidance does not matter. If the body tightens, something is being defended against.

The deeper failure: this stance feels like progress because it is the opposite of the old pattern. But the opposite of a defended position is usually another defended position. The cure is not the opposite. The cure is the absence of defense entirely. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for the clean version.

## The Judgment-of-Other-Caretakers Variant

This is the most common form because it arrives early and self-justifies easily.

"The first way that it's gonna happen is that you are gonna start judging other people who are being caretaking or caretaking of you. That's the first thing that happens in the process of learning about this. And what's actually happening is that you are judging your own caretaker, that you have started a war with yourself about your caretaker." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The structure: the person has seen the pattern in themselves, decided it is bad, and is now at war with it. The war is projected outward onto other caretakers. Every act of caretaking observed in others is unconsciously read as evidence of one's own failed pattern.

The trap: the war with the self's own caretaker is itself a form of management. The person is trying to change a part of themselves through hostility. This is the same operation caretaking ran on the other person, just turned inward. See [[tools-to-change-not-love]] for the underlying anti-pattern.

The exit: the caretaker part of the self needs welcoming, not eviction. It has been doing its best with what it knew. It is not the enemy. The judgment of it produces more rigidity, not less.

## The Pedestal Variant

The pedestal arrives later and is harder to spot from inside.

"Like, oh, you know what? I'm done caretaking. I'm not caretaking anymore. And what that means is that I'm over it. I'm done. I'm better. All these other people are expecting me to caretake them. And, you know, they just need to be experiencing their emotions. So I just need to let them experience their emotions because they need to process them. And then all of a sudden it becomes about everybody else having to do their process, their shit. And then I am just on this pedestal of like, okay, I'm good. And then that has always led to some kind of heartbreaker challenge." -- (Brett Kistler in conversation with Joe, Care over Caretaking)

The framing of "everyone else needs to do their work" is structurally identical to spiritual bypass. See [[spiritual-narcissism]]. The person has not actually become a clean carer. They have moved into a stance where their distance is justified by the inadequacy of the people around them.

The heartbreaker that follows is predictable. The pedestal stance produces relationships in which the person is increasingly isolated. The isolation eventually breaks down through a relational crisis that forces re-engagement with the very vulnerability the pedestal was constructed to avoid.

## The "Not My Job" Variant

The "I'm not responsible for your feelings" line is technically true and useful in certain contexts. The anti-pattern is the use of it as a wall rather than as a framing of clean responsibility.

The clean version: "I love you. Your feelings are yours. I am not going to manage them, because doing so would treat you as incapable. I am here, undefended, while you have them." This is care in the [[caring-vs-caretaking]] sense.

The weaponized version: "I don't care about your feelings. They're not my problem." Same words, different intent. The clean version opens the heart. The weaponized version closes it.

The test is the same as for [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]: does delivering this stance open or close the heart toward the other?

## The Forgetting-Boundaries Variant

Joe also names a less obvious failure mode that happens further down the path. Once a person reaches actual clean care, with the softness and openness that comes with it, they sometimes overextend. The clean care feels so good that the person forgets that even clean care has structure.

"You can't keep loving people without boundaries. A mom who doesn't have boundaries raises horrible children that eventually will be very abusive to the mom or dad for that matter." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

This is not an anti-pattern in the sense of weaponized non-caretaking. It is the opposite failure: forgetting the boundary structure that makes clean care sustainable. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]].

## The Clean Version: Naming the Caretaking and Inviting the Real Relationship

The clean alternative to weaponizing non-caretaking is to name the caretaking honestly to the person being caretaken for. The key elements: own that you have been caretaking, name that you are going to stop, name what the cost of the dynamic has been (often: the other person's resentment that the caretaker did not understand), and invite a real relationship in place of the caretaking one.

A client of Joe's described a years-long passive-aggressive friendship that transformed in a single conversation. The clean naming sounded like this:

> "I've been caretaking you, and I'm not gonna caretake you anymore. I'm gonna be really honest and straightforward with you. And there's a way in which you've been mean to me, like you're resentful of me, and I don't understand why or how you could be resentful of me, but I don't want a relationship where we have resentment. I don't want to be a part of that."
>
> *(Source: What's So Scary About Boundaries?)*

The response from the friend: "I was angry at you and I just couldn't tell why. I didn't know what was going on. But as soon as you said that to me, it just all dropped. I was resentful for the caretaking, and you weren't treating me like an equal. And now that we are, it's just wonderful."

This is what the clean version looks like in practice. It is not "I refuse to engage with your feelings." It is "I have been operating in a way that has been bad for both of us; here is what I see; let's relate differently." The first version is a wall. The second version is an invitation.

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective for all these variants is the same: keep welcoming the actual self, including the caretaker, including the judging-of-other-caretakers, including the pedestal, including the wall.

"Any way that we are defending ourselves is an opportunity to lean in, love more, and accept ourselves and others." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The body diagnostic is the live check. When the constriction shows up, something is being defended. The move is to drop the wall, not to switch which wall is being held.

For the judgment-of-others variant specifically: the path is to recognize that the judgment is information about the self, not about the others. Welcoming the inner caretaker dissolves the outer judgment.

For the pedestal variant: the path is the heartbreaker challenge that arrives. The pedestal is unsustainable. When it breaks, the welcoming of what is broken is the actual work.

For the "not my job" wall: the path is to find the version that opens the heart instead of closing it. Same content, different state.

## Joe's Words

"People will start weaponizing it and say, now I'm just gonna cut myself off from you in a different way. And our society likes to tell the story that if you're caretaking, then you're not cutting yourself off of people, you're caring for them. You're not being arrogant, you're not being rude, you're not being dismissive, but you're still cutting yourself off from them because you're not telling them your truth. You're managing them just in a different way. And so then all of a sudden, if you decide to cut off from them in another way, like I'm not gonna talk to you, I'm not going to basically feel or have empathy, then it's just another way of cutting them off." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking
