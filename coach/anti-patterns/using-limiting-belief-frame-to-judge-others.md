# Don't Use the Limiting Belief Frame to Judge Others

## The Anti-Pattern

You learn the limiting-belief framework. You start seeing them everywhere -- in your spouse, your colleagues, your parents, strangers. You start using "they have a limiting belief about X" as a way of explaining their behavior. You take a small position of superiority that you do not have to address what they are saying because they are just inside their limiting belief.

Brett names this directly:

"You see this in kind of what people describe as woke culture a lot, where somebody will see somebody having some unwanted consequence in their life and be like, 'Oh, look, they just have a bunch of limiting beliefs.' And then there's this kind of this way to be above them or better than them or to try to fix them or basically, you know, in some sense, sometimes just gaslighting people into believing that they are at fault for everything that happens rather than they're responsible for their experience."

Joe agrees and adds the diagnostic: the person doing this is themselves inside a limiting belief, and the body is reporting it through physical constriction.

## Why It Fails

The framework is designed for the user, not for others. Applied to yourself, it dissolves the belief and produces freedom. Applied to others, it does not dissolve anything in them and installs a superiority position in you that is itself a new limiting belief.

The structure is exactly [[comparative-mind]]: better than (they have limiting beliefs, I see through them). The Tibetan Buddhists treat all forms of comparative mind as misery, regardless of which side of the comparison the person occupies. The physical constriction confirms it.

"If a person is saying that, 'Oh, look, they're not as good as me,' they are having a physical constriction most likely, and that physical constriction is causing them pain, which is a sign of another limiting belief." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

It also fails ethically. Telling someone they are responsible for everything that happens to them collapses the distinction between [[fault-vs-responsibility]]. The framework is meant to expand a person's relationship to their own experience. Used to attribute fault to others for their suffering, it becomes a tool of dismissal.

## The Gaslighting Version

The most damaging form: using the framework to dismiss someone else's actual experience as "just their limiting belief." Someone reports a real harm. The response is "well, that sounds like a limiting belief you have about that situation."

This is not coaching. It is dismissal in transformational language. The framework gets used to deny the validity of the other person's account, which is a different and older anti-pattern wearing new clothing.

See [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the broader pattern of using transformational language to maintain a position of superiority.

## What Joe Says Instead

The framework is for noticing your own limiting beliefs, not theirs. If you find yourself reaching for "they have a limiting belief," check your body. The constriction will tell you what you are doing. Then ask what limiting belief is operating in you in this moment to need to see them that way.

"If a person is saying that, 'Oh, look, they're not as good as me,' they are having a physical constriction most likely, and that physical constriction is causing them pain, which is a sign of another limiting belief." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

The other person's growth, if it happens, will happen at their pace and in their way. See [[the-work-is-yours-not-mine]] and [[fixing-people]]. Your job is not their belief structure. Your job is yours.
