# Don't Tell Your Partner or Team to Make Their Passive Aggression Active

## The Anti-Pattern

You hear the teaching: "passive aggression is harder to work with than direct aggression, so the move is to invite the direct form." You think it is brilliant. You bring it to your spouse, your team, your mother:

- "You're being passive-aggressive. Just be direct."
- "Make it active. Bring it. I welcome your anger."
- "Enough of this — say what you actually want to say."
- "I just listened to this podcast and you're being passive-aggressive."

This is itself the same problem you are trying to address. You are trying to change them. You are using a new tool to do the same thing you have always been doing.

"Don't fucking do that. You're trying to change somebody else, you're doing the same thing they're doing just in a different way. It's the same, it's the same habit, it's the same pattern just recreating itself." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Why It Fails

The teaching about converting passive to active aggression works only inside a specific frame: a facilitated coaching context where the person has given consent, where the facilitator can hold the consequences, where the aggression has a container that does not exist in normal life.

Taken out of that frame and brought into a relationship, the same move becomes:
- A demand that the other person change their behavior
- A new vocabulary for the old project of fixing them
- A way to feel righteous about your own controlling impulse
- A way to apply pressure under cover of the teaching itself

The other person will hear this exactly correctly. They will hear it as you trying to control them. The passive aggression will continue, often with a new layer of guardedness because now they know you are watching for it.

## Why It Is Especially Sneaky

This anti-pattern hides better than most because the teaching it weaponizes is genuinely good. "Make the passive aggression active" really is what works in a coaching context. The thing the person did learn is real. The error is in the application.

The error has the same structure as the very behavior being corrected. The person doing the correcting is being aggressive about the other person's aggression, while disclaiming the aggression by pointing at the teaching. This is exactly the structure of passive aggression itself: aggression with a wrapper that disclaims it.

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement is to keep the focus on yourself rather than on changing them.

"It's not your job. If you want to be in a loving relationship, love the person and yourself the way that you are, and don't be trying to change them. And so that whole, you should be, you should do this, you da da da da, that's you trying to manipulate them." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The honest moves available to you:

1. **Draw boundaries on what you will accept.** Not "stop being passive-aggressive" but "when I am being guilt-tripped, I am going to walk away." Boundaries about your own behavior, not demands about theirs.
2. **Name what you are experiencing.** "Ouch, that hurt." (See [[say-ouch]].) Owning your experience without trying to make them different.
3. **Invite them as an invitation, not a demand.** "I notice this kind of thing is happening. How would you feel about listening to this podcast together?" The invitation respects their freedom to decline.
4. **Do your own work on receiving passive aggression.** If you are triggered by it, you have your own version somewhere — internal or external. The triggers point to your work, not theirs.

"Now you can invite them to it and say, hey, you know, I noticed this kind of thing is happening. How would you feel? Let's listen to this podcast together. But it's, if you're in there trying to change them to be different so that you can be happy, then you're as much in the fear triangle as they are." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Joe's Words

The bluntness of the response — "don't fucking do that" — is intentional. The anti-pattern is so common and so seductive that a soft warning would not land. The move is specifically the kind of move that personal-development consumers reach for the moment they learn the teaching, and the consequences for the relationships involved are significant.

"My guidance is don't fucking do that. Like, you're trying to change somebody else, you're, you're doing the same thing they're doing just in a different way." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## The Related Carve-Out: If You Welcome Their Anger

There is a related move that is not this anti-pattern: telling someone you welcome their anger if it arises. This is different from demanding that they produce it.

"If you're feeling that way, that's great. As long as you can not feel obligated to do that and be able to say stop when you need it to stop. But yeah, if you're like, I'm feeling what's happening right now as passive aggression, and I just want you to know I'm totally happy to hear what you want. I'm happy to hear your anger. I'm happy to hear the boundary that you might have. Sounds fantastic." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The distinction:
- **Anti-pattern:** "You should be direct. Stop being passive-aggressive. Bring it."
- **Valid:** "I'm happy to hear your anger if you want to share it. I'm happy to hear what you want."

The first is a demand for them to behave differently. The second is an offer about what you are available to receive. The difference is whether their freedom to do whatever they want is preserved.

## Boundary Conditions

This anti-pattern does not apply when:
- You are a paid coach or facilitator and the person has explicitly engaged you for this work
- The person has asked you to help them work with their own passive aggression and has consented to the move
- You are inviting (genuinely, with their option to decline) versus instructing

It does apply when:
- You are a partner, family member, friend, or colleague using the teaching to apply pressure
- The other person has not asked for help with this and you are introducing it unsolicited
- The teaching is in the room as leverage, even if you are not naming it directly

## When Someone Might Be Doing This Without Seeing It

- They have just learned about passive aggression and feel newly able to name it in others
- They feel a kind of relief or righteousness when they identify it
- They notice they want to share the teaching with a specific person in their life
- They are framing the conversation as "for your own good" or "for the sake of our relationship"
- They feel impatient with the other person's pace of change

If any of these are present, this anti-pattern is in the room. The move is to drop the project and return to working on yourself.

## Tone

Joe's tone is direct and unapologetic. The bluntness is its own teaching: the temptation to apply the teaching to others is strong enough that a gentle warning would not interrupt it. The "don't fucking do that" is a deliberate jolt.
