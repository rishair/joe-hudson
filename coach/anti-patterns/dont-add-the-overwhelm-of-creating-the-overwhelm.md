# Don't Tell Someone They're Creating Their Overwhelm Before They Can See It

## The Anti-Pattern

Telling an overwhelmed person "you are creating your own overwhelm" before they are able to see how they are doing it. The framing is true and eventually empowering. Delivered too early in the journey, it lands as one more thing the person now has to manage. The work that was supposed to free them becomes another item on the list of failures.

The same coaching tool that liberates someone in one moment can crush them in another. The variable is whether the person has the capacity to see what is being pointed at. Without that capacity, "you are responsible for this" reads as "this is your fault."

## Why It Fails

You cannot be responsible for something you cannot see. Responsibility, in the useful sense, requires the recognition of how you are participating in the pattern. Without that recognition, the framing of responsibility just becomes blame, and blame compounds rather than relieves overwhelm.

The person who is told "you are creating your overwhelm" before they can see it does not gain the insight that produces choice. They gain a new piece of self-criticism. The original overwhelm was about the external pressures. The new overwhelm is about being the kind of person who creates overwhelm. The load has doubled.

"There's another place in the journey where if you think, 'I am responsible for my own overwhelm,' it just makes you more overwhelmed. It's just one more thing that you then have to deal with that's on your plate that you're scared of that you have to manage." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

"You're not responsible for it until you can see that you're responsible for it. Once you can see it, then you have choice, but before then you don't." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## What Joe Says Instead

Joe is willing to hold the framing back until the person has the capacity to make use of it. He is precise about this because he has watched the wrong framing harm people.

Where the person cannot yet see how they are creating the overwhelm:
- Acknowledge the externals as real.
- Acknowledge that whatever is happening is hard.
- Work the immediate-moment regulation (breath, space, processing the unfelt emotion).
- Trust that the capacity to see the creation of the pattern will arrive when it arrives.

Where the person can see how they are creating the overwhelm:
- The framing of responsibility becomes liberating. It returns choice. It opens the door to changing the pattern.
- The person now has somewhere to stand and act from.

The diagnostic is in the response. If the person hears "you're creating this" and lights up with a sense of agency, the framing is right for where they are. If they collapse into shame, the framing came too early and the work now is to back off and meet them where they actually are.

## The Right Teaching, the Wrong Timing

Joe's stance here is part of a larger principle: the right teaching at the wrong time can be the wrong teaching. See [[stories-are-developmental-scaffolding]]. Different stages of someone's journey require different stories. The story that liberates at one stage can imprison at another.

This is also why Joe distinguishes [[fault-vs-responsibility]]. Responsibility without shame is the goal. Premature framings tend to collapse responsibility into fault, which is the trap. Patient framings hold the door open until the person can step through into responsibility without the shame.

## The Self-Diagnostic Version

The same anti-pattern operates internally. A person who has done some personal growth work may catch themselves in overwhelm and immediately add: "and it's because I'm not handling my life well." That meta-overwhelm is structurally the same as the coach landing it too early. The growth tool has become a stick.

If you find yourself in overwhelm and your first move is to blame yourself for being overwhelmed, notice the move. The blame is not the empowerment it pretends to be. The empowerment becomes available later, after the immediate-moment work has restored some baseline capacity.
