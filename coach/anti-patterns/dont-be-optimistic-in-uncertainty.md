# Don't Be Optimistic in Uncertain Times

## The Anti-Pattern

Faced with an uncertain situation, the person reaches for optimism. "It's going to be fine." "We'll figure it out." "I have hope that things will get better." "It'll all work out." The optimism is felt as a relief — the bracing temporarily releases, the body softens briefly, the conversation moves on. The relief is real and the relief is short.

The relief is short because the optimism is a prediction that the system has to keep defending against contradicting data. Defending costs energy. When the data finally arrives in a form that cannot be filtered, the system crashes harder than it would have if the optimism had never been used as the buffer.

This is not the same as the realistic positive expectation that things might work out. Optimism in the sense Joe warns against is a fixed assertion of a good outcome used as a way to not feel the fear, grief, or anger that is actually here.

## Why It Fails

**The optimism assumes a future the person does not know.** "I'll be out by Christmas" assumes a Christmas release that has not been promised. Each disappointed Christmas is the next blow to a system that has been calibrating itself to an outcome that is not arriving.

**The optimism filters out the data needed to respond skillfully.** The person hoping the bad thing will not arrive cannot prepare for it, because preparing would require admitting it might. The CEO whose company faces a real market shift and is in "we'll bounce back" mode is not making the pivots that the actual situation requires.

**The optimism is sugar.** It feels good for a minute and produces a crash. The crash is worse than the initial difficulty would have been if met directly.

"It's like sugar. I want it for a short period of time, but I don't like it for a long period of time." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

**The optimism is a confession of inability to handle what is here.**

"What you're basically saying to yourself is you can't handle it. You're basically saying you're not going to be able to handle this change." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The optimism is the system's report that it does not believe it can bear the actual situation. The cure is not better optimism. The cure is the recognition that the system can, in fact, bear the actual situation. See [[take-the-adventure-stance]] and [[the-certainty-that-is-actually-here]].

## The Vietnam POW Demonstration

The cleanest demonstration is the Stockdale story. The admiral was asked who survived the prison camp and who did not. His answer:

"The people who made it out are the people who knew they would make it out. The people who died were the optimists." -- as recounted by Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The optimists were the ones who said "I'll be out by Christmas." Christmas came. They were still in. Easter came. They were still in. Each holiday was a fresh blow because each holiday had been recruited into the prediction. The accumulating disappointment was what killed them.

The survivors were not optimists. They were also not pessimists. They had the stance described in [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]] — they knew they would survive eventually, without knowing when. They did not load any particular future date with their hope. See [[stockdale-paradox]] for the full example.

The lesson generalizes: optimism in genuine uncertainty does not just fail to help. It actively kills.

## The Body's Trained Recognition

Over time, a person who has been burned by their own optimism enough times will start to develop a body-level early warning. The early stages of optimism — the rise of "it's going to be fine" in the chest — will register as a small uh-oh.

"You do that for enough times and you get burned by it enough that even the feeling of optimism — luckily we have this capacity to start detecting. Like, we learn where that goes. If I feel a certain kind of optimism in my system, there's a part of me that's like, 'uh-oh. Something's going to go wrong,' literally, because I'm doing the thing that last time I did it brought something — brought about something I didn't want." -- Brett Kistler, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

This is not pessimism. It is the system having learned that this particular flavor of optimism precedes a worse outcome than no-optimism would have. The recognition is a developmental gain. It signals the system is ready to take the adventure stance instead.

## What to Do Instead

The intervention is not the opposite of optimism (pessimism — see [[dont-be-pessimistic-in-uncertainty]]). It is the dropping of the prediction altogether and the picking up of the question "how do I want to be in this?"

The Stockdale-style version of "I know I will get out": this is not optimism. It is a stance taken about how one will be inside the situation, without specifying when or how the situation will resolve. The survivors did not predict any particular date. They committed to surviving however long it took.

See [[take-the-adventure-stance]] for the operational move and [[how-do-you-want-to-be-in-this-uncertainty]] for the question that replaces the prediction-making.

The other component: feel what the optimism was defending against. The optimism was usually a defense against fear, grief, or helplessness. Letting these be felt is the actual move. The optimism only seemed necessary because the underlying emotion was not allowed to be present. See [[apply-view-to-fear]] and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

## Joe's Words

"There's either too much fear or too much rigidity or too much hope that basically makes it so that they're not reacting to the reality that's there and they're not taking ownership of it." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

"Optimism meant 'I'll be out by Christmas.' Optimism meant hope that it was going to happen soon, that I wasn't going to have to endure this thing." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

"You have to face what's true. You can not face what's true with negativity: 'oh my God, we're fucked, this is never gonna go well.' That's not true. 'This is gonna be great, it's gonna be awesome, we're gonna do fantastic.' That's not true." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- A person in a hard situation who keeps saying "it'll be fine" without evidence
- A CEO in a real market shift who is in cheerleader mode
- A family in denial of a terminal diagnosis using positive framing
- A team being managed by hope rather than honest assessment
- A person who has been hoping for change in a relationship for years without anything changing
- A person whose body language is rigid while the words are positive
