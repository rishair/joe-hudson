# Speed-Bumping

## The Anti-Pattern

Speed-bumping is the move of intellectually accepting the truth of what someone is telling you so that you do not have to feel it. The signature responses are "Yep, I got it." "Yeah, I know." "Blah, blah, blah." The verbal receipt is real — the person actually does know, intellectually, what is being said. The somatic landing is what is being prevented. The "I know" is the obstacle that keeps the message from going from the head to the body.

In live coaching or therapy, speed-bumping arrives as the client's response to a substantive observation, especially an observation that is compassionate or true. The coach says something direct. The client says "yeah, I know." The conversation moves on as if the message was received. The client's body is unchanged. The defense did its job.

"We call this speed bumping. Yep, I got it. Yeah, I know. Blah, blah, blah, blah." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of People

The name is precise. A speed bump is an obstacle on a road that slows you down but does not stop you from passing. The speed-bumper does not refuse the message outright (which would be obvious resistance and would invite re-delivery). They acknowledge it (which produces the appearance of receipt and gives the deliverer permission to move on). The message is allowed to pass — but slowly enough, and over a high enough bump, that it never actually arrives.

## Why It Fails

The reason speed-bumping is the anti-pattern (rather than just a defense to work with) is that it produces the appearance of progress without the substance. The coach feels heard. The client feels they've engaged. The session feels productive. Nothing has actually changed.

For the client, the cost is invisible at first and significant over time. The client has built a system that admits insights as words and refuses them as experiences. Each insight that gets speed-bumped is one more piece of intellectual furniture in a house that the client cannot live in. The client may accumulate dozens or hundreds of frames over years of work without any of them changing how the client's body feels in the morning.

For the coach, the failure mode is that the coach starts varying the content trying to get past the speed bump. The coach reframes, finds a new angle, brings a different metaphor. Each new content variant is a fresh thing for the speed-bumper to acknowledge intellectually. The variation gives the speed-bumper more to receive without landing. The coach's effort to break through is the speed-bumper's exercise material.

The deeper reason speed-bumping is structural rather than incidental: in the endurer pattern (see [[endurer-pattern]]), letting something land would require feeling the pain the endurer is built to not feel. The speed-bump is the front door of the endurer's defense system. The "yeah, I know" is the polite-looking refusal that protects the underneath.

## What Joe Does Instead

The move that meets speed-bumping is not to vary the content. The move is to hold the message and the open heart, and to repeat without varying. See [[keep-the-heart-open-and-repeat-the-truth]] for the full move article.

In the Good Will Hunting scene, the therapist says "It's not your fault." Will responds "Yeah, I know that." The therapist does not reframe. He does not say "but do you really know?" He does not bring a different angle. He says "It's not your fault" again. Then again. Then again.

"He keeps repeating it, which of course is going to bring out what he knows love to be. He's going to get this just direct bam of love, and he's going to respond to it the way that he has been taught to respond to love." -- Joe Hudson

The mechanism is that the repetition with the open heart, undeflected by the coach's eagerness to vary the message, eventually delivers the contact without the speed-bumper having a new content-item to acknowledge intellectually. The speed-bumper can dismiss "it's not your fault" with "yeah, I know" the first time. The fifth time, with the heart open, the same words have nowhere to be dismissed to. The intellectual receipt has already been performed and the message keeps arriving. Eventually the body has to do something with it.

The response when the breach happens is often anger (the defense gives way to discharge) before sadness (the underneath arrives). See [[good-will-hunting-its-not-your-fault]] for the canonical illustration of this sequence.

## What Speed-Bumping Is Not

Speed-bumping is not the same as actually receiving and integrating an insight. The difference is detectable in the body. Real receipt produces a softening, a settling, sometimes tears, sometimes a slow breath out. The speed-bump produces nothing. The body is unchanged. The speed-bumper may even smile politely while the body is doing nothing.

Speed-bumping is also distinct from [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]], though they often co-occur. Intellectualization is the active move of going to abstract conceptual territory to manage the inquiry. Speed-bumping is the more passive move of accepting the content without letting it land. A client can speed-bump without intellectualizing (they may have no theory to offer, just the bare "yeah, I know"). A client can intellectualize without speed-bumping (they may genuinely be wrestling with ideas, with their body responding). The two defenses are related but not identical.

Speed-bumping is also not the same as compliment-deflection (see [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]]). Compliment-deflection rejects the content ("no, I'm not the pretty one"). Speed-bumping accepts the content and rejects the experience ("yeah, you're right, I am" — said in a way that prevents the experience). The two are different shapes of refusal-to-receive.

## How to Recognize Speed-Bumping in Live Session

- The client's words say yes. Their body does nothing.
- The client says "yeah, I know" or equivalent more than once in a session.
- The client uses the right vocabulary for the work but does not appear to inhabit any of it.
- The client moves the conversation forward after each observation as if the observation has been processed.
- The client says "I've heard this before" or "my therapist said something similar" or "I read this in your book" in a way that frames the truth as already-known and therefore not requiring landing.
- The session feels productive from a content perspective and underwhelming from an energetic perspective.
- The client is articulate about their pattern and unchanged by their articulateness.

## What Joe Says Instead

The coach's job is not to vary the content when speed-bumping shows up. The coach's job is to hold the open heart, name the speed-bumping if it is becoming chronic, and continue the contact.

When the speed-bumping becomes the topic itself, it can be named. "I'm noticing that you said 'yeah, I know' just now, and your body did nothing. What is it that you actually know? What would it be like to let this in instead of just agreeing with it?" The naming has to be done from open heart, not from coach frustration. If the coach is irritated, the naming becomes another thing for the client to speed-bump.

The other move is to slow down and check for landing. "I want you to let that sit for a second. Don't agree with me. Just let it be in the room. What do you notice?" The slowdown interrupts the velocity that lets the speed-bumper acknowledge and move on. The client has to stop long enough for the body to register something.

## Joe's Words

"We call this speed bumping. Yep, I got it. Yeah, I know. Blah, blah, blah, blah. And he's addressing the shame that's directly happening, right? This guy's angry at himself. He's pissed at himself for breaking up with a woman who he obviously deeply cares about. And this guy's saying, 'It's not your fault.' And he's like, 'Yeah, yeah, I got it. Like, I know your language. I know your therapy speak. Yeah, yeah, yeah.' But he keeps — like, the therapist here is just keeping his heart open and just keeps on repeating it." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of People

The "I know your language. I know your therapy speak" is precise. The endurer-pattern speed-bumper has often done a lot of work. They are fluent in the vocabulary. The fluency is not the same as the felt experience. The speed-bumping uses the fluency as the defense — "I already know this material" becomes the move that prevents the material from doing anything.

## Boundary Conditions

Not every "yeah, I know" is speed-bumping. Sometimes a client really does already know the thing and the move forward is to honor that and find the next layer. The diagnostic is the body, not the words. If the body is alive with the recognition, the "I know" is a real receipt and the work moves on. If the body is doing nothing, the "I know" is the speed bump and the work is to stay with the message until the body registers something.

Speed-bumping is also more common in high-capacity, intellectually fluent clients than in clients who are new to inner work. The vocabulary is the material. A client without the vocabulary can't speed-bump in this particular form — they may resist in other ways, but the polite-intellectual-receipt move requires the polite intellectual vocabulary to perform.
