# Don't Suppress Emotion to Perform (The Deadpan Trap)

## The Anti-Pattern

A performer is told (by a coach, a mentor, another performer, a general piece of cultural advice) that the way to be good on stage is to suppress emotion. Deadpan delivery. Don't react. Don't show too much. Be cool. Stay composed. The performer takes the advice. They go on stage and hold back their emotional expression. The performance feels inauthentic to them, the audience can sense the absence of the person, and the performer concludes they are bad at performing.

The compressed form: someone is asking "how do I be authentic on stage?" while simultaneously following advice that requires them to suppress their authentic emotional expression. The advice and the goal are in direct contradiction. The contradiction is the trap.

The specific advice can take many forms:

- "Deadpan is good for you."
- "Don't react too much."
- "Be cool on stage."
- "Don't let them see you sweat."
- "Don't break character."
- "Don't laugh at your own jokes."
- "Stay composed, the audience needs that."
- "Hold the persona, don't drop into yourself."

Each of these is well-meaning. Each is also a specific instruction to suppress emotion. A performer following them cannot be authentic, because authenticity requires emotional fluidity and the advice requires its absence.

## Why It Fails

Authenticity on stage is not the absence of performance. It is the presence of the person inside the performance. The person inside the performance is having emotions. If those emotions are being suppressed, the person is not present — only the performance is. The audience feels the absence. The performer feels the absence. Everyone is operating with less than what is in the room.

The contradiction is structural. Almost every performer the asking-person admires is doing the opposite of what they have been told. Most stand-up comics are deeply emotional on stage. They feel what they are doing. They laugh at their own jokes. They cry on stage. They get angry on stage. The performers who are actually generating the effect the asking-person is chasing are doing it through full emotional presence, not through emotional suppression. The advice to suppress is being given by people who have not solved the problem they are advising on.

The deadpan advice in particular is often given because deadpan looks like a style. It is a style. But the deadpan comedians who land are still fully emotional; the deadpan is the surface. Underneath the deadpan is a person fully in their body fully feeling what they are saying. The advice "do deadpan" loses the substrate. What gets installed is the surface without the substrate. The result is dead, not deadpan.

The suppression also produces the very self-consciousness it was supposed to solve. A person actively suppressing their emotional expression is, by definition, monitoring their own emotional expression. The monitoring is the self-consciousness. The suppression instruction installs the self-consciousness pattern as the performance method.

Joe's direct response when the person reports the deadpan advice:

"That's not authentic. You didn't ask me, how do I do the things that people have told me to do? You asked me, how do I be authentic?" -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?

## What Joe Says Instead

The work is in the opposite direction. Rather than suppressing emotion to perform, bring the emotion into the performance. The fear that arises before going on stage is information about what the person cares about — see [[fear-as-signal-of-care]]. The spontaneous emotions that arise during the performance are the authenticity the person says they are chasing. The cut-off of those emotions is the actual problem — see [[cutting-off-your-own-authenticity-in-real-time]].

Joe reframes the entire stage practice as the place where emotional fluidity is being developed, not the place where it is being avoided:

"It's actually kind of cool, that I went— I got on stage to face my biggest phobia... So there is some sort of desire to have emotional fluidity, to not feel stuck emotionally." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?

The stage is the place to be in connection with the emotions, not the place to control them. The performance gets better as the emotional presence increases, not the other way around.

The operational principle: any advice that requires suppression of emotion to achieve "good performance" should be discarded. The performance one is chasing arrives through emotional presence, not through its absence.

When the person reports the deadpan advice, Joe's first response is to crack up — "First of all, I'm about to fucking crack up. That's like the best setup for a joke ever." The humor is structural. The contradiction is so direct that pointing at it through laughter is more effective than analyzing it. The deadpan instruction, delivered earnestly, by a performer trying to figure out how to be authentic, is already a punchline. Joe lets it land as one.

## When This Anti-Pattern Is Operating

- The person has been getting advice from multiple coaches and none of it has worked.
- The advice the person has been getting includes language like "deadpan," "cool," "composed," "don't react," "hold it together."
- The person has chosen a profession that puts them in front of audiences and is asking how to be present in front of those audiences while simultaneously following advice to be absent.
- The person uses "performance mode" or "act" language to describe what happens on stage, and reports that performance mode is not them.
- The person is cutting off their own spontaneous expressions in the coaching session (see [[cutting-off-your-own-authenticity-in-real-time]]).
- The person reports a phobia of performing — and is performing anyway. The phobia points at the desire for emotional fluidity that the suppression advice is preventing.

## What to Do Instead

1. **Refuse to engage with the advice.** "I don't want to hear that. Okay, I don't care." The advice is the source of the problem. Discussing it would keep the person in the problem.

2. **Name the contradiction.** "You asked me how to be authentic. The advice you got was advice to suppress your authenticity. Those don't go together."

3. **Point at the performers the person actually admires.** Most of them are emotionally fluid on stage, not suppressed. The model has been wrong.

4. **Reframe the phobia as desire for emotional fluidity.** The stage was chosen because emotional fluidity is wanted. The phobia is the signal of the desire, not the obstacle to it.

5. **Cure the phobia by loving the fear.** See [[love-the-thing-you-fear-cures-the-phobia]].

## What This Anti-Pattern Is Not

- This is not a critique of skill-building in performance. Technique matters. The objection is to specific techniques that require emotional suppression.
- This is not a claim that all performance advice is bad. Most performance advice is fine. The deadpan family of advice in particular is what produces this trap.
- This is not the claim that performers should be emotionally chaotic. The instruction is presence, not chaos. A performer fully in their emotions is also in their craft; the two are not opposed.
