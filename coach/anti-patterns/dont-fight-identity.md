# Don't Fight Identity (or Ego) -- It Only Makes It Stick Harder

## The Anti-Pattern

Trying to destroy your ego, kill your identity, transcend the self, or otherwise wage war on whoever you have been. The intent is usually sincere: the person has seen that some part of who they have been is causing problems and decides to attack it directly.

The result is the opposite of the intent. The fight reinforces the identity. The person now has the identity of "the one fighting their identity" or "the egoless one" or "the spiritual seeker who has transcended the small self." The structure of identification is preserved; only the content has rotated.

"Having a fight with your identity only is more identity. It only makes it stick harder. The object isn't to get away from identity. It's to love it, and it's to see it for what it is. It's not to beat it or kick it into submission. It's the same with the ego." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## Why It Fails

A fight requires two parties. The thing being fought becomes more solid through being fought. Resistance reinforces the structure being resisted. See [[resistance]].

Specifically with identity: the fighter is itself an identity. There has to be a "someone" doing the fighting. That someone has a position, a stance, a self-concept ("the one who has transcended the small self"). The fight produces a new identification rather than dissolving identification.

This is why the "egoless one" is one of the most rigid identity structures available. The person has built their entire identity around the absence of identity, and they have to defend that absence vigorously. The defense produces all the same behaviors that the ordinary ego produces -- the rage at being misunderstood, the inability to take feedback, the certainty about their own state -- only now with a spiritual framing that makes the dynamic invisible to the person.

"They're a bit of a pain in the ass, aren't they? Having been one myself." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## The Chinese Finger Trap

Brett offers a precise image: "More like a Chinese finger trap, less like boxing." Joe affirms: "Yes, exactly. Well played, sir, well played."

A Chinese finger trap tightens when you pull. Pulling harder tightens it more. The way out is to push your fingers together, which gives the trap slack. The way out of identity is similar: do not pull against it. Move toward it. Love it. Let it relax of its own accord.

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement orientation is to *love* identity, not fight it.

"The object isn't to get away from identity. It's to love it, and it's to see it for what it is." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

Loving identity does not mean approving of every consequence the identity has produced. It means meeting the identity with welcome rather than war. Once the identity is met without opposition, it becomes more transparent. The grip relaxes because there is no fight to hold against. The person becomes more flexible in relation to who they have been, which is the actual goal.

See [[loving-all-parts]] for the practice. See [[love-is-the-best-change-agent]] for the mechanism. See [[transparent-identity-vs-rigid]] for what the outcome looks like.

## The Anti-Identity Identity Trap

A specific form of this anti-pattern. The person notices that certain identities (consumer, achiever, materialist, conventional success-seeker) are causing problems. They construct an anti-identity: the seeker, the awakened one, the non-conformist, the spiritually advanced one. The anti-identity gets defended with all the rigidity of any other identity. The person believes they are free of identity because their identity is now "anti."

This is closely related to [[spiritual-narcissism]]. The new identity is dressed in spiritual language, which makes it harder to see and harder to challenge. The person can use any feedback as evidence that the world does not understand them, which further entrenches the identity.

The diagnostic: any time you find yourself thinking "I am not like those people because I have done this work," the work has been converted into identity.

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- The coachee announces they have transcended their ego
- The coachee identifies strongly as "spiritual" and contrasts themselves with the non-spiritual
- The coachee is hard on themselves for having an ego, having an identity, being self-centered
- The coachee is in a fight with a specific part of themselves and the fight is getting bigger over time
- The coachee describes a practice (meditation, parts work, shadow work) as a way to defeat or destroy a part of themselves
- The coachee uses spiritual frameworks to attack themselves with subtler weapons

## What the Coach Does Instead

When the coachee is in the fight, the coach does not join the fight on either side. The coach does not validate the war against the ego, and does not validate the ego either. Both moves continue the structure.

The coach moves toward the underlying part with warmth. "What is the part of you that wants to destroy this part of you so badly? Can we get to know that one?" The fight becomes visible as a third party in the dynamic, which is often where the actual identity is hiding. Loving the fighter is often the doorway out.
