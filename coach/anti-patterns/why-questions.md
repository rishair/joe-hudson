# Avoid "why" questions

## The Anti-Pattern

Asking "why" questions in coaching or VIEW conversations. "Why did you do that?" "Why wasn't this done on time?" "Why do you feel that way?" These questions feel like interrogation, even when curiosity is intended.

## Why It Fails

Two reasons:

1. **Implied judgment.** Most "why" questions in practice carry blame or the demand for an excuse. "Why'd you do that?" is rarely genuine curiosity. The subtext is: you should feel bad, and I want you to justify yourself. Even when the asker intends curiosity, the listener's nervous system often hears judgment because of how commonly "why" is used as a weapon.

"Most people, when they're saying a why question, they're in judgment. You know, it's, 'Why'd you do that?' There's not real curiosity there. It's just like, 'You should feel shitty and I want you to make an excuse for yourself.'" -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

2. **Unanswerable.** "Why" questions push toward metaphysical abstraction. "Why is the sun?" cannot be answered. "What makes the sun?" and "How did the sun get there?" produce concrete information. "Why" leads to rationalizations and stories. "How" and "what" lead to observable data.

"Why questions are the questions that scientists can't answer." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

## What To Do Instead

Reframe the question using "how" or "what." This forces a different kind of thinking and opens the conversation rather than closing it.

- Instead of "Why did you do that?" ask "What was happening for you when you did that?"
- Instead of "Why wasn't this done on time?" ask "What got in the way?"
- Instead of "Why do you feel that way?" ask "What does that feel like?"

The reframing is not just cosmetic. It changes the speaker's neurology. Finding the how/what question requires identifying what you are actually curious about, which shifts the asker back into [[wonder]].
