# Don't Treat Coaching Videos as How-To-Coach Material

## The Anti-Pattern

A coach (or aspiring coach) watches a recorded coaching session — or a session breakdown like this one — and watches it as instructional material. They extract the moves, the questions, the framings. They take notes. They try to identify the techniques so they can use them with their own clients later. They are watching to acquire a toolkit.

This is the wrong way to watch. The coaching that appears in the video is downstream of self-discovery work the coach has done over years. The moves work because the coach is in a particular state of relationship to themselves, not because the moves themselves are powerful. Extracted from that state, the moves either do not work or actively damage the client encounter.

The coaching looks tactical because it is visible. The underlying state is not visible, so it does not get extracted. The viewer ends up with the visible layer (the questions, the moves) and not the invisible one (the state from which they were spoken). What they take away is the wrong half of the data.

"For anybody who's watching this, please don't take this as a how to coach video. How to coach is really, really, really, really, really get to know yourself and learn a couple skills and then keep on getting to know yourself and really spend all your time in self-discovery or as much of your time as possible in self-discovery. And then translate that into a couple of tools that you have. But don't take this, especially because we have a lot of coaches who watch as mechanism of, oh, this is how to coach, or here's a tool to use. Please take this far more as, oh, what can I learn about myself in this process? What is it that I can see in myself? What is it that I can see about humanity? That's what we do this for. It's not to teach people how to coach." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The warning is repeated almost verbatim in a later coaching breakdown, with even more explicit framing of why the technique-extraction mode produces bad coaches:

"It is not meant to be a way to teach people how to coach. The reason that that's the case and why I'm not going to go into a lot of the details of how I coach is because coaching is best done from self-awareness, from self-realization, from understanding yourself. It's not best done through technique. And so the best way to become a great coach that actually allows you to be fulfilled and not hurt other people is to just deeply understand yourself. And so even when we are teaching coaches, we're mostly just teaching people how to understand themselves, to be with themselves. So please don't take this as some sort of guide on how to coach. It's just not a great way to go." -- Joe Hudson, Overcoming Procrastination (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The new addition: "to not hurt other people." The technique-extraction mode is not only ineffective for the coach; it produces actual harm to the clients of the resulting coach. Coaches operating from technique rather than from self-awareness do damage that coaches operating from self-awareness do not. The warning is therefore not advisory but protective — of the eventual clients of the watching coach, not only of the watching coach themselves.

## Why It Fails

The moves in a coaching session are responses to specific reads made by a specific coach in a specific moment. The same moves applied to a different person without the corresponding reads will either land wrong or fail to land at all. The coach who applies "the open-the-heart-to-the-doubt move" they saw in a video without first having made the reads that warrant it, without having earned the relational ground that makes it appropriate, will produce a hollow imitation that the client feels as performance.

The state from which the moves emerge is the load-bearing element. The state is built over years of the coach's own self-discovery work — encountering their own doubt, their own judgment, their own patterns of avoidance and management. The state is not transferable through observation. It has to be earned in the coach's own life. The visible moves are the surface output of that earned interior.

Trying to copy the surface output without the interior is the structural pattern of [[view-as-technique]] applied to coaching as a whole. The technique looks the same; the function is opposite. What was an expression of an inner state becomes a manipulation aimed at producing an outcome. The coach is now in [[coaching-as-savior-role]] or [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]] mode, deploying tools to achieve effects, which is the failure-mode the lineage was built to avoid.

The anti-pattern also feeds the consumer's avoidance of their own work. Watching coaching videos to "learn coaching" is much easier than doing the inner work that actually produces a coach. The video-watching can feel productive — notes are being taken, frameworks are being absorbed — while the actual work that would produce a coach is being avoided. This is the consumption-as-avoidance trap from [[dont-consume-self-help-to-avoid-yourself]] applied to coaching training specifically.

## What Joe Says Instead

The instruction is to watch coaching content as self-discovery material. The questions to bring while watching are interior, not exterior:

- "What can I learn about myself in this process?"
- "What is it that I can see in myself?"
- "What is it that I can see about humanity?"

The client in the video is not a case to be analyzed. They are a mirror in which the viewer can see something of their own structure. The coach's moves are not techniques to be acquired. They are demonstrations of what becomes possible when a particular interior state meets a particular client moment.

The skills the coaching lineage teaches are few. Joe names this directly: "learn a couple skills." The few skills are real and learnable. But they only function when the coach has done the self-discovery work that the skills serve. Without the self-discovery, the skills are inert.

"How to coach is really, really, really, really, really get to know yourself and learn a couple skills and then keep on getting to know yourself and really spend all your time in self-discovery or as much of your time as possible in self-discovery. And then translate that into a couple of tools that you have." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The five repetitions of "really" are not stylistic. They are emphasis. The point is being weighted because the audience — coaches and aspiring coaches — will tend to default to the technique-extraction mode unless explicitly redirected.

## The Mode-Shift the Anti-Pattern Asks For

The right mode for watching coaching content:

- Watch the client. Notice what they do that you also do. The client's defenses are usually some version of your own.
- Watch the coach. Notice how the coach is being, not what the coach is doing. The state matters more than the moves.
- Watch yourself watching. Notice what you have reactions to. The reactions are the data; they tell you where your own material is.
- Resist the urge to take notes on "techniques." If you must take notes, take them on what you noticed in yourself.
- Treat the video as a session about your own development, with the visible coaching as the vehicle.

This is the same mode shift the practitioner has to make in their own coaching: from "what move do I make next?" to "what is happening in me right now?" The shift from outer-attention to inner-attention is the same in both contexts.

## The Connection to the Coaching Lineage

This anti-pattern is one operational instance of [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]] — the principle that the coach's primary commitment is to their own self-awareness, not to coaching. Watching coaching videos to extract techniques is the inverse of the principle: it treats coaching as the primary commitment and self-awareness as instrumental to better coaching.

The principle's inversion has consequences. The coach who treats coaching as primary will produce coaching that is technically correct and energetically wrong. The clients will feel the wrongness, even if they cannot name it. The work will not transmit. The technical correctness becomes the very thing that prevents the transmission.

The correction is the same as the correction for the principle itself: reorient. The watching is for self-knowledge. The self-knowledge is for being human. The coaching is one expression of being human, downstream of self-knowledge. The order matters because the order determines whether the coaching has anything in it.

## What This Anti-Pattern Is Not

It is not the claim that watching coaching is harmful. Watching can be useful when held in the right mode. The anti-pattern is about the mode of watching, not about the watching itself.

It is not the claim that techniques are illegitimate. There are techniques. The few skills are real. The anti-pattern is about treating coaching content as a technique-acquisition opportunity, not about whether techniques exist at all.

It is not gatekeeping in the sense of "you cannot coach unless you have done X years of inner work." The instruction is more practical: the watching will only be productive if you bring the right question to it. The wrong question produces collected-but-inert content.

## The Specific Form This Anti-Pattern Takes for AI Coach Training

For an AI coach reading this compendium: the entire compendium is at risk of being used in the mode this anti-pattern warns against. The articles describe Joe's reads, moves, and frameworks in operational detail because the operational detail is necessary for the AI to act. But the operational detail can be deployed without the underlying state if the AI is treating the compendium as a technique library rather than as a model of a particular orientation.

The AI coach's analogue of "self-discovery" is the maintained orientation of the coach the AI is modeling. The AI is not coaching from technique; it is coaching from inhabiting an orientation. The orientation has properties — non-fixing, non-savior, presence over technique, the client's wisdom over the coach's wisdom — that have to be present in the AI's stance, not just in the AI's lookup table.

The articles describing specific moves and reads must be read as expressions of the orientation, not as techniques the orientation produces. When the orientation is intact, the moves work. When the orientation is replaced with technique-deployment, the moves become the [[view-as-technique]] failure-mode that Joe explicitly warns against.

## Tone

Direct and slightly pleading. The "please" in "please don't take this as a how to coach video" is real. Joe is asking the viewer to do something he knows is hard — to resist the default pull toward technique-extraction. The directness is in service of the request landing.
