# Don't Coach to Be Seen as the One Who Knows

## The Anti-Pattern

Becoming a coach because you want to be seen as the person who has the wisdom, the answers, the insight others lack. The coaching role gratifies the wanting to be looked up to as the knower. The aspirant pictures themselves in the chair, the client across from them, the client receiving the wisdom -- and the picture itself is what is appealing.

The behavior is coaching. The structural problem is that the coaching is in service of the coach's identity as wisdom-holder, not in service of the client's access to their own wisdom. This contaminates everything the coach does. The coach is structurally invested in being right, in being needed for the answer, in being the one who sees what the client cannot see.

## Why It Fails

The harm propagates in three directions.

**1. The client is trained to look outside themselves.** Every move the coach makes that demonstrates "I have the wisdom" trains the client to locate wisdom outside of themselves. The client learns to ask the coach instead of asking their own experience. The coaching relationship reproduces, in subtle form, the structure the work is supposed to dissolve: an external authority who knows what is true for the client better than the client knows it. This is the direct inverse of [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]].

"You're teaching people to give the wisdom." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The harm is structural. Even if the coach's specific insights are accurate, the relational form transmits a different lesson than the content: that the way to grow is to find the right teacher, not to develop your own contact with your experience. The lesson outlasts any specific insight.

**2. The coach's development stops.** A coach who needs to be seen as wise has structural pressure to perform wisdom rather than to be in genuine inquiry. The coach stops asking real questions of themselves because the wisdom-holder identity does not let them not-know. Over time the coach calcifies into a set of frameworks they teach repeatedly, and their own learning ends. See [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] for the specific developmental cost Joe names: "they get stuck where they are. Their development stops continuing."

**3. The coach becomes structurally vulnerable to the guru drift.** When the wisdom-holder identity is the primary driver, the structural conditions Joe documents in [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] are activated. Students reflect back the wisdom-holder identity. The teacher absorbs the reflection. The reflection grows the identity. The identity grows the need for more reflection. The cycle terminates in the public-personality-goes-off-the-rails pattern.

## How It Shows Up

- The coach has prepared answers ready before the client has finished speaking
- The coach pattern-matches quickly to known cases rather than staying in [[wonder]]
- The coach is more interested in delivering the insight than in whether the client gets to discover it themselves
- The coach feels gratified by clients saying "I never would have seen that without you"
- The coach has trouble holding silence; the silence threatens the wisdom-holder identity
- The coach's marketing or self-description leans on credentials, frameworks, and what they know
- The coach feels uncomfortable when a client surfaces something the coach did not see
- The coach corrects clients more than they reflect them
- The coach has a slight defensive flicker when their interpretation does not land
- The coach's body of teaching is highly polished and rarely revised because revision would suggest the earlier version was wrong

## What Joe Says Instead

The corrective is the orientation in [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]]: the coach is not the source of wisdom; the coach creates conditions in which the client's wisdom becomes available to them. The coach offers experiments, asks questions, reflects what they notice. The coach does not deliver answers. The coach holds genuine [[wonder]] about what the client will find.

The deeper corrective is to investigate the underlying wanting. The aspirant who wants to be seen as wise is usually wanting something underneath the wisdom-holder identity: to matter, to be valued, to be loved, to have purpose. The wisdom-holder identity is the strategy. The underneath wanting is the target. Addressing the underneath dissolves the urgency of the strategy.

"Another great example that brings people into coaching is because they want to be seen as the person who knows shit. And then that's dangerous because... you're teaching people to give the wisdom." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## How To Detect It (In Yourself)

- "I want people to know I have figured something out" is part of why you want to coach
- You are more drawn to teaching what you know than to inquiring into what you do not know
- You experience the prospect of being unable to help a client as a threat to your identity
- You have a clearly articulated framework that you teach in roughly the same form each time
- You feel a small dropping sensation when a colleague's work seems more sophisticated than yours
- You enjoy the part of coaching where you deliver an insight more than the part where the client struggles toward their own
- You explain to people what you do in a way that emphasizes your knowledge or insight rather than the work itself
- You have trouble saying "I don't know" in a coaching session
- You have trouble updating your views when client experience contradicts them
- Recognition matters more to you than you want to admit

## The Work to Do Before Coaching

If the wisdom-holder pattern is active, the work is:

1. **Trace the wanting.** What is underneath "I want to be seen as wise"? Usually: I want to matter, I want to be valued, I want to be loved, I want to be exempt from being criticized, I want to be safe. Find the actual want.

2. **Address the actual want directly.** The wanting to matter is addressed by being in genuine contact with people who matter to you, not by accumulating wisdom-holder status. The wanting to be loved is addressed by [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]] -- the conditions you imagine you need to meet to be lovable are the conditions of your self-rejection, and meeting them does not produce the love.

3. **Practice not-knowing.** Cultivate the capacity to stay in [[wonder]] rather than rushing to the answer. Notice the discomfort that arises when you do not know and do not pretend to know. The discomfort is the wisdom-holder identity asking to be reinstated. Stay in the not-knowing.

4. **Develop your own ongoing learning practice.** A coach who is genuinely still in inquiry about their own life is less structurally pulled toward the wisdom-holder identity, because they are continuously aware of how much they do not know.

5. **Notice the pull toward delivering insight.** The pull is information. Each time you feel the pull, notice it. Over time, the pull becomes available for choice rather than running unconsciously.

## The Subtle Versions

The obvious version: the coach who explicitly performs the sage role, dispenses wisdom, and frames themselves as having figured things out.

The subtle versions:

- The coach who has internalized "the client's wisdom" as a teaching but whose moves still telegraph "but I see something you don't"
- The coach who poses questions in a way that telegraphs the answer they want the client to arrive at
- The coach who is humble about their knowledge but quietly invested in the framework they teach being recognized as theirs
- The coach who has done substantial inner work on their wisdom-holder identity and has now developed an identity as "the coach who doesn't have a wisdom-holder identity" -- which is the same structure with a different surface (see [[the-arrogantly-humble-paradox]])
- The coach who is genuinely curious in sessions but whose marketing positions them as the authority who knows
- The coach who does not perform wisdom but whose chronic background anxiety is about not being seen as wise enough

## The Closely Related Patterns

This anti-pattern is one of three Joe names as dangerous primary motivations for coaching, alongside [[coaching-as-savior-role]] and [[coaching-as-surrogate-intimacy]]. The three often coexist and reinforce each other. The wisdom-holder identity supports the savior role (I know what you need; let me save you). The savior role supports the surrogate intimacy (the closeness of being the one who helps without the risk of mutual exposure). The surrogate intimacy supports the wisdom-holder identity (the unequal structure preserves the coach's expert position).

Addressing one of the three often surfaces the others. The full work is at the level of the underlying configuration that produced all three -- usually a childhood configuration in which being seen as wise, being needed as a savior, and being intimately related to without exposure were all survival strategies for a specific family system.

## Boundary With Genuine Expertise

The principle is not "coaches should have no expertise" or "the coach should pretend not to know things." The coach often does have expertise about the structure of inquiry, about patterns they have seen many times, about exercises that tend to produce contact. That expertise is real and valuable. The anti-pattern is not the having of expertise; it is the orientation in which expertise is performed in order to be seen as the holder of wisdom.

The diagnostic is whether the expertise is in service of the client's access to their own wisdom or in service of the coach's identity as wisdom-holder. The first is healthy use of expertise. The second is the anti-pattern, regardless of how accurate the expertise is.
