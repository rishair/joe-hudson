# Don't Comfort the Mourner From Your Own Discomfort (The Funeral Pattern)

## The Anti-Pattern

The standard failure mode of how communities show up for grief. Multiple specific moves, all driven by the comforter's inability to sit with what the mourner is feeling.

- Saying "are you okay?" or "it'll be okay" or any variant designed to make the mourner stop expressing what they are feeling.
- Bringing advice or solutions to the bereaved.
- Performing your own sadness in front of the mourner as a way to discharge it, requiring them to witness and receive your grief on top of carrying their own.
- Treating the mourner as if their job is to make you (and the other attendees) feel better about the loss.
- Loading obligation onto the mourner: thank-you notes, hosting, managing the social dynamics of the funeral, taking care of guests emotionally.
- Crowding the mourner with care so that they cannot actually rest in their own grief.

The pattern hides as kindness. The intent is rarely conscious cruelty. The structure is the comforter offloading their own discomfort onto the person they are nominally there to support.

"So many people who have had a loss, the funeral and that whole experience just kind of sucks. People are trying to make them feel better, which is not helpful, and/or wanting to experience their pain as a way to be able to purge their own sadness. And there's this feeling of obligation of the person who is mourning, like they're supposed to take care of people emotionally at least. And there's just all this stuff that happens." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

## Why It Fails

Two distinct failure modes operate at once.

First, the mourner does not get to grieve. The mourner's job at a functional ritual is to feel what is happening. The comforter who interrupts that — by trying to fix the feeling, by extracting reassurance, by adding their own emotional needs — pulls the mourner out of their own experience and into management of someone else's.

Second, the comforter does not get to grieve either. The whole maneuver was driven by the comforter's avoidance of their own sadness. By converting it into helping behavior, the comforter never lets it land. So the comforter leaves the funeral with the same unfelt sadness they arrived with, plus the additional satisfaction of having "helped." Nothing has moved for anyone.

The structural problem: this is [[caring-vs-caretaking]] in its most culturally sanctioned form. The whole society agrees that funerals are for caretaking the bereaved. Everyone is performing the role. The dysfunction is invisible because it is the norm.

The deeper consequence over generations: when this is the only model of community-in-grief that people have seen, they conclude that community in grief is unhelpful — and they isolate next time. The very pattern that was supposed to provide support produces the data that supports avoiding community when support is needed most. See [[community]]'s "Why It's Rare" section.

## What's Underneath It

The comforter is avoiding their own feelings. The most common ones:

- Their own sadness about the loss.
- Their own grief about losses they have not yet metabolized, which this grief is touching.
- Helplessness — there is nothing they can do, and that is intolerable, so they manufacture something to do.
- Fear of their own mortality or the mortality of their loved ones, which the funeral makes present.

None of these are wrong to have. The anti-pattern is not in having the feelings. It is in using "helping the mourner" as the mechanism for not having to feel them.

## What Joe Says Instead

The functional version of the same ritual: everyone present feels their own grief. The mourner is welcomed but not made responsible for managing anyone. The community holds space for each person to feel what is actually arising in them, including grief for the specific loss, and grief for whatever else that loss is touching.

The pre-frame Joe used with the mother of the deceased child before the community grief ceremony:

"Fuck all that. There's no obligation here, and I'm going to be really clear upfront, and our community generally knows this anyways, that it's not about trying to make you feel better, and it's not about having you feel the emotions for them, that it's about everybody feeling their own grief." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

Three explicit norms made the container work:

1. **No obligation on the mourner.** They are not hosting. They are not taking care of anyone's feelings. They are not expected to receive condolences gracefully.
2. **No fixing.** No "are you okay?" No "it'll be okay." No advice. No reassurance.
3. **Each person feels their own grief.** This grief is touching everyone's losses. The community welcomes whatever arises in each person.

When this is the structure, the mourner can finally rest in the grief that is theirs. And paradoxically, the community is strengthened. Because everyone is metabolizing their own version of what is being touched, the grief moves through everyone, not just through the bereaved.

"It was about them primarily, but it was also about everybody else's healing that they got to go through and how their story and what they went through healed the whole community." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

See [[your-pain-when-witnessed-heals-the-whole-community]] for the principle this expresses, and [[host-a-grief-ceremony-as-community]] for the structure of the container.

## The Diagnostic For a Supporter

Before doing anything at a funeral, a sick-bed visit, or any encounter with someone in grief, the supporter can run a quick check:

- Whose emotion am I about to manage? Theirs or mine?
- Am I doing this because they need it, or because their grief is uncomfortable for me?
- If I say nothing and just sit here and let them feel whatever they are feeling, what happens to me?
- What am I avoiding by acting?

If the answer to the last two reveals that the supporter is acting to relieve their own discomfort, the move is to feel that discomfort directly — usually their own grief, their own helplessness, their own fear — rather than to discharge it as comfort.

"I feel you. I am with you" delivered from a place of having felt what arose in oneself is structurally different from "Are you okay?" delivered from a place of discomfort with the mourner's tears. The words sound similar. The transmission is opposite.

## Joe's Words

"There's this feeling of obligation of the person who is mourning, and like they're supposed to take care of people emotionally at least." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

"When she saw other people grieving and feeling the pain, it immediately let her know she was in this safe place instead of like, 'Oh, are you okay?' It was like, 'I feel you. I am with you.'" -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

"Oftentimes when our community has problems, it's people coming in telling them how they should be because what's happening is they don't want to feel something.
