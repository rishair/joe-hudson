# Don't Run the 4 A's From a Should (The Boot-Camp Failure Mode)

## The Anti-Pattern

The [[four-as-of-behavior-change|4 A's]] are designed to be deployed immediately after a recognition — the moment you actually see, in your body, that you've been doing something you don't want to do anymore. Applied without that recognition, with only the thought "I should be more X" or "I shouldn't be so Y," the framework converts into a boot camp: forced action, pushed-down feelings, performance of identity. It produces messiness toward yourself and others rather than real change.

The diagnostic difference is whether the engine is *I see it* or *I should*. Same procedural steps. Completely different outcomes.

## Why It Fails

The framework's effectiveness depends on the feelings being felt at each step. When the engine is a should, the feelings get pushed down rather than felt. The framework becomes a procedural compliance exercise. The pattern doesn't move because the substrate work isn't happening.

"It does not work as well if you are forcing yourself through it. So it does not work as well if you do the following: I don't like the fact that I do X, Y, and Z, I need it to change, I'm gonna take these actions. Where it works really well is, oh, I see, that was completely unnecessary. That's not how I want to be. Then it works really well." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The should-driven version is the way most people are taught to change behavior: identify what's wrong with you, beat yourself up about it, enforce action through accountability buddies or coaches. The 4 A's *look* like that approach but operate by the opposite mechanism. Running them as if they were that approach disables what makes them work.

"If you're using this as a form of boot camp, I'm going to force myself to do it, then it doesn't work because what you're doing is you're pushing down all your emotions. You're not feeling everything. You're just telling yourself the way that you should be." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The substrate logic: each A is supposed to surface specific feelings (fear of reputation, shame, guilt about needing help, fear of rejection). When those feelings are pushed down rather than felt, the pattern stays in place. The procedural form completes but the energetic restructuring doesn't happen.

## The Worked Examples

**The Jim Carrey "Liar Liar" example.** Deciding to be "100% honest" because you've adopted that as a morality, without having seen a pattern in yourself, without having felt how dishonesty has been operating in your life. Result: saying mean and abusive things to people, calling it honesty, neither in service of the change nor in service of the relationships.

"An example might be deciding that I want to be 100% honest, like Jim Carrey in 'Liar Liar.' That's just maybe a morality that I've adopted, but I haven't seen a pattern. I haven't seen the way that it's affecting my life deeply. I haven't felt those emotions. So I'm just like, okay, I'm gonna say exactly what's on my mind. And it turns out that I just say a bunch of really mean and abusive shit to people that isn't really the way I wanna be with them either. But I'm pushing myself into a certain way of being." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**The commitment example.** "I should be more committed. I shouldn't be commitment-phobic." That's not a recognition — it's a should layered on top of something the person hasn't actually seen yet. Running the 4 A's on top of that should produces compliance theater, not change.

"The other way I would think about this is like commitment. There's a lot of folks who are like, I should be more committed. I shouldn't be as commitment-phobic. And so I should commit to stuff. But that's not a realization, that's a should. There's not, oh, oh wow, I'm not committing to stuff because I'm scared of getting rejected. Oh, and I'm scared of getting rejected because I've been rejecting myself. That's the moment to do it." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The contrast: the moment of "I'm scared of getting rejected because I've been rejecting myself" is a recognition. It has the *of course* quality, the felt sense of the truth landing. The 4 A's run on top of that work. They do not produce that work.

## What Joe Says Instead

Wait for the recognition. If it isn't there yet, the work is upstream: feeling, inquiry, looking at the pattern with [[recognition-with-open-heart-moves-pattern-recognition-with-shame-entrenches-it|an open heart]] until the *of course* arrives. See [[recognition-feeling-balance-then-call-emerges]] for the order of operations.

"It works really well when you have a recognition, when you have the realization, when you get off a coaching call with me and you say, oh, whoa, I've been doing that thing. I don't need to do that thing anymore. That's the best time to implement this. Just bam, I see it. I'm immediately going to take the 4 A's." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The pre-deployment test: can you locate the moment you saw it? Can you feel the *of course*? Is the change desired from inside (because you actually saw it wasn't how you wanted to be), or is it borrowed from outside (because someone told you, or because you think you're supposed to)? Only the inside version supports the framework.

If the answer is should: don't run the framework yet. Do the work that produces the recognition. Then the 4 A's are available.

## The Diagnostic Question

Before deploying the 4 A's: ask yourself or the client, "did you have a recognition, or did you decide you should?"

The honest answer to this question protects the framework from misuse. The should-answer needs different work first (often [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] or the kinds of inquiry that produce the *of course* moment). The recognition-answer is the green light for the framework.

The recognition has a felt signature: the body softens slightly, the pattern looks slightly absurd (in a non-shaming way), the next behavior feels like the obvious next move rather than the morally-required one.

The should has a different signature: the body braces, the pattern looks bad (and the self with it), the next behavior feels like an obligation rather than a release.

## What This Does To The Client

When the 4 A's are forced from a should, the client experiences:

- Mess in the announcement (it lands as performance or as identity-marketing).
- Shame in the apology (because the engine was self-rejection to begin with).
- Defensiveness when reminders come (because the asking didn't release any shame).
- Forced contrary actions that overshoot, undershoot, or land on the wrong people.

The cumulative effect is often worse than not running the framework at all. The client may conclude "the framework doesn't work" when really the framework wasn't being deployed in its operating range.

## When To Suspect This Is Happening

- The client uses the language of obligation: "I have to," "I'm supposed to," "I should be."
- The framing of the change is moral rather than relational: "people shouldn't X," "good people Y."
- The recognition can't be traced to a specific moment of seeing.
- The client is deploying the framework as a self-improvement project rather than in response to a fresh insight.
- The energy is bracing rather than softening when the change is named.
- The client is borrowing the recognition from someone else (a teacher, a partner, a book) rather than having had it themselves.
