# Don't Figure Out When You Can Feel It, Just Feel It When You Can

## The Anti-Pattern

After the person has begun to access a feeling — or after they have noticed the bifurcation between contexts in which they can feel and contexts in which they cannot — the mind reaches for the conditions question. "How do I feel it when I'm alone vs with others?" "Why can I feel here but not there?" "What are the conditions under which I can do this?" The question is the diagnostic. The mind has converted the feeling-work into a problem-to-solve, and the problem-to-solve is a flight from the feeling.

The anti-pattern: trying to figure out the structural rules of when the feeling is accessible, instead of just feeling it when it is accessible.

The mind that asks the conditions question is the same mind that has been pre-editing the feeling for years. The question feels productive ("if I understood the conditions, I could engineer more of them") but is operationally a way to not be in the feeling right now. The intellectual frame substitutes for the somatic experience.

## Why It Fails

The feeling-work is not a structural problem. It is a present-moment availability. The feeling is here or it is not. When it is here, the work is to feel it. When it is not here, the work is whatever else is here (the editing, the resistance, the avoidance) — felt as itself, not analyzed.

The conditions question fails on three levels:

**1. It removes the person from the present feeling.** The moment the person enters "how do I do this elsewhere?" they have left "what is this here?" The feeling that was accessible is no longer being met. The opportunity passes.

**2. The answer is structurally unavailable in the form the question is asking for.** There is no map of contexts that will make the feeling reliably available. The feeling is available when the editing stops; the editing stops in conditions that vary. Trying to specify the conditions in advance is asking for a recipe for spontaneity.

**3. The figuring-out is itself a form of editing.** The mind that wants to know the rules is the mind that wants to control the outcome. The control is the opposite of the surrender that lets the feeling move. The question installs more of what the work is dismantling.

The conditions question is therefore not just unhelpful — it is actively counter-productive. It reinforces the very pattern the work is meant to interrupt.

## What Joe Says Instead

"Those are all questions that are just taking you away from the feeling." -- Joe Hudson, I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me

"You don't ever need to figure those things out. Just feel it when you can feel it. Enjoy it." -- Joe Hudson, I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me

The instruction is the refusal of the framing the question is operating inside. Joe is not answering the conditions question; he is naming that the question is a distraction. The pivot is to the present: feel it now, while it is here. The rest will happen on its own.

The "enjoy it" is operationally significant. The instruction is not just to feel the feeling but to enjoy the feeling when it is present. The enjoyment is the body-side of the gratitude that operationalizes the [[be-grateful-for-moments-of-presence-to-accelerate-the-work]] practice. The conditions question and the enjoyment are mutually exclusive — the person who is figuring out the conditions cannot be enjoying the present feeling. The instruction redirects from the figuring to the enjoying.

## The Specific Form This Took in the Source Session

The client had been having an emotional conversation with Joe and was in a moment of presence. The client then asked: "There's something — it's kind of the same pattern, but it's like, I'm gonna say things. Maybe the question is, how do I feel it with — in the presence of other people? Maybe the question is, how do I feel it when I'm alone?"

The mind had reached for the conditions question. The question is plausible on the surface (it is the structural question implied by the bifurcation the client had been working on). But the client was already feeling it — in the presence of other people, on a Zoom call. The question was the move away from the feeling that was currently available.

Joe's response: "Um, that's all just taking you away from the feeling. Those are all questions that are just— You don't ever need to figure those things out. Just feel it when you can feel it. Enjoy it."

The exchange is brief. The refusal is direct. The conditions question is not answered; it is named as a distraction. The client accepts the refusal: "Okay, last question." The work continues without the diversion.

## What Joe Does Not Mean

The instruction does not mean the conditions question is forever unanswerable, or that no learning ever happens about the structure of when feeling is accessible. Over time, with experience, the person does develop a sense of what makes feeling more or less available. That development happens through repetition of the feeling work, not through figuring out in advance.

The instruction is contextual. In the moment when the feeling is present, the question is a distraction. In a later moment of reflection — possibly with a coach, possibly in journaling — the person can notice patterns about what was different about the contexts. The reflection is useful when it follows the experience. The question is unhelpful when it replaces the experience.

The diagnostic for which mode is operating: is the feeling present right now? If yes, the conditions question is a distraction; redirect to feeling it. If no, and the person is in a thinking/processing mode rather than trying to access the feeling, the structural question can be useful.

## What Joe Does With the Question

If the client asks the conditions question while the feeling is present:

1. Name the question as a distraction. "That's just taking you away from the feeling."
2. Refuse to engage the question on its terms. Do not explain the conditions. Do not validate the question by treating it as a real question.
3. Redirect to the present feeling. "Just feel it when you can feel it."
4. Optionally add the enjoyment instruction. "Enjoy it."

If the client persists with the question or returns to it:

1. Name the persistence as the same move. "You're doing it again."
2. Stay with the refusal. The work is the refusal, repeated as many times as needed.

The repetition is part of the practice. The mind that has been editing for years does not stop asking the conditions question after one refusal. Each refusal is an opportunity to redirect to the present. Over time the pattern softens.

## Why This Anti-Pattern Is Specific to Self-Editing Work

The conditions question shows up in many contexts (it is a general intellectualization move). It is especially counter-productive in self-editing work because the self-editor IS the questioning-and-analyzing function. Asking the editor to figure out when the editor should be off is asking the editor to do the work of dismantling itself. The editor will always conclude that more editing is needed, because more editing is what the editor does.

The way past the editor is not through more editor-work. It is through repeated experiences of un-edited presence, met with gratitude, allowed to move through. The conditions question is the editor's attempt to maintain control over the process. The refusal of the question is the practice of not giving the editor that control.

See [[grieving-the-self-editor]] for the larger frame — the editor's death is what the work is producing, and the conditions question is one of the editor's strategies for staying alive.
