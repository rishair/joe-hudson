# Don't Half-Ass the Experiment

## The Anti-Pattern

A person or team agrees to try a practice as a time-bounded experiment, then participates without genuine engagement. They show up to the gratitude rounds and list things they do not actually feel grateful for. They commit to the two-week practice and do it half-heartedly. They run the experiment without the felt-quality or full participation that the practice requires.

The result: the experiment produces no usable data. The practice may have actually worked, but the team will conclude it did not because the inputs were not real. The conclusion looks like evidence ("we tried it and it did not work") but is actually a failure of method.

"You're either running the experiment or you're not running the experiment. But don't half-ass it, because it's like, if the experiment isn't clean, you're never gonna learn anything." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

## Why It Fails

An experiment requires clean inputs to produce interpretable outputs. The half-assed experiment produces uninterpretable outputs. The team then has to choose between:

- Concluding the practice does not work (false negative -- the practice was never actually run)
- Concluding the practice does work (also unsupported -- there is no clean evidence)
- Concluding nothing and discarding the time invested

All three outcomes are worse than the outcome of either committing fully or declining the experiment up front.

The half-assed experiment is worse than no experiment, because it produces the social effect of having tried (the practice is now "discredited" in the team's lore) without the actual learning that running it would have produced.

## The Gratitude-Specific Form

The half-assed version of [[gratitude-rounds-before-meetings]] is the team member who participates verbally without participating somatically. They list "I'm grateful that I get a paycheck" because the format requires them to say three things. The body is not actually dropping into gratitude. The required substance is missing.

Joe names the contrast precisely:

"That person who you're speaking to would probably most likely also be the person in the room that's like, 'I'm really grateful that I get a paycheck.' Instead of, oh, I'm really grateful that this team is working together in such a way that allows us all to be paid and allows me to take care of my children and really feel the gratitude." -- Joe Hudson, How Does Gratitude Create Better Teams?

The form is correct in both versions. The substance is present in only the second. The first version produces a meeting that looks like the practice but does not function as the practice.

## What Joe Says Instead

The leader running the experiment has to be willing to call out the half-assing in the moment. This is what protects the experiment from being a false negative.

"I would also be quick to call that out. Like, hey, you're either running the experiment or you're not running the experiment. But don't half-ass it." -- Joe Hudson

The call-out is not punitive. The framing is: we agreed to run an experiment. Running an experiment requires clean inputs. If you cannot give clean inputs, withdraw from the experiment. You do not get to corrupt the data set and then claim the practice did not work.

This places the responsibility cleanly. The skeptic who agreed to the experiment is now bound to either participate fully or admit they are not participating. There is no third option of "showing up but not doing it."

## The Deeper Mechanism

The half-ass pattern is often a way to preserve the conclusion the person already had. They were sure gratitude was nonsense. They agreed to the experiment because refusing would have been socially expensive. They want the experiment to fail to validate their original position. Half-assing is the mechanism by which they engineer the desired failure.

This is not always conscious. The person may genuinely think they are participating. The leader's call-out is what surfaces the gap between the participation they think they are giving and the participation the experiment actually requires.

## The Broader Principle

This anti-pattern generalizes beyond gratitude. Any time-bounded experiment can be half-assed, and the half-assing produces the same false-negative effect. The principle is the same in personal practice:

- Two weeks of meditation done for five minutes a day with a wandering mind is not a clean experiment in meditation
- One month of a new diet eaten 60% of the time is not a clean experiment in the diet
- Three sessions of trying a new conversational pattern with your partner with one foot still in the old pattern is not a clean experiment in the new pattern

The general form: a practice run at less than the intensity it requires produces no information about whether the practice works.

See [[trying-vs-doing]] for the related distinction.

## When This Anti-Pattern Shows Up

- A team agrees to a new practice and goes through the motions
- A skeptic in a coaching session agrees to try an assignment and does it without engagement
- A leader institutes a culture change and the team does not call out the leader's own half-assing of it
- An individual takes on a practice and reports back that it did not work, with detail that suggests they never fully ran it

## The Diagnostic Question

If you suspect you (or someone else) is half-assing an experiment, the question is: "If I had to bet money on whether this experiment will work, what would I bet?" If the answer is "I'd bet it won't" and there is no actual evidence yet because the experiment is in progress, then the prediction is leaking into the execution. The expectation of failure is producing the conditions for failure.

This is one of the few places where the leader's gentle directness is appropriate. The half-ass cannot be left ambiguous, because the cost of leaving it ambiguous is the discrediting of a practice that was never really tried.
