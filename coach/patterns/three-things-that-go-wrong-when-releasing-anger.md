# Three Things That Go Wrong When Releasing Anger

## The Pattern

A person decides to release anger. They start moving it. Then it goes wrong in one of three predictable ways. Each failure mode tracks the form of repression the person habitually uses. Recognizing the failure as it happens (or in advance) makes it workable.

"Depending on kind of the way that it's kinked, [...] you're gonna go into collapse. The other thing that happens [...] is they become unregulated. [...] And then the other thing that happens is people recreate the shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

This is the operational map for what an AI coach should watch for when guiding someone through anger release.

## Failure 1: Collapse

The anger starts moving. Halfway through, the person folds. Body bends, energy drops, the release stops mid-motion.

"You'll see them, like they collapse into it." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

Specifically, the back curves, the body shrinks, the person goes into a posture of submission or shame. The anger does not complete. The clarity does not arrive.

This is not the same as choosing to stop. The body is being overridden by an older pattern that says "no, you cannot be angry." The collapse is the felt re-installation of the original injunction.

### Who Collapses

This is most common in people whose habitual form is Form 1 (anger inward) or Form 2 (passive aggression). The system has spent years sending the energy into the self or sideways. When the energy tries to move out, the deeper conditioning catches it and folds it back.

Particularly common in people from Origin 2a or Origin 3 (anger removed love, or anger was punished). The body's first response when anger leaves the container is to expect the punishment that originally followed.

### The Move

The instruction is to use will, the same way one uses will in a workout.

"You have the use of will and just be like, yeah, I'm not doing that, just like you would in a workout." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

Underneath the will is a specific micro-practice: find a phrase that pushes back against the collapse. Repeat it.

"Just repeat one sentence like, no, no, I won't collapse, I won't collapse, or you can't stop me. Just whatever that sentence is that triggers the anger response. [...] It's to feel the oppression of the collapse instead of the oppression of the anger, to feel the oppression of the collapse and respond to it and be like, 'No, I'm not going there. No, I will be empowered. You will not stop me. You will not stop me. You will not stop me from being angry.' That is a really good thing. It often has huge releases for people if they can just find that phrase in response to that very early childhood, 'No, you can't be angry.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The phrase that releases varies. Common ones: "I exist." "I will not collapse." "You will not stop me." See [[i-exist-affirmation-against-collapse]].

The reframe inside this: the felt oppression is not coming from the current situation. It is coming from the childhood injunction. Pushing back against the childhood injunction (with the phrase) lets the anger move through the gate the injunction was holding closed.

## Failure 2: Dysregulation

The anger releases. The person believes the story it is telling. They keep going. They get lost in it. They leave their body, lose track of time, do or say things they did not intend.

"They become unregulated. Then they actually lose control. So when people in our society say that person was angry, they're usually talking about a person who's become dysregulated. They're like out of their body, they're out of their experience, they're saying things that they don't wanna say." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

Dysregulation is what most people mean when they fear anger. The vision of "losing it" that prevents release is the vision of this failure.

### The Distinction From Healthy Loss-of-Self

Releasing anger does include some loss of self-coherence. That can be freeing. It is not the same as dysregulation.

"There are times where if you lose yourself in the release of anger, there's a huge amount of freedom there. But dysregulated is a little bit different. And so what I mean by that is like, where you've left your body, where you don't feel like you're in your body anymore. Maybe for some people, they are like looking at themselves from above, and then some people, they're not aware of the time and space. It's like they've completely lost themselves." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

See [[dysregulated-vs-released-into-freedom]] for the specific distinction.

The diagnostic: dysregulation lacks clarity at the end. Healthy release produces [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]]. If the anger has finished and there is no clarity, it was dysregulated, not released. The person was re-traumatizing themselves rather than working through.

### Who Dysregulates

Most common in people whose habitual form is Form 3 (anger at people) and from Origin 2b (anger got attention). The system has learned that anger is the way to engage. When the anger arrives, it carries decades of accumulated charge plus the relational reflex to dump it at someone.

### The Move

The instruction is to take the observer position. Treat the anger as a performance you are watching, not as a fact about reality.

"It's not believing the story. It's seeing yourself as an actor. It's like being in the observer position is a far better way to do it." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

"The exercise I give people is, be an actor playing the role of you getting angry so that they can see that it's like, oh, I'm just here to give a good performance, but I, on some level, I don't believe it." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

See [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]].

The inversion is important: this move is the *opposite* of what the collapser needs. The collapser needs to drop into the story to access the anger at all. The dysregulator needs to hold awareness outside the story so the anger does not capture them. See [[observer-vs-immersion-by-anger-type]].

## Failure 3: Recreating the Shame

The anger releases. The release damages something (a wall, an object, a relationship, the self). The damage produces shame. The shame confirms the original belief: "see, anger is bad, I should not have done that."

"They'll get angry in such a way that destroys something. They'll get angry in such a way, they'll get angry at somebody. They'll get angry in a way that hurts themselves. And so they'll do that so that they can then prove to themselves anger isn't good." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

This is the cycle that keeps the kink in place. Each release ends with the shame, which trains the system to repress harder next time, which builds more charge, which makes the next release more likely to do damage.

### Who Recreates Shame

Most common in people who have heavy shame loaded onto anger already. This often overlaps with Origin 3 (punished for anger) and sometimes with Origin 1 (witnessed destructive anger). The shame is the inherited belief that anger itself is wrong.

### The Move

The instruction is almost surprisingly simple: just be aware.

"All you need is awareness. It's just like, don't hurt yourself, don't hurt others, don't do it to anybody, don't break shit. Like, it's just that simple." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

"If you make it a should, then it's gonna take a lot longer." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The trap to avoid: making "don't recreate shame" into a should. "I should not damage anything. I should not hurt anyone. I am bad if I do." This is the shame mechanism getting reinstalled in the practice itself. The practice then runs on shame and produces more shame.

The cleaner version: notice when the cycle begins to repeat. Recognize it as the cycle. "Oh, that's me recreating the shame around it." That is enough. The recognition is the change. Awareness is the change agent. See [[awareness-as-change-agent]].

This is one of the cases where the lighter touch is the harder discipline. The mind wants to grab the failure with a moral correction. The practice is to let the moral correction not happen and let the seeing happen instead.

## Mixed Cases

The three failure modes are not mutually exclusive. A person can collapse first, then on the next attempt dysregulate, then on the next recreate shame. Or run all three in sequence in one session. The mapping by form is a starting orientation, not a fixed assignment.

"Some people, it might just be only one thing that they do. [...] For other people, it might be the exact reverse progression." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

What matters is the recognition of the failure as it happens. Each failure type has its own move. Naming the failure type makes the move available.

## Tone

Joe presents these as mechanical, not pathological. The failures are expected. They are not signs the person should not be doing the work. They are the form the early phase of the work takes. Approaching them with curiosity rather than self-criticism is itself part of preventing Failure 3.
