# The Stories That Block Want-Naming

## The Pattern

When a person resists stating what they want, the resistance is almost always carried by one of a small set of mental stories. The stories operate underneath the resistance and rarely get named directly. Identifying which story is operating is the first move toward dissolving it.

"A lot of people prevent themselves from saying their wants, and their stories are: people will let me down, people will get angry at me, or I'm gonna look selfish. Those seem to be the three stories that people tell themselves. Maybe also, I'm bad. I'm bad for having a want. Which is a version of I'm selfish." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The four stories are not separate. They are four faces of the same underlying configuration: the person was trained early to expect that wanting would produce some kind of negative consequence, and the training installed the stories as a self-protective layer over the wanting.

## The Four Stories

### Story 1: "I'll Be Let Down"

The person predicts that stating the want will produce disappointment. They will say what they want, the other person will not deliver, and the not-delivering will feel worse than the not-asking would have felt. The pre-emptive non-asking is positioned as protecting against the disappointment.

The fallacy: the not-asking is already producing the disappointment, just in chronic background form. The wanting is going unmet whether stated or unstated. The asking would add only the possibility of getting met. The avoidance of the disappointment is not actually avoiding the disappointment; it is making the disappointment permanent.

### Story 2: "They'll Get Angry"

The person predicts that stating the want will produce anger in the other person. They have either experienced this in the past (a parent or partner who reacted with anger to expressed wants) or projected it from their own internal critic. The non-asking is positioned as keeping the peace.

The fallacy: most people will not get angry. Some will. The people who get angry are usually the ones who have not owned their own wants and are operating from the same suppression pattern. Joe notes:

"Some people will think you're selfish, and some people will get angry at you, but not the majority. And typically over time, those people change their tune or they leave." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The pattern most people fear is much rarer than they think. And when it does happen, it is data about the other person's relationship with their own wants, not a verdict on whether you should have stated yours.

### Story 3: "I'll Look Selfish"

The person predicts that stating the want will produce a social judgment of selfishness. They have an internalized voice (often a parent's) that scored want-stating as selfish behavior. The non-asking is positioned as maintaining social standing.

The fallacy: this story confuses owning a want with imposing it. Stating "I want X" and then negotiating freely with whatever the other person says is not selfish. See [[wants-vs-partiality]]. Selfishness in the meaningful sense is the imposition of one's wants on others without regard for them. Stating a clean want and giving the other person room to respond is the opposite of selfish — it is the form of want-expression that gives the other person their full agency.

### Story 4: "I'm Bad For Wanting"

The deepest of the four. The person does not predict a consequence in the world; they predict a verdict on themselves. The wanting itself is read as evidence of being a bad person. The not-naming is positioned as keeping themselves acceptable.

This is the story most directly produced by [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]] — the developmental imprint where a child's wanting produced love-withdrawal and the child concluded that wanting itself was the problem. The "I'm bad for wanting" story is the residue of that wiring in the adult.

The fallacy: wanting is not evidence of being bad. Wanting is evidence of being alive (see [[wanting-is-aliveness]]). The story is an inherited judgment that does not match the structural reality of being human. Other people are not making this judgment in the moment; the person making the judgment is the inherited inner critic.

## Why Naming the Story Is Often Sufficient

The stories operate by being unnamed. When a person can identify which story is running ("oh, I'm in the 'I'll look selfish' story right now"), the story usually loses significant grip. The story is no longer the implicit governing assumption; it is one of several possible readings of the situation, and the person can choose whether to take it as final.

This is why the diagnostic move of "which story is operating?" is so high-leverage. The story does not need to be fought, refuted, or argued with. It needs to be named. Once named, it joins the conversation as a story rather than as the unspoken truth.

## The Combination Form

Most people do not run just one story. They run a combination, sometimes oscillating through all four in the same situation. The "I'll be let down" story produces the initial hesitation; the "they'll get angry" story produces the second-guessing; the "I'll look selfish" story produces the social anxiety; the "I'm bad for wanting" story produces the underlying shame. The four together can produce paralysis even though each individual story is small.

The work is to surface each story separately and ask whether it is true now, in this specific situation. The honest answer is usually "I don't actually know" or "it depends on the person." Both answers loosen the grip the story had as an unquestioned governing assumption.

## What This Reveals About the Long-Term Pattern

The four stories are conservative. They assume the worst-case prediction and then act to avoid it. The wanted thing is sacrificed in exchange for the avoidance of the predicted negative outcome.

The math over a lifetime: the person who runs the stories gives up countless wanted things in exchange for avoiding occasional negative outcomes that often would not have happened anyway. The unstated wants accumulate. The chronic background disappointment of unmet wants is much larger than the occasional acute disappointment of stated-and-refused wants. The avoidance is paying more than the risk it is supposedly avoiding.

This is the same calculus as [[owning-the-want-makes-you-less-manipulatable]] — chronic certain cost vs. intermittent contingent cost. The stories make the math invisible by framing the suppression as the safe move. The suppression is actually the more expensive move.

## The Coaching Move

When a person resists stating a want, the coaching move is to surface which story is operating:

"What do you think would happen if you said what you want? What's the story your mind is running about the consequence?"

The answer usually maps to one of the four. Once named, the next question is: "How often does that actually happen when you state a want? What's the actual base rate, not the predicted catastrophe?"

The base-rate question typically reveals that the predicted catastrophe is much rarer than the story claims. Once the actual base rate is visible, the want can usually be stated, and the result is usually closer to the base rate than to the catastrophe.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The body locks up before stating a want
- The mouth opens to ask and then closes
- The want gets rephrased as a question, then a hint, then nothing
- A wave of guilt arrives when even considering stating the want
- A wave of fear arrives at the thought of the other person's reaction
- The wanted thing gets sublimated into doing something nice for the other person in hopes of reciprocation
- The wanted thing gets channeled into the don't-want form (see [[dont-want-is-a-want]])
- The person catches themselves rehearsing many different ways to soften, hedge, or pre-apologize for the want
