# Impulse to Should

## Definition

A natural impulse arises: to move the body, to call a friend, to write something. This impulse is alive and motivating. Then [[perfectionism]] intercepts it and converts it to a "should." The should is dead weight. It is unmotivating. The original energy dissipates.

"We have all these impulses inside of us that are just popping up like, oh, I wanna work out or I wanna exercise or I wanna move my body. And then that impulse, which is the deep connection, immediately gets turned into a perfection of I should work out. And then it's like completely unmotivating." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

## The Mechanics

The pattern has a specific structure. The want is the alive impulse. The should is an egoic layer on top of it that slows the whole thing down.

"The want is just that impulse that moves through you, that animates your actions. That is what the want is. And the should is just this egoic layer on top of it that slows the whole thing down." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The conversion is illustrated cleanly: a person is sitting and the impulse arises to exercise. The impulse is itself motivating. Instead of acting on it -- "oh cool" and ten jumping jacks -- the person says "I should go to the gym." The should kills the impulse. The chances of working out drop sharply the moment the should appears.

"You're sitting and you thought -- think to yourself, I should exercise. What's actually happening is there's an impulse and a want to exercise, and it shows up. And instead of just like, oh cool, and doing 10 jumping jacks, you say to yourself, I should go to the gym. And then that just destroys your chances of actually working out." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## When Someone Experiences This

The person describes wanting to do something but not doing it. They describe the task as something they "should" do. They may have elaborate plans, workout schedules, writing commitments, or relationship goals that never convert to action.

Children do not have this pattern. They follow impulses directly. The pattern develops as the perfectionist overlay grows, converting "I want" into "I should" and then layering shame for not meeting the should.

The cycle: impulse arises, should intercepts, shame for not meeting the should, avoidance. See [[negative-self-reinforcement]].

## What Joe Does

Names the pattern. Points out the difference between the alive impulse and the dead should. The alive version: "I want to do this because it matters to me." The dead version: "I should do this." The intervention is to return to the impulse underneath the should. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] for the specific move.

The reframe is often somatic: not "I should work out for 30 minutes today" but "how do I want to be in my body for 30 minutes today?" The two questions sound almost identical and produce opposite results.

"How do I want to be in my body right now? And you just -- 30 minutes of how do I want to be in my body right now would get you exactly where you want to be in your body. And how much more appealing is that? I have to work out today, or how do I want to be in my body for 30 minutes? It seems like it's almost no different, and it's like worlds and worlds apart." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Connected Concerns

- Procrastination: the impulse is present but the should has killed the motivation.
- Stagnation: the person has goals but no movement toward them.
- Self-criticism: the person beats themselves up for not meeting their own standards.
