# The More-Avoidance-More-Abandonment-More-Anger Loop

## The Pattern

A specific feedback loop that runs inside many couple fights — and outside fights, as the chronic state of the relationship. Each partner's protective move is the exact stimulus that triggers the other partner's escalation. The loop is self-amplifying. Nobody is adding new energy. The energy comes from the loop itself.

"The more the person avoids, the more the other person feels abandoned. And then they get angrier to try to get that engagement. On the other side, the more angry she gets, the more he avoids and the more he feels disconnected. And so the more he runs away to feel that sense of safety, and then the more they're disconnected." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of Couples

The loop has four positions, each producing the next. The avoider avoids to feel safe. The agitator feels abandoned by the avoidance. The agitator escalates anger to produce engagement. The avoider feels attacked by the anger and retreats further to feel safe. The cycle is the engine. Both partners are doing exactly what makes the situation worse, and both are doing it for structurally good reasons.

## The Four Positions

**Position 1: Avoider retreats for safety.** The avoider pulls back — physically, verbally, emotionally. The motive is to escape the partner's activation, the loaded topic, the felt sense of impending conflict. The retreat is a protective move. It is rational from the avoider's perspective.

**Position 2: Agitator registers retreat as abandonment.** The retreat produces no actual physical distance, but the relational distance is felt instantly. The agitator's body reads it as the partner leaving. For agitators with abandonment trauma, the reading is amplified — the present retreat is being read against childhood templates of caregivers who left, refused, went absent. The body experiences existential threat.

**Position 3: Agitator escalates to produce engagement.** The agitator's reach for engagement intensifies. Volume, urgency, accusation, contradiction. The function is to force the avoider back into contact. Underneath the form, the agitator is reaching for connection. The form ensures the connection cannot land.

**Position 4: Avoider reads escalation as attack and retreats further.** The avoider's body registers the increased volume as confirmation that retreat was the right move. The threat the avoider was protecting against now appears to have materialized. The avoider retreats more. The loop returns to position 1, with both partners more activated and the distance greater.

Each loop iteration costs both partners. The avoider feels more attacked. The agitator feels more abandoned. The relational distance grows. The trust that the other will meet them erodes. After enough iterations, the relationship is exhausted by the loop independent of any specific content.

## Why It's Self-Amplifying

The loop is self-amplifying because each partner's protective move is precisely the stimulus that triggers the other's escalation. There is no neutral position. Every move by either partner adds energy to the loop.

The avoider cannot retreat in a way that reduces the agitator's activation. Any retreat is registered as abandonment. The avoider cannot engage in a way that reduces their own activation. Any engagement is registered as exposure to attack. The avoider's binary options both feed the loop.

The agitator has the same problem in reverse. The agitator cannot push for engagement in a way that does not register as attack to the avoider. The agitator cannot back off in a way that does not register as confirmation that the engagement was never worth fighting for. Both of the agitator's options feed the loop.

This is why the loop persists despite both partners "trying to do better." The trying happens at the level of the moves. The moves themselves are the loop's fuel. Neither partner has an option in the current frame that does not feed the loop. The exit requires leaving the frame, not optimizing within it.

## The Mistaken Read That Maintains the Loop

The loop is held in place by a specific misread: each partner believes the other partner is causing their stuck position.

The avoider believes they would not retreat if the agitator stopped attacking. The agitator believes they would not escalate if the avoider stopped withdrawing. Each partner experiences themselves as responding to the other. Structurally, each is the input that produces the other's response.

The naming of the loop dissolves part of the misread. Once both partners can see the loop — not just feel their own position inside it, but actually see the four-position cycle — the personal blame frame becomes harder to maintain. The fight stops being "you keep doing this to me" and starts being "we are inside a loop, and our individual moves are the loop's fuel."

This naming alone does not exit the loop. It opens the possibility of exit by making the loop visible.

## The Body's Contribution

Each partner's body is doing work that compounds the loop. The avoider's nervous system, under sustained activation from the agitator's escalation, eventually goes into freeze or numbness. The avoider's emotional availability decreases, which makes the next retreat both more total and less recoverable. The agitator's nervous system, under sustained un-met reaching, goes into sympathetic hyperactivation. The agitator's emotional self-regulation decreases, which makes the next escalation both larger and less measured.

The two nervous-system states are also opposite. The avoider's freeze and the agitator's hyperarousal are mirror images. Each state is the body's response to the other state. The longer the loop runs, the more entrenched each body's response becomes, and the harder it is to interrupt the loop from inside the bodies.

This is part of why long-married couples who have been running this loop for years cannot easily interrupt it through a single conversation. The bodies have been trained. The exit usually requires both partners to develop interrupt capacities outside the live moment — agreements made when the loop is not firing, somatic practices that the body has rehearsed when calm, and a willingness to use those instruments mid-loop even when the body is screaming for the familiar move.

## The Connection to Hiding-as-Control

This loop is the live mechanism by which [[hiding-as-control-of-partner-emotion]] produces the very outcome the hider was trying to prevent. The hider hides to avoid the partner's reaction. The hiding produces felt disconnection in the partner. The disconnection produces the partner's growing activation. The partner's activation eventually surfaces as the larger reaction the hider was trying to prevent in the first place. The hiding has produced its own failure.

"It makes her feel more disconnected, makes her feel like she's pulling away, that he's pulling away even more. And then she gets more and more angry." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of Couples

The loop is the mechanism. The disconnection is the input that loads the agitator. The agitator's loading is what eventually fires as the reaction the hider feared. The hider has structurally produced the feared outcome by trying to avoid it. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the broader principle.

## How This Differs From Other Couple Loops

This loop overlaps with but is distinct from several other named feedback loops.

**Versus [[defense-feels-like-attack-feedback-loop]].** That loop is about how each partner's defense reads as new attack to the other partner, producing the back-and-forth shame-throwing of the [[shame-hot-potato]]. The mechanism is similar (each protective move triggers the other), but the content is different: shame and defense rather than avoidance and abandonment. The two loops often run simultaneously in the same fight.

**Versus the [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]].** The anxious-avoidant dynamic is the cross-relationship attachment pattern. This loop is the in-fight escalation form. The anxious-avoidant configuration produces the conditions for this loop to fire reliably, but the loop itself is its own mechanism.

**Versus the [[chaser-and-love-remover-both-experience-death]] structure.** That principle is about each partner experiencing the other's natural move as existential death. This loop is about how the resulting moves amplify each other in real time. The two are layered: the trauma read (the other's move is death) explains why each partner's body activates so completely; the loop describes how the activations interact.

## The Exit

The exit from the loop is not optimizing each partner's moves within the loop. The exit is restructuring the loop itself.

Operational moves that interrupt the loop:

**Name the loop in the moment.** "We are in our loop right now." The naming is itself an interrupt. Both partners can see, briefly, the configuration they are inside. The brief seeing is enough to open a different next move.

**Use pre-agreed interrupts.** Both partners agree, outside the loop, on a phrase or move that signals "let's pause." The interrupt only works if both partners have agreed to honor it. See [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]] for the protocol.

**Take time apart with a timeline.** The avoider's instinct to leave is right; the avoider's typical execution (leaving without timeline, leaving without acknowledgment, leaving as withdrawal) is what triggers the agitator's abandonment activation. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for the distinction that lets the avoider's instinct serve the relationship instead of fueling the loop.

**The agitator names the wanting underneath the demand.** "I am scared we are getting more distant and I want to feel connected to you." The named want is what the agitator's escalation has been trying to deliver in a corrupted form. Naming it directly removes the load from the escalation. See [[speak-the-elephant-from-open-heart-not-fear]].

**The avoider names the activation underneath the retreat.** "I am flooded right now and I am about to go away in my body. I want to come back." The named activation lets the agitator see what the retreat is actually about — not abandonment, but the avoider's nervous-system limit. The agitator can then meet the avoider's limit rather than reading it as rejection.

None of these moves works as a one-time fix. Each is a practice. The loop has been running for years; the practices need to run for months before the body learns there is a different available move.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A fight starts about nothing in particular and escalates within minutes to an intensity that surprises both of you
- You can predict the next four moves of the fight and you watch yourself make your move anyway
- After the fight, both of you are exhausted in addition to whatever the fight was supposedly about
- You catch yourself making your protective move in the exact moment that you can see it is going to make things worse
- You realize the loop has been running not just in fights but as the chronic background state of the relationship
- One of you is described as "the difficult one" and one as "the avoidant one" and the labels feel true and incomplete at the same time
- You start fights to make sure your partner is still there and they retreat to make sure you cannot reach them and you both register the other's move as confirmation of your worst fear

Joe describes this with the easy clarity of system-dynamics observation. The naming is precise; the partners are not blamed; the loop is the subject, not the people. The tone carries the relief of "this is a structural thing, not a thing about you specifically being broken." The naming itself is the first lever.
