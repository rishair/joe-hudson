# Signs of a Dysfunctional Leader

## The Pattern

Certain leader-level behaviors function as linchpins. If the leader exhibits them, the team cannot become functional regardless of what else is in place. These are the patterns Joe scans for when called in to assess a team. They are the leader's contribution to team dysfunction, named directly.

"There's a couple like really big ones, like linchpins that if a leader shows these qualities, it's gonna be really, really hard for the team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Linchpin Signs

### Blame or Politics

The leader's mood, not the work, determines how to succeed.

"It makes it so people don't know how to succeed or how to win because it's really based on the mood of the leader. And blaming obviously just is a horrible way to motivate people. And it also doesn't solve anything." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

Blame motivates nothing and resolves nothing. Politics produces [[politics-from-unclear-standards]]: when success is based on relationship rather than performance, anxiety routes through alliances rather than through the work.

### Indecisiveness

Trust collapses fast around an indecisive leader. The indecisiveness is not about speed -- it is about the felt sense of being in a fear-based loop around every choice.

"Indecisive doesn't mean that you make every decision in a split second. It means that you can make decisions easily or that you don't even notice there are decisions. You're not in that fear-based place of like, oh my God, what should I do? That you're taking the next iterative step." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The functional version is iterative: the leader takes the next step, watches what happens, takes the next one. The dysfunctional version is paralyzed: every decision is a referendum on the leader's competence, and the team feels it.

### Conflict Avoidance

The leader's avoidance of conflict trades short-term discomfort for long-term dysfunction. Without conflict, trust cannot form, problems cannot be named, inefficiencies cannot be fixed. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].

"We are people, we have conflict. Avoid the conflict, you avoid building trust, you avoid sorting out the problems, you avoid finding the inefficiencies, you avoid seeing the problems that you need to fix. All those things happen if you avoid conflict." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The team does not want a leader who storms in shouting. They also do not want a leader they cannot trust to name what needs naming. The leader who optimizes for short-term comfort gets long-term discomfort.

"It's oftentimes you see the leaders trade the short-term discomfort for the long-term discomfort. And so if you're okay with the short-term discomfort, you have a lot less long-term discomfort." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

### Defensiveness, or Allowing Defensiveness

If the leader is defensive, or allows defensive behavior to operate in meetings, communication shuts down. People stop saying things to the defensive person, then stop saying things at all.

"If someone's getting defensive, then people on the team, if they're super functional, they'll be like, hey, you're defensive, what's happening here? But if they're not, then people start being scared to say things to that person and then communication gets all gummed up. And so allowing defensive behavior is just super toxic for a team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The response is direct: see [[coach-defensive-behavior-out-of-the-meeting]].

### Doesn't Lead AND Follow

A functional leader has to do both. Lead: be first in, take responsibility, lead with their own vulnerability, wonder, and not-knowing. Follow: actually listen to the wisdom of the executives, give them authority and autonomy. A leader who only leads makes a tyranny; a leader who only follows makes a vacuum.

"Leaders who don't lead or follow, either one of those can create super dysfunctional teams. So when I mean follow is that they're actually listening to the wisdom of their executives or the people that they're working with and letting them have authority and autonomy, super important. Also, leading, being the first in, taking the responsibility, having the quote unquote the buck stop with them, being the person who leads the charge in their own kind of vulnerability." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

This is the most subtle of the linchpins because it requires the leader to do two things that look contradictory but are not. See [[lead-and-follow]].

## Common Misreadings

- **"My team isn't reacting to me, so I must not be doing this."** The team's reaction to a dysfunctional leader is usually self-protection, not pushback. A team that has stopped offering counter-perspectives is not a team that agrees with the leader; it is a team that has given up.
- **"I'm not blaming, I'm just being direct."** Blame can be quiet and friendly. The test is what the body of the person on the receiving end registers, not what the leader intended.
- **"I'm decisive when it matters."** The signal is the pattern of all decisions, not the visible ones. The team reads the leader's hesitation around small decisions and predicts the hesitation around big ones.
- **"I'm not avoiding conflict, I'm just choosing my battles."** Choosing your battles is fine. Choosing none of them is conflict avoidance with a more flattering name.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The leader feels exhausted by every meeting and cannot say why
- The leader feels their team is "fine" but the team has stopped surfacing problems
- Direct reports start managing up rather than executing
- The leader hears about issues from outside the team that should have come from inside
- The leader's calendar fills with meetings about things that should have been decided already
- The team's best performers leave with vague reasons

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader presents a stagnant team and asks for diagnosis
- A leader senses something is off but blames the team or the market
- A team member describes their organization and the description matches one of the linchpin patterns
- A coach is asked to assess a leadership team and is running through the linchpin checklist
- An executive is hiring or evaluating a leader and needs criteria
