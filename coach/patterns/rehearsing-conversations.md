# Rehearsing Conversations

## The Pattern

Running future or past conversations through the head repeatedly. The pattern has several forms:

**Pre-rehearsal:** Imagining a conversation that has not yet happened. Trying various phrasings. Imagining the other person's responses. Imagining your responses to those responses. The conversation is "perfected" before it begins.

**Post-rehearsal:** Replaying a conversation that has already happened. "I should have said..." "If I had said it this way..." The do-over runs in the mind for hours, days, weeks.

**Even-when-it-went-well rehearsal:** The same compulsion to review even successful conversations, looking for what could have been better.

This is the [[voice-in-the-head]] in one of its most active expressions. It is a specific instance of [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]]: the rehearsal aimed at producing a good conversation reliably produces a worse one.

## How It Operates

The voice initiates the rehearsal with the apparent intention of preparation: "you should think through what you're going to say so the conversation goes well." The intention is reasonable on its face. What follows is not.

A few rounds of imagined conversation might genuinely prepare. After that, additional rounds produce anxiety, not clarity. The anxiety compounds. By the time the actual conversation arrives, the person is exhausted, tense, and primed for the imagined version rather than the real one.

"If you think about all the worrying that you did and all the scenarios that it went through and all the things you thought you were gonna say and all the ways you were gonna behave, like, how much of that was actually pertinent? How much of that was actually useful energy? How much of that actually helped you prepare? And how much of it just did nothing, it was just a waste of time, and how much of it actually hurt?" -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The honest accounting: a small fraction of the rehearsal helped. Most was waste. A meaningful portion actively hurt.

## The Disappointment Mechanism

When the actual conversation does not go like the rehearsal — which it never does, because rehearsals are with imaginary versions of the other person — there is a felt failure. The mind reaches for the rehearsed script and finds it irrelevant. The discrepancy registers as a shame spiral. "I rehearsed this. I should have been able to do better. Why didn't it go like I planned?"

Brett describes the experience in the transcript: "My experience of that is it also sets you up for major disappointment and self — like a shame spiral after the conversation doesn't go anything according to your rehearsal."

The rehearsal produces both the expectation and the failure to meet it. See [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]] for the broader pattern this is part of.

## Three Additional Failure Modes

Brett surfaces three further variations in the transcript that all run on the same mechanism:

**The perfect rehearsal kills the interest.** Once the conversation has been rehearsed to perfection in the mind, the actual conversation loses its appeal. "I don't want to break its perfect image." The mind prefers the imagined version. The real one is left undone.

**The unknown gets removed.** Once the conversation is "fully planned," it becomes boring. There is no discovery left. The energy that would have driven the actual conversation has been spent on the imaginary one. See [[wonder]].

**The mind mistakes rehearsal for action.** "Sometimes I'll actually trick myself into thinking I actually had the conversation, which can be disastrous." The conversation that was supposed to happen never happens, because the mind has so thoroughly imagined it that it feels handled.

These are different shapes of the same dynamic: the voice converts the live event into a mental event, and the live event suffers or never occurs.

## The Underlying Feeling

What the voice is trying to protect you from with the rehearsal is usually rejection. The feeling beneath the rehearsal is fear of being judged, dismissed, rejected, abandoned in the actual conversation. The voice's solution is to control the conversation in advance. Since real conversations cannot be controlled, the strategy fails.

The cleaner path is to let the feeling underneath be felt. The fear of rejection, fully felt, releases. The conversation becomes possible without the rehearsal. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]] and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

## The 4D Chess Variant

Jaime Waydo names a specific texture of the rehearsal pattern: 4D chess. The mind tries to map every possible move the other person could make, every possible counter-move, every possible response to every possible reaction. The mental load is enormous and the predictive value is near zero, because conversations are not chess and other humans are not simulators.

"You start playing chess in your head about 'if I say this, then they're going to do that or they're going to feel that.' And like that chess game starts happening." -- Jaime Waydo, Taking Care of Yourself First

The 4D chess pattern is the leadership version of rehearsing. It is particularly common in leaders who have been told to "consider all stakeholders" and have generalized that instruction into pre-simulating every meeting before it happens. The cost is the same as ordinary rehearsing: exhaustion, anxiety, and a worse actual conversation than would have happened without the simulation.

Jaime's experience of the shift: in a meeting where the boss was yelling, "it was the first time in a long time that I just shut the chess game off and was like, 'I'm not going to do that. I'm just going to go with the first thing that came in my head.'" The first thing was the truth. The truth produced a 180-degree relationship shift. See [[name-the-authoritys-fear]] for the move that became possible once the chess game was off.

## The Inversion: Stoic Visualization

The same machinery that produces destructive rehearsing can be turned around and used productively. Instead of rehearsing what to say in order to control the outcome, visualize all the possible outcomes -- including the worst -- and feel through them in advance. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].

The shift Brett names in the transcript:

"Often people rehearse a conversation so that they can play the chess game and avoid the consequence they're trying not to get, like being yelled at, rather than just seeing all the possible moves on the table that they could play and then all the counter moves they could play to all the possible moves that are played, you know, back at them, and just accepting the entire thing." -- Brett Kistler, Taking Care of Yourself First

The rehearsal strategy tries to avoid a consequence. The visualization strategy accepts all consequences. The first sustains anxiety. The second dissolves it. Same activity, opposite direction.

This is why telling someone "stop rehearsing" rarely works. The mind is going to run scenarios regardless. The question is whether the scenarios are being run to avoid an outcome (which keeps fear in place) or to fully meet an outcome in advance (which dissolves fear).

## When Someone Experiences This

- Spending hours each week reviewing past conversations.
- Going into important meetings exhausted from imaginary preparation.
- Having difficulty being present in conversations because the rehearsed script keeps interfering.
- Avoiding conversations that have been "fully prepared" because they feel done.
- Feeling like a fraud when the actual conversation does not produce the rehearsed wisdom.
- The shame after a conversation that "should have gone better."

## How to Work With It

The voice is not stopped by being told to stop rehearsing. The rehearsal is itself the voice. Telling yourself to stop rehearsing is a new should, which the voice will use.

The shifts that work:

- Notice the rehearsal without trying to stop it. The awareness changes it. See [[awareness-as-change-agent]].
- Drop into the feeling underneath. What are you trying to prevent from happening in the conversation? Feel it now, in advance. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].
- Recognize that you have rehearsed many times and the rehearsal has rarely been useful. The voice has lost credibility on this point. The credibility loss is itself the dismantling.
- Trust the actual moment. The conversation will have its own intelligence. The version of you that shows up live is more capable than the version that runs rehearsals.
