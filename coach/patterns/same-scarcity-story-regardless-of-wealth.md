# Same Scarcity Story Regardless of Wealth

## The Pattern

The thought "I do not have enough" runs at every level of actual wealth. The billionaire driving to a meeting and the person living paycheck to paycheck can have the identical inner monologue. The mind generates the scarcity story independent of the financial situation. Whatever the number is, the mind finds the reason it is not enough.

This is why the strategy "I will fix this when I have more" never completes. The thought is not produced by the absence of money. It is produced by a basic feature of how the mind operates with money. Adding money does not subtract the thought.

## How Joe Saw It

During a period of gratitude practice in his early thirties, Joe was driving and noticed his mind had turned to a familiar coveting -- needing more money, needing something. He had recently had contact with two or three billionaires through art and his environmental work. The thought arrived: the billionaires I know think this way too. I am a billionaire.

"I thought of the two billionaires and I'm like, they are also right now probably thinking they don't have enough. Because I knew them. And I knew even if they would have logically said, I have enough money, their minds were constantly going to what they didn't have. And I was like, oh, I'm a fucking billionaire, you know? And I saw that there's people on the earth, like if you're some of the poorest people in America, you're in the top 1% in the world. I'm like, there's people on earth who are like, how could that dude be unhappy and think he doesn't have enough when he can go to a restaurant and buy a pupusa, you know?" -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

"It was such a strange thought, like it's nonsensical, but it was just like, oh, I have the same reality as a billionaire. No matter how much money they have or how little money I have, we're both walking around the world having the same thought process." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

The recognition was structural. The scarcity story is not a calculation. It is a stance toward money that the mind takes regardless of how much money there is. The billionaire is not less in scarcity than the person on the street. And the comparison goes both directions: from the global perspective, an "American poor" is in the top 1% in the world, yet still produces the same scarcity story as the local billionaire produces from their position.

"I just saw the whole thing. This like the shackle of thinking that I didn't have enough. And in that I'm like, oh, I'm the same as a billionaire. There's no difference really. I mean, like, yeah, perhaps they can rent a jet or buy a jet and I can't. But my reality is either I have a ton or I have nothing or I don't have enough. And it doesn't really matter how much you actually have." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

## Why This Matters

If the scarcity story is independent of the actual wealth, then trying to solve scarcity by adding wealth is a category error. The fix is not at the level of the bank account. It is at the level of the relationship with money itself.

This is why [[if-i-had-more-money]] is structurally never satisfied. The premise -- that more money will resolve the not-enough -- is contradicted by the empirical record of what happens to people who get more money. The story persists. New benchmarks appear. The thought "I need more" continues.

## The Modern Translation of a Human Baseline

Underneath this pattern is a more general human condition: the mind tends to say "there is something more I should be doing, there is something more I can achieve, there is something I should do so I am safe." Mice exhibit the same get-up-and-go without the verbal narration. The mechanism is older than the story.

Modern society has chosen money as the most available material for that narration to attach to. The basic restlessness is human. The money form of it is cultural. When the form is dropped, the underlying restlessness becomes visible.

"There's something basic in the human condition that says, oh, there's something more I can be doing, there's something more I can achieve, there's something that I should be doing so that I'm safe. And the way that our society works is that money is the most obvious way to do something." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

## When People Drop the Pattern

The few who actually exit the scarcity loop fall into roughly two camps. One: depressed or acquiescent (the loop dropped by collapse, not by transformation). Two: at peace, having done significant work to feel the underlying discomfort and channel it differently. The second group is much smaller.

"If you meet somebody who doesn't have that, there's kind of one of two options. One, they're depressed, or they've become acquiescent. And in another way, there's a fewer number of them that actually are quite at peace. They've done a lot of work to really feel into that discomfort and to channel it in a different direction." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

## The Gratitude Lever

The practice that opened the recognition for Joe was a daily gratitude practice: 10 minutes per day, done with his wife Tara, deliberately oriented at what they already had rather than at what they did not have. The mind that has been training to scan for what is missing can be retrained to notice what is present. This does not eliminate the scarcity thought. It loosens its grip enough that other thoughts become available.

"The concept was that I'm constantly thinking about what I don't have. And what happens if I constantly think about what I do have? How does that change things? If I stop thinking about the lack and I start thinking about all that I do have, how does that change the way I look at everything?" -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

Within about six months of starting the daily practice, the perceptual shift had taken hold. The "billionaire" recognition emerged from within this same period of trained attention. The practice did not directly produce the recognition; it produced the conditions under which the mind could see what it had not been able to see before. See [[gratitude-around-the-problem]] for the targeted form of this practice.

## In Coaching

When a person describes a milestone that will end their scarcity, the question to test is: at the previous milestone, did the scarcity end? Usually the answer reveals the pattern. The person realizes they have made this prediction before and it did not come true.

The follow-up is not "stop wanting more." The follow-up is to investigate where the wanting is coming from. See [[money-as-surrogate]].

## The Jungle of Money Perception

The same gratitude practice that produced the billionaire recognition also produced a different perceptual shift in the same period: the recognition that opportunities for money were everywhere, all the time, embedded in every object in the visible environment.

"At some point, I could look out and I just saw that everything -- somebody made money on everything I was looking at. I was living in LA at the time and it was just like, oh, that concrete, 20 people made money on that concrete. You know, that road light, the electricity that it brings to the road light, the paint on the road light. I could just see like everywhere I looked, there was ways people were making money. Money was so available. It was all over. It was like endless. And I had never seen that before. I thought that there was a shortage of it." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

The image Joe uses: walking through a jungle of money where the resources are hanging on every tree, and the task is to reach up and pick them.

"This is like walking through a jungle of money and finances. You just have to reach up and pick stuff off." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

This is the inverse of the scarcity story. The same world that, under the scarcity scan, presented as "not enough" presented under the gratitude scan as so abundant that money was visibly being made on every surface in the visual field. The world did not change. The scan changed. The amount of money available to the person who could see this way was structurally different from the amount available to the person who could not.

Within roughly six to eight months of starting the gratitude practice, Joe was completely out of debt, making a living from art, and feeling settled enough to expand the family. The practice did not earn the money. It enabled the perception that money was available, and the perception enabled actions that produced money. The mechanism is partly perceptual (you see options you did not see when you were scanning for absence) and partly behavioral (you act differently when you are oriented toward what is here rather than what is missing).
