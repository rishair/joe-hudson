# Rebellion or Submission to Should

## Definition

When the nervous system reads a should as a threat (see [[should-as-threat]]), only two responses become available. Both are bad outcomes. Neither produces real movement. The mere fact that someone is in either response is the diagnostic that a should is operating.

## The Two Responses

**Rebellion:** The instinctive "no." Something innate refuses the imposition. The person does the opposite of what they "should" do, or they delay, sabotage, procrastinate. This is why the bad habits a person has been telling themselves they should stop for years are still in place. The internal should is producing the internal rebellion.

"There's just something innate in us that's like, no, no, right? Response. So, okay, that's not a really effective way to create anything, is just creating nos." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

**Submission:** The disempowered "yes." Not surrender. Submission. The person collapses into "you're right, I should have, I am weak and I will just do what you say." They do the thing, but from depletion rather than desire. This produces a population of compliant, drained, resentful people.

"It's not surrender, it's submission. It's like, I am weak and I will just do what you say. And then you've got a whole bunch of disempowered people, and that doesn't really help much either." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## The Diagnostic Use

The presence of either response is the signal that a should is in play. If a person describes wanting to do something but consistently not doing it, rebellion is running. If a person describes doing something they hate doing because they have to, submission is running. In both cases, the move is not to push harder on the should. It is to drop the should and locate the want underneath. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

## The Pattern Applied to Self

The same two responses run internally. The voice in the head says "you should exercise." Two things happen: the part being addressed either rebels (does not exercise, picks up the phone, opens a snack) or submits (forces through the workout from obligation, hates it, finds it unsustainable). Either way, the person is locked in a relationship with themselves modeled on a bad manager.

"Most of us who had a boss who spoke to us like the should voice in our head, we would quit or we would be miserable." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The boss-employee model of the relationship to self is the structure that produces both responses. When the model shifts -- when the voice becomes one that helps you find what you want rather than tell you what you should do -- both responses dissolve.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The thing they have been "trying to stop" for a decade is still there. (Rebellion.)
- They keep doing things they hate because they have to. (Submission.)
- Their inner critic has the cadence of a bad manager.
- They oscillate between forcing themselves and giving up.

## Both Running at Once: The Hidden Rebellion Under the Compliant Question

A specific operational form of the pattern: a person presents a habit they have been trying to change in a compliant-seeming question ("I'm struggling to decide about sobriety," "I don't know how to stop overeating"). The compliance is the submission half. The rebellion half is doing the drinking, the eating, the scrolling — keeping the habit alive against the conscious attempt to stop it.

Both halves are operating on the same self-should. Only the submission is in the room. The rebellion has not been named because the person has not been able to identify it from inside the compliance frame.

The coaching move is to surface the rebellion by asking about it directly. See [[hidden-rebellion-under-the-habit]] for the read and the surfacing move. The naming gives the rebellion air, and once spoken from, the rebellion often turns out to be protecting a real want that the should has been suppressing.

This operational form is most visible in long-running addictive patterns (drinking, eating, smoking, scrolling) because the looping has had years to install both halves of the pattern at high intensity. See [[should-i-stop-drinking]] and [[do-i-stop-drinking-session]] for the canonical case.

## The Pressure-Rebellion Loop as Defense Against a Feeling

A specific operational form of the pattern: a person describes decades of putting pressure on themselves and rebelling against the pressure. Both halves run continuously. The person presents the loop as the problem and asks how to stop. The loop has resisted all attempts to change it.

The structural insight: the pressure-and-rebellion loop is not the problem — it is the solution. It is the solution the person built to avoid feeling something specific. Usually sadness. Sometimes grief about the time the loop has cost. The pressure prevents the feeling from arriving; the rebellion prevents collapse into a structure that would also prevent the feeling. Both halves are doing the same job: keeping the feeling at bay.

The presenting form of this variant has specific markers:
- The pattern has run for decades, not years
- Both halves are clearly visible to the person; they can describe them precisely
- The person has tried many approaches to stop the loop and none have worked
- The presenting question is "how do I stop?" or "how do I get out of this bind?"

The coaching path for this variant is structurally different from the both-halves-running-at-once form above (which centers on naming the hidden rebellion under the compliant question). Here, both halves are already named. The work is to:

1. Mirror back the recursive question to dissolve the fix-frame. See [[mirror-back-the-recursion-of-the-fix-question]].
2. Ask the inversion: how do you stop pushing without pushing? The person finds the word (soften, let go).
3. Ask [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-you-soften]]. The feeling arrives in the asking.
4. Ask [[how-much-of-this-game-is-to-avoid-the-feeling]]. The entire loop is reframed as the defense.
5. Address the mythology about the feeling if it surfaces. See [[the-minds-stories-about-emotions]].
6. Stay with the feeling. The body discharges what the loop has been holding off.

The variant connects to [[i-put-pressure-on-myself-and-rebel-against-it]] as the concern article. The coaching path is a fast, efficient sequence that can resolve in a single short session because the diagnostic work is already done — the person has named both halves; only the underneath-feeling needs to arrive.

## The Rebellion-Plus-Belief Variant (Loud Rebellion, Quiet Belief)

A specific form of the both-running-at-once pattern surfaces in clients whose childhood featured the parents installing the "you're too much / you're not enough" verdict. The pattern: the child rebels loudly against the verdict (acting out, defying, refusing) and simultaneously believes the verdict at a level underneath the rebellion. The rebellion is on the outside. The belief is on the inside. The two have no apparent contradiction from inside the system.

The diagnostic naming from the source session:

- **Joe:** "My guess is that when you were a kid, my guess is you were a kid, you were spunky and willful, and it was too much for some parents who were not spunky and willful. That's my guess. And you believed them."
- **Client:** "I did. I believed them. And it was crazy because I rebelled really hard, but I believed them."

The variant is widespread in adults who present as rebellious, anti-authority, or independent and who simultaneously have a fierce inner critic and chronic shoulds. The outward independence and the inward suppression coexist because they come from the same source: the parents' verdict was both fought (rebellion-outward) and absorbed (belief-inward).

The implication for coaching: addressing only the rebellion-half does not resolve the pattern, because the belief-half is still running underneath. A client who has "freed themselves from authority" by leaving the family, the religion, or the job and who still runs a constant inner cage of shoulds is in this variant. The work has to surface the belief explicitly, name it as the parents' verdict the client has been carrying, and let the inward-turned anger (see [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] for the parents-rebellion-shoulds chain) find its outward direction.

The variant connects to [[get-angry-in-the-car]] as the structural corrective: the daily discharge gives the unmoved anger at the parents its outward channel, which removes the fuel from the inward-turned form that has been showing up as the should-cage.
