# We Make It Hard to Be Seen Right at the Moment We Most Need to Be Seen

## The Pattern

A trauma response is, in its original purpose, a reach for recognition that the person did not get as a child. The rush was trying to get the care seen. The rage is trying to get the hurt seen. The withdrawal is trying to get the longing seen. The controlling behavior is trying to get the love seen.

At the surface, the trauma response looks like the opposite of the want. The rush looks like control, not care. The rage looks like attack, not hurt. The withdrawal looks like rejection, not longing. The controlling behavior looks like meanness, not love. What is visible at the surface is precisely what hides the underneath thing the trauma is reaching for.

The result: at the exact moment the person most needs to be seen in what they actually want, they have made themselves opaque to it. The deformation of the want is what blocks the want from being met.

"When we're in fights like this, it's, it's we are in our trauma and we're not being seen the way we weren't being seen as a kid. And now we're making it really hard to be seen. Like I was making it really hard to be seen. I was in my drama when I'm freaking out and rushing the kids and honking the horn and like, come on, we're late for the party or whatever the hell. Like all anyone can see is that I'm being a dick, right? So like, it's— it was really hard for anyone to see it." -- Joe Hudson, Unlocking your trauma pattern

## The Mechanism

The trauma was installed because the original want was not seen. The child wanted to be seen in their care, their fear, their hurt, their longing. The caregiver, for whatever reason, could not see it. The unmet want got encoded as a particular response pattern: a way of trying harder to make the want visible, or a way of giving up and going underground with it.

Decades later, the same pattern fires. The body tries again to make the want visible by running the response. But the response is now a deformed adult behavior — honking the horn, controlling the schedule, raising the voice, going silent, withdrawing affection. The behavior is loud enough to demand attention. It is also obscure enough that the attention it gets is on the surface behavior, not on the want underneath.

The partner, friend, child, or coworker who is on the receiving end of the behavior sees: "this person is being controlling / aggressive / cold / impossible." They do not see: "this person is desperate to have their care / fear / hurt / longing seen." The visible thing eclipses the underneath thing. Because the visible thing is hard to be around, the response from others is usually withdrawal, defense, or counter-attack, none of which deliver the recognition the trauma was reaching for.

The trauma keeps firing because the want keeps not being met. The want keeps not being met because the firing is what blocks the meeting.

## The Childhood Source of Joe's Example

Joe traces his own rush trauma to a specific scene from childhood:

"I'm hanging out with my mom. My dad's supposed to meet us at dinner. He was like 5 minutes late, which he seldom was. And my mom was like, I think, full panic attack. That's how I was raised with time. So you can imagine I have like some trauma with time." -- Joe Hudson, Unlocking your trauma pattern

What was happening in his mother: the panic was an expression of care. She cared whether her family was safe. She cared whether her husband had been in an accident. She cared whether everyone made it home. The care was real.

What was visible to a child: panic, urgency, distress, the demand that everyone match her timing. The care was not visible. The care was buried under the panic that was trying to express it.

Joe encoded the panic as the way care expresses itself. As an adult, when he cares about his kids being on time for a party, the panic fires. He honks the horn. He rushes them. He becomes "a dick." And the same thing happens to him that happened to him as a child watching his mother: nobody can see the care. They can only see the controlling adult who is making them miserable about a five-minute delay.

## What the Recognition Phrase Does About This

The pre-agreed repair phrase (see [[repair-phrase-as-trauma-deactivator]]) works because it pierces the surface to name the underneath. Tara saying "I see how much you care" to Joe, mid-rush, gives the trauma exactly what it has been reaching for: the care is seen. The rush no longer has a job to do. The behavior releases.

This is the structural reason the phrase works. The phrase has to address the want under the trauma, not the trauma itself. A phrase that addresses the surface behavior ("calm down," "stop honking") fails because it confirms the original wound — once again, what is visible is the bad behavior, and what is underneath is invisible. A phrase that addresses the underneath want ("I see how much you care") delivers the recognition the trauma was working so hard to extract. The recognition lands, and the trauma stops needing to work.

This is also why a partner can build the phrase only with knowledge of what is actually underneath the partner's trauma. Without that knowledge, the phrase would address the wrong thing.

## The Double Irony

The pattern contains two interlocking ironies:

1. **The trauma response is designed to produce recognition and it prevents recognition.** It is an unsuccessful strategy that the body keeps running because no better strategy was ever installed.

2. **The people most invested in the person — partners, children, family — are the ones most exposed to the deforming surface behavior.** The strangers, who do not have to live with the rush, are spared most of it. The people the trauma is most desperate to be seen by are the ones who have the hardest time seeing through the surface to the underneath, because the surface is what they have to manage in their daily life.

The combination produces the predictable arc of long relationships: the people who could have given the recognition the trauma was reaching for spend years experiencing the surface deformation instead, and conclude that the person is just controlling, just angry, just cold. The underneath want, never named or seen, eventually gets buried under the relational damage that the trauma created in its attempt to surface.

## The Way Out

The pattern is exited through a specific structure. The trauma-firing partner cannot make themselves un-trauma-firing. The recognizing partner cannot make themselves see through the surface automatically. The exit requires:

1. **Naming what is underneath, outside the fight.** Both partners have to develop language for what the trauma is reaching for. This requires the conversations described in [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]] and the question "what is the thing you want most that you never got as a kid?" See [[repair-phrase-as-trauma-deactivator]] for the question and how the answer becomes a phrase.

2. **A phrase or action that addresses the underneath, agreed in advance.** See [[repair-phrase-as-trauma-deactivator]] for the move.

3. **Delivery of the phrase mid-trauma by the recognizing partner.** The phrase, said cleanly, gives the trauma what it was reaching for, which dissolves the loop.

4. **Internal practice on the trauma-firing partner's side.** Over time, the trauma-firing partner can build the recognition themselves — see [[see-yourself-when-they-cant-see-you]] — so that the loop does not depend on the partner's availability.

Both partners have to participate. Neither can fix this alone. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] for the structural fact that the wound made in relationship heals in relationship.

## The Inversion Read for the AI Coach

When a person describes being misunderstood by their partner, treated like they are mean or controlling or impossible, the inversion is often: they are running a trauma response whose original purpose was to be seen, and the response is what is blocking them from being seen. The coaching path is to surface what they were actually reaching for under the behavior and to see if the partner has access to seeing that.

When a person describes their partner being mean or controlling or impossible, the inversion is the same in reverse: the partner is running a trauma response whose underneath want is the opposite of what the surface behavior conveys. The coaching path is to help the person look for the underneath want under the surface behavior they have been reacting to.

In both directions, the operative question is: *what is the want that this trauma response is trying to make visible? What would it take to see that?*

## Tone

Joe describes the pattern with rueful self-recognition. The "I was being a dick" line is delivered without softening; it is an accurate description of how he appeared to his family in the moment, and the recognition does not require pretending otherwise. The compassion comes alongside the unsparingness: the dick-behavior was a desperate trauma firing, the trauma was real, the want underneath was real, and naming all of it accurately is what opens the way out.
