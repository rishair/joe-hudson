# Addiction to Shame (The Shame Adds to the Thrill)

## The Trigger

A natural appetite (sex, eating, spending, attention) has had shame piled onto it, usually by family, religion, or society. Over enough repetitions, the appetite and the shame fire together. The body wires them as one experience. From that point forward the appetite cannot be activated without summoning some shame, and -- here is the trap -- the shame becomes part of what makes the experience compelling.

The dog at the electric fence learns it can run through quickly: the shock comes, but it is brief, and the freedom is on the other side. Some people do this with shame. They reach for the shamed thing knowing the shame will arrive and accepting that as part of the cost. Over time the shame stops being a cost and starts being a flavor of the appetite itself.

"If you throw shame onto that urge, right, which most of society has done, or onto eating for that matter, then you start associating shame with -- I mean, it's like things that wire together fire together. So every time sex happens, shame happens. There's actually something that happens where people get addicted to shame, I would say, where the shame is the addiction. And so sex isn't as thrilling unless there's a little bit of shame with it, or eating isn't -- or shopping isn't as thrilling if there isn't a little bit of shame with it." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

## The Cycle

1. The person feels the appetite. The appetite arrives wired with shame.
2. They reach for the thing the appetite is pointing at -- but the version they reach for is the one that produces shame, not the version that wouldn't. Sneaking the cookie at midnight, not enjoying dessert with friends. The pornographic version, not the connected version. The impulse buy, not the considered purchase.
3. The act happens, the shame arrives, the small thrill of shame is part of what made the act compelling.
4. Because shame [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] the satisfaction is hollow. The person never quite arrives.
5. The appetite reactivates. The wiring fires again. Repeat.

The empty-ghost quality of the experience drives the repetition. The hunger is for the satisfaction the shame is blocking, not for more of the activity.

## The Tell

The diagnostic: the version of the activity the person actually does is the version that produces shame. There is also a non-shameful version available. They consistently choose the shame-wired one.

"I still recreate sex in a way that I get to feel shame instead of having sex in a way that would make me feel no shame, I continually choose to have sex in a way that gives me shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

The choice is not conscious. The wiring drives it. But it is observable from outside: the person could have had the cleaner version of the experience, and they did not.

## The Dog Running Through the Fence

Brett offers the electric fence metaphor. Joe extends it. Some dogs learn that if they run through the fence quickly, the shock only hits for a moment and then they are out in the open. They start using the fence as part of the experience of escape.

"There's dogs that learn that if they just go through that fence really quickly, it's worth it. So they just like run really quickly and that like it shocks them, but it only shocks them for like a little bit of a time because they're out of the zone because it only shocks you within the zone. And that happens with people too as far as shame. So let's say you have a natural habit like sex... if you throw shame onto that urge... then you start associating shame with [it]." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

See [[electric-fence-with-dog]] for the base metaphor. This pattern is the dark extension: the dog that has incorporated the shock into the route.

## Why Removing the Behavior Does Not Work

People with this pattern often try to abstain. The abstinence does not address the wiring. The shame is still wrapped around the appetite even when the appetite is not being acted on. The appetite returns. The wiring is still there. The act resumes, often more compulsively than before.

The pattern also resists the simple "drop the shame, enjoy the thing" prescription. The wiring is bidirectional: the shame has become part of the pleasure. Removing the shame removes part of what made the thing appealing in its current form. The person has to discover what they actually want -- not the shame-wired version of the appetite, but the underlying want -- and let that become the new attractor.

"To really feel the want in the shame itself, meaning like really feel the desire to -- like, what is the sexual experience you do want? What is the eating experience that you do want? Right? Because usually the one that's wired with shame is not the one that you actually want." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

## When Someone Experiences This

- You keep doing a thing you know you do not really want to do, and the doing of it produces a familiar shame that you almost expected.
- The version of the activity you actually engage in is consistently the one that produces shame, even though cleaner versions are available.
- The thrill of the activity has a sneaking, hiding, transgressive quality to it.
- You have tried to stop and the stopping did not stick, because the appetite kept returning with the shame still wired to it.
- After the activity you feel hollow more than satisfied. The hunger does not subside, it just goes quiet for a while.

## Connection to Other Patterns

This pattern is the felt-experience side of what [[shame-drives-behavior]] describes mechanically. Where that principle says "shame holds the bad habit in place," this pattern names how the holding feels from the inside: the shame becomes part of what makes the habit appetizing.

It is a special case of [[familiar-over-safe]]: the wired association is familiar even when it is not nourishing. The body returns to the familiar wiring.

It also explains why [[negative-self-reinforcement]] cycles persist: the self-criticism after the act provides the shame that the next round of the appetite will be wired to. The reinforcement is reinforcing the wiring.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A natural appetite (sex, eating, spending, attention, substances) that the person was taught early to feel bad about
- Reports of an activity that consistently leaves the person feeling hollow rather than satisfied
- Describing the activity in language that includes hiding, sneaking, or "I shouldn't"
- A long history of attempts to stop the behavior, each followed by resumption
- The person says they want the thing and also do not want the thing, both at once

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward feeling the actual want underneath -- not the shame-wired version, but the clean appetite. What experience do you actually want?
- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] applied to the shame: let it be welcomed so the wiring loosens
- Toward [[shame-stagnates-emotion]]: explaining why the activity never satisfies
- Toward [[shame-as-disconnection]]: locating the shame at the right address (it is signal, not verdict)
- Toward [[inherent-goodness]]: if I were inherently good, what would I make of this appetite?

## Tone

Curious, not pathologizing. The pattern is framed as fascinating wiring rather than moral failure. The person did not choose to wire shame to their appetites; the wiring happened to them. The work is to notice the wiring, not to condemn the person for having it.
