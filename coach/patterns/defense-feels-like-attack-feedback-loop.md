# Defense Feels Like Attack (The Feedback Loop That Powers the Fight)

## The Trigger

Two people in a recurring fight. Neither describes themselves as attacking the other. Each describes themselves as defending against the other's attack. Both descriptions are accurate from inside. The pattern persists because the defenses, perceived from outside the defending body, register in the other body as fresh attacks.

The full cycle, as Joe names it:

"Each of them wants to be seen, and in their attempt to be seen, they're defending themselves. And that defense seems like an attack to the other person. And so both people feel like they're defending themselves and both people feel attacked." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The pattern is symmetric. It does not require an aggressor. It requires only two people who feel a need to be seen and who reach for defense as the means of being seen.

## The Micro-Mechanism

The cycle has a four-beat structure that compresses to seconds in a live fight:

1. **Person A says something** (often unloaded — a complaint, a request, a description).
2. **Person B feels attacked** by it (the body registers a threat to being seen as good).
3. **Person B defends** ("no, that's not what I said," "I only said that because you asked," "wait, that's not fair").
4. **Person A feels attacked** by the defense (the body registers that B is fighting them, denying their reality, making them wrong).

Person A then defends. Person B feels attacked by A's defense. The cycle has now turned over once. After three or four cycles, both people are fully in the fight. Neither can locate the moment it started. Neither feels they began it. Both feel they are defending against the other.

The crucial structural fact: no intentional attack is required for this cycle to run. The defense itself is the attack from the other body's perspective. The attacker exists only as a misperception, but the misperception is symmetric and self-sustaining.

## The Concrete Couples Example

Joe gives the concrete shape this takes in a marriage. The example is dense with the mechanism:

"It's like, you know, 'no, that's not what I said. You said this. Yeah, that's not what I said.' Or 'I said that because, you know, I said that you were ugly only because you asked me, like, "How ugly am I?" So I was like, "Well, you're about this ugly." So I had no choice.' And so I'm sitting there saying, like, 'I'm not wrong. Like, understand me, see me.' But you're hearing, 'Wait, you just called me ugly again.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The speaker is defending an earlier remark ("you asked, so I told you"). From inside the speaker's body, the speech is defense — "I'm not wrong, see me." From inside the listener's body, the speech is attack — "you just called me ugly again." The same utterance is both, simultaneously, depending on the receiving body.

The argument cannot resolve because both bodies are correctly reporting what they experience. Each accuses the other of attacking. Neither is lying. Both are right.

## Why "Trying to Be Seen" Is the Engine

The motive that fires the defense is not malice. It is the wanting to be seen. Each person wants the other to understand them — to recognize that they are not bad, that they did not mean it that way, that they had a reason. The wanting is real and not pathological. What makes the wanting destructive is that the means of pursuing it (defense, justification, the case for one's own innocence) reads as attack to the body it is directed at.

This is why the exit move is not "stop wanting to be seen." It is "release the urgency to be seen right now." See [[i-dont-need-to-be-seen-right-now]]. The wanting to be seen is fine. The compulsion to be seen this minute is what fires the defense that fires the cycle.

## Indicators

The diagnostic signs that this loop is running.

- **Both people describe themselves as defending.** When asked separately what happened in the last fight, both will say the other started it and they were responding.
- **Neither person can locate the moment of attack.** When pressed for the specific moment one of them attacked, neither can find it. Both can find moments they were defending against attack.
- **The fight is about who said what** — exact phrasing, exact tone, exact intent. The fight has become an argument about the words because the words are where the defense-attack confusion lives.
- **Each defense seems to escalate the conflict** rather than resolve it. The pattern: A defends, B gets angrier; B defends, A gets angrier.
- **The fight is the same fight you have always had.** The content varies; the loop is identical. See [[the-built-up-resentment-story]] for the longer-arc form of this.

## Why This Is Not Just the Hot Potato

This pattern and the [[shame-hot-potato]] are closely related but operate at different scales of description.

The hot potato describes the overall game: shame is being passed between two people who refuse to hold it. This pattern describes the micro-mechanism by which the throws actually get made — through defenses that are not experienced as throws by the thrower but land as throws on the receiver.

The hot potato can be diagnosed at the level of "who is feeling shame and trying to install it in whom." This pattern can be diagnosed at the level of "what utterance just got read as attack despite being intended as defense." The two diagnostics are useful at different moments. The hot potato frame is useful when stepping back to see the structure. This frame is useful when slowed down to the second-by-second mechanics of how the fight escalated.

## Where the Pattern Is Resilient to Good Intentions

The pattern persists even when both people have good will toward each other, even when both have read books about communication, even when both want the relationship to work. The reason: the defense-as-attack misperception happens at a body level, below the level where good intentions operate.

Person A's body registers Person B's defense as attack regardless of whether A consciously wants the relationship to work. The nervous system does not consult the conscious intention. It registers the threat and fires the response. Then the response (A's defense) hits B's body the same way. The intentions of the conscious selves are not what is running the cycle.

This is why "trying harder to be a good partner" does not break the pattern. The trying is happening at the wrong level. The intervention has to happen at the somatic level where the defense fires. See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for the somatic stance that interrupts the cycle.

## The Scale-Invariance

The same pattern runs at every scale of human conflict. Joe names this directly:

"It doesn't even matter if this is a fight between a man and a wife or like NATO and Russia. Both people feel like they're defending themselves on some level for some reason, and both people feel the other person's defense is an attack." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

Two nations, each preparing defenses against the other, both reading the other's defenses as evidence of attack-readiness. Two political parties, each rebutting the other's claims, both reading the rebuttals as confirmation of the other's bad faith. Two teams in a company, each protecting their resources, both reading the protections as territorial aggression. The structure is invariant. The exit moves are the same at every scale, but they require people willing to enact them at scale.

## The Asymmetry That Breaks It

One person stopping the defense is enough to collapse the cycle. The cycle requires both defenders. Removing one defender removes the energy that keeps the loop alive.

This is the structural argument for unilateral non-defense. The fight does not require both people to agree to stop. It requires one body to stop firing the defense. The other body, deprived of the input that was generating its threat-response, eventually settles.

See [[drop-the-hot-potato]] for the exit move and [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] for the stance that makes it possible.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You replay the fight in your head and cannot find the moment you attacked, only moments you defended
- You ask your partner what happened in the fight, and they describe a fight you do not recognize because they describe themselves as defending and you as attacking
- The same fight has been running for years and you cannot remember who started it the first time
- You feel attacked when your partner explains something they did, and you cannot articulate what about the explanation was the attack
- A defense you offer to clarify your position seems to make your partner angrier, not less angry
- You walk away from a fight feeling you were the one being attacked, only to learn your partner walked away feeling the same way

## Tone

Joe describes this with a mix of recognition and mild humor. The mechanism is so universal and so structurally absurd that naming it tends to produce relief. Most couples in a recurring fight recognize this immediately when it is named. The recognition itself is part of what allows the loop to be interrupted — once both people can see the structure, the defenses lose some of their compulsion.
