# Team Feels Oppressed by the Dynamic Rather Than Accountable for It

## The Pattern

In a super-functional team, members take responsibility for the team's dynamic itself, not just for their own work. They notice when a meeting is not working and they say so. They notice when the conversation is going somewhere unproductive and they redirect it. In a dysfunctional team, members feel oppressed by or controlled by the dynamic, as if the dynamic were a weather system that happens to them rather than a thing they participate in creating.

"People don't feel accountable for the team. They feel oppressed by or controlled by the team dynamic. So super functional teams, you'll hear somebody say on occasion, wow, this meeting's really not working for me. How do we make this meeting work better? You'll hear people say, oh, the way this conversation's going doesn't feel optimal. They're all taking responsibility for the dynamic in which the team interacts rather than thinking that the leader is responsible for it." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Recursive Trap

The most insidious form of this pattern: members of a dysfunctional team conclude "I'm in a dysfunctional team and there's nothing I can do about it." This conclusion is itself a piece of the dysfunction. The thought that one cannot do anything is the engine of doing nothing.

"The more accurate thing is I am participating in the dysfunction and the thing that, the idea that I can't do anything about it is a great part of the dysfunction." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The trap is not the member's fault -- no one is given the toolbox. The patterns of victimhood about team dynamics are not character flaws; they are the predictable result of being in dysfunctional environments without being taught the moves that would shift them.

"You never blame people for that 'cause nobody's given us the toolbox, right? It's like, okay, you're in a dysfunctional organization and you're the administrative assistant to the, you're low level on this team, nobody's told us, like, here are the 5 moves you can make to help that team become more functional, right?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## The Moves Available to Anyone

Joe lists a handful of moves that any team member can make, regardless of seniority. These are the toolbox the pattern presumes does not exist.

- "Wow, this meeting doesn't feel productive."
- "I notice that people seem to be interested in getting it right instead of getting to the best solution."
- "Who are the people in the meeting that should be here, and who are the people in the meeting that shouldn't be here? It's not a good use of their time."
- "Is this meeting a decision-making meeting, or is it an execution meeting, or is it a discovery meeting? What is our intention? What's the goal of the meeting?"

"Those kinds of things, some less, some more scary, are once scary, and most people don't know that that will make a huge difference. So once they've done it a couple of times, it's a lot less scary 'cause it's like, oh crap, that works. And everybody now respects me even more. But most people are scared to say it and/or just don't know to say it." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The discovery the moves enable: the team is more receptive to being shaped than the member believed, and the member's stature with the team often increases rather than decreases from making the move.

## The Side-Door Failure Mode

The member who has been bottling up the observation for months and then blurts it out under pressure produces a different result than the member who makes the move cleanly. The bottled version comes out harsh or under-confident; the team reacts defensively; the member concludes the move does not work and goes back to victimhood.

"If you're saying it, if you built it up so much and then you say it with harshness or you say it with a lack of confidence, you have a good chance of getting a suboptimal result." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The toolbox includes not just the moves but the state in which to make them. The condition that makes the move land is not the wording -- it is whether the speaker is in their own ground when they say it.

## Accountability Distributed, Not Concentrated

The functional version: the team holds itself accountable for its dynamics rather than waiting for the leader to do it. The leader can still hold people accountable; the difference is that they are not the only person who can.

"You'll also see this in accountability where the team holds themselves accountable instead of the leader being the only person to hold people accountable, that the team takes responsibility for their dynamics instead of just the leader taking responsibility for the team dynamics. And if I don't see that, then there's lots of very easy room for growth." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

This is one of the easiest dimensions on which a team can grow more functional, because the moves are small and the team is usually receptive once one person starts.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You attend the same draining meeting weekly and never say anything about it
- You complain to a peer after the meeting about what should have been said in the meeting
- You feel like the leader needs to fix the dynamic and resent that they don't
- You have a clear observation about what would help the team and assume it is not your place to offer it
- You believe the only way to escape the dynamic is to leave the team

This pattern often coexists with the concern [[i-feel-trapped]]. The trappedness is real-feeling, but the trap is mostly a story about what is not allowed.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A team member presents frustration with their team or leader and assigns the cause entirely outside themselves
- Someone says "there's nothing I can do about it" about a recurring team dynamic
- A coach sees the same complaint about a team across multiple sessions with no movement
- A leader is asking why their team waits for them to solve every problem
- A coach is working with a low-status team member who feels powerless

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[fault-vs-responsibility]]: the deeper distinction that lets a person take responsibility without taking blame
- Toward the specific moves above, treated as small experiments
- Toward [[deconstruct-the-victim-to-release-anger]] when the victimhood is fueled by suppressed anger
- Toward [[fear-triangle]] when the team member is locked in a role (victim of the dysfunction, savior trying to fix it, bully venting about it)
- Toward [[five-star-meeting-commitment]] when the person is in a leadership role and can use the practice
- Toward the recognition that the leader cannot make a team functional unilaterally; functionality requires distributed accountability

## Tone

Joe is careful not to blame the team member. The pattern is named with sympathy because it is the predictable outcome of inadequate training, not a character defect. At the same time, the way out is named: the moves exist, they work, and the member can start.
