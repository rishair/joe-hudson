# Athletes Unlocking Body Intelligence in Business (the Click)

## The Pattern

A recurring pattern in coaching former athletes who have become executives, leaders, or knowledge workers. The athlete has highly developed body intelligence from their sport: the capacity to read a moment, time a movement, sense a teammate's position, respond to the field without conscious deliberation. In their playing years, this intelligence was operational and trusted.

Then they move into business. The new context does not have the same somatic feedback loops. The intelligence that ran the field appears to have nowhere to go. The athlete defaults to the cognitive modes the business context seems to require: analysis, preparation, strategy, planning. The body intelligence sits unused.

Joe's observation across multiple coaching engagements with former athletes:

"What I notice in that work is that there's this moment where it clicks, that they have a body and an emotional intelligence that they can access the same way they would access it on the court or on the field. And when they can access it that way, in business, it's like a superpower gets unlocked for them." -- Joe Hudson, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The pattern: the athlete already has the intelligence. The coaching work is not to develop it. The coaching work is to surface that the same intelligence transfers, and to support the recognition that the boardroom, the negotiation, the difficult conversation, can be navigated with the same body sense that the court was navigated with.

## What the Click Looks Like

The click is recognizable across cases. It typically involves several components arriving together:

1. The athlete recognizes that a felt sense they had on the field is still available in their body now.
2. The athlete tests the felt sense in a low-stakes business situation and finds it gives them information.
3. The athlete recognizes that they have been overriding the felt sense in business situations because the business context did not seem to call for it.
4. The athlete starts trusting the felt sense as a primary input, alongside the analysis.
5. The "superpower" report: the combined use of cognitive and embodied intelligence produces an effectiveness in business that was not available with cognition alone.

The click is usually fast once the conditions are present. The intelligence does not need to be installed. It needs to be recognized.

## Why the Pattern Recurs

The pattern recurs for structural reasons:

**Sport selects for body intelligence.** People who reach high levels in athletics have developed a particular kind of attention to their body's signals. The selection pressure is real; people without it do not reach those levels.

**Business culture devalues body intelligence.** Most business cultures privilege analysis, data, and verbal articulation. The athlete entering business is being trained to use the modes the culture rewards, not the modes they already developed.

**The transfer is not obvious.** The body intelligence that ran the field used a different vocabulary than the cognitive analysis that runs the boardroom. The athlete may not see that the same instrument is available for both. The cognitive translation is what makes the transfer visible.

**The coach often shares a body-intelligence orientation.** Joe's own orientation (see [[head-heart-gut]] and [[staying-in-your-body]]) puts him in a position to recognize the intelligence in the athlete and to name its transferability. A coach without that orientation might never name the pattern, and the athlete might spend a career operating with only the cognitive intelligence the new context seemed to require.

## What This Pattern Means for Coaching Former Athletes

When a coach is working with a former athlete who has moved into business:

- Use body metaphors. The athlete has the somatic vocabulary already. Speaking in body terms gives them an entry point that abstractions do not. See [[head-heart-gut]]: "If I'm coaching a CEO who was an athlete, I'll use the body metaphor all the time because they get it."
- Ask about the felt sense, not just the analysis. "What does your body know about this deal?" gets a different answer than "what do you think about this deal?"
- Name the click when it is happening. The athlete often does not realize they have made the transfer; the coach can name what just happened so the recognition is conscious.
- Use sport examples for general teachings. The athlete's pattern-recognition is calibrated to athletic situations. A teaching anchored to an athletic example tends to land more directly than the same teaching anchored to a generic example.
- Look for over-preparation. The athlete-turned-executive often over-prepares for the same reason they over-prepared for sport: anxiety. The body-intelligence access that calmed the over-preparation on the field can do the same work in business, once the transfer is visible.

## Aaron's Particular Case

Aaron Taylor's autobiographical report is a case where the click happened around a specific event rather than as a gradual recognition. The story is documented in [[aaron-wife-fumble-coaching]]. The salient feature for this pattern article: Aaron, despite a 14-year broadcasting career and a Hall of Fame football career, reported that the broadcast that worked was the *only* time he had ever accessed body intelligence in the broadcast context. The pattern Joe described -- that the intelligence is available and the coaching work is to surface its availability -- was confirmed by Aaron's report that he had been unable to access it for the first 13 years of his broadcasting career.

This is not an indictment of Aaron. It is the pattern in operation. The intelligence was there. Nothing in the broadcast culture invited him to use it. The wife's intervention (which used body-level language deliberately; see [[aaron-wife-fumble-coaching]]) provided the bridge that the culture had not.

The implication for the pattern: athletes-in-business may have the intelligence dormant for a very long time before something or someone surfaces its availability. The click is not automatic. The coaching work is to provide the conditions for it.

## Where This Pattern Touches Other Patterns

The pattern intersects with several others in Joe's work:

- [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]: the click is a recognition of authenticity, not the addition of improvement. The intelligence was already there.
- [[undoing-not-doing]]: the work is undoing the override of the body intelligence, not adding a new skill.
- [[feel-over-figure]]: the click moves the athlete from figure-first to feel-first.
- [[decisions-are-emotional]]: the athlete who recognizes their body intelligence as a decision-making input is doing what the neuroscience says all decision-makers actually do, just consciously.
- [[fear-vs-excitement]]: the athlete who can feel the difference between bracing and flow on the field can, once the transfer is named, feel the same difference in a pitch or negotiation.

## When This Pattern Does Not Apply

- The former athlete whose body intelligence was about a narrow technical skill (a particular throwing motion, a specific endurance pattern) that does not transfer to general body-reading. The pattern requires that the sport developed broader body-sensing, not just technique.
- The athlete who is in active trauma response from injury or burnout, where the body has become unsafe and approaching it is the work, not transferring it.
- The athlete who has explicitly chosen to leave the body-intelligence mode behind because of its association with the playing identity. In that case, the pattern's invitation can land as an unwanted return to the past.

## The Generalization

The pattern is most visible in athletes because of the strong contrast between their previous and current contexts. The pattern operates more quietly in other groups whose previous training developed body intelligence: dancers, actors, musicians, surgeons, certain craftspeople, certain therapists. In each case, the same dynamic can be present: highly developed body intelligence from the previous context, sitting dormant in the current context because the current context did not seem to call for it.

The coaching move is the same: name the intelligence, name its availability, support the transfer. The "superpower unlock" report is similar across these groups, not just for athletes.

## The Pattern Applied to Felt-Sense Access, Not Just Business

A specific extension of the pattern: the same body intelligence that runs an athlete's sport is also what a person uses to feel their emotions, feel their body, and notice the inner state at all. When an athlete reports they cannot feel their emotions or their body, the pattern operates in a tighter form than the business version: the very intelligence the person uses every day in their sport is what they are saying they don't have.

The coaching move in this version is captured in [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]]. The form: name the felt difference between in-flow and not-in-flow in the sport, name that the in-flow state IS body intelligence, ask the person to do that internal move in the conversation now. The transfer is from the field into the chair, not from the field into the boardroom.

A live example: a tennis coach reported he couldn't feel his body — "everything seemed to be kind of from a heady space, and I feel nothing." After Joe ran the [[attention-is-feeling]] sequence to rebuild the operational definition of feeling, the transfer happened through tennis:

"If you can play tennis, you definitely can feel your body, or you wouldn't be able to play tennis. The state you get into when you're in flow — like, you're in your body, right, in that state. So just do that, but just be doing it while we're sitting here, not playing tennis." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Feel My Body?

The tennis coach briefly entered the state during the conversation. Joe named it ("you just did it. It was like you were in that flow state in your talk"). The recognition was the same recognition Aaron Taylor had after his 14 years of broadcasting without access: the intelligence was always available; the field's call to use it was missing.

The closing image of the session — "you're playing tennis in life" — is the compressed pattern. The athletic body intelligence transfers into the conversation, the relationship, the meeting, the moment of stillness. The same instrument. Different context.

## Variants Across Domains

The pattern applies to any developed body-intelligence practice, and the coaching move adapts. The mastery domain need not be a sport.

- **Tennis coach who couldn't feel his body:** flow state on the court as the template for body intelligence in life (How Do I Feel My Body?).
- **Former NFL player turned broadcaster:** body intelligence on the field as the template for connection during the broadcast (Aaron Taylor's coaching with Joe).
- **CEO who was an athlete:** body metaphors in coaching because the somatic vocabulary already exists.
- **Musician, dancer, surgeon, craftsperson:** the felt sense that runs the performance, the dance, the operation, the craft is the same felt sense the person can use anywhere else.

The structural move in each case: name the in-flow state in the domain, name that the in-flow state IS body intelligence, ask the person to do that internal move in the current context. The domain shifts; the structural move does not.

## Tone

When Joe names this pattern in conversation with a former athlete, the tone is recognition, not instruction. He is describing what he has seen across multiple cases and asking whether it matches the athlete's experience. The naming is offered for the athlete to test, not asserted as the answer.

The tone matters because the pattern can land as condescension if delivered as "here's what you do not know about yourself." Delivered as "here's what I have noticed across people in your position; does it match?" it lands as observation and invites the click.
