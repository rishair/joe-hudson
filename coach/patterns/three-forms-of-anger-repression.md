# Three Forms of Anger Repression

## The Pattern

Anger that cannot move cleanly comes out in one of three directions. All three are repression. The differences in shape come from how the system learned to kink the energy.

"There's kind of 3 ways that it goes, right? There's the anger inwards. So that's like the self-abusive talk. There's the anger sideways, right? Which is kind of passive aggression. It's like, I'm angry, but I'm not allowed to be angry. So I do a whole bunch of things. And then the other kind of repressed anger is getting angry at somebody." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

This is the operational taxonomy of [[kinked-emotions]] applied specifically to anger. The garden hose is kinked in one of three predictable ways.

## The Three Forms

### Form 1: Anger Inward (Self-Abuse)

The anger is turned on the self. The self-talk is the visible form.

- Abusive self-talk: "you're an idiot," "what is wrong with you," "you always screw this up"
- Chronic self-criticism that runs in the background
- Low-grade depression and dysthymia
- The body holding tension that punishes itself

"One of the quickest ways to help people with low-level dysthymia, depression, is just allow them to move their anger." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The signature: the self is the target. The energy that would have moved out instead bites inward.

### Form 2: Anger Sideways (Passive Aggression)

The anger is real and aimed at a person, but the form denies it. The person doing it usually does not see it as anger.

- Passive aggression: subtle undermining, being late, not taking out the trash
- Guilting another person
- "Getting sad at" someone to produce behavior change
- Blaming the other person for your emotional experience
- Codependent caretaking that creates obligation
- Freezing in your own head
- Stonewalling and cold withdrawal

"Guilting somebody is another great example of passive aggression. [...] When you're being sad at somebody, in a weird way, that is passive aggression." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The signature: the aggression is real, the form is deniable. The person being passive-aggressive feels stuck, oppressed, like they have no choice. They do not recognize the form as aggression. See [[passive-aggressive-vs-aggressive-aggressive]].

### Form 3: Anger Outward (Yelling, Rage)

The anger is named and aimed openly at a person.

- Yelling, raging
- "Rah, rah, rah, rah" at the person in front of you
- Taking your anger out on someone

"Like that is repressed anger. That's anger not coming out clearly either." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The misconception: this looks like the un-repressed form. It is not. It is anger that has been accumulated and is being dumped at a person instead of being released cleanly. The release is at someone, which is the kink. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]].

## Why This Is the Operational Taxonomy

The diagnostic value: each form needs different coaching, different self-work, and different mind-traps to watch for. Lumping all "anger problems" together loses the leverage. Sorting by form provides the leverage.

For the AI coach, recognizing which form a person uses determines:
- What mind-tricks they will deploy to block release (see below)
- What will go wrong when they do release (see [[three-things-that-go-wrong-when-releasing-anger]])
- What practice tweaks they need

## Mind Tricks by Form

The voice in the head deploys different blocks depending on which form is habitual.

**For Form 1 (Inward) and Form 2 (Sideways):** the mind tells the person they are stuck and alone.

"The way that the brain is talking to you in that is like, you believe that you're stuck, you believe that you're alone in it. And that's how the brain convinces you to repress it in that way because stuck just means that you don't want imagined consequences. So like, I'm stuck because I can't tell my wife that she's a crappy cook 'cause then she'll get mad at me." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The trick: "stuck" is reframed as a fact about the world. The truth is "I do not want the imagined consequence." The stuckness is the story, not the situation.

**For Form 3 (Outward):** the mind tells the person they are ignorant for losing control.

"You'll hear your brain telling yourself you're ignorant. If you've lost control and you're angry, you're ignorant." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The trick: shame about the outburst becomes the gate that keeps the anger from being worked with cleanly next time. The shame produces a cycle in which the next release is also dirty because it has the shame loaded on top.

## Mind Tricks That Apply to All Three

Whichever form a person uses, the mind has additional general blocks:

- **The compassion jump.** "I should be compassionate. They had their reasons." This is not false. It is order-of-operations wrong. The compassion arrives naturally after the anger moves; if it comes first, the anger does not move. See [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]].
- **The reasonableness jump.** "Their point of view is totally reasonable." Same structure: true in retrospect, sabotaging if it arrives before the anger has moved.
- **The moral block.** "Anger is bad. Good people are happy and don't get angry." Common for those with religious or moralistic conditioning.

"My mind is gonna start saying things like, well, you know, Brett's point of view, it's totally reasonable to see it his way. [...] And then the other one, which is true, I'm not saying that that's not true, by the way. It's just, it's an order of operations thing. If you do that first, then you're not gonna get through the anger." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

## The Coaching Leverage

Asking which form is dominant for the person opens specific paths:

- **If Form 1 (inward):** the work is releasing the anger somewhere external. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]]. The body needs to discover that anger does not have to point at the self.
- **If Form 2 (sideways):** the work is making the anger visible to the person themselves first. Passive-aggressors usually do not know they are doing it. The first move is the [[invite-the-aggression-out]] move applied to the self: "what if you were just directly angry about this?" See [[passive-aggressive-vs-aggressive-aggressive]].
- **If Form 3 (outward):** the work is moving the release away from the person. The body and voice can do the same release at the ocean or in the car, and the relational damage stops. See [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]] for the stance that prevents getting captured by the story while letting the anger move.

Most people use more than one form. They have a dominant one in a given context (with partners, with bosses, with family) but use different forms in different contexts.

## The Pair Dynamic

Two people with different forms often lock into each other.

"One person feels oppressed by the anger that's coming out, right? So one person in the relationship, someone's like, 'Rah!' That form of repressed anger makes somebody else feel oppressed. The other person's form of repressed anger, which is like, 'I'm fricking outta here,' freeze, like, then that makes this person feel abandoned, and they both feel stuck. And they're both angry, but nobody's actually just allowing that free flow of anger to happen." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The outward-anger person feels abandoned by the freezing/withdrawing person. The freezing/withdrawing person feels attacked by the outward-anger person. Both forms of repressed anger reinforce the other. Each form is the trigger for the other form. Naming this dynamic in the room is often the move that unblocks the pair.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Notices they cannot stop the self-abusive voice and feels worn down
- Has been told repeatedly they are being passive-aggressive but does not see it
- Has explosive outbursts followed by deep shame
- Lives with someone whose anger style triggers their anger style
- Cannot identify where their anger goes but knows it goes somewhere

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When chronic depression or dysthymia is present and the person reports they "do not really get angry."
- When a partner reports the person is passive-aggressive and the person rejects the label.
- When a person reports they explode and then hate themselves for it.
- When two people are stuck in a cycle and each blames the other's anger style.
