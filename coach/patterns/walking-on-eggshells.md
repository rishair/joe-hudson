# Walking on Eggshells

## The Pattern

A relationship organizes itself around preventing one or both partners from being triggered. Each person edits what they say, hides parts of themselves, and modulates their behavior so the other person stays comfortable. The relationship feels manageable. It is also slowly killing both people.

The pattern is most insidious because it does not look like dysfunction. It looks like "we don't fight." It looks like a "low-conflict" marriage. The cost is hidden until it has accumulated for years.

"I can't bring this part of myself, this part of myself can't be accepted here. This part of myself has consequences. And so on some level, you know that you're not being seen and you're not being loved." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## How It Looks From Inside

Both partners are consciously trying to maintain love by hiding what would disturb it. Each thinks they are doing something protective and generous: I am holding this part of myself back to keep us okay. Neither realizes the hidden part is what produces the resentment, the disdain, and the eventual collapse.

"Usually people don't consciously realize that they are feeling unloved. And so it just can go on for a really long time. Often they think that they're doing something so that they can maintain the loving feelings in the relationship." -- Joe Hudson (paraphrasing Alexa), Triggered

This is the central deception of the pattern. The behavior that feels like care is the behavior that produces the deadness.

## How It Ends

Joe describes the trajectory: either the relationship ends "all of a sudden" -- the partner one day realizing they were in a happy marriage and now it is just over -- or it devolves into chronic disdain, contempt, or low-grade pissedness.

"Whether it's just all of a sudden, wait, I was in a happy marriage and now it's just over, or whether it devolves into disdain or something like that, that's how I see that end up." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The couple Joe describes in the transcript: 16 years married, kids, and their entire job had been to make sure the other person was not triggered. The result: "they're just pissed all the time, all the time at each other. I don't even know if they're aware of it, but it's like that never got the expression in the relationship. And you just look at their faces and it's just like, wow, you guys are both pissed all the time."

## The Mechanism

The mechanism is identical to [[subjugation-prevents-healing]] applied to dyadic relationships. When a person sacrifices parts of themselves to maintain group (or pair) cohesion, the parts do not disappear. They go underground. They convert into resentment, passive aggression, or numbness. The system that was supposed to protect connection actively destroys it.

The same mechanism that produces resentment in a "happy" marriage is the mechanism by which the relationship's [[trigger]] material never gets processed. Without expression, the trigger stays installed. The triggers do not get smaller. They get more numerous, because every adjacent topic becomes a new trigger to walk around.

## The Diagnostic

The pattern is often invisible to the people inside it. The diagnostic is not "do we fight?" The diagnostic is closer to: do you have thoughts you do not say because of how your partner would react?

"Notice any time that you hesitate to say anything because your partner is gonna have an emotional reaction or your partner is too weak to handle it or because you're trying to protect your partner... any way that you're not actually -- that you have a thought to say something and you don't say it, those are great places to find the triggers." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

If the answer is "constantly" or even "regularly," the pattern is active. See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] for the practice that addresses this directly.

## The Eggshells Diagnostic for Conflict Avoidance

The eggshells image is a specific marker for [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing|conflict avoidance]] when the question is about the avoider's own life rather than the relationship dynamic. Joe gives the marker plainly:

"Typically one of the ways to really notice there's conflict avoidance happening is you, what I call walking on eggshells, where you're like careful of the other person's mood and you're trying to manage the other person's mood and what happens if you don't." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

If you can name a person whose mood you are managing -- whose reaction you organize your speech and behavior around -- that mood-management is the trail back to a conflict you are not having. The practice from here is to visualize the consequences of stopping the management and to feel through every emotion that arises. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] for the practice. The fear underneath the management is what holds the eggshells pattern in place.

## Why People Do It

The frame is almost always "I am protecting them." See [[you-think-they-cant-handle-it]]. The hidden frame is "I am protecting myself from their reaction." The hidden-hidden frame is "I do not yet have the capacity to be with their emotion or my own." See [[dont-make-it-your-job-to-prevent-their-triggers]].

The path out is not heroic disclosure. It is gradual practice with saying what is true, learning to do it kindly, and growing the capacity to be present with the partner's reaction without abandoning the self.

## The Self-Sealing Quality

This pattern reinforces itself. Once both people are suppressing, both are less and less in touch with what they actually want, and so less and less able to articulate it. The longer the pattern runs, the harder it is to surface what is being suppressed. By year 16, neither person remembers what they wanted to say in year three.

The first articulation breaks something. It also tends to terrify both partners, because the room is so unaccustomed to actual content. Joe's frame for this: "It took like a couple months for them just to see that the work is say what you want, be yourself, don't worry about the consequences, don't get angry at each other."

## What Joe Means By "Get Angry Elsewhere"

The corrective is not "yell at your partner." Joe is explicit: see [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]]. The pattern is broken by saying the suppressed thing, not by discharging accumulated anger at the partner who happens to be standing there. Anger gets moved in the car, in the woods, in a private space, before it shows up in the room. Then the conversation in the room can be kind without being hedged.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A relationship that "rarely fights" and feels slightly flat
- A partner you cannot bring certain topics to
- A constant low-grade calculation of what is safe to say
- A growing sense of not being loved that the person cannot point to specifically
- A pattern of suddenly noticing how much you have not been saying
- The realization, after years, that you have been hiding the version of yourself that you most are
- Persistent low-grade pissedness or contempt that has no specific trigger

## Tone

Joe holds this pattern with sadness and matter-of-factness. He does not condemn the people inside it. He treats it as one of the most common and most tragic configurations he sees. The "wow, you guys are both pissed all the time" line is delivered with the recognition that they did not know, and that the work to undo it is doable but takes time.
