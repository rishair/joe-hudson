# Three Signals of an Unfelt Emotion

## The Pattern

When an emotion is unfelt, the system tries to find ways to manage what it will not feel. Three observable signals reliably indicate that an emotion is being avoided. Any one of them is sufficient evidence.

1. **Recursive thought loops.** The same thought keeps returning. It does not resolve, no matter how many times it is reviewed.
2. **Lack of clarity in a pending decision.** A decision needs to be made and the person cannot get clear, even with sufficient information.
3. **Judgment of another person.** A persistent negative read on someone the person cannot shake.

"If you notice that you keep on looping on the same thought, it means there's an emotion that you're not feeling. If you are not clear about a decision you need to make, then there's an emotion you're not feeling. If you are judgmental towards another person, there's an emotion you're not feeling." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## Why Each Signal Works

### Recursive Loops

A thought loop is the mind trying to think its way past a felt experience that wants to come through. The intellect cannot do the feeling's job. So it keeps running the same circuit. The loop will not break until the emotion underneath moves.

A common surface form: "Should I do X or not do X?" running repeatedly with no resolution despite no new information. The decision is not stuck because of insufficient data. It is stuck because the emotion underneath the data has not been felt.

### Lack of Clarity in Decisions

This is the externalized version of the recursive loop. Clear decisions require clean emotional data. When an emotion is being avoided, the data is contaminated by the avoidance. The person cannot tell what they want because the wanting is mixed with the not-wanting-to-feel. See [[decisions-are-emotional]].

Clients who learn this signal often report recognizing it retroactively in years of stuck decisions. Carla Piñeyro Sublett, a CMO who suppressed emotion for decades as a survival strategy in corporate America, describes how she eventually noticed the pattern:

"I started to realize that decision-making became difficult. I started to loop and get stuck. And I learned through this work that that was a sign of emotion that needed to be moved. And it was extremely clearing for me once I had let myself have access, once I learned to have access to things like fear and grief and anger. It was immensely clarifying, and it enabled me to make better decisions." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The recognition matters: the difficulty in deciding was not a deficiency in her analytic capacity. It was the body trying to push an unfelt emotion through. Once she gave the emotion access, the decision clarified. The clarification was not the result of more thinking. It was the result of less suppression.

### Judgment of Another Person

Judgment is the most relational of the three signals and often the hardest to recognize. The mind frames the judgment as accurate perception: "they really are uptight." But every judgment that will not release is reliably traceable to an emotion the judger does not want to feel.

"If I judge you for being angry, I don't want to feel out of control. If I judge you for being angry, it's because I don't want to feel the potential that I have to lose you. If I judge you for being uptight, it's 'cause I don't want to feel controlled or I don't want to feel rigid." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

The judgment is the system's way of pushing the unfelt emotion out into the world. See [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]]. The judgment is the leak.

## A Fourth Signal: Binary Thinking

Joe names a related signal: when the mind frames a situation as binary ("I either buy the car or I don't"), there is fear underneath that is not being felt. The binary frame is the cognitive shape of avoided fear.

"Whenever you're in that binary black or white thinking, then you know that there's some fear there that's not being felt." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

See [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]] for full treatment.

## The Public-Facing Expansion: Guilt and Dissociation as Additional Signals

In the public-facing teaching aimed at beginners, the same diagnostic logic extends to two further signals. The expanded list of five — judgment, indecision, guilt, dissociation, recursive loops — all share the same structure. Each is the system finding a way to handle what it will not feel. Each is "toxic" in a specific way: each one carries a felt cost in addition to the cost of the unfelt emotion underneath.

"This video is going to be all about the 4 ways that we can see we're holding back emotions and the toxic effects of them, and then we're going to talk about how to feel the emotions in such a way that we're not holding them back and managing them, but we're not being swept away by them." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

The expanded list is the entry-point version. The three-signal version is the precise diagnostic. Both name the same underlying mechanism: when an emotion is being avoided, something else takes the channel. The expanded list adds the two channels that show up most visibly to beginners.

### Guilt as Signal

Guilt cuts off natural compassion. It converts something the body wants to do into an obligation. The person ends up doing things for others not from the heart but because they feel they have to. See [[guilt-cuts-off-natural-compassion]] for the toxic-effect treatment. The signal that an emotion is being avoided: the person is acting from guilt rather than from felt care.

"What guilt does is it cuts you off from your natural compassion. That's what makes it toxic. It makes something, doing something for somebody else, an obligation, rather than actually coming from your heart, actually coming from a place of care." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

The diagnostic question that follows the same shape as the others: "What do I have to feel if I couldn't feel guilty?"

### Dissociation as Signal

Dissociation is the system leaving itself because it thinks it is going to be overwhelmed by the emotional process. It shows up as trancing out, intellectualizing, going up into the head, becoming the person nobody can quite remember in the room. The toxic effect: dissociation slows down transformation dramatically because the body's signals are no longer being read.

"The thing about it is, is that it really slows down a process of transformation. It really slows down how quickly you can figure something out, because in dissociation, you're not reading the signals in your body. It's like, imagine trying to play tennis and not read the signals in your body. It'd be really hard to learn how to play tennis." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

The tennis metaphor is the operational frame: the body is the signal-reader, and a person who has left the body has lost the instrument by which learning happens. See [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]] and [[disassociating-in-the-coaching-room]] for the related reads.

### The Toxic-Effect Frame

The public-facing teaching adds a layer to the three-signal diagnostic by naming the specific felt cost of each signal:

- **Judgment disconnects.** It cuts off connection between people who otherwise love each other. The Thanksgiving-dinner political-judgment example demonstrates: a family that genuinely cared about each other lost the connection because nobody wanted to feel the grief, the heartbreak, the helplessness of disagreement. They reached for judgment instead. See [[judgment-hurts-the-judger]] for the somatic version of the cost.
- **Indecision destroys clarity.** Analysis paralysis is the operational form. See the divorce example in [[decisions-are-emotional]] — the question is not "should I stay?" but "what would I have to feel if I stayed / left?"
- **Guilt cuts off natural compassion.** See [[guilt-cuts-off-natural-compassion]].
- **Dissociation cuts off the body's learning channel.** The tennis metaphor.

Each toxic effect also takes joy away. This is the unifying point. "Every time that we're feeling a sense of judgment towards another person, what's happening is that we are not allowing ourselves to feel the underlying emotion." Each signal is one of the muscles the body has to contract to stop feeling. The contraction blocks the joy that would otherwise be flowing. See [[joy-as-matriarch]].

### The Thanksgiving Political-Judgment Example

A worked instance of judgment-as-emotion-avoidance from the same public-facing teaching. The setup: a family at Thanksgiving dinner with members on opposite sides of the political aisle. Everyone wanted to avoid the conversation. The conversation became inevitable. When it happened, the family shut each other out.

"You could just see they wanted to not have the conversation, but when they had the conversation, which was inevitable because they were all itching, they just shut each other out. And so here was this family that clearly loved each other on so many levels, and they would just stop the connection. It's really devastating when you think about how many homes have their connection cut off from each other because they have this judgment of, you don't understand, if you only got it right, if you could see my point of view. But instead of feeling the hurt of that disconnection, instead of feeling the helplessness that often comes with politics, what they did instead was they judged one another. They got hard. They tightened up." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

The structural reading: the love between the family members was real. The disagreement was real. The unfelt emotion was the grief, hurt, and helplessness about not being able to bridge the disagreement. Each member reached for judgment as the way to not feel the grief. The judgment cut the connection. The connection-loss was the cost. Each member was paying it to avoid feeling the heartbreak.

The example also functions as a permission frame. "It doesn't mean I have to agree with you to get the wisdom. It just means I can't cut you off to get the wisdom." Disagreement is not the problem. Judgment is the problem. A person can hold a strong contrary position without judging the other person. The judgment is the move that closes the channel.

### The Retreat-Person-Nobody-Could-Remember Example

A worked instance of dissociation from the same teaching. At an early retreat, the staff held nightly check-ins about each participant. One person kept getting forgotten in the rounds. Three days in, the staff realized why.

"After 3 days of this, I realized, oh, this person is like dissociated so much they've disappeared from the group. They're the ones that nobody can remember. And very quiet person and very just not there in a lot of ways. And what we also realized is that they were making the least amount of progress of everybody else in the group. And it was because they were literally leaving their body. So they didn't get the signals of this is uncomfortable, this is good, this works for me, this is a lesson to be learned. All of those signals were not getting were not getting processed because they weren't actually feeling their body." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

The structural reading: dissociation is the disappearance of the person from the field. The disappearance is not just relational; it is informational. The body's continuous signal-stream — this works, this hurts, this is the lesson — stops getting read. Without that stream, learning slows to a crawl. The retreat participant was present in body but absent in the body. The work was to put attention back into the body's sensations.

The corrective the staff used was repeated pointing-back-to-the-body. "How does that feel in your body? How does that feel in your stomach? What does that feel like in your chest?" The progress, once the person started feeling the body, was rapid. This is the same operational answer as way 3 of [[four-tactical-ways-to-feel-your-emotions]] — feel it in the body.

## How to Use These Signals

The signals are reads. When any one is present, the coaching path is not to engage the surface content. It is to ask what emotion is being avoided.

- For a recursive loop: "What might you feel if you let the loop drop?"
- For a stuck decision: "What would you feel if you made the wrong choice?"
- For a judgment: "What might you feel if this person were not the way you say they are?"
- For guilt: "What would I have to feel if I couldn't feel guilty?"
- For dissociation: "What is this emotion I'm feeling that apparently is overwhelming?"

The work is feeling the emotion, not solving the content. Once the emotion moves, the loop drops, the decision clarifies, the judgment dissolves, the guilt releases, or the body comes back online.

## The Inventory-And-Inquiry Protocol

A two-step procedure Joe offers in the public-facing teaching. The procedure works on any of the signals.

**Step 1: Inventory.** Write down ten recent instances of the signal. Ten times the person was judgmental, or ten decisions that have been stuck, or ten times they acted from guilt, or ten times they checked out.

**Step 2: Ask the avoided-emotion question for each.** "What would I have to feel if I couldn't be judging myself or if I couldn't be judging this person?" "What are the things I'm scared to feel as a consequence of my decision?" "If I couldn't feel guilt, what would I have to feel?" "What is this emotion I'm feeling that apparently is overwhelming?"

The inventory matters because the signal is often invisible until it is named multiple times. A single judgment, decision, or guilt-instance feels justified by its specific content. Ten of them, listed together, reveals the pattern. The pattern is the signal that the system is avoiding something.

Then the second step does the actual work: not to argue with the surface content of the signal but to find the emotion the signal is making invisible. Then feel the emotion. The inventory is the way to make sure the inquiry has actual material to work on rather than abstract intent.

The protocol pairs naturally with the [[emotional-inquiry]] practice as the work that gets done once the avoided emotion is located.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Caught in the same thought spiral for days
- Cannot decide despite having had weeks to think
- Cannot stop disliking a person whose presence somehow disturbs them
- Framing every choice as a binary
