# Good-Jobbed as a Child

## The Pattern

The childhood installation that produces imposter syndrome in adulthood: the child receives praise and love conditional on performance. "Good job!" comes when they bring home good grades, when they do the dishes, when they perform well at the recital, when they are useful. The child learns that worth is what you do, that love is what you earn through producing what the parent values.

"You know, a lot of us, I think, were -- I'll call it good jobbed as a kid... It is some version of you need to get good grades to get my love, you need to perform, you need to do the dishes, you need to do X, Y, and Z for me to give you love and approval." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The "good job" is not the problem in itself. The problem is the structural pattern it lives inside: love and approval are coming as a reward for output, and not arriving (or arriving in reduced form) when the output is absent. The child cannot easily distinguish between "you did well at this thing" and "you are lovable because you did well at this thing." The latter is what gets internalized.

## What Gets Installed

The installation has several components, all of which set the conditions for adult imposter syndrome:

1. **Worth is conditional.** "I am worthy because I produce." The corollary: if I do not produce, I am not worthy. The child internalizes that being itself is not enough; doing is required.

2. **The standard is external.** What counts as "good" comes from outside -- from the parent's metrics, the school's metrics, society's metrics. The child does not learn to evaluate their own experience; they learn to optimize for external grading.

3. **Self-reflection is unavailable.** The child does not learn to ask themselves "how did that feel? what was that like for me?" because the question that was modeled was "did I do well by the metric?" The internal experience of doing the thing becomes invisible; only the rating matters.

4. **Performance becomes the love-delivery mechanism.** The child cannot relax into being loved-for-existing because love arrives through producing. The producing has to keep happening, or the love stops.

These are exactly the structural prerequisites for adult imposter syndrome. The adult is now running a high-stakes version of the same loop: produce or be unworthy, optimize for external metrics, ignore internal experience, perform-for-love. The imposter feeling is the body's report that the loop is running.

## The Alternative Modeling

Joe describes the alternative: parents who, when their child did a painting, asked "how did that feel to paint that painting?" instead of "good job!" The question redirects the child to their own internal experience of the activity. The child learns to evaluate from inside, not from outside, and learns that what they are interested in is how the experience landed, not whether the output meets a metric.

"If we're working with our kids, especially when they were younger and they did a painting, we weren't like, 'Good job!' We said, 'Oh, how did that feel to paint that painting?' So that they could reflect, so they could see their own experience and relate to their own experience." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The same approach generalizes. If the kid is doom-scrolling, the question is not "bad job, stop that." It is "how does it feel to doom-scroll? What's going on in your system? What does that feel like? What makes you want to feel that way?" The child is being trained to notice their own experience and relate to it directly, rather than being trained to optimize behaviors to earn approval.

The kids raised with this modeling do not develop the conditional-worth structure that produces imposter syndrome later. They have an internal compass for evaluating their experience that does not require external metrics. When they enter performance-graded domains as adults, the not-knowing feels neutral or interesting, not threatening, because it is not adjudicated against a worth-determining metric.

See [[replace-good-job-with-how-did-it-feel]] for the parenting move.

## The Reason the Pattern Persists

The pattern is hard to interrupt because it works -- at least in the short term, for the metrics it is optimizing. Good-jobbed kids often become high achievers. They get the grades, they get the awards, they get the jobs. The strategy of performing for love produces visible success.

"That's one way. Yeah, that's one way to do it. It does work. Yeah, it does work if they don't become drug addicts." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The cost is paid downstream. The high achievement is accompanied by imposter syndrome, shame, abandonment of self, and often a life trajectory that ends in midlife crisis when the produced-success-without-internal-compass collides with the realization that the person never knew what they wanted.

"Once I have my $10 million, I'm gonna finally, you know, retire. Maybe if I'm lucky I get it or maybe I don't and I'm 65 and I retire and I'm like, yeah, I'm fricking miserable. Yeah, so if you want to raise your kids as high achieving miserable people, yay, go for it." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The pattern is also self-perpetuating across generations. The good-jobbed adult becomes the good-jobbing parent because the structure they internalized is the only one available to them. The cycle continues unless someone interrupts it through awareness and a deliberate change of modeling.

## When This Pattern Shows Up

The adult version is visible in:

- Imposter syndrome in performance-graded domains (see [[imposter-syndrome-only-attaches-to-performance-identities]])
- Doubt loops that won't resolve regardless of achievement
- High-achiever burnout
- The "I have to be valuable to be loved" structure underneath people-pleasing and codependent service (see [[open-hearted-service-vs-codependent-service]])
- Inability to enjoy success when it arrives because the worth-via-output structure cannot register anything other than the next production
- The "midlife crisis" of realizing the achievement was on the wrong axis

When the coach hears these in a client's report, the upstream pattern of good-jobbed-childhood is often visible if asked about. The naming of the source can produce immediate relief -- the client realizes the loop is not about them or their current competence, it is a structural residue of a long-installed pattern.

## The Repair

The repair is not to undo the childhood. It is to install the alternative modeling for the self now. The adult learns to ask themselves the same question their parents did not ask: "how did that feel?" The internal-experience-as-data orientation replaces the metric-as-data orientation that the good-jobbing installed.

This is one of the central moves in the work. See [[how-did-that-feel]] (if it exists) and the various practices around accessing internal experience. Combined with the open-hearted service prescription (see [[open-hearted-service-cures-imposter-syndrome]]), the repair gradually relocates worth from output to being.

The repair is also intergenerational. The adult who has done this work and has children can interrupt the pattern by giving their children the alternative modeling that they did not receive. See [[parenting-as-the-most-effective-change-agent]] and [[replace-good-job-with-how-did-it-feel]].

## Gender Note

The pattern is installed somewhat differently by gender in the upper-middle-class American context Joe is describing. Boys are often given conditional worth via "you need to impact the world / be successful / provide for your family." Girls are often given conditional worth via "your value is being of value to others." Both are versions of the same structural installation; the specific metrics differ. See [[imposter-syndrome-only-attaches-to-performance-identities]] for how this maps to the differential expression of imposter syndrome.

## Tone

Direct, with a slight edge of clarity-about-the-cost. The pattern is common and most clients have been inside it. Naming it without dramatizing it allows the client to see what has been operating without becoming ashamed of having been good-jobbed. The good-jobbing was not malicious; it was the parent's available repertoire. The work is to update the repertoire for the next generation and to install the alternative for the current self.

The "high achieving miserable people" line works because it is both true and slightly funny. The client recognizes themselves in it, and the recognition is the door.
