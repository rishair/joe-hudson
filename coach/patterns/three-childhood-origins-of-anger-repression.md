# Three Childhood Origins of Anger Repression

## The Pattern

Anger repression is taught, not innate. Children come into the world able to feel and express anger cleanly. Something happens, and after a while the system has learned to kink the flow in a specific way. Joe names three basic configurations under which this learning happens.

"Got 3 basic general ways that it happens." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

This is the upstream story for [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]]. Different origins tend to produce different adult forms of repression.

## Origin 1: A Caretaker's Anger Was Destructive

A parent, sibling, or other significant figure was chronically angry. The anger was at the child, manipulative, used to change them. The child experienced the anger as completely unsafe. The vow that formed: I will never do that to other people.

"You had an experience of somebody who was always angry as a child, so like a mom or dad or a teacher, and that anger became at you, it was manipulative, it was trying to change you, and then that became completely unsafe. And so you vowed to yourself some way that, I will never do that to other people again." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The vow is internalized as a constraint on the self. The system shuts down the anger entirely rather than risk doing what was done to it. The adult version tends to be Form 1 (anger inward) or Form 2 (passive aggression). Direct expression of anger is intolerable because it would mean becoming the abuser.

The specific kink: this person often becomes very good at sensing other people's anger and very bad at sensing their own. The hyper-attunement to others' anger is the residue of having had to manage the caretaker's.

## Origin 2: Anger Was Tied to Love (Removed or Granted)

The household had a particular relationship to anger. Two sub-shapes:

**2a. Anger removed love.** Whenever the child was angry, love was withdrawn. The household did not permit anger. Connection got cut off if the child showed it.

**2b. Anger got attention.** Anger was the way to get engagement. The household engaged with the angry child more than the calm child. Being angry became the way to be seen.

"There's another way that's like anger was just never allowed in the house, or whenever anger happened in the house, it was all at people. So like that comes out. That's the form of repression that comes out. You either got love removed from you if you were angry because it was just not allowed, or you got attention by having the anger that was, 'Grrr,' like that got you the engagement and love as a kid that you wanted." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

These are opposite shapes that often produce the same adult pattern: anger is the medium for connection. In 2a, the person learned to never use the medium. In 2b, the person learned to use it constantly.

Joe identifies himself as 2b: "That was my childhood. Like I got the most engagement from my family when I was angry."

The adult versions diverge:
- 2a usually produces Form 1 (inward) or Form 2 (sideways), with the child rarely accessing anger.
- 2b usually produces Form 3 (outward) with the adult defaulting to direct anger as the way to engage.

The 2b pattern often pairs in adulthood with the 2a pattern in a marriage. One person uses anger to engage; the other freezes when anger appears. Both feel abandoned: the angry person ("you're abandoning me by checking out"), the frozen person ("I have to leave to make myself safe"). See the pair dynamic in [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]].

## Origin 3: Anger Was Punished

The child got angry and was directly punished or rejected for it. Temper tantrums were not allowed. The full expression was shut down at the source.

"You were rejected, punished if you got angry. If you got angry in your house, it was like, you know, and this happens at such a young age, like temper tantrums, like just like not allowing your kid to have like that full out." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

The body learned: do not express this. The expression itself produces the punishment. Over time, even the felt sensation of anger becomes dangerous, because the felt sensation might leak into expression.

The adult version: anger often becomes inaccessible at the body level, not just the behavioral level. The person reports they "do not really get angry." What is more accurate is that the body has shut down the feeling early in its lifecycle, before it can surface.

The kink: Form 1 (inward) is the most common adult shape. The anger has nowhere to go externally, so it goes at the self.

## Why the Origin Matters

The origin is informative for two reasons.

**First, the appropriate practice differs.** A person with Origin 1 (abusive caretaker) often needs to release anger somewhere completely physically removed from any other person. The body cannot relax into release if it is near anyone, because the conditioning was that anger near others causes harm. The car, the woods, the ocean. Not even practicing with a partner is safe in the early stages.

A person with Origin 3 (punished anger) may need to start with [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]. The body does not know how to do the motion. The performance teaches the body the shape so the real thing can find its way.

A person with Origin 2b (anger got attention) needs less help accessing the anger and more help separating release from relational engagement. The work is moving the anger somewhere not-at-a-person, because the lifelong pattern has been to make the anger relational.

**Second, the body fears that arise during release differ.** See [[fear-of-self-destruction-by-anger]] for the general fear. Specific fears tend to track the origin:

- Origin 1 produces fear of becoming the abuser when anger releases.
- Origin 2a produces fear of being abandoned or rejected when anger releases.
- Origin 3 produces fear of being attacked when anger releases.

"You're gonna have fear. And I've seen this, I've seen people release their anger for the first time and then as soon as they've done it, they're like, ah, and then we've watched this in slow motion, it's so cool, they go, ah, and then as they pull away and realize they got angry, their eyes widen, they're like, they're just totally frightened." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

Naming the specific fear that arises in the moment of release helps the system update. The fear is being responded to as if the original scene were happening. Recognizing it as the original scene allows the body to learn that the current scene is different.

## In Coaching

The diagnostic conversation:
- "What happened when you got angry as a kid?"
- "Was anyone in your house chronically angry?"
- "What did your parents do when you had a tantrum?"
- "Did anger get you engagement, or did it get you cut off?"

The origin does not need to be precisely diagnosed. A rough sense is enough to orient the practice and the expected fears.

The origin also is not the whole story. The system is not deterministic. People with Origin 1 sometimes default to Form 3. People with Origin 3 sometimes find Form 2. The mapping is a starting point, not a verdict. The body's actual response to release work is the ground truth.

## Tone

Joe holds these matter-of-factly. The origins are mechanical descriptions, not pathologizing labels. The child responded to the situation the way a child responds. The adult is now working with what installed.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person says "I do not really get angry" or "I am scared of my own anger."
- When a person has explosive anger and intense shame about it.
- When a person reports that anger in others (a partner, a colleague) causes them to freeze.
- When working on anger and the work is not landing, and the body-level fear suggests something older is being touched.
