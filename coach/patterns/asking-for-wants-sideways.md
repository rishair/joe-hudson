# Asking for Wants Sideways

## The Pattern

When a want cannot be owned and spoken directly, it does not disappear. It surfaces in distorted forms. The relational level produces a predictable set of distorted forms — the ways one person asks for what they want when they cannot just say what they want.

"When they have the wants, they ask for the wants sideways. And so that creates a tremendous amount of disturbance in a relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

This is the relational-couple analog of [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]], which describes the organizational form. The mechanism is the same: unspoken wants do not disappear; they route through whatever channels are socially available. In couples and friendships, the channels are different from organizations, but the operation is identical.

## The Three Sideways Forms

Joe identifies three distinct distorted forms the unowned want takes when surfacing in a relationship:

### Form 1: Asking Through Pleasing

The person who wants X tries to get X by pleasing the other person until the other person reciprocates. Cooking the favorite dinner, doing the chores, being unusually agreeable — all aimed at producing a state of indebtedness that will be paid back in the wanted thing.

The structural cost: the pleasing is conditional, even when it does not look conditional. The other person eventually senses the contract. The pleasing reads as transactional, even when both parties are pretending it is not. The wanted thing arrives or does not, but either way the relationship has been corroded by the transactional shape underneath.

### Form 2: Asking Through Assumption

The person who wants X simply assumes the other person knows they want X. The want is never spoken because "they should know." When the other person does not deliver, the result is treated as evidence that they do not care.

"Instead of saying, 'Oh hey, I would really like this,' they'll try to please you to get it, or they'll assume that you know it." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The structural cost: the other person cannot read minds. The want is operationally invisible to them. They are then blamed for failing to deliver something they did not know was wanted. The relationship accumulates resentment for a withholding that never actually happened — because nothing was asked for in a form the other person could respond to.

### Form 3: Asking in a Distorted Tone

When the want has been suppressed long enough, the eventual ask comes out distorted. Three sub-forms:

**Meanness** — "they'll ask for it in this really mean way because they were wanting it for the last two weeks, but they didn't get it." The accumulated charge from the suppression weaponizes the ask. The wanting is finally spoken but the form is now an attack. The listener responds to the attack, not the want, and the want goes unmet again.

**Meekness** — "when they just ask for their wants, they say it really meekly, like, 'Oh, I was wondering if you could, are you sure?'" The pre-apology and the hedge mean the ask is already half-withdrawn before it lands. The listener can dismiss it without ever having to refuse it, because the asker has not committed to wanting the thing.

**Too-strong** — "or they ask for it way too strong. Like, 'Why don't you ever give me what I want? Why don't you take care of me?'" The desperation of the long suppression spills out. The ask is no longer about the specific want; it is a charge against the entire history of unmet wants compressed into this moment. The listener cannot respond to the specific want because the ask was not about the specific want.

"So that it disturbs the communication between people, and therefore the relationship starts to fall apart if people can't own what they want with one another." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

## Why The Sideways Forms Are Self-Defeating

Each of the three forms has the same structural failure: the wanted thing is less likely to arrive than if the want had been asked for cleanly.

- The pleasing-ask makes the eventual reciprocation feel obligated rather than freely given, so the wanted thing arrives with the wrong texture even when it arrives.
- The assumption-ask is operationally invisible, so the wanted thing almost never arrives, and when it does it is by coincidence.
- The mean-ask, meek-ask, and too-strong-ask all distort the ask in ways the listener responds to as something other than a want, so the want gets lost in the response to the distortion.

The unowned want is doing more work to not get met than the owned want would do to get met. The avoidance of vulnerability costs more than the vulnerability would have.

## The Marriage Diagnostic

A specific marker for the pattern operating at relationship scale:

"If I see a marriage that's falling apart, one of the things I guarantee is that nobody has set forth recently what they want in the marriage. Like, this is what I want. They don't even have that conversation." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The absence of clean want-stating is the operational marker for relationship deterioration. The wants are still present and still operating — through the three sideways forms — but no one is naming them as wants. The relationship is being run on the distorted communication, with both parties responding to the distortions rather than to the wants the distortions are covering.

## What Makes the Pattern Stick

The three sideways forms are sticky because each has a hidden gain.

- Pleasing-asks preserve the asker's identity as generous, even though the giving is conditional.
- Assumption-asks preserve the asker's right to be hurt by the not-receiving, because they did not have to risk the vulnerability of asking directly.
- Mean-asks let the asker express the suppressed charge while pretending the charge is the listener's fault.
- Meek-asks let the asker disclaim the want before refusal arrives, so the refusal does not feel personal.
- Too-strong-asks let the asker dump the accumulated suppression while the listener is the focus of the dump.

In every case, the sideways form is paid for by relational damage. The cost is real but distributed, so the asker does not have to face it as a coherent cost. The clean ask, by contrast, requires facing the cost up front — the vulnerability of asking, the risk of refusal, the exposure of having wanted.

## How To Recognize the Pattern in Yourself

The first move is recognition. Ask:

- Do I find myself doing nice things in hopes that the niceness will be reciprocated?
- Do I assume that if my partner cared, they would already know what I want?
- Do I get sharper when I am about to ask for something I have been wanting for a while?
- Do my asks come out apologetic or hedged when I finally make them?
- Do small asks sometimes balloon into accusations about everything that has been wrong for years?

A yes to any of these is a sign the sideways pattern is operating. The corrective is not better technique on the sideways form. It is to drop back to the clean want and ask for it cleanly.

## What Resolves It

- The asker learns to name what they want directly. See [[the-want]] and [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]].
- The listener learns to receive wants cleanly. See [[i-love-that-you-want-that]].
- Resentment is treated as the diagnostic for unspoken wants. See [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]].
- Both partners agree that the relationship is a place where wants get named, not hinted at.

The shift is operational, not just attitudinal. The clean ask has a different shape — the body changes, the voice changes, the timing changes. Both partners can learn to recognize the shape and to recognize when an ask is in one of the three sideways forms instead.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Watching yourself do the favorite dinner trick and notice you are calculating the trade
- Realizing your partner could not possibly have known what you wanted because you never said it
- Hearing your own voice come out meaner than the situation warranted and recognizing the accumulated charge
- Hearing yourself say "I was wondering if maybe" and notice the pre-withdrawal in the ask
- Compressing a year of small unmet wants into one cataclysmic Friday night argument and recognizing the pattern
- The relief of finally just saying "I want X" without any of the distortion
