# Everyone Is Pretending (The Collective Bravado)

## The Pattern

In any culture where showing fear is punished, every member is privately afraid and publicly pretending. Each member believes the others are the real ones and they themselves are the imposter. The pretending is what keeps the culture going. The recognition that everyone is doing it is what dissolves the necessity of the pretending.

This pattern shows up everywhere the rule "do not show fear" is in force: prisons, gangs, hyper-masculine households, juvenile facilities, boardrooms, elite militaries, certain elite schools. The setting changes; the structure is the same.

## The Survival Logic

The rule "do not show fear" is not arbitrary. In environments where showing fear marks you as prey, the rule originates as accurate survival information.

Joe, recounting what he learned teaching Head Start in the Hayes Valley Projects in San Francisco: "Nobody shows any fear here. And they said, 'Look, if you show fear, you're prey. So you're either predator or prey. If you show fear, you're prey. You don't show fear.'"

The same rule operated in Oakland in the late 1990s, where Emile DeWeaver was selling drugs in the rolling 100s. The same rule operated in juvenile hall, county jail, prison. The rule is not paranoid. It is correct about the local conditions.

## What the Rule Produces in the Individual

Each child or adult inside the rule is afraid -- because all humans are afraid -- and cannot show the fear, because showing it endangers them. The fear has nowhere to go. So it gets covered: with anger, with bravado, with rage manufactured for the purpose of overriding the fear that is actually there.

Emile, describing his juvenile years: "I had this, like, pretend person that I was. I was, like, play-acting hardcore, like the hardest method acting you have ever seen. Like, I have never liked to fight. Fighting scares the shit out of me. But, like, no one could ever know that. And I had to be able to fight on a dime."

The cost to the person is twofold. First, the chronic suppression of the fear (see [[im-not-an-anxious-person]] and [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]]). Second, the chronic shame about the fear that the culture says should not be there. The shame compounds the suppression. The pretending becomes the identity. There is no way to authentically show up because the thing being hidden is true.

"There was no way that I could authentically show up as myself in any situation because, like, it felt like I was hiding this thing that was very true about me that was saying that there was something that was wrong about me." -- Emile DeWeaver

## What the Rule Produces in the Group

Every member is doing this individually. None of them know the others are doing it. Each believes they are the only one who is secretly afraid and pretending. The culture is sustained by the very pretending it requires.

The reveal usually only comes after the conditions change. People who grew up together in the system, run into each other twenty or thirty years later, and finally have a conversation about what was actually happening, all discover the same thing.

"Come to find out, everyone was pretending. Everyone was pretending in the name of survival, saying that, like, if I don't do this pretend, I won't be able to survive because other people are somehow different than me. I'm somehow different because, like, I'm like this impostor in this thing. I'm like afraid all the time. I don't want to fight. I don't want -- everybody was thinking that and acting on this pretend character that they think -- thought they had to be to be safe, not knowing we were all pretending." -- Emile DeWeaver

This is the heart of the pattern: the rule produces a population in which everyone is hiding the same thing from everyone else. The hiding is the culture.

## The Boardroom Variant

The pattern is not specific to physically dangerous environments. It runs at full intensity in many corporate settings, particularly elite ones. The penalty for showing fear is reputational and economic rather than physical, but the structure is identical.

"This sounds a lot like a lot of boardrooms that I have been in. There's a whole bunch of people who are anxious and scared who are doing everything they can not to show it and not to react from it and trying to have the bravado and, and they're scared the whole thing's going to go away." -- Joe Hudson, Life After Murder

Same dynamic, different consequences for being seen. The executive worries about being replaced, about losing the deal, about being marked as not-up-to-it. The fear is real, the pretending is sustained, and each executive believes the others are the real ones who handle this without being afraid.

The recognition of the pattern in business is harder because the consequences of showing fear feel more real than they actually are. In the projects or in prison, showing fear could literally cost you your life on a given day. In a boardroom, showing fear costs you something that feels mortal but is mostly identity-extension. See [[identity-extension-expands-fear]] for why the fear feels physical even when the threat is reputational.

## The Cross-Domain Mapping

Emile makes the parallel explicit, with sharp observation:

"Much of what happens in prison is paralleled also in society, in workplaces like that. Think about prison as a mechanism of disposability. And then think about how many people feel in an office." -- Emile DeWeaver

And later, about the healing journeys of people he meets in tech, philanthropy, the nonprofit world: "Everyone is trying hard to heal from trauma in their life. And I'm like, wait, in that -- like, it's different details, but there's something very similar about my healing journey and the healing journeys of many people around me who have seen nothing of like what I have seen."

The disposability is the substrate. The pretending is the surface. The structures repeat across domains because they share an underlying logic: when belonging is contingent on performance of strength, every member becomes a performer of strength, and the population of performers can never truly know each other.

## Why the Pattern Is So Stable

Three reinforcing dynamics:

1. **First-mover risk.** Whoever shows fear first risks the consequences alone. The collective benefit of the reveal (everyone discovers they are not alone) requires someone to bear the individual cost first.

2. **Inability to verify others' privacy.** Each member can only see the others' performances. They have no access to the others' inner life. The inner life of the others is inferred from the performance, which is itself fake. The whole field of evidence is fabricated.

3. **Shame about the fear.** Each member's private fear is layered with shame: not only am I afraid, but I shouldn't be, because none of them are. The shame ensures the fear stays hidden even when conditions ease, because the hiding has become identity. See [[authenticity-reduces-shame]] -- the shame of inauthenticity compounds the shame the inauthenticity was supposed to hide.

## What Breaks the Pattern

The pattern breaks when one person tells the truth about their fear and survives. The survival is the proof that updates everyone's model of the cost of showing fear. The first reveal usually has to come from someone with enough rank or distance that they will not be punished -- a senior leader, a returning member, someone with a story too undeniable to dismiss.

Joe's coaching version of this in leadership: the leader who can name their own fear in front of the team gives every other member permission to do the same. See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] and [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]]. The hidden fear in the room is the organization's fear; the leader who hides it teaches the team to hide; the leader who shows it teaches the team that showing is safe.

In Emile's case, the breaking happened decades after the fact, when men in their 30s and 40s who had been together in juvenile facilities finally had the conversation no one had been able to have at 17. The relief, even thirty years late, was substantial.

## The Coaching Move

When someone presents as an alumnus of a no-fear culture -- military, gang, elite prep school, hedge fund, special operations, founder culture, prison -- the coaching path includes the recognition that they have been operating in a population where everyone was pretending and that the bigger truth was always available but was systematically hidden.

This is not the same as the coaching move for [[im-not-an-anxious-person]]. The person here is not denying their fear. They knew they were afraid the whole time. They thought they were the only one. The work is to unwind the shame of having been afraid, which is downstream of the false belief that the others were not afraid.

The named relief: "It turns out you were not the imposter. You were the only one telling yourself the truth about what was happening for you. The others were telling themselves the same lie you were performing." This often produces something close to grief -- grief for the time spent alone with the fear that, had it been named, would have produced solidarity.

## Tone

When this pattern is named in coaching, the tone is matter-of-fact rather than therapeutic. The structure is not personal. It is the architecture of any culture built on the no-fear rule. Naming it as architecture takes the personal shame out and replaces it with recognition.

## When Someone Experiences This

- "I felt like I had to fake it the whole time."
- "I knew I wasn't really like that, but I had to pretend I was."
- "Everyone else seemed to have it together."
- "I was the only one who didn't actually know what I was doing."
- "I had to manufacture the anger / confidence / aggression because the real thing wasn't there."
- The reveal years later: "Wait, you felt that way too?"
