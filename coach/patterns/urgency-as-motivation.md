# Urgency as Motivation

## The Pattern

A person discovers that they can get themselves to act when something feels urgent. So they wait until it feels urgent. Or, when nothing is urgent, they manufacture the urgency. Each act of work runs on the adrenaline produced by the urgency. The system trains itself: only urgency produces action, so urgency must be produced.

Joe's investor example shows this at its purest:

"I had a really famous investor that I worked with for a while, and he just wouldn't do anything until it became urgent. And then he would use the urgency to motivate him. And then the whole team realized urgency is what motivates this person. And so they would create these urgencies and then they'd be motivated, but all it did was make him more and more and more burnt out." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

The whole team learned the pattern. Every request that came to the investor had to be framed as an emergency, because non-emergency requests would be ignored. The cost of this was paid by his adrenals. The benefit (action happens) was real but localized; the cost (chronic exhaustion) was systemic.

## The Procrastination Variant

The same pattern shows up in individual life as the standard procrastinator's loop. Tim Urban's TED talk metaphor (which Brett references) is recognizable to most people: the person is distracted up until the moment of the deadline, then uses the deadline-panic to power through. The work gets done. The cost is paid by the body and by the quality of the work.

"That monkey is the thing that like motivates -- like, so you find yourself just very distracted up until the moment of the deadline, and then you use that deadline to motivate yourself. And it just seems like a very common, very common cycle. I relate to it so much through much of my life and the languaging around ADD, ADHD very much fits that pattern as well as many other like depression, anxiety." -- Brett Kistler, The Burnout Cycle

The diagnostic label (ADD, ADHD, depression, anxiety) often gets attached to the pattern, but the underlying mechanism is the same: urgency is the only motivator that has been wired in. Without urgency, no action. With urgency, adrenaline-fueled action. Eventually, burnout.

## What This Implies

A person locked into urgency-as-motivation has organized their internal life around a single energy source. Inspiration, enjoyment, wanting, vision, curiosity -- the other possible engines of action -- have all been crowded out by the single dominant one. The person literally does not know what action without urgency would feel like.

This is one of the [[burnout-cycle]] engines. The adrenaline produced by urgency is the same adrenaline that, sustained over years, depletes the adrenals and produces the bathrobe-on-the-couch collapse.

It is also a manipulation pattern at the organizational scale. Teams learn to manufacture urgency for urgency-driven leaders. Marketers learn to manufacture urgency to drive sales. Political movements learn to manufacture urgency to mobilize supporters. See [[us-vs-them-as-universal-manipulation-pattern]] for the broader version of this in social manipulation. The pattern that powers the individual procrastinator also powers societal cycles of boom and crash.

## What Joe Does

Joe names the pattern and asks what other motivation source could be operating. The work is not to remove urgency but to develop the other sources -- enjoyment, inspiration, vision, wanting -- so urgency is one option among many rather than the only one.

"So that's another thing is people will use it as a form of motivation rather than, say, enjoyment or inspiration or, um, vision or any of those things to find their motivation." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

The diagnostic question: when you do something without it being urgent, what does that feel like? Many people cannot answer because they have never operated outside the urgency engine for long enough to find out.

The follow-up: where could enjoyment, want, or inspiration produce the action that urgency is currently producing? See [[how-could-you-do-this-and-gain-energy]] for the move Joe uses with CEOs around this.

## Tone

Joe describes urgency-as-motivation matter-of-factly, almost as a structural observation. There is no shame in it. It is what happens when a person's life and nervous system have wired in one motivator and no others. The work is to wire in additional motivators, not to feel bad about the existing one.

## The Structural Reframe: Pressure Makes You Present, Not Motivated

A subtle but important reframe that clarifies why urgency-as-motivation produces output: the urgency is not motivating the action. The urgency is forcing presence. Under sufficient time pressure, the head's judgment-and-perfectionism loop has no time to operate. The body just does the thing because the head can no longer dominate the operation.

> "If you have 30 minutes to write your paper, you're not gonna be in your head judging yourself. You don't have time to be all looping around trying to make it perfect. You got X amount of time, I'm gonna just do what comes out, I'm just gonna —" -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

This reframe matters because it changes the prescription. If urgency-motivates is the model, the answer is more urgency. If urgency-makes-you-present is the model, the answer is to find sustainable ways to produce presence (enjoyment, iteration, wonder, dropping importance) that do not deplete the body the way urgency does.

See [[pressure-makes-you-present]] for the standalone treatment of the reframe. The urgency-as-motivation pattern produces short-form output well (cleaning the house, writing the paper at the last minute) but fails on long-arc projects (building a business, sustaining a creative practice) because the presence-by-emergency cannot be sustained across years without burnout. The other presence-producing mechanisms can.
