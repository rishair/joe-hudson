# Three Strategies of Not-Belonging

## The Pattern

People who have been trained that they don't belong (see [[belonging-is-trained-not-true]]) run one of three operational strategies to keep that training in effect. The strategies are not chosen consciously; they are automatic. Each one produces real social feedback that the person then reads as confirmation that they don't belong. The strategy generates the evidence.

The three strategies:

1. **Provoke.** Test the environment by acting in ways that might produce rejection. "Will you love me if I do this? Will you love me if I do that?"
2. **Hide.** Become invisible. Watch yourself not-be-seen, then conclude that nobody sees you.
3. **Go superior.** Locate yourself above the group. Find the ways they don't measure up. Notice how you have nothing in common.

Most people primarily run one. Some run two in different environments (often hide at home, provoke at work, or some variant). The naming is usually a relief: the behavior had felt like just-who-I-am and is now visible as a move.

## Strategy 1: Provoke

The provocateur acts in ways that might cause rejection, then watches what happens. The acts can range from harmless (unusual dress, odd humor, deliberate edge in conversation) to costly (being late, being unreliable, picking fights, sabotaging closeness).

The underlying question being asked: "Will you still want me here if I do this?" The provocation tests the environment. A pass produces (briefly) a sense of having been accepted as oneself. A fail produces confirmation that the original rejection is the universal one.

Most environments register the provocation as off-putting, which the provocateur reads as evidence that they don't belong. The strategy is rarely conscious. The person experiences themselves as just being themselves, and experiences the environment's response as just-how-people-are-when-you-don't-belong.

"I just didn't belong. I didn't belong in my family. I didn't belong in school. I didn't belong anywhere. And so I was out to prove it. I was going to annoy people to prove I didn't belong. I was going to look away to prove that I didn't belong. I was saying, 'Will you love me if I do this? Will you love me if I do that?'" -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here" (describing his own 13-year-old green-mohawk version of this strategy)

Joe's example illustrates that the provocateur often has high apparent confidence. The green mohawk reads as bold self-expression. The structural fact is that it was a test, asked again and again, in a form that almost always produces the rejection it was checking for.

## Strategy 2: Hide

The hider stays in the room but recedes. They speak rarely. They contribute minimally. They watch themselves go unseen. They conclude that they don't belong because nobody is reaching for them.

"The other way to do it is just to disappear. See, nobody sees me. I don't belong here. Nobody gets me. I don't belong here." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The mechanism is the inverse of the provocateur's. The provocateur tests by adding signal; the hider tests by withholding it. Both produce the same end result: the environment doesn't engage with the real person, because the real person has either been distorted by the test or kept entirely out of view.

The hider often experiences themselves as a passive observer of the not-belonging, when in fact they are actively producing it through their hiddenness. The not-being-seen is something they are doing, not something that is being done to them.

This strategy connects to [[the-desire-to-be-seen]] (the "I like to hide myself" / "I'm either putting on a mask or running away from people" pattern) and to the conflict-avoidance loop in [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]] ("you cannot be loved for what you hide").

## Strategy 3: Go Superior

The superiority-runner stays present but interpretively distances. They notice the ways the group doesn't measure up. They have private commentary running about what others are missing. When attacked, the superiority doubles down: the attack becomes more evidence that the group is operating at a lower level.

"The third way that people go about proving that they don't belong is that they are constantly in their mind saying, 'Oh, I'm a little bit better. I know, I know, I got this. They don't got it.' That typically comes because you had highly critical parents and you've cut out your emotions. And so the only way that you protect yourself is to go, 'Oh, I'm better.' And you can tell that it's a form of protection because it doubles down when you get attacked." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The developmental marker Joe names: highly critical parents combined with cut-off emotions. The superiority is what the body installed when emotions stopped being usable. Without the felt-experience available as a guide, the comparison-mind takes over and provides a different form of self-location: I am here, you are below, the not-belonging is because I am too good for here.

The diagnostic that distinguishes this from healthy assessment: it intensifies under attack. Real assessment relaxes when challenged because the assessor is interested in the truth. The superiority strategy doubles down because its function is protection, and the attack is what it's protecting against. See [[superiority-and-shame-erode-together]] for the related principle: superiority and the underlying shame are paired and erode together.

This strategy connects to [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] (the comparative-mind move blocks self-listening) and to [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] (the recognition-seeking under the surface).

## The Common Function

All three strategies do the same work: they produce evidence of not-belonging while protecting the person from the original felt-experience of the essential rejection. The strategies differ in style but match in function.

- The provocateur gets rejected for the provocation, not for what they essentially are.
- The hider doesn't have to find out whether they would be accepted, because they never showed up to be assessed.
- The superiority-runner has decided in advance that the group is below them, so the group's lack of recognition doesn't carry weight.

Each strategy hides the original wound from being touched again. Each one also produces the felt-pattern of not-belonging that the person experiences as their reality.

See [[belonging-is-trained-not-true]] for the underlying training. See [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]] for the developmental wiring that produces the essential-rejection imprint.

## When Someone Experiences This

You are running strategy 1 if: you find yourself doing things that you can almost predict will produce distance, you have a track record of being the one who said the awkward thing, you experience the test as just being-yourself.

You are running strategy 2 if: you have been in a group for months and people don't know basic things about you, you find yourself watching the conversation more than participating, you take the lack of reach as evidence of being unwanted.

You are running strategy 3 if: you have private commentary running about how the people in the room don't quite get it, you find yourself sharper and more dismissive when challenged, you locate yourself above the group in the way you describe them later.

You are running more than one if: the patterns vary by environment, you provoke at work and hide at family events, you go superior at parties and provoke at home. The strategy is responsive to which form has the highest social cost in the given context.

## What to Do Once the Strategy Is Visible

The naming itself opens a gap. The strategy ran below conscious recognition; once it is visible, it can be paused. The pausing is not the work; the willingness to be in the situation without running the strategy is the work.

The available alternative is [[am-i-in-alignment-with-me-right-now]] as the operating question. Instead of producing evidence of not-belonging through one of the three strategies, the person checks alignment with themselves and proceeds from there. The belonging-question fades because it has been replaced by a question that is actually answerable.

See [[be-yourself-despite-consequences-undoes-stuckness]] for the principle that frames the exit.

## Tone

Joe describes all three strategies with the same matter-of-factness. None of the three is presented as worse than the others. The teaching has the quality of "here are the three forms; one of them is probably you; the recognition is half the work." He uses himself as the illustration for strategy 1, which removes the implied judgment from the naming.

The tone is also slightly playful — the three-strategy taxonomy has the satisfying clarity of a small useful framework, and Joe presents it that way.
