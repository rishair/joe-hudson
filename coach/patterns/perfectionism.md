# Perfectionism

## Definition

Perfectionism is the critical parent's voice internalized. It is the attempt to satisfy a standard that was never satisfiable, because the original standard was never about the child's performance. It was about the parent's needs.

Perfection does not exist. What one person considers perfect, another sees as rigid, hesitant, or imperfect. The only honest description of perfectionism: trying to satisfy a critical voice in your head that is never and can never be satisfied.

"The way I pick those people out is because I can see which ones of them had super critical parents, and you can see it in everything that they do. So on some level, perfectionism is just trying to make the critical parent pleased." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

## How It Shows Up

Observable signals: rigidity in the musculature, excessive precision, second-guessing, stunted tones, hesitation. The body of a perfectionist is tight.

Part of being perfect is not being a perfectionist. Naming someone as a perfectionist triggers them because it exposes the contradiction. The standard demands its own invisibility.

The deepest depression correlates with the loudest perfectionist voice. People who feel most stagnant have a brain telling them they are not perfect and need to be, all the time.

## The Mechanism

Perfectionism converts natural impulses into "shoulds." The body produces a desire to move, to write, to connect. That impulse is alive, connected, and motivating. Perfectionism intercepts it: "I should work out." The should is unmotivating. See [[impulse-to-should]].

"We have all these impulses inside of us that are just popping up like, oh, I wanna work out or I wanna exercise or I wanna move my body. And then that impulse, which is the deep connection, immediately gets turned into a perfection of I should work out. And then it's like completely unmotivating." -- Joe Hudson, Connection over Perfection

Children follow impulses without perfection. As they age, the bigger the perfection overlay, the more stilted, the more stunted.

## Perfectionism and Fear

Perfectionism is fear-based. The belief that there is a right answer, a right way, a standard to live up to is driven by the fear of what happens if you fall short. Fear does not make great decisions. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]].

Perfectionism creates a finite game: achieve the standard or fail. Connection is an infinite game: keep iterating, keep evolving, no end state. See [[connection-over-perfection]].

## Perfectionism Produces the Failure It Tries to Prevent

The perfectionist voice trying to ensure quality work prevents quality work. The pressure produces tension; tension blocks flow; without flow, the work cannot be excellent. This is the specific case of [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] applied to perfectionism.

"Having the voice in the head trying to tell you, convince you that you need to be perfect about something. And that incessant nature makes it very hard to even do something really well." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The same pattern shows up in conversations. The perfectionist voice rehearsing a conversation to make it go well produces anxiety that arrives at the actual conversation and makes it go worse. See [[rehearsing-conversations]].

The artist case Joe describes: "That's why you see so many artists get hooked on heroin or alcohol or anything to just silence the voice in their head so that they can be in that flow state so that they can create their best work." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head? The substance is a workaround for the perfectionist [[voice-in-the-head]]. The flow it enables is what the voice was preventing.

## Perfectionism and ADD/ADHD

What looks like attention deficit can be perfectionist pessimism: "I'm never gonna get this right, not right now, so why even bother?" The mind skips off the task not because it cannot focus but because approaching the task triggers the perfectionist voice, which triggers avoidance. See [[negative-self-reinforcement]].

The label "attention deficit disorder" can be reframed: not a deficit in the capacity to pay attention, but a deficit in the attention that was paid to the person. A deficit of connection. The disconnection creates the pattern.

## When Someone Experiences This

When stuck in a loop of planning but not executing. When the inner voice says "not good enough" before the work has begun. When iteration feels dangerous because each attempt could be judged. When procrastination is driven by "I won't get it right" rather than "I don't want to do it."
