# Signs of a Dysfunctional Team

## The Pattern

The team-level patterns Joe scans for when he walks into a room or sits in on a meeting. These complement [[signs-of-a-dysfunctional-leader]]; together they form the diagnostic Joe runs through when called in to assess a team.

## The Signs

### Talking Without Listening

A conversation requires both someone who wants to talk and someone who wants to listen. When a team has talkers without listeners (or worse, only talkers), the meeting is performance, not communication.

"If I hear people talking without listening to each other, that's a huge sign. A conversation requires both somebody who wants to talk and somebody who wants to listen." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

Joe describes walking into an executive team where the CEO spoke for five minutes while everyone visibly checked out, then asking the team: "What makes it that you're not listening to the CEO? I just watched all of you guys check out while the CEO was talking, so what's happening there?" -- and then asking the CEO what made that acceptable to live with.

### Uneven Participation

Who is talking and who is silent, and what the distribution looks like. A team where two people speak and six watch is a team where six people are not in the room even though they are in the room.

### Rabbit Holes

People get lost in examples instead of the higher topic being discussed. The rabbit hole feels productive -- everyone is engaged in the detail -- but the actual work the meeting was for is not getting done.

"I look for people doing rabbit holes where everybody gets lost in the example instead of the higher topic that's being talked about. That kind of level of distraction is sometimes incredibly useful in certain kinds of conversations, but in execution conversations, really not useful." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

Brett's read: the rabbit hole can be a hiding place. "Somebody wants to be seen in a certain feeling and maybe they're going down a rabbit hole into an example to hide in that example and hope that the example can prove what they want to say without them having to fully own it."

### Unclear Roles and Responsibilities

The meeting ends and people do not know what they are supposed to do. No accountability, no execution.

"Any time that I see something where at the end of the meeting, people don't know what they're supposed to do at the end of it, if it's a meeting about execution, that's another example of it. Means that they either — there's no accountability, or people don't know what they're supposed to be doing." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

### Context Switching Without Recognition

Two people argue with each other while talking about completely different things, neither realizing they are not in the same conversation.

"I also look for context switching where like if one person is talking about this and they're arguing with someone who's talking about that and they're not even talking about the same thing and they don't even recognize that they're not talking about the same thing. Happens all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

### Talk Preferred Over Action

The team likes hearing itself talk but is not interested in execution. People get lost in the thought rather than the doing. Common in larger organizations but not exclusive to them.

"Teams that prefer talk to action, especially in bigger organizations, but not only where people like to hear themselves talk, but they're not interested in like making it all happen, and they can get lost in the thought instead of in the doing." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

### Being Right Is Important

When people defend their positions because they need to be right, they have made being right a proxy for their value to the business. See [[needing-to-be-right-in-teams]].

"If I see it so that, you know, like people are defending themselves because they need to be right, that means that they think being right has something to do with their value to the business, and that's a horrible thought process." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

### Team Feels Oppressed by Dynamic Rather Than Accountable for It

People feel done-to by the team dynamic rather than responsible for shaping it. See [[team-feels-oppressed-not-accountable]] for the full treatment.

## Strategies the Team Uses to Avoid Emotion

Beyond the surface signs, Joe reads which emotions the team is avoiding and which strategies are being used to avoid them. The strategies are diagnostic.

"What emotions are they avoiding and what emotions are they embracing? And how are they, what are the strategies that people are using to embrace or avoid the emotion or to bring teamwork together or create conflict? And those strategies really will tell you a tremendous amount about where the dysfunction lies and what the root cause of the dysfunction is." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The room of a dysfunctional team is dripping with suppressed emotion, and in that emotion are the ingredients for the team's repair. The suppression is what produces the dysfunctional behavior; the welcoming of the emotion is what produces the functional alternative.

## Example: The Yelling Leader

A leader yells at the team. The suppressed emotion is usually fear or hurt and aloneness. They feel that way because stagnation is happening and they know it will hurt the company.

"The suppression of that emotion creates the frustration, and the frustration often drives some sort of movement. The anger drives some sort of movement, which is a surrogate for the best kind of movement, but at least it's movement. And so that's a great example of how the unfelt emotion turns into a strategy that is less functional." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The functional alternative: "Wow, I noticed that I have very little confidence that we're gonna succeed and that scares me. And the reason that I have no confidence that we're gonna succeed is because I don't see movement happening. And I don't wanna be a part of a team where there's no movement happening. So how do we get to movement? And how do we all take responsibility and ownership for that?"

The shift is from acting on the unfelt emotion to naming and being in the emotion.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You walk out of a team meeting and cannot say what was actually decided
- You realize the same arguments keep happening because no one is really listening
- You watch two colleagues fight about different things and feel powerless to intervene
- You feel like meetings are happening to you rather than with you
- You notice that the people who talk the most also commit to the least
- You catch yourself defending a position you do not even particularly hold because backing down would feel like losing

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A team member describes their team as "frustrating" without being able to name why
- A leader asks for help assessing the team
- A coaching session begins with someone in a team they cannot leave or change
- A new hire is trying to read the dynamics of a team they have just joined
- An organization is investigating why initiatives keep stalling

## Tone

Joe runs through these signs as a working list, not a manifesto. The diagnostic is the work; the moralism would be in the way. The patterns are observable, predictable, and addressable -- once they are named.
