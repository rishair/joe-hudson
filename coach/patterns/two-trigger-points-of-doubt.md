# The Two Trigger Points of Doubt

## The Pattern

In an ambition arc — the arc from idea to project to delivery — doubt fires in two distinct, predictable places. The two places are not the same, do not have the same trigger, and often expose different underlying emotions. Naming the two as a typology helps the coach locate which one is operating in a given session and trace it to its source.

The two trigger points:

**Trigger 1: While living into the project.** The person is handed a project or generates one themselves. They get excited. They sit with the ideas, develop them, think them through. Then, somewhere in the sitting-with, doubt arrives. The doubt is not in response to any external feedback yet — it is arriving in the inner space, before anyone else has seen the work.

"You're handed a project, or you have a project that you want to start, and you get excited about it. You're thrilled, maybe a little but you're like, woo, let's do this project. And then you put some ideas together, you sit with the ideas, and then doubt shows up." -- Joe Hudson, Ambitious But Afraid

**Trigger 2: After receiving a response you didn't want.** The person has put the work in front of someone — a boss, an investor, a partner, a collaborator. The other person's response is not full enthusiastic buy-in. They have questions; they push back; they look unconvinced; they say something critical or even just neutral when the person was hoping for excited. Doubt arrives in response to the under-enthusiastic reception.

"The second place where doubt typically arises is then you put it in front of somebody. Maybe it's your boss, or maybe it's an investor, or maybe it's your husband, and you put this idea out, you make a presentation, or you just talk about it, and you see that they aren't completely bought into the idea." -- Joe Hudson, Ambitious But Afraid

## How the Two Differ

The two triggers expose different underlying emotional sources, even though both are forms of [[doubt-is-protection-from-a-feeling]].

**Trigger 1 (living-into) typically exposes projected-feeling doubt.** The person is sitting with the project and the body is forecasting forward — what would it feel like if I actually shipped this and failed? What would it feel like if I succeeded? The doubt is the brace against the forecasted feeling. This is the [[fear-of-success-as-projected-failure]] dynamic operating in real time. The trigger is internal: the body is doing the forecasting and protecting against the forecast.

**Trigger 2 (post-reception) typically exposes past-feeling doubt.** The current under-enthusiastic response is the stimulus that activates the body's installed sensitivity to criticism. The five-year-old who was criticized by a parent goes into "what did I do wrong?" mode the moment any current other-person delivers anything less than full approval. The doubt is the felt-pattern of the original criticism arriving via the current event. See [[doubt-requires-closed-heart]] and [[what-did-i-do-wrong-response-to-aggression]] for the structural mechanism.

The two can overlap. Often both are operating — the person who has doubt while sitting with the project (forecasting forward) also has doubt after presenting it (criticism-sensitivity). But the two have different intervention surfaces.

## Why Naming the Two Matters

The pattern is operational because the diagnostic question is different in each case.

**For Trigger 1 doubt:** "What feeling would arrive if you successfully completed this?" The work moves toward [[fear-of-success-as-projected-failure]] and the welcoming of the projected feeling.

**For Trigger 2 doubt:** "When the criticism arrived, what did your body do?" "How early in your life did this pattern of going-into-doubt-when-someone-is-not-thrilled-with-you get installed?" The work moves toward [[doubt-requires-closed-heart]] and the original feeling the doubt was developed to avoid.

The same client can be working with Trigger 1 doubt on Project A and Trigger 2 doubt on Project B. The pattern lets the coach distinguish without assuming both are the same kind of work.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A coachee who reports their excitement about a new project consistently turns into doubt after a few days of working on it (Trigger 1 signature)
- A coachee who reports they were doing fine until they showed the work to someone and the someone wasn't excited enough (Trigger 2 signature)
- A coachee who oscillates between energized and doubting in the same project (probably both triggers firing at different stages)
- An executive who reports going into self-doubt every time a board member raises a concern (Trigger 2 signature; criticism-sensitivity)
- A founder who reports they can't get past the early-stage planning into execution (Trigger 1 signature; the forecasted-feeling brace is firing before any external event)
- An artist who reports that they were excited about the piece until they showed a draft to one person and now they don't want to finish it (Trigger 2 signature)

## What Joe Does With This Pattern

The first move is locating the trigger. Often the client knows; they can name "I was fine until X said Y" (Trigger 2) or "I just started doubting myself while I was thinking about it" (Trigger 1). Sometimes the client doesn't know; the coach can ask the trigger-locating question directly: "where in the arc did the doubt arrive?"

Once the trigger is located, the work moves to the underlying feeling specific to that trigger. For Trigger 2, this often involves tracing the criticism-sensitivity back to its installation point — the parent who was angry, the teacher who shamed, the coach who berated. The current criticism is the activation; the original criticism is the source. The work is at the source.

For Trigger 1, the work involves naming the forecasted feeling. "What would it feel like if you actually delivered this and people loved it?" Often the client cannot stay in the imagined experience for long — the body braces against the forecasted feeling. The bracing is the doubt's source. The work is to welcome the forecasted feeling enough that the brace releases.

In both cases, after the underlying feeling work, the client can return to the project with the doubt's grip loosened. The doubt may still arrive in the same place next time, but the client now has a relationship to it; they know what it's protecting against; they know what to do.

## The Coaching Application

When a client presents "I keep getting stuck in doubt on my projects," the trigger-point question is the first move. "When does the doubt arrive? At the start, while you're thinking about it? Or after you've shown it to someone?" The answer determines the path.

If the client says "both," that is data too. The work then moves through both — the projected-feeling work and the criticism-sensitivity work. The two can be sequenced; the client doesn't need to do both at once.

If the client cannot answer (the doubt feels like background weather rather than triggered by specific moments), the work is to slow down enough to find the triggers. Often the client has been treating the doubt as a constant ambient state and has not noticed it has trigger points. Finding the triggers is itself a coaching move.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not treat the two triggers as the same work.** They expose different underlying emotions and require different intervention paths.
- **Do not let the client treat their doubt as ambient.** Locate the trigger. The trigger is where the work begins.
- **Do not focus on the content of the doubt without locating its trigger.** The content is the cognitive cover. The trigger is the operational signal.
- **Do not assume a Trigger 2 client just needs more confidence in their work.** The doubt was activated by criticism-sensitivity, not by an actual flaw in the work. The work is at the sensitivity, not at the work's quality.

## Tone

Joe names this pattern as background teaching, not as a dramatic frame. The two triggers are described as "the two places typically where the doubt arises." Typically — not always, not for everyone, but commonly enough that naming them helps locate where the listener's own doubt is firing. The casualness invites self-recognition rather than installing a framework the listener has to fit themselves into.
