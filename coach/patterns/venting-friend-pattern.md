# The Venting Friend Pattern

## The Pattern

Two friends. One has a complaint — about the boyfriend, the boss, the family member. The complainer vents. The friend joins. "Yeah, he's such an asshole." Both nod. The complainer feels briefly better. Both walk away.

Then it happens again the next week. And the next. Eventually one or both can name the dynamic from the outside: "I don't even want to go over to the friend's house because they're just going to bitch about the boyfriend again."

"You see this all the time where friends are saying, 'oh, he's such an asshole.' And I was like, 'yeah, he's such an asshole.' And they all kind of like nod. And then they walk away from the conversation. They might feel a little better for a little while, but like 10 of those conversations later, they all feel like shit all the time after these conversations. We've all had the friend that's like, 'I don't even want to go over to the friend's house because they're just going to bitch about the boyfriend again. It's like the same old routine story.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself

The pattern looks like supportive friendship. Structurally it is a compound failure: the venter does not move, the listener gets depleted, the friendship becomes a routine that both quietly start to dread.

## What Is Actually Happening

The listener is being in the venter, not with them. See [[being-with-vs-being-in]]. The listener has merged into the venter's story, adopted the venter's view of the boyfriend, joined the chorus of agreement. This feels like empathy to both parties. It is not empathy. It is empathic merger, which is a failure mode of empathy. See [[losing-yourself-in-others]].

The venter is in their story. See [[in-their-story]]. They are recounting events, recycling the same grievance, getting agreement, and not actually feeling the underlying emotion that the venting is circling around. The story is the wall against the feeling. Each venting session reinforces the story without surfacing the feeling. The structure of the venting prevents the very release the venter is reaching for.

The friend's agreement is the structural confirmation that the story is the appropriate response. If a thousand friends say "yeah, he's such an asshole," the story is now thoroughly socially-confirmed. The venter has even more reason to stay in the story. The agreement makes the loop tighter, not looser.

## Why It Feels Good Briefly

The short-term feel-good is real. Validation from another person produces a small wave of relief. The venter feels seen. The friend feels useful. Both have done the work of friendship, by the standard model where friendship = mutual support = agreeing with each other's complaints.

But the relief is shallow and the bill compounds. The shallow part: the story has been confirmed, not moved. The underlying feeling has not been touched. The venter walks away with the same complaint, the same boyfriend, the same loop. The compounding part: each session has slightly increased the venter's investment in the story and slightly decreased the friend's tolerance for hearing it.

## The Long-Run Cost

After ten of these conversations:
- The venter has not moved. The boyfriend situation is the same or worse, because the energy that would have gone into either transforming the situation or transforming themselves has been spent on confirming the story.
- The friend is depleted. Each conversation drained them and produced nothing.
- Both feel like shit after the conversations. The empathic merger means the friend has been carrying around the venter's grievance for the days between.
- The friendship has acquired a routine that both privately want to avoid. The dread is the friendship's own report on the dynamic.
- The venter has become the friend who is "always complaining about the boyfriend," which is a social identity that further locks the loop. The story is now both internal and reputational.

## What The Alternative Looks Like

Being with the venter instead of in them. See [[being-with-vs-being-in]]. The listener stays in their own body, registers the venter's emotion as information about the venter, and brings curiosity instead of agreement.

The alternative response: "I see that you really think that they're an asshole. Oh, what's going on?"

"If you're actually with them, oh, I see that you really think that they're an asshole. Oh, what's going on? Like, and being curious about that instead of buying into the story, then there's just a lot more room for the transformation." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself

Notice the shape of the response. It does not deny the venter's view. It does not redirect. It does not advise. It does three small things at once:
- Acknowledges the venter's experience: "I see that you really think they're an asshole."
- Returns the story to the venter as their experience rather than as a shared fact: "you think" rather than "yeah he is."
- Opens curiosity about the venter, not the boyfriend: "what's going on?" The subject of the curiosity is the venter's state, not the boyfriend's behavior.

This shape leaves room for the venter to drop into what they are actually feeling underneath the story. The story was the wall. With the friend not joining the wall, the venter has the chance to feel through it. This is the transformation that joining-the-venting structurally prevented.

## Why It Is Hard to Do This as the Listening Friend

The standard friendship script says: agree with your friend's complaints. To not agree feels like betrayal. To bring curiosity instead of solidarity feels cold, or therapist-like, or judgmental. The friend who tries the with-not-in response often feels like a bad friend in the moment.

The reframe: agreeing-with-the-story is the betrayal, not the support. The agreement keeps the venter stuck. The curiosity is the actual support, even though it does not feel like support in the moment. Over time, the venter who has friends who can be with them rather than in them is the venter who moves. The venter whose entire social circle joins the chorus is the venter who stays in the loop.

This is also why some friends become uncomfortable around therapist-trained people. The therapist-trained friend stopped doing the chorus a long time ago. The chorus-doing friends feel the loss of solidarity. The reality is that the therapist-trained friend is the one offering real friendship; the chorus-doing friends are offering routine.

## Why It Is Hard to Do This as the Venter

When the listener does not join the chorus, the venter feels temporarily un-met. The expected response did not come. The venter has to do something different — either escalate (and notice the listener not joining), or pause, or actually feel something. The pause is the door. The pause is what venting-with-agreement was preventing.

If the venter is committed to the story, they will look for a different friend who will join the chorus. The venter who is starting to want to move will register the with-stance as relief: finally, someone who is not just confirming the loop.

## The Application Back Into Romantic and Family Relationships

The same pattern operates inside primary relationships. A partner who is venting about work. A parent who is venting about a sibling. A child who is venting about a teacher. The temptation in each case is to agree with the complainer's view of the third party. The agreement looks like loyalty. It produces the same compounding failure.

Being with the partner, parent, or child — without joining the case against the absent third party — is what actually allows the person to move through what they are circling. The fight is more available. The transformation is more available. "Fighting is all about figuring out how you can transform together. And if you're in the person, it's very hard to see yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself. The same is true outside the fight: the venting is asking to be moved through, not confirmed.

## Detection Cues

- The same complaint about the same person, recurring across many conversations, with no movement in the situation.
- The friend is becoming someone you or others quietly want to avoid.
- The conversations feel routinized — the same structure each time, the same conclusions.
- After the conversation, both parties feel slightly worse, not better.
- The complaint has become part of the person's social identity.
- Multiple friends in the venter's social circle are all giving the same agreement, and nothing in the situation is moving.
- You catch yourself dreading the next conversation with this person while still loving them.
- You notice you have started joining the chorus automatically, with no actual evaluation of the third party.
