# Pendulation

## Definition

Human learning moves back and forth. A baby does not crawl, then stand, then walk, then never crawl again. They go back and forth between stages. This pendulation is not failure. It is the mechanism of learning. Without it, the brain develops differently. If you pull a butterfly from its cocoon, it cannot fly. The struggle is the process.

The brain requires the back-and-forth to lay down a learning. Understanding, then not understanding, then understanding, then not understanding. This is not the loop the conscious mind would design. It is the loop the brain actually runs. The conscious mind would prefer a single forward sweep with no regression. The brain does not work that way, and any attempt to force it to work that way slows the learning.

"It's really important that you titrate, that you go pendulate back and forth to be able to learn something. And so if every time you're like in the natural learning process from understanding to not understanding to understanding to not understanding, if you beat yourself up, then you slow down that entire process." — Joe Hudson, 3 Mindsets That Transform Self-Development

## When Someone Experiences This

The most common expression: "It was going so well and now it's all gone." The person had a breakthrough, felt expanded, and now feels contracted, stuck, or back to baseline. They interpret the return as evidence that the work failed.

The reframe: "I am in the learning process. This is how learning goes. I am getting closer because it's gone."

"'Man, it was like I was feeling so blissed out and now all that's gone. How do I get it back?' The other way to look at it is to say, oh cool, that's gone. I am in the learning process." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

Another version: the person starts feeling more negative emotions after beginning the work. Two things may be happening. They may be recognizing emotions that were always present but previously unconscious (taken out on others without awareness). Or their capacity has increased, and what was unlovable is surfacing to be loved.

"When people can love themselves, every part of themselves that was unlovable comes to the surface to be loved, or the next wave of them." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

## What Joe Does

Names the pattern. Normalizes it. The pendulation is not the problem. The resistance to the pendulation is the problem. If you buy into the story that you have lost the progress, enjoyment disappears. If you recognize it as learning, enjoyment returns and the learning accelerates. See [[enjoyment-as-path]].

The specific anti-pattern within pendulation: beating yourself up for the "out" half of the cycle is the move that converts a healthy pendulation into a stalled one. The "out" is the brain doing what it has to do to integrate. Attacking yourself for the "out" trains the nervous system to avoid the moment of catching the out — which is the moment of being back in the practice. See [[punishing-the-remembering]] for the full mechanism. The principles work together: pendulation describes the structural pattern; punishing-the-remembering names the specific anti-pattern that breaks the pattern.

## The Tennis Frame

A practical image for how the pendulation works in a learnable skill: you hit, then miss, then hit, then miss. The hits and misses are not a problem to solve. They are the data the body uses to refine the swing. Each miss is part of how the next hit gets dialed in. There is no learning curve that skips the misses.

Applied to self-development: you are listening with an open heart, then you forget and you are not, then you remember and you are again, then you forget and you are not. The cycle is the practice. Trying to eliminate the "forget" half is trying to eliminate the mechanism of learning.

"Let's say you're playing tennis and you hit the ball when you're playing tennis and then you miss and then you hit and then you miss and then you hit. There's this idea that if you beat yourself up every time you miss, you're gonna somehow improve. And that's not how it works at all in self-development." — Joe Hudson, 3 Mindsets That Transform Self-Development
