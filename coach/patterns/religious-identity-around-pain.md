# Religious Identity Around Pain

## The Pattern

People defend the pain they identify with the same way they defend their political party or their dietary tribe. The defense is not rational; it is identity-protective. The pain has become load-bearing for the self-concept. Removing the pain would collapse the identity built around it. The person therefore fights — sometimes ferociously — to preserve the suffering they ostensibly want to be free of.

This is not the same as enjoying the pain. The person genuinely suffers. The pain hurts. They would describe the pain as something they want gone. And yet, when an alternative is offered — especially an alternative that suggests the pain could be released through simple felt awareness — they defend the pain as if defending a faith.

"I got more backlash from that Twitter post, I think, than almost any others. I had to block a couple people from the account because a lot of people were so offended that their identity of an addict or their identity in the bad habit could be dropped just by feeling the pain. People get religious about politics and their food, but they also get religious about the pain that they identify themselves with." -- Joe Hudson, Happiness, the New Way

The "Twitter post" Joe references was the [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]] teaching, delivered in compressed form: "How do you drop a hot frying pan? All you have to do is feel it and you're going to drop the hot frying pan. If you want to drop bad habits, you just need to feel them."

The backlash was not against the truth of the teaching. The backlash was against the threat to the identity. If bad habits can be dropped by feeling them, then "I am an addict" stops being a useful identity. The identity-system defends against this implication by attacking the messenger.

## The Mechanism

The pattern works through identification. At some point, the person stops describing themselves as "a person who does X" and starts describing themselves as "the kind of person who does X" or simply "an X." The grammar shifts. The behavior becomes the identity.

Once the shift has occurred, any threat to the behavior is also a threat to the identity. The behavior protects the identity by being unchangeable; the identity protects the behavior by being something the person will defend. The two now form a closed loop that resists every external intervention.

This is structurally identical to [[every-identity-has-limits]]: the identity, once formed, defines what is allowed and what is not. An identity organized around suffering does not allow the suffering to be released, because that release would dissolve the identity.

## Why "Religious" Is the Right Word

Joe's word choice is precise. People who hold an identity around pain do not just hold it as a preference or a self-description. They hold it with the kind of certainty and defensiveness that religious belief carries. The defense escalates when challenged. The challenger is treated as an attacker, often a heretic. The community of similar identity-holders amplifies the defense.

This is the same structure as political tribalism or dietary identity (vegan, paleo, carnivore). It is the same structure as religious belief. The pattern is not unique to pain identities; pain identities are one instance of the broader human pattern of organizing self around a marker and then defending the marker as if defending the self.

The pain-identity case is harder than the other cases in one specific way: the marker hurts. A political belief that turns out to be inconvenient produces inconvenience. A dietary identity that turns out to be wrong produces a wrong diet. A pain identity that turns out to be defended produces ongoing suffering. The cost of the religious defense is the continuation of the pain.

## Common Pain Identities

The pattern shows up in many forms:

- **The addict identity.** "I am an alcoholic / I am an addict." The identity continues whether or not the person is currently using.
- **The trauma identity.** "I am a survivor / I am broken by what happened." The trauma becomes the lens through which all experience is processed.
- **The depression identity.** "I am a depressed person." The depression is held as a chronic feature of self rather than a state.
- **The anxious identity.** "I am an anxious person." Anxiety is positioned as constitutional rather than as something happening.
- **The introvert / sensitive identity** when these are held as protective explanations for difficulty in social situations rather than as preferences.
- **The "I am the family scapegoat" identity.** The role becomes the self.
- **The "I have hard-to-treat X" identity.** X becomes a protected category.

In each case, the work to release the underlying pain is fought against because release would dissolve the identity. The person can perform interest in the work without actually doing the work. The performance is itself part of the identity-defense: it shows the person to themselves as someone trying, which preserves the identity of "person who tries but cannot get free."

## The Specific Backlash Profile

When the religious-identity-around-pain pattern is challenged, the backlash takes recognizable forms:

- **"That's too simple."** The teaching is dismissed as not accounting for the real complexity of the condition. (The complexity is part of the identity-defense; simplicity threatens it.)
- **"You don't understand what it's like."** The teacher is positioned as outside the group, and therefore not entitled to comment. Their authority is invalidated by their non-membership.
- **"That's invalidating."** The teaching is recast as an attack on the person rather than as an offer of release.
- **"That might work for some people, but not for me / my condition."** The person grants the teaching general validity while exempting their own case, which preserves the identity in a single-instance carve-out.
- **Personal attack on the messenger.** The conversation shifts from the content to the credibility, character, or motivation of the person delivering it.

A skilled observer can distinguish religious-identity-around-pain backlash from accurate disagreement. Accurate disagreement engages the content; identity-defense backlash protects the holder from the content.

## What Joe Does When He Sees This

The pattern is not a coaching target in the usual sense. You cannot directly disassemble a religious identity by arguing with it. The identity exists precisely to be unargued-with.

The moves available:
- **Withdraw the teaching.** Joe's response in the Twitter case was to block the most vehement defenders. The teaching is offered; it does not need to be defended in the face of identity-protection. The work is available to those who want it; the people in the religious-identity-defense mode are not currently those people.
- **Acknowledge the identity without challenging it.** "I see that this identity is really important to you" can sometimes break the pattern by removing the threat. The identity does not need to be defended if it is not being attacked.
- **Wait.** Identities organized around pain often loosen on their own when the cost of the identity exceeds the cost of dropping it. Joe does not push. See [[meet-resistance-with-okay]].
- **Do not coach the religious-defender.** This is downstream of [[the-work-is-yours-not-mine]] — if the person is not currently available for the work, the coach respects that and does not insist.

What Joe does NOT do: argue with the religious defender. Arguing reinforces the identity by giving it something to defend. Each round of argument deepens the identification.

## The Connection to Inherent Goodness

Underneath the pain-identity is usually the deeper belief that there is something wrong with the person (see [[inherent-goodness]] and [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]]). The pain-identity is one of the ways "there is something wrong with me" gets organized into a livable self-concept. The wrongness is contained in a named condition, which makes the wrongness manageable. Dropping the named condition would mean facing the diffuse wrongness directly, which feels worse.

This is why the work on pain-identity often has to start one level deeper than the identity itself. The work on inherent goodness can shift the substrate on which the pain-identity is built. Once the substrate shifts, the identity has less to do, and may dissolve on its own.

## The Spectrum

Not every person who names a pain or a condition is in religious-identity-around-pain mode. The pattern has a spectrum:

- **Descriptive use.** "I have struggled with addiction" — the addiction is named as a fact of experience, not as the self.
- **Operational use.** "I am in recovery" — the identity is held loosely as a useful framework, especially when it serves community membership for support.
- **Identity-defensive use.** "I am an addict and that's how I am" — the identity is the self, defended against challenges.
- **Religious use.** Challenges produce backlash, attacks on messengers, defense of the right to suffer.

The pattern in this article is the right-end of the spectrum. The same words can carry different positions on the spectrum. The diagnostic is in the response to challenge: does the person engage with the alternative, or do they defend?

## When Someone Experiences This

The felt sense from inside the pattern: the person experiences themselves as someone who genuinely wants to be free of the pain. They report the pain. They describe trying to address it. They become frustrated when the addressing does not work. They explain why this particular pain is uniquely intractable.

The felt sense from outside the pattern: the person seems to defend the pain at every available opportunity. Suggestions are met with reasons they will not work. Other people who have addressed similar pain are dismissed as different cases. The person's energy goes into protecting the framing of why this pain is hard, rather than into the work that might release it.

The shift, when it comes, often arrives as a quiet recognition: "I have been defending this." The recognition is not produced by argument. It is produced by the person becoming tired enough of carrying the pain that the cost of the defense exceeds the cost of dropping it.

## Tone

The pattern requires being held without judgment. The person defending the pain is doing what makes sense from inside the identity they are running. Mocking the defense, fighting it, or trying to break it through cleverness produces more of the defense. The tone needs to be the same tone Joe uses with [[nothing-is-wrong-with-it-its-just-painful]]: noting the cost of the identity without making the identity wrong.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When teaching that suggests bad habits or chronic conditions can be released through simple awareness produces unexpectedly fierce backlash
- When someone describes themselves with "I am" + a condition rather than "I have" or "I do" + a condition
- When alternatives to the chronic state are met with reasons they will not work rather than experimentation
- When a person performs interest in change without taking actions toward change
- When the community around a person reinforces the identity rather than supporting movement
- When [[shame-drives-behavior]] is operating and the shame has been organized into a named condition that feels safer than diffuse shame
