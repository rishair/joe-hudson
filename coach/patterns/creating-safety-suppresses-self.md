# Trying to Create Safety for the Partner Suppresses the Self

## The Pattern

A common shape in long-term partnerships, especially after one or both partners have experienced significant loss or fear: one partner takes on the felt obligation to be safe, stable, predictable, and dependable for the other. They modulate their behavior, their risk-taking, their expressiveness, their plans, all in service of producing a feeling of safety in the other person.

The cost is the suppression of the self that was being chosen. The partner who is performing safety becomes a smaller, more constrained, less alive version of themselves. The relationship becomes a smaller, more constrained, less alive version of itself. The very thing that was being protected (the partner, the relationship) is being degraded by the protection.

From the transcript, Brett describing his own version of it:

"Yeah, inside there, there's the fear of being loved as I am, inclusive of my, uh, some form of like not knowing my risk tolerance, my naivete, my not being able to guarantee any form of safety... Yeah, underneath that there's a grief of -- there's a grief underneath feeling like all of that has ever been my responsibility. Yeah, or so beautiful feeling -- yeah, feeling penned into my own cage of that I need to be some sort of like safe, stable, predictable, alive for someone else." -- Brett Kistler, Marriage, Love, and Death

The phrase "penned into my own cage" carries the felt sense. The person built the cage themselves, out of the felt obligation to be safe for the other. The bars are the suppressed risk-taking, the unspoken wants, the truncated expressions of self. The person is the captor and the captive. The partner did not put them there. The pattern did.

## How the Pattern Forms

Often after a loss. One partner has lost a previous partner (to death, to illness, to abandonment) and the current partner takes on the felt task of being the one who does not produce another loss. The task feels noble. The task is also self-suppression.

Often after a partner names a fear of loss. The eggs-in-one-basket fear, expressed by one partner, lands in the other as an injunction to manage the risk. The managing produces the cage.

Often in childhood patterns where one parent was felt as unsafe and the child grew up with the imperative to be the safe one. The pattern then transfers to adult partnerships.

Often around extreme sports, risky work, or expressive ambitions. The partner who would otherwise pursue these things suppresses the pursuit to spare the other partner the fear. The suppression is rarely named as such. It is named as "being responsible" or "growing up" or "settling down."

## Why the Pattern Is Self-Defeating

The partner being protected does not actually want the protected version. What was attracting in the first place was the alive version. The suppressed version, performed as care, registers as a kind of absence. The relationship loses what it was about.

Alexa names this directly in the transcript when Brett describes his version of the cage:

"I want to own that it doesn't feel like I chose this part of you. Like, this doesn't feel like the thing that I'm attracted to, but I do feel like I'm saying yes to all of you... And I have a partiality towards you not having, yeah, like the feeling of obligation to stick around for me, or like the resentment that's in there and the as Tara said, the sort of like ping-ponging or swinging back and forth." -- Alexa, Marriage, Love, and Death

The partner did not ask for the cage. The partner is asking for the whole person, including the parts that produce fear in the partner. The cage is being maintained by the cage-builder's projection of what the partner needs.

This is the same mechanism as [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]: the strategy meant to prevent the loss (suppressing the self to be safe) produces a version of loss (the partner is losing the actual person they chose).

## What's Underneath the Pattern

Several layers usually stack underneath the safety-creation behavior:

**Fear of being responsible for the partner's pain.** The partner who creates safety often imagines, vividly, the partner's devastation if loss occurs and treats prevention of that imagined devastation as their personal responsibility. See [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] and [[dont-make-it-your-job-to-prevent-their-triggers]].

**Identity wrapped up in being the safe one.** "Being the safe partner" can become a self-concept that the person defends even as it costs them. Letting the identity go feels like a betrayal of love, when actually it is the precondition for love being fully present.

**Grief about the responsibility itself.** Often the felt sense underneath the cage is grief -- not about anything that has happened, but about how long the person has carried the felt obligation to be safe for others. The grief becomes available when the cage is named.

**The ping-pong of responsibility vs. rebellion.** The pattern often coexists with periodic rebellion against the suppression. See [[dont-ping-pong-on-responsibility]]. Neither end of the ping-pong addresses the underlying grief.

**Projection.** Often the partner who is creating safety is projecting their own fear of being alone or abandoned onto the partner, then trying to prevent the imagined version of their own fear. "I have to keep them safe" becomes the surrogate for "I have to keep myself from feeling the version of the loss that I am most afraid of."

From the transcript:

"Yeah, it feels like there's like the -- there's a fear of being alone that I project onto her. Like, I project that she wouldn't be able to handle losing me if -- when she speaks of the eggs in one basket thing, when in reality I feel like I wouldn't be able to handle it." -- Brett, Marriage, Love, and Death

The pattern is most movable when this layer is reached. Once the safety-creation is recognized as containment of the cage-builder's own fear, the work moves from "should I be safe for them" to "what am I avoiding feeling in myself."

## The Apology That Often Emerges

When the pattern is fully felt, the move that often comes is an apology -- not for being unsafe, but for the suppression itself. See [[apologize-for-trying-to-control]] and [[upright-apology]].

From the transcript:

"What comes up is sort of strange, but it's like a, an apology for any way that I've tried to create safety in any way that suppresses myself or us." -- Brett, Marriage, Love, and Death

The phrase "or us" is the recognition. The suppression cost not only the self. It cost the relationship. Both are being apologized for.

The apology, when offered cleanly, is often received as relief by the partner who was on the receiving end of the safety-creation. The partner had been feeling the missing aliveness without being able to name what they were missing.

## What to Do Instead

The corrective is not to become unsafe. Recklessness is not the alternative to safety-creation. The corrective is to:

**Stop performing safety.** Be the actual person, with the actual risk tolerance, the actual desires, the actual range. See [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]].

**Let the partner have their own fear.** The fear of loss is theirs. Trying to prevent the fear by suppressing yourself does not actually prevent it. It just adds your suppression to the situation. See [[dont-make-it-your-job-to-prevent-their-triggers]].

**Process the fear together rather than alone.** When both partners are willing to feel their fears of loss in the other's presence, the fear is shared rather than managed. The vulnerability often deepens connection rather than threatening it. See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]] and [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]].

**Apologize for the suppression.** The clean upright apology for the safety-creation often does more than years of careful behavior. It reopens the space the cage had constrained.

**Find the boundaries that include you.** Often the safety-creation has been a substitute for actual boundaries. See [[heart-opening-needs-boundaries]]. The way to include yourself in the love is not to be safer; it is to be more clearly bounded.

## When This Pattern Shows Up

- A partner who has been described by themselves or the other as "responsible," "stable," "the safe one" for years and is now feeling penned in.
- A partner whose risk-taking, expressive, or wild parts have been quietly suppressed since the relationship began.
- A partner who, when invited to be fully themselves, immediately feels guilt about the impact on the other.
- A relationship that feels good and safe but is missing aliveness, sexuality, surprise, range.
- A partner who has been managing the other's emotions for so long that they have lost track of their own.
- A person who reports being exhausted by being the strong/safe one. See [[im-exhausted-holding-space-for-my-partner]] for the related concern.
