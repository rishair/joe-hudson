# The Quit-Hope-Lose Cycle

## Definition

A predictable cycle in personal change:

1. "I should change this." Building motivation through shoulds.
2. "Why haven't I done it yet?" Self-criticism.
3. The change happens. Relief.
4. "I hope this lasts." Gripping the new state.
5. "It's already going away." Fear of losing it.
6. The old pattern returns. Despair.
7. Back to step 1: "I have to change this."

Each stage feeds the next. The whole cycle is self-reinforcing.

"The idea that it's gonna go away is the thing that creates it going away." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

## The Intervention

The easiest place to break the cycle is at step 4: when the change has occurred. Instead of gripping ("I hope this lasts"), appreciate. Stay in enjoyment of this moment rather than fearing the next one.

"One of the easiest places to cut it off is when that moment when you actually have quit smoking is to appreciate it, is to actually just appreciate that moment and to keep on appreciating it instead of trying to hold on to it." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

The same applies at every stage. If you are still smoking, how do you enjoy each cigarette? If you are yelling at your spouse, how do you enjoy the yelling? Nobody actually enjoys yelling. Trying to enjoy it changes the behavior or stops it.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone has a history of starting and stopping the same change
- When the language includes "I hope this lasts" or "I'm afraid I'll go back to..."
- When [[process-over-result]] has not been internalized: the person is attached to a fixed state

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[enjoyment-as-path]]: appreciation of the present moment replaces gripping the future
- Toward [[pendulation]]: understanding that the return of the old pattern is part of learning, not evidence of failure
- Away from [[wants-over-shoulds]]: the cycle is powered by shoulds; replacing them with wants changes the engine
