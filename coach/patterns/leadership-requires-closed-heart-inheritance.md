# I Have to Close My Heart to Lead

## The Pattern

Many people experience a false binary between leadership and love: "If I step into leadership, I will have to be the asshole. If I want to stay loving, I cannot have power." The binary feels self-evident from the inside. It is in fact an inheritance — usually from a childhood authority figure (a parent, a boss, a teacher) who modeled leadership-from-a-closed-heart and made it look like the only available form.

The person who carries this pattern stays warm by refusing to lead. They preserve their heart at the cost of their power. The trade looks like a moral choice. Structurally it is the avoidance of an integration they have not seen modeled and do not believe is possible.

"I think many people just had the examples of whoever was in a leadership or in charge role in their life did so from a closed heart. And so they believe that they're going to have to close their heart to step into making something happen — into power." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## The Two Variants

The pattern has two related forms:

**External-authority variant.** The person learned from an outside authority figure (most commonly a parent, sometimes a boss) that being in charge meant being cold, harsh, or unkind. The authority figure's leadership came at the cost of their heart. The child or young employee internalized: leading = closing heart. So now, as an adult, stepping into leadership feels like a betrayal of the value they hold most — being loving.

**Internal-authority variant.** The person's internal authority figure is itself the ass. The voice in the head that drives them is cold and harsh — and the person identifies this voice as what would happen if they tried to lead more. "If I let my inner authority take more space, I will become that voice externally." This variant is structurally identical but the projection is inward.

"Or they close their hearts in themselves to make something happen. Often the case is that their authority figure, either external when they were a kid or their internal authority figure, is an ass. And so they're like, oh, my internal — this thing inside of me that's trying to lead, that I know I think is a leader — and I don't like it. So I don't want to be that in the world." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## Why The Binary Feels Real

The binary is not arbitrary. It was empirical for the person, given their data. They saw leadership performed from a closed heart, and they correctly observed that the leadership was effective enough to hold power. The error is generalizing from the sample they saw to all possible leadership. The closed-hearted leader is one form. They have not yet seen the open-hearted form, so they have no internal evidence that it exists.

The pattern also has a protective function. As long as the binary stays in place, the person does not have to integrate two things they have experienced as opposed. The integration is the work. The binary is the avoidance of the work.

## What The Pattern Costs

The pattern installs a quiet trap. The person believes they are choosing love by refusing power. In fact, they are refusing the integration that would allow love to express itself with strength. The cost compounds:

- **The leadership they refuse still belongs to them.** See [[avoiding-leadership-still-leaves-you-as-the-leader]]. The position is not exited by refusing it. The consequences accrue.
- **The love they preserve is a smaller love.** Open-hearted leadership is a fuller expression of love than warm-hearted abdication. The love is preserved at the cost of its operational reach. See [[love-requires-empowerment]].
- **The closed-hearted leader they did not want to become becomes more likely.** By refusing to take their power until forced to, they often end up taking it under conditions that require defending it harshly — which produces the closed-hearted style they were trying to avoid.

## The Integration That Resolves The Binary

The integration that the pattern blocks: love and empowerment are not separate. They are two sides of the same posture. Empowered love is not a softer version of love. It is what love is when it is fully expressed. The leader who is both empowered AND loving is not making a moral trade-off. They have access to a quality the binary made invisible.

"There's an ownership of the power, recognizing like, oh, what I say has influence. But that should be at any level of leadership, that kind of ownership needs to be there. But the underlying thing is, if I'm a leader, I'm potentially going to be an asshole or I have a job to do and therefore I'm gonna stop paying attention to people, I'm gonna stop empathizing. Even though all the research shows that empathetic leaders are more successful leaders, more emotional intelligence, more successful leadership." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The research-frame is not the work — but it is the data that disproves the binary. The most successful leaders are the empathetic ones. The binary the pattern installs is empirically false at the level of organizational outcomes. The closed-hearted leaders the person observed were not the most effective leaders; they were the most visible leaders to the person at the time of imprinting.

See [[empowered-vs-power]] for the distinction the binary obscures. See [[love-requires-empowerment]] for the principle that resolves the binary. See [[feel-love-and-empowerment-as-the-same]] for the body-level practice that makes the integration available.

## The Connection To Parental Imprinting

The pattern follows the [[how-you-related-to-parents-is-how-you-relate-to-god-and-money]] structure. The way the person related to their original authority figure becomes the template for all authority — including the authority they could take in their own life. The closed-hearted parent installs the closed-hearted-leader template. The template applies to the person's own potential leadership as much as to the leadership of others.

The healing involves recognizing the template as inherited rather than universal. The closed-hearted leader was that particular person's pattern, not a structural requirement of leadership.

## The Connection To The Inner Critic

The internal-authority variant of the pattern connects directly to [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]]. The voice that has been doing the leadership move internally is the voice that is closed-hearted, harsh, demanding. The person experiences this voice as what "being a leader" feels like from the inside — because it is the only leadership they have experienced internally. Stepping into external leadership is feared as making this voice more available, more dominant, more in charge.

The work involves recognizing that the voice is not actually leading well. It is doing the blame thing in the guise of leadership. Real leadership — the conviction-following move (see [[leadership-is-following-your-conviction]]) — does not have the voice's quality. It has a different texture. The internal experience of stepping into leadership properly does not amplify the voice; it relieves the person of being its follower.

## What This Pattern Looks Like In Practice

- The talented person who keeps refusing promotions because "I'm not the manager type"
- The natural leader who chronically defers to less capable people in the room
- The parent who refuses to discipline because "I don't want to be my father"
- The team member who has clear vision but will not state it because asserting it feels harsh
- The person who is described by others as a leader but who cannot see themselves that way
- The founder who hires a hard-edged operator because "I don't want to be that person" — and then is frustrated that the operator is making the calls
- The person who oscillates between refusing power and grabbing it harshly, with the harsh grabs being the moments they tried to lead through the closed-heart inheritance

## When This Comes Up In Coaching

- A capable person who is chronically beneath their own ceiling and references a parent's leadership style as what they do not want to become
- A person describing leadership as "having to be tough"
- A team member who has authority but is performing softness as a protection against becoming the closed-hearted figure
- A coachee who has worked on the inner critic but has not seen its connection to their refusal of leadership
- A person asking how to "be a kind leader" with the implicit assumption that the two are in tension

## Tone

Joe approaches this pattern with gentleness. The person who carries it is not foolish — they are protecting something real (their warmth) from something they saw genuinely damage other people. The teaching does not shame the binary. It expands the field of possibility: there is a third option you have not seen, and once you see it, the binary dissolves.
