# Push-Pull Dynamics in Dating

## The Pattern

The whole family of dating problems -- not just the anxious-avoidant dance -- has the same structure. The conscious mind is reaching for love. The body is using a specific behavior to keep love from arriving. The reaching and the pushing-away are the same person. The behaviors differ. The structure is constant.

"All of the ways in which we do it are consciously saying come here, come here, come here, to love, and then subconsciously pushing it away." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The pattern is governed by [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]]. The push-away is the protective half of a configuration that is trying to get closeness without crossing the threshold where the feared self-loss would happen. The push-away usually succeeds at its actual job. The relationship does not arrive, or arrives in a form that cannot deepen, or arrives and then collapses before the threshold is crossed.

This is the same structural mechanism as [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]] applied to romantic partnership specifically. The wanting and the not-wanting are the same move.

## The Family of Forms

The same structure shows up in multiple costumes. Each form looks like a different problem. Each form is the same configuration in different clothes.

**Jealousy.** "I want you, I want you, I want you, but I'm going to abuse you and gaslight you, so you're not going to want to be around me, but I want you, I want you, I want you." The intensity of wanting is precisely matched by the intensity of the behavior that drives the partner away. The jealous person is unable, from inside the configuration, to see that the jealousy is the push. From outside, the structure is obvious.

"I've never heard a jealous person on their own come to me and say, like, yeah, I'm actively pushing away that person that I really want. But when I look at them in the eye and I'm like, hey, why are you pushing away this person with your jealousy? They kind of get this, like, subconscious grin on their face, and they're like, ooh, busted." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The grin is the body's recognition that the conscious story has been pierced. The subconscious knew the whole time.

**Neediness.** Nobody wants a sexual relationship with someone they have to take care of. The needy person is reaching for connection ("be here for me, hold me, fix this") while installing a parent-child dynamic that kills the peer relationship the connection would require. The neediness is the push. The partner withdraws because they are being asked to caretake, not to partner.

"Nobody wants a sexual relationship with their, like, someone they have to take care of, right? So on one level it's like, oh, I want you, why aren't you here for me? On another level, I'm being needy and I'm pushing you away because we want to have relationships with peers, not people we have to caretake." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

**Hyper-independence / self-reliance.** The independent person says they want a partner who will really be there for them, while the self-reliance prevents anyone from being able to be there. The strong, capable person who never shows weakness is hard to help, hard to support, hard to partner with. The self-reliance reads as not needing, which reads as not letting in. This is the push. See [[self-reliance-pushes-people-away]] for the developed form.

"That self-reliance makes it really hard for people to show up for you... they're hard to help because they never show any weakness. They're hard to help because they say I've got it I've got it I've got it. They're hard to help because when you help them, they get angry at you." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

**Transactionality.** The transactional person trusts only the explicit deal. "I know what you want, you know what I want, here is what we are exchanging." This protects against the disorientation of love, which is not transactional. The constant negotiation is the push. The intimacy that wants to arrive cannot land in a configuration that requires every exchange to be specified. See [[transactional-relationships-as-defense]].

**The checklist.** The list of six things the partner must have is a structure that filters out almost everyone, and reliably filters out the people the body might actually fall in love with. The person never ends up with what was on the checklist when love actually arrives. The checklist's function was not to find the partner; the checklist's function was to keep love at a manageable distance. See [[the-partner-checklist-doesnt-survive-the-actual-relationship]].

**The "I'm not enough" frame.** "I am not pretty enough, rich enough, accomplished enough to be loved." The frame functions as a wait gate: once I become enough, then I will deserve to be loved. The gate never opens. The frame is the push. The Walmart counter-example: people across all body types, income levels, and accomplishment profiles have found partners. The absence of partnership is not explained by inadequacy. See [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]].

**The chronically-attracted-to-the-unavailable pattern.** This is the developed form documented in [[i-keep-chasing-emotionally-unavailable-people]] and [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]]. The chasing is the push. The available person is unattractive precisely because availability is what the configuration is set up to refuse.

The list is not exhaustive. Any behavior that simultaneously reaches for and rejects love is a member of the family. The diagnostic is the structure, not the specific behavior.

## What Holds the Pattern in Place

Each form serves the same function: it keeps the person away from the configuration where the feared loss-of-self would happen. The jealousy keeps me from the vulnerability of trust. The neediness keeps me from the disorientation of equal partnership. The self-reliance keeps me from the helplessness of needing. The transactionality keeps me from the unpredictability of love. The checklist keeps me from the inconvenience of falling for the wrong person. The "not enough" frame keeps me from the exposure of being seen as I am and choosing whether the love that finds me is enough.

All of these are forms of [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]] in action. Each form has its own historical origin (the parental relationship, past romantic injuries, the specific imprint of what love produced in the early environment) but the function is the same.

The pattern is held in place because, from inside, the push-away does not feel like a push-away. It feels like a reasonable behavior in response to a partner problem. The jealous person feels jealous because the partner did something. The needy person feels needy because no one is showing up for them. The transactional person feels transactional because everyone has agendas and at least theirs are explicit. The configuration is self-justifying.

## The Multiple-Form Reality

Most people running the pattern run multiple forms at once. The chasing-of-the-unavailable rarely shows up alone. It is usually paired with the checklist, the neediness, sometimes the transactionality. The forms are not exclusive; they are different facets of the same underlying configuration.

"There's plenty of examples of the ways that people push and pull. And usually there's multiple if someone's either only attracted to the emotionally unavailable person or the physically unavailable person, or whether they're continually searching for the right person. It's all another version of it." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The coaching implication: do not address one form at a time. Address the structure. Once the person sees that all of these are the same machinery, the work moves from "fix the jealousy" or "fix the checklist" to "see what the pattern is keeping you safe from."

## The Subconscious Grin

The subconscious grin is the diagnostic moment. When a person who has been running one of these forms is asked directly -- "why are you pushing this person away with your jealousy / your neediness / your self-reliance / your transactionality?" -- the conscious story falters and the body responds with the recognition. A small smile. A "busted" feeling. The body has known the whole time.

This is the workable moment. The conscious framing was protecting the pattern from inspection. The recognition opens the pattern to the work. The work that follows is at the level of the [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]] that the pattern was protecting.

The coach who does not see the grin can wait for it. The coach who pushes the recognition before the body is ready re-cements the conscious defense. The naming has to be direct enough to be heard and gentle enough not to provoke defense.

## The Beckoning-While-Pushing-Away Structure in Long Relationships

The same structure applies in established relationships, not just in dating. The marriage where sex has been dead for ten years is usually the marriage where one or both partners have been doing this dance throughout. The needy partner has been pushing the other away with neediness. The self-reliant partner has been pushing the other away with self-reliance. The jealous one with the jealousy. The transactional one with the transactionality. The forms persist; the relationship constrains them; the cost compounds. See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]], [[walking-on-eggshells]], and [[we-have-built-up-resentment]] for the long-form manifestations.

The work is the same as in dating. The configuration is the configuration. The fact that there is a partner present does not change what is happening.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A repeating pattern of "I really want this and it keeps not working out"
- The recognition that previous partners have all been the same in some specific way
- The felt sense that the configuration is producing the outcome they keep blaming on luck
- The "subconscious grin" moment when the structure is named accurately
- The experience of any of the specific forms (jealousy, neediness, hyper-independence, transactionality, the checklist, the not-enough frame) running despite knowing better

## Tone

Joe is direct about this. He names the structure without softening it. The directness is what allows the subconscious grin to appear. Soft delivery preserves the conscious story; direct delivery pierces it. The tone is not cruel; it is the tone of pointing out the obvious-once-named. The slight humor in the "busted" moment is part of the teaching.
