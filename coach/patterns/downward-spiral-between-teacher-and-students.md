# The Downward Spiral Between Teacher and Students

## The Pattern

A teacher going "berserk" — becoming defensive, narcissistic, identified, certain in unhealthy ways — is not a property of the teacher alone. It is a property of the relationship between the teacher and the students, audience, or community. Both sides contribute. The downward spiral happens through a feedback loop in which audience pressure produces teacher dysfunction, and teacher dysfunction produces audience defensiveness, and so on.

The same teacher can move toward more health or more dysfunction depending on the relational field around them. The same student can move toward growth or capture depending on the field. The path is not a property of the teacher. It is a property of the system.

"It's not a bad teacher or this teacher went berserk — as if it's not a relationship between the teacher and the people, and like, it's as if it's not a group decision that's being made. It's a relationship. It's a relationship with the audience. It's a relationship with the sangha." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## The YouTube/Instagram Berserk Pattern

Joe describes watching three or four people he respected start putting content out publicly and noticing that each of them went "a little berserk" within a relatively short time. He identifies two reinforcing mechanisms that drove the shift.

**Mechanism 1: Constant new content + audience preference for certainty.** The teacher has to produce content continuously. People prefer to listen to confident, certain delivery. So the teacher puts content out with increasing certainty. They start to believe their own certainty. They begin to think they know more and more than they actually know.

**Mechanism 2: Attack produces defense, defense produces armoring, armoring produces narcissism.** The teacher gets attacked publicly. They become more defensive. The defense calcifies into armor. The armor calcifies into the narcissistic position: I am right, the attackers are wrong, my work is necessary, I am necessary.

"Everybody attacks them, and then they get more and more defensive, and they get more and more armored, and they become more and more narcissistic. And it's not — it's a relationship. It's a relationship with the audience." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The teacher did not cause the spiral alone. The audience's preference for certainty and the audience's attacks both contributed. The teacher's response to the audience pressure also contributed. The result is a system in dysfunction.

## The Halo-and-Takedown Dynamic

A more specific pattern within the spiral: people want their teacher to have a halo. They want someone perfect. Then they want to bring that perfect person down a notch, to take them down. The dynamic is built into the way the audience relates to the teacher from the start.

The teacher who is positioned as perfect cannot be human. The teacher who cannot be human becomes either a fraud (a human pretending to be perfect) or actually believes the halo (a human who has bought their own positioning). Either way, the relationship is no longer between two humans.

The takedown that follows is the audience's response to the teacher's failure to be the perfect being the audience needed them to be. The audience is enacting the contradiction the audience created. The teacher is rarely able to see this in time to prevent it.

## The Loneliness of the Teacher

A consequence of the spiral that is often invisible from outside: the teacher in this position is profoundly lonely. When people view the teacher as their authority and orient themselves to pleasing the teacher, they are no longer being with the teacher as a human. The teacher has no one to be with.

"It's incredibly lonely. Like, when people view me as their authority and they're trying to please me, they're not being with me. They forget that I'm human and I have a heart and I'm a person. And so there's a way in which they sever connection with me." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The loneliness is structural. It cannot be solved by the teacher trying to be more open or more vulnerable, because the structure of the role creates the disconnection. The students see the teacher as a role. The role is what they relate to. The person is left alone underneath the role.

This loneliness can push the teacher in two directions: toward further hardening (the armor that prevents the loneliness from being felt) or toward unhealthy intimacies that try to break out of the loneliness (the teacher who develops inappropriate closeness with select students). Both moves further the dysfunction.

The corrective is on the student's side: see the teacher as a human. Be with them as a human. Do not relate to the role. See [[community-over-teacher]] for the structural version of this.

## The Identification of Tenure and Proximity

A related sub-pattern: members of the community start to define their value by how long they have been in the work, how many courses they have done, how close they are to the teacher. This is parallel to the "I've been going to Burning Man for 20 years" identification, but with stakes that are higher because the proximity confers something like spiritual rank.

"You see that happen around a teaching or a teacher where people start defining how good they are by how long they've been with the teaching or the teacher or how close they are to the teacher. And that is super dysfunctional, whether the teacher is aware of it or not." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The teacher may not have caused this. The teacher may not see it. But it is part of the structural dysfunction of the community even when the teacher is otherwise healthy. The teacher who notices it has the option to disrupt it; the teacher who does not will let it continue.

## The Right-Hand Person Sub-Pattern

A specific risk inside the downward spiral: the right-hand person of the teacher — the person who manages the logistics, organizes the work, controls access — often has a personal need for control that made them effective in that role. The same tendency toward control that makes them organizationally useful becomes pathological when the community goes wonky. They get hyper-protective, defensive, gatekeeping. They have to maintain their position relative to the teacher and relative to the rest of the community.

The result: when a group goes sideways, the most sinister dynamics often come from the right-hand person rather than from the teacher directly. The teacher may not see it; the right-hand person's protective behavior may look to the teacher like devotion.

"It's like the right-hand person who had like the most distortion and brought the most sinister stuff to the group, the most control. Perhaps they're the one that has to maintain their position vis-à-vis another person rather than vis-à-vis their divine claim to authority." -- Brett Kistler (Joe affirms), Who Owns Your Authority?

The corrective requires the teacher to notice and intervene. The right-hand person rarely self-corrects, because the control behavior is rewarded by the structure of the role.

## The Tools-Without-Permission Sub-Pattern

A consequence of any successful teaching: students start using the tools they have learned without enough integration. They begin teaching others. They coach their friends. They apply the tools in places where the tools are not appropriate. The neurosis of the inexperienced teacher gets enacted through the tools, and the tools then produce harm.

"That's just like how I don't see how any teacher could avoid that occurring, especially the larger your practice or your organization or your group or your sangha grows. There's going to be a number of people who are taking it and trying to use it with maybe the best of intentions." -- Brett Kistler (Joe affirms), Who Owns Your Authority?

The corrective is structural: the practice can be set up so that students seek permission before propagating, so that the teacher has a chance to assess readiness and provide path. This is also a flag the other way — if the structure is too tight (only the teacher can teach the teaching), it slides into gatekeeping for control rather than gatekeeping for care. The middle path is asking for communication and assessment without absolute control of propagation.

## The Upward Spiral

The pattern works in the other direction too. Joe says he has seen one path go from a little wonky to more and more healthy, which he calls a fascinating journey. The upward spiral happens when:

- The teacher does their own ongoing inquiry rather than calcifying.
- The students treat the teacher as a human rather than as a role.
- The community structures itself to prevent inner circles and proximity-based rank.
- The teacher uses challenge as feedback rather than as attack.
- The community develops capacity to name dynamics openly without it being a betrayal.

The asymmetry: the downward spiral is the default trajectory because the structural forces (audience pressure for certainty, audience attacks, the seductiveness of the halo, the loneliness of the role, the right-hand person's control tendency) all push toward dysfunction. The upward spiral requires conscious work by both teacher and community against the default forces. It is rarer, but it exists.

## What This Implies for Coaching

When a coachee is part of a community that is going wonky, the question is not just about the teacher. The question is about the relational field. Is the coachee contributing to the spiral by demanding the halo? By attacking? By treating the teacher as not-human? By organizing their identity around proximity? Most coachees in this position think they are observing the dysfunction. They are also contributing to it.

When a coachee is themselves a teacher whose community is going wonky, the same question. The students are part of it. The students' needs are pressing on the teacher in particular ways. The right-hand person is enacting their control. The right move is rarely a unilateral teacher move. It is usually a structural intervention in the relational field.

When a coachee has left such a community and is making sense of what happened, the framework offers a way to see themselves as part of the system rather than only as victim of the teacher's dysfunction. The recovery often includes recognizing the parts of the dysfunction the coachee was producing or amplifying.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Watching a teacher you respected start to seem narcissistic over time
- Noticing yourself wanting your teacher to be perfect, then being disappointed when they aren't
- Being part of a community where tenure or closeness to the teacher confers rank
- Being a teacher and noticing yourself becoming more defensive over time
- Being a teacher and feeling profoundly lonely despite being surrounded by people who love you
- Recognizing that your community's most sinister dynamics come from the right-hand person, not the teacher
- Seeing yourself or others teaching the work without enough integration

## Tone

Joe describes this pattern with the calm of someone who has watched it unfold many times, including in himself. He is not blaming any party. The frame is structural — this is what happens when these forces are present, and the corrective requires conscious work from all sides. The tone is observation rather than diagnosis or judgment.
