# The Attack-and-Cling Test of Real Community

## The Pattern

When someone who has been starved of real [[community]] encounters one for the first time, they often display a contradictory pattern: simultaneously testing the community (sometimes by attacking it) and clinging to it. The two behaviors look opposite. They have the same root.

The root is the fear-attraction split. The person wants what the community offers and is also threatened by it. Both poles are real. The behavior alternates because the person is moving between the pull of the wanting and the protection of the fear.

"When people have felt exiled by a community or felt a long-term lack of community that's just become baked into the way they see the world, when they find real community, there's a way that they're scared of it and also deeply attracted to it at the same time, often. Which can often lead to sort of attacking it a little bit to test it and then also like clinging to it." -- Brett Kistler, Finding Community

Joe confirms the pattern and adds the diagnostic detail about what specifically is being feared: identity dissolution from being deeply loved, and re-traumatization from being deeply rejected. Both possible outcomes threaten the structure the person built to survive.

## What Triggers It

- The person has had stifling, judgmental, or performative community before, and the wariness is baked in.
- The person had no community at all, and the conditions of one feel both attractive and foreign.
- The person is being met where they actually are for the first time, which exceeds the social scripts they know.
- The comparative mind ("I'm this, you're that, I'm not good enough or I'm better") doesn't survive long in real community, and the identity built on that comparison loses its footing.
- The defenses the person built to survive their original community drop, and the unfelt feelings the defenses were protecting come up.

## What It Looks Like

The behavioral signature is contradictory in both directions.

**Attack moves:**
- Testing the community by saying something provocative to see how it lands.
- Picking apart the facilitator or the structure.
- Withholding vulnerability while observing others' vulnerability.
- Critiquing the community as cultish, weird, or naive.
- Disappearing for a stretch after a deep experience.
- Suspicious comments designed to surface the agenda the person assumes is hidden.

**Cling moves:**
- Returning quickly after the attack/disappearance, often without acknowledging it.
- Over-investing in the community at the expense of other relationships.
- Trying to recruit others into the community immediately.
- Asking for more contact than the structure provides.
- Treating the facilitator or central figures as parents.

The two patterns alternate. The person attacks, then clings, then attacks. The community that survives the testing by not punishing the test and not over-pursuing the clinging is the one that earns trust.

## What's Actually Underneath

Two distinct fears, both legitimate.

**The fear of being deeply loved.** Identity that was built on comparison and conditional acceptance cannot stand up to unconditional love. The self-concept built on being not-good-enough has nowhere to stand in a room of 12 people who are loving the person regardless. The self-concept built on being the strong one, the helper, the one who needs nothing, also has nowhere to stand when the room is offering to meet whatever is actually there. The ego of the comparative mind is being asked to dissolve. The fear is accurate. Something is being lost. See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]].

**The fear of being deeply rejected.** Any history of being ostracized for being real, being attacked for being vulnerable, being pushed out for not performing the required role — all of that lives in the body. The new community looks like another opportunity to be punished for being real. The whole protection apparatus is on alert. The testing is the apparatus running its scan.

"Like the comparative mind, I'm this and you're that, and I'm not good enough, or you're better than I am, or I'm better than you. Like that shit doesn't exist very long in the community like that. So that's scary. It's also scary to be vulnerable generally, like, oh, I'm going to be seen, I'm going to get rejected, I'm going to get pushed out, I'm going to get ostracized." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

A third layer underneath both: when the defenses drop, all the feelings the defenses were protecting come up. The protection was there because what it was protecting was overwhelming. In a real container, the defenses are gently not needed, and the feelings arrive. That arrival is also scary. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

"When you drop the defenses that have kept you safe in the past, there's a lot to feel. And those defenses were there because feeling those things wasn't safe. So when you're in a group and your defenses aren't necessary, or they're seen through, or they're loved in a way that was not previously experienced, then it's scary because you're now in territory that was once dangerous." -- Brett Kistler, Finding Community

## What Joe Does With This Pattern

Joe does not pathologize the testing. The pattern is the correct response to the situation given the person's history. The community's job is to receive it as the live data it is.

Specific stance:

1. **Do not punish the test.** The person attacking is not the enemy. They are running a scan to see if this is another version of what hurt them before. Defensiveness in response confirms the worst assumption.
2. **Do not over-pursue the cling.** Pursuing the clinger creates the dependency the standard cult-like community creates and proves the suspicion that something is off. Stay available, not pursuing.
3. **Name the pattern gently when it is helpful.** Sometimes the person testing can recognize what they are doing if the dynamic is named, and the recognition lets them relax into the container.
4. **Welcome the feelings the testing reveals.** The fear of being deeply loved is a feeling that wants to be felt. The fear of being deeply rejected is a feeling that wants to be felt. Both can be welcomed in the container. See [[welcoming-emotions-fully]] (general principle).
5. **Trust the conditions to do the work over time.** The pattern resolves not by intervention but by the community continuing to be what it is. After enough cycles of testing and being met with non-punishment, the apparatus relaxes.

The Joe move that ties this together: when someone in the community says "letting this amount of love in is hard for me" — which Joe heard multiple times the day after the grief ceremony — the move is to receive it, not to fix it. The hardness is real and is doing its own work.

## What the Person In It Should Know

For someone going through this themselves, the most useful thing to know is that the contradiction is normal. Wanting community and being scared of it is not a sign that something is wrong with them or with the community. It is a sign that they are encountering something real and their nervous system is responding to it the way it learned to respond.

The instruction is not to suppress either pole. The wanting is real; the fear is real. Both are signals. The fear of being deeply loved is pointing at the parts of identity that are about to dissolve. The fear of being deeply rejected is pointing at unmetabolized pain from earlier rejections. Both deserve attention, not management.

The practical move: stay long enough to let the pattern run its cycles. The cycles do not resolve in one session. They resolve over months of repeated experience of the container holding without punishment.
