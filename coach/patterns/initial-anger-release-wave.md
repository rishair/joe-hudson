# The Initial Anger Release Wave (The Champagne Bottle Pop)

## The Pattern

When a person starts releasing anger after a long stretch (months, years, decades) of suppression, the initial period is flooded. They suddenly notice anger everywhere. Things they would have walked past without flinching now register as anger. The volume increases before it settles.

"If you start expressing anger, especially if you haven't expressed any for a long time, it's like a lot of anger is going to possibly come up. So you're going to be noticing, oh my God, I'm getting angry at this, I'm getting angry at that, which just means you need to move more anger in a safe place." -- Joe Hudson, How Anger Works

The pattern then resolves. As the held charge dissipates, the noticing returns to a normal baseline.

## The Metaphor

"It's like a champagne bottle. The pop happens, all the champagne comes out, and then you can learn to just enjoy it and have the full experience of the champagne. It's the same with anger. As that channel becomes unconstricted, anger just looks like this: 'Oh no, I'm not willing to accept that.' 'Oh, that doesn't work for me.' It's just clear boundaries." -- Joe Hudson, How Anger Works

The cork is what the suppression has been. The pressure built up behind it is the years of held anger. The pop is the first deliberate releases. The overflow is the immediate flood — anger noticing everywhere, anger about old situations, anger about today's situations, anger about being angry. The settled champagne is what is available after the surge has cleared.

## Why It Happens

The body has been holding a charge for a long time. The first releases lower the pressure on the holding. Released-but-not-yet-discharged anger surfaces into awareness because the gate that was keeping it out of awareness has loosened. The volume of anger appearing in consciousness in any given day is now closer to the actual volume of anger in the system, rather than the volume the suppression was filtering through.

The pattern is mechanical, not pathological. It is what unkinking a long-kinked hose looks like (see [[kinked-emotions]]). The water that was held back by the kink rushes through when the kink loosens.

## What This Looks Like in Daily Life

In the early phase of an anger practice (first two-to-four weeks of [[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[free-write-the-anger]], or [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]):

- The person reports being more easily triggered, not less.
- Small annoyances feel larger.
- Old grievances surface unbidden in the shower, on the commute, while falling asleep.
- The person may dream more vivid or angry dreams.
- The body may feel more agitated, more tense.
- A self-doubt arises: "this practice is making me worse."

The self-doubt is the most diagnostic. It is the predictable mind-response to the predictable body-process. Naming the pattern in advance prevents the person from concluding the practice is broken and quitting.

## The Anti-Pattern: Concluding the Practice Is Broken

The person starts moving anger. The flood begins. They conclude: "this is not working, this is making me worse, I am more angry now than before."

The conclusion misreads the surfacing as a problem. The surfacing is the practice working. The held charge is becoming available to discharge. The way through is more practice, not less.

"Which just means you need to move more anger in a safe place." -- Joe Hudson, How Anger Works

The instruction is to keep going. The volume drops. The post-pop champagne arrives.

If the person stops at this stage, the released charge stays partially released, the gate re-closes, and they end the practice with more present-tense anger than they began with. This is the version that gives anger work a bad reputation.

## The Resolution

"Eventually that starts going down. [...] As that channel becomes unconstricted, anger just looks like this: 'Oh no, I'm not willing to accept that.' 'Oh, that doesn't work for me.' It's just clear boundaries. And it's clarity of like, 'Oh, I'm gonna do this and I'm not gonna be stopped because of what other people think.' That's what happens when that anger is completely unmoved." -- Joe Hudson, How Anger Works

After the surge clears, the operating mode is different. Anger arrives, registers as a clear "no" or "yes, this," moves through quickly, leaves the body, and produces a clean action or boundary. It does not accumulate. The big ebb and flow dampens. Anger becomes a coherent part of life, not a periodic crisis.

This is the steady-state Joe describes in [[joes-pancake-anger-story]] and that the executive in [[joes-angry-executive-commute]] reached after 2-3 weeks of nightly release.

## How to Coach Through the Wave

When a person reports being more angry since starting the practice:

1. **Affirm the practice is working.** This is the expected phase, not a failure.
2. **Name the pattern.** Tell them about the champagne bottle. "You're in the pop. The pressure that was held is now leaving. The volume is temporary."
3. **Increase practice, do not decrease.** "The way through is more release, not less. Move more anger in the available safe container."
4. **Adjust nervous system support.** The body is processing more in the early phase. Rest, hot baths, less stimulant, more nature. See [[dont-go-after-every-emotion-at-once]] for the parallel pitfall in broader emotional work and the nervous-system care that prevents it.
5. **Track the curve, not the day.** Daily volume will be noisy. The two-week-on-two-week-off comparison will show the descent.

## The Pair With Don't-Go-After-Every-Emotion

The companion warning: this pattern is real and survivable, *and* it is a reason not to go after every emotion at once. See [[dont-go-after-every-emotion-at-once]]. The body's nervous system can metabolize a surge in one emotion. It cannot metabolize surges in all emotions simultaneously without becoming ungrounded.

The healthy version of working with the wave: pick one emotion (often anger, because of its high leverage on depression and stuck-ness), commit to the practice, ride the wave, let it settle. Then, when the baseline has restabilized, the next emotion is available to work with if needed.

The unhealthy version: try to work with anger, sadness, fear, grief, shame, and joy simultaneously, all in the surge phase, while sleeping poorly. The result is ungrounded chaos that confirms the suspicion that emotional work is destabilizing.

## When the Wave Does Not Settle

If the practice has been consistent for more than 4-6 weeks and the wave is not settling, check:

- **Is the release clean?** See [[three-things-that-go-wrong-when-releasing-anger]]. If each release is collapsing, dysregulating, or recreating shame, the held charge is not actually discharging. It is being re-stored under a different label.
- **Is the anger covering a deeper emotion?** See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] and [[recycling-anger-without-clarity]]. The wave may be persistent because what is underneath the anger has not yet been allowed to surface.
- **Is the practice in service of bypassing rather than feeling?** A person who is doing the release as a project to "be done with anger" may be holding subtle resistance throughout the practice that prevents real discharge.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Starts a daily anger practice and reports being more reactive than before.
- Feels like a quieter version of themselves before the practice and a more volatile version after starting.
- Has a partner who has noticed they are more easily set off in the early weeks of the practice.
- Considers stopping the practice because "it's making me worse."
- Notices old grievances they thought were resolved are coming back.

## Tone

The pattern is presented with friendliness. The champagne metaphor itself carries the tone — there is celebration in the pop, and the post-pop enjoyment is the actual point. The discharge phase is treated as the pleasure-on-the-way-to-pleasure, not as a crisis to survive.
