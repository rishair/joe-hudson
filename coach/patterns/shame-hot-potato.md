# The Shame Hot Potato

## The Trigger

Someone feels shame. The shame is intolerable. They try to hand it off — to a partner, a co-worker, a colleague, a political opponent, a country. The other person, also unwilling to hold shame, tries to hand it back. The potato bounces. Both succeed, repeatedly, at inflicting shame on each other. The original problem the fight was supposedly about never gets addressed.

Tara coined the name. The dynamic shows up everywhere two or more people are stuck.

"Somebody wants to make somebody else ashamed, or is defending themselves in such a way that puts shame on somebody, or the person just feels ashamed and hears whatever the other person is saying as shame. So, whatever. But they feel shame. They don't want to feel it. So, they try to hand it to the other person." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

"The shame hot potato is one person tries to give it to the other, and then the other person tries to give it back, and they just go back and forth trying to, and usually successfully inflicting shame on one another, which doesn't solve shit." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## The Relationship to the Shame Avoidance Loop

This is the *interpersonal* form of [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]. The avoidance loop runs inside one person: trying to avoid feeling shame produces shame from a different direction. The hot potato runs between people: each person's attempt to expel their shame onto the other gives the other shame to expel back. The two patterns are usually running simultaneously. Each person is in their internal loop, and the two loops are coupled by the throws.

The shame in the avoidance loop is felt as disconnection. The shame in the hot potato is being actively passed. Both rest on the same underlying refusal to feel the shame as it is.

## The Hot Potato Family of Patterns

The same structural pattern shows up for any emotion the system collectively refuses to feel. [[fear-hot-potato]] runs the same dynamic for fear, with the family-anxiety-carrier role as its most common form. [[one-team-member-holds-the-helplessness]] runs it for helplessness in workplace teams. In each case, the unfelt emotion consolidates in one carrier rather than dispersing, the carrier is rejected for naming what the system refuses to acknowledge, and the carrier's removal does not solve the pattern -- a new carrier emerges from the next-lowest-threshold member. The shame version is the most extensively studied because shame is the most chronically stagnant of the throwable emotions, but the structure is generalizable.

## Indicators

The diagnostic signs that a hot potato is running. Several of these together make it almost certain.

- **Blame is in motion.** Someone is being blamed; the blame is being defended against; the defense includes counter-blame. Energy is going into who-is-wrong, not into what-to-do.
- **Focus on what the other person is doing.** People are focused on what the other team, partner, or country did. The conversation is about the other side's behavior, not about one's own contribution to creating the situation.
- **The same fight, again.** "If your marriage has the same kind of fight, or if your company has the same kind of problem over and over again, or if you're a country with the same problem over and over again," shame is involved. The repetition is the tell. Other emotions move and resolve. Shame stagnates and recurs. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] for the underlying mechanism.
- **Defensiveness as breadcrumb.** Wherever defensiveness shows up in yourself, another person, a team, or a dynamic, look for the hot potato underneath. Defensiveness is one of the two mechanisms by which the potato is thrown (the other is anger / removal of love).
- **Energy spent identifying who's responsible rather than how to learn or prevent.** "Every time there was a mistake, there was like so much energy put towards who is responsible for the mistake and like partially this person, partially that person. Everybody's just like looking instead of, oh, how do we make sure we don't make that mistake again? Instead of what can we learn here? Instead of how do we fix it? It was who's to blame? And that was like the way that they processed mistakes." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## The Mechanisms by Which the Potato Is Thrown

There are two channels. One is somatic / emotional. The other is intellectual.

**Channel 1: Anger and removal of love.** Aggression or passive aggression. In a marriage, often one partner gets angry while the other withdraws love. Both moves install shame in the other on a nervous-system level.

"On a nervous system level, like, the often the only thing you have to do to make somebody feel like they're bad is get angry at them. It's not actually what you said. It's just like, oh, if I get angry at you, a lot of people immediately go, oh, what did I do wrong? Or if you remove love they immediately go, 'Ooh, what did I do wrong?' on a nervous system level." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

This is why the channel works even when the content is absent or innocuous. The anger and the withdrawal *are* the throw. The verbal content is decoration.

**Channel 2: Arguing about who is wrong.** The intellectual form. The argument is about who is right. Each side mounts evidence. The construction of the argument is itself the throw, because winning the argument is a way of installing shame in the loser.

"Trying to figure out who the fuck is wrong, like trying to figure out who did it wrong... almost all of YouTube's political diatribes are people making the case that the other side is wrong. Like, how often do you see a political diatribe saying, oh, here's how we might be able to correct ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The intellectual channel can feel like reason, debate, or accountability. Its diagnostic is the same as the somatic channel: the activity recurs without resolving anything, because shame-installation is not problem-solving.

## Why Both People Keep Throwing

Two reasons the potato gets thrown back instead of held.

**Reason 1: The sugar-high of relief.** Successfully shaming the other person produces a quick hit of relief, similar to a video game win. The relief is real but thin. "It's like the kind of relief that a video game gives you. It's like, oh, you can, you can be out of it, but it never really actually heals the pattern."

The relief is the bait that keeps the dynamic alive. People learn that throwing the potato produces relief. The relief is short-lived and produces another round, but the immediate reinforcement is strong enough that the loop persists.

**Reason 2: Subconscious "if I'm going to feel it, you should too."** Underneath the rational moves there is a quiet logic: "If I am feeling shame, I want you to feel shame too." It is rarely conscious. When it is named and looked at, most people do not want this. But it is operating, and it powers the throw.

This is the diagnostic Joe uses with couples: "do you really want this person to feel ashamed?" Most people, when they sit with the question, recognize that the wanting-other-to-feel-shame is part of what is in motion. The recognition is the first opening. See [[state-no-wish-for-their-shame]] for the move that uses this opening.

## The Indicator That You Are In One

The simplest behavioral indicator: you are in the same fight you have been in for a long time. Marriage, company, political conflict, internal split. The stagnation itself is the signature. Other emotions resolve. Shame stays. So shame-driven dynamics stay.

"If you're in a war with yourself, if you're in a war in your marriage, if you're in a war in the company, that's an indicator of shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

Inversely: where things are moving, shame is probably not the engine. Where things are not moving, shame probably is.

## Why "Defending Yourself" Is the Tell

A person who does not believe the accusation does not need to defend against it. If someone says "you have a horrible blue sweater" and you have no part of you that thinks the sweater is bad, you do not get triggered. If they say "you're a horrible father" and you have no part of you that thinks you are a horrible father, you do not get triggered.

"If somebody said to me, you know, 'you're a horrible fucking father,' that would not create shame in me. There's nothing in me that feels like, 'Oh, I'm a horrible fucking father.' So that wouldn't create shame in me. But if somebody said something that actually would trigger me, then something that I actually believed about myself, then there would be this need to defend myself typically. So, the important thing there is that if you actually believe the thing, then you'll think you need to defend it. If you don't believe it, you won't need to defend it. So, you have to buy into the shame to start really defending yourself." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The defense reveals that the accusation has landed on a part of the self that already agrees. The hot potato runs between two people whose shame about themselves is being externalized as a fight about each other. This is the same mechanism as [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] operating in the relational form.

A practical implication: when defensiveness shows up, the productive question is not "how do I argue back" but "what part of me is this landing on." That part is what the work is on. The fight is the distraction from the work.

## Why It Doesn't Take Both People to Stop

In a marriage, both partners ideally want the same growth, but in a workplace, a political conversation, or any conflict where the relationship is not the central commitment, one person can stop the potato by simply not throwing it back.

"In a political conversation or a business conversation, if the shame hot potato is going back and forth, it only requires one person to stop it." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The throw-back is what keeps it alive. Let it land. Feel it. Listen. Ask actual questions. The dynamic deflates.

For marriages specifically, Joe is unequivocal: a marriage where one partner cannot meet this work is one he would not suggest entering. The point of marriage is shared growth; without willingness on both sides, the hot potato will run for the life of the relationship. See [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]].

See [[drop-the-hot-potato]] for the specific exit move.

## Why Defending Doesn't Heal Even When You "Win"

The hot potato can produce winners. The winner gets relief. The relief does nothing to heal the underlying pattern. The next throw comes anyway.

"There's a lot of, you know, a lot of relationships. It's like whoever can argue better is the right one instead of, oh, let me fully understand what makes you think you're right. You can fully understand what makes me think I'm right, and together we can both learn how to grow and become better people from it. That's when you're not passing the shame hot potato." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The exit is not winning. The exit is leaving the argument-about-who-is-right entirely. See [[apologies-as-power-struggle-surrogate]] for the related pattern in apology dynamics.

## Why Shame Inflicted Doesn't Stop the Behavior

A common subconscious bet: if I make them feel ashamed, they will change. The bet pays out in the short run (sometimes — shame can produce a momentary behavioral shift) and fails reliably in the long run. Shamed people repeat the behavior. This is [[shame-drives-behavior]] applied to the other person. You are installing the lock on their bad habit, not removing it.

"Anytime someone feels ashamed, they just like repeat the pattern. So it's a horrible thing to want people to feel." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

This is the rational case for not wanting the other person to feel shame. The emotional case requires sitting with the part of you that does want them to feel it, and feeling whatever is underneath that wanting (often grief, helplessness, hurt). See [[state-no-wish-for-their-shame]] for the practice.

## The Group / Team Form

The same dynamic in companies. The marketing team and the engineering team in chronic conflict. Sales and product in chronic conflict. The signature: each side is focused on what the other team is not giving them, not on its own resourcefulness. Each side feels wrong but cannot acknowledge it, so each defends. The defense is the throw.

"Typically what it is, is everybody's focused on what they don't have, what the other part of the team isn't giving them instead of being focused on their own resourcefulness. And so, that's also a really good indicator that there's a shame hot potato happening where everyone's trying to defend, 'Oh, I'm not wrong. I'm not bad,' but yet feeling wrong and bad." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The exit move in a team context often involves a leader explicitly taking responsibility without taking shame. See [[fault-vs-responsibility]] and the "buck stops here" stance Joe describes.

## The Silent Form: The Unspoken Hot Potato

The hot potato does not require active throwing. It can run silently, with each person holding the shame for fear of being the one to start it. The rock-star-on-set scene from [[makeup-artist-and-the-rock-star]] is the canonical example of the silent form.

Brett names it directly in the 2024 conversation:

"It points to the interesting game of shame ball, the shame hot potato going on under the surface, where this person showed up and he on some level felt ashamed, like, 'I'm bad, I'm wrong for showing up to this multimillion-dollar shoot in this kind of a state.' And many of us, I was relatively new to the scene and I was like, I am not going to be the person to break the ice, break this situation. But, man, I really felt like we were all doing it wrong until that was spoken to." -- Brett Kistler, The Elephant in the Room

Joe responds: "They didn't want to be the wrong one. They didn't want to be the one that fucked up the shoot. Right. And that's typically as if it was their fault for saying something that was true instead of the rock star's fault for whatever the rock star had done or had found themselves in or didn't postpone the shoot."

The structure: the rock star is holding shame about looking like death. The crew is holding shame about being the one who would "ruin" the shoot by naming what the rock star looks like. Each side is silent because each side is protecting itself from being made the wrong one. The potato is in the room. Nobody is throwing it. Everyone is being burned by it.

This is the form the hot potato takes in workplaces, families with a difficult member, and any social setting where the obvious unaddressed thing is producing low-grade collective shame. The exit is the same as the active form: someone has to be willing to hold the potato — to risk being made the wrong one by naming what is in the room. The makeup artist's "you look like shit" was that move. When the rock star said "yeah, I feel like shit," the hot potato dissolved. The shame that had been distributed across everyone now had nowhere to live — it had been received, named, and acknowledged.

See [[the-elephant-in-the-room]] for the operational frame and [[speak-the-elephant-from-open-heart-not-fear]] for the inner posture that makes the naming-move land.

## The Political and National Form

Politics is the hot potato at scale. The signature is identical: each side focused on the other's wrongdoing, each side defended, each side certain that the other is the aggressor. Both sides are ashamed; both sides are throwing.

"If you look at it, it's a whole bunch of people feeling like somebody else is wrong and bad. And it also indicates that they feel like they're wrong and bad. There's no way you can look at any two sides of politics or war and not see that like there is some way in which both sides are being defensive, both sides are ashamed, both sides are not acting in full alignment with who they are. They're — everybody is making some sort of compromises and it feels like shit. And so they're just passing the shame hot potato back and forth." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The same exit applies at the national scale: feel the grief and helplessness underneath the shame, see the other side's truth, stop trying to win the argument. The wars and political messes stay locked because the conditions for this are not met at scale. See [[anger-and-politics]] for the political application.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You are in the same fight with your partner for the seventeenth time and you can predict the next four moves
- A team meeting devolves into "who's responsible for this" instead of "what do we do about it"
- After a conflict, you replay the argument in your head, sharpening the case for why you were right
- You notice that "winning" the argument with your partner only feels good for a few hours, then irritation returns
- You find yourself wanting the other person to admit they were wrong, and the wanting is more intense than the desire to solve the underlying problem
- Political news produces a sensation of needing to prove the other side wrong, and the sensation is identical day after day
- The relief you feel after a conflict is the relief of having shifted the blame, not the relief of resolution

## Tone

Joe describes this with a kind of cheerful matter-of-factness. The frame is structural, not moral. Neither side of the hot potato is a villain. Both are people in pain who would rather throw the pain than feel it. The naming is offered as a way out, not as a diagnosis of bad character. The lightness is part of why the frame can land — being told "you're in a shame hot potato" is easier to receive than being told "you're shaming each other."
