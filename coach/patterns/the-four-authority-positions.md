# The Four Authority Positions

## Definition

People with [[authority-issues]] occupy one of four structural positions in relation to authority. The positions are not personality types; they are patterns that show up situationally and can rotate within a single person across different relationships. The value of mapping them is that each position has a distinct origin, a distinct cost, and a distinct coaching path.

Joe lays out the four positions at the end of the "Authority Issues" episode in response to "what do you do about it":

"There's like the authority — what do you do when you have the authority issue where you're rebelling against the person? Like, fuck you, I don't trust you. What do you do if you're like, I'm trying to get the authority person to approve of me? And then the person who is the authority who's like, I don't want to be the authority, what do you do there? And then there's the person who's the authority who's like completely lost in the on the other side, like, I'm all alone in this, you know, that whole thing. And so there's kind of four major categories." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## Position 1: The Rebel

**The signature.** "Fuck authority. Don't tell me what to do." The person organizes their behavior around opposing or undermining whoever is in the authority slot. The form ranges from open rebellion (Joe's college expulsion) to subtle dismissal (the "you need your face on a book" thought Joe had about Adyashanti before recognizing him as a teacher).

**The origin.** Joe's read: when it was not safe to have wants as a child, the rebellion preserved a self that could have them. The "no" to the authority is also the "yes" to the suppressed want.

"What I noticed is that somebody who has that kind of authority issue, there, it wasn't safe to have their own wants. And that's one of the reasons that the rebellion to the authority happens." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**The cost.** Mentorship is inaccessible. Teachers all fail. The person reinvents the wheel. They get good at experimentation by necessity, but they lose access to accumulated wisdom. Joe describes both halves: the rebellion enabled the experimental capacity that built this work; it also meant he could not conceive of having a mentor until his 30s.

**The coaching path.**

1. **See them as human, all the way human.** Treat the authority figure as the person they are, not as the projection of "authority." The same way you would treat the person sitting next to you in the class — that is the corrective reference.
2. **Give them compliments if you see them, when authentic.** The capacity to recognize the authority's strengths is the same capacity that the rebellion has been blocking.
3. **Apologize when you are rebelling against them.** Not for being rebellious as a stance; for the specific moves that the rebellion has produced.
4. **Notice that you are rebelling against yourself.** Joe's phrasing: "I'm such a rebel, I even rebel against telling myself what to do." The rebellion against external authority is the felt expression of the same rebellion against the internal authority. Owning this transfers the work to where it can actually be done.
5. **Get in touch with wants.** Daily practice: write down what you want, how you want to exist in the world. Live from those rather than from "no." When the wants are identified and seen, the rebellion against authority becomes unnecessary — what the rebellion was actually after was the recognition of those wants.

"The more you can, every day write down, what are my wants? How do I want to exist in the world? And be really in touch with those and act from those, then the rebellion against authority stops because what you really are after is getting your wants met and identified and seen." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

See [[wants-over-shoulds]] for the underlying mechanism and [[i-freeze-around-authority]] for the freeze-flavored variant.

## Position 2: The Approval-Seeker

**The signature.** "Tell me I did well." The person organizes their behavior around earning the authority's approval. The form ranges from explicit approval-chasing to the more subtle pattern of unconsciously sacrificing parts of themselves to keep the relationship safe.

**The origin.** Joe's read: when being good (helpful, valuable, achieving) produced love in childhood, the seeking preserved access to that love. The person learns that the love comes through the channel of performance.

**The cost.** The person does not get to think for themselves. They cannot find their own wants. They lead their life from what they assume the authority wants from them. Over time, resentment naturally builds — because they have been sacrificing some part of themselves to get the approval, the cost compounds even as the approval keeps arriving.

"If you're really looking for that, eventually you become resentful of the authority figure too. So there's usually some way in which — because you are sacrificing some part of yourself, some part of your truth to get approval, so that resentment will like naturally build." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**The coaching path.**

1. **Apologize to the authority figure for what you have been holding back.** Specifically: "I have had some things that I've wanted to tell you that I'm not telling you because I want to keep our relationship safe." This is scary but it interrupts the sacrifice loop.
2. **Get in touch with wants** (same as Position 1's core move). The wants are what the approval has been substituting for.
3. **Say things the authority figure does not want to hear, when those things are valuable.** Start small, then build. This is the fastest dissolver.

Joe's elaboration on why this works:

"The best thing to do if you really want to dissolve that quickly is to say things that you think the authority figure doesn't want to hear from you that you think are really valuable and just say those, start small and then get bigger." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**The structural insight that powers the move.** Joe names that in every C-level team around a CEO, there is one person who says the hard truth — and that person is the closest to the CEO and the one most trusted. The fear that "I'll be fired if I say it" is almost always wrong. The person who can say the hard thing without defensiveness becomes the leader's real thought partner.

"I have worked with a lot of people who report to a CEO or report to maybe even a narcissistic CEO, and they'll be like, 'I can't say this. They'll get mad at me. I can't say this. They'll fire me.' And I'm like, 'hey, yeah, you can.' And they're like, 'no.' I said, 'I can prove it. There's somebody in that C-level executive who says the hard truth to them, and they're actually the closest person to that CEO.' And I'm always — it's that's always the case. That CEO always has one person who they — and that's the person they trust the most because they can say the hard thing to them. They don't do it in a way that's defensive. They're just like, oh no, this is, you know, and this is who their real thought partner is." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**The critical qualifier.** The truth has to be delivered without fear. If you are scared when you say it, they will not trust it. The baboon analogy: the troop follows the baboon who walks confidently in a direction, not the one who walks uncertainly. The same mechanism runs at the executive level — a scared truth-teller will not be heard. The truth has to come from clarity and undefendedness. See [[name-the-authoritys-fear]] for the related move from a subordinate.

See [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] for the broader concern article and the full coaching arc.

## Position 3: The Reluctant Authority

**The signature.** "I don't want to be the authority because I hated being controlled by one." This is most often the rebel who has become the leader. They built or led the company partly to escape oppressive authority, and now they cannot inhabit the authority role their team needs because they associate the role with what was done to them.

**The origin.** A specific developmental trajectory: rebellion against the original authority → success through experimentation → becoming the authority by virtue of that success → unwillingness to act as the authority because the role itself is the threat.

"Often CEOs are people who had authority issues. So they're like, 'fuck that, I'm going to be my — I don't — I'm not going to have an authority above me,' right? That's often the case. So I'm going to do this thing, which is an interesting check... I've seen a lot of times CEOs are like, 'yeah, I don't like authority.' So they — entrepreneurs particularly, not somebody who's run, like, rose through a bureaucracy to become a CEO, but entrepreneurs, they're like, 'I don't want authority.' And so then they are like, 'I don't want to be that oppressive authority that my dad was.' And so then they don't actually stand into their authority completely, and then everybody feels lost in there. In their organization." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

**The cost.** The team feels unsafe. The structural insight underneath: when authority is needed and not provided, other people start vying for it, which produces chaos. The under-assertion is not neutral; it is a vacuum that gets filled by something worse. See [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]] for the principle.

**The coaching path.**

1. **Ask the people you lead what they want.** Specifically: what is the structure they want, where do they feel unsafe, what do they not know? Every place they report unsafety is a place where the leader is not providing the authority the role requires.
2. **Provide process and principles rather than directives.** The middle path: not telling people what to do, and not leaving them in a vacuum. Provide the structure (how decisions get made, how meetings run, how we solve problems, what our principles are). Inside that structure, let people have autonomy.
3. **Recognize that under-assertion is its own form of authority dysfunction.** It is not the absence of the rebel position; it is the rebel position turned inward against the role itself. The work is to inhabit the role on its own terms, distinguished from what the original authority did with it.

"This is, these are the principles, this is how we operate. This is the structure. This is the process. This is, these are the roles. If you want to change them, I'm — it's open to discussion. But this is how we're doing things so that people feel safe." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for the broader org-design version and [[no-doctrine-leadership]] for the related under-assertion failure mode.

## Position 4: The Alone-At-The-Top Authority

**The signature.** "I'm carrying it all and nobody really sees me." The leader is surrounded by people who are trying, and the leader cannot feel that. The position is full of people; the person inside it reports feeling alone.

**The origin.** Several pathways converge here. Sometimes it is the childhood abandonment imprint extended into the leadership role (see [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]]). Sometimes it is the leader's own walls keeping the team at distance. Most often, both: the original imprint produces the walls, and the walls maintain the loneliness.

The structural feature that intensifies Position 4: the social field around the authority. People do not show their full selves. People kiss up. People attack for no reason. The authority does not get real feedback, real bad news, real human contact. Even when the leader wants connection, the field they sit in makes it harder to receive. See [[visualize-the-reality-of-leadership]] for the move that surfaces this for the leader.

**The cost.** The documented loss of empathy in long-term power-holders. The selfishness that arises from not having enough of oneself accessible anywhere else. The conviction that ordinary people need to be told what to do. The deep loneliness that runs through people at the top of large organizations.

**The coaching path.**

1. **Start asking for help.** This is the entry move.
2. **Ask with specifics — including the criteria for what would actually constitute help.** The trap Joe identifies is that lonely leaders typically ask for help in ways that ensure they will not receive it. See [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]] for the full move with the example of how to ask vs. how not to ask.
3. **Apologize to the team for not seeing the help they have been giving.** This converts the internal recognition into a relational act. See [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]] for the full move.
4. **Make sure everyone feels seen.** Joe's first move when things are going wrong in his company is to make sure everyone feels seen — including himself, by making it possible for them to see him. The dissolving move at the top of the org is the same one as in any relationship: the felt experience of being seen. See [[make-everyone-feel-seen-when-things-go-wrong]] for the full move and [[sarah-i-love-your-frustration]] for the story that produced this practice.

"Typically what happens there is they ask for help, but in a way that proves that they're not gonna get the help. So the way to ask for the help is to say, 'this is what I need specifically, and these are the criteria of what would make it actually work for me.'" -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

See [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] for the presenting concern.

## Overlap and Rotation

A single person rarely runs only one position. The rebel-turned-CEO often runs Position 1 with the board, Position 3 with the team, and Position 4 with themselves at the end of the day. The approval-seeker promoted into leadership often runs Position 2 upward and Position 4 downward.

The diagnostic question is not "which position is this person?" It is "which position is operating right now in this relationship?" The position rotates with the relationship. The coaching moves are deployed accordingly.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person realizes they have systematically dismissed every teacher they have encountered (Position 1)
- A person catches themselves performing for their boss's approval and resents the performance (Position 2)
- A founder realizes their team is asking for guidance they have been refusing to give (Position 3)
- A CEO feels alone in a building full of people who are trying to make them successful (Position 4)
- A leader recognizes they have been the angry-when-scared authority and want to break the pattern (a cross-position recognition)
- A person realizes the same dynamic is running in their marriage that runs in their workplace

## Tone

Joe walks through the four positions briskly, with the recognition that this list is a long answer to a simple question. The tone is diagnostic without being clinical — he speaks about each position with familiarity, recognizing his own movement through several of them. The framing is practical: each position has a move that works, and the moves are not interchangeable. The map is the map.
