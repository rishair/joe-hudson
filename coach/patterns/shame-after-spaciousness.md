# Shame After Spaciousness

## The Pattern

The person can access a spacious, big, peaceful, welcoming state — sometimes easily, sometimes for years. They know it. They've been there many times. Then, every time, shame arises after the spaciousness and pulls them out. They beat themselves up, become self-critical, find a reason to dismiss what they just had. The shame is not random. It is the lockout mechanism.

The shame after the spaciousness usually comes with a specific story: "being big is shameful." "I shouldn't be taking up this much room." "Who do I think I am?" The story masks the structure underneath, which is that someone in the person's history — most often a parent, sibling, or early caregiver — was jealous of the person's bigness or threatened by it, and shamed them out of it. The system internalized the shaming and now does it preemptively.

The pattern's signature: a brief experience of expansion, followed reliably by contraction with a shame quality, followed by a self-critical story explaining the contraction.

## The Mechanism

The shame is doing a job. It is closing down the territory that, in childhood, caused harm. If a child was punished, mocked, or withdrawn from for being too much — too loud, too confident, too perceptive, too radiant — the child learns to shut down those qualities to maintain attachment. The shutdown becomes automatic. By adulthood, the person has internalized the shaming and no longer needs the external source to deliver it.

The mechanism is structural, not personal. The shame is not evidence that the person actually shouldn't be big. It is evidence that being big was once unsafe. The unsafety is gone. The mechanism persists.

The reason this pattern is so common in people who do self-development work: the work successfully delivers the spacious state. The state then triggers the shutdown. The person experiences the back-and-forth as failure to sustain the state. Structurally, it is success at producing the state followed by the shutdown firing as it was trained to do.

## Where It Comes From

The most common source: someone in the family of origin was threatened by the person's natural bigness. Possible shapes:
- A parent with their own unaddressed wound around being seen, who could not tolerate the child outshining them
- A sibling who would withdraw or attack when the person was happy or accomplished
- A parent who modeled "humility" as smallness and shamed the child for displays of self
- A culture or religion that taught that being big was sinful, prideful, or against God
- A peer group in adolescence that punished anyone for standing out

The specific origin matters less than the structure. The person learned: bigness is dangerous. The body installed: contract automatically before anyone else can punish the bigness.

The shame that arises after spaciousness is the body executing the old protective instruction. It is not the person's actual ethical assessment.

## The Tall-Poppy and Big-Head Wiring

The cultural and family transmission of this pattern often comes through specific transmitted phrases that, repeated, install the lockout in the child without any single act of overt shaming being required. Two common forms:

- **The tall-poppy cut.** The cultural pattern in which the tallest poppy gets cut. The child who stands out, shines, or shows joy gets the head trimmed by a parent's subtle attention, the sibling's withdrawal, the teacher's correction. Over time the child learns to do the trimming preemptively.
- **"I don't want you to get a big head."** A common parental phrase. Surface meaning: don't become arrogant. Felt meaning: do not feel too good about yourself. The phrase installs the wiring that feeling good is itself the danger.

"It's because there was a parent where your joy was overwhelming to their senses. So they would make sure the tall poppy was cut, so to speak. Or they would make sure that — like, you've heard this phrase, 'I don't want you to get a big head,' right? Which basically, 'I don't want you to feel good.'" — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

The structural translation: "I don't want you to feel good" is the actual instruction the child internalized, even though the surface phrase was about arrogance. The instruction does its work invisibly for decades after the parent who delivered it is gone or no longer relevant. The shame that arises after spaciousness is the body executing this instruction, on its old schedule, with no one currently around to enforce it.

The reason this matters for the work: when the person can see the structural source of the shame ("a parent was overwhelmed by my joy, so the shame is the body executing the don't-feel-good rule"), the shame loses some of its moral charge. It stops feeling like a verdict about how the person should live and starts feeling like an outdated instruction the body is still running.

## What Joe Says About It

In the source session, Joe names the pattern directly:

> "The pattern is that you know that place, but after you are in that place, somehow you have a learning that you need to feel shame after that place. That being that big and peaceful and spacious is a shameful thing because someone was jealous of you and so shamed you out of it when you went there. Something like that."

The "something like that" matters — Joe is offering the shape of the explanation, not insisting on the specifics. The person fills in their own version. The point of the naming is to make the pattern visible as a pattern, not to identify the specific shamer.

## What This Pattern Is Not

- It is not evidence that the person should be smaller. The shame's verdict ("you shouldn't be this big") is the trained instruction, not a truth about how the person should live.
- It is not the same as humility. True [[humility]] does not produce a contraction after spaciousness. See [[humility-vs-disempowerment]]. The shame-after-spaciousness pattern is the false-humility variant — it is making oneself small as a strategy, dressed up as a virtue.
- It is not solvable by deciding to keep being big. The deciding is head-level; the shame fires at the body level. The work is in the body.
- It is not a sign that the spaciousness wasn't real. The spaciousness was real. The shame is the lockout, not a correction.

## When Someone Experiences This

- After a meditation retreat, when the felt expansion fades on the drive home and self-criticism arrives
- After a successful presentation or performance, when the sense of being seen contracts into "I shouldn't have been so much"
- In coaching sessions, after a breakthrough — the person reports feeling great, then within minutes is in self-attack
- After receiving a compliment that lands, followed by an inner voice saying "you're getting too full of yourself"
- After speaking in a group and being well-received, followed by replaying the words critically
- Repeatedly across a self-development practice — "I know this place but I can't stay" — when the not-staying is the shame firing

The pattern is structural. It will keep firing until met directly.

## The Path Through

The path is not to prevent the shame. The shame will arise. The path is to welcome the shame too. When the shame arrives after the spaciousness, treat it as another thing to welcome rather than another reason to exit the spaciousness.

In the source session, Joe's instruction:

> "So go back there to the welcoming everything for a minute. And when that shame arises, welcome that too."

The somatic test: when the shame is welcomed rather than fought, the resistance to the shame is often less than expected. The person reports "I don't feel as much resistance as I would have thought." This is the signal that the shame is starting to lose its enforcement function. It is no longer being treated as a verdict; it is being treated as a passing weather event.

The longer work is to make the welcoming of the shame so reliable that the shame stops needing to fire. The shame fires because the system expects punishment for bigness. If the bigness is met internally with welcoming each time, including welcoming of the shame that arises, the system gradually learns that bigness is no longer the dangerous territory it once was.

This is the same mechanism as [[unwelcomed-emotions-return-until-felt]]: the shame returns until felt, then loses its grip. Each round of welcoming reduces the charge.
