# Business as a Spiritual Practice

## Definition

Business is one of the cleanest containers for spiritual development available. The same work that happens on a meditation cushion or in a therapy session happens, more directly and with less ability to fool oneself, in the daily operation of getting things done in the world. Treating the business as the practice — not as a means to an end — produces both better businesses and faster personal development.

"Business" here means whatever you are doing to get crap done in the world. The Tibetan term is *skillful means* — the practice of chopping wood as spiritual practice. It includes running a company, working a job, making art, marketing, parenting as a system of operations. Anything where you are creating, making, or producing.

"I mean it in that very broadly applied thing. I mean the getting crap done in life. It doesn't mean running a company. It might be being in a job. The Tibetans called it skillful means... So I don't care if it's marketing something or if it's starting a business, or if it's being an artist, it's like all that is what I mean by business." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

"Spiritual" here means the natural maturation of consciousness that happens when a person keeps developing — moving from seeing themselves as an individual into seeing themselves as part of an ecosystem; from a short timeframe into a long one and eventually into infinity-in-the-moment; from doing into being. "Practice" is the awareness brought to that evolution. The two together: bringing awareness to your evolution in an applied way, applied to whatever you are getting done.

## The Core Move

The methodology of business as a spiritual practice is the same as everything else Joe teaches. Three prongs:

1. **Embrace intensity.** Go into the difficulty that presents itself rather than running away from it. See [[embrace-intensity]].
2. **Create connection.** With yourself, with the people you work with, with your customers. See [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]].
3. **Discern internal alignment.** Move toward alignment, not away from it for short-term gain or out of fear.

The result is what Joe calls finding the $1,000 bills on the floor. Aligning the business with who you are is not a luxury that costs you money; it is an efficiency that produces money. See [[walmart-environmental-profitability]] for the Walmart sustainability story that anchors the metaphor.

"It actually kind of goes the other way. It is finding what's not in alignment, putting it into alignment, which allows for the money to come is typically how it works." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

## Why Business Is a Cleaner Container Than Meditation

Three structural reasons business strips the bypass that other practices permit:

**1. You cannot fool yourself.** A meditation practice can be performed. A retreat can be performed. A workshop can be performed. The rubber does not hit the road. Business meets you with the actual conditions of life every day. Something always goes wrong. Something always demands a response. The state you actually have, not the state you claim to have, is what shows up.

"It's like the rubber hits the road every day. Every day, things are going to go wrong. How are you going to let your ego dissolve in the face of things going wrong? Every day is going to have some level of discomfort in it. And so there's something real about it. You can't fool yourself as easily." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

This is the same principle as [[parenting-as-real-world-test-of-inner-work]]. Parenting and business are both load-bearing tests of practice. The crying infant and the angry customer both detect the gap between the performed state and the actual one. See [[practice-cannot-fool-itself-in-real-conditions]] for the principle that unifies these two domains.

**2. The false-separation fantasy collapses.** A common move in spiritual practice is to imagine that the practice would deepen if only you could withdraw from your life. "If I could just meditate all the time, I could be happy." "If I could just hang out in a cave, I'd be free." Business does not permit this fantasy. If you withdraw from the business, the business fails. The pretense that liberation requires escape from the world dies because you cannot test it without losing what you built.

"You can meditate 2 hours a day and think to yourself, 'Oh, if I could only just give up this life, give up my wife, give up everything and hang out in a cave, then I could be happy.' That sense of false separation just doesn't get to be there. So that fantasy doesn't get to live there." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

**3. There is less navel-gazing.** Some spiritual practices reward — even produce — a kind of beautiful narcissism. The person agrees with the vocabulary, gets lost in the scripture, refines the way they describe their inner state. Business does not permit this. If you constantly think about yourself in business, you fail. The corrective is immediate.

"There's these backwaters in traditional spiritual practice where you can kind of agree with the words and the terminology of a practice and you can get lost in the scripture, if you will, and instead of actually the felt sense of what's happening for you. And that can allow a lot of navel-gazing. It can be a lot of like a kind of a really beautiful form of narcissism of constantly thinking about yourself in the process." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The signal arrives quickly. If you are doing the business as a practice, you notice the dysfunction quickly and address it quickly. This is the actual mechanism behind the "$1,000 bills on the floor" metaphor: the practice produces the alertness that produces the early correction that produces the efficiency.

## Means-to-End Is the Trap

The opposite of business as a spiritual practice is business as a means to something else: money, power, fame, the ability to eventually stop working. The fantasy is that if the business succeeds in those terms, life will be good.

The structural problem with this orientation: once the means produces the end, attention drifts away from the business. The CEO who built the company to make their fortune turns toward enjoying the fortune. The business suffers. The signal that the company is no longer the focus is observable in concrete behaviors. See [[ceo-big-house-market-cap-drop]] for the tax-record study showing that CEOs who build houses over 5,000 square feet preside over a roughly 10% drop in market cap.

"If you're doing it for power, if you're doing it to win, if you're doing it to make a name for yourself, all this stuff, all the places you don't embrace intensity, it's a lot easier to fool yourself. It's a lot easier to say, 'Oh, well, the business is going good, so everything's good.'" -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

When business is the practice — when it is the means and the end — attention does not drift. The work is the work. There is no after.

"I remember I was recently at a a group of people doing online courses, and a lot of the people's fantasy was, oh, I could work 5 hours a week and then the rest of my time I can do what I want. And I was like, how is this not doing what you want, right? Like there's no means to an end here. For me, this is very much about — it's the practice, and it keeps me engaged." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The reframe is not that you should never reach a comfortable place. It is that the comfort cannot be the orientation. The orientation has to be the practice, or the practice degrades the moment the comfort arrives.

## The Specific Diagnostics in Business

Several common business pathologies have direct emotional roots that the practice frame makes visible.

**Excessive second-guessing.** A leader or team that constantly re-questions its decisions is avoiding an emotion. The decision-quality problem is downstream of the feeling-avoidance problem.

"That means that there is an emotional experience you're avoiding. So you can really use embracing intensity in that moment. You can ask in a business context, what are the really hard questions? What are we trying to avoid? What are we really trying to get to? What's got us scared? How are we not being courageous?" -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

Inside a company, the same second-guessing usually traces to alignment failure: "I think this is what we should do, but I don't want to get in trouble." The political layer is misdirected fear. See [[politics-from-unclear-standards]].

**Defensiveness.** A leader who defends is a leader who stops hearing. The team stops sharing wisdom. The customers stop volunteering feedback. The business gets dumber.

"Being defensive in business is horrible for business because you're not listening to great ideas. You're not being open to other people's wisdom. You're making it so other people don't share their wisdom with you. You're making it so customers don't share with you. You stop asking customers. All of that is defensiveness." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The practice frame names the cost. Each instance of defensiveness is the practice asking to be done.

**Conflict avoidance.** The team that talks around the hard thing produces worse decisions, fewer innovations, and more drift. The leader who cannot say the hard thing trains the team not to say the hard thing back. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].

Each of these is the practice operating in the open. The business gives you the diagnostic data; the practice is whether you act on it.

## The Ego-Obliteration Move

One reliable feature of business as a practice: it dissolves ego. Not by attacking the ego deliberately, but by exposing it to enough friction that it cannot hold its shape.

"If you allow your business to constantly shatter your ego, by being non-defensive and feeling all the things and letting yourself be wrong and letting yourself be right when you are, even when people disagree, all of that work is a disintegration of ego." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

A common misunderstanding: ego means thinking you are great. Joe's definition is broader. Any way you define yourself is ego — including defining yourself as scared, unclear, second-guessing. When the business obliterates the unclear-and-scared ego, the second-guessing stops. The person becomes more free to act.

This connects to [[identity]] and [[dont-fight-identity]]. The ego does not get attacked frontally. It gets dissolved in the heat of the actual work. The willingness to be wrong, to hear judgment, to receive negative feedback without bracing — these are the moves that, repeated, soften the structure that the ego was holding in place.

## The Downsides

Business as a spiritual practice has two known costs.

**It is hard mode.** Joe uses the term "level up mode." Business produces continuous distractions — ten phone calls a day, a customer crisis, an investor concern. Asking in the middle of those "how am I disconnected from myself right now?" is an advanced move. The signal-to-noise ratio is low. The person who tries to use only business as their practice is doing a particularly demanding version.

The practical response: pair business as a practice with time away from the business for focused inner work. Time alone, sustained, where the only object is the internal state, produces depths that no amount of in-the-business attention can produce.

**It requires time the business often pretends not to allow.** Twenty minutes of internal attention is a different thing from three days, five days, ten days. The business has its own logic of urgency, and the urgency claims time the practice needs. The leader who never withdraws to do the deeper work loses access to discoveries that are only available at length.

"It's that there needs to be a certain amount of time to be able to focus on your internal state. If I just sit with myself for 20 minutes, it's a different thing than if I sit with myself for 3 days or 5 days or 10 days. And there's discoveries that happen with a length of time. Oftentimes, business seems to tell you that you can't have the time to do the work. But there's a necessity for time to do some of this work." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

Both of these costs reduce to the same recommendation: business is a powerful container, but it is not the only container. It works best alongside dedicated time for the kind of work the business itself cannot provide.

## What the Practice Looks Like in Real Operating Decisions

Two concrete examples Joe gives:

**Gratitude at the start of meetings.** Beginning every business meeting with rounds of gratitude shifts the solving frame from "what's wrong and how do we fix it" to "what's right and how do we grow it." The second frame produces more solution sets. It also produces more connection and team cohesion. See [[gratitude-rounds-before-meetings]].

**The empower-over-power leadership move.** Joe walks leaders through a visualization: feel into the actual reality of being in your meetings. People are more concerned with you than with their peers. People want to please you. People are rebelling against you. People are passive-aggressive to you in ways they are not to their teammates. More conversations happen about you than about anyone else.

The point of feeling this reality is not to make leaders comfortable with the dynamic. It is to move them out of power (where they are unconsciously enjoying the deference, or unconsciously suffering it) into empowerment (where they can lead from truth). See [[visualize-the-reality-of-leadership]] for the full move.

One leader in such a session had this realization: "I was doing all this stuff to make people happy, and now I actually see that they need me to tell them what makes me happy for it to work." Her business changed quickly afterward: she let two people go, settled an investor, became more directive while still listening. The visualization shifted the orientation from power to empowerment, and the business shifted with it.

## What This Path Does Not Look Like

A common worry: do I have to do everything as a spiritual path? Can I have a business that makes bombs and treat it as the work? What if I just need to make money for my family?

Joe's answer is simple. If you do not want to do this, do not do this. Doing it from obligation will not work.

"If you don't want, you don't. Goodness gracious, like, like, if you don't want to have a spiritual practice, don't do it because of some sense of should or have to or because it makes your business better. That won't work. Don't do it." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The second response: many CEOs who have built billion-dollar companies in eight years carry the limiting belief that running a company in this way is impossible. Joe asks them: "How is it that you can build a multi-billion-dollar company in eight years and then the idea that it could be a spiritual path is just not possible?" The contradiction usually surfaces the belief, which usually then dissolves.

## The Outcome Is Not What You Expect

The mind imagines that this path either looks like radical visible change (everything falls apart) or like serene success (nothing changes externally, internal peace inside). The actual texture is usually different.

"It's not so much that your world will fall apart, it's your idea of the world will fall apart, or your — who you think you are in the world that will fall apart. So sometimes everything looks exactly the same, but everything is entirely different. And sometimes actually you pivot off of one business to a whole different business... and you end up making less money, but you're 10 times as happy, and then that happiness is contagious, which allows you to make 20 times as much money. It's just — it is a circuitous path for sure. But what it is, is it creates deeper alignment, more joy, more happiness, more flow. It just doesn't look the way your mind might want it to look." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The outcome is alignment, joy, flow. The form those produce is variable. The form is not the metric. The internal state is.

## Business as Art Form

A complementary framing: business is one of the few sustained art forms in adult life. The painter needs pigment. The business person needs revenue. The medium is different. The artistry is the same.

Unlike the plastic arts, which solidify, a business has a life of its own. It changes, evolves, has new conditions every year. It is closer to conducting a symphony — every conductor does the same piece differently, and the performance is alive in the moment.

"Whether you like it or not, it is an art form. It is a creation that is being made, and that creation that is being made is affecting people who interact with it. And that's the same thing art is." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

A business can put light in the world or create darkness in it. It can cut trees down or plant trees. The artist is responsible for the work. The same responsibility applies to the business builder. The art form Joe pursues is self-discovery — building a thing that helps people see themselves more clearly. Other people's art forms will be different. Whatever the art form is, the orientation that produces the best version is the practice.

See [[art-of-accomplishment]] for the underlying framework: ease, self-awareness, consciousness as the product.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A CEO is hitting a ceiling and the standard moves (more hours, more pressure, more strategy) are producing diminishing returns. The practice frame opens different leverage.
- A founder has succeeded financially and now feels empty in the work that used to motivate them. The means-to-end orientation has run its course. The practice frame reorients the work as the destination.
- A leader notices their team is afraid of them and they cannot understand why. The empower-over-power visualization surfaces the actual social structure of being a leader. See [[visualize-the-reality-of-leadership]].
- An entrepreneur is using business success as their primary self-development feedback and is hungry for a more conscious version of the same.
- A person on a spiritual path keeps experiencing the limits of cushion practice and is starting to see that the inner work needs a load-bearing container. Business is one of the cleanest available.
- A coachee is treating business and personal development as competing for the same time and energy. The application teaches that they are the same project.

## Tone

Practical. Confident. Joe speaks about this with the certainty of someone who has tested it across decades of running businesses, working with CEOs, and watching the pattern repeat. There is no spiritual gloss in the delivery. The vocabulary is operational: alignment, efficiency, leverage, $1,000 bills on the floor.
