# Relating to Someone Whose Mental Health Has Changed

## The Situation

A close relationship -- partner, parent, sibling, friend, child -- has been changed by the other person's mental health. The connection that used to be available has narrowed, distorted, or become unsafe. The person remains. The connection that used to exist between you may not be available in the same form anymore.

The question someone in this situation asks: how do I approach this relationship now? What can I do for them and what can I do for myself?

## The Core Frame: The Paralyzed Friend Analogy

The starting move is to recognize that the relationship needs to change shape because the other person has changed. If a friend had a physical injury and was paralyzed, you would not go for walks with them anymore. The activity that was the relationship is not available. The relationship continues in a different form.

"If I have a friend who had an injury and is paralyzed, I'm not going to go for walks with him anymore. So your friendship is going to change." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The same applies when a loved one's mental health has changed. If your friend is going through mental health changes, depending on the specific situation, you cannot expect them to:
- Be rational
- Be connected with you in the same way
- Not be paranoid around you (in certain conditions)
- Be even in this reality, depending on the situation

Letting go of these expectations is the first move. Not as a verdict on them, but as recognition of what is actually available.

## The Alzheimer's Pattern (Why People Stay Stuck)

A common failure mode appears most visibly with Alzheimer's, but it generalizes. The family interacts with the changed person as if they are still the unchanged person.

"You see this happen with people with Alzheimer's all the time. There's a dad and a mom and there's kids, and the dad gets Alzheimer's, and they're not even close to as functional. They can't say the same things, but everybody's treating them the same way. Everybody's like, 'Oh, that's Dad, I have to be scared of his anger, so I'm going to do whatever he says.' But the guy's got Alzheimer's, you shouldn't be doing what he says. You shouldn't probably shouldn't have before. But you're interacting with them as if they're still coherent when they're not coherent." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The bind: roles assigned when the person was unchanged stay in place even after the person changes. The fear, the deference, the assumption of authority -- all of those continue automatically. Updating the relationship to who the person actually is now requires conscious work.

The pattern applies to many forms of mental health change. The mother who was always emotionally volatile and is now in psychosis still gets treated as someone whose anger must be managed -- but now the anger is not even hers in the same way. The brother whose addiction has changed his personality still gets treated as the trustworthy younger sibling. The partner whose anxiety has become severe still gets treated as someone who can hold the difficult conversations they used to be able to hold.

In every case, the work is recognizing what is actually available now and interacting based on that, not based on the prior version.

## Self-Care Is Not Optional

The principle: you cannot be good for them if you are not being good for yourself.

"There's no possible way for you to be good for them if you're not being good for yourself. So self-care is an incredibly important part of that process. You don't want to enter into their mental health with them. You want to make sure that you are taking care of yourself and your own mental health in that process. And it's really critical because just like if all of a sudden you're living with somebody who needs you to cook for them and clean for them and wipe them and all that stuff, it's going to take a lot. It's going to take a lot if you're all of a sudden dealing with somebody who's constantly walking around the house thinking that there's bugs in the wall that are listening to them." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The two failure modes if self-care is neglected:

1. **You join them in the swirl.** Extended exposure to disordered thinking, paranoia, or psychic disturbance without your own grounding pulls you into it. You start losing the difference between their reality and yours.

2. **You resent them and lash out.** The accumulated cost of caretaking without renewal eventually shows up as either coldness or as the very anger you have been trying not to express. Either is corrosive to the relationship.

"Eventually, if you do sacrifice yourself in it long enough, either you're gonna join them in their swirl, or you're gonna just resent them and lash out at some point, neither of which is going to feel good." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

Self-care here is not a luxury or a self-indulgence. It is the operational prerequisite for being able to remain in the relationship at all.

## Boundaries Are Central

Joe returns to boundaries specifically at the end of his answer:

"Boundaries are really an important process. So listen to the boundary thing. Boundaries are really important with mental health. That's like some of the biggest part of the self-care is the boundaries." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

In this context, boundaries are not punishment of the changed person. They are the structure that allows the relationship to continue without consuming you. The boundary may be:
- A limit on time spent in conversation when the conversation is destabilizing
- A topic that is not available to be discussed
- A behavior of theirs you will not be present for
- A physical distance that lets you stay connected without being absorbed
- A schedule of when you are available and when you are not

The boundaries are about you, not about them. They state what you will do and not do, not what they must do. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] for the loving form. See [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums]] for what to avoid.

The boundary work is also the practical version of [[is-this-mine]]: noticing what is theirs to carry and what is yours to carry, and not picking up the parts that are theirs.

## Find Professional Support

The third move is to get professional support. For them, for you, or for both.

"The last thing is go find a professional who can help you. It's really important to find a professional who can help them and to help you." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

Joe acknowledges the practical difficulty: there are not enough therapists or psychiatrists right now for the people who need them. But the principle stands. Do whatever you have to do to get the help, including online reading if professional access is not available.

The model Joe references: addiction recovery has long had a parallel structure of support for the family of the addict, not just for the addict (Al-Anon for families of alcoholics). The same need exists for families of people with mental health issues. You need support too.

Brett's articulation in the same conversation:
"It reminds me of something you've mentioned before where like a lot of the Addicts Anonymous groups have groups for the people who are supporting or like entangled. And I think the same thing can be true for mental health. You need support." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A January 2023

## Grieve the Connection That Was

The deeper work that holds everything else together is grief for the connection that used to be available. The connection the person could have with you when they were not in the grip of mental health changes is, for now, not available. That loss is real. Treating it as not-a-loss is what produces the magical thinking that keeps the family interacting with the changed person as the unchanged person.

Brett's reflection that Joe affirmed:

"A lot of what you're speaking to there is grieving the connection that you had so that you can find the connection that is available, and how deeply can you connect to the person as they are and stay with yourself and your needs without sacrificing yourself." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A January 2023

Joe: "That's right."

The grief work makes everything else possible. Without grieving the lost form of the connection, you keep reaching for it -- which keeps the disappointment fresh and keeps the energy of the relationship locked in the prior pattern. Grieving it frees you to be in connection with the person as they actually are now.

See [[mourn-the-breakup-fully]] for the structurally similar principle in romantic relationships. See [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]] for what the grief actually contains.

## The Sequence

1. **Recognize the change.** The person is not who they were in the relevant respects. Stop relating to them as if they are.

2. **Grieve the connection that was.** Let yourself feel the loss of what used to be available. Without this grief, the rest of the work is undermined by reaching for the prior form.

3. **Find the connection that is.** Some form of connection is usually still available. What is it? Notice it. Be in it. Stop comparing it to the prior form.

4. **Set the boundaries that allow your self-care.** Specific to the situation. Often more boundaries than feel comfortable at first. The boundaries protect the connection that still exists by keeping you well enough to be in it.

5. **Get professional support.** For them if possible. For you regardless of whether it is possible for them. If professional support is hard to access, find reading, online groups, or any structured help.

6. **Continue checking what is actually available.** Mental health changes. The relationship needs to be checked and recalibrated. What was available six months ago may not be available now, or new things may be available now that were not before.

## Joe's Caveat

Joe is straightforward that this is not his specialized area:

"There are probably better people to ask that question to than me. I don't deal with a lot of folks who -- I've had some have mental health issues in my friend group and family, but it hasn't been something that I have a ton of experience with." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

His coaching here applies general principles from the larger work (boundaries, self-care, grief, accurate seeing of the other person) to this specific domain. Domain-specific expertise about particular mental health conditions belongs elsewhere. Joe's contribution is the relational frame within which the domain-specific work can land.

## Anti-Patterns Specific to This Domain

- **Treating the changed person as unchanged.** The Alzheimer's pattern. Update your relating.
- **Joining them in the swirl.** Losing yourself in their reality to stay close to them. See [[losing-yourself-in-others]].
- **Self-sacrifice as love.** Long-term self-abandonment will produce resentment or breakdown. See [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]].
- **Caretaking without limits.** See [[caring-vs-caretaking]]. The unlimited caretaker eventually collapses or becomes resentful.
- **Trying to fix them.** They have a condition. You are not the cure. Trying to be the cure increases the harm to both. See [[fixing-people]].
- **Refusing to grieve.** Without the grief for the prior form, the new form cannot be accepted.

## When This Concern Comes Up

- A family member has had a major mental health change (psychotic break, depression, addiction, dementia, anxiety disorder)
- A long-time friend has become someone the person no longer recognizes
- A partner is in a mental health crisis and the speaker is exhausted
- A parent's mental decline has changed the dynamic in the family
- A child has developed mental health issues the parent does not know how to be with
