# Welcoming Fear in Leadership

## Definition

The leader's relationship to fear sets the team's relationship to fear. A leader who suppresses their own fear, treats team fear as a morale problem, and projects unbroken confidence trains the team to do the same. The result is a team that cannot surface concerns, cannot bring early warnings, and discovers problems too late to fix them.

The alternative is a leader who feels their own fear, welcomes others' fear as information, and treats the conversation about fear as a normal part of how the work gets done. This produces a team that catches problems early, makes better decisions, and trusts the leader's reads when the leader does project confidence.

## What This Replaces

The default leadership stance: "My job is to keep it all together. My job is to make sure that nobody else is afraid. So everybody just believes that everything is going according to plan."

Brett describes this stance and its predictable failure:

"Starting to become a CEO from being freelance started to be like, okay, my job is to keep it all together. My job is to make sure that nobody else is afraid. So like everybody just believes that everything is going according to plan. And that also didn't work very well. It definitely blew up in my face a number of times." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

The blow-ups have a consistent shape:

1. Team members raise small concerns early.
2. The leader, operating under "keep it all together," treats the concerns as noise to manage rather than as data to investigate.
3. The team learns that raised concerns are not acted on. Concerns stop being raised.
4. The underlying issues, now invisible to the leader, grow.
5. A crisis arrives that "no one saw coming," except that the team did see it coming and was not heard.

## The Pattern of Suppressing the Team's Fear

Specific forms the suppression takes:

- "We got this" said quickly when a concern is raised.
- Treating expressions of fear as evidence of low morale or insufficient buy-in rather than as information about the project.
- Reframing concerns as positivity problems: "let's stay focused on what's working."
- Praising team members who do not raise concerns and quietly distrusting those who do.
- Bringing the team news of a setback packaged as "this is fine, here is the spin."
- Holding back from saying "I'm scared" because the leader has internalized that the leader does not get to be scared.

Each move is locally rational and globally destructive. The leader is protecting the team from disturbance. The team is being protected out of the information loop that the leader depends on.

"Ways that would blow up is like people would start to bring fears and concerns to the surface and those concerns would be uncomfortable and inconvenient and I might even see them as potentially damaging morale or distracting people from the goal of the project. And oftentimes those concerns were very, very valid." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

## The Alternative: Permissioning Fear

The shift is to treat fear as a permitted, expected, and useful part of the workplace conversation. Three components:

1. **The leader names their own fear.** When something is scary, the leader says so. Not as a performance of vulnerability. As an honest report of internal state. "I'm scared about this. Here is what specifically I'm afraid of."

2. **The leader receives others' fears as information, not as morale problems.** When a team member raises a concern, the response is to investigate the concern, not to manage the team member's emotional state. Even when the concern turns out not to be valid, the feeling that produced it is treated as real and worth understanding.

3. **The leader makes room for the team to feel afraid together when something hard happens.** Not in a way that requires the team to perform fear. In a way that does not require the team to perform certainty.

The example Brett gives of a major client cutting their contract:

"Hey, so, you know, this has happened. This is good for a lot of reasons. It's also scary and we're going to flow with this and do what we need to do. But like, there are many reasons that we are in a good position right now. And, you know, there's also uncertainty. And let's step into it and see what we can do." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

The framing holds three things at once: real reasons for confidence, the actual scariness of the situation, and an invitation to act inside the uncertainty. The team's response was not collapse. It was largely excitement, with some natural departures.

## Why Welcoming Fear Builds Trust

Counterintuitively, the leader who says "this is scary" produces more trust than the leader who says "we got this." The reason: people can feel when something is being managed. When the leader projects unbroken confidence in a situation that is plainly difficult, the team senses the gap and stops trusting the leader's reads. When the leader's stated emotional position matches what the team is also feeling, the team trusts that the leader's other reports are honest.

"Clients and I feel much more comfortable that neither of us are going to move forward into something that really doesn't work for us or that isn't set up for success. And there's also trust that if anything changes course, as something always does, that we will be able to have the conversations to correct course and flow with reality as it's coming at us, which leads to a much deeper sense of safety." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

The leader who can say "I don't know" or "this is scary" or "we might not have this figured out" is the leader who is also believed when they say "I'm confident this will work."

## Stacked Fears Heard Early

A specific compounding benefit: when fear is permissioned, small concerns get raised and addressed before they become large problems. By the time a major event arrives (a client loss, a market shift, a key hire leaving), the team has been making small adjustments along the way that turn the major event into a manageable transition rather than a catastrophe.

"A lot of that came down to listening to earlier fears of like, well, what would happen if, you know, we lost a bunch of business? Well, let's be ready for that. So it was a bunch of stacked fears having been listened to that led us to be in a position that we could turn it around that quickly." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

The two-month turnaround from a major client loss to cash-flow positive was possible because earlier, smaller fears had been heard and acted on. The system had built the pipeline that the loss now needed.

## The Internal Work Comes First

Welcoming the team's fear is downstream of the leader welcoming their own fear. A leader who is still in [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]] cannot hear the team's fear cleanly because they cannot hear their own. The team's fear shows up to them as friction, complaint, or insubordination because their own internal signal-detection is offline.

This is why leadership development that focuses only on external skills (giving feedback, holding meetings, communicating vision) often fails to change the actual culture. The culture is downstream of the leader's internal relationship to their own fear. Until the leader can feel their fear, they cannot make space for others to feel theirs.

The internal work: feel the numbness, let the anxiety differentiate, hear what the specific fears are signaling about what is cared for and what is threatened, and ask for what is needed. See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]], [[fear-vs-anxiety]], [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]].

## The Caretaking Trap

A common error in leadership: treating the team's fear as something to caretake rather than to welcome. The leader hears "I'm worried about timeline" and tries to make the worry go away through reassurance, problem-solving on their behalf, or emotional smoothing.

This is [[fixing-people]] in a leadership context. The team member did not need their fear taken away. They needed it heard and the underlying issue addressed. The caretaking move bypasses both.

The welcoming move: "Tell me more about what specifically you're afraid of. What do you see that I might not be seeing?" The fear is treated as containing information, and the team member is treated as a capable adult whose emotional process belongs to them.

See [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]] for the general move applied to any difficult emotion. The leadership application is the same pattern in a workplace context.

## Boundaries Still Apply

Welcoming fear does not mean indulging every concern or letting fear run the decisions. The leader is still responsible for making calls. The difference is that the calls are now made with the team's fear-information available rather than suppressed.

Sometimes the leader will hear the team's fear, take it seriously, investigate the underlying issue, and still proceed in the direction the fear was warning against. That is a clean decision. The fear was heard. The decision incorporates the information. The leader is responsible for the call and for the consequences.

This connects to [[vulnerability-in-the-workplace]]: vulnerability does not require tolerating bad behavior or making decisions by committee. It requires that the decisions be made with all the information available, including the emotional information that the suppressing leader has been throwing away.

## What Changes for the Leader

When a leader makes this shift, the person-level changes show up first:

- Stops overpromising. The fear of looking weak by being honest about uncertainty drops.
- Underdelivers less often. The reality-based plans replace the optimism-distorted plans.
- Has the difficult conversations earlier. The conversation about a misaligned contract, a struggling team member, a failing initiative happens before the situation has compounded.
- Builds different relationships with clients. Trust replaces transaction.
- Sleeps better. The energy spent on managing the appearance of having-it-together becomes available for the actual work.

"There's just a much deeper sense of trust. Clients and I feel much more comfortable that neither of us are going to move forward into something that really doesn't work for us or that isn't set up for success." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear
