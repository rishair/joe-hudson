# Body Awareness in Meetings and Work

## The Application

Teams and businesses default to long uninterrupted intellectual work — three-hour meetings, all-day strategy sessions, head-down knowledge work — as if that were the most effective configuration. Artists know this is not true. Musicians, dancers, and improvisers rely on body intelligence as a prerequisite for creativity. The application is to import what the arts have learned about embodiment into the contexts where business defaults to disembodied intellect.

"It's interesting because the artists have really figured this out. They know it to be very creative. This is a requirement, is to be in your body. Yet teams and businesses often don't utilize this tool at all. They'll sit for a meeting for two or three hours straight as if that's the best way to be creative or solve problems." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

## Simple Practices That Will Feel Odd at First

**Permission to move.** Make it explicit that anyone in a meeting can stand up, stretch, shake out, or take deep breaths without it being a disruption. Most cultures implicitly forbid this; the application is to lift the prohibition.

**A bell or signal for body check-in.** One company keeps a bell in each meeting room. When struck, everyone pauses for a moment of silence to check in with their bodies. They do not call it "be in your body" — they call it "get in touch with what's happening right here and now." The signal is the intervention; the body returns whether the language is somatic or not.

**Five minutes of meditation at the start of a board meeting.** A board Joe sat on opened every meeting this way. The shift in the quality of the meeting was the point — not the meditation itself.

**Gratitude practice as an opener.** A gratitude practice does two things at once: it grounds attention in the body (it is hard to be genuinely grateful without dropping into the body) and it changes what people will look for in the meeting. Most meetings look for what is wrong and how to fix it. Gratitude-opened meetings look for what is right and how to build it. Different problems get solved by each orientation.

## The Programmer's 45-15 Rhythm

A programmer measured his own output across years and arrived at: 45 minutes of programming, 15 minutes in his body, repeated. Six hours of programming a day in this rhythm produced the most code, and the most good code, more reliably than other configurations he tried.

"And you know, being a programmer, he every day measured his practices and measured his results, like how good of programming he did, how effective it was. And so he had done years of experiments to get to this." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The principle generalizes. Novelists Joe knows have arrived at three hours a day, first thing in the morning. The exact ratio matters less than the recognition that the productive period is finite and the non-productive period is not idle — it is body-time that processes what the head was working on.

This is related to but more specific than the Pomodoro technique. Pomodoro emphasizes the break. The 45-15 emphasizes what the break is for: getting into the body so the brain's other modes of processing can take over.

## Why This Works: The Background-Processing Mechanism

When a problem is salient enough, the system processes it whether the conscious intellect is engaged or not. The intellect can only hold about four chunks of information in working memory. For problems with more complexity than four chunks, conscious thought is not enough. Letting the conscious thought drop and giving the body's other processing modes their turn is how the larger problem gets worked on.

This is the mechanism behind insights in the shower, on a walk, in the moment of stepping away. The work continues; the conscious narrator has stepped aside.

A famous Nobel laureate's technique: when stuck on a problem, forbid the intellect from approaching it until the whole solution arrives. "Anytime his brain would go to think about the problem, he's like, nope, nope, not thinking about the problem, not thinking about the problem, until the entire solution was like, foomp." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The instruction was paradoxical and effective. The solution arrived whole, not piece by piece, because the body's processing produces wholes that the intellect's serial processing does not.

## When to Use Body Intelligence in Decision-Making

A common misreading: "Body intelligence is for when the chips are down — when I have to act fast in the clutch. The rest of the time, I will calculate."

This is partially right. Body intelligence is best for situations with no time to calculate: shooting a three-pointer, flying a wingsuit, playing jazz, having a child cry in your face.

It is also useful when there is plenty of time to calculate. Most consequential business decisions get worse, not better, by adding more weeks of data collection. A person who has done the calculation and then drops into the body — checking in with the heart and the gut on the decision the intellect has worked out — often arrives at a clearer decision than the same person who keeps researching.

"You see this happen all the time where people can spend months calculating all the data to make a decision. And if they get in connection with the rest of their being, not just the intellect, that decision can be made far better and more effective." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

See [[decisions-are-emotional]] for why this is structurally true.

## The Pattern Interrupt Function

In a meeting that has gotten heated or has gone down an intellectual rabbit hole, a body check-in is a useful pattern interrupt. It is hard to keep arguing while paying attention to your body. The body's presence dissolves the rigidity of the argument enough that people can recover a wider field of options.

This is structural, not psychological. People who are upset are typically not in their bodies. Returning to the body collapses some of the upset.

## Bringing the Pre-Decision Grief Into the Meeting

A specific move for big decisions: before making the decision, grieve all the possible failures. Feel the consequences in the body in advance.

"And there's also like the emotional side of that too, which is like, oh, what's making you need to have the decision be right? What happens if you get it all wrong? What happens if you fail? And to grieve all that before you make a decision can clarify your decision to no end." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

This is a body-intelligence move in a business decision-making context. The grief is what allows the decision to be clean. Without it, the decision is contaminated by avoidance of the feared outcome. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]] and [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].

## Cultural Risk: Feeling Odd at First

These practices feel awkward in conventional corporate settings. The discomfort is the same discomfort that prevents most people from doing them. Naming the awkwardness as part of the practice — not as a sign that the practice is wrong — is part of the application.

For a leader bringing this into an organization, the practice has more weight when the leader does it visibly themselves, including the awkward early period.
