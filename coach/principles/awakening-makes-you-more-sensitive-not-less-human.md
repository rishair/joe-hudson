# Awakening Makes You More Sensitive, Not Less Human

## The Principle

The mythology of awakening imagines that the person becomes less human, more transcendent, less affected by ordinary life. The reality is closer to the opposite. After awakening, the person is more human — more sensitive to what is happening internally and externally, more affected by misalignment, less able to tolerate the chronic small distortions they used to absorb without noticing.

Joe's formulation: "Before awakening, chop wood, carry water. After awakening, chop wood, carry water. My expression of that is before awakening, daddy issues, after awakening, daddy issues." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The daddy issues do not get transcended by the recognition. They remain. What changes is the sensitivity to them. The person who used to operate around the issues without noticing the wear they were producing now feels the wear in real time and cannot help but address it.

## What Specifically Increases

After the recognition lands and integrates, the system becomes more sensitive on at least three dimensions:

**Sensitivity to internal misalignment.** Things the person used to put up with — relationships, work arrangements, food, environments that did not fit them — become unbearable. The body that has stopped its chronic bracing can feel the friction of the mismatched arrangement clearly. The mismatch was always producing the friction; the bracing was concealing the perception of it.

**Sensitivity to others' states.** The same increased capacity to feel internal states extends to picking up the emotional reality of other people. The person becomes a sensitive reader of the room without trying to be. See [[sensitivity-as-byproduct]] for the parallel mechanism that arrives through emotional embracing work.

**Sensitivity to subtle wrongness.** Small misalignments in conversation, in environment, in routine that used to be background noise become foregrounded. The threshold for noticing has dropped.

"There's another thing, which is you just become a lot more sensitive to stuff that is not — that doesn't feel right. And if you don't go into dissociation, then you're stuck with basically a more sensitive system that just cannot put up with the same kind of crap that you put up with before." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

## The Fork: Dissociation vs. Sensitivity

The increased sensitivity arrives whether or not the person can hold it. There is a fork.

The person who can hold the sensitivity uses it as operational information. The sensitivity becomes a guide. They make decisions based on what their now-more-sensitive system reads about what fits them and what doesn't. The decisions tend to be cleaner because the readings are more accurate.

The person who cannot hold the sensitivity goes into dissociation. There is one available exit from the increased sensitivity: the spacious awareness that the awakening also makes available can be used as a place to withdraw to. The person uses the recognition as a dissociation tool. They feel less because they have retreated into the awareness rather than inhabiting the body that is doing the sensing.

This is one of the failure modes of post-awakening life. See [[zen-sickness]] for the syndrome. The structural reason it happens: the same recognition that produces the sensitivity also provides an escape from it. The person who is overwhelmed by what they now feel can disappear into the awareness, and the dissociation looks like spiritual maturity from outside.

## Why Old Bad Habits Fade But Remaining Ones Get Sharper

A subtle consequence of the principle: some bad habits drop away after awakening (the ones that were being held in place by the bounded-self that the awakening dissolves), while others get *more* uncomfortable, not less.

"There's one aspect where you can always go into the spacious awareness where you can become dissociated. But there's another thing, which is you just become a lot more sensitive to stuff... So on some weird level, it's like some bad habits will like immediately fade and some won't, but the ones that remain will become so much more uncomfortable, will become like — you'll become so much more sensitive to the things that aren't in alignment with you." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The fading habits are the ones the bounded self was supporting. Without the bounded self, they have nothing to attach to. They just stop.

The persisting habits are usually the ones rooted in the body's nervous-system patterning or in unprocessed emotional material. The awakening at the head level doesn't reach them. But the increased sensitivity *does* reach them — and now the body that has them feels them with more clarity, which makes the discomfort sharper. The person becomes less able to ignore them, which often forces engagement with them rather than around them.

This is why post-awakening life often involves an unexpected round of practical work: the things that did not fade automatically now have to be addressed, and the sensitivity is making them impossible to live with unaddressed.

## The Connection to Decisive Action

The increased sensitivity to misalignment translates into more automatic action. When the body reads that something is not aligned, the action to address it becomes near-immediate. The deliberation phase shortens.

"To have that sensitivity is to give yourself a strong impulsive direction. I don't mean impulsive in like obsessive compulsive or something. I mean like the impulse in me when I'm feeling sensitivity to something that's not right, that doesn't feel aligned with me, then, it becomes an automatic action rather than something I need to sit down, write a pros and cons list, check with 16 people and make sure I'm seeing it correctly. Just like, 'Oh no, this doesn't work for me.' And I'm gonna step into without considering that that's not who I've been before or part of my identity. It's just a different action I'm taking and it's gonna get a different result and I'm here for it." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The mechanism: the bounded self was doing the deliberation. It needed to check whether the action was consistent with its identity, whether it would be approved, whether it was safe given the bounded-self's vulnerabilities. Without the bounded self, the deliberation has nothing to deliberate. The reading is the action.

This connects to [[decisions-are-emotional]] from a different angle. After awakening, the emotional reading is more accurate and the bounded-self interference with it has dropped, so the decisions get cleaner and faster.

## Why This Disarms the Awakening-as-Transcendence Picture

The standard picture of awakening: the person rises above ordinary human life. They no longer get triggered by their parents. They no longer feel the small slights. They no longer have personality patterns. They have transcended their humanity.

This picture is wrong, and the wrongness produces specific harms. People who buy the picture either give up on awakening (because they cannot imagine becoming that disconnected from themselves) or pursue awakening as a way to escape their humanity (which produces the dissociated half-awakening that looks like the picture from outside but is not the recognition).

The accurate picture: the person becomes more human. The recognition does not transcend the personal; it dissolves the bounded-self that was producing distance from the personal. With the bounded self released, the person is more in their humanity, not less. The daddy issues are still daddy issues. They just get felt more cleanly, addressed more directly, and often resolved through the doing of the work rather than transcended around.

This is also the structural reason that post-awakening integration takes years. The increased sensitivity exposes everything that was previously hidden. All of it then needs to be welcomed. The welcoming is not done by the recognition. It is done by the person who now has both the recognition and the increased perception of what needs welcoming.

## Tone

Joe presents this with the dry humor that the daddy-issues formulation makes available. The joke is also the teaching: the spiritual reframe of an old Zen koan ("before enlightenment, chop wood, carry water; after enlightenment, chop wood, carry water") into the language of contemporary therapy ("daddy issues") makes the principle accessible without losing its content.

The delivery is matter-of-fact. There is no implied critique of people who hold the transcendence picture. The picture is wrong but understandable. The principle is offered as a correction, not a polemic.
