# Find Your Optimal Effort Window by Experimenting

## The Principle

There is a maximum amount of effort beyond which more effort decreases output. Not increases more slowly. Decreases. Past the optimal window, additional hours actively destroy the work the earlier hours produced. The basketball player who trains 24 hours a day is not the best basketball player; they are crippled. The factory that runs 24/7 is not the most productive factory; it is the one that breaks down first.

The optimal effort window is specific to the person, the activity, and the conditions. It cannot be derived from a formula or copied from someone else. It has to be found through experimentation. Once found, operating inside it produces more output than operating beyond it, by a wide margin.

"There is a maximum amount of effort that you can put in that's going to get the best results. And to find out what that is, is the first step of making sure you get the rest that you need." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

## The Basketball Player and the Factory

The two arguments Joe uses to establish that an optimal window exists at all:

**The basketball player.** A basketball player who works out 24 hours a day is not going to be the best basketball player. They are going to be crippled. They will not even be alive. The body has a maximum dose of training it can absorb before training becomes injury. The same is true for any sustained effort.

"If a basketball player worked out 24 hours a day, they clearly are not going to be the best basketball player. They're going to be cripple, right? That they're not going to actually even be alive. So there's a certain amount that you can work and there's a certain amount where you can't work because you need rest and you need rest physically so that your muscles can recover. But you also need rest mentally so that you can learn, so you can process the information, so that you come at it fresh, so that you have the right energy." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

**The factory.** Even economists know this about factories. A factory cannot run 24/7 at its optimal production rate. The factory needs rest, maintenance, downtime for the machines, time for adjustments. The 24/7 factory is not the most productive one. It is the one that breaks down soonest and produces the lowest quality output during its breakdown.

"Even economists know this about factories, that you can't run a factory 24/7 and have it be in its optimal production rate. You need to actually rest the factory as well. You need to be able to have maintenance. There's other things that you have to do." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

Both arguments are uncontroversial. Nobody disputes them. The principle extends: if the basketball player and the factory have an optimal window, the person doing knowledge work has one too. The person who works 14 hours a day is not the most productive version of themselves. They are the version closest to the basketball player who trains 24/7.

See [[rest-is-an-accomplishment]] for the related weightlifter argument: the forms of weightlifting that actually build muscle are 20 minutes 3-4 times a week with recovery. The 24/7 weightlifter does not get ripped. They break down.

## The Author Experiment

A specific worked example Joe uses: an author who tested two different writing schedules to find his optimal window.

"He said, I'm gonna work 10 hours a day and see how much that produces, or I'm gonna do 3 hours a day for 7 days straight and see how much that produces. And over time, what he found out was through this experimental process, he would take 2 15-minute breaks where he got in his body, where he would dance or walk or something that got him grounded in his body. Even if he was in flow, he found out that, oh, if I stop and I get in my body, then I get more good pages in." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

Two operational features in this example:

- **The experiment was time-bounded and comparable.** Not "I'll just see how it goes." Specific schedules. Specific outputs. Comparable measurements over time. The person could see what was actually working because they had set up the comparison.
- **The surprising finding: even mid-flow body breaks improved output.** The author found that interrupting himself for body movement, even when he was in flow, produced more good pages. The counterintuitive nature of the finding (stop when it's going well?) is exactly why it required the experiment. No theory would have produced it. The data did.

The takeaway is not "everyone should take 15-minute body breaks." The takeaway is that the optimal window for any specific person is non-obvious and must be experimentally determined. The author's window included 15-minute body breaks. Someone else's might not. The discovery requires the experiment.

## The Bezos 10am-to-5pm Decision Window

A high-leverage version of the same principle, applied to decision-making rather than general work:

"It was Jeff Bezos. Jeff Bezos, starting at about 10 o'clock after 2 hours of what he called wandering and ending meeting at 5 was the optimal amount of time that he could use to make great decisions. Otherwise, he started getting fatigued and he would make really bad decisions." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

Several things to notice in this framing:

- **The window is shorter than most people would guess.** Seven hours of meetings, not twelve. The person running Amazon found that more than seven hours of decision-making produced worse decisions, not just slower ones.
- **The window is preceded by unstructured time.** Two hours of "wandering" before the first meeting. The wandering is not a luxury; it is what makes the seven hours of decision-making work at full capacity. See [[jeff-bezos-calls-his-mornings-wandering]].
- **Past the window, decisions actively get worse.** Not "marginally worse." Fatigued decisions are reliably bad decisions. The person who keeps pushing past their decision window is not making suboptimal decisions; they are making damaging ones.

The application: if a person's job involves making decisions, finding their decision window is the highest-leverage thing they can do for the quality of their work. A single good decision can save years of life. A single bad decision can cost years. Operating outside the window produces bad decisions. The window-finding is therefore not a comfort issue. It is a quality-of-life issue.

"Think about a decision that you made that cost you years of your life. Maybe it's somebody that you dated, or maybe it's the way that you raised your kids. One good decision can save you years upon years of your life." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

## How to Run the Experiment

The operational form Joe gives:

1. **Pick one thing that matters most to you.** Not everything. One activity where the quality of the output matters and where you have control over the effort. "Pickleball or creating great art or making great decisions" — the example doesn't matter; the specificity matters.

2. **Test different effort windows.** How many hours can you do this thing well in a row? How many days a week? With what breaks? Run two schedules for a few weeks each. Compare the output.

3. **Measure output, not effort.** The whole point is that effort is not the metric. The metric is what got produced. Pages written. Decisions made well. Pickleball games won.

4. **Notice what supports the window.** The author needed 15-minute body breaks. Bezos needed 2 hours of wandering before the window started. Other supports are personal: walks, naps, particular foods, certain time-of-day windows, specific environments. Find yours.

5. **Operate inside the window.** Once you know where the window is, stop working outside it. The "extra" hours outside the window are not bonus production. They are subtracted from the production inside the window.

## Why Most People Have Not Done This Experiment

The cultural assumption is that more effort produces more output, full stop. Inside that assumption, the experiment is unnecessary — you already know what to do (more). The experiment becomes available only after the assumption has been disturbed.

The disturbance is what episodes like the basketball-24/7 argument provide. Once the person sees that an optimal window must exist (because the basketball player would be crippled, because the factory would break down), the question becomes: where is mine?

Most people, once they have run the experiment honestly, find that their optimal window is smaller than they have been pushing. The over-effort has been producing under-output for years. The discovery is often deflating in the short term ("I've been wasting hours") and freeing in the medium term ("I can do less and produce more").

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A few worked applications:

- **A writer who has been writing 8 hours a day with declining quality.** Experiment: 3 hours, 7 days a week, with body breaks. Likely finding: more good pages.
- **A CEO making decisions all day in back-to-back meetings.** Experiment: protect a decision-window (e.g., 10am-3pm) and refuse meetings outside it. Likely finding: better decisions, less burnout.
- **A founder grinding on the company 80 hours a week.** Experiment: 40 high-quality hours with protected rest. Likely finding: the company moves faster.
- **A parent trying to be present with their kids for every waking hour.** Experiment: 3-4 hours of fully present time, separated by rest. Likely finding: the present hours are actually present.

In each case, the principle is the same: the additional hours were not producing additional output. They were eating into the output that the optimal hours were generating.

## When Someone Experiences This

- When they have been working harder and harder and the results have plateaued or declined.
- When they look at their week and cannot account for what the additional hours produced.
- When they notice the quality of their work dropping later in the day or week.
- When a decision they made when tired turned out to be bad in a way it would not have been if they had made it fresh.
- When they realize they have not actually tested whether more effort produces more output; they have assumed it.
