# The Solution to Perfectionism Is to Change Your Approach

## The Principle

When perfectionism is operating, the productive move is not to try harder to be perfect, and it is not to abandon caring about quality. It is to swap the organizing principle of the work. The question shifts from "how do I get this perfect?" to "what is the approach I am bringing to this?"

"One of the solutions for perfectionism is to take a different perspective and say, oh, is — how is my approach — it's not how do I get it perfect, it's how do I change my approach." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The principle is meta. It does not prescribe one alternative to perfectionism; it identifies the move that lets the person see they have been gripping one organizing principle (perfection) and could be gripping a different one. Several different organizing principles work better than perfection. Pick one.

## The Four Approach Alternatives

In the dedicated perfectionism conversation Joe names four orientations that consistently outperform perfectionism on both the outcome side and the felt-experience side:

**Connection.** Make the organizing question: how do I connect with this? Connection with the work, with the people, with the customer, with the team. See [[connection-over-perfection]] for the dedicated principle. The work becomes a place to be in relationship with what is in front of you rather than a thing to be made to fit a standard. Joe's specific example: in coaching videos, the three things he is focused on are "is my heart open, am I following them, am I making sure I'm not trying to get them anywhere." Those three connection variables are the organizing principle. Everything else falls out of those three being right.

**Excellence.** Make the organizing question: how do I just constantly improve? How do I do my best work? Excellence is not perfection. Excellence is the orientation toward iteration and continuous improvement, accepting that any particular attempt is one moment in a long arc. See [[iterative-mindset]] for the underlying capacity. The team that orients toward excellence outperforms the team that orients toward perfection by roughly 30%, by the data Joe references; the same dynamic operates inside an individual.

**Enjoyment.** Make the organizing question: how do I enjoy this process? What's the version of doing this work that I would actually like? See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] for the operational move and [[enjoy-over-manage]] for the broader distinction. Enjoyment is a sustainable engine; perfectionism is not. Work that gets enjoyed gets repeated. Work that gets perfected gets dreaded. Over a long arc, enjoyed work compounds into mastery; perfected work compounds into avoidance.

**Process.** Make the organizing question: what process do I think will get to the conclusion in the best way? The orientation is to the path rather than to the endpoint. See [[process-over-result]]. Each step becomes the place where the quality is being made, rather than each step being judged against an imagined finished product.

"Any of those things work a lot better than perfectionism because you're seeing it, you're seeing a broader context in it. You're seeing the how you get from one place to another rather than 'I know what this is supposed to look like and now my job is to try to make it look like that.'" -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The four are not exclusive. A single piece of work can run on a combination -- connection plus excellence, enjoyment plus process. The principle is not that one of the four is correct. The principle is that perfection is not on the menu of organizing principles that produce good work; any of these four are, and the work is to pick one.

## Why Approach-Substitution Works Where Standard-Lowering Doesn't

A common mistake when working with perfectionism is to try to lower the standard: "don't try to make it perfect, just make it good enough." This rarely lands. The reason: the perfectionist's mind has no felt-stable definition of "good enough." Any standard the mind is asked to settle for becomes the next thing the mind says is not perfect. The standard-lowering frame leaves the perfectionist still operating on the perfection algorithm; it just asks the algorithm to use a lower setpoint. The algorithm renormalizes immediately.

Approach-substitution works differently. It replaces the entire operating principle. The work is no longer being judged against any standard at all; it is being done in a different way. Connection is not a lower standard than perfection; it is a different operating mode. The same is true of excellence, enjoyment, and process. The perfectionist mind cannot renormalize an approach the way it renormalizes a standard, because the approach is not a number to move; it is a kind of relationship to the work.

The "loss of context" that defines perfectionism (see the perfectionism article's section on this) is also what makes standard-lowering fail. The perfectionist has narrowed onto the immediate detail and cannot see why getting this detail right matters. Lowering the standard on the detail still leaves the person locked into the wrong layer of attention. Approach-substitution restores the wider context by changing what the work is for, which changes what the relevant attention is.

## How to Run the Substitution in Coaching

The move in session is concrete. When a person describes being stuck or paralyzed by perfectionism, redirect away from the standard and onto the approach.

The questions that operate this move:

- "What if your job here wasn't to get it right but to enjoy doing it?" -- the enjoyment substitution
- "What if your job was to stay connected to the person you're making this for?" -- the connection substitution
- "What if your job was to make this and then iterate on it?" -- the excellence substitution
- "What if your job was to follow a process you enjoy that gets you to a reasonable result?" -- the process substitution

Each question lifts the person out of the perfection frame by handing them a different operating principle. The shift is felt, not just cognitive. The body relaxes when the perfection frame is set down. The work suddenly becomes possible.

The frame is also useful for diagnosing what kind of perfectionism is operating. A person who has not been able to start the work (see [[the-perfectionism-that-stops-you-from-starting]]) often responds best to the enjoyment substitution -- the entry door is reopened by making the task something that has a felt reward. A person who has been redoing the work endlessly often responds best to the iteration substitution -- the obsessive redoing was a denial that the work is iterative anyway. A person stuck in performance anxiety responds to the connection substitution -- the shift to connection with the audience pulls the attention off the self-monitoring loop.

## What This Is Not

It is not a permission to be sloppy. The four approaches all produce higher-quality work than perfectionism does. Connection produces products that fit the customer better. Excellence produces continuous improvement. Enjoyment produces sustained engagement. Process produces results that follow a sound method. The principle is not "stop caring." It is "care from a different operating principle than perfection."

It is not the claim that any approach is fine. The principle is that the four approaches Joe names (connection, excellence, enjoyment, process) all produce good work. Other operating principles do not necessarily work. Cynicism, indifference, fear, and shame are not on the list. The substitution is from perfection to something that holds the work seriously while being structurally non-self-defeating.

It is not a one-time move. The perfectionist habit is durable. The approach-substitution has to be made repeatedly, often inside the same task. The work is to notice when the perfection-frame has crept back in, and to consciously swap the operating principle again. Over time the substitution becomes the default mode.

## Joe's Coaching-Video Example

Joe applies this principle to his own work to demonstrate it. When making coaching videos -- a context where perfectionism would be easy to engage -- the organizing principles are explicit and few:

"For instance, like when I'm doing coaching videos, I'm focused on, is my heart open? Am I following them? Am I making sure I'm not trying to get them anywhere? If I do those three things, everything else works out. Everything else becomes automatic. And yes, I improve as a coach over time and all those little things improve, but because I am not trying to get it perfect and I'm focused on those three things, I actually get to iterate more, which actually gets me to higher and higher windows of tolerance over time." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The structure is operational. Three things, all connection-related. Not "every coaching video must be perfect." Just the three things. Everything else falls out. The iteration happens because the perfectionism is not present to prevent it. The compounding improvement happens because the iteration is allowed.

This is the principle running in plain view. The work being demonstrated is high-stakes (coaching is the core craft) and high-visibility (the videos are public), and the substitution still works. The size of the stakes is not what makes perfectionism the right operating principle. The size of the stakes is what makes approach-substitution most valuable.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, a little freeing. The frame is delivered as good news -- the person has been gripping the wrong operating principle, and there are at least four others that work better. The tone is not "you've been doing it wrong"; it is "there's a whole menu you haven't seen yet." The lightness allows the perfectionist's defensiveness around their standard to not engage.
