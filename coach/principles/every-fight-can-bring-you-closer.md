# Every Fight Can Bring You Closer

## The Principle

A fight in a relationship is not, in itself, a problem. Fighting that heals is the medium through which two people get closer, become more of themselves, and let the relationship become the developmental work. Fighting that retraumatizes is the medium through which the same fight keeps recurring with new content.

The distinguishing feature is not the volume, the intensity, or the topic. It is whether the fight produced something. After a fight that healed, both people can look back with pride. After a fight that retraumatized, both people look back and ask "what happened?"

"I can't wait to have a fight because every one is this opening and becoming more of ourselves. And it requires each person to learn how to fall into non-defensiveness quickly." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The frame is precise: every fight is an opening into more of yourself. This is the orientation of someone who has been through enough healing fights to trust the medium. It is not a starting orientation. It is a destination orientation, available after the fighting practice has produced enough healing that the fight itself becomes anticipated rather than dreaded.

## What Fighting Well Means

Fighting well is fighting in a way that produces three results:

1. **Every fight brings you closer.** Not closer despite the fight. Closer because of it. The fight is the medium of the deepening.
2. **Every fight brings you closer to yourself.** You learn something about your own patterns, your own trauma, your own holding back. The fight is a mirror.
3. **Every fight grows your capacity to be with each other.** The next time, the same content arises differently. You have more skill at meeting it.

"What is, is more loving, more yourself, more connected. And then the relationship itself becomes this growth opportunity. It becomes a spiritual path. It becomes a self-development path or whatever you want to call it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

When all three are happening, the relationship is functioning as developmental container. The fights are not in the way of the relationship; they are the work of the relationship.

## Diagnostic: Same Fight Over and Over

A fight that keeps recurring with no movement is the marker that the fighting is not healing. The content may shift; the underlying structure stays.

"If you are fighting over the same thing over and over again, then you're fighting isn't healing. Then you might fight over the same thing 3, 4, or 5 times as you're working it out. But every conversation is a little different." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The carve-out matters: working through one issue across multiple conversations is fine, even necessary, as long as each conversation is a little different. The pattern to notice is the literal repetition. The same fight, same positions, same conclusions, no learning, no shift. That is the diagnostic for fighting that is recycling rather than healing.

## Diagnostic: Can You Look Back With Pride?

The other diagnostic operates on a longer timescale. Two or three days after the fight, can you look back with pride at how you showed up?

"If you can't look back on that fight with pride, then you're cycling and you're retraumatizing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The wait time matters. Immediately after a fight, almost no one looks back with pride. The body is still flooded. The 2-3 day mark is when the nervous system has settled enough that the recollection is more reliable. If the recollection still produces shame, regret, or "that's not how I want it to be," the fight was retraumatizing.

## The Underlying Mechanism: Trauma Healing Through Loving Witness

What makes a fight healing rather than retraumatizing is the presence of one specific element: love showing up for the trauma as it surfaces.

When two people fight, they are both usually in their trauma -- the present partner becomes the original caregiver, the present complaint becomes the old wound. See [[i-cant-trust-my-thoughts-right-now]] for the felt sense of being in trauma. If the partner can stay loving while the trauma is firing, the trauma gets met with the love it could not access the first time. This is the healing.

"Oftentimes the way that we heal in the fights is by actually going into our trauma with somebody loving us. And that's the opportunity in almost every major fight. In almost every major fight, one of the biggest opportunities that you can have is somebody there loving you in your trauma, someone there giving you what you couldn't get when you first received that trauma. That's the opportunity. That's why so much fucking growth is possible." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This is why the fight, properly held, is so generative. The original wound is being met by the original missing love. The relationship becomes the place where what could not be healed in childhood gets healed now.

## Why This Is Not Quick

The orientation "I can't wait to fight" sounds extreme and is. It is not a starting point. It is the late-stage stance of two people who have had enough healing fights that they trust the medium. Most people start somewhere on the [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]] -- either fighting badly, not fighting at all, or fighting with techniques that have not yet been integrated. The "I can't wait to fight" orientation is the end-state, after the trauma has been seen as trauma, the partner has been seen as partner-not-caregiver, and the medium of the fight has been trusted enough times that anticipation replaces dread.

The principle is offered not as a should but as a description of what is available. It is what the practice produces, not what it requires going in.

## The Capacity the Practice Builds: Falling Into Non-Defensiveness Quickly

A specific capacity is named as the requirement that makes this orientation possible: the ability to move from defensiveness to non-defensiveness fast, mid-fight.

"It requires each person to learn how to fall into non-defensiveness quickly. How do I go from defensive to non-defensive really quickly? And when you've done it 2, 3, 4, 5 times, it's really easy because it's so fucking cool. It's so good when you can move from, 'No, that's not'— 'yeah, yeah, you're right. I see that.' Or, 'You're right. I see how you can see that. I see how that reality is true for you and mine is true for me. And I'm not saying yours is wrong or mine is right.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The move has a recognizable shape. The first part is "no, that's not" -- the defense. The second part is "yeah, you're right, I see that" -- the drop. The interval between the two contracts with practice. After enough repetitions, the interval becomes very short. The capacity is what allows the fight to convert from positional combat into mutual seeing.

The micro-move at the heart of this is the recognition that the partner's reality is true for them and your reality is true for you. See [[truth-is-perspective-with-context]] for the underlying principle. The non-defensiveness move is the practical application of the principle inside a live fight.

The "it's so fucking cool" tone marker matters. The move is not effortful once it has been done a few times. It produces pleasure -- the pleasure of the fight dissolving as it converts into contact. The pleasure is what makes the practice self-reinforcing. Once a partner has felt the move work, they start reaching for it sooner.

## What This Is Not

This is not a principle about tolerating abuse. Fights that bring you closer require the fighting to not be abusive in the first place. See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the boundary. Two people retraumatizing each other through unmoved trauma is not "fighting well that hasn't worked yet." It is a different category, and the principle does not apply to it.

This is not a principle that requires never being scared during the fight. Joe describes a period where he and Tara had a 6-month run of vicious fights that questioned everything for both of them. The principle held even then; the trust in the medium had to be re-earned across that period. See [[tara-fights-that-destroyed-joes-narcissism]] for the example.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Looking back on a recent fight with peace and seeing the growth in it
- Anticipating a difficult conversation with curiosity rather than dread
- Noticing that the same issue keeps coming up in a different way each time, with movement each round
- Finding that the relationship has gotten deeper after years of working through hard moments

## Tone

Joe says this with the easy conviction of someone who has been through it. The "I can't wait to have a fight" line is delivered with energy and humor. It is not a moral teaching. It is a description of what the practice produces, offered as encouragement rather than instruction.
