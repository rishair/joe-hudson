# The Empire-Rules Curve

## The Principle

The compliance an institution gets from its members is inversely related to the number of rules it has. At the beginning of an empire (or a company, or any institution) there are few rules and everyone obeys them. At the end of an empire there are many rules and nobody obeys them. The institutional lifecycle is the slow trade of compliance for rule-proliferation, and the proliferation is what produces the loss of compliance.

The principle is a structural observation about how rule-density and rule-compliance trade against each other over time. It applies to nation-states, to religious institutions, to corporations, to community organizations, to family systems. Wherever a system has had time to accumulate rules in response to specific perceived problems, the accumulation tends to exceed the system's compliance capacity, at which point the rules become symbolic rather than operational.

"There's this great thing that says -- I think this is true for companies, but it's also true for societies -- which is at the beginning of an empire, there's few rules and everybody obeys them. At the end of an empire, there's a ton of rules and nobody obeys them. And that's true with companies." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism Creates the Chaos it's trying to Prevent

## How the Curve Forms

The mechanism is local and intuitive at each step. One person gets hurt; a rule is made to prevent the risk. One person gets offended; a rule is made to prevent the situation. One project fails; a process is added to prevent recurrence. Each rule is a reasonable response to a specific event. The reasonableness of each step disguises the cumulative effect.

Over years and decades the rules accumulate without limit. The accumulation has several structural costs:

**The cognitive load exceeds compliance capacity.** No member of the institution can hold all the rules in mind. The members start operating on the rules they remember, which is a subset of the rules that exist. Selective compliance becomes the operating mode. The rules that are remembered are the ones that have been recently enforced or the ones that are easy to remember -- not necessarily the ones that matter most.

**Rules begin to contradict each other.** As the rule-count grows, internal contradictions become inevitable. The early rules and the later rules were made in different contexts and serve different purposes. Members find that following one rule requires violating another. The system has no clean answer for the contradictions, so members use judgment about which to follow -- which means the rules have been replaced by informal judgment, which is what the rules were supposed to prevent.

**The cost of compliance overwhelms the work.** Past a certain rule-density, simply following all the procedures takes most of the available time. The actual work the institution exists to do becomes secondary to the procedure of doing it. Members who are evaluated by output have to choose between getting the work done (which requires bypassing rules) and following the procedure (which prevents the work from being done). Most choose the work, which is another form of selective non-compliance.

**Rules become symbolic markers.** When most rules cannot be followed in practice, the rules that remain are followed selectively as markers of in-group identity rather than as operational constraints. Some members emphasize one set of rules; others emphasize another. The rule-system becomes a way of signaling allegiance rather than a way of coordinating behavior. The institution has the appearance of being rule-governed while actually being governed by informal coalitions.

The end-state Joe describes -- "a ton of rules and nobody obeys them" -- is what this curve produces over enough time. The institution still has all its rules. The rules still have official force. But the actual behavior of the members is determined by their own judgments, their informal networks, and their relationships with enforcement. The rules have become decorative.

## Why This Sits Inside the Perfectionism Teaching

Joe introduces this principle in the perfectionism conversation because the rule-accumulation curve is the institutional form of the perfectionism dynamic. The rules are added to prevent risk, to mitigate failure, to make sure the institution does not produce mistakes. The motive is perfectionist at the system level: make this institution perfect, prevent every failure, eliminate every risk.

The result mirrors the personal perfectionism case. The attempt to make the system perfect produces the breakdown of the system. The rules added to prevent risk produce the stagnation that becomes the real risk. The bureaucracy added to ensure quality produces the loss of quality. See [[bureaucracy-amplifies-perfectionism]] for the dedicated principle about how this loop scales.

"These more rules start getting populated as a way to stay safe, as a way to mitigate risk, as a way to make sure people don't get hurt, which seems like such a caring, compassionate thing to do. But it's aiming for perfectionism, right? And so what it actually does in a company, in a society, is it creates stagnation. And so there is a way that we are sacrificing the whole for the individual." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism Creates the Chaos it's trying to Prevent

The sacrifice-the-whole-for-the-individual frame is important. Each rule was made to prevent harm to a specific individual or a specific category of people. The cumulative cost falls on the whole institution. The institutional capacity erodes in service of preventing the next individual harm. Past a certain point the institution loses the capacity to serve any of its members well, including the ones the rules were meant to protect. The protection mechanism has consumed the thing being protected.

## The Application Across Domains

The principle applies at every scale:

**Nation-states.** The historical pattern Joe references — empires accumulate laws over generations until the rule-system is unsustainable. Late-stage empires are characterized by complex legal codes that members navigate through informal patronage and selective enforcement. The early-stage empires that succeed are typically characterized by simple, broadly-known rules that members actually follow.

**Companies.** As companies grow, the policies, procedures, and processes multiply. Mature companies often have manuals no one reads, training programs no one remembers, and rules everyone selectively ignores. The pattern often accelerates after a public failure or a high-profile lawsuit, each of which produces another wave of rule-additions.

**Religious institutions.** The same pattern operates in religious orders, congregations, and denominations over centuries. The simple practices of the founding period become the elaborate ritual codes of the late period, which are typically followed less rigorously than the simple practices were.

**Family systems.** Even at the family scale, the same dynamic can operate. Families that started with simple agreements accumulate rules in response to specific incidents. Past a certain point, the family operates with implicit "we ignore that rule" understandings that everyone shares but no one acknowledges.

The fractal nature of the pattern is part of what makes it useful to recognize. Once seen at one scale, it becomes visible at others.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not a recommendation to have no rules. Rules at the right density are functional and necessary. The principle is about the dynamic where rule-density exceeds compliance capacity, not about rule-density itself.

It is not a claim that all rule-additions are mistakes. Many specific rules were good additions at the time they were made. The aggregate effect is the issue. Each addition can be defensible in isolation while the accumulated weight produces the dysfunction described.

It is not a deterministic curve. The pattern can be interrupted. Some institutions periodically clear out rules that have become obsolete. Some leaders explicitly resist the rule-accumulation reflex. The principle describes the default trajectory; it does not require the trajectory.

It is not a romanticization of the beginning. The beginning has its own pathologies (lack of process, dependence on charismatic leadership, fragility to founder departure). The point is not that beginnings are better than ends. The point is that the trade between rule-density and compliance is real and that institutions usually go too far toward rule-density.

## How to Use This Principle in Leadership

For leaders trying to keep their organizations functional over time, the principle suggests two practices:

**Rule audits.** Periodically review the rule-system and remove rules that are no longer enforced, no longer applicable, or that have been replaced by informal practice. The audit itself is unusual enough to be valuable -- most organizations never remove rules, only add them.

**The two-question filter for new rules.** When a new rule is being proposed, ask: (1) will this actually be followed by everyone who is subject to it? (2) is the cost of compliance proportional to the harm being prevented? Many proposed rules fail one or both questions. Refusing those rules slows the accumulation curve.

The deeper move is to address the underlying dynamic. The rules are usually being proposed in response to fear -- of the next failure, of the next lawsuit, of the next bad press. If the leadership can hold the fear without converting it into more rules, the accumulation can be slowed. This connects to [[bureaucracy-amplifies-perfectionism]] -- the leadership's relationship with fear is structurally upstream of the organization's rule-density.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with a slight historical sweep. The reference to empires acknowledges that the pattern is old and well-documented. The personal application to companies makes it operationally useful in the present. The tone is closer to "this is how institutions work" than to "this is a problem we need to fix urgently."

The principle is often useful in leadership coaching as a way of giving a leader cover to do something they have been wanting to do anyway -- delete a policy, simplify a procedure, push back on the addition of another rule. The historical scale makes the move feel less arbitrary.

## When This Principle Applies

- A leader is wrestling with whether to add another rule, policy, or procedure
- An organization has reached the size at which the rule-accumulation pattern is becoming visible
- A team is reporting that they cannot navigate the existing rule-system
- A leader is asking how to preserve organizational vitality over time
- A founder is in transition and the company is at risk of becoming rule-heavy
- An institution is in late-stage decline and the leader is asking what is structurally driving the decline
