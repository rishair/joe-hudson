# Welcome the Dumpster Fire (Things Going Wrong Is the Best Material)

## The Principle

When something goes wrong in a facilitation -- a group falls apart, an exercise lands badly, a dynamic explodes, the planned arc collapses -- the facilitator's job is not to manage it back to working order. The facilitator's job is to be present with what is actually happening and trust that what is actually happening is the material. The "things going wrong" moment is often where the deepest learning is available, for everyone in the room and especially for the facilitator-in-training.

The principle held in a sentence: this dumpster fire is not going to stoke itself. The facilitator is not here to put out the fire. The facilitator is here to be present with the fire.

"I love it when shit goes haywire. So I don't love it when someone breaks a principle. Like if one of our folks started yelling at somebody or come back, get back to unconditional love. That's one thing. But if a dumpster fire starts, that's fucking great." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## Why This Is Counter-Intuitive

The standard relationship to a facilitation going wrong: anxiety, scramble to restore order, repair the agenda, smooth over the conflict, get the room back on track. The facilitator's nervous system reads "things falling apart" as a threat to their competence and to the safety of the room. The reflex is to manage.

This principle inverts that reflex. The "things falling apart" is not a threat to the work; it is the work. The chaos is what the room actually has to work with. The breakdown reveals the underlying material that the orderly version of the facilitation was just sitting on top of. The facilitator who scrambles to restore order is suppressing the very thing the work is for.

This connects to [[flow-vs-managing-reality]]: the move is not to manage what is happening but to flow with what is happening. The dumpster fire is what is happening. Flow goes through it, not around it.

## What the Principle Does Not Mean

The principle is not "anything goes." It is not "let people harm each other." It is not "abandon the facilitation." Joe is explicit about the boundary: principles still hold. If someone is violating the orientation -- yelling at a participant, breaking a safety container -- the facilitator intervenes. The dumpster fire that is welcomed is the dumpster fire of the work surfacing what is hard, not the dumpster fire of someone causing harm.

"I don't love it when someone breaks a principle. Like if one of our folks started yelling at somebody or come back, get back to unconditional love. That's one thing. But if a dumpster fire starts, that's fucking great." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The distinction: a principle-violation is intervened on. A dynamic-explosion is welcomed. The first threatens the container; the second is the container doing its job.

## The Mechanism: Things Going Wrong Reveals the Unconscious

The orderly version of a facilitation is what people are putting on top of themselves. The breakdown is what is underneath. When the breakdown happens, the underneath becomes available. The facilitator who can be with the breakdown gives the room permission to be with their own underneath. The facilitator who scrambles to restore order is, in effect, putting the lid back on the underneath.

The breakdown is also where the facilitator's own work shows up. If they have an authority issue, the breakdown that challenges their authority will surface it. If they have a control issue, the breakdown that requires letting go of control will surface it. The breakdown is the curriculum for the facilitator too, not just for the participants.

## The Example: Three Groups, One Going South

A specific example from the transcript. Three groups running in parallel, two functional, one going completely south. The plan: all three move to the next step together. The functional groups are ready; the dysfunctional group is not. What to do?

The move: take the dysfunctional group, put them last, have them watch the functional groups process first. The dysfunctional group sat in the middle, watched the functional groups work. The first read on their faces was "is this a fucking joke." The second read was "oh shit, this is possible." By the time it was their turn, the dysfunction had largely dissolved -- they had seen the proof that the work could work, and they fell into it.

The breakthrough came specifically because the dysfunctional group went south. Without the breakdown, the moment would not have existed. The "most beautiful moments" in Joe's facilitation history have all come from things going wrong.

"That only came -- that beautiful moment only came because shit went south. And so the most beautiful moments I've ever had facilitating are when things have gone south. The most beautiful." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## What the Principle Asks of the Facilitator

The principle requires:

1. **Release of the result orientation.** The facilitator is not here to make the facilitation succeed by some external measure. They are here to be present with what is happening. See [[process-over-result]].

2. **Capacity to be unsteadied.** The facilitator has to be able to sit with their own dysregulation when the room goes sideways. If they need the room to be steady in order to be steady themselves, they cannot welcome the dumpster fire.

3. **Trust that what is happening is material.** This is operationally close to [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] applied to the group level: the room knows what it is doing, even when what it is doing looks chaotic from outside.

4. **The willingness to look bad.** A facilitator whose identity is tied to running smooth sessions cannot welcome dumpster fires. The fire makes them look like a bad facilitator (by conventional measures). The fire is the work (by this principle's measure). The conflict is between the identity and the work.

5. **Ongoing practice with the facilitator's own breakdown experiences.** A facilitator who has not metabolized their own dumpster fires is structurally less able to welcome them in others.

## What the Principle Produces

When the facilitator can welcome the breakdown rather than manage it:

- The participants get the experience of having their actual material met, not their managed presentation met
- The room develops genuine trust because the facilitator has demonstrated they will not abandon the difficulty
- The participants learn, through example, to be with breakdowns rather than to suppress them
- The facilitator's own development continues because they are taking on what the breakdowns surface in them
- The trainee-facilitators in the room learn the most about facilitation precisely when things are hardest, because the easy moments do not require the work that the hard moments require

"I would be very happy if 5 people went out to facilitate something and they're just like, that went horribly wrong. I would be like, great. Maybe not great for the students. That would make me sad, but I know I will have much better facilitators on the other end of that." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The cost-honesty matters: it is not great for the students who experience the failed facilitation. The principle is not "celebrate failure." It is "trust that failure is material, including for the next generation of facilitators."

## The Trainee Application

For a trainee facilitator, the operational principle: when your facilitation goes wrong, that is the curriculum, not a failure of curriculum. Do not retreat from facilitation because it went wrong. Take what went wrong as the material you now get to work with. The trainee who has had three or four dumpster fires and metabolized them has more capacity than the trainee whose first ten facilitations went smoothly.

This connects to [[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]]: if your work right now is to learn to be with breakdown, then a smooth facilitation is not what you need next. A breakdown is.

## The Underlying Posture

The deeper posture under this principle: the facilitator is not in charge of the outcome. The facilitator is in charge of being present. The presence is the offering. The outcome is what the room does with the presence. A facilitator who needs a particular outcome to feel competent has not yet released the result-orientation. A facilitator who can be present regardless of outcome has.

"This dumpster fire is not going to stoke itself. Really reminds me that I'm not here to make it work. I'm here to be present with what the hell is happening. And I'm here to not look for a result as much as look for how am I going to be in this moment with these people. And it reminds me of that. And the more I'm reminded of that, the more I let go of the results, the better the results typically are." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The paradox: the release of the result-orientation produces better results than the pursuit of results. Following the river goes faster than rowing against it. The pursuit is fighting the room. The release is moving with the room. The room knows what it needs more than the facilitator's pre-planned agenda does.

## In Coaching (Not Just Facilitation)

The same principle applies in one-on-one coaching, though it is less dramatic. If the client argues, the coach can welcome the argument. If the client cries unexpectedly, the coach can be with the crying. If the session goes nowhere the coach expected, the coach can trust that where it went was where it needed to go. The coach is not in charge of the result. The coach is in charge of presence.

"If someone argues with me in a coaching session, I'm like, oh, cool. Someone cries, cool. So whatever, there's no result that I'm looking for. I'm just like, what's happening? How can I be with you?" -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## Tone

Plain, slightly amused. Joe describes this with light delight rather than gravity. The dumpster-fire language itself carries the tone -- not technical, not pious. The principle is held cheerfully because the deeper trust (that what is happening is the material) is itself a relaxed orientation rather than a strained one.
