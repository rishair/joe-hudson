# Find a Healthy Replacement (Every Behavior Is Giving You Something)

## The Principle

Every behavior you cannot stop is meeting a need. The not-working-out is meeting a need. The video games are meeting a need. The coffee, the cigarette, the scroll, the late-night snack — all of them are giving you something. The reason willpower attempts fail is that they try to subtract the behavior without giving the underlying need anywhere to go. The need does not go away. It finds another vehicle, usually a worse one.

The reliable move is to look honestly at what the behavior is giving you, then find something else that gives you the same thing but in a form that does not have the bad side effects. The principle reframes behavior change from subtraction to substitution.

"Everything that you're doing satisfies you in some way. Not working out satisfies you in some way. Drinking coffee satisfies you in some way. Playing video games satisfies you in some way. It's giving you something." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Disciplined Without Having Discipline

## The Underlying Function, Not the Surface Behavior

The first move is honesty about what the behavior is actually providing. The honesty often surprises the person, because the behavior they want to stop has a layer of denial around its function. Once the function is named, the substitution becomes findable.

"Maybe the video games is giving you a distraction from a deeper pain. Maybe drinking coffee gives you energy. Maybe not working out makes it so that you don't beat yourself up for not working out enough when you go to the gym. There's always something that what you're doing is giving to you." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Disciplined Without Having Discipline

Note the third example. "Not working out makes it so that you don't beat yourself up for not working out enough." This is the kind of nested function the principle exposes. The not-doing-the-thing is itself protecting the person from a worse version of the shame they would feel if they did the thing imperfectly. Until that function is seen, the not-doing cannot be changed by force.

The diagnostic question: **What is this behavior actually giving me?** Not the surface answer ("I just like it"). The honest answer ("it lets me forget the day," "it gives me a break from being a person who tries," "it lets me feel something other than emptiness for an hour").

## The Substitute Has to Give the Same Thing

The substitute is not just any healthier behavior. It has to provide the same function as the original. If the cigarette is giving you a five-minute break from the office, the substitute has to provide a five-minute break from the office, not just be a vegetable. If the video games are giving you a way to forget your problems for a while, the substitute has to give you a way to forget your problems for a while, not just be on the "good" side of the ledger.

"The idea behind find a replacement is to look at what it's giving you honestly, really take a look at it. Does a cigarette give you like a little relief, a break? Does the video game allow you to just forget about your problems for a while? Whatever it is, and then find a replacement that you can do that gives you the same thing, but in a more healthy way." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Disciplined Without Having Discipline

Joe's example of replacing video games:

"If you ride horses, you're also going to not be able to think about anything else when you're on the horse. So that could be like a video game replacement or golf or pickleball, any kind of exercise that is competitive. Look for something else that you can do that gives you that same break." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Disciplined Without Having Discipline

The match is on the function: total absorption that displaces the day's problems. Riding a horse and playing pickleball both deliver that. The form of the replacement does not matter; the match to function does.

## The Substitute Has to Be Enjoyable

A critical specification. The replacement only works if you actually enjoy it. A replacement that requires willpower to choose is not a replacement; it is the original problem in different clothes.

"And when you find that thing, make sure it's something that you enjoy doing. So it doesn't take any discipline to do. Nobody says, 'Oh my God, I gotta be disciplined. I really have to play more video games,' right? Like, 'I gotta play video games harder and more because I need more discipline.' The things that are enjoyable to do, you can do and they can give you the same thing and they can be good for you. And that's the trick, is to find those enjoyable replacements." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Disciplined Without Having Discipline

The joke ("I gotta play video games harder, I need more discipline") makes the point. Nobody needs discipline to do the thing they enjoy. The discipline framing only appears around the thing that is being should-ed. If the replacement is genuinely enjoyable, no discipline is required to choose it.

This means the search for a replacement is not a moral search. It is a search for what you actually like that happens to also give you the function. The "should" filter — the replacement that you think you should enjoy — does not produce a viable substitute. The actual-enjoyment filter does.

## Why Subtraction Without Substitution Fails

Trying to remove a behavior without replacing its function leaves a hole. The need that the original behavior was meeting is still there. It either reasserts the original behavior (relapse) or finds a different equally bad vehicle (substitution into another bad habit).

This is why people who quit smoking gain weight. The cigarette was meeting a function (oral fixation, breaks, anxiety regulation). Removing the cigarette without giving those functions a new home produces a food-based replacement that the person did not consciously choose. The replacement happens whether you plan it or not. The principle is: plan it.

It is also why "I am going to stop playing video games" fails predictably and "I am going to take up pickleball" succeeds where the first one failed. The first only subtracts. The second substitutes. The need that was being met has a new home before the old home is removed.

## Where This Fits With the Other Discipline Moves

Three structural moves for behavior change form a set. Each addresses a different layer of why the unwanted behavior persists:

1. [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]] — feel the actual pain of the behavior so the body stops wanting it.
2. [[avoid-the-decision-by-design]] — remove the decision from the environment so willpower is not the bottleneck.
3. **This principle** — give the underlying need a healthy vehicle that you actually enjoy.

These layer. Sometimes one is sufficient. For deeply entrenched behaviors, all three move together. The full-feeling reduces the pull. The environmental design reduces the encounters. The healthy replacement gives the function a new home.

## What This Connects To

This principle has structural overlap with [[joes-pot-habit]], where the underlying emotional need (sadness, anger that Joe was avoiding) was the function the pot was meeting. Once Joe addressed the underlying emotion directly, the pot lost its function and dissolved without willpower. In that case, the "replacement" was the felt emotion itself — meeting the need directly rather than via a substitute.

This is the upstream version of the same principle. Sometimes the deepest replacement is to feel the unfelt emotion the behavior was avoiding. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. That is the most upstream form of replacement: not finding a different vehicle for the avoidance, but no longer needing the avoidance.

The principle also connects to [[enjoyment-as-path]]. The enjoyable replacement works because it joins the deepest current — the pull of what you actually want — to the function the bad habit had been meeting. The path of behavior change runs through enjoyment, not through force.

## Where This Differs From "Just Pick Up a Hobby"

Surface advice often says: just find a healthier hobby. That advice is incomplete because it does not require that the hobby match the original behavior's function. A person who replaces nighttime drinking with running may find the running does not stick, because running does not provide what the drinking provided. The principle is more specific: the replacement must give the same function. Match function, not just "healthiness."

## Tone

Practical and slightly amused. Joe delivers this as observation, not prescription. The honesty about what the bad habit is giving you is gentle, not exposing. The person is not being caught at a moral failure; they are being shown that the behavior was working for something, and now there is a way to give that something a better home.

The joke about needing discipline to play more video games carries the relief of the principle. The disciplined people are not disciplined. They have built lives full of things they enjoy that also happen to be good for them. There is no inner force happening. There is just a good match between what they want and what their lives are made of.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents [[i-cant-be-disciplined]] and the behavior is one they have tried to stop directly without success.
- When [[avoid-the-decision-by-design]] has been applied and the pull is still strong because the underlying need has nowhere to go.
- When [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]] has reduced the appeal of the behavior but the function it was meeting is still calling for something.
- When the person describes a behavior they keep returning to even after multiple attempts to quit. The return is the diagnostic that the function is still active.
- When relapse keeps producing the same behavior or jumping to a closely related one. The function is moving from vehicle to vehicle.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] when the function the behavior is meeting is the avoidance of a specific feeling. In that case the deepest substitute is the felt emotion itself.
- Toward [[joes-pot-habit]] as the worked example of the underlying-emotion route.
- Toward [[enjoyment-as-path]] as the broader orientation. The replacement principle is enjoyment applied to behavior change.
- Back to the full set of discipline moves if the substitution alone is not enough: layer it with environmental design and felt-pain practice.
