# People Want to Buy the Bridge — They Will Follow Anyone Who Seems Assured

## The Principle

A structural feature of human social organization: we want to feel safe, and one of the most reliable ways we know to feel safe is to find someone who seems to know what is going on and follow them. The someone does not have to be right. They have to be assured. The signal of assuredness — internal conviction, lack of self-consciousness, the felt sense of "this person believes what they are saying" — is what triggers the followership.

This explains why we keep falling for the bridge-sellers, the snake-oil salesmen, the polarizing demagogues. We are not, in those moments, evaluating the content of what they are saying. We are evaluating the signal of their conviction, which feels like the signal of safety, which is what we are reaching for.

"We all really want to buy the bridge until we don't. So there's something natural in us in the evolutionary path to have somebody who has the answer, has somebody who feels assured, who feels conviction. And we want that. If you look at, like, whether it's our rock stars or our politicians, it's like there's something in us that wants to feel safe. And if we follow somebody who looks like they're assured, and, and in their minds they are, I mean, empathetically, we — they are convicted." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The phrase "buy the bridge" is the operative compression — referring to the con of selling someone the Brooklyn Bridge they do not actually own. We keep falling for it. The principle says: of course we do. The wanting is structural.

## The "Slightly Ahead" Pattern

A specific form the followership takes: we are most followable by people who appear to be just slightly ahead of where we are. Not impossibly far ahead. Just enough ahead that following them is conceivable.

"My daughters are 3 and a half years apart. What I notice is that's who kids adore, is like they listen to other kids that are just slightly older than them more than — my whole life I have been following, reluctantly questioning, the authorities that are just have a little more understanding than I have at this moment, and I'm following that. So I think that there's a natural — one part is I think there's a natural peace to that." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The mechanism: a person five years ahead is relatable. A person fifty years ahead is foreign. We can imagine becoming the person five years ahead. We cannot imagine becoming the person fifty years ahead. The relatable version is the one who pulls us forward; the foreign version is admired from a distance but does not produce movement.

This is also why developmental teachers tend to be most powerful for the stage just beneath their own and lose traction with audiences much further behind or further ahead. The teacher has to be visible as "the next step" for the audience to follow them. Too far ahead, the audience cannot orient. Too close, no movement is offered.

## Why the Conviction Does Not Have to Be Real

A surprising and slightly dark feature: the conviction the audience reads does not have to be original conviction. A person who says something they are not sure of often becomes sure of it the third time they say it. The repetition produces the conviction. The conviction produces the followers. The followers produce the reinforcement that further deepens the conviction. The whole loop runs on the appearance of certainty rather than on the underlying truth.

"And, and if they don't, the third time they say it, they will." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

This is the operating mechanism of much of political demagoguery, religious fervor, and the social formation of cults. The figure at the center may have started with very little internal conviction. The performance of conviction, repeated, becomes felt conviction. The felt conviction is what the followers respond to. The followers' response is what makes the original figure now actually convinced. The cycle is self-reinforcing.

## What This Costs the Follower

The reason the principle is dangerous, not just descriptive, is what the followership exchanges. When we follow an assured person, we are not just receiving information from them. We are transferring responsibility to them. The relief we feel is the relief of not having to figure it out ourselves.

"So, so there's like this part of us that it's like, 'oh, cool, there's some conviction that I now I don't have to actually take responsibility for myself.'" -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The transfer is structurally the same as the structure of dependency. The follower outsources their own discernment, their own conviction, their own sense of direction. In exchange, they get the relief of having a leader. The exchange feels good, especially in moments of uncertainty (see [[how-do-i-navigate-this-uncertain-time]]). It is also what makes large groups of people followable into terrible directions by anyone who delivers the convincing signal.

## The Connection to the Charisma Conversation

The principle sits next to [[less-self-conscious-wins]] and explains why that principle operates as reliably as it does. The presidential-election diagnostic (the candidate with less self-consciousness wins, almost regardless of articulation or intelligence) is the macro version of this principle. The voters are not evaluating policy content; they are reading the signal of assuredness. The candidate with less self-consciousness emits a stronger signal. The signal triggers the followership.

The Andrew Tate and Donald Trump cases are the polarizing version. Both deliver the assuredness signal at unusually high volume. Both are charismatic to their audiences in part because of the assuredness alone, regardless of the content. The audiences that follow them are the audiences for whom the assuredness was missing in their lives. See [[charisma-is-dyadic]] for the structural reason the audience matters.

The principle also explains why the opposition to such figures often does not work. Attacking the content of what the figure says does not address what the followers are responding to. The followers are not responding to the content. They are responding to the signal. The signal cannot be attacked by attacking the content. The signal can only be matched by another signal of equal or greater assuredness, which the attackers usually do not have because they are operating in critical-rebutting mode rather than convicted-delivering mode.

## The Healthy Use of the Principle

The principle has a positive direction. Joe immediately moves to it after naming the dynamic.

"But to me, the more productive question is when you're faced with somebody like that, what's how can they be an avenue to your freedom, to your unwinding, to your, and can you see their point of view? Can you understand the value that they provide? Can you not see their point? Can you see outside of their point of view? Can you not see that can you use them as a way to dissolve your ego?" -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The healthy use: rather than fighting the figure or condemning the followers, use the encounter as material for one's own work. What triggers about this figure? What does the trigger reveal about what is alive in you? Can you see their point of view (which is required to dissolve the projection)? Can you see past their point of view (which is required to retain your own discernment)?

The principle, used this way, becomes a doorway out of the followership pattern itself. The person who can do this work does not need an assured leader. They have become their own assured-enough authority. They can engage with leaders and movements without being absorbed by them, because their own discernment is intact.

## The Self-Application

The principle also applies to one's relationship with oneself. We follow assured voices internally just as we follow them externally. The internal critic that "knows" what we should do is followed for the same reason — it sounds assured. The conviction in the critical voice is what gives it authority over the rest of the psyche. The work of [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]] is in part the work of recognizing this dynamic and refusing the followership.

The same applies to teachers, frameworks, and inherited beliefs. The assuredness of the source is what made us follow. The followership remains operative even after the original source is gone, because the conviction we absorbed continues to feel like the truth. See [[every-epiphany-is-a-rut-waiting-to-happen]] for the related principle about settled convictions becoming the next thing to outgrow.

## Joe's Own Refusal to Be the Assured Leader

The conversation contains a small but important moment in which Joe demonstrates the counter-stance to the principle. He explicitly refuses to be a guru in the standard sense — partly because of the dynamic this principle describes.

"I'm just like a guru. I'm just the next iteration where I want all the power to be in your hands. Not in mine." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The move is a deliberate refusal of the followership relationship. The standard guru accepts the projection, runs on it, becomes increasingly assured (in the sense the principle describes), and the followers increasingly outsource their authority. Joe's stance is to receive the projection while immediately returning the power. The conviction he holds is not "follow me." It is "find your own conviction."

This is also why his methodology has the explicit anti-attachment to his own frameworks (see [[every-epiphany-is-a-rut-waiting-to-happen]] and [[dont-take-my-word-for-it]]). The frameworks could become the bridge people are buying. The constant refusal to position them as the answer is the practice of not letting that dynamic establish.

## The Coach's Use of This Principle

When a person describes following a leader, a teacher, a movement, or a public figure with what feels like devotion, the principle gives the coach a diagnostic. The question is not whether the leader is good or bad. The question is what is the person reaching for through the followership. Often: certainty, relief from responsibility, belonging, a felt sense of safety. Each of these can be approached directly without the intermediary of the followed figure.

The coaching move is not to argue against the figure. The coaching move is to surface what the followership is doing for the person, name what the underlying need is, and explore whether that need can be met more directly. Sometimes the followership is healthy enough to remain. Sometimes it dissolves once the underlying need is seen and met another way. Either is fine; the figure becomes optional rather than load-bearing.

The same principle applies when the coach is the one being followed. The coach who notices a coachee installing them as the assured authority should not accept the installation. The installation will reproduce the very pattern the coaching is trying to dissolve. The clean coach returns the power immediately, repeatedly, structurally, until the coachee can hold their own conviction without needing the coach as the source.
