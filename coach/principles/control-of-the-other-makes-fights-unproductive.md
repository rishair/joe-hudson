# Controlling the Other Person Makes Fights Unproductive

## The Principle

When you are judging your partner from a position of superiority during a fight, what you are actually doing is trying to control them. The judgment is the cognitive method by which the control is being attempted. The reason you are trying to control them is that there is an emotion you are not feeling, and controlling them is the strategy for not having to feel it.

This makes the fight structurally unproductive. Nobody signs up to be controlled. Nobody offers themselves as a body to be remade into something easier for you to be in a room with. The partner's resistance to being controlled is not a defect in their cooperation; it is the natural response of any organism to being managed against its will.

"Controlling somebody else doesn't make fights productive. Whenever we're judging anybody, including ourselves, it's because we don't want to feel a certain emotion. And so that makes the fight detrimental because basically we're trying to control the other person so that we don't have to feel a certain emotion." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

## The Causal Chain

The mechanism runs in a precise sequence:

1. **There is an emotion you do not want to feel** — usually shame, vulnerability, fear, or a felt sense of not-enough. See [[superiority-and-shame-erode-together]].
2. **You produce a judgment of the partner** as the method of not feeling the emotion. Judgment lifts you above the emotion by lifting you above the partner. See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] and [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]].
3. **The judgment is structurally a control move.** You are now trying to get the partner to be different (less emotional, more reasonable, less wrong, more compliant) so that the conditions producing the emotion you do not want to feel go away.
4. **The partner registers the control attempt** and resists, withdraws, escalates, or counter-controls. None of these are paths to a productive fight.
5. **The fight calcifies** around the control-and-resistance dynamic. The original emotion stays unfelt. The fight stays unresolved. The pattern repeats next time.

The kill point in the chain is step 2: the judgment. Stop the judgment and the chain does not assemble. The way to stop the judgment is to feel the emotion the judgment was avoiding. See [[what-am-i-not-feeling-right-now]] for the in-the-moment self-check.

## Why "Nobody Signs Up" Is the Structural Argument

The "nobody signs up to be controlled" point is not a complaint about your partner's stubbornness. It is a structural claim about consent.

"Nobody wants to be controlled. Nobody's like, 'hey, could you control me, in a way that I'm not okay with?'" -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

The qualification matters. Control in a relationship can be consensual. Joe gestures at the BDSM exception: there are contexts where one partner explicitly invites a kind of control from the other, and that control is the play. The principle does not address those cases.

"There's obviously, there's a whole bunch of BDSM stuff that says, 'hey, yeah, control is.' But we don't want to be controlled in a way that we're actually going against our will. We want to have maybe some play there, but it's not something that anybody signs up for." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

The principle addresses the non-consensual control: the control that is being deployed inside a fight without the partner having agreed to be the object of it. That control will be resisted because being controlled against one's will is something organisms reject by nature. The reception is structural, not stylistic. There is no version of "I just need to control you more skillfully" that gets around it.

## Why This Specifically Makes the Fight Unproductive

A productive fight is one that produces something for both partners. Closeness, mutual seeing, a new solution, a healed wound. See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] for the framing.

The control-aimed fight cannot produce these because the control move splits the field. One partner is positioned as the controller; the other is positioned as the object to be controlled. The "productive" outcome the controller has in mind is the object being remade into the desired shape. The "productive" outcome the object has in mind is no longer being treated as an object. The two productivities are not compatible. Neither can be reached while the control-and-resistance dynamic is operating.

The fight then settles into the holding pattern of mutual non-yielding. The controller refuses to drop the control (because dropping it would require feeling the avoided emotion). The object refuses to be controlled (because submitting would require abandoning their own integrity). The fight loops, exhausts both partners, and resolves the same way the previous fight resolved.

## What "Productive" Actually Requires

The fight becomes productive when both partners exit the control-and-resistance dynamic. The exit is asymmetric: one partner moves first. The move is to feel the emotion that was being avoided, which dissolves the need for the judgment, which dissolves the control attempt.

"By feeling that emotion, that's how you make the fight really productive." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

Once one partner has dropped out of the dynamic, the other partner's resistance loses its target. The fight stops being two opposed positions and becomes the open field where the actual material of the fight can be seen. The solution that was invisible from the controlling position becomes visible from the position of having felt the avoided emotion.

"That's where the productivity is going to be. That's where you're both going to be able to find a solution that you couldn't see before. And without that, you're never going to get there." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

The strong form of the claim: there is no other path to the solution. The controlling position cannot find it because the controlling position is the structural obstacle to it. Stopping the controlling is not a soft niceness move; it is the operational prerequisite for the fight finding what it is reaching for.

## The Relationship to Verbal Abuse

This principle and [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] share the same structural diagnosis: controlling the partner makes the fight harmful. The difference is the energy. Verbal abuse is control attempted through the threat of anger; the judgment-as-control described here is control attempted through the cognitive position of superiority. Both are control moves. Both produce the same non-cooperative response in the partner. Both kill the fight's productivity.

The judgment-as-control form is the more socially-acceptable version. It does not get named as abuse because it does not raise its voice. It can be performed by someone who considers themselves above abuse. But the structural dynamic is the same: the partner is being treated as the object to be remade rather than as the person to be encountered. The principle here makes that visible without needing the abuse framing.

## What This Looks Like in Coaching

When a client describes a recurring fight in which they are in the position of the "rational one" trying to get their partner to be more reasonable, the move is not to validate the rationality. The move is to name what is structurally happening.

A typical sequence:

1. The client describes the fight as the partner being too emotional, irrational, out of control.
2. The coach offers the structural reframe: when you are judging your partner during a fight, you are trying to control them so you do not have to feel something. The judgment is the cognitive method; the control is the motive; the avoided emotion is the cause.
3. The client may resist this framing initially. The reframe puts the responsibility for the unproductive fight on the rational partner, not the emotional one. This inverts the surface narrative the client has been operating with.
4. The coach asks: "what is the emotion you are not feeling when you are in that judgment of them?" See [[what-am-i-not-feeling-right-now]] and [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]].
5. The emotion surfaces. Often shame, often fear of being equal, often grief about how the relationship is being lost in the same motion as the fight being won.
6. The coach lets the emotion be felt briefly. The reorganization happens in the body, not in the conversation.

The client now has a different relationship to their next fight. The next time the superiority shows up, it can be recognized as the control move it is, and the emotion underneath it can be reached for instead.

## What This Is Not

This is not a principle that fighting is wrong. See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]]. Fighting in the right structure is the medium of relational growth.

This is not a principle that the emotional partner is the right one. See [[intellectual-emotional-polarization-in-couples]] for the more complete reading: both partners are usually inside the polarization, both are running their own version of the same avoidance, the structure asks for integration on both sides. This principle addresses one specific failure mode (the controlling rationality) but does not let the emotional partner off the hook for their own integration work.

This is not a principle that all attempts to influence the partner are control. There is a difference between asking the partner to consider something and trying to remake them so that you do not have to feel something. The principle addresses the second.

## Boundary Conditions

- The principle applies in the context of an intimate relationship fight, not to all conflict everywhere. Some conflicts (with strangers, with adversaries, in negotiations) operate by different dynamics.
- The principle does not require the absence of all judgment in fights. Judgment that arises and is felt as data about the self (what is being triggered, what emotion is being avoided) is different from judgment that is being deployed as a control move on the partner. The first is workable; the second is the failure mode.
- The principle does not say that the controlling partner is morally worse than the partner being controlled. Both are running structures they did not invent. The principle is descriptive of the dynamic, not prescriptive of blame.

## Tone

Direct, structural, slightly dry. The "nobody signs up to be controlled" framing carries a small dark humor — the controlling partner has been working hard at something that was structurally impossible to succeed at. The naming is delivered without moralizing. The principle is offered as a structural explanation for why the fight has not been working, not as a condemnation of the controller.
