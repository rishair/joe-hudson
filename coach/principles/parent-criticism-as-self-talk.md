# The Parent's Criticism Was the Parent Talking to Themselves

## The Principle

When a parent chronically criticizes a child for some trait — "don't be so sensitive," "stop being weak," "why can't you just get over it" — the criticism is usually not directed at the child at all. The parent is talking to themselves. The trait being criticized in the child is the trait the parent could not bear in themselves and never managed to feel. The child becomes the visible surface on which the parent's self-rejection plays out.

The child experiences it as criticism directed at them. The structural truth is that the parent was managing their own internal voice and used the child as the available object.

"So in other words, when she was saying, 'You're the sensitive one, don't be so sensitive,' she was talking to herself the whole time?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

The realization, when it lands, reframes the entire history of the criticism. It was not about the child. It was the parent's own unfelt voice coming out at the available target.

## How the Mechanism Works

The parent has some trait they have been taught to reject in themselves — sensitivity, emotionality, fear, neediness, anger, weakness. They cannot feel the trait directly because it has been disallowed since their own childhood. The trait still operates in the parent, but it has to be managed without being acknowledged.

When the child displays the same trait — often because the child has it natively, or because the child has picked it up from the parent — the parent's internal voice activates. The voice that the parent uses on themselves about the trait gets externalized at the child. The criticism the parent directs at the child is the criticism the parent has been silently directing at themselves for years.

This is why the criticism is often disproportionate to the child's actual behavior. The parent is not responding to the trait the child is showing. The parent is responding to the trait in themselves, with the child standing in as the visible target.

This is also why the criticism tends to repeat in the same shape, regardless of what the child is actually doing. The criticism is the parent's looped self-talk. The child's behavior is incidental.

## What the Child Inherits

When this mechanism runs over years, the child develops the same internal voice. They do not just hear "don't be sensitive" from outside; they internalize the voice and direct it at themselves. The parent's self-rejection becomes the child's self-rejection.

This is the standard pathway by which inner-critic voices get installed. See [[apply-view-to-the-voice]] for the work on these voices. The voice the adult child hears in their head — telling them to be less sensitive, less needy, less emotional, less themselves — is often the literal voice of the parent. And the voice the parent had was often the literal voice of the grandparent.

The recognition that the parent was talking to themselves the whole time does not erase the inherited voice. It does change the relationship to it. The voice can be recognized as borrowed, not as truth. The criticism can be heard as the echo of the parent's own self-rejection, not as accurate diagnosis of the child.

## The Specific Variant: "You're the Strong One"

A common shape: the parent who criticized the child for being sensitive simultaneously praised the child for being "the strong one." The two seemingly opposite messages come from the same root. The parent could not feel their own fear; the child, who was actually scared like hell, learned to hide the fear and was rewarded for the hiding. The "strong one" praise was the parent's relief at not having to witness fear they could not bear to feel in themselves.

"I just saw the fear in her all the time. So she always called me, you're the strong one, because, you know, I didn't show any fear even though I was scared like hell." -- Client, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

The child learns: do not show the fear. Earn the love by carrying what the parent cannot. This is a precursor to chronic caretaking. See [[i-caretake-people]].

The recognition that the "strong one" praise was the parent's fear-management does the same work as the recognition that the "sensitive one" criticism was the parent's self-talk. Both messages were about the parent, not about the child.

## How Joe Surfaces This

The principle usually surfaces after the client has done some work seeing the parent's underlying state. In the source session, the client had just recognized — from inside the role-play in [[coach-demonstrates-via-reversed-role-play]] — that her mother's guilt was unexpressed fear. Joe extended the recognition:

"So in other words, when she was saying, 'You're the sensitive one, don't be so sensitive,' she was talking to herself the whole time?"

The client confirmed: "Yeah, yeah, for sure."

The question form matters. Joe does not declare the principle as a generalization. He asks the client to test it against their own data. "She was talking to herself the whole time?" The recognition has to come from the client's own felt sense of the parent, not from Joe's framework.

Once the recognition lands, the principle does not need elaborating. The client carries it forward. The parent's voice in the head can now be recognized for what it always was: the parent's own self-talk that the child happened to be the audience for.

## Why This Matters for the Coaching Work

Without this recognition, the inner-critic work tends to stay stuck in arguing with the voice. The voice says "you're too sensitive," the person tries to argue "no I'm not," or counter-suggest "it's good to be sensitive," and the voice continues. The voice continues because the voice is not actually about the person; arguing with the voice is arguing with a tape that is playing for reasons that have nothing to do with the person.

With the recognition, the voice can be heard as a borrowed tape. The work shifts from arguing with the voice to having compassion for the parent who lived with the voice all their life, and from there to releasing the inherited installation. The voice can soften because it has been correctly identified as not-mine.

This is also part of why the parent often produces such heat in the adult child's inner-critic work. The voice has the parent's tone, the parent's specific phrasing, the parent's emotional charge. Recognizing the source allows the voice to be heard for what it is rather than mistaken for the person's own assessment.

## The Empathy That Becomes Available

When the criticism is recognized as the parent's self-talk, an unexpected empathy often arrives. The parent was living with that voice. The parent had to manage that voice without ever being met in it. The criticism the child experienced was a small visible portion of what the parent was doing to themselves continuously, silently, for their entire life.

This empathy is not a forced compassion. It arrives because the parent suddenly becomes a person who was struggling, rather than just a person who hurt the child. The parent is not absolved; the harm is not erased. The frame just enlarges to include the parent's interior.

In the source session, this empathy arrived as the client's recognition that her parents were "in such insane fear" and that the criticism and the guilt were both expressions of unmet terror. The empathy was not soft; it was structural. The parent's behavior made sense. That is different from making it acceptable.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not always apply. Sometimes a parent's criticism is direct expression of who they are toward a child they actively dislike. Sometimes it is sadism, not projection. The principle fits the chronic, repetitive, trait-specific criticism that has the quality of a tape on loop. It does not fit isolated criticisms or criticisms of specific behaviors that the parent is genuinely responding to.

The signal that the principle applies: the criticism has a quality of being aimed at something that is not exactly what the child is doing. The child can be still and quiet and the criticism still arrives. The trait being criticized is more general than any specific behavior. The criticism has emotional charge that exceeds the situation. These are the markers that the parent is talking to themselves, not to the child.
