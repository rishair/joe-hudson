# Don't Coach If You Have a Choice

## The Principle

If you are considering becoming a coach and feel like you have a choice about it, the answer is no. The role demands a level of integrity, exposure, and structural temptation toward narcissism that does not survive being chosen for any reason other than the call itself. The person who chooses coaching for any of the standard reasons -- making money, helping people, being respected, having an aha experience that they want to share -- will be metabolized by the role rather than able to hold it.

The test is simple. The call is the test. If the call is present, you will know because the alternative does not register as real. If the alternative still registers, the call is not yet what is operating, and entering the role at that stage will produce damage to both the coach and the people they work with.

"What I want to say to the people who are potentially wanting this is: if you think you have a choice, don't fucking do it." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The phrasing is deliberately blunt. The conventional self-help framing welcomes anyone who feels drawn to help. This principle does not. The bar is the absence of the option to refuse.

## The $100 Million Test

The principle has a fast operational form. Imagine someone arrives and says: "I'll give you $100 million right now to buy your coaching practice. The only condition is that you can never coach again for the rest of your life."

The response is the diagnostic.

"If somebody said, 'You know what? I'll give you $100 million right now to buy your practice, your coaching practice. The only thing is you can never coach again for the rest of your life.' No fucking chance. So unless that's true for you, no chance." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The host and Jerry Colonna gave the same instant answer: no chance. The amount of money is not the variable. The principle is that the call cannot be traded for any amount of money because the call is not in the same category as money. The call is the structure of who the person is and how they have to be. Selling the call would be selling the self.

If the answer to the $100M offer is "let me think about it," the call is not yet operating at the level that makes coaching the right vocation. If the answer is "yes, absolutely, I'd take that deal," coaching is something the person is doing for reasons that will be insufficient to hold them through what the role asks.

The diagnostic is fast because choicelessness is fast. See [[choicelessness-as-purpose-marker]]: the felt sense of having no choice is not slow; it arrives immediately. When you do not have to deliberate about the offer, you have your answer. When you have to deliberate, your answer is the deliberation itself.

## Why This Bar Is So High

The role has structural pressures that destroy practitioners who hold it for lesser reasons:

- **The savior pressure.** Clients in distress will activate the rescue impulse in any coach who has unmetabolized caretaking wiring. The coach without the call will get fed by the saving and eventually consumed by it. See [[coaching-as-savior-role]].
- **The validation pressure.** Being looked at as the wise one, the one who knows, the one with the answers -- this is structurally addictive to anyone who is in the role for recognition. See [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]].
- **The intimacy substitute.** Coaching offers deep contact without the risk of an actual relationship. The coach without the call will use the role as a substitute for relational risk in their own life. See [[coaching-as-surrogate-intimacy]].
- **The narcissism trap.** The role amplifies whatever narcissism is present. The coach who entered for the recognition gradually believes their own press. See [[own-your-narcissism]] and [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]].
- **The depletion pressure.** Sustained attention to other people's emotional material requires the coach to be sourced in something other than the work itself. The coach without the call burns out.

The call is the structural protection against all of these. The person who literally cannot not do the work is held by the work in a way that the person who chose the work is not. The work itself sustains them because it is what they are. For the chooser, the work depletes them because it is what they are doing for some other reason.

## "You'll Do Damage"

The blunt extension of this principle: if the call is not the source and the person enters the role anyway, they will do damage. The damage is not always visible immediately. It compounds. Clients are trained into dynamics that look like growth but are actually deepening dependencies. The coach calls them transformations. They are configurations.

"So if you're considering it, don't do this lightly. You'll do damage." -- Jerry Colonna, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

Joe affirms this directly: "You'll do damage."

The damage is not a probability. It is a structural consequence. When the coach is in the role for reasons other than the call, the coaching is in service of those reasons. The client is structurally being used to meet the coach's needs. The client may not name this; the relational field will register it.

## The Common Self-Sabotaging Reasons to Enter the Role

Joe and Jerry name several:

- **Wanting the moniker.** "If you want the moniker over the being of service, I would say that that's a pretty good sign that something's wobbly." Being called a coach is different from doing the work; if the title is what is wanted, the work is being used to obtain the title. See [[be-of-service-doesnt-require-the-moniker]].
- **Wanting the money.** "If you're doing this to make money, I would also say there's something that's wobbly." Money is not wrong as a downstream effect; money as the engine produces a different orientation than the one the work requires.
- **Coming off an ayahuasca experience or a peak insight.** The aha is not the call. The aha is data. The call is what is structurally happening regardless of whether the aha occurred. People who confuse the aha with the call enter the role at the moment they are least equipped to hold it.
- **As the alternate to the failed plan.** Jerry's specific naming: "It's not vocation, whatever vocation you choose or whatever vocation calls you, is not the alternate to the original plan. Meaning, I tried to do this startup and it failed, so now I'm going to help other CEOs. Now that impulse I honor. Go be a peer mentor. Go work at Techstars or Y Combinator. Go help in that way. But if you're not answering the vocational aspect of it, that calling from the divine, what's happening is you're trying to help from an empty vessel." The failed-startup-to-coach transition is a common shape. The transition is fine if the call is what is actually driving it. The transition is destructive if the failure is what is driving it.

The unifying problem with all of these reasons: they are reasons. The call does not need reasons. The presence of reasons is itself the data that the call is not what is operating.

## The Natural Filling of the Roster

A correlated observation: when the person is genuinely called and has done the work, they do not have to chase clients. The coaching that emerges happens naturally in their life. People line up.

"What happens is there's a lot of coaching that happens just naturally in their life. People just start lining up. And then once there's enough people happen, they're never going through this process of, can I find somebody to coach? Can I fill my roster? It just happens very naturally. And then all of a sudden their roster is filled." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The reverse is also diagnostic. The person who has to hustle for clients, who is constantly marketing, who is asking how to "build a coaching practice" -- the roster is not filling naturally. Something is structurally off. Often the issue is that the coaching is in service of the wrong engine and the market is reading what the engine actually is. See [[performing-for-love-becomes-savior-career]] for the financial-symptom mechanism.

This is not a guarantee that genuine coaches never market. It is an observation about the underlying engine. When the call is the engine, the work fills the time available. When something else is the engine, the work has to be constantly pushed.

## "If You Want to Be of Service" Is Different From "If You Want to Coach"

Service is available everywhere. The role of coach is a specific instrument. Confusing the two produces the trap.

"If you want to be of service, you can do that in every single aspect of your life. So you don't need to coach to be of service." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The person who wants to be of service can be of service as a peer mentor, as a friend, as a parent, as a teammate, as a member of a community. None of these require taking on the role of coach. The role of coach has specific structural features that the other forms of service do not have -- it is professionalized, paid, holds a particular kind of authority, attracts particular projections. If the desire is service, service is the answer. The coach role is an answer to a different question.

The question the coach role is the answer to: am I being called specifically to hold this particular kind of container, with this particular kind of authority and exposure, as the structure of how the call wants to move through me? If yes, the role is the answer. If the answer is "I want to help people," the role is not the answer; helping is the answer, and helping does not require the role.

See [[be-of-service-doesnt-require-the-moniker]] for the broader distinction.

## What This Principle Protects

The principle is not gatekeeping for the sake of exclusion. It is structural protection for three parties:

1. **The would-be coach.** Entering the role without the call leads to the depletion, distortion, and identity-trap that destroys practitioners. The principle saves the person from a role that will consume them.
2. **The clients.** The damage Jerry names is real. Clients deserve coaches who are sourced in the call, not coaches who are using them to meet some other need. The principle protects the clients from coaches who would harm them.
3. **The field.** Every coach who flames out, harms clients, or runs the role badly degrades the field. The principle protects the integrity of coaching as a vocation by keeping the entry point honest.

The principle is not a moral judgment of people who want to coach. Many of the surface reasons are honorable. The principle is a structural observation about what the role asks for and what survives inside it. Honoring the principle is honoring the reality of the role.

## Tone

Direct, blunt, weighted. Joe's tone with this teaching does not soften the bar. He uses "fucking" deliberately because the conventional softness around vocational questions is what allows the wrong people to enter the role with the wrong motives. The bluntness is the protection. He pairs it with grief, not judgment -- "it comes more with grief because I've just seen the damage that can be done." The bar is held because the consequences are real, not because the bar is a status marker.

Jerry's tone matches: serious, structural, willing to name damage as damage. The two of them deliver this teaching together because the unanimity is part of how the principle transmits. Anyone listening should hear: this is not one person's idiosyncratic view; this is what the role asks of anyone who would hold it.
