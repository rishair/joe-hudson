# Action Toward What You Want IS the Self-Validation

## The Principle

Self-validation is not a feeling you give yourself before you act. It is not an affirmation you say to yourself. It is not a state of mind. It is the action of doing what you want to do. When you do the thing you want to do, the doing IS the validation. When you don't do the thing you want to do, the not-doing IS the un-validation.

The inversion is structural. There is no separate "validation" step that happens after the action. The action carries the validation. Stop the action, and you have stopped the validation. Stop the action repeatedly, and you have installed a standing un-validation that no amount of external approval can outweigh.

"There's no amount of validation that she's going to give you that's going to work if you're not giving the validation to yourself." -- Joe Hudson, I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me

The young man Joe was coaching had been waiting for the woman he wanted to date to give him the validation that would let him approach her. The structure was self-defeating: the not-approaching was already telling him he was not worthy. No "you're worthy" from her could have outweighed the "I'm not worthy" he was telling himself every time he didn't approach.

## The Operational Definition

The client's own articulation of the principle in the live session: "My current guess at giving validation to myself is not blocking the natural flow of myself."

Joe's confirmation: "That's right."

The definition is operational, not conceptual. Self-validation is not a thing you produce. It is the absence of the thing that has been blocking what is naturally moving. The natural flow of the self — the wanting to approach, the wanting to express, the wanting to cry, the wanting to speak — is what the body produces when it is not being interfered with. The validation is the non-interference.

This is why affirmation practices often fail. "I am worthy" said by a person who is currently blocking their natural flow does not produce self-validation. It produces a managed self-talk on top of the un-validation that is being installed by the blocking. The blocking is what needs to stop. The flow is what produces the validation.

## The Winning Frame

The principle has a vernacular form the client surfaced himself: "I know I'm winning right now."

Joe asks what that means.

"I know I'm doing the thing I want to do."

The "winning" frame is the felt-sense of the principle. When you are doing the thing you want to do, you know you are winning. When you are not, you know you are not winning. The body keeps the score even when the mind is not paying attention.

The winning is not contingent on outcome. The winning is contingent on whether you are acting in accordance with what you actually want. The young man was "winning" in the coaching session because he was being intimate with everyone on the call — that was the thing he wanted to do. He was "not winning" when he did not approach the woman he wanted to approach — even before any response from her, the not-approaching was the un-winning.

The frame inverts the standard logic. Standard logic says: I'll approach her, she'll respond well, I'll feel validated, I'll be winning. The principle says: the approaching IS the winning, regardless of her response. The standard logic locates the winning in her response, which is unavailable until after the action; the principle locates the winning in the action itself, which is always available.

## Why External Validation Cannot Solve This

If self-validation is the action of doing what you want, then external validation is structurally incapable of providing it. Other people can tell you they approve of you. They cannot do the action for you. The validation that would arrive from their approval is not the validation that the not-doing has been removing. The two operate on different channels.

This is the structural reason approval-seeking does not satisfy the approval-seeker. The seeker is delivering the un-validation to themselves continuously through their own not-doing, and the external approval cannot reach the channel where the un-validation is being installed. The compliment lands on the surface; the un-validation continues underneath; the hunger persists.

The cure is not to extract more external validation. The cure is to stop installing the un-validation. And the way to stop installing the un-validation is to start doing the thing — not for the validation, but because the doing IS the validation. The principle is the missing piece for the [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] arc: the corrective is not "stop wanting their approval" (which the mind cannot do directly); it is "do the thing you want to do" (which is concrete and actionable).

See [[chase-your-own-approval]] for the broader practice this principle operationalizes. See [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] for the parallel mechanism in the speech-channel — speaking your truth is the same kind of action-as-self-approval as the broader doing-the-thing-you-want.

## What Counts as "the Thing You Want to Do"

The principle requires the person to know what they actually want. Many people running on approval-seeking have lost touch with their wants because the wants have been pre-edited by what would be acceptable. The principle therefore needs to be paired with the work of locating the actual want (see [[what-do-you-want]]).

"The thing you want to do" does not mean every impulse. It means what would emerge if you were not editing for acceptability. The young man wanted to approach the woman; the wanting was clear. He was not approaching because he was editing for what would happen if he did. The thing he wanted to do was approach. The editing was what was blocking it.

The principle also does not mean "do whatever you want at any cost." Real wants typically include the integrity of considering others. The principle is not a license for impulsivity; it is a relocation of the validation source. A person who does what they want from the place of self-validation typically acts with more care for others, not less, because the approval-seeking that was distorting their interactions is no longer running.

## The Specific Application to the Approval-Seeking Loop

The young man in the source session was caught in this loop:

1. Want to approach the woman
2. Don't approach (because I want her approval first to know it's safe)
3. Tell myself by the not-approaching that I'm not worthy
4. Want her approval more (because I now feel less worthy)
5. Don't approach (because I want her approval first)
6. Loop

The loop runs forever because the not-approaching at step 2 is producing the worthlessness at step 3, which is producing the increased want for approval at step 4, which is producing the not-approaching at step 5. The loop is self-sustaining. Each pass through it deepens the un-validation.

The principle breaks the loop at step 2. Approaching is the validation. The approaching does not require her response to be the validation; the approaching itself is the validation. Once that has been seen, the loop has nowhere to anchor. The person is no longer waiting for her approval to give themselves permission to approach; they are approaching as the giving of permission.

## What This Principle Pairs With

The principle is structurally connected to [[grieving-the-self-editor]]. When a person starts doing the thing they want to do, the system that has been editing the doing has to die. The dying is grief. The grief is not a sign that the principle is wrong; it is a sign that the principle is working. The constant self-editing identity is being dismantled. See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]].

The principle is also connected to [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]] — once the person is doing the thing they want to do (and giving themselves the validation by doing it), the extraction-quality drops out of their interactions. Other people stop feeling managed. The relating becomes real. The downstream consequence of self-validation through action is more, not less, contact with others.

## Tone

Joe delivers the principle with the energy of an inversion that surprises people. "There's no amount of validation that she's going to give you that's going to work if you're not giving the validation to yourself" lands as a structural fact, not as advice. The tone is matter-of-fact. The principle is not being argued for; it is being named.

The conviction in the delivery comes from having watched the loop work in many people. The "I know this is a brain twister, right?" Joe says in the live session signals that the principle takes a moment to land. Once it lands, the loop becomes visible. Joe gives the person time for the landing without softening the claim.

## What This Principle Is Not

- **It is not "fake it till you make it."** Faking is the opposite of doing what you actually want. Faking installs the un-validation just as not-doing does, because the action is not aligned with the want.
- **It is not "force yourself to do what you don't want to do."** The principle is about doing what you do want to do, not about overriding the body to do what someone else (a coach, a partner, a culture) says you should want.
- **It is not "ignore the fear and approach anyway."** The fear is real and often contains information. The principle does not say bulldoze the fear; it says recognize that not-acting is also a message, and that message has costs that need to be weighed against the costs of feeling the fear while acting.
- **It is not a one-time fix.** The principle has to be enacted repeatedly. Each act of doing-the-thing-you-want is a small installation of self-validation. The cumulative effect is what shifts the underlying structure.
