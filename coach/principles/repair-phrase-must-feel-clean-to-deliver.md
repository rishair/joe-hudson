# If the Repair Phrase Feels Like Shit to Say, Don't Say It

## The Principle

The pre-agreed repair phrase move (see [[repair-phrase-as-trauma-deactivator]]) requires the recognizing partner to deliver a specific sentence cleanly — without sarcasm, resentment, or backloading. If they cannot do that, they should not deliver the phrase. A contaminated delivery does worse than no delivery: the trauma-firing partner hears the contamination instead of the recognition, and the phrase that was supposed to dissolve the loop becomes evidence that the recognition was performative.

When the phrase feels like shit to say, the recognizing partner has work to do on their side before the move becomes available. The work is not to override the bad feeling and say the phrase anyway. The work is to figure out what is in them that makes them unwilling or unable to deliver the recognition.

"You can't give it to them if it feels like shit to you, right? Like, I couldn't say you were right for a while until I got it into my bones what it was actually. But it's really important that you can do that if you're capable of it and if you want to, and if you're not, to figure out what it is in you that makes it so you don't want to support them in that way. But if it doesn't feel right, don't do it." -- Joe Hudson, Unlocking your trauma pattern

## The Structure of the Principle

Three nested clauses:

1. **If you can do it cleanly, do it.** The phrase works.
2. **If you cannot do it cleanly, do not do it.** A faked phrase damages more than no phrase.
3. **If you cannot do it cleanly, figure out what is in you that is blocking it.** The block is information about your own material, not a verdict on the move.

The principle is not "you have to do this for your partner." It is closer to "you cannot do this for your partner from coercion or duty. You have to find your way to actually being able to give it."

## Why a Faked Delivery Damages More

The pre-agreed phrase is loaded with meaning by the agreement. Both partners know what it is supposed to mean. When the recognizing partner says it without being able to mean it, the gap between the loaded meaning and the actual energy of delivery is precisely what the trauma-firing partner hears. They are already in a hypersensitive state, scanning for whether the contact is real. A phrase delivered with internal resistance reads as "you are going through the motions of recognition because you have to, not because you see me." This re-confirms the original trauma message ("the people who should see me don't") rather than dissolving it.

A faked delivery is worse than the recognizing partner simply saying "I can't say that right now." The honest version preserves the trust in the move; the faked version corrupts it.

## What the Resistance Is Information About

When the recognizing partner cannot bring themselves to say the phrase, the resistance is usually not about the phrase. It is about something in their own material that the phrase or the situation is pressing against. Joe describes his own block on saying "you're right" to Tara:

The phrase felt impossible to deliver because Joe was running his own truth-and-falseness frame on the situation. He kept hearing "you're right" as a content-level concession — am I conceding she is correct about the disagreement? — when the phrase was supposed to operate at a different layer entirely. To say the phrase cleanly, he had to first take in *what the phrase actually does for Tara* (meet her wrong-making childhood trauma) so that his own attachment to the literal content of the dispute could relax.

What was in Joe blocking the phrase: an attachment to being right himself, a fear that conceding rightness gave up something he needed to hold, an under-recognition of what Tara was actually reaching for. None of these were faults; all of them were material to work through. The work was internal. Once it was done, the phrase delivered cleanly.

In other couples, the blocks look different:

- Resentment about not getting the same recognition back in their own trauma
- Conditions the recognizing partner is silently attaching to the recognition ("I'll see how much you care if you also stop honking the horn")
- Underlying disagreement with the framing of the trauma ("I don't actually think your rush is just trauma; I think you're being controlling")
- A position the recognizing partner holds about who should be apologizing to whom

Whatever the specific block is, it is the work the recognizing partner has to do before the move opens up. Naming it together can be part of [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]]: "I notice I am still resistant to saying that phrase to you; let me tell you what is in me that is making it hard."

## Why This Is Not An Out

The principle is not "if you don't feel like supporting them, don't." That misreads it. The principle is that the recognition has to be real to work, and faked recognition fails the move. The implicit task remains: become someone who can give the recognition cleanly. The principle protects the trauma-firing partner from receiving fake recognition while obligating the recognizing partner to do the work that makes real recognition possible.

The two readings:

- *Misuse:* "I don't feel like saying it, so I don't have to. Joe said don't do it if it feels like shit." This makes the principle into an exit ramp.
- *Correct use:* "I notice I can't say it cleanly. There is something in me to work on. Until I work it through, I'll be honest about not being able to deliver it, and I will not fake it." This makes the principle into a marker for personal work.

The difference is whether the resistance is investigated or accepted as a fixed fact about the relationship.

## The Companion Principle: Don't Coerce Your Partner Into the Phrase

The trauma-firing partner also has a responsibility here. If the recognizing partner says "I can't say that cleanly right now," the trauma-firing partner does not get to demand that they say it anyway. Demanding the phrase from a partner who is not yet able to deliver it cleanly will produce the contaminated version, which is worse than no version. The trauma-firing partner has to honor the recognizing partner's truthful "not yet."

This is part of what makes the move co-created (see [[agreements-must-be-co-created-not-prescribed]]). The phrase belongs to both partners. Either partner can flag that their side of the move is not currently available. The relationship has to be able to hold the absence as well as the presence.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognizing partner who has been pretending to deliver a phrase and feels the dishonesty accumulating between them.
- The trauma-firing partner who notices the phrase landing without the warmth it used to have and cannot tell why.
- A couple who has set up a repair phrase that one partner privately resents.
- A recognizing partner who is starting to feel coerced into validation they do not currently mean.
- A trauma-firing partner who is starting to demand the phrase as proof of love.
- The moment a recognizing partner discovers what specifically in their own material is blocking the delivery.

## Tone

Plain, with the practical kindness of "don't put yourself in an impossible position." Joe delivers this principle the way he delivers most of his couple-work principles: it is not a moral instruction, it is a structural fact about how the move works. The phrase needs to be clean. Faking it does not work. So if you cannot be clean, do not fake it, and do the work on your own side until you can.
