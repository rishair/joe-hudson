# Don't Hate Your Kids and They Won't Hate You (The Teen Rebellion Reframe)

## The Principle

The cultural script says teenagers will hate their parents. Enjoy them while they are young because the relationship will go cold. This is a script, not a law. The teenagers who hate their parents are mostly the teenagers who have been told, in subtle and not-so-subtle ways, that they are not okay. The "hating" is the response to years of feeling that some part of them was unacceptable.

"Hey, don't hate your kids and they won't hate you." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The reframe inverts the standard expectation. The teen rebellion that older parents warn you about is not automatic. It is the predictable downstream effect of a particular kind of parenting that most parents are doing without realizing it.

## What "Hating Your Kids" Looks Like (Even When You Love Them)

No parent thinks they hate their kids. Most parents love their kids fiercely. But "hating" in the sense Joe means is not a feeling — it is a transmitted signal. The signal is: some part of you is not okay.

"They've been told their whole life that there's some part of them that's not okay. It's not okay for you to be sad. It's not okay for you to be angry. It's not okay for you to be scared." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The parent is not consciously telling the child they are unworthy. The parent is telling the child to stop crying, to calm down, to not be so angry, to grow up, to behave. Each of those instructions, repeated across childhood, installs the felt sense that some part of the child needs to be different from what it is. The cumulative message is: you, as you are, are not acceptable.

"Why the fuck should they want to be with or not hate somebody who's been telling them that they're not okay on some level for years?" -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The teenager pulls away not because teenagers naturally pull away but because they have spent years receiving the message that their actual self is unwelcome. Once they have the autonomy to leave, they leave. The leaving registers to the parent as rejection. It is actually self-protection.

## Why Parents Send This Signal Even When They Love Their Kids

The mechanism is not malice. The mechanism is that parents send themselves the same signal all day long.

"Mostly because the parent is sending themselves the signal all the time that they need to be adjusted." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

A parent who lives in self-criticism cannot help installing it in the child. The voice that tells the parent they are too much, not enough, doing it wrong, falling behind — that voice does not turn off when the parent looks at their child. It transmits.

The fix is not at the child layer. The fix is at the parent's relationship to themselves.

"To not do that to your children, to not do that to your children, requires you to love yourself. You don't want to fuck your kids up, love yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

A parent who has done the work to stop sending themselves the not-okay signal stops sending it to the child. The child receives a different message: you, as you are, are welcome here. The teenager raised by that parent does not have a self to protect against the parent. The teenager can be a teenager — testing, exploring, pushing — without having to break the relationship to find themselves.

## The Swimming Pool Metaphor for Teen Behavior

The teenage years involve a real, predictable pushing-off. This is not the same as hating.

"There's something about, like, when the kids hit teenage years, there's something that happens where their job is to push against the pool. Their job is to push off against the edge of the pool, and you're the edge of the pool." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The pushing-off is developmental, not personal. It is the kid building the muscle to swim into their own life. The parent who recognizes the pushing-off as developmental can be the edge of the pool without taking the push personally.

What the parent provides is the lane to swim in.

"You treat people with respect, you contribute around, you contribute, you treat people with respect. And when you show that you can take responsibility, you can have the freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

That is it. Three things. Treat people with respect. Contribute. Demonstrate you can take responsibility and earn the freedom. The boundaries are wide. The structure is firm. The teen pushes within the lane. The pushing produces the swim. No hating required.

This is the teenage version of [[stay-close-and-insist]]. The parent stays close (the lane is held) and insists (the lane exists). The teen has the room to push off.

## What Disrupts This

The disruption is the parent who continues to send the not-okay signal into the teen years. The teen who is told their anger is wrong, their sadness is too much, their choices are bad, their preferences are weird — that teen has more to push against than the lane. They have to push against the parent's continued attempt to make them different.

Now the pushing-off is not just developmental. It is also a fight. The teen needs to be themselves and the parent is in the way of that. The relationship gets tense not because of teen biology but because of years of accumulated friction over the question of whether the teen is acceptable as they are.

## The Reframe for Parents Anticipating the Teen Years

Most parents go into the teen years braced for the relationship to go cold. The bracing itself is part of the problem. The parent who expects the teen to hate them communicates that expectation, which the teen registers, which contributes to the dynamic.

The reframe is: this does not have to happen. Joe reports that his teenagers come to him for advice from time to time. His teenagers' friends like being around him. The cold-relationship trope is not his reality. He is not special. He has done the work of not telling his kids they are not okay. That is the whole move.

"I notice that like all my kids' friends who know us want to be around us." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

If the parent has been sending the not-okay signal for fifteen years, the reframe does not change the situation overnight. But the move is still the same: stop sending the signal. Start sending the welcome. The relationship can shift in either direction even with a teenager.

## The Connection to Self-Love

This principle is one of the cleanest illustrations of [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]]. A parent cannot consistently send the message "you are welcome as you are" to a child if the parent is not consistently sending it to themselves. The internal message leaks into the external one. The teen reads the field, not the script.

"To not do that to your children requires you to love yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This is also why "the most important thing in a marriage is to take care of yourself" — see [[take-care-of-yourself-then-marriage-then-kids]] — is so directly relevant to whether the teen years go cold. A parent who has not been taking care of themselves has been carrying around a long-running internal not-okay signal. That signal radiates into the parenting, regardless of the parent's intentions.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is not "if your teen pulls away, it's your fault." Teens have their own developmental trajectory and their own circumstances. Mental health issues, trauma from outside the home, peer dynamics, and biology all play roles. The principle says that the cultural expectation that teens will hate their parents is largely the predictable outcome of a particular kind of parenting, not an inevitability. It also gives parents a useful question: where in this relationship have I been telling my child they are not okay?

The principle also does not say teens should not push back, set their own boundaries, or have their own opinions. They should. The pool-edge metaphor describes healthy adolescent development, where the teen pushes against the parent as part of becoming their own person. The principle distinguishes healthy pushing from the cold rejection that comes from accumulated not-okay messages.

## Tone

Slightly provocative. Joe leads with "hey, don't hate your kids and they won't hate you" — a half-joke that is also a serious teaching. The tone gives the parent permission to question a cultural assumption that has been treated as gospel. Hold that tone: a little dry, willing to challenge a script that most people have absorbed, but not preachy. The teaching is offered as a real possibility, not as a guarantee.
