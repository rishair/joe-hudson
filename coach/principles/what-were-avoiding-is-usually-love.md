# What We're Avoiding Is Usually Love

## The Principle

When a person has been avoiding something for a long time and the avoidance has the catastrophic-image shape (a black hole, an abyss, a spiral, being absorbed, being made helpless), the thing being avoided is usually love.

Not love as concept. Not love as romantic feeling. Love as the specific kind of contact — with self, with another, with what is real — that dissolves the identity that has been doing the avoiding. The catastrophic image is structurally correct about what contact with love does. What the catastrophic image misnames is whether the dissolution is the catastrophe it's anticipating.

"It's a human thing. The more we avoid something, the scarier it gets... And typically the thing that we're avoiding is love." — Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

The principle has two halves that operate together. The first half is the standard avoidance dynamic: the longer something is kept out of awareness, the more catastrophic the imagined contact with it becomes. The second half is the specific finding: when you finally enter what's been avoided, what you find is most often love.

## Why This Is the Pattern

The mechanism has a structural reason behind it. The identity that does most of the daily work of being a person is the small, defended, managing self. Its functioning depends on a certain amount of separation from intimate contact — with feelings, with longing, with other people, with what's real. The managing self does its job through the gap.

Love is what closes the gap. The closing of the gap is what dissolves the managing self. Not in a catastrophic way — the person is not destroyed — but in the specific sense that the management becomes unnecessary. The system that has been organized around producing love through effort gets to drop the effort and receive the love that was already available.

For a system organized around the effort, the receiving feels like death. The drop-of-management feels like dissolution. The image of the abyss is the felt sense of the dissolution happening. The dissolution is real but not the catastrophe the image makes it out to be. What survives is what the managing was always trying to become but could not produce: the self in contact with what is real.

This is the same mechanism described in [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]], applied to the specific case where peace is itself love. Peace and love are not different things at the depths Joe is pointing at. The system resists both for the same reason: they dissolve the structures organized to seek them.

## The Loneliness Variant

A particular operational form: a person describes a chronic loneliness or longing. The conventional interpretation is that the loneliness is about lack — the absence of something. The principle inverts the interpretation. The loneliness is the felt shape of love being avoided. The longing is the love trying to be felt.

In the source transcript, the person describes the feeling as "a disconnection and a longing more than love." Joe's response: "Yeah, how is that not love?" — and then he asks the person to go right into the middle of the longing.

The structure: the longing felt as a disconnection is the love trying to make contact. The disconnection is the resistance to the contact. The longing is the love itself, kept in its un-met form by the resistance. Entering the longing is contacting the love.

This is also why loneliness is often described as bottomless. Love is bottomless. The longing for it inherits the property. The person who is afraid the loneliness will swallow them is correctly sensing the size of what is being avoided. The size is love's. The fear is of meeting it.

## The "Only Thing That Longs to Be Loved Is Love" Formulation

A particularly compact and useful formulation of the principle, deployed for the alone-in-room concern (see [[i-feel-alone-in-rooms-despite-success]]): the part of self that feels "not enough" or "not good enough" is, on direct contact, the part that has always wanted to be loved. And the only thing that wants to be loved is love itself.

"What we find out is that part of ourselves that feels like they're not enough is actually our longing to be loved. And the only thing that longs to be loved is love." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The formulation works as a meditation prompt during practices like [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]]. The practitioner who has just made direct contact with the "not enough" feeling can use the formulation to recognize what the feeling actually is. The feeling reads as deficiency. The formulation reframes it as the longing-to-be-loved, which is structurally love seeking its expression.

This is the same principle in a different formulation. The avoided thing (the not-enough feeling) is the love (the longing-to-be-loved). The formulation collapses the gap between the avoidance and the discovery in a single sentence, which makes it useful for the practitioner who has been doing the contact work and needs the recognition framing.

The follow-up move that the formulation supports: once the practitioner recognizes the part as love-in-its-longing-form, they can become the source of the love the part has been waiting for. The internal source of love comes online. The external chase for love can quiet.

"Eventually, if you spend enough time with it, you go, oh, well then I'll give you the love. I'll show up for you. I don't have to reject this part of myself." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

See [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]] for the principle that makes the practice of meeting the longing actionable.

## The Avoidance-Makes-It-Scarier Mechanism

The first half of the principle states a general law: the more we avoid something, the scarier it gets. This operates on whatever is being avoided, not only on love. But the principle's most consequential application is to love, because love is what most people most consistently avoid most thoroughly across the longest time horizons.

The mechanism: each avoidance increases the imagined catastrophe of the avoided thing. The system builds up the feared image to justify the continued avoidance. Years of avoiding produce years of escalating imagery. By the time the person attempts contact, the image has become so catastrophic that the actual contact will be a relief — but only if the contact happens. The avoidance itself maintains the image's terrifying status.

This is why "I've been avoiding this for decades" is not evidence that the thing is genuinely terrifying. It is evidence that the avoidance has had decades to produce the terror. The thing itself has not changed. The image of it has been compounding.

The fix is structural: contact. One contact dissolves much of the image. The image that has been built up over years cannot survive a few seconds of actual encounter with the thing it was about. This is why brief, direct contact with the avoided territory ([[lets-go-there]]) is often dramatically more dissolving than years of working on the avoidance from outside.

## What Love Specifically Does

The principle is more useful if it is precise about what kind of contact "love" names. Joe's working definition across the compendium is that love is deep welcoming — see [[love-as-deep-welcoming]]. Love rips away identity — see [[losing-self-in-love-two-forms]]. Love is what makes the heart break open — see [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]].

The contact-with-love that is being avoided is contact with any of these. With the welcoming that doesn't filter. With the dissolution of the defended self. With the heart that can break and expand. The specific flavor of the avoidance — whether the person is avoiding being loved, or avoiding loving, or avoiding self-love, or avoiding the love that's available with what's true — varies. The principle holds across the variations.

This is why the principle is so reliable as a diagnostic. The question "what would I have to be in contact with if the thing I'm avoiding wasn't avoided?" almost always yields a love-shaped answer. Not always immediately. The first layers of answer are usually fear, shame, grief, helplessness. Underneath those, more reliably than not, is the love that the fear/shame/grief/helplessness has been holding off.

See [[heartbreak-is-the-love-underneath-the-blockers]] for the specific structural form of this: the layered emotional descent that finds heartbreak (which is love in its grieving form) at the bottom.

## The Identity Stakes

The principle has stakes that make it hard to apply. If the thing being avoided is love, and love dissolves the managing identity, then entering the avoided territory is, in a real sense, the end of who the person has been being. The dissolution is not metaphorical.

This is why the avoidance is so durable. It is not the avoidance of an unpleasant feeling. It is the avoidance of the disappearance of who the person has been. The defensive structure is doing exactly what it was built to do. The structure protects the identity that built it.

The work is not to bypass the defense by force. The work is to slowly establish that the dissolution is not the catastrophe the structure is anticipating. What survives is more alive than what is being protected. The system has to discover this experientially. The first dissolution is the hardest. After the first, the second is easier — the system has lived through the dissolution and noticed that it is still here.

This is the mechanism behind [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] and behind why the work of allowing love is iterative across a life. Each cycle of allowing dissolves a layer of the defended identity. The person who has been through many cycles can drop into love faster because they have evidence that they survive the drop.

## The Coaching Application

When a person is in the territory of [[i-feel-stuck]] or [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]] and has named the catastrophic image, the diagnostic question is: is this love that's being avoided? Often the answer is yes, but the person does not have the language for it until the contact has been made.

The move that surfaces this: enter the territory ([[lets-go-there]]), let the felt material complete, and then ask — gently, in the person's terms — what is here. If the answer has a longing-shape, a loneliness-shape, a disconnection-shape, a wanting-shape, it is usually love.

The naming has a specific reframe function. Once the person knows that what they've been running from is love, the relationship to the running changes. You don't run from love the same way you run from a threat. The naming converts the avoidance from a survival behavior into a heartbreak that the person can now feel about how long they have been keeping love at bay.

The coaching does not require certainty that the avoidance is of love. The naming is offered tentatively, in question form ("yeah, how is that not love?"), letting the person verify. If the person resonates, the naming sticks. If not, the work continues at the felt layer until what is actually being avoided becomes clear.

## What This Principle Is Not

This principle is not the claim that all avoidance is of love. There are specific avoidances — of pain, of helplessness, of shame, of grief — that operate in their own right and need their own work. The principle states a tendency: at sufficient depth, in patterns that have lasted long enough, the avoided thing is reliably love-shaped. It is not a universal substitution for working with the actual felt material.

It is also not the claim that all loneliness is bypassable through one contact with love. The loneliness is real. The longing is real. The contact with love does not eliminate them — it changes their quality. They become felt longing rather than chronic loneliness. They become the felt sense of love being received in real time rather than the felt sense of love being kept away.

It is also not a romantic-love claim. The "love" in this principle is broader. It includes self-love, contact-with-what-is, devotion, intimate presence, the felt sense of being alive. Romantic love is one expression. The principle operates across all of them.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person who has been avoiding intimate contact (with themselves, with a partner, with a feeling) for years and reports the avoidance as protection against a catastrophic outcome
- A person who describes a chronic loneliness that has not been touched by any of the social/relational solutions they've tried
- A person who experiences longing as overwhelming and has organized their life to keep the longing at bay
- A person who enters the avoided territory and discovers something there that doesn't have a name yet — and the contour of the something matches love
- A person who has been working on a stuckness for a long time and the stuckness keeps regenerating because the thing underneath the stuckness has not been named or met

## Tone

Direct and quiet. The teaching is delivered without dramatization. The discovery that the avoidance is of love is enough on its own. Joe does not heighten it.

The phrasing "typically" matters. Joe does not claim universality. He claims a reliable pattern. The hedge keeps the principle operating as a diagnostic rather than a doctrine. The person is invited to verify rather than instructed to believe.

When the person makes the contact and finds the love, the tone stays quiet. No celebration. The naming is enough. The body knows what just happened.
