# See Through "There's Something Essentially Wrong With Me"

## The Principle

Shame depends on a deeper belief: there is something essentially wrong with you, not just with what you did. The intellectual work of seeing through this belief is one of two main moves for dissolving shame. The other is feeling the emotion underneath. Both are needed; this principle covers the head-side of the work.

The move: distinguish what you did from what you are. You did a thing you do not want to have done. You are not, in your essence, the doing. There is some world in which the thing you did is met with understanding rather than condemnation. That world is not imaginary — it exists. The condemnation is one possible response, not a verdict from the universe.

"It's a little bit beyond right and wrong, good and bad. It's essentially, there's something wrong with me. And if you can see that — I'll give you a really good example. If you can see that there's not something inherently wrong with you, and that is like a great relief of the shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## The Milk-Spilling Example

Joe's pointer at this principle: a kid spills milk. One parent gets angry; the other does not. The kid has done the same thing in both cases. The kid feels wrong only if the parent gets angry.

"Anybody gets angry at one of their kids. Okay, there's another parent, the kid could do the same thing. So let's say the kid spills milk. One parent gets angry, one parent doesn't. The kid hasn't essentially done anything wrong, but they're going to feel wrong if one — if their parent gets angry. They're not going to feel wrong if their parent doesn't get angry." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

This is the structural argument. Wrongness is not a property of the act. It is a property of the relational response to the act. The kid is the same. The milk is the same. The wrongness is constructed by one of the two responses.

The principle generalizes. Whatever you did, there is some response that would have made you feel wrong, and there is some response that would not. The wrongness is not in the act. It is in the response. And the response is the variable, not the constant.

## The "Somebody Understands" Frame

The intellectual move that follows: if you look at anything you have ever done, somewhere on this planet there is a person who would have understanding for it. Not approval necessarily — understanding. The capacity to see why you did it, what was happening in you, how it made sense from where you were.

"If you look at anything that you've done, there's somebody on this planet that's going to have understanding for that, who's going to have gentleness for that. So, there's some world in which like you never deserve the punishment. There's some world in which you're not essentially wrong." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The point is not to find that person and feel forgiven by them. The point is to recognize that essential wrongness is not the only available reading. The reading you have inherited (probably from a parent, probably installed before age seven) is one possible reading. Other readings exist. The wrongness was not delivered by the universe.

## What This Move Is Not

**It is not "what you did was okay."** The deconstruction is at the level of essential wrongness, not at the level of action evaluation. You can fully recognize that what you did was harmful, costly, not how you want to be, and not derive from that the conclusion that you are essentially bad.

"I'm not saying that you don't want to fix it. I'm not saying that you don't want to take action to like understand yourself more clearly and to not repeat patterns that are destructive. I'm not saying any of that. I'm just saying that essence of 'I'm bad, I'm wrong because I did this.' If you can see through that intellectually through deconstruction, that offers a tremendous amount of relief from the shame hot potato." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

**It is not bypass.** The work is not to skip past having done the thing. The work is to address the shame layer so that the actual work of addressing the action can happen. Shame stops the work; deconstructing the essential-wrongness layer is what lets the work proceed.

**It is not a one-time insight.** The belief in essential wrongness is usually embodied. The intellectual deconstruction is necessary but not sufficient. It works in concert with feeling the emotion that the shame is locking in place. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] and [[shame-as-disconnection]].

## The Both-And Stance: Responsibility Without Shame

The principle makes available a stance that is rare and useful: full responsibility, no shame.

"I can take responsibility for this and I don't have to be ashamed in the taking of responsibility. I can be wrong. And I don't need to take on any kind of shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The two pieces are usually fused. People take responsibility *as* a shame move ("yes, it was me, I am terrible") or they avoid responsibility *to* avoid shame ("it wasn't really me, here are the reasons"). The principle lets them come apart. You can be the responsible party and you can not be in shame. The two halves are independent.

This is the leadership signature Joe points at: the leader who says "the buck stops here" without taking on shame. The leader who says "we're not going to spend any time figuring out who's to blame; we're going to figure out how to make sure this doesn't happen again." The taking of full responsibility is empowering, not collapsing. See [[fault-vs-responsibility]] for the dedicated treatment.

"You see great leaders do this all the time. They'll say like, oh, we're not going to spend any time figuring out who's to blame. We're going to figure out — we're going to spend time figuring out how to make sure this doesn't happen again, is the way that a leader would happen. Or another one would be a leader saying, the whole thing is my responsibility. I'm the leader. I take full responsibility. Now, how are we going to fix it? Like somebody who can show that I can take responsibility for this and I don't have to be ashamed in the taking of responsibility." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## The Pragmatic Argument

If the conceptual argument does not land, there is a pragmatic one. The intellectual case for the belief "I am essentially bad" is harder to make than usually assumed. The pragmatic case against it is straightforward: it doesn't work.

"If you tell a kid they're naughty all the time, they're gonna start behaving naughty. If you tell a kid they're dumb all the time, they're going to behave dumb. Same if you do it to yourself, if you label yourself that way. So whether it's true or not true is somewhat irrelevant. What's relevant is what's effective. Like, we all know that companies that are constantly blaming one another are less effective than companies where everybody takes responsibility. Same thing with us." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The argument is not "you are inherently good, so stop believing you're bad." The argument is "regardless of what's true, the belief produces the behavior, and the behavior produces more reason to hold the belief, and the whole thing is a low-effectiveness configuration."

## Connection to Inherent Goodness

The principle is the deconstructive companion to the constructive principle [[inherent-goodness]]. Inherent goodness says: you are good in your essence. Seeing through essential wrongness says: the alternative belief (you are bad in your essence) is not actually well-founded.

You can arrive at the goodness side from either direction. Some people land it via the positive frame (the oak tree never has to earn its existence). Some people land it via the negative-frame deconstruction (the wrongness was never as solid as it appeared). Both are paths to the same recognition.

The question [[if-i-were-inherently-good]] uses the principle as a probe: how would you interpret this want if you were inherently good? The answer makes the inherent-goodness reading available as a felt option.

## The Body Catches Up Slower

A specific failure mode: the intellectual deconstruction lands, the person can articulate why they are not essentially wrong, and yet they still feel wrong. Brett raises this directly in the source transcript:

"If you do the intellectual deconstruction on the belief system of that I'm bad or wrong, but it still exists somewhere deep somatically, then my taking responsibility for it could still be done in a subtly shamey way that I might not see. What do I do about that?" -- Brett Kistler, The Shame Hot Potato

The answer is to do the emotional work in parallel: feel the grief and helplessness underneath. The intellectual deconstruction prepares the channel; feeling what is underneath actually clears it. The principle never works alone. See [[shame-as-disconnection]] and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

"The most important thing is to feel the emotion that's underneath that. So if you've done something that you really feel is essentially bad, so a way that you don't want to be, then there's probably a tremendous amount of grief there and probably a tremendous amount of helplessness. And if you feel that grief and helplessness, then the behavior is far more likely to stop than if you feel shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## Why This Is Empowering Rather Than Letting Yourself Off the Hook

A common worry: if I let go of "essentially wrong," won't I do bad things without consequence? The empirical answer is that the opposite happens. The person who is in shame keeps doing the thing. The person who can take responsibility without shame stops doing the thing. The principle [[shame-drives-behavior]] holds.

"It is very much an upright, very empowered. When somebody says the buck stops here, or when somebody says, no, I'm not going to defend myself around that, that like, I won't be defensive, then it's a deeply empowering act." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The upright-undefended stance ([[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] and [[upright-undefended-love]]) is what becomes available once the essential-wrongness belief is loosened. That stance is more empowering and more accountable than the shamed stance, not less.

## The Lifetime-Scale Instance: The Dying Woman's Verdict

The principle is usually applied to specific actions or recent regret. There is also a lifetime-scale instance worth naming: when "there is something wrong with me" has been the chronic operating belief across decades, the resulting life is built around the contorted-because-something-is-wrong-with-me version of the self. The deathbed verdict reveals what the belief produced.

The canonical illustration is the dying woman in [[dying-woman-what-a-waste]]. Her final words: "All my life I felt like there was something wrong with me. What a waste." The two clauses are causally linked. The waste was the structural consequence of the belief. She did not get to live as herself because the belief told her the self was not okay to be. The life that was built was the contorted-self's life, and at the end the verdict came in: that was not me.

This is the lifetime-scale stakes of the principle. The intellectual deconstruction matters not only because it relieves the daily shame, but because the chronic belief produces a chronic non-life. The teaching is therefore not just therapeutic; it is structural prevention of the deathbed verdict.

"What she was saying there was that all my life I wasn't being myself because I felt like there was something wrong with me. And because of that, I had a life that wasn't designed for me, and that was a waste. I wasted my life. So if you just be yourself, you realize there's nothing wrong with you. You'll realize that there is a whole world designed -- whole world designed for you, waiting for you to be you." -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

The closing claim is the constructive companion to the deconstruction: the world is not just neutral about who you actually are. There is a life-shape available for the actual you. The wrongness-belief has been preventing the recognition of that shape and the entry into it. See [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]] for the structural mechanism by which the world organizes around the actual self once the contortion stops.

The order of operations is sometimes the reverse: the person stops contorting (often via the practice in [[the-seven-day-be-yourself-iteration]]), notices that the consequences they feared do not arrive, and the wrongness-belief begins to dissolve from below. The intellectual deconstruction in this principle and the behavioral experiment of being-yourself are two sides of the same work.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You have done something you regret. The shame is heavy. You notice that under "I shouldn't have done that" there is "I'm a bad person." The first sentence is reasonable. The second is the work.
- A coachee comes in carrying chronic shame about a long-past action. The action has been thoroughly processed at the level of behavior. The shame remains because the essential-wrongness belief has not been touched.
- You are in a [[shame-hot-potato]] and notice that the throw lands because you do believe, at some level, that you are essentially the thing being accused of
- You are a leader who needs to take responsibility for a failure and notices an impulse to either defend (avoid shame) or collapse (perform shame). The principle offers the third option.
- You are working on a behavior pattern and notice that the more you shame yourself the more the pattern persists. The wrongness-belief is the lock.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with care and precision. The principle has to be stated carefully because the slide to "what you did was fine" is easy to make and is the wrong reading. The careful both-and ("you can take responsibility AND not be ashamed") is the actual point. The tone is steady and clarifying — not heavy, not bypassing.
