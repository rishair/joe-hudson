# Wants Over Shoulds

## Definition

Following wants is the most effective way to transform. Following shoulds is the least effective. A should in its nature says: who you are needs to be controlled, managed, modified. A want carries trust in who you are.

"The journey of self-development is the discovering of the self. It is self-realization. It is self-awareness. And a should in its nature is saying that you need to be controlled." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

"Should is a mechanism of shame. There's a saying that shame is the locks that keep the chains of bad habits in place. So should is like a really bad management technique." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Should Operates on Four Layers

A should is not just a thought. It hits the system across four registers simultaneously, which is why it produces such consistent damage.

- **Energetically:** oppressive. The energy in which one person says "you should" to another carries threat regardless of the words.
- **Intellectually:** control-based. The mental frame is policing rather than discovery.
- **Emotionally:** rigid. Flexibility, curiosity, and play are squeezed out.
- **Neurologically:** a threat. The nervous system reads the should as danger.

"Energetically, it's oppressive. Intellectually, it's control-based. Emotionally, it's rigidity. And neurologically, it's a threat." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

See [[should-as-threat]] for the full breakdown.

## The Verbal Should and the Energetic Should

The voice in the head saying "you should do that" is the obvious form. There is also a non-verbal should: a muscular or neurological response that hits the body before the words form. Reaching for the second piece of cake and feeling that familiar internal pressure is the energetic should without language. Both forms produce the same damage and both must be recognized.

"You could just reach for the double flourless chocolate cake and you'll just feel that kind of, uh, inside of you. And that, that is just a nonverbal should. So I think it's really important to see it as, as both." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## In Practice

The subtle trap: as people mature, they stop wanting to throw temper tantrums. But instead of recognizing this as an evolved want ("I don't want to throw a temper tantrum"), they convert it into a should ("I shouldn't throw a temper tantrum"). The natural impulse is replaced by a rule. The energy shifts from desire to obligation.

The mechanism of the conversion is worth seeing. A five-year-old genuinely wants to throw a temper tantrum and does so. At twenty-five, the same person has iterated; they have developed. They actually want different things now. They could just notice: "I have evolved. I now want something different." Instead, they layer a should on top of the old behavior and treat it as a moral correction. The iteration that happened naturally gets reframed as a rule that has to be enforced. The natural impulse inside us gets converted into a thing we should do.

"That isn't -- we have iterated, we have evolved, we now want something different. But we turn that into a should, we change that natural impetus inside us into something that we should do." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

Finding the want behind every should is the practice. "I should stop smoking" becomes "I want to breathe freely." "I should exercise" becomes "I want to feel strong." The should drags. The want pulls. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

This connects to [[shame-drives-behavior]]: shoulds are often the voice of shame. They reinforce the belief that you are not good enough as you are. Wants trust the self. Shoulds police the self.

## The Developmental Argument

The strongest case for wants over shoulds is developmental: children develop more in the first seven years of life than at any other time. In those seven years, they have no shoulds. There is no "should" in their brain. They follow wants. When shoulds appear, development slows.

"If you think about the first 7 years of your life, you couldn't even have shoulds. And then all of a sudden shoulds show up and every -- all your development slows down. You get more development in the first 7 years of your life than you do pretty much at any other time of your life. And it's when you stop following your wants and you start following your shoulds that everything gets slowed down." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

"If you think about like kids from 0 to 8 years old, there's no internal should, and they're doing all sorts -- they're developing crazy amounts compared to any other time in life. They're learning all sorts of things, and it's all just because they're following their wants." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

This is not romantic. It is a mechanical claim about what produces development. Wants do. Shoulds do not.

## Why Shoulds Persist Despite Failing

A manager who says "you should, you should, you should" is obviously ineffective. Hundreds of management books and psychology studies confirm it does not work. People recognize it instantly when they see another person do it. They cannot recognize it in themselves.

"You can see it from a manager 10 miles away. You're sitting there and you see a manager like, you should, you should, you should. You're like, oh my God, that's not going to work. But we'll do it to ourselves all freaking day long, and we will recognize it outside, but we won't recognize it inside." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

The reason it stays invisible is shame. The should requires the person to believe they are bad. To see the should clearly, the person would have to drop the premise that they need correction. This is the same dynamic as a war between countries: both sides must believe the other is bad for the war to continue. The internal war works the same way.

"To have that internal war of should means that you have to think you're bad, and that's what makes it so hard to see through the should, to see through the war." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## One of the Seven Limiting Beliefs

In the Art of Accomplishment framework, "shoulds before wants" is one of approximately seven limiting beliefs. Like the others, it rests on the foundational belief that you are not inherently good. See [[seven-limiting-beliefs]] and [[inherent-goodness]]. If you trusted your wants, you would follow them. The should is evidence that the want is not trusted.

"The only way you would think I should do that instead of I want to do that is because you think that you aren't inherently good." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## How Long It Takes to Drop Shoulds

For Joe, dropping the verbal shoulds in his own head was fast once he understood they did not work. The analogy: if a screw gun strips every screw, you stop using it. You do not need a course to stop.

What takes years is the meta-version: telling yourself you should stop using shoulds. That move adds another layer of the same pattern.

"For me, turning off the shoulds in my -- in the voice in my head was very quick. It didn't take a tremendous amount of time once I really just understood. If you start telling yourself you should stop using shoulds, yeah, it could take years." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is grinding through self-improvement with obligation
- When "I should" language dominates how a person talks about change. See [[i-should-be-doing-this]].
- When effort is high and movement is low
- When the work has become joyless (see [[enjoyment-as-path]])
- When someone fears that without shoulds they would become lazy. See [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]].
- When [[perfectionism]] has converted natural impulses into obligations. See [[impulse-to-should]].

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward discovering the underlying want behind every should. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].
- Toward [[the-want]] as something to feel, not just to chase.
- Toward [[enjoyment-as-path]]: wants are inherently more enjoyable to follow
- Toward [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]]: trusting the deep want leads to compassionate action
- Toward [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]]: when a want is fully felt, it often transforms or releases.
