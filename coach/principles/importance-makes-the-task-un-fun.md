# Importance Makes the Task Un-Fun

## Definition

When a task is loaded with importance, the task stops being enjoyable. When a task is unenjoyable, the body refuses to engage with it — what looks like procrastination is the body's correct response to a felt state of dread. The cause of the procrastination is not the absence of discipline. The cause is the importance that has been loaded onto the task.

The compression: **you procrastinate on things that feel like shit.** And the thing making the task feel like shit is, almost always, the importance you have somehow assigned to it.

> "The only thing making it not fun is that you somehow think it's important." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

This is the principle that organizes [[the-perfectionism-that-stops-you-from-starting]]. Importance produces the perfectionist standard. The standard produces the inability to start. The body knows the cost of engaging with the loaded task and protects against it through procrastination.

## In Practice

The diagnostic question that exposes the principle: where does the person procrastinate, and where don't they? The same person who cannot get through their side project will move through identical tasks for other people without friction. The work is the same. The importance assigned to it is different.

> "If every time you played the guitar, you were like, 'oh, I gotta do this fucking perfect,' you would not play guitar. If every time you doom scrolled and you're like, 'I've gotta fucking like do my finger perfectly on that scroll,' you would stop doom scrolling." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The everyday cases make the principle visible. The activity is the same; the importance-loading is the only variable; the engagement availability follows the loading.

The principle also runs in reverse. A task that has been ground down by importance — joyless, dreaded, procrastinated for years — can become engageable in real time when the importance is removed. The man in the website-coaching session who had not built his niche site for years immediately reported "a lot more energy" and "way less friction" the moment Joe proposed "let's say you just have fun doing it. The only requirement is that I have fun." The body responded to the change in framing before any change in the work itself.

## The Inversion Joe Often Makes

A worked move from the principle: when someone defends the importance of a task ("but it really matters, I have to get this right"), invert. Ask them to imagine the same task without the importance. What changes?

The person almost always reports lightness, more energy, less friction. Then ask: what would the quality of the work be in the unloaded state versus the loaded state? Many people predict the unloaded version would be of higher quality. (This is consistent with the [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] principle: the importance that demands quality prevents quality.)

But Joe pushes further: even if the unloaded version produced lower quality, the unloaded version gets done and the loaded version doesn't. A shitty version of the work that exists beats a perfect version that doesn't.

> "Let's assume that it's shitty quality compared to the perfect one. And then you're like, 'you know what, I'm gonna have fun redoing my website, improving my website.' Then eventually you'll, you'll get the website." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The iteration frame matters because it preempts the "but quality" defense. The unloaded version is iterable. The loaded version is unstartable. Iteration of the unloaded version converges on quality. Refusal to start the loaded version produces nothing.

## Why This Mechanism Operates

The mechanism has two converging causes:

**The importance produces a standard.** When work has been loaded with stakes — identity, money, freedom, validation — the body sets a standard for the work proportional to the loaded stakes. The standard is almost always unreachable in any given session of engagement, because no first hour of work on a side project can deliver financial freedom or prove the person's worth. The mismatch between the standard and what is reachable produces the experience of friction, which the body reads as cost. The body protects against cost.

**The importance activates the perfectionist voice.** Perfectionism is the internalized critical-parent voice (see [[perfectionism]]). Loaded importance activates the voice at full volume. The voice creates fear. Fear-driven engagement is constricted engagement. Constricted engagement produces worse work, which the voice attacks, which produces more fear. The loop is the [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] mechanism applied to high-stakes work.

The result, observed from outside, is procrastination. The result, observed from inside, is dread. The dread is real. The cause of the dread is the importance-loading, not a character flaw.

## What This Means for the Coach

Coaches who try to address procrastination at the behavior level — habit-stacks, time-blocking, accountability — operate on the wrong layer. The behavior is downstream of the felt state. The felt state is downstream of the importance-loading. The coaching work is at the importance layer.

The intervention is [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] in the variant that explicitly removes the importance. "What if the only requirement was that you had fun?" The redirect drops the load. The body releases the dread. The work becomes available.

The intervention requires the person to accept that the work might be of lower quality without the importance. Most people resist this on principle. The move is to compare two scenarios: low-quality work that gets done and iterated, versus high-quality work that does not exist. The first beats the second on every dimension that matters in the long run.

## Why People Resist Letting the Importance Go

The defense of the importance is usually defended as "but this really does matter." Joe's response is structural: yes, the outcome may matter. But making the work feel important does not produce the outcome. It produces the procrastination. The importance is the mechanism preventing the outcome, not delivering it.

> "Now I'm going to make you really uncomfortable. Prove to all of us that it's important, your website."
>
> "If I don't do it, I will be working for somebody else and that I don't wanna do that anymore. And I won't get to where I want to be, and I won't be able to give my kids the lifestyle that I want them to have."
>
> "You are stuck."
>
> "I know."
>
> "It's so important that it makes you stuck." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The compression — "it's so important that it makes you stuck" — is the principle stated in the form that exposes the cost. The importance the person is defending is the very thing preventing the outcome the importance is supposed to protect. See [[prove-why-its-important]] for the move that surfaces this in coaching.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not say "nothing should be important." It does not say "high standards are the problem." It says: the felt experience of importance, loaded onto a task in the moment of engaging with that task, produces dread that produces procrastination.

High standards are compatible with the unloaded engagement. The standards become parameters of the craft. The same standards, loaded as identity-defining stakes in the moment, produce paralysis.

The principle also does not apply to tasks where genuine urgency is the issue. A deadline an hour away is not "loaded importance" in the relevant sense — it is real urgency, and the body's response to it is appropriate. The principle applies to chronic procrastination on work that has no real time pressure, where the importance is internally constructed rather than externally given.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They cannot start the work they care about most while flowing through work they care about less
- They feel dread at the thought of sitting down to a specific task and the dread cannot be explained by the task's actual difficulty
- They have been "about to start" the same project for months or years
- They feel a small relief — almost a release — when imagining the work as something they could just have fun with
- Their body shows lightness when imagining doing the work for someone else and heaviness when imagining doing it for themselves

## Tone

Direct and slightly dry. Joe delivers this principle without sentimentality. The mechanism is mechanical — importance produces dread produces procrastination — and the compression "you procrastinate on things that feel like shit" is matter-of-fact. The lightness comes from the contrast with how heavy the person had been making it. Joe makes it feel ordinary.
