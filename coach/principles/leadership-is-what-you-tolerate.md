# Leadership Is What You Put Up With (And What You Don't)

## The Principle

The leadership a person exhibits is visible in two specific places: what they will not tolerate, and what they walk away from. Both are forms of leadership — and both forms are operating at the same time, in everyone. The leader without a formal position leads through what they refuse to accept and what they refuse to participate in. The leader with a formal position leads through what they require of their team and what they refuse to overlook.

The principle has two readings, both true, that together describe the leadership-as-integrity phenomenon.

## Reading One: What The Formal Leader Will Not Tolerate

In the leadership-as-formal-role frame, what you put up with from your team is the quality of your leadership. If you tolerate defensiveness, defensiveness is your culture. If you tolerate substandard performance, substandard performance is your standard. The leadership is not in what you say — it is in what you accept.

"As a leader, what you put up with is the quality of your leadership. I will not put up with defensiveness, or I will not put up with this level of performance, or whatever." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The diagnostic for a leader: walk through your team and list what is currently operating that you wish were not. Each item on the list is either something you will address, or something you are tolerating. The toleration IS the message to the team about what is acceptable. The team learns the actual standards from what is tolerated, not from what is declared.

This is why "we value X" statements that are not paired with refusing-to-tolerate-not-X are inert. The team reads the toleration as the true value. The leader's job is to align the two — make the declared standards and the tolerated standards match.

## Reading Two: What The Person On The Cliff Will Not Tolerate For Themselves

A second reading: the leadership is also in what YOU will not tolerate for yourself. In a self-organized group on a cliff with poor jumping conditions, one person says "what do you guys think, the conditions are terrible." Another person says "this doesn't work for me. I'm going down." The second person is the leader. Their leadership is enacted through what they will not put up with — for themselves, in this moment, with no claim on what anyone else should do.

"It can be taken a different way, which is the way you're describing it, as the person on the cliff saying, this isn't right, I'm walking away, I'm not putting up with this. When I thought about that, that just kind of blew my mind, that it's saying that I am, I have like an internal integrity, which by the way, the studies show that that internal integrity or that moral compass is one of the things that we look for in our leaders that make for great leaders. And that's another way of saying like what you'll put up with. It's not just performance, not just accountability." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

This reading reframes leadership-as-tolerance from a position-bound concept to an everyone-already-operating-this concept (see [[leadership-is-for-everyone]]). What you will not put up with for yourself is showing the people around you what they could refuse too. The "I'm going down" is leadership by example. The person who refuses the bad jump is leading the people who were about to follow into it.

The leadership in this reading is independent of any formal authority. It is the integrity-driven move to act in alignment with what you actually know is right for you, in front of others, with no claim that they must follow. The acting is the lead. The integrity is the source.

## The Integration: Internal Integrity As The Source

Both readings point to the same underlying source: internal integrity. The formal leader's "I will not tolerate defensiveness" and the cliff-walker's "this doesn't work for me" are both expressions of internal integrity meeting the situation. The integrity is what holds the line in both cases. The line is different (one is about the team, one is about the self), but the source is the same.

This is why integrity shows up in the leadership research as a top predictor of leadership effectiveness. The integrity is the thing that produces both kinds of refusal-to-tolerate. The people being led can tell the difference between a leader who has an internal compass and one who does not. The internal compass produces the actions; the actions get followed because they read as principled rather than arbitrary.

The principle clarifies the conviction in [[leadership-is-following-your-conviction]]. The conviction has to be lived in what gets refused, not just in what gets pursued. The leader who pursues a vision while tolerating things that contradict it has a vision but not a conviction. The conviction is verified by the refusal of what does not align.

## What This Is Not

**Not "be intolerant."** The principle is not a prescription to be hard on everything. The refusal-to-tolerate operates against things that violate the leader's integrity — not against ordinary friction or honest mistakes. A leader who refuses to tolerate everything has lost the calibration that makes the refusals meaningful. Selectivity is part of the integrity.

**Not "every walk-away is leadership."** The principle is not validating every refusal as a leadership move. The cliff-walker's refusal is leadership because it comes from internal integrity. A refusal driven by fear, contempt, or score-keeping is not. The diagnostic is whether the refusal is sourced from "this does not work for me" (integrity) or from "I'm going to make them pay" (reaction). The first leads. The second wounds.

**Not a contract with the followed.** The cliff-walker's leadership move makes no claim on the others. They are not saying "you should come down with me." They are saying "I am going down." The leadership operates by example, not by demand. The principle that this is leadership does not give the cliff-walker the right to require others to follow.

## How To Use The Principle As Diagnostic

For the person trying to recognize their own leadership: look at what you have refused to tolerate this week. Each refusal — for yourself or from others — is a leadership act. List them. The pattern of refusals tells you what your leadership has actually been about, regardless of what you say it is about.

For the formal leader: list what you wish were different in your team. For each item, ask "is this something I have made clear is not acceptable, or is this something I have been tolerating?" The items in the second category are your leadership — by default. You are the author of the tolerations even if you did not explicitly choose them.

For the person who is unsure they have any leadership: notice the things you refuse to participate in. The integrity that produces those refusals IS your leadership. The recognition is enough — once it is conscious, the leadership can be more deliberate.

## The Connection To Clarity And Principles

The leader who can articulate what they will not tolerate has done specific work. They have located their values. They have made them legible enough to themselves to be applicable in the moment. Without this work, the refusals happen reactively rather than principled — sometimes catching the thing that violates the integrity, sometimes missing it, sometimes refusing things that do not actually violate it.

The clarification work is upstream of clean tolerance-based leadership. See [[principles-free-you]]. The internal principles are what give the refusal-of-toleration its consistency and its readability to the people watching. Without them, the leader's "I will not tolerate this" reads as moody or arbitrary, and the team cannot orient around it.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the team behaviors they complain about are behaviors they have been tolerating
- The reframe of "I'm not really a leader" into "look at what I refuse to participate in — that's my leadership"
- The reorientation of the cliff-walker's "I'm going down" as a leadership act rather than a non-participation
- The work of articulating what they will and will not tolerate from themselves and from others
- The discomfort of seeing how much they have tolerated without recognizing they were doing it
