# Kids Respond to Your Emotional Presence, Not Your Words

## The Principle

Small children do not respond to your sentences. They respond to your nervous system. The reasoning, the logic, the lecture, the explanation — the child is not processing any of it the way the parent thinks. The child is reading the parent's emotional field and matching it. The words are noise around the signal. The signal is the parent's actual state.

"They're not responding to the words, they're responding to like — and that was the amazing thing, is to watch kids just respond to how you are emotionally is — it's amazing. Like, you get wound up, they get wound up. You calm down, they calm down." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This is true for infants and is mostly still true until about 8, when intellect comes online enough to also pick up on the words. Imaginary thinking does not come online until around then. Theoretical thought does not come online until 15 or 16. So for the entire first decade of the child's life, the primary communication channel between parent and child is the parent's emotional presence, not the parent's speech.

## The Failure Mode

A parent who does not know this talks to their 2-year-old like a 25-year-old. "Okay, Johnny, well, you shouldn't do this and you should do this." The 2-year-old has no prefrontal cortex to process this. The words land on a child whose only available channel is the parent's felt state. What the child actually receives is whatever emotional charge the parent is carrying while they are explaining. If the parent is frustrated, the child receives frustration. If the parent is exasperated, the child receives exasperation. The lesson the words were supposed to teach is not what gets installed; the felt state is what gets installed.

"You'll see these parents and they'll be like, okay, Johnny, well, you shouldn't do this and you should do this. And the kid's 2 years old, but the kid is just 100% responding to the emotional presence of their parent." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The parent is having one conversation. The child is having a different one. The parent leaves convinced they have explained the rule. The child leaves having learned that the parent is unsafe when the rule is not followed.

## The Implication

The corrective is not to talk less, exactly. It is to recognize that the felt state is the message and to do the work of regulating the felt state before issuing the message. A parent who is wound up cannot effectively communicate with a 2-year-old by adopting calm words. The child will read the wound-up state through the calm words. The only way to communicate calm to the child is to actually be calm.

This is why [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]] is the inevitable companion principle. The parent cannot hide their nervous system from the child. The work the parent has done or has not done on themselves is what the child receives, regardless of what the parent says.

## The Experimental Proof: Logic Calms The Parent, and the Calm Is What The Kid Reads

A specific observation Joe made by running the experiment several times. The standard parenting scene: the kid is dysregulated, the parent shifts into logic mode — "okay, well, let me logically explain why you shouldn't cross the street" — and the kid calms down. Most parents conclude that the logic worked. The actual mechanism is different.

What is happening: when the parent shifts into logic mode, the parent themselves becomes more grounded. The voice gets quieter. The pace slows. The body settles. The parent's nervous system, which had been dysregulated by the kid's dysregulation, returns to baseline. The kid is reading the parent's baseline state and calming to match.

Joe tested this. He talked to his kids about the sun and the stars, or anything content-irrelevant, at the same regulation quality he would have used to deliver the logical explanation. The kid settled the same way.

"I actually did that experiment a couple times. They could literally be talking about the sun and the stars, and if they hit that same quality of emotion and nervous system regulation, the kid, you'll see the kid settle as well." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The implication is significant. The intellectual content of what the parent says when delivering logic is irrelevant. The kid is not learning anything about street-crossing from the logical explanation. The kid is learning that the parent has now become a stable nervous system to calibrate to. The lesson the parent thinks they are teaching is invisible to the kid. The lesson the kid is actually receiving is "this is what calm looks like in this room."

This experiment is the cleanest proof of the principle. The parent who recognizes it has the option to skip the logic and go directly to the regulation. Talk to the kid about anything, or about nothing, while genuinely being in the regulated state. The kid will calm.

## Co-regulation Runs Both Ways

The same channel that lets the parent's state install in the child lets the child's state install in the parent — and lets the child come find the parent's state when it is missing.

Joe's example: when Una was small, if Joe was not present, she would walk over to his lap, grab his face, and physically turn it toward her. Sometimes she would say "I love you, Daddy." The grab was not random. She was saying, in body language, "our field is not coherent right now. Our field is not relaxed right now. My job as a kid is to get us back into connection."

"I see that with kids all the time. Like they're constantly looking, they're like, they feel safe when there's that connection between them and their parents. And if they don't have it, they freak out a little bit and then need to regulate through emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The child is not passive in this. The child is constantly working to maintain the emotional coherence of the field with the parent. The child knows when the field is broken. The child has tools to repair it (touch, demand for eye contact, declarations of love). The parent who registers this is being given information by the child about when the parent has drifted from presence.

## Where This Connects to Other Principles

This is the family-scale version of [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]]. The leader's nervous system is the team's nervous system. The parent's nervous system is the family's nervous system. Whatever the parent is carrying propagates outward. The principle scales up (leaders, organizations) and down (parent-child, romantic partner).

It is also a specific application of [[head-heart-gut]]: the child is operating primarily from the heart and gut centers. The head center is not yet online. The parent who is operating from the head center is trying to communicate on a channel the child cannot receive.

## The Parenting Application

The practical move that follows from this:

1. **Before issuing words, register your own state.** What are you actually feeling right now? If you are frustrated, the child is receiving frustration regardless of how careful your words are. If you are scared, the child is receiving fear regardless of how calmly you ask them to brush their teeth.
2. **Do the regulation first, then engage.** This may mean walking out of the room for a moment, breathing, or releasing the emotion somewhere safe. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the related practice. The point is to be in a state you actually want your child to absorb before you act as if absorbing it is on the table.
3. **Recognize when the child is asking for connection.** The tantrum, the demand for attention, the grab of your face — these are often signals that the child has registered a break in the field and is asking you to come back to it.
4. **Stop trying to reason with a state-mismatch.** If the child is dysregulated, more words will not help. Presence with whatever they are feeling will. See [[hand-in-hand-parenting]] for the practice of stay-listening, which is built on this principle.

## What This Looks Like When It Works

A parent who internalizes this stops mistaking the conversation they are having with their child for the only conversation in the room. They register their own state. They regulate when needed. They let the words follow rather than lead. They learn to recognize when the child is asking for emotional contact rather than instruction.

A child raised by a parent who works this way develops the felt sense that the field can be repaired. The child develops the capacity to ask for connection rather than to act it out. The child develops co-regulation skills the parent will not have had to explicitly teach.

A child raised without this — by parents who imagine their words are the medium — develops the felt sense that the words mean one thing and the felt reality means another. The child learns to mistrust either the words or themselves. Many adults are still untangling this exact thing in their own therapy.

## The Principle Operates Beyond Early Childhood

The principle is most acute with infants and young children, but it does not stop operating when the prefrontal cortex comes online. Teenagers and adults also calibrate to the felt state of those around them, regardless of the words. The channel is bidirectional — the parent transmits their state to the kid, and the kid transmits their state right back, and both parties read each other's actual condition through felt sense regardless of any verbal narrative.

This is why teenagers in a school can reliably read which of their peers come from homes where the parents are fighting. The kid's behavior at school is downstream of the felt environment at home, and a peer with any developed sensitivity can read it. Esme describes this directly:

"Whenever the parents are fighting, you can see it in the kids. How clear is it for you to be able to walk around your school and know whose parents have been like where there's like home strife and has been for like more than a couple months? I would say it's incredibly clear. And sometimes I don't notice it right away, and then I'll meet the parents, I'm like, ah, that's why." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The kid carries the home into the school. The school behavior is a faithful transmission of the home conditions, regardless of what the kid says about home. See [[criticism-posture-vs-shame-posture]] for the somatic forms this transmission takes, and [[esme-basketball-teammate-attention-seeking]] for the specific example of a teen reading her teammate's home situation through the teammate's behavior.

For parents, the implication is that the home transmission shows up in places far from home — at school, on teams, in friendships. The kid is carrying the parent's state into every social context. The home is not contained inside the house.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent who is exhausted because their words are not landing and they are repeating themselves louder
- A parent whose child is "acting out" and the parent is escalating to bigger consequences
- A parent who has been told they are not present and is confused because they are saying the right things
- A new parent confronting that their toddler does not respond to reasoning
- A parent realizing their child is calibrating to their actual state and not to their performance of being calm

## Tone

Direct and slightly humorous. Joe says "their brain isn't online" and "they're just full feeling machines, they're like in a dream state." The tone is matter-of-fact, not pious. The principle is a statement of how nervous systems work between people, not a moral injunction about how to parent. Use the same tone: explanatory, plain, with room for the listener to recognize the situation in their own life.
