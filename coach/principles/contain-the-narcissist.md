# Contain the Narcissist (Boundaries Are the Tool, Not VIEW)

## The Principle

For most people, [[view]] is the tool that produces connection. Vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, and wonder bring most people into relationship. For the roughly 5% of the population whose felt machinery is shut down (see [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]]), VIEW does not produce connection. It produces exploitation. The vulnerability becomes the leverage point. The wonder gets weaponized against the wondering person.

With this population, boundaries are the tool. Containment is what lands.

"View really doesn't have much of a place with somebody who's like very far out there on the narcissistic scale. It's like you can do it for your benefit and for your love and joy, but if you're really, really far out there, then boundaries are really the tool that you want to be able to use." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

## Why Containment Is What the Narcissist Actually Needs

Narcissistic behaviors function in the system as containment-tests. The yelling, the manipulation, the passive aggression — these are not designed to win the way they appear. They are designed to find out whether the environment will hold. If the behavior works (people get scared, comply, get smaller), the system has not been contained, and the behavior escalates. If the behavior does not work (the boundary holds, the other person stays in their own love), the system has been contained, and the nervous system can finally rest.

"You want to make sure that you can contain the person, that you're in love with your own anger enough that their anger doesn't affect you, that you're in love with your own passive aggression enough that their passive aggressiveness doesn't affect you. And so that you can actually contain them because without that containment, they can never feel safe." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The narcissist whose behavior keeps working is not being helped by the people enabling the behavior. They are being kept in a state where their system cannot find rest. The boundary that does not bend is, paradoxically, the most loving thing available — though it is rarely received as love in the moment.

## What "Loving Your Own Anger" Means Here

The containment Joe describes is not a posture or a technique. It is a state. You can hold a clean boundary with a narcissist only if their anger does not destabilize you, their passive aggression does not pull you off your center, their manipulation does not get inside.

The work that makes this possible is not done in the relationship. It is done with your own anger, your own passive aggression, your own manipulation. The more in love with your own version of each of these you are, the less the other person's version has power over you. See [[every-trigger-with-your-kid-is-projection]] for the same mechanism in a different relationship. The work on the trigger is the work on the relationship.

A person who has done this work can be in the same room as a narcissist, hold the boundary, not collapse, not perform virtue, not need the narcissist to be different — and that is the field that contains.

## Why VIEW Becomes Technique With a Narcissist

When you use VIEW with someone whose machinery cannot receive it, the use itself converts the practice into a strategy. The state of VIEW is impartial; using VIEW to get a result is partial. The moment you are using vulnerability to soften the parent, you are no longer being vulnerable; you are deploying vulnerability as a tool. See [[view-as-technique]].

"When people are using view, they drop... like using view in itself is dropping, is becoming partial. So if you're using view as a way to get somewhere with the parent, then you're not actually in view because view can't be used particularly." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

This is not a special failure of VIEW with narcissists. VIEW-as-technique fails with anyone. The narcissist case makes it especially visible because the narcissist's apparatus is specifically tuned to detect and exploit any opening that vulnerability creates.

A person far out on the narcissistic spectrum will find the gap created by your performed vulnerability and use it. The vulnerability lands as ammunition rather than as connection.

"It's like someone who's really narcissistic, your vulnerability is something they'll exploit." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

## You Can Still Be in VIEW — Just Not at Them

The instruction is not "stop being vulnerable, impartial, empathic, or wondering." Those states are still available. The instruction is "don't direct those states at the narcissist as a strategy to change them."

You can be in VIEW with yourself in the room with a narcissist. You can be impartial with reality (impartial to the fact that you do not want to engage in this dynamic; impartial to the fact that you want to draw a boundary). You can be vulnerable about your own emotional experience. You can be in wonder about your own reactions.

You cannot be in VIEW directed at the narcissist with the intention of producing relational change. That use is no longer VIEW.

Brett's articulation of the move:

"What you're saying there is if you're using view as a tool to get somewhere with your parents... it's not a failure of the tool, it's that you're actually using it as a tool, which has pulled you out of view, which would actually be impartial, which might be impartial to the reality that I don't want to be engaging in the way that we're engaging right now, impartial to the reality that I want to draw a boundary." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3

## Separation Is a Valid Move

The principle does not require staying in the relationship at all. Containment can take the form of a clean separation.

"It's absolutely 100% totally wonderful and acceptable to just separate yourself from a narcissist, even if it's their parent. Like there's, there's nothing that says that we have to interact with narcissists to be good people or something like that. It's totally okay to say that relationship is toxic and I'm going to limit it. Whether that's all the way or just to limit it in such a way that works." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The full menu of containment options:

- Stay in the relationship and hold the boundary that does not bend.
- Limit the contexts and frequency of contact.
- Limit the modes (text but not in person; gatherings but not one-on-one).
- Separate entirely.

All of these are valid. The choice is calibrated to your capacity, the cost of the relationship, and the actual function of the contact in your life.

## Connection Is Still Possible

Limiting or ending contact does not require ending the internal relationship with love.

"You can say, oh, hey, Mom, narcissistic Mom, I don't want to hang out with you in these ways anymore. I'm only going to really interact with you in this way, or I'm not going to interact with you at all, and still have a sense of connection for that for the, for mom. And that is all about you, like your ability to connect, your ability to stay open-hearted towards a narcissist, your ability to contain them, your ability to draw boundaries. That's all great stuff for that you get to learn." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The internal love is separate from the external contact. Both can be tended independently. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] for the concern that often arrives after the separation has been made and the love work is still pending.

## What the Narcissist Reveals

The triggered moments with a narcissist are some of the highest-quality data available about your own patterns. The work has multiple dividends:

- The boundaries you learn to draw with them are boundaries you can draw anywhere.
- The capacity to stay in your own love through their storm is capacity you can deploy in any storm.
- The projections you see in yourself in their presence are projections that have probably been operating in many of your relationships.
- The compassion for them that arrives once the containment is in place is compassion you can extend to your own narcissistic places.

"Just because they're not capable of a human interaction that you want to have doesn't mean that they hold no use to you. It doesn't mean you can't love them. It doesn't mean you can't be in connection with them, even if you're separating yourself from them." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

## The Two Subtypes Joe Names

Joe distinguishes two forms of narcissistic behavior that respond to containment differently:

**The overt form** (yelling, anger, demanding): contained when the anger does not produce its intended effect. The boundary that does not move and the person who stays in their own state will land as containment.

**The malignant or passive form** (passive aggression): contained when the passive aggression does not work. The person sees through it, names it without judgment, does not take the bait, and does not become the manager of the unstated emotion. The unstated thing stops being a successful lever.

In both cases, the principle is the same: the behavior does not work. The system, finding the limit, can rest.

## Tone

Even and firm. Joe does not dramatize the narcissist as villain or perform pity for them. The teaching is clinical: here is what is happening in their system, here is what works with that system, here is what you get to learn by interacting with that system. The moralizing is absent on every side.
