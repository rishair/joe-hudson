# Every Identity Has Its Own Limits (Including the 'Good' Ones)

## The Principle

There is no such thing as a good identity that escapes the cost of being an identity. Every way you define yourself creates a corresponding blindness and a corresponding rigidity. "I am hardworking" prevents rest. "I am loving" prevents seeing where you are not loving. "I am vulnerable" prevents seeing where you are armored. "I am the egoless one" is one of the tightest identity grips available.

"Every identity has some component of it that limits us." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

"Every way that we define ourselves has its own limitations." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## Why This Matters

The common trap in self-work: realize that one identity is causing problems, then try to replace it with a "better" one. The new identity has its own limits. The replacement project never finishes. Eventually the person has accumulated a stack of supposedly enlightened identities, each of which is also a cage.

The principle blocks the substitution move. The work is not to find the right identity. The work is to be flexible enough that identity can be set down when it stops serving.

"Even those identities create some limitations. So caretakers are often identified as the person who cares or altruistic. And so every identity has some component of it that limits us." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## The Specific Examples

**Identity: "I am loving."** Limit: cannot see where I am not loving. Filters reality to confirm the identity. Misses the feedback that would allow actual growth in loving.

**Identity: "I am vulnerable / in VIEW."** Limit: cannot see where I have dropped out of vulnerability or VIEW. The identity becomes the new defense.

**Identity: "I am not vulnerable enough."** Limit: cannot see the evidence that I am vulnerable or moving toward vulnerability. Negativity bias around the identified deficit.

**Identity: "I am a hard worker."** Limit: cannot rest, cannot be lazy when laziness is what is called for, cannot love the lazy part of self.

**Identity: "I am the one who cares for others."** Limit: cannot stop caretaking. Cannot let others struggle when struggle is what they need. Resentment builds because the identity is enforced by self.

**Identity: "I am egoless / I have transcended ego."** Limit: rigid identification with non-identification. Self-righteousness about absence of self. Often a pain in the ass.

"They're a bit of a pain in the ass, aren't they? Having been one myself." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

## The Filter Effect

A particularly costly form of the limit: once an identity is in place, perception is filtered to confirm it.

"If I take on the identity that I'm a vulnerable person, I'm loving, I'm in VIEW, to the extent that that becomes an identity, then I also start filtering my reality to see that it is true and to see less of the evidence to the contrary." -- Brett Kistler, paraphrased by Joe and affirmed, Seeing Identity for What It Is

This is the standard mechanism of confirmation bias applied to self-perception. The data that would let the person update gets filtered out. The identity remains intact because the system is protecting it from disconfirming evidence.

The same applies to negative identities. "I am not enough" filters out evidence of enoughness. "I am broken" filters out evidence of intactness. The filter does not care about the valence of the identity. It just defends whatever has been installed.

## The Universal Identity Trap

This principle scales all the way up. As people work through layers of identity, they reach states that feel like the "real" answer: oneness, nothingness, awareness itself.

"At some point you will identify -- all those identities also become their own roadblocks. Like the identity of oneness has its own limitations. At some point you're like, 'oh, I'm oneness and I'm everything and I'm nothing.' And to be able to see both aspects of that and then more fluidity, more transparency [is what serves]." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

The "I am pure awareness" identity has its own limits. So does "I am the witness." So does "I am everything." Each becomes the next rut to be seen through. See [[epiphany-becomes-rut]] for the related mechanism.

## What This Implies

The implication is *flexibility over content*. The question is not which identity is right. The question is whether you can hold any current identity loosely enough to set it down when it stops serving.

"It's not an idea of 'I'm going to have no identity' because there's no possibility of that. It's far more about finding the flexibility in the identity." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

The same identity can be useful and limiting depending on whether it is held tightly or loosely. Identifying as a loving partner is "a lot of beauty and awesomeness in that." It is also a thing that can be defended rigidly to the cost of actual love. The content is not the issue. The grip is.

## The Coaching Move

When a coachee announces a new identity they have reached -- "I am now [the loving one / the awakened one / the vulnerable one / the empowered one]" -- the coach's job is not to congratulate them and stabilize the identity. The coach's job is to invite them to notice the limit that this new identity is also producing.

The principle does not require this to be done critically. It can be done with warmth and humor. "Great. And now -- where does even that identity get in the way?"

This protects against the most common form of stuckness after a breakthrough: the breakthrough becomes the new cage.

## Connection to Loving All Parts

The deeper resolution: the rigidity of any identity is a refusal to love the opposite. "I am hardworking" rejects the lazy part. "I am loving" rejects the cold part. The flexibility comes from loving both sides, which lets the identification soften.

"Your identity is exactly the parts of yourself that you can't love. As you learn to love these parts, the identity becomes far more fluid. It becomes far more transparent. It starts to identify with oneness or nothingness or love or peace or inherent goodness. The identification becomes softer, more universal, more gentle, less rigid, more fluid." -- Joe Hudson, Seeing Identity for What It Is

See [[loving-all-parts]] for the practice. See [[transparent-identity-vs-rigid]] for what flexibility looks like in operation.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a coachee has had a breakthrough and is now identifying with the new state
- When someone is trying to swap an old identity for a "better" one
- When a person announces they have transcended ego, identity, the self, etc.
- When a positive self-image is preventing the person from seeing where they fall short of it
- When someone is using a spiritual or developmental concept (oneness, awareness, vulnerability, love) as a new rigid identity

## Tone

Joe holds this with a knowing humor. He has been the egoless one. He knows the trap. The tone is "I get it, and now let's notice the next thing." Not corrective. Not pious. Honest about the universal nature of the trap.
