# Follow Where the Client Points

## The Principle

The client picks the topic. Even when the coach sees other layers, other angles, and possibly more powerful entry points, the door the client opens is the door the work goes through. The coach who substitutes their own read for the client's chosen topic violates the client's agency, undercuts the work, and often misses the layer that the client's choice was actually pointing at.

This is a discipline, not a default. The coach who has seen a thousand sessions has many possible reads available in any given moment. The temptation to bring the "better" angle into the room is constant. The principle says: don't. The client said procrastination. The work is on procrastination, even if the coach can also see the money pattern, the codependency pattern, the inner-critic pattern. Those other layers can come into the work if the client opens those doors. They do not get imported by the coach.

> "I'm not going down that money route with him because he started by saying procrastination. So I'm following where he wants to go. And because he didn't say I have a money issue, I have a procrastination issue. And that's why we're going to go down this route." -- Joe Hudson, Overcoming Procrastination (Coaching Session Breakdown)

## Why the Coach's Better Read Doesn't Justify Switching

Several reasons the coach's superior diagnostic doesn't override the client's chosen topic:

**The client's topic is a chosen doorway.** Whatever else is going on, the topic the client chose is the topic their system is ready to look at right now. Other layers may be more central in some abstract sense; this one is more available. Available beats central. The work moves on what can be moved on.

**The substitution undercuts agency.** A coach who says "I hear procrastination but I think the real issue is your relationship to money" has just told the client that their self-knowledge is less accurate than the coach's. Even when the coach is right about the deeper layer, the move teaches the client that they cannot trust their own reading of what to bring. The cumulative effect over a coaching relationship is dependency on the coach's read instead of access to the client's own.

**The client's framing often contains the work.** The procrastination framing in the website case turned out to contain the money work, the codependency work, the perfectionism work, the critical-parent work, and the self-importance work. The session went into all of them — but through the door the client opened. The coach didn't have to substitute a "better" door because the door the client brought opened into the same house.

**The substitution often reflects the coach's preference, not the client's need.** The coach who keeps redirecting to "money" or "childhood" or "trauma" usually has a favored frame they bring to many sessions. The favored frame is the coach's, not the client's. The coach who follows where the client points has to keep doing the harder work of finding the angle in the client's chosen territory. This work is harder than running the coach's familiar play.

## What the Principle Doesn't Mean

It does not mean accepting the client's framing of why the problem exists. See [[dont-let-the-story-stand]] for the contrast. The client's topic is followed (procrastination); the client's story about the topic (fear of success and failure) is deconstructed. The two are different:

- **Topic:** the domain the work is happening in (procrastination, the relationship, the career move)
- **Story:** the explanation the client has assigned to the topic ("I'm scared of success," "my partner is the problem," "I haven't found my purpose")

The topic is followed. The story is not. The coach who confuses these will either bulldoze the client's chosen territory or get trapped inside the client's rehearsed narrative. The principle requires holding both lines simultaneously: stay in the territory the client chose; refuse the story they brought about the territory.

It also doesn't mean the work has to stay surface-level. Going deep into the chosen topic is allowed and encouraged. The principle is about the entry, not the depth. Once inside the topic, the work can go as deep as the client can hold. What it cannot do is start in a different room than the one the client walked into.

## The Edge Case: When the Client Opens a New Door

Sometimes mid-session the client opens a new door themselves. They came in with procrastination; ten minutes in they say "you know what, this is really about my marriage." The coach follows the new door because the client opened it. This is not the coach substituting; this is the client navigating.

The diagnostic for the difference: who introduced the new framing? If the coach says "let's actually look at your marriage," that's substitution. If the client says "this is really about my marriage," that's a new door the client opened, and the coach follows. The principle is about who chooses, not about whether the topic is allowed to evolve.

## What Following Looks Like in Practice

- The coach stays with the language the client used for the topic ("procrastination," not "money block")
- The coach takes the specific example the client offered (the niche website, not a hypothetical the coach finds more interesting)
- The coach asks for more specificity within the topic ("you procrastinate on what?") rather than redirecting laterally ("when did money first feel hard?")
- The coach lets adjacent layers surface only when the client surfaces them ("the perfectionism is also at play" — yes, follow that, because the client just opened it)
- The coach does not save their "better" angle for later in the session — they let it go entirely and trust that the client's chosen door will open into the same territory if it's relevant

## The Connection to Coaching as Self-Awareness, Not Technique

The principle is downstream of [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]]. A coach who has not done their own work tends to bring their own work into other people's sessions — the issue they have been working on becomes the issue they see in every client. The principle "follow where the client points" requires the coach to have enough self-awareness to notice their own preferred frames and not impose them.

The coach who is doing their own work in the background can hold many possible reads simultaneously and follow the client's choice without resentment. The coach who is not doing their own work needs the client's session to be about the issue the coach is unconsciously trying to work on. The principle reveals the coach's interior. Following well is a developmental capacity, not a technique.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not a refusal to bring expertise to the work. The coach has reads, has frameworks, has knowledge about what tends to be underneath certain presentations. The principle is about the entry door, not about whether the coach brings their reads into the work once they are inside.

It is not a permission slip for sycophancy. Following the client's chosen topic does not mean validating the client's wrong framing of the topic. The coach still does the deconstruction work. They just do it inside the territory the client chose.

It is not a denial that some redirects are necessary. If the client opens with "tell me how to be a better leader" and the coach senses that the actual material is a relational rupture the client is avoiding, sometimes the move is to name what's underneath. But the naming is offered as an observation ("I notice you're describing the work problem when there's something in the room that feels personal"), not as a substitution ("we're not going to talk about leadership today, we're going to talk about your relationship"). The client retains the choice.

## Tone

The principle is held quietly, as a professional discipline rather than as a moral position. Joe doesn't moralize about following the client; he just does it and notes it in passing during the breakdown. The discipline is part of the craft; the coach who has internalized it doesn't have to think about it.

In session, the following looks like ordinary conversation. The client says procrastination; the coach asks about procrastination. The mechanics are invisible because the principle is operating cleanly. The visible signature of the principle is the absence of redirects, not any positive move the coach makes.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The client opens with a specific topic and the coach has additional reads available
- The coach has preferred frames that tend to show up in many sessions
- The temptation to redirect is present in the coach's body
- The session is at the early entry-point stage where the topic is being established
- The coach has noticed they want to take the session somewhere the client didn't ask to go
