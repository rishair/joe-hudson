# The Meaning You Make of the Overwhelm Determines Whether It Builds or Destroys

## The Principle

Overwhelm is not in itself constructive or destructive. The meaning made of it determines which direction it runs. The same external situation produces empowerment in one person and disintegration in another, because the meaning each makes of the situation is different.

The same overwhelm that becomes a war of national solidarity in one country becomes a war of demoralization in the country on the other side of the same line. Same intensity. Different meaning. Opposite outcome.

"It's the meaning that you make of it. If somebody makes the meaning of it, 'I'm bad' or 'they're bad' in the overwhelm, then it's going to be destructive. And if somebody makes the meaning of it, 'I have an opportunity. Here's a great way for me to change. This is something for me to learn. There's a way for me to understand myself better. I look forward to my overwhelm' -- then if you make that meaning of it, it's far more likely to be productive." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## The Ukraine-Russia Example

Joe pairs Ukraine and Russia in the same war as a structural example of the principle. Both populations are overwhelmed. The meaning each makes of the overwhelm is different.

For Ukrainians, the meaning is: "We want to survive. We can survive. We are going to beat this off." The overwhelm becomes fuel for collective action, mutual aid, and a sense of purposeful struggle. Communities tie together. People who would have stayed apart find each other.

For Russian soldiers, the meaning is: "This is a stupid war. Why are we here? We're just fodder. We're being treated like shit, so we'll treat everything around us like shit." The overwhelm becomes corrosive. It destroys cohesion, destroys morale, destroys the person.

Same war. Two populations. Two opposite outcomes. The variable is meaning.

"In the Russian, the meaning is, this is a stupid war. Why are we here? We're just fodder. And yeah, we're getting treated like shit, so we're going to treat everything around us like shit." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## Meaning Is the Choice Point

The principle locates choice in a specific place. The person rarely has full control over whether overwhelm arrives. They have a lot of control over the meaning they make of it once it does. That meaning is the lever.

"It's the meaning that you make of the overwhelm, and that's where the choice is." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The meaning is not a story you tell after the fact. It is the frame inside which the experience is happening. The same physical sensations, the same external pressures, the same intensity in the body -- inside one frame they read as "I have to push through this terrible thing" and inside another they read as "this is where I get to learn something I could not have learned otherwise."

## The Self-Blame Trap

The single most common meaning-making error: "I am bad" or "I am broken" as the meaning of the overwhelm. This compounds rather than relieves. The overwhelm was already heavy. Adding "and it's because there is something wrong with me" doubles the load.

This is also why Joe is careful about the framing "you are creating your overwhelm." See [[dont-add-the-overwhelm-of-creating-the-overwhelm]]. The framing can be empowering when the person is ready to hold it. It can be devastating when the person is not. The same words land differently depending on where the person is.

The meaning that opens: "I have an opportunity. This is a way for me to change. This is something for me to learn about myself."

The meaning that closes: "I am bad. I am broken. This proves I cannot handle my life."

Both meanings are available in any given moment of overwhelm. The work is to notice which one is being made and, where possible, choose the one that opens.

## Why Meaning Has This Power

Meaning organizes the response of the body and the action of the person. Inside the meaning "this is fuel for me," the body activates resources. Inside the meaning "this is proof I'm broken," the body collapses or fights itself. The biological response follows the cognitive frame.

This is not positive thinking. It is not pretending the difficulty is not difficult. The Ukrainians did not pretend the war was not happening. Their bodies are doing the work the war requires. The meaning they hold makes the work possible. The Russian soldier holding "we are fodder" cannot mobilize the same resources, regardless of how much external support is given.

## Connection to Adversity-as-For-You

This principle is a special case of [[adversity-as-for-not-against]]. The same loss can be interpreted as random damage or as refinement. The same disappointment can be interpreted as proof of badness or as redirection. The events of life do not arrive with their meaning attached. The meaning is added by the person experiencing them.

The principle here applies the same insight to the specific case of overwhelm. The overwhelm is the adversity. The meaning made of the overwhelm determines whether it serves the person or breaks them.

## The Common-Enemy Caveat

Brett raises a useful objection: the Ukraine example works partly because there is a clear common enemy. Common enemies have always united groups. In business or family, there is often no common enemy, and people end up making each other the enemy instead.

This is true. It does not refute the principle. It clarifies the work. In the absence of a clear external enemy, the meaning has to be constructed deliberately rather than borrowed from the situation. "This difficulty is something we can grow through together" has to be chosen and re-chosen, because nothing in the situation is offering it for free.

In Ukraine, the meaning is partly given. In a marriage, the meaning has to be made. The principle is the same; the labor is different.

## The Coaching Implication

When someone presents overwhelm, ask: what meaning are you making of it?

Often the meaning is implicit and not yet articulated. Pull it into the open. "I'm bad" "I'm broken" "I can't handle this" "they're doing this to me" -- whatever the operating meaning is, name it. Once named, ask: is there another meaning available?

This is not asking the person to lie to themselves. It is asking them to notice that the meaning they are making is one of multiple meanings available, and that the choice of meaning is going to determine whether the overwhelm builds or destroys.

## Why Joe Is "Conscientious About This Kind of Thing"

Joe explicitly says he is not careful about font choices or website design. He is careful about meaning-making in overwhelm because it makes a huge difference in someone's life. The precision matters not for its own sake but because the wrong meaning can compound someone's suffering at exactly the moment they are most vulnerable.

The care here is the care of someone who has watched the same framing destroy a person on one day and free them on another.

## Tone

Careful, precise, slightly insistent. Joe uses an unusual degree of conscientiousness when working with the framing of overwhelm because he has seen the framing land in destructive ways. The tone communicates: this distinction is not a technicality. The wrong framing can hurt people.
