# Trying to Generate the Feeling Prevents the Feeling

## The Principle

When a person tries to push themselves into feeling an emotion, the emotion withdraws. The trying becomes a new layer of resistance. The harder the push, the less the emotion is available.

"Oftentimes, when we try, we're pushing the emotions away. And so there's a whole group of people that have the tendency to push the emotions away by trying. You'll see that in the emotional inquiry work where the job is to be in wonder and curiosity about some emotional experience, and they'll try so hard and everything goes away." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The mechanism: emotions arrive in the body when the body is permitting them. Trying is a posture of gripping. Gripping is the body's posture for not permitting. So trying to feel an emotion produces, in the body, the precise conditions in which the emotion cannot land.

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

In most domains, more effort produces more result. If you try harder at math, you solve more problems. If you try harder at running, you cover more distance. If you try harder at communication, you get better outcomes (sometimes).

The emotional domain inverts this. The reason: emotions are not produced by the self. They arise in the self. The self's job is to permit, not to create. Effort in the relevant sense is trying to do the producing. The producing was never going to be the self's role. So the effort is applied to a task that does not exist, and meanwhile it disables the actual function (permitting).

This is the same architectural principle as [[undoing-not-doing]]. The work is to release a constraint, not to produce a result. Effort applied to releasing is paradoxical: the effort itself is part of what is being released.

## The Inquiry-Practice Application

The principle is especially important in [[emotional-inquiry]] practice. The practice asks the person to bring wonder and curiosity to an emotion. If the person mistakes this for an instruction to make wonder and curiosity happen, the practice collapses.

The instruction is to be in wonder and curiosity, which is a permitting move. It is not to perform wonder and curiosity, which is a producing move. A person who is producing wonder is not in wonder. A person who is in wonder is not producing it. The difference is the body's gear.

When a coach sees inquiry collapse for a client, the most likely culprit is that the person has begun trying. The intervention is not to push harder but to back off. "Don't try. Don't manufacture anything. Just let what is there be there." See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] for what the trying looks like in session.

## The Body Diagnostic

The body knows the difference between trying and permitting. The diagnostic signal:

- **Trying:** A gripping in the shoulders, jaw, or belly. A forward-leaning quality. A sense of effort in the wrong place. A subtle holding of breath.
- **Permitting:** A settling. A slight downward release in the shoulders. A softening of the breath. A receptive quality in the chest or belly.

If the body is in trying mode, the practice has slid out of permitting. The move is to notice the trying, soften, and re-enter the permitting mode. This is not done by trying to soften (the same problem). It is done by noticing the trying and letting it relax on its own when seen.

## The Pattern This Trap Catches

Joe identifies a specific population that is most susceptible to this trap: people who use effort to solve everything else in their life. They are competent. They achieve. They push and produce. They bring the same gear to the emotional work.

The gear that works for everything else does not work here. The realization is sometimes painful for the person who has built their identity around capability. The medicine is the same as for most spiritual progressions: the strengths that produced your success in one domain are the obstacles to your progress in another. The willingness to bring less rather than more is what unlocks the territory.

See [[dont-go-after-every-emotion-at-once]] for the related warning to high-effort practitioners. The two principles share a common message: in the emotional domain, the rate-limiting step is not effort.

## The Specific Failure Mode in Coaching

A coach who has not internalized this principle can produce the failure mode in clients by accident. The coach asks the person to feel something. The person tries. The emotion goes away. The coach interprets the disappearance as resistance and pushes harder. The person tries harder. The emotion stays gone.

The skilled coaching move is the opposite: notice the trying, name it gently, invite less rather than more. "You don't have to manufacture anything. You don't have to try. Just as it is."

See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] for the read that surfaces this in session, and Joe's specific intervention language.

## The Application to Calling Up a Missed Emotion

The principle is especially relevant when someone wants to access an emotion that arrived earlier and is now gone. The intuitive move is to try harder to feel it. The intuitive move is exactly the wrong gear.

The correct move is to either:

1. Wait for the emotion to return (see [[unwelcomed-emotions-return-until-felt]]). It will.
2. Hold the memory of when you felt it most completely, and let whatever arises be enough (see [[hold-the-memory-of-when-you-felt-it-most]]).

Both moves replace trying with receiving. Both work better than trying.

## Why This Is Not Permission to Slack

The principle is not "don't engage with emotional work." It is "engage with the right gear." The engagement is real. The practice is real. The capacity for full feeling does develop through sustained practice. What does not work is bringing the wrong gear.

Sincerity is required (see [[sincerity-is-the-prerequisite]]). The body has to actually be willing. What is not required, and what actively interferes, is effort in the producing sense.

The distinction can be felt: a person who is sincere but not trying is leaning in with their attention while their body is open. A person who is trying is leaning in with their attention while their body is bracing. The first works. The second does not.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They are working hard on emotional inquiry and getting nowhere
- The emotions seem to evaporate when they pay direct attention to them
- They feel frustrated that the practice is not producing the results they want
- They are bringing the strategies that work in their professional life to their inner life and finding them counterproductive
- They notice that emotions arise most easily when they are not trying
