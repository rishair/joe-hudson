# If You're Doing an Emotion At Someone, You're Not Really Feeling It

## The Principle

When an emotion is aimed at another person to change their behavior — guilting with sadness, controlling with anger, controlling with fear, love-bombing with love — the person aiming the emotion is not actually feeling it. They are using its form to do work on someone else, which is the same gesture as not letting it move through themselves.

The manipulation of the other person is therefore also a self-manipulation. The emotion does not get felt. So the emotion keeps coming back.

"That's a beautiful point... Someone's guilting you with sadness — they're not actually feeling their sadness. So they want you to feel it for them. They want you to feel the fear for them. But it also means that they're not fully feeling it, because if they were fully feeling it, they wouldn't be trying to get it out of their system onto you." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3—Ambition, Narcissistic Parents, Addiction to Stress, Parenting Emotional

The original framing comes from Brett: "If you are using them to manipulate, you are essentially self-manipulating your experience of the emotion. Which is one reason why it might just continue showing up in your life. In the same way, without really moving and transforming."

## The Recurrence Diagnostic

The most useful operational form: if an emotion keeps showing up in the same form, with the same flavor, in the same situations, that is evidence the emotion is not being fully felt. The recurrence is the diagnostic.

The mechanism: emotions felt cleanly move through and transform. An emotion that is being used as a tool to change someone else is not moving. So the body keeps presenting it. The person believes they are "feeling sadness all the time" but actually they have been using sadness as a lever — and the body has been waiting for the sadness to actually get felt.

This is the same mechanism as [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]], read from the other direction. The leaks are the evidence. The recurrence is the evidence. Both point to the same thing: the emotion has not been allowed to move through the body that owns it.

## The Test Across Emotions

The principle applies to every emotion, not just anger:

- **Sadness at someone** (guilt-tripping). The sadness keeps coming back because the guilt-tripping is not the same as feeling sadness.
- **Fear at someone** ("I won't be safe if you do that"). The fear stays chronic because deploying fear is not the same as feeling fear.
- **Anger at someone** (controlling, attacking). The anger keeps recurring because aiming it is not the same as feeling it. See [[recycling-anger-without-clarity]].
- **Love at someone** (love-bombing, ingratiation). The love-as-strategy is not real love and produces neither the felt experience of loving nor the connection that would come from it. See [[loving-to-transform]].

The bright line: am I directing this emotion at someone with the intent (subconscious or conscious) to produce a change in them? If yes, I am not feeling the emotion. I am using it.

## The Self-Test

A clean somatic test, restated from the broader teaching: if the other person did not change their behavior at all in response to what I am doing, would I still be okay? If the answer is no, the emotion is being aimed at the person rather than felt by the self.

"If you look and you're like, oh, if that person doesn't change their behavior at all, am I still okay? Then if the answer is no, then you know you're in that place of doing it at somebody." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The need for the other person's behavior to change is the signal. The unfelt emotion is using the other person's behavior as the lever it expects to move it.

## Love at Someone Is Not Love

A particularly important corollary, because love is the emotion most commonly assumed to be exempt from this principle: love directed at someone to get something is not love.

"If it's love at somebody, it's not love. There's that. But there's this thing that looks like love at somebody that is definitely like, I'm going to love you up so that you love me back, or I'm going to love you up so that I can feel like a sense of connection that you don't particularly want to give me, or I'm going to endear myself with you... all that stuff that you can use love for, but then it's not really love, but it looks like love, and it might feel like love a little bit, but not — it isn't love." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The reason love bombing produces the same hollow recurrence as the other forms of emotion-at: the person is not actually loving. They are deploying the form of love as a tool. The actual experience of loving has not happened. So the wanting-to-love keeps presenting itself, with all the energy and none of the fulfillment.

See [[love-as-deep-welcoming]] for what love actually is when it is being felt rather than deployed.

## Why This Reframe Matters

The standard frame for "don't do emotion at people" is moral: it is wrong because it hurts the other person. That frame is true but incomplete. This principle adds a self-interested motivation that lands differently for some people: it is also failing you. The emotion you keep wanting to feel is the one you keep deploying instead of feeling. The cycle continues until the deploying stops.

For coachees who are resistant to "you are hurting your partner" but more open to "you are stuck in this pattern because you have not yet felt the underlying emotion," this principle is the gateway. The intervention is not about the other person at all. The intervention is about the self that has been using the other person as a destination for what wanted to be felt.

## What the Pattern Looks Like in Practice

- The person who is "always anxious" and brings everyone they meet into their anxiety. The anxiety persists because it is being externalized instead of felt.
- The chronic guilt-tripper whose sadness never resolves. The sadness has been the tool, not the experience.
- The frequently angry person whose anger does not produce clarity (see [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]]). The anger has been directed outward, not metabolized.
- The serial love bomber whose connection-attempts fail. The love-form has been deployed; the love itself has not been felt.
- The person who recruits others into their fear ("don't you think this is scary?"). The recruitment is the avoidance of feeling their own fear.

The diagnostic in all cases: the same emotion keeps showing up. That is the recurrence signal.

## The Path Out

Once the diagnostic is recognized, the prescription is the same as for [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]]: feel the emotion by yourself first. Not at the person. Not with the person as the destination. By yourself, in your body, until the body's report of "I have not felt this" stops being the dominant signal.

The companion practice: [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. The companion principle: [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] (which states the ethical version of this principle; this article is the self-interested version).

When the emotion has actually been felt, the conversation with the other person — if there still needs to be one — comes from clarity, not from need-to-discharge. The other person's response is no longer the thing the emotion is waiting on.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, almost surprised. Joe receives Brett's framing as a fresh insight ("That's a beautiful point") and treats the principle as something newly clarified. The teaching does not moralize. It points at a structural fact: the manipulation does not work for you either. The person aiming the emotion at someone else is not enjoying the secret victory the behavior implies. They are stuck in the cycle the behavior creates.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents a recurring emotional state (chronic anxiety, chronic sadness, chronic anger) and the discussion reveals the emotion is being used to manage other people.
- When the coachee reports being upset and looks for the other person's reaction as the resolution.
- When a partner has been guilt-tripping or anger-controlling and the coachee asks "why does this keep happening?"
- When someone describes their love-bombing pattern and asks why the connection it produces never feels real.
