# A Good Decision Is How You Make It, Not Whether the Outcome Was What You Wanted

## The Principle

A decision is not made good or bad by its outcome. Outcomes contain too much randomness, too much factor that was never available to the decider at the moment of choosing. The quality of a decision lives in how the decision was made: from what state, by what process, with what presence.

The standard frame: "Good decision" means I got the result I wanted. Bad decision means I didn't. This frame is wrong both descriptively (it doesn't fit how good deciders actually evaluate their record) and prescriptively (it makes you a worse decider over time).

"Most people will use that the way that they think they're making good decisions. They're thinking, oh, okay, here's a good decision. It's a decision where I avoided the consequences. I made the decision that I avoided the consequences and I got the reaction that I wanted to get, that I got the thing that I wanted to get. So a good decision in a business or an investment is that the investment went well, right? But my experience as a venture capitalist was that my best investment was the one that I made like not so good money in. And the worst investment was where I made the most money." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## The Venture Capital Test

The cleanest demonstration: ask any experienced VC about their best and worst investments. Their best by outcome and their best by process diverge regularly. A well-run company can fail because the market timing was off. A poorly-run company can succeed because something stochastic broke its way.

"I can't tell you how many VCs have felt that same way where they have like, this was a great investment, but it didn't make money. And this was, this was like, God, it was a shit show, but it made a ton of money." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

The VC who measures by outcomes alone cannot replicate the wins. The VC who measures by quality of decision can: they know what to look for next time, regardless of how that particular investment ended up.

## The Portfolio Frame

The principle becomes operational when decisions are seen as a portfolio rather than as discrete events. See [[decisions-as-portfolio]].

"If I made the kind of decision that I made with the company that didn't do so well 10 times, my portfolio would be freaking amazing. If I did 10 decisions with the one that did well 10 times, I would be bankrupt." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

A single decision's outcome contains too much variance to tell you much about the quality of the deciding. Ten decisions of the same kind average out the variance. The pattern you can act on is the pattern that emerges across the portfolio.

This applies beyond business. A skydiver who survived a particular jump cannot conclude that the jump was a good decision; they have to ask whether they would have survived 100 jumps of that kind. A relationship that worked out cannot be evaluated by its endpoint alone; the question is what kind of deciding produced it.

## The Reframe in the Moment

The practical move: when standing in front of a felt decision, do not ask "which option will give me the result I want?" Ask: "which option lets me make this decision in a way that feels great right now?"

"What I would suggest is that when you're looking at how to make a good decision, the other ways to look at what a good decision is, is that feels really good inside of you. You're like, 'Oh, this, like I could be happy with the consequences either way because I made a decision in a way that felt great. It felt like it was a part of my authenticity. It felt like who I am.'" -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

The shift dissolves the deciding-feel. When the criterion stops being "predict the future correctly," the binary frame relaxes, fear drops, more options become visible, and the action that emerges is the one consistent with who the person actually is.

"Because we're in fear, we're thinking about it in a binary way. And so we're not seeing all the alternatives. And as soon as you say to yourself, okay, instead of trying to get the best outcome or get the right outcome, I'm going to change my focus to, to how does it feel best in me, then immediately the fear starts evaporating, new options get seen." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

A small example: a job offer. The outcome frame collapses to "take it or leave it." The how-frame opens: take it with a renegotiation of these specific terms, take it conditional on this resource being delivered, take it after this relationship issue is addressed first. The decision was never binary. The fear made it look binary.

## Comfort vs. Aliveness

The principle gets misread as "feel comfortable in the moment." That is not what it says. The decision that feels great is not always the comfortable one. The decision that feels great is the one where the person can be themselves in the deciding -- where the deciding action expresses who they are rather than violating it.

Mandela breaking rocks in the quarry was not comfortable. But he could be himself in it. That is what "feels great" means in this principle.

"I think about like Mandela working in like breaking stones in the quarry and being in, in jail for a long period of time. That wasn't going to be comfortable, but it felt good. There's some aspect of him that felt true to himself, that felt like he was being himself in that process. And so the good that I'm feeling, that I'm speaking to, like, you feel good in the decision means that you're in yourself, you're empowered, you're saying, yes, this is who I am. Not in an identification way, but in a way of like presence. I can be present in this decision-making. I'm not saying I'm going to be comfortable." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

See [[mandela-empowerment]] for the longer arc of this same teaching.

## The Reverse Symmetry With Future-Located Happiness

Brett's articulation of the structural problem: if a person makes decisions based on what they imagine their future self will feel, they are training a life-orientation where the present is permanently sacrificed for an imagined future.

"If you make decisions from what you think your future feeling is going to be, then you're basically telling yourself that I am prioritizing my imaginary fantasy future over my present. So you're going to end up living a life where your present is always organized to be sacrificed for some future." -- Brett Kistler, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

Joe affirms this directly: "That's so beautifully seen. That's exactly right."

The principle is the inverse: a life of well-made decisions accumulates as a life lived in the present, because each decision was evaluated by the quality of the deciding-moment, not by the projected future. See [[no-finish-line]].

## The Welcome-All-Emotions Requirement

This principle is downstream of the willingness to feel any emotional state. The reason fear-driven decisions are bad decisions is that the intellect is weighting the avoidance of a feared feeling more than it is weighting the actual question. See [[decisions-are-emotional]] for the underlying principle.

"Clarity and decision-making comes from being willing and able and welcoming all emotional experiences... if you're happy to feel any emotion, good or bad, if you welcome any of these emotions, your decision-making becomes very clear." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

This is also why [[can-you-look-forward-to-feeling-it]] is the diagnostic at the root. If the person can welcome any outcome's emotional consequence, the decision-feel collapses and the action emerges.

## What This Reframes

The standard frame: "Should I take the job? Will I be happier with it or without it?"

The reframed view: "How do I want to be in this decision? What's the action that I can take from who I am, with the resources to be present, that I won't be calling a mistake five years from now regardless of the outcome?"

The standard frame produces anxiety, polling, pro-con lists, attempts to predict an unpredictable future. The reframed view produces present-tense action consistent with the deciding-self.
