# The Aloneness Is the Quickest Route to Freedom

## The Principle

The felt sense of aloneness or not-enough-ness that a person has been avoiding their entire life is, on direct contact, the shortest path to the freedom they have been chasing. The thing the avoidance has been treating as the catastrophe is actually the doorway.

"That feeling is actually the quickest way to their freedom... That place that we consider to be a void is often the most direct route to our freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The principle inverts the structure of the avoidance. The avoidance has been organized around the assumption that the avoided thing is the threat — and that freedom is somewhere else, away from it, on the other side of avoiding it well enough. The principle's claim is that the avoided thing is the freedom, that it has always been the freedom, that the avoidance is what has been preventing the freedom from being available.

## Why This Is the Case

The mechanism has structural reasons behind it. The aloneness has been doing work the person did not consent to. As long as it is avoided, it is in charge — every action shaped by its avoidance, every decision filtered through the question "will this prevent me from feeling that?" The avoidance is exhausting and chronic and total.

Meeting the aloneness directly ends its underground authority. The person discovers what is actually there, which is consistently not the catastrophe the avoidance has been preparing for. The discovery releases the energy that was being used for avoidance. The released energy is the felt experience of freedom.

The avoided thing's actual content turns out to be love, in its longing form. See [[what-were-avoiding-is-usually-love]]. The longing-to-be-loved is itself love. The avoidance has been preventing the person from registering that the love they have been seeking has been with them all along, in the form of the very feeling they have been running from.

"What we find out is that part of ourselves that feels like they're not enough is actually our longing to be loved. And the only thing that longs to be loved is love." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The "quickest" claim is structural. Any other route to freedom requires fixing all the conditions the avoidance has been generating — the approval-chasing, the achievement-engine, the pushing-support-away. The direct route bypasses all of those by addressing what they were all in service of avoiding. One contact replaces years of working on symptoms.

## Why It Sounds Counterintuitive

The principle reliably produces resistance. The person hearing it for the first time often experiences it as: "What you're saying is I should go look at the thing I've been using my entire life not to look at? That doesn't sound smart."

Joe acknowledges this directly:

"What do you mean I gotta look at the thing that what I've been using my entire life not to look at, right? Like, what? That doesn't sound smart, Joe." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The resistance is intelligent. The system that built the avoidance built it for reasons that made sense at the time. The reasons are usually: the person did not have the resources to meet the feeling when it first arose; meeting it would have produced overwhelm; avoidance was the available adaptation. The system that learned this lesson is correct that the original meeting would have been dangerous.

What the system is incorrect about: that the danger is still present. The person now has resources they did not have as a child. The feeling that was overwhelming then is not overwhelming now. The avoidance that was protective is now the thing producing the chronic state the person is trying to escape.

The principle's verifiability handles the resistance:

"All I can say is, if you do it two or three times for two or three hours, you'll notice it gets easier right away." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

The principle does not have to be believed. It has to be tested. The test takes 2-3 sessions of 2-3 hours. See [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]] for the practice form.

## What Freedom Means Here

"Freedom" in this principle does not mean the absence of all difficulty. It means specifically:

- The end of the chronic background work of avoidance.
- The ability to be in rooms without the front that the approval-chasing requires.
- The ability to receive support that is actually being offered.
- The capacity to be alone with oneself without it being a threat.
- The release of energy that was being used to maintain the avoidance.
- The relocation of the source of love from outside (where it could never reliably be) to inside (where it has always been).

The person discovers that what they were chasing through achievement, relationship-acquisition, and approval-collection was the felt experience that the avoided contact actually delivers. The chasing was always going to fail because the chasing was a workaround for a contact that only direct meeting can produce.

## Why "Two or Three Hours, Two or Three Times" Is Enough

The principle's specificity on dose is part of what makes it actionable. Joe does not say "spend years working on this." He says 2-3 hours, 2-3 times. The reason this works:

- The avoidance's catastrophic image of the feeling has been built up over decades.
- The catastrophic image cannot survive direct contact.
- A few seconds to a few minutes of actual contact dissolves much of the image.
- 2-3 hours is enough for the busy-ness defenses to exhaust and the actual contact to happen.
- 2-3 sessions is enough for the practitioner to verify the principle and start changing the daily baseline.

The principle is therefore unusually high-leverage. Most psychological work is iterative across years. This work is iterative across a few sessions. The leverage comes from the structural mismatch between the avoidance's image and the actual content.

## The Friend's Mourning Example

Joe illustrates the principle with a specific story. A friend had been drinking heavily, partying, out of shape, with a failing business, in a relationship that was about to end. The girlfriend broke up with him. Joe's prescription: mourn the breakup, face the experience, allow the grief.

The friend had a regular long commute from Flagstaff to Yuma. He used the commute for the mourning. Two weeks in, he reported howling — sounds coming out of him he had never made. A couple of months in, he reported that he was still mourning, but no longer mourning the breakup — he was mourning all the patterns in his life that had led to the breakup. Six months later, he was in shape, not drinking, his business was thriving.

"That's what it is when you actually face that abyss. What you're doing is you're facing the thing that's been driving you your whole life because you're not taking a look at it. And if you take a look at it, yeah, it's uncomfortable at first, but it really quickly becomes a direct path to freedom." -- Joe Hudson, Connection Is Hard Until You Face Your Aloneness

See [[joes-friend-mourning-the-breakup-flagstaff-to-yuma]] for the developed example.

The story illustrates the principle's mechanism: the friend faced the avoided feeling, the feeling moved through stages, and the conditions that had been holding the chronic dysfunction in place dissolved. The other-life-changes (shape, drinking, business) were downstream of the inner contact, not prerequisites for it. The work was done in the cave. The downstream effects followed.

## The Principle's Application Beyond Aloneness

The principle is stated specifically about aloneness in the source transcript because the source is about feeling alone in rooms. The same principle generalizes:

- For chronic anxiety: facing the avoided feeling under the anxiety is the quickest route to freedom from the anxiety. See [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]].
- For chronic depression: facing the avoided grief or anger under the depression is the quickest route. See [[depression-as-the-feelings-you-arent-feeling]].
- For chronic shame: facing the avoided self-contempt or pain directly is the quickest route. See [[shame-the-default-emotion]].
- For chronic relational stuckness: facing the avoided heartbreak or fear of intimacy is the quickest route.

The general form: whatever you have been avoiding longest, the direct contact with is the quickest route to the freedom you have been chasing through avoidance. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]] for the related principle. See [[purposelessness-is-freedom]] for the related case where the avoided thing is meaninglessness.

The principle is one of the load-bearing principles of the methodology. Most of the longer work is on the avoidances. This principle states what the work is for.

## What This Principle Is Not

- **Not a guarantee of one-shot transformation.** The contact opens the path. The path still has to be walked. The principle states that the path is direct, not that it is single-step.
- **Not a denial of the difficulty of the contact.** The first contact is uncomfortable. The principle does not promise comfort; it promises that the discomfort is much shorter than the alternative.
- **Not a substitute for trauma-informed work where trauma is present.** Some avoidances are bound up with trauma that requires specific support. The principle's general form applies to the general case. The trauma-bound case needs adjusted application.
- **Not a recommendation to bypass professional help when it would be useful.** The principle's claim about the directness of the route does not say the route should be walked alone. Many people walk it more effectively with a coach, therapist, or community.
- **Not the only principle that matters.** The methodology contains many principles. This one operates specifically when the avoidance has been long and deep enough that the direct contact becomes the high-leverage move.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person who has been avoiding a specific feeling for years and finally meets it briefly and experiences relief disproportionate to the meeting's duration.
- A person who has been chasing external conditions (success, partnership, community) and discovers on the other side that the felt experience they were chasing is in the contact with the avoided feeling.
- A person who reports years of personal-growth work without the felt freedom they were working toward, and discovers that the work has been organized around the avoidance rather than the contact.
- A person who completes 2-3 sessions of [[spend-a-few-hours-with-the-not-enough-part]] and verifies the principle's claim.
- A practitioner who notices that the work that produced the most freedom in their life took the least time and was the work they had been most resisting.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with a particular blend of practical certainty and acknowledged counterintuitiveness. He knows it sounds wrong. He knows the listener's instinct will resist. He names the resistance and then asks the listener to test rather than to believe.

The "it's amazing how short of a period of time" phrasing carries the practical certainty. The "I know it sounds really unappealing" phrasing carries the acknowledged counterintuitiveness. Both together produce the tone: "I'm not asking you to take my word for this. Try it. The cost of the test is small. The result is verifiable. If I'm wrong, you've lost 2-3 hours. If I'm right, the lifelong work of avoidance ends."

When teaching the principle, hold both tones. Do not minimize the resistance — that produces collusion with the listener's defense of why the work won't apply to them. Do not overstate the certainty — that produces the same defense in a different form. The principle's authority is in the verifiability, not in the teacher's confidence.
