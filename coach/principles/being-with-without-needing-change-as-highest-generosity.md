# Being With Someone Without Needing Anything to Change May Be the Highest Generosity

## The Principle

There is a form of generosity that involves nothing being transferred. No money, no admiration, no recognition. Just presence with the other person while needing nothing about them to be different. This may be the deepest form of generosity available.

"Maybe that should be the highest act of generosity. It's not giving at all. It's just being with somebody without needing anything to change." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The principle reorganizes the entire concept of generosity. If giving is not required for generosity, then generosity is not fundamentally about transfer. It is fundamentally about a quality of presence that lets the other person be exactly as they are without the giver's preferences pressing on the situation.

## Why This Is the Highest Form

The hierarchy of generosity is measured by how much the act dissolves the giver's self-definition -- see [[generosity]]. By that measure, being-with-without-needing-change tops the scale because it requires the giver to release more than any other form:

- No "I am the one who has" (no resources are being transferred)
- No "I am the one who knows" (no advice is being given)
- No "I am the one who fixes" (no problem is being solved)
- No "I am the one who feels for" (no sympathy is being offered)
- No outcome the giver is attached to

What is left when all of those are removed is presence without agenda. The giver brings themselves and asks nothing of the situation -- not even that the situation get better. This is structurally similar to [[empathy-vs-sympathy]] (beside rather than above) but goes further: empathy still has the implicit "I am with you in this hard thing." Being-with-without-needing-change drops even the "in this hard thing" framing. The other person is allowed to be exactly where they are without being held in any category, including the category of "person needing my presence."

## The Scene That Produced the Principle

Joe names this principle in response to Brett's account of praying with his mother and sister on a phone call while his brother is dying.

"It's the first time I've unironically prayed with my mom in decades. ... What it felt to me there was simply the three of us letting go of everything in the way and surrendering to being there with each other." -- Brett Kistler, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

Joe's response immediately follows: maybe that should be the highest act of generosity. The principle is being extracted from the specific moment. What was happening in that prayer was not transactional. Nothing was being asked. No one was being fixed. The brother was dying and would still die. The mother was grieving and would still grieve. The sister was holding everything and would still be holding everything. And yet the prayer was real, and it changed what each of them experienced.

The "letting go of everything in the way" is the operational instruction. The "everything in the way" is everything the giver normally brings to a situation -- their fix, their interpretation, their need for the situation to be different. Letting it go is the act of generosity. What is left is presence that does not require anything of the other.

## The Coaching Implication

The principle has a direct implication for coaching. The most generous coaching move is often not to make something happen. It is to be with the person without needing the session to produce a result. The coach who needs the client to break through is not being generous; the coach is asking something of the client. The coach who can be with the client exactly as they are -- including stuck, including unchanged, including unwilling to do the work -- is offering the rare thing.

This connects to [[people-want-you-in-it-with-them-not-advice]]. The advice-giving move is the failure mode: it positions the coach above and asks the client to receive a fix. The being-with move trusts that the presence itself is what is needed, and that whatever wants to move will move when there is room for it.

## The Caregiving Application

The principle is especially relevant in caregiving for the dying, the sick, the grieving. These are situations where the impulse to fix is strongest and the structural reality is that fixing is not available. The dying are dying. The grieving are grieving. The sick may or may not recover. The caregiver who cannot stop trying to make it better adds the weight of their need-to-fix on top of what the person is already carrying.

Being-with-without-needing-change is what allows the dying to die in peace, the grieving to grieve fully, the sick to be in their illness without performing recovery for the caregiver. It is also what allows the caregiver to sustain the role without burning out, because the caregiver is not constantly losing the battle against an unfixable situation.

See [[caregiving-softens-the-self]] for the longer-term effect this stance produces in caregivers who sustain it for years.

## The Pre-Requisite: Being With Your Own Pain

The structural reason this is hard: being-with-the-other without needing them to change requires being-with-your-own-evoked-pain without needing it to change either. The other person's situation evokes pain in the giver. The impulse to fix the other person is usually the impulse to fix the giver's own evoked pain by removing its source.

If the giver can be with their own evoked pain, they can be with the other person without trying to make it stop. If they cannot, the fixing impulse will run automatically, and the generosity will collapse back into agenda-driven helping.

"Being with another's emotion is actually being with your own pain." -- see [[being-with-anothers-emotion-is-being-with-your-own-pain]]

This is why people who have done their own emotional work tend to be the ones who can offer this form of generosity. Not because they are good people. Because they have the internal capacity to stay with what gets evoked.

## The Felt Signature

When this principle is being lived rather than performed, there is a specific felt signature:

- The presence has spaciousness rather than urgency
- There is no inner narration of "I need to do something"
- The other person can talk or be silent; both are received the same way
- The giver is breathing easily
- Nothing is being managed
- The giver can be moved -- can cry, can be undone -- without it becoming about them

When the principle is being performed but not lived, the giver is sitting still on the outside and frantically problem-solving on the inside. The other person usually feels this. The performance does not produce the benefits of the real thing.

## Contrast With Sympathy, Advice, Reassurance

This principle distinguishes itself from three common moves that look like generosity but are different:

**Sympathy** ("you poor thing") -- positions the giver above the receiver. Implies the receiver's situation should be different. See [[empathy-vs-sympathy]].

**Advice** ("have you tried...") -- positions the giver as knowing more than the receiver. Implies the receiver needs to do something differently. See [[people-want-you-in-it-with-them-not-advice]].

**Reassurance** ("everything will be okay") -- imposes the giver's preferred outcome on a situation the giver does not actually control. Implies the receiver's current feelings are inappropriate to the actual situation.

All three add weight. Being-with-without-needing-change subtracts weight. The receiver is allowed to be in their actual experience without anyone trying to relocate them.

## In Coaching

When working with someone who is overwhelmed by their inability to fix a loved one's situation (a dying relative, a struggling child, an unrecoverable diagnosis), the move is to introduce this principle. The relief of recognizing that fixing was never the assignment is often immediate.

The move is also useful when a person reports they have "no idea what to do" in a situation. The "no idea what to do" presupposes that doing is required. Naming this principle releases that presupposition. Sometimes the most useful thing to do is to be there without doing.

For the coach themselves, this principle is the structural reminder that the work is not produced by the coach's effort. The coach is the conditions under which the work can happen. The being-with-without-needing-change is the conditions.

## When This Principle Is Active in a Coaching Conversation

- A person is caring for someone who is dying, sick, or in unfixable pain
- A person is overwhelmed by the felt impotence of not being able to fix someone they love
- A person describes constantly offering advice or fixes that are not landing
- A person is exhausted from trying to make someone else's situation better
- A person reports a moment of unexpected closeness that did not involve doing anything
- A person is processing a death or grave illness and asking what they should have done
- A person in helper/coach/therapist role is burning out from need-to-produce-results

## Tone

When delivering this principle in conversation, Joe is quiet and slow. The principle is itself a demonstration of itself -- the way it is named has the spaciousness it points at. Loud or fast delivery would undercut the teaching. The principle invites slowing down. The coach delivering it should slow down.
