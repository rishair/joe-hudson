# Owning the Want Often Dissolves It

## The Principle

When a want is fully owned and felt -- without judgment, without strategy, without rush to satisfy it -- the want often shifts on its own. The surface want releases or transforms into a more accurate want underneath. The wanting itself does not go away; it refines.

"The amazing thing is when you totally own a want, oftentimes the want goes away almost immediately. You know, I really want $10 million in the bank. And if you fully feel that all the way and you're just like, oh yeah, $10 million, you know, and just feel that want, oftentimes it just, it just starts shifting and it's just like, oh, what I want is security. And then it's like, oh, what I really want is to feel empowered in every situation." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## The Sequence

The pattern repeats reliably:
1. The person names a surface want ("I want $10 million").
2. The want is owned fully, felt as a feeling rather than chased as a target.
3. The want shifts: "what I really want is security."
4. The new want is felt fully again.
5. It shifts again: "what I really want is to feel empowered in every situation."
6. The process continues until the most accurate want surfaces, which is usually something close to love, peace, or full presence.

This is not a technique to get rid of unwanted desires. It is a mechanism that the want has when allowed to be felt. The wanting reveals what it is actually after.

## Why This Works

The surface want is usually a stand-in. A symbol for the deeper want. The deeper want is harder to name and more vulnerable to admit, so the system substitutes a more acceptable surface version. The substitution works only as long as the surface want is not fully felt. The moment the person sits with the surface want without trying to get it, the substitution starts to fail and the real want surfaces.

This is why hiding a want keeps it sticky. The want stays as a craving because it cannot move through. Allowing it to move through transforms it.

"If you don't allow yourself to have the want, it can't move through you. It's just like if you don't allow yourself to get angry, you can't move through you. If you don't allow yourself to get sad, it can't move through you. It's just another emotion that needs to move through you and is so pleasant when it does." -- Joe Hudson, Want over Should

## The Connection to Impartiality

This principle is the inner version of [[wants-vs-partiality]]. In conversation, owning a hidden agenda often dissolves it. The internal version: owning a hidden want often dissolves it.

"When you own your want completely, ownership can actually make the want less intense and allow you to see what the world is like." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality

In both cases, the unowned form is the sticky form. Ownership is the release.

## What This Is Not

This is not a way to manipulate yourself into wanting different things. It is not "fake owning so the want goes away." That is just shame in a different costume. The principle only works when the wanting is actually allowed and felt.

It is also not a promise that the want disappears. Sometimes the want is exactly what it appears to be, and after being fully felt, the person moves toward it. The dissolution happens often, not always. The point is that the feeling-of-the-want clears the obstruction either way: either the want moves toward action or it reveals what was underneath.
