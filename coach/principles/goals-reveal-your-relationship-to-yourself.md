# How You Relate to Goals Reveals How You Relate to Yourself

## The Principle

The way a person relates to their goals -- how they set them, how they pursue them, how they react to missing them, what they feel when they hit them -- is a direct readout of how they relate to themselves. The goal is the canvas on which the self-relationship plays out.

Someone who brutalizes themselves when they miss a goal is someone who brutalizes themselves generally. Someone who treats themselves with curiosity and warmth around their goals is someone who treats themselves with curiosity and warmth in other domains. The goal-relationship is not a separate domain; it is a window into the underlying relationship to the self.

"Simply the way that you relate to goals is a really good indicator of how you're relating to yourself, and you can trace your relationship with goals and the need behind the want, play that need behind the want game, all the way to What is it that you really want? Which is coming from just the place of being." -- Brett Kistler / Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The tracing goes both ways. From the goal-relationship you can read the self-relationship. From the self-relationship you can predict the goal-relationship.

## The Two Visible Modes

The two failure modes are observable in how a person handles goals:

**The brutal mode.** The person sets goals as demands. Missing produces self-attack. Hitting produces brief relief followed by the next demand. The internal voice is the inner board, issuing decrees. The person describes themselves as "behind," "failing," "not doing enough." This is a person who is brutal with themselves across domains. The goal-relationship is one symptom.

**The avoidant mode.** The person refuses goals to escape the brutality. They say "I don't do goals." They live without explicit targets. The avoidance is real but the cost is loss of orientation. This is also a person who has not made peace with themselves; they have just chosen to flee the battlefield. The unaddressed self-relationship continues to operate, just without the goal-relationship as its expression. See [[goals-make-me-miserable]] for the concern that oscillates between these modes.

The third option -- holding goals gently, with love, with awe and wonder, without being defined by them -- is the visible signature of a person who has a kind relationship with themselves. The goal-relationship is the direct expression of the self-relationship that has been worked on.

## Why This Principle Is Diagnostic

The principle is operationally useful because the goal-relationship is more visible and easier to discuss than the self-relationship directly. A person may not be able to articulate how they relate to themselves. But they can usually describe what they do with goals: how they set them, how they react when they miss, what the goal-pursuit feels like in the body.

The goal-description, listened to carefully, contains the self-relationship in compressed form. The brutality, the avoidance, the perfectionism, the grace, the warmth, the gentleness -- all of these show up in how the goals are held. The coach who hears the goal-relationship is hearing the self-relationship by proxy.

This is also why working on the goal-relationship and working on the self-relationship are the same work. Changing how you hold your goals requires changing how you hold yourself. Changing how you hold yourself changes how you hold your goals. The two cannot be separated.

## The Tracing Move

The practical application of the principle is the tracing move. Start with the goal. Ask what is the need behind the want (see [[need-behind-the-want]]). Each layer drops further toward what is actually wanted. At the bottom is usually something close to "to be okay" or "to be loved" or "to feel alive." These are not goals; they are statements about the self.

"You can trace your relationship with goals and the need behind the want, play that need behind the want game, all the way to What is it that you really want? Which is coming from just the place of being." -- Brett Kistler, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The endpoint of the tracing is the self-relationship. The goals are the surface; the wants are deeper; the deepest layer is what the system is reaching for, which is some form of being okay with the self. The work at that layer is the work that makes the goal-relationship change at the surface.

The traceability runs the other direction too. From "I need to be loved," upstream are the wants ("if I had X I'd be loved"), and upstream of those are the goals ("achieve X"). The self-relationship sits at the bottom and produces everything above it.

## The "Are They Helping You Feel More Connected to Yourself?" Question

The diagnostic question Joe lands on at the end of the transcript:

"Are your goals helping you feel more connected with yourself or not? Your personal goals. The goals in the company helping you feel more connected to the company, the company more connected or not? If they're not, then there's a way to make those goals better." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

This is the operational form of the principle. The metric for whether the goal-relationship is healthy is connection: to self, to the company, to the people involved. Goals that produce connection are working. Goals that produce disconnection -- from the body, from the heart, from the team, from the customers -- need to be revised.

The question can be asked of any goal. Is pursuing this making me more connected to myself, or less? If the answer is less, the goal is misaligned somewhere. Either the want is wrong, or the way of holding it is wrong, or the source of the want is wrong. The investigation can start from the connection signal.

## What This Implies for the "Goals Make Me Miserable" Concern

When a person describes their goals as crushing them, they are also describing how they crush themselves. The brutalization of the goals is the brutalization of the self. The work cannot just be at the goal-design layer ("set better goals"). It has to be at the self-relationship layer.

Conversely, when a person says "I just need to be more gentle with my goals," they often cannot just do this. The brutality has roots in how they hold themselves. The gentleness with goals will come naturally if the self-relationship softens. The self-relationship is the upstream variable.

This is one of the reasons the work on goals in this body of teaching is also work on the inner critic, the voice in the head, the inherited childhood scripts. The goal-relationship cannot be reformed independently of these. See [[my-inner-critic-wont-stop]], [[apply-view-to-the-voice]], [[the-inner-board]].

## What This Implies for Goal-Setting in a Group

The principle scales to teams and companies. The way a team relates to its goals reveals how it relates to itself as a team. Brutal goals indicate a brutal team culture. Gentle, wonder-held goals indicate a culture that can hold the people inside it the same way.

The principle also reveals when the team has not yet become a team. A team where each person is pursuing their goal in isolation is not relating to itself as a team. A team where everyone is celebrating each other's failures publicly is. The goal-relationship reveals the relational structure.

See [[celebrate-failures-publicly]] for the practice that builds the team-relating-to-itself version of this.

## What This Implies for the Self-Aware Person

For someone working on themselves, the goal-relationship is a feedback loop. Notice how you set the next goal. Notice how you react to the next miss. Notice what happens in the body when you hit the next target. The noticing is direct information about the state of the self-relationship.

The progression is usually:

1. The person notices the brutality of their goal-relationship. The self-attack around missing is named.
2. The person works on the self-relationship layer. The inner critic is examined. The childhood imprints are surfaced. The voice in the head is met with [[apply-view-to-the-voice]].
3. The goal-relationship softens as a byproduct. The person notices they are setting different goals, holding them differently, recovering from misses faster, taking less from hits.
4. The new goal-relationship is itself confirmation that the self-relationship has changed.

This is one of the cleanest feedback loops in this body of work. The self-relationship is otherwise hard to see directly. The goal-relationship makes it visible.

## The "Just Like Good Sportsmanship in Life" Frame

Joe's compressed image for what the healthy goal-relationship looks like:

"It's kind of good sportsmanship in life is an interesting way to put it. So we got to play the game, we got to learn, we're improving. And yeah, we really go after our goal. I mean, we really go after our goals. Yeah, but that doesn't mean that we are fucked up if we're failing. Yeah, we're just growing." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The sportsmanship frame captures the principle. The good sportsman plays hard. They want to win. They take the loss as part of the game. They do not conclude they are broken when they lose. The healthy goal-relationship is structurally the same. The person who has the healthy goal-relationship has the healthy self-relationship: they engage, they want, they sometimes miss, none of it changes who they are.
