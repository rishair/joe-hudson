# If You Fully Understood the Behavior, You Wouldn't Do It

## The Principle

Bad habits persist because the person doing them does not yet fully understand or fully feel what they are doing. The standard response is to try to stop the behavior. The principle inverts this: stop trying to stop, and instead fully understand and fully feel the behavior in the act. Full felt understanding dissolves the behavior on its own.

"You don't fully understand it. If you fully understood it, you wouldn't do it. Like, there's just no way, if you fully understand it. So let's just work on the understanding of it instead of the doing, because you've been trying to do stuff over it for a decade. It hasn't worked. Let's just understand it more fully." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The principle reframes the entire approach to unwanted behavior. The behavior is not a failure of willpower. It is a deficit of understanding. The person is acting through a partial blur, dissociating from some part of the experience to make the behavior bearable. The blur is what keeps the behavior available. Remove the blur, and the behavior loses its grip.

## The Body Mechanism

To do a behavior repeatedly, the person has to numb part of the felt experience of doing it. The eating-at-night does not work if you actually taste every bite and feel the body's response. The cigarette does not work if you take in the full sensation of inhaling smoke. The criticism of your spouse does not work if you fully feel what is happening in your body and theirs in the moment. The numbness is the maintenance ritual that allows the behavior to continue.

When the person stops numbing and lets the full felt experience arrive, the behavior often loses its appeal. Not through judgment. Not through deciding it is bad. Through accurate perception of what is actually happening.

## The Smoking Example

Brett offers the standard form of this:

"A really good way to approach dropping the habit is just mindfulness, just pay really close attention to what it feels like to smoke a cigarette and pay really close attention to what the sensations actually are and the taste and the flavor. And you just might find that it's actually not as you'd actually been dissociating or suppressing some of the actual sensation to get something else and then shame locked that into place." -- Brett Kistler, The Upright Apology

The mindfulness is not a moral practice in this frame. It is an undoing of the dissociation that made the behavior possible.

## The Young Woman With the Boys

A young woman in her twenties noticed that she could "wrap boys around her finger" because she was beautiful. She did not like this in herself. She asked Joe for the solution.

"I was like, 'Just feel it. Next time you're doing it, just feel it. Feel it all the way through. Allow your, like, don't stop feeling any part of it. Have the full and total experience of it.' And two days later, I was talking to her again and I was like, 'Well, how'd it go?' And she said, 'Well, I saw myself doing it. I felt it all the way through.' I was like, 'Oh, and what happened?' And she goes, 'Well, I didn't figure anything out, but boy, I just stopped doing it. Didn't feel good.' And that was it." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

Two days, no analysis, no plan, no willpower. The behavior dissolved when she stopped dissociating from the experience of doing it. The didn't-feel-good was always there. She had been blocking it. Feeling it all the way through removed the block, which removed the behavior.

## What the Person Is Not Doing

The instruction is precise about what it is not.

- Not figuring it out. The young woman explicitly said "I didn't figure anything out." Cognitive analysis is not the mechanism. The understanding is felt, not thought.
- Not making a plan. The behavior change is a byproduct of the perception, not the goal of the practice.
- Not deciding it is bad. The didn't-feel-good is discovered, not imposed.
- Not adding shame. Shame holds the behavior in place. See [[shame-drives-behavior]]. The full felt experience is the opposite of shame: a clean perception of what is actually happening.
- Not stopping the behavior on this occasion. The instruction is to do the behavior and feel it fully, not to interrupt it.

## What This Differs From

This is not [[joes-pot-habit]], although it is related. Joe's pot habit dissolved when he felt the underlying emotion (sadness, anger) that the pot was avoiding. This principle is one level different: feel the behavior itself in the act, not the emotion underneath it. Both are routes to the same place. Sometimes the underlying emotion is the leverage point. Sometimes the felt experience of the behavior is the leverage point. Often the second leads to the first.

This is also not [[awareness-as-change-agent]], although it is a special case of it. Awareness-as-change-agent is the general principle that putting attention on something changes it. This principle specifies the form of attention: full felt experience, in the act, of the behavior itself. The instruction is specific because the absence of specificity tends to slide back into management.

This is also distinct from [[dissolution-not-behavior-control]], which is about limiting beliefs dissolving when realized. That principle works at the level of belief. This principle works at the level of sensation. A person can have the limiting belief dissolved and still have the behavior, because the behavior may also be maintained by the dissociation from its felt experience.

## How to Apply

The instruction has a simple form, but doing it requires honesty.

1. **Pick the behavior.** One specific behavior you want to change. Not a vague pattern. A specific act.

2. **Next time you do it, do it.** Do not try to stop. Do not interrupt yourself. Do the behavior as you would have done it anyway.

3. **Feel it all the way through.** Every sensation. Every part of the experience. The desire that precedes it, the doing of it, the feeling during, the feeling after. Do not block any of it.

4. **Notice what you have been blocking.** There is almost always a part of the experience that you have been not-feeling in order to do the behavior. Notice what that part is.

5. **Do not figure anything out.** Resist the urge to extract insight. The felt experience is the work, not the thinking about it.

6. **Repeat until the behavior dissolves or you understand why it persists.** Often a few times is enough. Sometimes it takes longer. The change is a byproduct.

## Why Trying to Stop Fails

The standard response to an unwanted behavior is to apply willpower to stop it. This fails for the same reason fighting any pattern fails. See [[resistance]]. The fight against the behavior installs the behavior more deeply, because the fight is itself a form of attention that strengthens the dynamic.

Trying to stop also depends on the behavior being unbearable to continue. But the person has already been doing the behavior for years, which means the felt experience of doing it is currently bearable. The trying-to-stop does not change the bearableness. It only adds the shame of not-stopping.

The full-felt-experience approach changes the bearableness directly. Once the felt experience is fully perceived, the behavior may no longer be bearable. The person stops not because they decided to stop but because the action no longer fits what they are willing to do.

## Connection to the Upright Apology

In the same conversation, Joe connects this principle to apology. "If you're apologizing from an upright place, from an empowered position, it does require you to feel it." The upright apology requires the same full feeling. The apology that is delivered without feeling what is being acknowledged is a performance, not an apology. See [[upright-apology]].

This is why the daily upright apology for a bad habit (see [[apologize-as-daily-practice-for-bad-habits]]) works as a complement to this principle. The apology is the verbal acknowledgment from an upright body. The full-feeling-of-the-behavior is the somatic acknowledgment in the act. Both routes lead to the same place: behavior change as the byproduct of felt accurate perception.

## The Paradox of Doing Nothing

The principle sits inside the larger pattern of Joe and Brett's coaching: the apparent paradox that doing nothing produces more change than trying to change.

"It's kind of a weird thing because we're saying don't do stuff, but at the same time we've said, oh, here's an apology practice... it's the pointer of don't do anything is, it's not that we're saying actually don't do anything. What we're saying is that trying to figure it out and getting an action plan and a strategy doesn't work as good as understanding and feeling." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

The action plan and the strategy are the doing that is being pointed away from. The feeling and the understanding are the doing that is being pointed toward. The distinction is real. Most action plans and strategies are designed to keep the person out of the feeling. The behavior persists because the strategy is part of the behavior's defense system.

Brett refines this further:

"The action plan and the strategy will be effective to the extent that it is structured to bring you into yourself, into your feeling, into awareness." -- Brett Kistler, The Upright Apology

Joe agrees. Action is not the enemy; action that prevents feeling is. A practice that brings the person into the felt experience is congruent with this principle. A practice that lets the person bypass the felt experience reinstalls the dynamic.

## Tone

Joe holds this principle with confidence and lightness. The confidence is because he has seen it work repeatedly. The lightness is because the work being asked of the person is, in one sense, easier than what they have been trying. They do not have to stop the behavior. They only have to feel it. The relief in this can be palpable when the person hears it.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents a habit they have been trying to stop for years using willpower.
- When [[shame-drives-behavior]] has been established and the person is ready for a non-shame approach.
- When the person has tried every "should" version of behavior change and is open to something different.
- When [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]] is being introduced and the felt experience of the behavior is itself the blocker.
- When [[apologize-as-daily-practice-for-bad-habits]] has been proposed and the person wants the complementary in-the-act practice.
