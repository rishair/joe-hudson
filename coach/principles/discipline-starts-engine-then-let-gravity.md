# Discipline Starts the Engine, Then You Let Gravity Take Care of It

## The Principle

There are two phases to any practice that leads to a natural state. The first phase requires discipline. The second phase requires letting go of the discipline. Both are real. Confusing one for the other produces the predictable failures of spiritual and personal-development work.

In the first phase, discipline gets the engine running. You set aside time for emotional yoga. You practice allowing pleasure. You live by principles. You read. You meditate. The work is sustained, effortful, and produced by will.

In the second phase, the discipline becomes the obstacle. You have to do the downhill slide. You have to let gravity take care of stuff. The discipline that started the engine becomes friction if it does not eventually release into receiving.

"There's lots of ways that discipline can kind of get the engine running. The management can get the engine running, but at some point you have to do the downhill slide. Like, you have to let gravity take care of stuff." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

## Why This Resolves the Effort Paradox

The teachings around joy, presence, authenticity, and surrender often contain an apparent contradiction. On one hand: you cannot try your way into these states; trying is the resistance. On the other hand: the path is sustained and requires discipline. Both are true. They are true at different phases.

The contradiction dissolves once the two-phase structure is visible. The discipline is real and required for as long as the system needs to be trained out of its default mode. The discipline must eventually be released because the destination is not a disciplined state; it is a state that operates without effort.

The error is staying in either phase too long. The person who never disciplines never gets the engine running -- they wait for inspiration that does not arrive. The person who stays disciplined forever turns the practice into management. Neither produces the state the discipline was originally serving.

## What Discipline Is For

Specifically, discipline is for:

- **Getting the body familiar with the shape of the state.** Five minutes of breathing as pleasure, three times a week, teaches the body that this is a thing it can do. The repetition installs the channel.
- **Stretching the capacity.** [[pleasure-capacity]] is trainable. The discipline is the training. Each session extends the ceiling a little. Without sustained sessions, the ceiling does not move.
- **Reading time.** A reading practice keeps the mind on the work even when the felt-state is not present. The reading is preparation for the moments when the state is available.
- **Living a principled life.** See [[principles-free-you]]. Principles are a form of structured discipline that produces the conditions for the natural state to arrive.
- **Daily emotional yoga.** See [[daily-emotional-yoga]]. Time set aside to feel what is not being felt, until the body learns the shape of the feeling.
- **Practicing pleasure as a stretch.** Letting yourself feel pleasure on purpose, allowing it to expand you and stretch you, like a yoga class. The yoga-class analogy is exact. You are conditioning a capacity.

"Saying, oh, I'm going to feel my emotions every day for 20, 30 minutes. I'm going to let my body move and I'm going to say what I got to say, yell what I got to yell. Shake where I have to shake, that's also going to be incredibly helpful. And that can be a disciplined approach." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

## What Has to Eventually Release

The discipline has to release because the destination is not a forced state. Trying to force yourself to feel joy is not effective. The same is true of peace, of authenticity, of presence. These respond to allowing, not to effort.

"As soon as you start using it all to — you try, you're pushing, like the inner energetic is, I have to get there rather than I am already there. Like, oh, I don't — all I have to do is like, like, take off the layers of shit and like, then that's an attitude of like it's already there within me that is going to be necessary." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The internal energetic is the variable. "I have to get there" is the trying frame. "I am already there" is the receiving frame. The first produces forward motion that obscures the destination. The second produces the recognition that the destination was never anywhere else. See [[joy-as-natural-state]].

The discipline that lasts past its usefulness keeps the system in the "I have to get there" energetic. The release of the discipline allows the "I am already there" frame to land. The release is not casual. It is the next phase of the practice, and it requires its own kind of orientation.

## The Zen Variant: Trying Until You Give Up

A specific variant Joe names: in some Zen lineages, the practice is to try with increasing intensity. The teacher keeps adjusting your posture -- no, sit more like this, sit like this. Try, try, try until the trying becomes unbearable. At the moment when the trying gives up, the awakening arrives.

"You can go that way too, where you just like try so fucking hard that you finally give up. I've seen that work as well." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The structure is the same as the discipline-then-gravity teaching. The trying is the engine. The give-up is the gravity. The Zen approach maxes out the trying so that the giving-up is forced. The gentler approach lets the discipline build the capacity, and then trusts the practitioner to know when to release.

Both routes work. They differ only in how aggressively the first phase is run. The principle -- discipline produces the conditions, then the discipline has to release -- is the same.

This is also why [[joes-woods-fast-stopped-fighting]] is an instance of this principle. Joe's fast in the woods at 24 was the Zen variant played out unintentionally. The recursive fight with himself maxed out the trying until the give-up was the only move left. The give-up was the first non-effort moment of his life.

## Where the Fake-It-Till-You-Make-It Teaching Breaks Down

The fake-it-till-you-make-it framing captures the first half of the principle but misses the second. The framing is partly right -- you do have to start somewhere, and the discipline of acting-as-if can install the capacity. But the framing leaves out that the "making it" is not produced by sustained faking. The faking produces the conditions; the actual state arrives when the faking is released.

Joe's specific warning:

"You got to watch out for that one a little bit, right? Like, we all met that person in college who's like, great, everything's great. It's like they're pretending happiness so that they might one day actually be happy. Like, that doesn't work. Like, unfortunately, that doesn't work." -- Joe Hudson, Joy

The person who keeps faking joy without ever releasing into the actual feeling is doing only the first half. The faking becomes the substitute for the state. The state never arrives because the faking is still the resistance.

The corrective is not "stop the discipline." It is "let the discipline give way once it has done its job." The faking-it can produce the channel; the channel only conducts the actual state when the faking releases.

## Operational Test

How to tell if you are in the right phase:

- **If the discipline has not produced any felt change after sustained sincere effort:** the engine has not started yet. Continue.
- **If the discipline produces brief openings into the state and then closes:** the engine is running. The work is now to extend the openings by releasing the discipline when the state begins to arrive.
- **If the discipline is producing a sustained but effortful version of the state:** you are past the point of usefulness. The state is being held in place by the same trying that originally produced it. The release is the next move.
- **If the state arrives spontaneously without discipline being applied:** the gravity phase is operational. Trust it.

## In Coaching

The principle helps when a client reports either:

- "I have been working on this for years and nothing changes." Often the discipline is sincere but is being held in the trying mode. The teaching is that the trying eventually has to release. See [[opposite-of-trying]].
- "I can do this in practice but it doesn't come through in real life." The discipline has produced the capacity but the release into the actual state has not happened. See [[practice-cannot-fool-itself-in-real-conditions]].
- "I have tried letting go but I just end up doing nothing." Letting go is being mistaken for doing nothing. See [[letting-go-vs-doing-nothing]]. The discipline phase still applies until the system can distinguish receiving from collapse.

The reverse error: a client who treats every teaching about allowing as a license to stop doing the work. Here the principle is delivered with the first half emphasized. The discipline is real and required. You do not get to skip the engine-starting phase.

## Why This Is Not Two Different Teachings

A common confusion is to treat "discipline matters" and "trying creates resistance" as competing schools of thought. They are not. They are two halves of the same teaching, operating at different phases. A teacher who only emphasizes the first creates students who never release into the actual state. A teacher who only emphasizes the second creates students who never develop the capacity in the first place. Both halves are necessary.

This is also why the same teacher (Joe, in this case) can be the source of both teachings without contradiction. The teachings are sequenced. Which one applies depends on where the practitioner is. The coaching brain has to read which phase the person is in to know which half of the principle to bring.

## Tone

Practical, slightly amused at the apparent contradiction. Joe does not present this as a deep teaching. He presents it as the obvious nuance once you have lived with the practices long enough to notice the failure modes of each half done alone. The phrasing -- "you have to do the downhill slide... you have to let gravity take care of stuff" -- carries the relief of the second phase. The first phase was uphill; the second is downhill. The body knows the difference.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person reports doing the practices for years without the state arriving.
- When a person hears "trying is the resistance" and concludes that no discipline is required.
- When a person hears "you have to do the work" and concludes that the work is endless effort.
- When [[enjoyment-as-path]] has been taught and the person asks where discipline fits.
- When the fake-it-till-you-make-it framing has been delivered as gospel and is producing pretended joy without actual joy.
- When [[opposite-of-trying]] has landed but the person is now refusing to do any work.
- When a person is approaching the give-up moment after sustained effort and needs to know that the give-up is part of the path, not its failure.
