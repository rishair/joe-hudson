# Stress Tolerance Is Personal and Built (Pickleball vs. Running)

## Definition

There is no universal "right amount" of stress. The same level of activation that is growth for one body is corrosion for another. The same level that was corrosion for a body last year may be growth for that same body next year, because the body has built tolerance.

The implication: the "amount" question is the wrong question. The right question is whether the stress is producing growth or deterioration in this body, in this moment, given what this body has been trained for.

## The Pickleball-vs-Running Example

Two people. One runs every day; the other has not built that muscle. The runner can run far without breaking down. The non-runner's knees give out, ankles strain, the body deteriorates.

Reverse the activity. The non-runner plays pickleball; the runner has not built that muscle. Now the pickleball-player can play long without deterioration. The runner's joints, unaccustomed to the lateral movement, break down.

"Stress for the stress of pickleball to me isn't destructive. The stress of pickleball to you, if you played it for a long time, would be destructive. Your knees or your ankles or something would start giving because I built up some muscles to it." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

The same external activity produces different stress signatures in different bodies. The variable is not the activity. The variable is the tolerance the body has built.

## The Consequence: External Measures Do Not Work

The pickleball example dismantles a category of advice: "you should not be working more than X hours" / "you should not have more than Y commitments" / "you should not endure more than Z conflict." These prescriptions assume a universal calibration of stress that does not exist.

"You can't even get to say, okay, it's the right amount of stress that's good for you and the right amount of stress that's bad, or the wrong amount of stress that's bad for you. You can't determine what the good amount of stress is from anything besides the way that it feels in your system at the moment, and if it's pushing you into destructive behavior or if it's pushing you into growth behavior." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

The only measure is the felt sense in the body right now. The body's read on the activation — and what behavior the activation is producing — is the only reliable diagnostic. See [[shame-as-the-stress-quality-diagnostic]] and [[growth-stress-vs-corrosive-stress-memory-experiment]].

## Built Tolerance Is Not Permanent

The body that has built tolerance for a particular kind of stress does not have that tolerance forever. Tolerance fades when not used. The runner who stops running for a year loses the running tolerance. The same run that was growth becomes corrosion.

Tolerance is also context-dependent. The body that handles work stress well may have no tolerance for relational stress. The body that handles physical stress well may have no tolerance for emotional stress. The category of stress matters. Tolerance does not transfer freely across categories.

This means the calibration is not a one-time thing. The body's felt sense in the moment is the only reliable diagnostic, precisely because the underlying tolerance is always shifting.

## How Tolerance Is Built

Tolerance is built by doing the thing while the system can still metabolize it, and resting. The standard learning loop: activation, then recovery. The body uses the recovery period to build the capacity to handle more next time.

See [[tension-and-release-cycle]] for the structural mechanism. The activation-rest cycle is what builds tolerance. Constant activation without rest does not build tolerance; it produces collapse. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]].

The rate of build depends on the body, the kind of stress, and the quality of the rest. There is no formula. The body's read on whether the activation is becoming growth or corrosion is the only signal.

## The Implication for Coaching

When a person presents stress, the coach cannot say "you have too much" or "you have too little." Neither claim is structurally available. The coach can only ask: in your body, right now, is this activation producing growth or producing compensatory behavior?

If growth: keep going. Build the tolerance. Trust the body's read.

If compensatory: the system is overloaded for the current tolerance level. The work is some combination of:
- Reducing the load temporarily so the system can recover.
- Investigating what is producing the overload — often not what the person thinks. See [[investigate-the-actual-source-of-stress]].
- Building tolerance over time through the cycle of activation and rest.

The coaching does not impose an external standard. It supports the person's developing capacity to read their own body's signal and respond.

## The Connection to the Window of Tolerance

This principle is the broader version of [[you-are-responsible-for-your-own-window-of-tolerance]]. Each person has their own window. The window cannot be set from outside. The person's read on their own window is the only reliable read, and the window itself shifts over time.

The window-of-tolerance language is most often used for emotional and nervous-system activation. The stress-tolerance principle here applies to all forms of activation — physical, emotional, cognitive, social. The same logic holds across all categories.

## The Inverse: Tolerance Is Not Always Good

A complication: built tolerance can become its own trap. The body that has built tolerance for chronic stress may stop noticing the corrosive signature. The system adapts to the dysfunctional level and treats it as baseline. See [[identity-reproduces-childhood-stress-levels]] and [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]] for related mechanisms.

The diagnostic still works, but the person may have lost the felt sensitivity to use it. In that case, the work begins with re-sensitizing — usually through [[notice-sensations-as-pleasure-experiment]] or related practices that restore the body's capacity to feel itself accurately.

Built tolerance is not always growth. Sometimes built tolerance is collapse adapted to.

## Tone

Practical, structural. The principle is delivered without judgment. There is no "right" amount of stress to aim for. The principle removes the moral framing of "too much stress" and replaces it with the empirical question of what the body in this moment is actually doing.
