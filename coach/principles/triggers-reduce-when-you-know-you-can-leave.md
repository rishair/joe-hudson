# Triggers Reduce When You Know You Can Leave

## The Principle

Two things cause triggers to diminish over time. The first is loving the part of yourself the trigger is pointing at. The second is knowing you do not have to accept the thing that is triggering you. The second is often the underappreciated one.

"Triggering does go away, but part of the way that it goes away and diminishes over time is that we love that part of ourselves. That's one way. The other way is that we no longer think that we have to accept something." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The mechanism: a trigger gets its charge from a sense of being trapped with the trigger-source. If you know you can leave, the charge drops. If you believe you have no choice, the charge stays.

## The Underlying Claim

"It's very hard to love and not be triggered by something that you think can dominate you. Unless you know you can leave, unless you know that you can draw the boundary, then you will feel dominated and then you'll be triggered. So learning to leave is a huge part of how to not be triggered by folks." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

This makes "the capacity to leave" a core element of triggered/non-triggered status, alongside the more familiar element of "doing the inner work on the pattern."

A person who has done extensive inner work but believes they cannot leave will still be triggered. A person who has done less inner work but knows they can leave may be less triggered, because the trapped quality of the situation is no longer in play.

## The Coaching Application

When a client is repeatedly triggered by a specific person, two avenues open:

1. The work on the pattern itself (the usual move): trace the emotion (see [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]]), feel what is unfelt, love the part of self the trigger is highlighting.
2. The work on the capacity to leave: do they actually believe they have to be in this relationship? In this job? In this dynamic? What would they have to face if they truly knew they could leave?

The second avenue often produces faster movement on the trigger than the first, especially when the first has been worked extensively without resolution. The unresolved trigger is sometimes not pointing at unresolved pattern work; it is pointing at unconfronted captivity.

## The Joe Example: The Business Partner Handing Off Fear

"I was working with a client the other day and she told me, 'Oh, I don't really want to address this boundary.' And the boundary was about someone handing off fear, a business partner handing off fear to her. And I said, 'It's not about the other person, it's about you.' And two things I can tell you. One is you somehow think it's okay for someone to hand fear off to you, and when you don't, it'll stop or at least become a lot less. And the second is that this is not the only place it is in your life." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

Two moves in one coaching sequence:

1. **Generalize the pattern.** The dynamic is not specific to this person. It is happening in multiple places. The client is the variable, not the partners.
2. **Locate the consent.** "You somehow think it's okay for someone to hand fear off to you." The boundary the client has not drawn is the boundary that allows the trigger to keep happening.

The boundary, drawn cleanly, often makes the trigger disappear. Not because the other person stops doing the thing, but because the client is no longer in the dominated position. The dominated position is what produces the charge.

## Why This Works Mechanistically

A trigger is, in part, a body remembering the experience of being trapped. Childhood trauma is unique partly because the child genuinely had no escape. The adult body remembers that signature. When the adult finds themselves in a situation where they cannot leave (or believe they cannot), the body re-enters the trapped state and the trigger fires with full force.

The same situation, encountered with the felt knowledge of "I can leave at any moment," produces a smaller charge. The body is not trapped. The remembered trauma is not being re-activated by current conditions. The trigger has less material to work with.

This is why [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]] -- as the capacity to leave develops, the range of situations that can be tolerated without triggering expands. Not because the person is harder. Because the situations are no longer carrying the trapped quality.

## The Refinement: Even When You Stay

The principle does not require leaving. It requires the felt-sense of being able to leave.

"When we are there or we do have to be there and accept it because we have another choice that we're making or there's something more important, it is a lot less triggering because we know we don't have to." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

A person who chooses to stay in a difficult situation (because they value something else more, because the situation has redeeming features, because they are doing it for a defined period) is in a different position than a person who feels they must stay. The choosing produces the freedom. The freedom produces the reduced trigger.

This is why two people in identical external situations can have very different internal experiences. The one who feels they have no choice is being triggered. The one who knows they are choosing it is not. Same situation, same other person, same difficult behavior. Different internal stance.

## The Marriage Version: 'It's Hard to Love What You Think Is Oppressing You'

The principle has a specific application to marriage and long-term partnership. The partner who feels they cannot leave the marriage will be triggered by everything the partner does. The same partner who has internalized "I can leave" -- not as a threat but as a felt-sense capacity -- can be far less triggered by the same behaviors.

"It's really hard to love something that you think is oppressing you. So the other way I say this, the same thing slightly differently is there is no unconditional love without empowerment. There is no real empowerment without unconditional love." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The structural claim: you cannot fully love a person you experience as the cap on your freedom. The body will not produce love toward what it experiences as cage. The capacity to leave is therefore not opposed to love; it is the prerequisite for love. The relationship that has "I can leave" inside it can also have "I am here by choice," and the second is the precondition for the love being uncoerced.

The other side of Joe's compressed teaching matters too: there is no real empowerment without unconditional love. Empowerment without love can be a cold autonomy that is also not the destination. The pair-bond of empowerment-and-love is what produces the relationship that is neither trapped nor cold.

The dynamic this principle interrupts is one of the most common shapes of relationship failure: a partner stays out of fear of leaving (financial, logistical, identity-based) and slowly accumulates rage at the partner for the trapped-feeling. The partner is not actually trapping them; the trappedness is the partner's own unprocessed fear of leaving. But the body experiences the partner as the cap and produces the corresponding triggers. The relationship deteriorates because the love cannot operate inside the experience of oppression.

The exit move is to do the work on the can-leave capacity. Sometimes this means actually leaving (briefly or permanently). Sometimes it means doing the inner work to know the leaving is possible without enacting it. Either way, the unblocking of love depends on the unblocking of the leave-capacity.

"There, you have to know that you are empowered to leave, to be able to love. You have to know that you're not trapped or you beautifully said fight like a caged animal. And that, that, that, that is not fucking pretty in a marriage." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The "fight like a caged animal" image is the specific failure mode this principle prevents. Animals fighting from inside a cage do not fight to win or to communicate; they fight from terror. The partner who feels trapped fights from the same place. The fights are bigger, more destructive, less productive than they would be from outside the cage. Knowing you can leave moves the fight out of the cage even when you stay in the relationship.

## The Tara Example: 'It Was About Her Knowing She Could Leave'

Joe gives a specific example from his and Tara's early relationship that illustrates how this principle operated between them.

"I felt the same thing that Alexa felt, which was, 'I'm angry. Why don't you meet me in my anger? This is my form of vulnerability. Why are you running from me when I'm like this?' And the more that she ran, the more I felt abandoned, the angrier I got. And she would say, 'Okay, if you treat me like this, I'm going to go, or I can't handle this, I'm leaving.' And I thought that it was about me. And what it was really about was she needed to know she could leave. She needed to know that she didn't have to put up with it. And as she stopped putting up with it, as she saw that she could leave, it wasn't about me telling her what was right or wrong for her. Then she could more and more show up for my anger and show up as herself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The structure of the example:

1. Tara's "I'm going to go" statements were initially read by Joe as being about him -- as either a threat or a rejection.
2. The real function was about her capacity to leave. She needed to confirm to her own body that the staying was a choice and not an obligation.
3. As her felt-capacity to leave developed, her trigger-response to Joe's anger reduced.
4. The reduction in her triggering produced the unexpected outcome: she became more able to be present for Joe's anger, not less.

The dynamic is counterintuitive. Joe's initial reaction was to want Tara to stay, because her leaving felt like abandonment. But her leaving (or her capacity to leave) was the work that built her ability to stay productively. Trying to keep her from leaving would have kept her stuck inside the dominated position, which would have kept her triggered, which would have kept the fight unresolvable.

The corollary: a partner who needs to leave, and is leaving in service of building the capacity to choose-to-stay, should be allowed to leave. The principle from [[you-are-responsible-for-your-own-window-of-tolerance]] is the structural fact this rests on. Their leaving is part of their work. Your work is not to prevent it.

## The Connection to Boundary Work

This principle is the underlying reason boundaries are so powerful. A boundary is the operational form of "I know I can leave."

"Hey, if you're gonna yell at me, I'm gonna ask you to stop yelling at me. If you continue to yell at me, I'm gonna leave." -- Joe Hudson's canonical boundary, [[boundaries]]

The boundary is not primarily a request that the other person change. It is the speaker establishing for themselves and the other person that exit is an option. Once exit is established as an option, the captivity-charge drops. The trigger has less to grip.

The boundary may or may not produce the behavior change in the other person. That is not what makes it powerful. What makes it powerful is what it does inside the speaker: it converts the dynamic from "I am trapped here" to "I am here by choice."

## Distinction From "Just Leave"

This principle is not "leave the people who trigger you." Leaving without the inner work tends to recreate the dynamic somewhere else. The trigger travels with the person.

This is "know that you could leave, while doing the inner work to love the part of yourself the trigger is pointing at, and let the trigger reduce through both mechanisms simultaneously."

A person who only does the leaving without the work has not done the work. A person who only does the work without the felt knowledge of being able to leave has missed half the mechanism. Both are operating with one of the two levers.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They have been doing inner work on a triggering relationship for a long time without resolution
- They keep telling themselves they should be more evolved, more accepting, less triggered
- They describe the situation as "I have to deal with X" or "I have no choice"
- They are in a relationship, job, or family system they cannot see leaving
- They are triggered by someone they could leave but have not let themselves seriously consider leaving
