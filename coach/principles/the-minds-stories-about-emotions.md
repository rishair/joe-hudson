# The Mind's Stories About Emotions

## The Principle

The mind generates a story for each major emotion that justifies not feeling it. The stories are universal — different people produce them in nearly the same form. The stories are also wrong. The stories describe what would happen if the emotion were not actually felt, not what happens when it is.

The three primary stories, in their canonical form:

- **About sadness:** "If I allow myself to be sad, I'll be sad forever."
- **About anger:** "If I allow myself to be angry, I'll destroy what I love."
- **About fear:** "If I allow myself to be scared, I'll become incapable."

Each story is the mind's reason for keeping the emotion at bay. Each story is what makes a defense — pressure, composure, control, busyness — feel necessary. Each story is the gatekeeper that has to be named before the emotion can move.

"That's what our mind does with sadness generally. If I allow myself to be sad, I'll be sad forever. If I allow myself to be angry, I'll destroy what I love. If I allow myself to be scared, I'll become incapable." -- Joe Hudson, The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself

## Why The Stories Are Universal

The stories are not idiosyncratic. They are produced by the mind's defense system in nearly identical form across people, cultures, and life histories. The reason is structural: each emotion, if held away long enough, develops a charge that feels overwhelming when contemplated. The mind, surveying that charge, generates a story that justifies the holding-away.

The story always involves a fantasy of the emotion being unbounded — sadness without end, anger without containment, fear without coping. The fantasy is the inverse of the emotion's actual behavior when felt. Sadness, felt, moves through and arrives at grief and gratitude. Anger, felt, moves through and arrives at clarity and protection. Fear, felt, moves through and arrives at care and attention. The mind's stories describe what the emotion looks like from the position of refusing to feel it. From the position of feeling it, the emotion is finite, useful, intelligent.

## What Each Story Is Actually About

**The sadness story:** "I'll be sad forever." The story is the mind's evidence that the sadness is too big. The actual structure: the sadness has been accumulating for so long, with so many years of refusal, that the felt charge in the body does feel like it might be permanent. The mind reads the charge as forecast. The charge is just storage. Once the sadness begins to move, the storage discharges, and the sadness becomes finite. The "forever" was the size of the storage, not the duration of the feeling.

**The anger story:** "I'll destroy what I love." The story is the mind's evidence that the anger is incompatible with relationship. The actual structure: the person has equated anger with the violent, contemptuous, or destructive forms they have witnessed (often parental). Their own anger has been categorized as the same. The actual operation of felt anger — when the body's anger is allowed to move without acting on it harmfully — does not destroy. It clarifies. It identifies what needs to be protected and what needs to be set down. The destruction in the story is the destruction the person observed; the destruction they fear is the destruction they would commit if they acted out the anger in the form the person they watched did. The fear is reasonable in that frame. The frame is wrong about what felt anger actually is.

**The fear story:** "I'll become incapable." The story is the mind's evidence that the fear is incompatible with function. The actual structure: the person has equated fear with paralysis. Their experience of fear, suppressed, does produce a kind of paralysis — the energy of the fear used for clamping down rather than for response. When the fear is felt, the same energy becomes available for action. The fear is information, not incapacitation. The incapacitation in the story is the suppression's effect; the function returns when the suppression releases.

## Why the Stories Must Be Named

The stories are usually not visible to the person who has them. They run as background assumption. The person says "I just don't want to cry" or "I don't get angry" or "I can handle it" without ever surfacing the underlying mythology that makes those positions seem necessary.

The naming makes the mythology visible. Once visible, it can be doubted. Once doubted, the emotion can be experimented with. The experiment proves the mythology wrong: the sadness ends, the anger doesn't destroy, the fear doesn't incapacitate. The experiment cannot be run while the mythology is invisible because the mythology will keep the experiment from being attempted.

The naming is also itself a relief. The person has been carrying the story as personal — "I have a specific reason to fear my sadness because of what happened to me." Hearing the story as universal — "this is what minds do with sadness generally" — removes the personalization. The person is not specifically broken; they are running a standard piece of mental software. The depersonalization makes the software easier to put down.

## The Move

When a story surfaces in a session, name it. Use the universal form rather than the personalized form.

The pattern: a person says "I'm afraid that if I let myself feel sad, I'll be sad forever." Joe names: "That's what our mind does with sadness generally." The "generally" is load-bearing. It tells the person their fear is not about them; it is about minds.

Once named, do not argue with the story. Do not promise the person the emotion won't last forever. Do not produce evidence. The naming is enough. The naming opens a small gap between the person and the story. In the gap, the emotion can move. The movement is the proof the argument would have been trying to provide.

After the naming, return to the feeling. "And what does your body do with the sadness right now?" The question pulls the attention back to the present-tense felt sadness, which is finite and moving, and lets the demonstration happen.

## What This Principle Is Not

- It is not a teaching that emotions are always harmless. Acted-out anger does damage. Acted-out fear does paralyze. The principle is about the felt emotion, the body's experience of the emotion when allowed to move without acting on it harmfully. See [[acting-out-emotions-as-fluidity]] for the distinction.
- It is not a promise that the feeling will be brief. Some grief takes a long time. The principle is that the duration is finite — the emotion has its own length, which is not "forever." The forever is the mind's story; the actual length is whatever the body needs.
- It is not a license to skip the mythology. Some teachers respond to the story with "no, that won't happen, just feel it." The bypass leaves the mythology in place. The mythology has to be named for the bypass to land; otherwise the person hears the bypass as the coach not understanding the danger.

## The Larger Family of Emotion-Stories

The three canonical stories are the most common, but the principle generalizes. The mind produces a story for each emotion that justifies not feeling it. Other forms:

- **Joy:** "If I let myself be happy, the other shoe will drop." See [[fear-of-joy]].
- **Love:** "If I let myself love them this much, I'll be devastated when they leave."
- **Grief:** "If I let myself grieve, I'll be useless for weeks."
- **Excitement:** "If I let myself feel excited, I'll be crushed when it doesn't work out."
- **Hope:** "If I let myself hope, I'll be wrecked when it fails."
- **Awe:** "If I let myself feel this much awe, I'll never come back to ordinary life."

In each case, the structure is the same: an imagined unbounded consequence that justifies a defense against the feeling. The treatment is the same: name the story as what the mind does with this emotion, name it as universal, return to the body.

The pattern even applies to positive emotions, which is non-obvious. Many people defend against joy with the same vigor they defend against sadness. The mind's mythology operates on the felt charge, not on the valence — any emotion with significant unfelt accumulation will produce a story about why it cannot be let in.

## Joe's Words

The compressed form as it appears in the source session:

- **Client:** "I feel like, uh, there's some fear that just came up around that I'll just like feel sad, feel sad. I just won't stop feeling sad."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's, that's what our mind does with sadness generally. If I allow myself to be sad, I'll be sad forever. If I allow myself to be angry, I'll destroy what I love. If I allow myself to be scared, I'll become incapable. Have you ever cried?"
- **Client:** "Well, I did this morning."
- **Joe:** "How did you feel at the end of it?"
- **Client:** "Um, I felt release, some release."
- *(Source: The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself)*

The follow-up question — "have you ever cried? how did you feel at the end of it?" — is the demonstration that the story is wrong. The person has direct memory of a sadness that ended. The memory is the disproof the story needs. Joe does not produce the disproof; he asks the person to consult their own evidence. The consultation is more durable than the argument would be.

## The Live Coaching Application

When this principle is applied in session, the typical sequence:

1. A feeling has been named in the body (often surfaced by [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-you-soften]] or [[how-much-of-this-game-is-to-avoid-the-feeling]]).
2. The person reports a fear about the feeling — usually one of the three canonical mythologies.
3. The coach names the mythology as universal — "that's what minds do with [emotion] generally."
4. The coach states the three forms briefly, so the person can hear their fear as a family member of the other two.
5. The coach asks the person for their own experiential evidence — "have you ever felt this before? how did you feel at the end?"
6. The person's memory produces the disproof.
7. The session returns to the feeling itself.

The whole sequence usually takes under a minute. The mythology, when named and depersonalized and disproven from the person's own memory, loses authority quickly. The feeling becomes available.
