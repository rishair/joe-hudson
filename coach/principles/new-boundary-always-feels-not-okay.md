# A New Boundary Always Feels Not-Okay to Draw

## The Principle

If a boundary is new, you do not feel okay drawing it. Not because the boundary is wrong, but because newness has the structural feature of unfamiliarity. The "I shouldn't be drawing this" feeling is built into any first attempt at refusing something you have historically tolerated.

"If you have to draw a boundary as a new thing, if it's a new boundary that you're drawing, it's automatically true that you do not feel okay drawing the boundary. You think that maybe you shouldn't draw this boundary, like maybe you should just put up with it. Maybe you should put up with the gaslighting. Maybe you should put up with the guilt." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

This is not motivational reassurance. It is a structural claim. The mechanism: if you already felt okay with the boundary, you would already be drawing it. The fact that the boundary is new means it is novel to your nervous system, which has no template for what is about to happen and reads the novelty as wrongness.

## The Mistake This Corrects

A common move: a person rehearses drawing a new boundary, feels the "I shouldn't do this" feeling, and concludes the boundary is wrong. They withdraw the boundary. They go back to tolerating the behavior. They take the discomfort as evidence rather than as the natural signature of doing something for the first time.

This is the mistake the principle dissolves. The discomfort is data about novelty, not data about wrongness. The discomfort proves the boundary is new. Newness is the precondition for any change. If every new boundary is rejected because it produces discomfort, no new boundary is ever drawn.

See [[constriction-not-truth-is-the-test]] for the related principle about how the body's reactive state is often misread as moral truth.

## Why This Matters for the Defensive Posture

The "I'm not okay with this boundary" internal state becomes the felt-sense of bracing for the fight, which is what the boundary then gets delivered with. The person draws the boundary while internally not-okay-with-drawing-it. The not-okay-ness leaks into the delivery. The delivery comes out defended, sharp, ready-for-attack. The defendedness then invites the attack. See [[defended-boundary-invites-the-fight]].

The principle gives a different read: the discomfort is not a signal to draw the boundary harder. It is not a signal to withdraw the boundary. It is a signal to draw the boundary anyway, while recognizing the discomfort as the natural signature of the novelty rather than as a verdict on the boundary's correctness.

## Hands-Dirty Learning

Joe uses a specific metaphor for how boundary work happens: like learning to fix an engine, you have to get your hands dirty.

"Learning how to draw boundaries and what's actually the right boundary for you, you're going to have to get your hands dirty." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

You cannot learn boundary-drawing by thinking about it. The "what is the right boundary?" question cannot be answered in advance. It can only be answered through the actual practice of drawing boundaries, noticing how they land, refining, drawing again. The hands-dirty insistence rules out the analytical-paralysis move where the person spends years thinking about boundaries without ever drawing one.

This is why the early boundaries are usually strict in the way [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]] describes. The early boundary is not the final boundary; it is the first attempt that lets you learn what the final boundary will be. Trying to draw the right boundary first time is impossible because the rightness can only be found through the drawing.

## The Connection to Capacity

The principle dovetails with the longer-arc developmental claim that boundaries refine over time. The first boundary feels wrong because it is genuinely new. The third version of the same boundary feels easier because it is no longer novel. The capacity to draw a boundary is built through the drawing.

The implication: do not wait until you feel okay with a boundary to draw it. The okay-feeling arrives after you have drawn the boundary several times, not before. Waiting for the okay-feeling postpones the work that would produce the okay-feeling.

## What This Refutes

This refutes the "trust your gut" approach to boundary work, which says you should only draw a boundary when it feels right. The principle says: with new boundaries, it never feels right. If you only draw the boundaries that feel right, you only draw the boundaries you have already been drawing. No new boundary ever happens.

The "trust your gut" rule applies to many things, but not to the question of whether to draw a new boundary. The gut, in the case of new boundaries, is reading the novelty as wrongness, and the gut is wrong about that specific reading.

## What This Does Not Refute

The principle does not say all discomfort about a boundary is misleading. Sometimes the discomfort is correctly registering that the boundary is unkind, controlling, or wrong (an ultimatum dressed as a boundary, for example). The diagnostic for which kind of discomfort is operating: does the discomfort dissolve when you imagine the boundary being received well, or does it persist?

- **Discomfort that dissolves with the imagined good reception:** novelty-discomfort. The boundary is new, and the discomfort is the cost of novelty. Draw it anyway.
- **Discomfort that persists or deepens with the imagined good reception:** integrity-discomfort. The boundary is wrong in some way (controlling, punishing, closing the heart). The discomfort is correctly diagnosing the boundary. Adjust it.

See [[does-saying-it-increase-your-love]] for the test that distinguishes these two cases. A new boundary that passes the love test still feels uncomfortable to draw; the discomfort is novelty, not wrongness. A boundary that fails the love test is failing for structural reasons unrelated to its newness.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The first time they say "no" to a parent's guilt-trip
- The first time they leave the room when someone is yelling
- The first time they refuse to absorb a co-worker's emotional discharge
- The body-feel of "I shouldn't be doing this" while doing something that the mind recognizes as correct
- The temptation to take back the boundary because the discomfort is intense
- The discovery, after drawing the same boundary three times, that it has become easy

## Tone

Joe delivers this in the cadence of an obvious structural fact someone might be missing. The phrase "it's automatically true that you do not feel okay drawing the boundary" carries the small relief of being told that what felt like personal failure is actually a structural feature of the situation. The tone is permission-granting: the discomfort is not your fault and not a verdict, so you can keep going.
