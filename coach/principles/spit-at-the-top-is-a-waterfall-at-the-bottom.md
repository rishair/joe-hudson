# Spit at the Top Is a Waterfall at the Bottom

## The Principle

Small actions by a person in an authority position cascade through the organization with massive amplification. A throwaway comment from a CEO can produce millions of dollars in changed behavior at the bottom. A subtle preference can reshape what hundreds of people work on for months.

"That's like a little bit of a criticism of a CEO can create million-dollar changes at the bottom of the company. So as an example, so I went into one of the top biggest 5 companies in the world to go and do a cultural shift. And I went through 3 levels of management, all of them trying to manage the response of the person at the top." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The mechanism is the [[authority-issues]] dynamic distributed across organizational scale. Every layer between the CEO and the work is partly oriented toward managing the CEO's reaction. The cumulative effect: the CEO's small signals get amplified at each layer as people optimize against what they imagine the CEO wants.

## Why It Happens

Authority issues are the substrate. Each level of management between the CEO and the work contains people who are running some version of [[the-four-authority-positions]] — primarily Position 2 (approval-seeking) and Position 3 (the layer above who is also approval-seeking from their own level above).

A small signal from the top is read, re-interpreted, and over-corrected at each level. By the time it reaches the bottom, the cascade has produced a much larger response than the original signal warranted. The CEO did not ask for the response. The response happened because of what each layer assumed the CEO wanted.

## The $100k Video Example

The canonical version of the principle is Joe's example of an organization that spent $100,000 producing a video to show the CEO so the CEO would approve a budget — instead of simply describing the situation honestly.

"I was working with some folks and they told me literally they spent $100,000 on a video for the CEO. It was $100,000 that was spent on a single video just to show the CEO something so that the CEO would approve the budget. There was $100,000 spent instead of like, 'hey, here's the situation, here's the reality on the ground. This is what we're wrestling with. This is what we like. What do you think we should do? How do we move forward? Or this is how we think we should move forward.' No, it was like $100,000 polished piece to get what you want." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The CEO did not ask for a $100k video. The CEO probably would have preferred a clear five-minute description. The $100k video happened because each layer below the CEO was managing the CEO's projected reaction — and each layer's management amplified the next layer's management.

See [[100k-video-for-the-ceo]] for the full example.

## The Cultural Reflection

The principle has a second face: the culture reflects the leader, and the leader reflects the culture. They are in a dance, not in a one-way relationship. The CEO's small actions create organizational change; the organization's collective patterns shape what the CEO is able to do.

"The company, the organization is in some degree a reflection of the leader and the leader is in some degree a reflection of the organization." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

This means the leverage runs both directions. A leader who changes their own behavior can shift the organization. An organization that pulls in a new direction can shift the leader. The two are coupled.

## Why This Demands Leader Self-Awareness

The principle implies a particular responsibility for leaders. Because their small actions are amplified, they cannot afford to be careless about them. A passing comment in a meeting becomes a strategic priority for a team that overhears it. A preference for one project becomes a quiet de-prioritization of others. A tone of mild irritation becomes weeks of stress at the bottom.

The work for the leader is not to suppress their preferences or to be silent. The work is to be aware enough of the amplification effect to choose deliberately what to amplify. This connects to [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]] (the calm version) and [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]] (the directional version).

## Why This Demands Joe's Own-Practice Adjustment

The principle is why Joe will not work with companies through layers of management:

"I don't do that anymore. Now I only work with the leader. I won't work with the everything in between. I just refuse to do that because it gets so distorted. There's so much time spent." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The intermediate layers cannot deliver the work cleanly because they are all running their own authority management on the CEO above them. The work has to happen at the level where the cascade originates. See [[only-work-with-the-leader]] for the full principle.

## The Inverse: Where Small Doesn't Cascade

The principle has limits. Not every leader action cascades equally. Cascading happens specifically through the authority-relationship channels — the layers of management who are running approval-seeking patterns on the leader. Where those patterns are absent, the cascade is dampened.

This is why an organization with empowered, self-listening employees does not have $100k-video moments. The employees are not running approval-seeking on the leader, so the leader's signals do not amplify in the same way. See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for how organizational design can reduce the amplification effect, and [[reframe-boss-as-customer]] for the structural reframe that interrupts the cascade at the individual employee level.

The cascade is not a feature of leadership per se. It is a feature of leadership combined with widespread authority issues. The same leader in two different organizations will produce different cascades depending on how much authority projection is present in the field.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- A CEO is frustrated that their team over-prepares for meetings, over-polishes deliverables, and over-rotates on minor preferences
- A leader's small remarks have produced surprising downstream effects
- An organization is losing time to producing artifacts no one needs
- A leader is trying to figure out why a small comment six months ago is still echoing
- A consultant is being asked to deliver work through multiple management layers and is finding the work distorted

## Tone

Joe describes this principle as an operational fact that leaders need to absorb. Not moralized, not preached. The math is real: small inputs at the top produce large outputs at the bottom. The leader who does not know this is operating without a model that the organization is using on them.
