# You Can't Make Agreements During the Fight

## The Principle

Agreements about how to fight must be made when both nervous systems are calm. Mid-fight agreement-making does not work, because by the time you are in the fight, both parties are activated, and the activated state cannot make good agreements.

"That doesn't mean like, oh, we're about to get in a fight now, let me — how are we going to do this? That doesn't work because by the time you're getting in a fight, your nervous system is already wild. So this is about like sometime when you're not fighting, sit down and say, how do we want to fight together?" -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The timing is the load-bearing piece. Without the right timing, the practice of fight agreements (see [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]]) cannot work.

## Why the Activated State Cannot Decide

The mechanism is straightforward. When the nervous system enters sympathetic activation, the prefrontal cortex partially goes offline. The capacity for slow, integrative thinking — the capacity that good agreement-making requires — is exactly the capacity that gets reduced.

In addition, the trauma firing in a fight (see [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]]) routes the partner from "person I love and am collaborating with" into "threat I am defending against." Agreement-making with a perceived threat does not produce collaborative agreements. It produces appeasement, control attempts, or capitulation.

The principle is the same principle that underlies why you cannot make good decisions about a relationship at the moment of greatest distress, why you cannot promise reliably in a state of fear, why you cannot negotiate well when activated. The activated state is not the agreement-making state.

## The Calm-State / Activated-State Pattern

The structural move is: decisions made in the calm state are inherited by the activated state.

When two people sit down outside a fight and agree "we don't threaten divorce as a fight move," the agreement exists. The next time one of them is activated and the impulse to threaten divorce arises, the existing agreement is what is present. The activated state does not have to figure out whether threatening divorce is okay; the calm state already figured that out.

This is a more general principle than fight agreements. It is the same logic as setting up your environment when calm so the activated future-you cannot reach for the bad option (see [[avoid-the-decision-by-design]]). The activated state is real, will arrive, and cannot be trusted to make first-order decisions. The work is to load up enough decisions in the calm state that the activated state can run on rails.

## The Failure Mode This Refutes

The failure mode the principle is interrupting:

A fight begins. One partner, recognizing the fight is starting, says, "Okay, let's talk about how we want to handle this." The other partner, already activated, hears this as a stalling tactic, a power move, or a refusal to engage with the actual content. The "agreement-making" becomes a new fight.

Or: the fight escalates to a particular bad point. One partner, mid-escalation, says, "We can't keep doing this. Let's agree right now to stop yelling." The other partner, in full activation, agrees in order to end the moment. The agreement is not real because it was made by an activated nervous system trying to escape a flooded state.

Both failure modes look like agreement-making and produce nothing usable.

## When the Right Timing Is

The right timing is some time when there is no current conflict, both partners are regulated, there is enough relational warmth available to do collaborative work, and there is no looming pressure that would activate either nervous system.

This often means: not right after a fight has ended, when the nervous systems are still settling. Not right before bed. Not when one or both partners are in another stressor (work crunch, child crisis, illness). Not while drinking. Not while one partner is trying to fix the other.

A good window is a calm weekend afternoon, a walk together, a conversation after a meal. The window is whatever produces the conditions of two people who are present, warm to each other, and not currently in or near activation.

## Why This Principle Is Easy to Miss

The intuitive impulse is the opposite: agreements about how to fight should be made when the fight is happening, because that is when the need is obvious. The need is most visible in the moment; the moment seems like the time to do something about it.

This intuition is wrong. The need is most visible in the moment because the activation makes the cost of the current pattern most felt. But the visibility of the cost does not generate the capacity to address it. The capacity to address it has to be there before the cost shows up. The agreement has to be in place before the activation, so that the activation can run into the agreement and find it as a structure that is already holding.

## The Connection to In-the-Moment Moves

This principle does not say that the calm state has to do all the work. It says that the calm state has to do the agreement-making work. There is still significant work that happens in the activated state: regulating the nervous system back down (see [[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]], [[hands-on-body-to-regulate-nervous-system]]), naming what is happening to the partner (see [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]]), executing the agreements that were made in the calm state.

The split is: agreement-making in calm, agreement-keeping in activation. The activated state is responsible for trying to honor agreements that the calm state made. It is not responsible for inventing agreements while activated.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the conversation about how to fight, attempted in the middle of a fight, made the fight worse
- The relief of discovering that the conversation can be had at a different time entirely
- The understanding that an agreement made during one fight, on the spot, did not hold when the next fight came
- The realization that all of the in-the-fight moves they have been trying require pre-loaded agreements that were never made

## Tone

Joe states this principle as obvious-once-you-see-it. The "that doesn't work" delivery is matter-of-fact. He is not warning against a sin; he is naming a physical fact about how nervous systems work. The corrective is correspondingly simple: do it at a different time.
