# All Team Dysfunctions Reduce to a Lack of Trust

## The Principle

Every team dysfunction, when traced to its root, is a trust failure. The Lencioni framework lists five dysfunctions (absence of trust, fear of conflict, lack of commitment, avoidance of accountability, inattention to results). The four downstream dysfunctions are forms of the first. They are the shapes the team takes when trust is missing.

"I encourage you to see that all is, the first dysfunction, which is a lack of trust. So they talk about things like accountability and not paying attention to results and avoiding conflict, but all of that is ways to deteriorate trust." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Dysfunctional Team

A team that avoids conflict is a team that does not trust each other to handle truth. A team without commitment is a team where the members do not trust the decisions enough to bind themselves to them. A team that avoids accountability is a team where peers do not trust each other enough to call out underperformance. A team inattentive to results is a team where the members do not trust that everyone is rowing in the same direction.

## Why This Reframe Matters

The five-dysfunctions presentation can be read as five separate problems requiring five separate interventions. The reframe is that there is one problem with five visible symptoms. The intervention is the same: build trust, in the multiple dimensions described in [[trust-as-multifaceted]].

The downstream effect is diagnostic clarity. A team that is "not paying attention to results" can be worked on at the symptom level (better metrics, better dashboards, more accountability rituals) or at the root level (what is the team not trusting each other to do?). The first produces compliance theater. The second produces actual functionality.

## The Mechanism

Trust is built through conflict survived together. See [[trust-requires-conflict]]. The conflict-avoidant team cannot build the trust it needs to function. The lack of trust then produces more conflict avoidance because conflict feels too risky. The loop continues. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].

This is why interventions that try to fix the downstream dysfunctions without addressing trust fail. Adding accountability rituals to a low-trust team produces resentment, not accountability. Pushing for commitment in a low-trust team produces performance of commitment without actual commitment. The downstream dysfunctions are stable because the upstream condition (low trust) has not changed.

## The Lencioni Recommendation

The book *Five Dysfunctions of a Team* by Patrick Lencioni is the standard reference for the framework. Joe endorses the book directly.

"There's a book called *Five Dysfunctions of a Team*, which I think is an excellent book. There's the 5 dysfunctions, but they all -- I encourage you to see that all is the first dysfunction, which is a lack of trust. So they talk about things like accountability and not paying attention to results and avoiding conflict, but all of that is ways to deteriorate trust. But it's, it's told in some, in a very, very beautiful way. It's like a story. It's easy to read. So I highly recommend the book if you want to dig deeper in this topic." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Dysfunctional Team

The book's value, in Joe's framing, is that it makes the dynamics visible through narrative. The risk is reading the five dysfunctions as a checklist of separate problems. The reframe collapses them into one and makes the work more focused.

## When This Reframe Lands

- A leader is overwhelmed by the number of things wrong with their team. The reframe converts five problems into one.
- A team has tried multiple structural fixes and none of them have stuck. The reframe explains why -- the fixes have been at the symptom level.
- A team is in agreement that they have a "communication problem" or an "accountability problem" without being able to make those problems shift. The reframe redirects attention to the trust dimension underneath.
- A leader is selecting from competing frameworks for team improvement. The reframe simplifies the selection: any framework that produces trust will help; any framework that bypasses trust will not.
