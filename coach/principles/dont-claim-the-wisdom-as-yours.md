# Don't Claim the Wisdom as Yours

## The Principle

The wisdom that moves through a teacher, coach, speaker, or facilitator is not theirs. It is being channeled. The same wisdom is available to everybody else in the room. The teacher's job is to be a clean conduit, not to take credit for what comes through them.

The principle has a hard edge. If you start to claim the wisdom as yours, a predictable cascade of consequences follows. The cascade is not a moral verdict. It is a structural pattern. Take ownership of the wisdom, and you become responsible for it. Responsibility becomes obligation. Obligation becomes resentment. The resentment cannot be released because the position has perks (money, attention, the sexy girlfriend, the people looking at you) that are hard to walk away from. The locked-in resentment looks for a release valve. The release valve is typically an addiction, a need for control, or a slide into narcissism.

"When I see the people who have done it the other way where they get, oh, it's me, then with that, oh, it's me, there's a responsibility that becomes an obligation that turns into a resentment, but they can't leave it because the girlfriend's too sexy. It's like making them lots of money and everybody's looking at them, and then it leads to like some crazy addiction typically, and like need for control. So to me, in my mind, having worked with people where that has happened, where it was them, it was all about them, eventually I've just seen where that lands in a, like, a very terrible place." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

## The Cascade in Sequence

The mechanism is sequential. Each step follows from the previous one. Naming the steps lets the coach (or the teacher being coached) catch the cascade before it locks in.

1. **The teacher does work that lands.** Insights arrive. Coaching shifts people. Speeches move audiences. The work is genuinely producing change.

2. **The teacher misreads the source.** Instead of seeing the work as something being channeled, the teacher starts to think the work is theirs. "I'm doing this. I'm changing lives. I'm the one who knows."

3. **Ownership produces responsibility.** If the work is theirs, then the responsibility for the work is theirs too. The students' growth, the audience's response, the outcomes -- all become things the teacher is responsible for producing.

4. **Responsibility becomes obligation.** Responsibility taken on without choice slides into obligation. "I have to keep producing. I have to keep showing up. I have to keep delivering." The freedom that was in the work disappears.

5. **Obligation produces resentment.** Whatever a person does out of obligation rather than desire produces resentment. The teacher starts to resent the students, the audience, the work itself. The original love is buried under the weight of the must.

6. **The resentment cannot be released.** The teacher cannot simply walk away. The structure that has built up has too many rewards: financial security, public recognition, status, often romantic options that did not exist before, identity as a teacher or expert. Walking away would mean losing all of it. The teacher is stuck.

7. **The release valve becomes pathological.** Resentment that cannot be released has to leak somewhere. The common forms: a serious addiction (alcohol, drugs, sex, food, work), a hyper-developed need for control over the staff or students, sliding into narcissistic certainty, or all three.

8. **The teacher goes wonky.** What outsiders see as the teacher "going berserk" or "becoming a guru" is the late-stage result of this cascade. See [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] for the full pattern of how the relationship with the audience compounds this trajectory.

## The Alternative: Channel, Not Source

The alternative the principle prescribes is to acknowledge that the wisdom is not the teacher's. It is being channeled. The teacher's relationship to the work is the relationship of a conduit, not a source.

"I can acknowledge that it's not me, it's never been me, it's like I'm channeling something, or I can be, you know, with some terrible habits trying to get the fuck away from this feeling of I'm responsible for everything and I'm alone in it. So those seem to be -- eventually those seem to be the options when the pressure gets too big." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The two options Joe describes are stark because the pressure of teaching at scale is real. Some teachers manage the pressure by claiming the work and going pathological. Some teachers manage the pressure by recognizing the work is not theirs and staying clean. There is rarely a viable middle path. The pressure forces a resolution one way or the other.

## What "It's Not Me" Actually Looks Like

The recognition that "it's not me" is not a humility performance. It is a felt-sense relocation of where the wisdom lives.

It looks like:

- **Acknowledging the lineage.** Joe describes the moment a long-term student told him "it didn't change my life, it changed generations" -- and Joe's recognition that this transformation was not actually his work. It was the work of the twenty people who had taught him, and the twenty people who had taught them, and the people before that, some of them two thousand years ago.

- **Recognizing the room.** When Joe is in a group of thirty students and an insight is about to arrive, he often does not speak, because he is fairly certain someone else in the room will say it within the next few minutes. The wisdom is in the room. He is not the only available channel.

- **Naming what arrived without claiming it.** When something comes through that feels especially clean, the orientation is "look at what just came" rather than "look at what I just did."

- **Refusing the projections.** Students will sometimes try to make the teacher the source. The clean move is to refuse the projection, redirect to the student's own experience, and keep the wisdom relocated where it actually belongs. See [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]].

## The Sexy Girlfriend Test

The most colorful piece of the cascade is the lock-in mechanism Joe names: "the girlfriend's too sexy." Translated out of metaphor: once a teacher's life starts to depend on the rewards of the position (money, recognition, status, sometimes romantic options), it becomes very hard to walk away even when the work has become resentment.

The test is to ask: if all the perks were stripped tomorrow, would I want to keep doing this? The answer reveals whether the teacher is still in the channeling orientation or has slid into the it's-me orientation. The teacher who is in the channeling orientation can walk away and would, if the channeling stopped feeling right. The teacher who has organized their life around the perks cannot walk away even when they should.

This is also why the cascade is hard to interrupt mid-flight. The lock-in is real. The teacher who is six years into the cascade has often built infrastructure (staff, financial commitments, public identity) that makes the recognition of the cascade terrifying rather than freeing. The earlier the recognition arrives, the more options remain.

## The Macro of What Joe Teaches in Speaking

Joe extends Tristan's speaking teaching into this larger frame. Tristan's whole methodology is about teaching speakers to enter the state where they are channeling rather than producing. The same principle Tristan is teaching at the speech level applies at the life level.

"If you come in thinking you're responsible, thinking it's about you, thinking about it's about your reputation, thinking about like it's your wisdom, you're gonna give a shitty speech. If you can walk in and you can be like more in faith, more in trust, more waiting for the butler, knowing that it's about the butler, it's not about you, then the speech goes well. It feels like that's like a life thing. It's not just a speaking thing." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The speech that goes well comes from the channeling orientation. The life that goes well comes from the channeling orientation. The cascade described in this principle is what happens when the channeling orientation is lost at the level of how a person organizes their work. The speech that goes wrong is a small version of the same misreading. The life that goes wrong is the same misreading at scale, with locked-in consequences.

## What Channeling Looks Like in Practice (Concrete Markers)

A teacher in the channeling stance:

- Refuses to defend the teaching when challenged.
- Names the lineage. Talks about who they learned from, how the wisdom has been carried, what is borrowed and what is integrated.
- Holds the work loosely enough that it can be updated, revised, abandoned, or transformed.
- Encourages students to find their own teachers, their own versions, their own integrations.
- Treats the work as a service to those it helps, not as a possession to be protected.
- Notices if income or fame becomes the binding constraint and works to keep the option to walk away alive.

A teacher in the authorship trap:

- Defends the teaching against criticism, often with subtle aggression.
- Erases the lineage and presents the work as original.
- Holds the work rigidly. Any deviation from the original framing feels like betrayal.
- Discourages students from learning from anyone else.
- Treats the work as a possession to be protected. Becomes territorial about students, ideas, methods.
- Cannot imagine walking away. The work is the identity. Loss of work would be loss of self.

The diagnostic for which stance is operative is not what the teacher says. It is whether the teacher can walk away. A teacher who internally recognizes channeling can stop teaching tomorrow and feel relief. A teacher who internally claims authorship cannot. The trapped-ness is the symptom.

## This Is Not False Humility

A natural objection: "Isn't this just performative humility? Of course the teacher built something. Saying 'it's not me' is just modesty theater."

The objection misses the point. The principle is not about how the teacher *presents* themselves. The principle is about how the teacher *holds* the work internally. The internal holding determines whether the cascade begins. A teacher who internally claims authorship while externally performing humility is still in the cascade. A teacher who internally recognizes channeling and externally takes appropriate ownership (signing their name, getting paid, accepting feedback) is not.

See [[own-your-narcissism]] for the related principle that points at the same dynamic from the other side. You can own that you wanted to be heard, you can own the role, you can own the income -- and still recognize that the wisdom itself is not yours. Both moves are happening at once.

## Why Teachers Slip Into Authorship

The reason teachers fall into the authorship trap is not malice. It is that authorship is reinforced by everyone around the teacher.

Early-stage teachers and entrepreneurs are particularly vulnerable. Tristan names this directly:

"It's almost like you have no choice but to pump up your chest and say, what I'm doing is so important and so amazing and I'm changing lives. And you can, you can get caught into that so easily." -- Tristan de Montebello, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The marketing requires it. The funding requires it. The identity-building of the early phase requires it. The teacher gets pulled into authorship without consciously choosing it. Each step of the cascade then follows naturally because the first step was rewarded.

The reversal of the cascade requires conscious work. It requires the teacher to internally pull themselves out of authorship even when externally the role demands a degree of authorship-performance. The internal work is harder than the external work. But the internal work is what determines whether the cascade gets installed.

## The Connection to Burnout in Helpers

This principle is closely related to the burnout patterns Joe sees in helping professions. Therapists, coaches, doctors, pastors, founders, leaders all show similar cascades. The mechanism is the same: claim ownership of the work, become responsible, become obligated, become resentful, cannot leave, addiction or control.

The relevant fix is not "set better boundaries" -- that addresses the symptom. The relevant fix is to revisit the original framing of the work. Was the work being claimed as personal authorship? If so, the cascade was inevitable. If the framing can shift to channeling, the obligation can release. From there, the burnout has somewhere to go.

This is why Joe often points clients in helping professions back to the question: "Whose work is this? Are you generating it, or is it moving through you?" The diagnostic surfaces the trap.

## Why This Is Hard to Hold

The channeling orientation is constantly under threat from a few specific forces:

- **Audience preference for certainty.** Audiences want teachers who claim the wisdom. A teacher who keeps saying "this isn't mine, it's been around forever, you have access to it too" is less marketable than one who claims authority. See [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] for how this preference shapes teachers over time.

- **The need to monetize.** The work has to pay for itself. Paying for itself usually requires positioning that involves claiming expertise. The positioning slides back into ownership of the wisdom.

- **The seduction of being seen as wise.** It feels good to be the one who knows. Even teachers who have explicitly committed to the channeling orientation feel the pull. The pull is normal. What matters is whether the pull is recognized and resisted.

- **Loneliness of the role.** The teacher who claims the wisdom gets to be the protagonist of the story. The teacher who keeps the wisdom relocated has to do without that ego nourishment. The role of being the conduit is structurally less rewarding than the role of being the source.

These forces are why even healthy teachers slip. The principle is not "you have to hold this perfectly." The principle is "notice when you have slipped and relocate." The slipping is normal. The relocation is the practice.

## Joe's Own Realization

Joe describes a specific moment that crystallized this for him: a conversation with three years' worth of his masterclass graduates, where someone said "it didn't change my life, it changed generations." Joe had felt that for himself but had never felt it for the people he worked with. The full felt sense of generational impact hit him.

In the same moment, the recognition arrived that it wasn't his work. It was the lineage. The twenty teachers who taught him. The two-thousand-year-old teachers who taught them. The cascade of channels stretching back.

"In that recognition, there was also the recognition that that wasn't actually my work. Like, it wasn't me doing that. It was like the 20 people who had a huge influence and taught me, and it was the 20 people, and some of them 2,000 years old, and the people who taught them, and the people who taught them. And it's like, it's such this interesting thing. It feels so good in my system when I see [it that way]." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The body marker matters: "it feels so good in my system." The channeling orientation produces relief, lightness, and aliveness. The it's-me orientation produces weight, isolation, and grasping. The body knows which orientation it is in.

## The Archaeological Frame

Tristan offers a related framing Joe affirms: the work is archaeological. The teacher is not inventing anything. They are unearthing something that already exists and packaging it in a way that the current zeitgeist can receive. The truth being shared was discoverable before; it will be discoverable after; the teacher is one channel among many.

"It feels like we're just unearthing something that already exists like an archaeologist, and that we're just unearthing the truth, sharing it, packaging and sharing it in a way that can be understood in our current zeitgeist. But really, we're not inventing anything. I'm just bringing this stuff back up and presenting it to people so that they could take advantage of it." -- Tristan de Montebello, as quoted by Joe in agreement, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

The archaeological frame is another version of the channeling frame. It locates the wisdom in something older than the teacher. The teacher is one excavator. There have been many. There will be many more. The teacher's job is to dig carefully and present what is found in a usable form. The find is not the digger's.

## The Channel-Recognition Is Not the End: The Integration Where Personal Power Returns

The channeling orientation is structurally necessary to avoid the cascade. It is also, held alone, incomplete. The teacher who has spent years internalizing "this isn't me, it's the lineage moving through me" eventually reaches a stage where the next move is to integrate the recognition with its complement: this is also me. The work I have done is mine. The skill is mine. The presence is mine. The influence I now hold is mine. Denying this becomes its own form of identity-defense -- the defense of "the one who has transcended ownership."

Joe articulates this directly in the context of his own developmental arc:

"There was a very non-personal form of humility. And now there's this recognition of, oh no, it's me. I've done this work and this shit wouldn't be happening without me. And really, this is not me at all. It's holding both of those two things." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The integration is not a retreat from the channeling principle. The channeling principle is what allowed the practitioner to do the work without being consumed by the cascade. The integration is the next stage, in which the channel-recognition is preserved while the personal-power recognition is added. Both are held at the same time. The teacher recognizes that the wisdom moves through them and also that the wisdom would not be moving through this particular channel if the channel had not been cleared by this particular work.

Why this matters specifically for the cascade-avoidance work:

- The teacher who refuses to acknowledge their personal contribution at all becomes structurally unaccountable for their actual influence. The "it's not me" framing, used as a way to avoid the power that the role actually holds, leaves the practitioner unable to take responsibility for what they do with the platform.
- The teacher who holds only the channeling stance can develop a particular kind of subtle narcissism: the defended position of being the one-who-doesn't-claim. The defense is identical in structure to other identity-defenses, just with humbler content.
- The integrated stance ("it's the lineage" *and* "I'm the channel and I have actually done the work") is the structural location from which the role can be held over time without either the cascade or the inverted-cascade.

This is the third stage of the [[humility-developmental-arc]]. The principle Don't Claim the Wisdom as Yours is the operational form of Stage 2 (the channel recognition). The integration with personal power is Stage 3. Both stages are real. Stage 3 does not negate Stage 2; it integrates Stage 2 with what Stage 2 left unaddressed.

The order matters. A practitioner who tries to install Stage 3 without first having genuinely occupied Stage 2 will produce a kind of narcissism dressed up as integration. The integration requires the prior dissolution of authorship. The personal-power recognition then arrives as a recovery rather than as a claim.

See [[humility-developmental-arc]] for the full developmental view and [[own-your-narcissism]] for the related operational principle that handles the personal-power side of the integration.

## Tone

Direct and slightly grim. Joe describes the cascade with the affect of someone who has watched it land people in very bad places. The naming of the perks ("the girlfriend's too sexy") is colorful but the underlying observation is sober. The implication is not "be humble." The implication is "if you misread the source of the wisdom, here is what will happen to your life over the next ten years." The principle is offered as a warning, not as a moral injunction.
