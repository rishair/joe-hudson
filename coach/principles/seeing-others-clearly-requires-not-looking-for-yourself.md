# Seeing Others Clearly Requires Not Looking for Yourself in Them

## The Principle

The leader who has the capacity to observe other people in a detached way, see what each person's actual strengths and styles are, and place them where they will excel, has usually achieved that capacity not by becoming better at people-reading in general but by removing themselves from the variable being measured. As long as the leader is looking for themselves in the other person (consciously or unconsciously), the observation is contaminated. Once the leader has stopped looking for themselves, the other person becomes visible as they actually are.

This is why the leader who has done internal work often becomes much better at sorting people, hiring, promoting, and placing -- not because they learned a new technique, but because they stopped doing the thing that was blocking accurate observation. The unblocking is the move.

"One thing that I have gotten good at is figuring out how to sort people, like figuring out what people's inherent strengths and abilities and styles are, and figuring out how to sort them into the right roles. I can't articulate exactly how, but objectively, I think I have been much better at this than most people running companies. And it's been a great asset for us." -- Sam Altman, Sam Altman

The mechanism Sam offers, after Brett suggests "the more you understand yourself, the more you can see the parts of yourself and others that they might not see":

"Maybe, or maybe you can just observe other people in a really detached way because you're not trying to do it in relation to yourself. You're not looking for yourself, you're not -- I think most people do end up just promoting people that look like themselves. And instead, you can just, when you've got that out of the way, you can just sort of really focus on what is this person's -- what makes them them? What are they going to excel at? And not, you know, have to relate it to yourself and make a better decision." -- Sam Altman, Sam Altman

## Why Most People Promote in Their Own Image

The default mode of leadership observation is comparative. The leader assesses each person against the leader's own template: how does this person handle pressure compared to how I handle pressure? How does this person think compared to how I think? How does this person communicate compared to how I communicate?

The output of comparative observation is a ranking by similarity. The most-similar person wins, because they most closely match the template the leader is unconsciously using as the standard for "good." Promotion goes to the person who looks like the leader. The team becomes homogeneous. Capacities the leader does not have become invisible to the leader, which means they cannot be promoted, which means the team systematically lacks them.

This is the structural reason that homogeneous executive teams persist even at companies that nominally want diversity. The leader's eye, untrained out of comparative observation, can only see what is in the leader's own template. Diverse hires get evaluated against the same template and graded down on the dimensions where they differ from the leader, regardless of whether those differences are strengths or weaknesses.

## The Mechanism of Detached Observation

When the leader is no longer using themselves as the template, the question they ask about each person changes. The old question was: "How does this person compare to me?" The new question is: "What makes this person them? What are they going to excel at?"

The new question opens an empirical inquiry. The leader is looking for the person, not the deviation from the leader. The person's actual shape becomes visible because the leader is not measuring distance from a fixed point.

This is the same as [[impartiality]] applied to hiring and promotion: removing the personal agenda from the observation. The agenda the leader usually does not see is the agenda of finding-themselves-elsewhere. Removing it reveals what was already there.

## Why Internal Work Produces This Capacity

The reason this capacity comes from inner work rather than from training is that the impulse to look for the self in others is driven by an underlying self-need. The leader is unconsciously seeking confirmation that they are the right kind of person by surrounding themselves with people who reflect them back. The unconscious belief: if others look like me and they are successful, that proves I am okay.

Removing this looking-for-self requires that the leader has gotten the okay-ness somewhere else. Once the leader does not need other people to confirm them, the leader stops looking for the confirmation, and the looking-for-self drops out of the observation. The drop is not a discipline; it is a consequence of no longer needing what the looking-for-self was unconsciously providing.

This is why Sam pairs his observation about sorting people with his observation about meditation. The two are not separate skills. The meditation work is what made the sorting possible. The sorting is what the meditation work made possible. The capacity at the surface is downstream of the work underneath.

This connects to [[expanding-sense-of-self]] from the other direction. As the self enlarges, the leader less needs the other person to be a confirmation of the leader's specific personal identity, because the leader's identity is less narrowly localized. The leader who has identified with something larger than their personal style can promote someone whose personal style is very different without it threatening anything, because nothing personal is threatened.

## The Projection Frame

The same teaching is available through the projection frame. When the leader looks at another person and sees something they evaluate, the evaluation is often about the leader, not about the other person. See [[projections]]. The dimensions on which the leader evaluates positively are the dimensions the leader values in themselves. The dimensions on which the leader evaluates negatively are often the dimensions the leader cannot accept in themselves.

[[what-you-cant-love-in-partner-is-what-you-cant-love-in-self]] is the relational form of this principle. The leadership form is the same: what you cannot see in an employee is what you cannot see in yourself, and what you over-promote in an employee is often what you over-value in yourself.

Inner work that makes more of the self visible makes more of the other visible too. The same opacity is releasing in both directions. The leader who can see their own shadow can see employees who carry shadow without flinching. The leader who can love their own difficulty can love employees whose difficulty is different from theirs.

## Why This Connects to [[hire-by-dream-job]]

The [[hire-by-dream-job]] move depends on this principle to work correctly. Asking a candidate "what's your dream job?" only produces useful data if the leader can actually see the candidate's answer for what it is. A leader who is still looking for themselves in the candidate will hear the answer through the filter of "is this what I would have said?" The candidate whose dream job is genuinely the role on offer but whose path to that role is completely different from the leader's will be misread as a bad fit.

The combination is what works: ask the dream-job question, then observe the answer with the self removed from the variable. The first move surfaces the data. The second move lets the data be seen accurately.

## What This Is Not

This is not detachment as not-caring. The leader who has done this work cares deeply about each team member as an individual. The care is more specific and more accurate than the care of a leader who has not done this work. It is care directed at the person actually present, not at the leader's image of the person. The accuracy is what makes the care effective.

This is not the absence of any preference. The leader still has preferences about who they want on the team. The point is that the preferences are based on the team's needs and the candidate's actual fit, not on a comparison to the leader's own template. The leader can still want someone with particular qualities; the question is whether those qualities are required by the role or required by the leader's unconscious need for validation.

This is not a technique that can be applied without the underlying inner work. A leader who tries to "observe people without comparing to themselves" while still carrying the underlying need will produce a performance of detached observation that is still governed by the unconscious comparing. The instruction does not work without the dissolution of the need it would address. This is why the principle is a consequence of internal work, not a substitute for it.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A leader notices that their direct reports all sound and act surprisingly like them.
- A leader is frustrated that they can never find good candidates while the candidates they are evaluating are objectively strong.
- A leader has been told the team lacks diversity in styles or perspectives despite efforts to hire differently.
- A leader is wondering why their hiring instincts keep producing the same kind of mistake.
- A leader notices that the team members they like the most are the ones who remind them of themselves.

## The Coaching Move

When a leader presents hiring or promotion struggles, the surface question is often "how should I evaluate candidates?" The underlying question is usually "what am I looking for that is blocking me from seeing what is here?" The coaching path is to surface the comparative template the leader is using, then trace it to the underlying self-need that is producing the template.

Questions to open:
- Who is the person you most enjoy working with on your team, and what do they have in common with you?
- What would you have to feel or see in yourself in order to promote someone who is very different from you?
- What is the team currently missing that would scare you to add?
- If you were not in the room, who would you hire?

The last question often surfaces the dynamic quickly. The candidate the leader would hire if the leader were not in the room is usually different from the candidate the leader would hire from inside the room. The difference is the unconscious template.

## Tone

State this conversationally to a leader. The principle is counterintuitive enough that it usually needs a beat to land. Most leaders assume that better people-reading comes from more attention to other people. The reframe -- that better people-reading comes from less attention to oneself -- is the move. Once it lands, the leader often recognizes their own pattern of promoting in their own image without needing further argument.
