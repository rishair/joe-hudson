# Perfectionism Is Arrogance

## The Principle

The perfectionist presents as humble (never satisfied, always striving, never quite enough). The structure underneath is the opposite. The perfectionist is asserting their own idea of what perfection looks like as the standard against which everything is measured. That assertion is not humble. It is arrogant on three counts at once: arrogant to think perfection exists, arrogant to think one knows what it is, arrogant to expect the world to share the same definition.

"It's extremely fucking arrogant, right? It's really arrogant to think that you know what perfection is. It's extremely arrogant to think you can get to perfection. It's extremely arrogant to assume that your idea of perfection is actually what matters to other people, or is right. So the whole, the whole thought process of perfection is just full of holes." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The three arrogances stack:

1. **The arrogance of believing perfection exists.** Perfection is not a feature of the world; it is a story the mind generates. The mind treats the story as if it were objective. Two different minds given the same problem would generate two different stories. Both stories would be treated by their respective minds as "the perfect version." Neither would be it, because there is no it.

2. **The arrogance of believing you know what perfection is.** Even granting the existence of perfection for a moment, the claim that this particular person knows what it looks like is a claim about the person's own special access to a truth no one else has. The claim does not come with evidence. It comes with the conviction the mind generates around the standard it has produced.

3. **The arrogance of believing your perfection matters to others.** The perfectionist standard is asserted as the standard. The other people in the situation -- the customer, the audience, the team, the family -- have their own ideas of what would be good. The perfectionist's standard often does not match theirs, but is held as if everyone should care that it has been met or violated.

## Why This Reframe Lands

The arrogance frame is useful in coaching because it interrupts the surface presentation of the perfectionist. The perfectionist is usually defending the perfectionism by appealing to humility ("I just want to do good work, I'm just trying to do my best, I'm not satisfied with anything less than excellence"). The humility framing makes the perfectionism feel like a virtue and makes any challenge to it feel like an invitation to be sloppy.

The arrogance frame disables this defense. The perfectionism is recast not as humility but as its opposite. The recasting is not an insult; it is a structural observation. The person is being asked to look at what they are actually doing -- holding a standard nobody agreed to, asserting it as the standard, judging everyone (including themselves) against it.

The recognition tends to land hard because most perfectionists value humility and would not consciously want to be arrogant. Seeing that the perfectionism is structurally arrogant makes continuing the pattern uncomfortable in a different way than before. The discomfort had been "I haven't met the standard"; the new discomfort is "I have been imposing a standard." The new discomfort is more workable because it has a clear exit -- stop imposing.

## The Reframe in Practice

When a person describes their perfectionism with pride or with the surface presentation of humility ("I just have high standards"), Joe sometimes names the arrogance directly:

The move is to ask, in some form: "What makes you think you know what perfect is?"

The question is delivered without sarcasm. It is a real question. The person usually does not have a good answer, because the perfection-standard is generated by the mind without explicit grounds. The grounding has felt self-evident from inside the standard. Asked to articulate the grounds, the person finds the grounds aren't there.

This connects to the broader move of [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]]. Once the perfection-standard has been seen as an assertion the person has been making without grounds, the question "what would it be like to enjoy this instead of try to make it perfect?" becomes possible. The perfectionism does not have to be argued away; it just has to be seen as one possible operating principle the person has been treating as the only one. See [[change-the-approach-not-the-target]] for the substitution move.

## How This Connects to Humility

The reframe is not a teaching that humility is bad. The reframe says that perfectionism is not humility, even though it has been presenting as humility. Real humility looks different.

Real humility allows for multiple valid versions of the work. It accepts that someone else might solve the same problem differently and equally well. It treats one's own standard as one possible standard among many, held lightly. See [[humility-developmental-arc]] for the deeper treatment of how humility matures.

Perfectionism collapses all the valid versions into the one version the perfectionist's mind has generated. That collapse is the arrogant move. Humility would have left the space open for other versions to be also good.

A practical test: how does the person respond when someone else does the work differently and it turns out well? The humble person can recognize the other version as also good. The perfectionist often cannot -- the other version, having not met the perfectionist's standard, is judged as deficient even when it succeeded on its own terms. The inability to recognize valid alternatives is the arrogance showing.

## The Multiple-Solutions Argument

The James Webb Space Telescope is the example Joe uses to make this concrete. A different group of scientists, given the same charter, would have built a different telescope. The different telescope would also have worked. Both would have been successful. There would have been no objective sense in which one was "the perfect version."

"I am very, very confident if you would have gotten another group of scientists together to do that, and it would have been successful, the telescope would not look the same." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The argument generalizes. There is no project, business decision, work of art, parenting choice, conversation, or relational move with one perfect version. There are tolerance windows within which many options work. The perfectionist treats the tolerance window as if it had one point in it. The world treats the tolerance window as it actually is -- a range with many viable points. The perfectionist's insistence on the one point is the arrogant move; the world's allowance of the range is the structural reality.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not a license to do bad work. The work still has to be done well. The argument is about *how* the work gets organized, not about whether it gets done. Replacing perfectionism with arrogance-avoidance produces nothing; replacing it with [[change-the-approach-not-the-target|a different organizing principle]] (connection, excellence, enjoyment, process) produces good work without the cost.

It is not a moral judgment of the perfectionist. The arrogance is structural, not characterological. Most perfectionists did not choose the pattern; the pattern was installed by a chaotic childhood (see the perfectionism article). Seeing the structural arrogance does not make the person bad; it makes the operating principle visible enough that they have the option of dropping it.

It is not the same as humility-shaming. The reframe does not require the person to feel bad about themselves for being arrogant. It just asks them to see what they have been doing structurally and notice that their own self-image as humble has not matched the structure. The seeing is what creates the room to change.

## Tone

Direct, slightly provocative, never contemptuous. The reframe is delivered as a structural observation, not as a put-down. Joe uses light profanity ("extremely fucking arrogant") which functions to break the seriousness of the perfectionist's frame -- it is hard to maintain the seriousness of one's perfectionism while being told it is fucking arrogant, and that is part of the move. The provocation makes the seeing possible.
