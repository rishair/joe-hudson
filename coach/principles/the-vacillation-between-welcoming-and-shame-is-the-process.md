# The Vacillation Between Welcoming and Shame Is the Process

## The Principle

When the work of self-welcoming starts to produce the spacious, expansive, big state, shame arrives shortly after. The person contracts. Then they may welcome again. Then the shame arrives again. The back-and-forth is not failure. It is the process.

The mistake is to expect that once the welcoming has been touched, it should be sustained continuously. That expectation pathologizes the structurally normal in-and-out. The person concludes they are doing it wrong. They try harder to stay in the welcoming. The trying becomes another layer of pressure that interrupts the welcoming. The shame arrives faster.

The correct frame: the alternation is the work. Each contraction is an opportunity to meet the shame with welcoming. Each return to welcoming after the contraction is a strengthening of the system's capacity to hold the welcoming without the shame having to fire.

"So there's this natural in and out from the welcoming to the shame that happens, and it's part of the process. And it's to be felt and invited. It's not to be resisted." — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

## Why the Vacillation Happens

The pattern is the body's way of testing the new territory. The system has been organized around the rule "if you go big, something bad will happen." When the person goes big and nothing bad happens, the system gets confused. The shame fires anyway, on schedule, because the trained response has not updated yet.

The training was thorough and the updating is slow. Each round of welcoming-then-shame-then-welcoming is one increment of the system learning that the bigness is not in fact dangerous. The alternation is the actual mechanism by which the update happens. Removing the alternation by trying to stay continuously in the welcoming removes the mechanism.

See [[shame-after-spaciousness]] for the full account of the lockout pattern. This principle is the developmental complement: the lockout pattern doesn't dissolve in one move. It dissolves through repeated cycles of welcoming, contraction, welcoming again.

## The Voice of the Vacillation

The internal narrative during the vacillation usually sounds like:

"Okay, I feel good. Wait, no, I must be ashamed. Oh no, I might get too big for my britches. Oh no, I might be too much for people I love who will reject me if I feel this good."

This is not a sign of confused thinking. It is the voice of the lockout mechanism reasserting itself when the spaciousness has been touched. The voice is doing exactly what it was trained to do. The principle says: the voice's arrival is expected and is not a problem. The voice's content is not a verdict; it is the lockout firing.

"So this is part of the not seeing yourself. One of the typical patterns... is they'll — you'll notice a lot of hedging in their words." — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

The hedging in speech and the vacillation in inner state are forms of the same dynamic — the system protecting against the territory it learned to fear, even when the protection is no longer needed.

## What This Reframes

The principle reframes several common self-development misperceptions:

- **"I touched the state and then lost it, so I must have done it wrong."** The state is not lost. It returned according to the system's training. The next round of welcoming is available immediately.
- **"I should be able to sustain this if I'm doing it right."** Sustained continuous welcoming is the long-term direction, not the immediate target. The current target is to meet each contraction with welcoming when it arrives.
- **"The shame after the welcoming is evidence the welcoming was fake."** The welcoming was real. The shame is the protective firing, not a correction.
- **"I'm not making progress because I keep cycling."** The cycling *is* the progress. Each cycle is one increment of the system's update.

## The Coaching Implication

When a client reports the vacillation as a problem ("I keep getting back to it and then losing it"), the move is to reframe the report as the structurally normal pattern.

The coach can name it:

> "The pattern is that you know that place, but after you are in that place, somehow you have a learning that you need to feel shame after that place."

Then immediately offer the operational instruction:

> "So go back there to the welcoming everything for a minute. And when that shame arises, welcome that too."

The instruction does not promise the shame will stop. It says the shame is also welcomed. Over time, with this instruction running, the shame's grip loosens. But the loosening is gradual, and the alternation persists for longer than the person expects.

See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] for the live move that operationalizes this instruction. See [[shame-after-spaciousness]] for the underlying pattern.

## The Spiritual-Journey Misconception

A common entrance into self-development work is the belief: "I'm not good enough. If I do all this work, I'll become good enough to feel good." This frame implies feeling good is the destination and the work is the route.

The actual structure is the inverse. The work is allowing yourself to feel good. The "I'm not good enough" is the part that has to be welcomed, not the part that has to be fixed. The journey doesn't produce some new self that is finally good enough to feel good. The journey is the gradual undoing of the lockout that prevented feeling good in the first place.

"Oftentimes people come into the spiritual journey with the 'I'm not good enough.' And, or I'm sorry, the self-development journey with the 'I'm not good enough. And therefore, if I do all this work, I'll be good enough to feel good.' Only to discover that the journey is actually allowing themselves to feel good. It's nothing about them has to change. There's no perfection needed to feel good. There's just a welcoming of yourself." — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

This is related to [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]]. Both principles point at the same architecture: the orientation of "I need to fix myself to deserve good things" is itself the lockout. The orientation of "I welcome myself as I am" is the way through. The vacillation is the system slowly updating from the first orientation to the second.

## What This Principle Is Not

- It is not permission to stop welcoming. The principle says the vacillation is normal; it does not say the contractions are the goal. The active practice continues to be welcoming, including welcoming the contractions.
- It is not a claim that all back-and-forth in inner work is the same as this specific vacillation. Some oscillation is the result of distraction, environmental change, or fatigue. This principle is specifically about the shame-after-welcoming pattern.
- It is not a promise that the alternation will stop. For some people, this rhythm continues for years, with the amplitude gradually decreasing. The principle does not require the alternation to end for the work to be working.

## When Someone Experiences This

- "I had a great session and then felt terrible the next day."
- "Every time I touch the welcoming, I lose it within hours."
- "I've been working on self-love for years and I still keep falling back."
- "I felt amazing on the retreat and now I'm worse than before I went."
- After a coaching session in which the person reached a real spacious state, followed by a follow-up report of contraction
- After receiving a compliment that landed, followed by an inner voice critiquing the receiving
- After a meditation that opened the heart, followed by the heart contracting on the drive home

In every case, the principle says: the contraction is part of the rhythm. Welcome the contraction. The welcoming-shame-welcoming cycle is the actual shape of the work, not a deviation from the work.
