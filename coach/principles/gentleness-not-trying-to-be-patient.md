# Gentleness, Not Trying to Be Patient

## The Principle

When someone asks how to be patient with their own growth process, the question has a structural problem inside it. Patience cannot be tried. The act of trying to be patient is not patience -- it is impatience attempting to perform itself as its opposite.

"What I notice is that if we're trying to be patient, we're not. That's what I notice. The act of trying to be patient is not patience." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The substitute is gentleness. Gentleness is a quality that can actually be brought to a relationship -- with oneself or with another. Patience aimed at oneself reliably becomes another should. Gentleness aimed at oneself softens the very mechanism that would have produced the impatience.

"Don't try to be patient with yourself. It isn't patience. What I would say is maybe the word that's more easy is, is gentleness. How do you be gentle with yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

## Why the Substitution Matters

Patience as a concept tends to live as a should. "I should be patient with my growth." The should converts the supposed virtue into a new bar to fail at. Now the person is impatient about not being patient enough. The voice has captured the practice. See [[should-as-threat]] and [[shame-drives-behavior]].

Gentleness does not work the same way. Gentleness is not aspirational in the same way. "I should be more gentle with myself" can still happen, but the word itself has less moral weight. It is harder to use gentleness as a stick. The practice is more available.

The deeper mechanism: patience implies waiting for something to be different. The person being patient is waiting for the unsatisfactory current state to become a more satisfactory future state. That waiting is itself a form of resistance to the present. Gentleness does not require the future to be different. Gentleness is about how the current thing is held.

## What Gentleness Actually Looks Like

Gentleness with the growth process means being grateful for the transformation that is already happening. Not the transformation that is supposed to happen. The actual transformation, in this small day, in this small moment.

"Being gentle with yourself is being grateful for the transformation that's been happening. And that also accelerates the transformation and the growth." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

This converts the action from "I will try to be patient with how slow this is" to "I will notice and be grateful for what has actually happened." The first is a stance against the speed of change. The second is acknowledgment of the speed of change that is already happening.

The shift mirrors a broader principle: [[love-is-the-best-change-agent]]. Loving the current state moves the current state. Fighting the current state (including fighting it by being impatient with how slow it changes) freezes it.

## The Gratitude-Acceleration Effect

Joe makes the operational claim directly: gentleness accelerates the very transformation the person was being impatient about.

The mechanism is not motivational. It is not "be grateful so you feel good so you do more." It is structural: when transformation is being met with gratitude, the system experiences transformation as wanted. When transformation is being met with impatience for it to go faster, the system experiences transformation as something it has to push against more resistance to deliver.

The push-ups example Joe gives makes this concrete:

"If I'm like, 'Okay, I've done 100 push-ups. I need to do more push-ups,' okay, doing push-ups isn't fun. If I'm like, 'I did 100 push-ups. Woo! Hell yes!' I'm more likely wanting to do push-ups the next day." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The push-ups are the same. The relationship to the push-ups is different. The relationship determines whether the next round of push-ups happens with momentum or with resistance.

## What Gentleness Sounds Like (Toward Yourself)

Gentleness is not effusive cheerleading. It is plain acknowledgment.

"Wow, thank you, [name], for showing up again today and noticing that these thought patterns were getting in your way. And thank you for being here doing this gratitude practice again. And thank you for asking the question. Thank you for meditating today." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The form is simple thanks. The targets are small and specific: the showing up, the noticing, the asking, the practicing. The voice is the voice you would use to a friend who had done a small ordinary good thing.

The form is not: "You crushed it today!" That is performance gratitude, often the voice trying to take credit. The form is also not: "It's okay that you didn't do more." That is gentleness inverted into a backhanded should.

The form is acknowledgment of what actually happened in the day, named without inflation, with thanks.

## The Encouragement-Not-Fixing Distinction

A subtle move in the principle: the encouragement is not encouragement to fix yourself. It is encouragement that is just acknowledgement.

"How do you give yourself the encouragement that isn't meant to fix you, but just the acknowledgement, the encouragement that is just acknowledgement of what you have done." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The distinction matters. Encouragement-toward-improvement is the voice in disguise. "Good job, now keep going" carries the implicit "you are not yet enough." Encouragement-as-acknowledgement is just the noticing that something good happened. There is no implicit next step. The day's transformation is complete in itself.

This connects to [[improvement-vs-authenticity]]: the work is not to keep improving the self. The work is to recognize what the self is, and the daily acknowledgement of small movements is part of that recognition.

## Why the Word "Patience" Is Itself Suspect

The question "how do I be patient with myself?" has a structural twin: "how do I be patient with somebody else?" The second question is hard to make sense of in the same way. Patience with another person is not really something one tries to do. It is what is present when one is not in a hurry. The same is true for self-patience.

"I'm wondering what the question would be like if you asked me, what is it like to be patient with somebody else? And how that question is hard to make sense of. It's hard to make sense of how do you be patient with yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

What makes the second question hard is the same thing that makes the first question hard: patience is not a quality one summons. It is a quality that arises when one is not summoning anything.

Reframing the question to "how do I be gentle with myself?" makes the question answerable. Reframing the question to "how do I be grateful for what's happening?" makes the question actionable.

## The Connection to Nature

This principle relies on a deeper truth: growth happens. It does not need to be forced. The oak tree grows on its own. The river runs on its own. The person learning to do this work is already learning. See [[nature-improves-itself]].

Trying to be patient with growth implicitly assumes that the speed of growth is a problem -- that without one's vigilance, growth would slow down or stop. Gentleness assumes that growth is the natural state and is already happening. The work is to stay out of the way of it, by not making it a project.

The combination of the two principles produces the move: notice the growth that is already happening, thank yourself for it, and let the gratitude be the relationship to the process. Not "I should be growing faster." Not "I should be more patient about how slow it is." Just "thank you for what happened today."

## In Coaching

When a person asks how to be patient with themselves, do not coach them in patience. Coach them in gentleness.

When a person reports they are trying to be patient, name the trap. The trying is the impatience. The work is not to try harder at patience. It is to stop trying.

When a person reports they are failing at being patient, redirect. The failure report is the voice. The move is to introduce gratitude as the practice and let the question of patience dissolve.

When a person is grinding on themselves about how slow their growth is, treat that as a [[should-as-threat]] phenomenon. The shoulds are the thing slowing the growth. Releasing the shoulds via gentleness is the actual work.

## Tone

Warm and slightly amused at the structural trap inside the question. Joe leans into the paradox -- "if you're trying to be patient, you're not" -- without making the person wrong for having asked. The redirect to gentleness is offered as a gift, not as a correction.
