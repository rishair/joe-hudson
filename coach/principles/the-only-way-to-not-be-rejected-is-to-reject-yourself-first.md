# The Only Way to Not Get Rejected Is to Reject Yourself First

## The Principle

The only way to make sure you cannot be rejected is to reject yourself first. The not-going-for-it, the holding-back, the not-asking — these are not failures of nerve. They are a strategy. The strategy: if I reject myself first, you cannot reject me. Or more precisely: whatever you do cannot land, because I have already done the harder thing to myself.

The cost is exact: the self-rejection IS the rejection, and it is more painful than the external rejection it was supposed to prevent. The protection from rejection is delivered by performing the rejection yourself.

"The only way in life to not get rejected is to make sure you reject yourself first." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Ask for What You Want

"If I fully go for it and it doesn't happen, then I get rejected. So I'll reject myself so that I don't have to go for it, so I won't get rejected." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Ask for What You Want (compressing the structure the client surfaces)

## The Inversion at the Heart

When the client says "I'm afraid they'll reject me," the principle inverts the worry directly:

"Why — what on earth would make you worry about being rejected by somebody if you're already rejecting yourself? You've rejected your wants, you've rejected your sadness, you've rejected your resistance. Like, how on earth could somebody else reject you in a way that's going to have any kind of dent on that? If I said to you right now, I'm done with you, I'm not helping you anymore, and I reject you, is it going to hurt more than what you're doing to yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Ask for What You Want

The structural fact: external rejection has a ceiling. It is capped by the self-rejection already in place. No one can reject you more thoroughly than you have already rejected yourself. The fear of external rejection is operating on top of a self-rejection that already produced the feared outcome — and the self-rejection is the more painful one.

The client's own confirmation when the principle is named: "Probably not. I guess I'm just not noticing that I'm rejecting myself. It's very unconscious, you know." The recognition is enough to drop a layer of the defense.

## The Strategy Made Explicit

The principle works by surfacing what the not-going-for-it is actually doing. The mind frames it as "I can't go all in." The structural reality is: "I am rejecting myself so that the going-all-in can't happen, so that the external rejection that would follow going-all-in can't happen."

The structure:
1. Wanting the thing produces vulnerability to being rejected for wanting it.
2. Going for the thing produces vulnerability to being rejected for going for it.
3. Asking for the thing produces vulnerability to being rejected when asking.
4. Pre-rejecting yourself prevents you from going all in.
5. The non-going-all-in cannot get rejected externally because the going-all-in never happened.
6. The cost: the self-rejection is the rejection. The protection is the wound.

The strategy is structurally airtight from one angle (external rejection cannot occur for an action that did not occur) and structurally self-defeating from another (the rejection has happened, you are doing it, and it hurts more than the external version would have).

## Why It Hurts More Than External Rejection

External rejection is partial. It addresses a specific instance, a specific moment, a specific person's response. They reject the proposal, the ask, the audition, the application. The rejection lands on what was offered, not on the self that offered it (unless the self collapses the distinction).

Self-rejection is total. It addresses the self before the offer is made. The message is: I cannot be trusted to be okay as I am. Not okay enough to ask. Not okay enough to want. Not okay enough to be seen. The rejection is of the person doing the asking, not of any specific ask.

This is why the principle has the form it has: not "self-rejection is also rejection," but "self-rejection is the more painful rejection." The first is a comparison; the second is a structural claim about which form of rejection actually does the damage the person fears.

See also [[job-interview-3d-chess]] for the developmental version of this insight: the early-30s job-search where the rejection hurt for two reasons (they rejected me; I had rejected myself first) — and the self-rejection was the more painful one.

## The Asking-Rejection Inventory

When the principle is in play, the client has typically rejected multiple things at once. The naming makes the inventory visible:

"You've rejected your wants, you've rejected your sadness, you've rejected your resistance." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Ask for What You Want

The inventory pattern: the not-asking is a downstream effect of a comprehensive self-rejection package. The wants get rejected first (so there is no want to ask about). The feelings get rejected (so there is no felt sense to act from). The resistance gets rejected (so there is not even a clean experience of the not-asking). What remains is a person who has rejected their interior so thoroughly that the question of external rejection is moot.

This is why the principle pairs with [[find-anything-worth-rejecting]] as a follow-up move. The principle surfaces that the rejection is happening; the follow-up asks what could possibly justify it.

## What This Principle Does in a Session

The principle is delivered as a question, not a statement. The form matters. Stating "you are rejecting yourself" makes the client defend. Asking "why would you worry about being rejected by somebody if you're already rejecting yourself?" makes the client notice. The notice is the work.

The client's response after the principle lands tends to be immediate and structural: clearer speech, less hedging, a felt drop. From the source session: "As soon as you said it, you started speaking clearly. As soon as you said that, you're like, oh, you're like, you're here with me. You're not — that was like a full sentence."

The clarity is not a coincidence. The mechanism that was producing the verbal hesitation (the self-rejection running underneath every utterance) has been named and at least partially seen-through. The hesitation drops because the engine driving it has been outed.

## Tone

Direct. The principle is not delivered as comfort. It is delivered as a structural observation. The question form ("what on earth would make you worry about being rejected by somebody if you're already rejecting yourself?") carries a slight surprise — as if the obviousness of the inversion just landed on the coach in real time. The surprise makes the recognition shareable rather than imposed.

There is a small warmth underneath. The principle is being offered not as a critique but as an exit. The client has been suffering inside the self-rejection. Seeing the structure offers the possibility of stopping the suffering, which is a kindness even though the form is blunt.

When the client confirms ("Probably not. I guess I'm just not noticing"), the coach receives the confirmation without celebration. The recognition is the work; making a fuss about it could pull the client back into management.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is about preemptive self-rejection as a defense against anticipated external rejection. It is not a claim that all rejection is internal, or that external rejection does not happen, or that external rejection does not hurt.

It is also not a claim that the client should just go for it and stop hedging. Telling someone "stop rejecting yourself" without first surfacing the structure produces a new self-rejection ("I'm rejecting myself, I'm bad for that, stop it"). The principle works when it is offered as a structural observation that produces seeing, not as a directive that produces compliance.

The principle is about a specific defense pattern: preemptive self-rejection to avoid external rejection in a context where the client wants to go for something. It does not apply to situations where the action genuinely should not happen (the want is not aligned, the timing is wrong, the relationship is not safe). Distinguishing the two requires the body-level work — does the not-going-for-it feel like a clean no, or does it feel like a hedge that is also producing pain? The latter is the principle's territory.
