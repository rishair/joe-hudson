# You Can't Be Seen or Heard When You're Not in Yourself

## The Principle

There is a structural precondition for being received by another person: you have to be in yourself when you are speaking. If you are not in your own body — if your attention is at a meta-position monitoring how your speech is landing — there is no *you* present for the listener to receive. The listener may try. The listener may even succeed at the words. But the contact the speaker is hungry for cannot land, because no one is home to receive the landing.

"You can't fucking be seen or heard when you're not in yourself. How could I ever see you? You're not you. You're running away from yourself to manage me." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

The principle is the structural ground underneath the complaint "they don't see me" / "they don't hear me." Often the person making the complaint is the one not present at the seeing-place. The listener may be doing their part. The receiver is missing.

## Why It Is True

Speech that comes from a person who is in themselves has different information in it than speech that comes from a person who has vacated. The latter is delivered from a model — the speaker's anxious model of how they are being perceived — not from the speaker themselves. The listener's nervous system registers the difference even when the listener cannot name it.

Consider what is happening in the speaker who has left. They are monitoring two things simultaneously: the content they are producing, and the reception they are tracking. The attention is split between speaker-as-producer and speaker-as-monitor. Neither half is in the speaker's body. The body, which carries the felt truth of what the speaker actually means, is bypassed.

The listener receives this bypassed speech as managed, performed, or pressured. Even when the words are clear and well-chosen, the speech feels like it is being installed rather than offered. The listener's response is to drift, to resist, or to pull back. None of these are the response the speaker wanted. All of them follow structurally from the absence of the speaker.

When the speaker is in themselves, the same words land differently. The body is present in the speech. The listener registers that the speaker is here, not over there. The contact becomes possible.

## The Live Demonstration

The principle is most easily taught not by argument but by demonstration. The standard sequence is to ask the person to say the same sentence twice — first as they normally would, then with 10% more attention on listening to themselves. The shift is audible. The second version sounds like the person; the first sounded like a performance.

In a coaching session this can be even more direct. Joe asks Savannah to talk about her favorite topic in the world while listening to herself 10% more. She talks about teaching kids to swim and notices it was effortless. He then asks her to do the same with a topic she does not love and the difficulty shows up immediately. The diagnostic is in the body — when she is in herself, the words are easy. When she is not, the words are work.

The mirror works in both directions. The person being talked *to* can also feel the shift. Joe asks Savannah to focus on his listening to her. She notices it has the same quality as listening to herself — expansive, slower, more aware. "There's not like an outcome in mind. It's just like you pay attention to the details more." The listening of another that is most desired is the same shape as the listening to oneself. People who are listening to themselves find that other people's listening is more available to them, and that they can recognize it when it arrives.

## Why People Leave Themselves

The leaving is not a moral failure. It is a competent strategy, usually installed in childhood, that worked for the situation it was developed for. A person who grew up with an unpredictable caregiver — an alcoholic, a depressed parent, a mentally ill or abusive figure, anyone whose reactions were not about the child — learned early to put their attention at the meta-position. The job was not to be in themselves. The job was to monitor the other person's state and adjust accordingly. That was where survival lived.

"You think you got taught sometime in an early time that your happiness was dependent on other people's reactions. And the person whose reaction you thought your happiness was dependent on was having reactions for reasons that were completely not about you. And so your whole lesson in life when you were young was, how do I find my happiness by trying to make them happy? And I can't really make them happy." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

The adult who is still doing this is following a pattern that once worked. The cost has shifted. The pattern no longer protects the adult from anything; it produces the very disconnection it was once designed to prevent. The answer is not to fight the pattern but to give the system the new instruction: come back into yourself. Listen to yourself. The instruction is small. The undoing of the pattern is in the small repeated practice of returning.

## The Two Hurts in Not Being Seen

When this principle is being violated, the hurt has two layers. The first layer is the obvious one: I do not feel understood. The second layer is rarely named but is often the deeper wound: the goodness I am bringing is not being recognized. The person is offering something — care, intention, gift — and the failure to be received feels like rejection of the offering, not just of the content.

"There's almost two levels of hurt when someone doesn't understand you. The first level of hurt is that you don't feel understood. The second level of hurt is like I would say that you don't feel like the goodness in which you bring is being recognized." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

Both layers are real. Both can be addressed only when the speaker is back in themselves. From outside the self, no amount of being-understood by the listener can land at the goodness layer, because the goodness is the speaker's own — and the speaker cannot register its reception when they are not present to register anything.

## What This Replaces

This principle replaces a popular but incorrect theory: that being heard is a function of how clearly you communicate, and that the lever for being heard is on the speaker's verbal-skill side. The theory predicts that better word choice, better tone, better structure, better timing will produce being heard. In practice, all of these techniques fail when the speaker is not in themselves, and most of them succeed when the speaker is, regardless of how technically polished the speech is.

The actual lever is not the words. The actual lever is the speaker's presence in their own body while speaking. From there, almost any words work. Without that, even the best-chosen words land as management.

This is also why technique-only communication trainings (assertiveness training, nonviolent communication taught as protocol, scripts for difficult conversations) often produce diminishing returns over time. The technique is being applied from outside the self. The very application is a form of leaving the self to control the conversation. The result is more sophisticated chasing, not more contact. See [[view-as-technique]] for the parallel anti-pattern in VIEW.

## What This Does Not Mean

This is not the claim that the speaker is responsible for whether the listener actually hears them. The listener still has agency. The listener still has their own state, history, and capacity. A speaker who is fully in themselves cannot make a closed listener open.

The claim is structural: presence in the self is the *precondition* for being heard. It does not guarantee being heard. It just makes being heard possible. Without it, even an ideal listener cannot bridge the gap, because there is no one on the other side of the bridge.

This is also not a claim about confidence or assertiveness. A person who is in themselves while speaking may be unsure, may be hesitant, may be vulnerable. The presence is not a tone. It is a location. The person is in their own body. That is the thing that makes them receivable.

## The Move That Restores It

The smallest viable intervention is the 10% instruction: "Listen to yourself 10% more while you say it." See [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]]. Most people can find 10%. The shift in their speech is immediate and observable to the listener.

A near-equivalent: "Put your attention in your inner ear." See [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]]. The ear is a body-part already involved in speech; the redirect is small enough to be doable in any moment.

Either move serves the same function. The person returns. The contact becomes available.

A third move applies when the coachee cannot find the felt sense of self-listening at all: [[have-them-receive-your-listening]]. Direct the coachee's attention to the listening you are giving them in the room, then name that the felt quality is the same as listening to themselves. The borrowed template installs what direct instruction could not.

## Tone

Direct, sometimes blunt. Joe is not soft about this one. "How could I ever see you? You're not you." The bluntness is intentional. The pattern of self-abandonment has been competent for a long time; the language has to be vivid enough to break the spell. The warmth is in the underlying message — *I want you here, I am not interested in the version of you that is not in your body* — but the surface is firm.

When the person makes the shift, the tone changes immediately to acknowledgment and softness. The shift is itself the teaching; Joe lets the moment land rather than reaching past it.
