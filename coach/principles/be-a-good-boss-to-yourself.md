# If You Work for Yourself, You Have to Be a Good Boss

## Definition

When you work for someone else, the boss is external. The boss's quality is fixed; you adapt to it. When you work for yourself, the boss is internal. The boss's quality is now your work to maintain — and most people who go independent install an internal boss that is worse than any external boss they have ever had.

The trade is real: you can quit working for an external bad boss, but you cannot quit the internal one without doing the work. Most self-employed people who are struggling to engage with their own work have not seen that they are working for a tyrant — themselves.

> "You want to work for yourself, you got to be a good boss." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

## The Trade-Off Made Visible

The diagnostic Joe uses: ask the person who is chronically procrastinating on their own work which they would rather have — to work for somebody else, or to be constantly under the yoke of a perfectionist.

> "Which would you rather, just curious — to work for somebody else or to constantly be under the yoke of a perfectionist? Oh, because that's your choice right now, and I see which one you're making." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The exchange exposes a structural mistake. The person left the external boss because they did not want to work for somebody else. But the internal replacement is worse: it never goes home, never approves, never allows enjoyment of the work. The person is now under a 24/7 perfectionist instead of a 40-hour-a-week boss. They have made the situation strictly worse.

The recognition tends to land hard because the person can usually see the comparison clearly the moment it is named. They had not considered themselves in the role of boss at all. Naming the role makes the question of what kind of boss they are an actionable one.

## The Job-Interview Variant of the Diagnostic

A close cousin of the trade-off question, used when the person has named explicit standards the bad boss is operating from. Instead of comparing two boss options, Joe offers himself as a hypothetical hire who would operate by the exact standards the client has been applying to themselves. The move is [[offer-yourself-as-the-bad-boss-they-installed]] — and it is one of the cleanest demonstrations of this principle in live coaching.

> "Okay, check it out. You're gonna hire me as a coach, right? You're like, Joe, I'm thinking about hiring you as a coach. I'm like, cool. Here's the thing is I'm gonna always feel like I'm not spending enough time with you. I'm never gonna spend enough time with you. And if I don't make you absolutely perfect on the first attempt, I'm gonna think I'm a bad coach. Wanna hire me?" -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The client's immediate response: "All right, makes sense." Joe then doubles the move with the inversion:

> "Also, do you want to work for me? Do I want to work for you? Like, you're like, if I was a programmer and you were like, hey, you, I want to hire you. Here's the thing is I'm always going to tell you, you're not doing enough. And if you don't get it absolutely perfect on the first attempt, I'm going to say that you failed." -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The double inversion exposes the asymmetric absurdity. The same standards that sound like high ambition when said in one's own internal voice sound like an unconscionable contract when offered as job terms. The client would never accept them as an employee. They would never offer them as an employer. Yet they have been running both sides of the contract on themselves continuously. The compression: **"You're a shitty boss."**

The variant is structurally identical to the trade-off question (both expose the bad-boss installation) but operates on different material. The trade-off question works when the person has been chronically procrastinating on a started-but-stalled project (the niche website case). The job-interview variant works when the person has been unable to start at all and can articulate the specific standards (one attempt = proof of failure, never enough time) that are blocking them.

Both variants land the same recognition: the person has been operating under a contract they would never accept from anyone else. The recognition opens the door to the question of what contract they actually want to be operating under.

## The Pivot to Design: What Kind of Boss Do You Want to Be?

Once the bad-boss recognition has landed, Joe's pivot question is generative rather than diagnostic: "What kind of boss do you want to be?"

The question moves from "look what you've been doing" to "what would you actually want instead?" The articulation tends to land in a softer-but-still-ambitious register. From the canonical 2025-10-30 session, the client's answer:

> "One that kind of lets things develop at the natural pace, but still kind of reaches goals and still is ambitious. It's not like, oh, today I feel like working and in 2 weeks I might feel like working again."

The articulation is itself the design of the new internal contract. The new boss has standards (still reaches goals, still ambitious), but the standards are not abusive (lets things develop at natural pace). The person is not being asked to abandon their ambition. They are being asked to articulate a contract their actual self could agree to.

The articulation also surfaces the false dichotomy the person had been operating inside. They had assumed the only alternative to the bad-boss contract was an even-worse no-boss contract ("if I just work whenever I feel like it, I'm never gonna get there"). The question allows them to articulate the third option that has been invisible to them: ambitious without abusive.

## What a Good Internal Boss Does

Joe's compressed instructions to the man in the website case give the contour of the good internal boss:

> "For the next week, I want you to sit down in front of your computer with the intention to work and enjoy yourself. And the most important thing is to enjoy yourself. The second most important thing is to work. So it's just, oh, if I'm not enjoying myself, stop what I'm doing, figure out a way to do it in a way that I enjoy it." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

In the 2025-10-30 session, Joe gave a more compressed version of the same contract, framed as Joe-as-the-good-boss giving instructions for the next day:

> "I'm gonna tell you your job is to work on it 1 hour a day. I don't give a fuck if you accomplish anything for that 1 hour. The only thing I care about is that you enjoy yourself. That you work on the project and you enjoy yourself. That's the only thing I care about." -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The two contracts are structurally the same. Both make enjoyment the only success criterion. Both explicitly remove the accomplishment requirement. Both bound the time so the work cannot be infinite. The casual profanity in the second version ("I don't give a fuck if you accomplish anything") signals that the relaxation of the accomplishment requirement is operationally serious, not a soft platitude — the good boss really does not care about the accomplishment, only the enjoyment, because the boss knows from the principle [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] that the accomplishment will follow.

The follow-up exchange in the 2025-10-30 session was the test of the contract:

> "You wanna work for me?"
>
> "Sounds awesome."
>
> "How effective are you gonna be?"
>
> "Probably pretty effective."

The point lands without argument: the good boss outproduces the bad boss because the good boss does not extract energy through pressure. The good boss makes the work doable.

The structure of the instruction is itself the structure of being a good boss:

1. **Set the ordering deliberately.** Enjoyment first, work second. Most internal bosses invert this — they demand productivity first and treat enjoyment as a reward (or a luxury, or a distraction). The good internal boss reverses the order. Productivity is downstream of enjoyment, not the other way around.

2. **Allow stopping.** A bad boss forbids stopping. A good boss notices when the worker is grinding without engagement and intervenes. As an internal boss, this means noticing your own un-engagement and stopping rather than pushing through.

3. **Solve for engagement.** "Figure out a way to do it that I enjoy" is an active reconfiguration. The good internal boss does not just permit enjoyment — they treat the absence of enjoyment as a design problem to solve.

4. **Bound the time.** "One hour a day" is itself a good-boss move. The bad-boss contract is unbounded — the work could always take more time, the worker could always be putting in more. The good boss says: this much, today, then we are done. Bounding the time is what allows the enjoyment criterion to actually operate; an infinite obligation cannot be enjoyed.

5. **Maintain the parameters of winning without dictating the method.** The work has to get done; the way it gets done is open. See [[enjoy-over-manage]] for the broader version of this leadership stance.

This is the internal counterpart of [[enjoy-over-manage]] applied to self-employment. The relationship to yourself as the worker mirrors the relationship a good leader has with their team.

## Why the Internal Boss Tends to Be Worse Than the External One

Three reinforcing reasons:

**The internal boss never leaves.** An external boss has hours. Even a difficult external boss is not in your head when you are with your family or asleep. The internal boss is continuous. Every moment the work is not being done is a moment the internal boss can rule.

**The internal boss inherits the worst voice in your head.** The voice that talks to you about your own work was usually trained on a critical-parent voice (see [[perfectionism]]). The voice that you would have rebelled against if your boss had used it is the voice you have installed as your own internal management.

**The internal boss has no accountability.** An external bad boss can be confronted, reported, or quit. The internal bad boss has nobody above them. The only person who can fire the internal bad boss is the person being managed by them. Most people do not see that they have the authority to do so.

The result, in practice: most self-employed people who are struggling have built a worse working environment for themselves than the one they left.

## The Recursive Trap

A specific failure mode worth naming. When the principle is named — "you have to be a good boss to yourself" — the person often tries to install the principle as a new instruction: "I gotta take the pressure off myself" or "I gotta be a better boss." This is the same internal bad boss issuing a new directive. The pressure-to-take-pressure-off is itself pressure.

> "Yeah, I got to take the pressure off myself."
>
> "That, that is a way to put pressure on yourself. 'I've got to take the pressure off myself.'"
>
> "Yeah, yeah, that's pressure, isn't it?"
>
> "That's more pressure." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The good internal boss is not installed by command. The good internal boss is the relationship that emerges when [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] is recognized and the importance is allowed to drop. The redirect is not "be a better boss" but "stop trying to enforce; redirect to what would be enjoyable." See [[dont-make-stop-judging-into-a-should]] for the structural anti-pattern this recursive trap belongs to.

## Joe's Own Demonstration

Joe used himself as the live example, mid-coaching:

> "Could you imagine how horrible I'd be at what I'm doing right now if I needed to get a result?"
>
> "Very. Yeah, yeah. Probably wouldn't be sat here still."
>
> "Yeah, I would, I would hate my job. Right. Would you know how bad this session would be if you needed to be perfect?"
>
> "Yeah, it'd be pretty awful. It'd be pretty awful." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The demonstration is structural. Joe is doing skilled work in real time. The work is going well. The reason it is going well is that he is not being a bad boss to himself about it — he is not demanding the session be perfect, he is not gripping for a result. The example is the principle made flesh.

The implicit invitation is for the person to see that the relationship to the work Joe is demonstrating is available to them too. Joe is not special. He is just a person who has done the work to install a good internal boss. The work is available to anyone who recognizes the role they have been playing for themselves.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You left an external job and find yourself working harder and enjoying it less
- The work you do for yourself feels heavier than the work you used to do for others
- You catch yourself making demands of yourself you would have considered abusive coming from an external boss
- The thing you wanted to do for yourself has become the thing you most dread
- You feel like you have to earn the right to enjoy your own work

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone has gone self-employed (or is a founder) and is procrastinating chronically on their own work
- When the person reports they could work fine for others but not for themselves
- When [[i-procrastinate-only-on-the-thing-that-matters-most]] is the presenting concern
- When [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] has surfaced as the mechanism
- When the person is describing the felt experience of working for themselves as worse than the felt experience of working for anyone else

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] — the operational move that drops the bad-boss pattern in real time
- Toward [[enjoy-over-manage]] — the broader leadership principle this is the internal application of
- Toward [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] — the upstream mechanism producing the bad internal boss
- Toward [[voice-as-care]] — the reframing of the internal boss voice as a misguided form of care
- Toward [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] — when the internal boss has converted the original want into a should that produces resistance

## Tone

Slightly humorous. Joe delivers this with a small smile in his voice — the recognition is genuinely funny once seen. The person has been enduring a worse boss than any they would have tolerated externally, and they had not noticed because the boss was them. The humor is what allows the recognition to land without producing more self-attack.
