# Don't Use "You" in an Apology

## The Principle

A clean apology does not contain the word "you." The moment "you" appears, the apology has slipped into blame, deflection, or shame-induction of the other. The hack functions as a fast diagnostic and a constraint that forces the apology into integrity.

"There's a quick hack, which is when you apologize, never use the word 'you.' Never make it about them. It's always about you. When we do our exercises, we are always very clear, like, the word 'you' is the, if you're using that, it's not a real apology." -- Joe Hudson, The Upright Apology

## Why It Works

The word "you" routes the apology toward the other person. Once routed there, it almost always becomes one of:

- A passive-aggressive blame: "I'm sorry you felt that way."
- A non-apology with a hidden demand: "I'm sorry if you misunderstood me."
- An induced-shame move: "I'm sorry you had to deal with me."
- A surrogate accusation: "I'm sorry you keep doing this."

The rule constrains the speaker to stay on their own side of the line. What did I do? What is mine to own? What do I want to acknowledge about my behavior? The grammar enforces what the integrity requires.

## What the Clean Version Sounds Like

- "I'm sorry for raising my voice."
- "I'm sorry. I don't want to be talking in this way."
- "I'm sorry for being in a power struggle."
- "I'm sorry I was trying to control."
- "I'm sorry, I made a mistake."

Each sentence has the speaker as subject and the speaker's behavior as object. The other person is not mentioned. The apology is about what the speaker did, not about how the other person received it.

## The Forgiveness Mirror

The same constraint applies in the inverse to forgiveness. Forgiveness, like apology, is for the speaker, not for the other person. Both are practices of taking back your own ground.

"There's this way that the forgiveness is for you. It's for your own freedom, for your own purification. And I see that also just having a nice mirror here on the apology side. What is it to just be apologizing for yourself and not to be trying to make anything happen in the other person." -- Brett Kistler, The Upright Apology

## When the "You" Sneaks In

Notice when the apology drifts into:
- "You" -- this is the rule violation.
- "If you" -- this is the conditional non-apology.
- "Because you" -- this is the blame disguised as apology.
- "That you" -- this is often the shame-induction.

If the apology cannot be reconstructed without these phrases, the apology is not actually being made. Something else is being delivered under the form of apology. See [[forms-of-dysfunctional-apology]].

## Boundary Conditions

The "you" rule is for the apology itself, not for the entire conversation. After delivering the upright apology, the speaker may absolutely talk about the other person, raise grievances, or make requests. The constraint is on the apology sentence.

It is also a diagnostic, not a religion. Some apologies legitimately reference the other person ("I'm sorry I hurt you"). The rule's purpose is to catch the cases where "you" is doing covert work. Test each "you" by asking: is this owning what I did, or is this routing the apology toward blame?
