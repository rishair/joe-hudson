# Emotional Movement (Not Figuring Things Out) Unsticks Stuckness

## The Principle

When a person reports being stuck, the instinct is to figure it out — analyze the situation, generate options, develop a plan. This usually fails. The stuckness is rarely a problem of analysis; it is a problem of stalled emotional movement. The cognitive layer is where the stuckness presents; the emotional layer is where the stuckness resolves.

The diagnostic move: if a person feels stuck, ask them to move some emotion and check whether the stuck-feeling is still there. Often it is not. The stuckness was the felt sense of held emotion, and once the emotion moves, the stuckness moves with it.

"I think oftentimes, oftentimes when people are stuck, it's because of a thought pattern. It's not because of reality. And another part is that it's like, there's also — means like, stuck is often like stuck in your head. And so let's get into the emotion because that's where oftentimes if you, if you find yourself stuck, oftentimes it's emotional movement, emotional fluidity that's going to actually change something. It's not figuring something out typically." — Joe Hudson, How Do I Get Unstuck? (Coaching Commentary)

## What This Reframes

The standard relationship to stuckness is to treat it as a problem requiring more thought. "I need to figure this out." "I need to analyze why I keep doing this." "I need to make a plan." These all locate the work in the cognitive layer.

The principle relocates the work. The cognitive layer is where stuckness presents, not where it lives. The actual living material is held emotion — sensation in the body that has not completed its movement. The cognitive activity is downstream of the held emotion; thinking harder about the situation does not move the emotion any more than thinking harder about a stiff shoulder loosens it.

This is why people can spend years thinking about their stuckness without resolving it. The thinking is not the wrong tool because it is shallow. The thinking is the wrong tool because it is operating on the wrong layer. The right tool is whatever moves the held emotion: feeling it, expressing it, letting it complete in the body, or — as in the source session — amplifying it deliberately to surface what is being held. See [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]].

## The Direct Implication for Coaching

When a client presents stuckness, the coaching does not begin with strategy questions. It begins with sensation questions. "How does the stuckness feel?" "Where is it in your body?" "What is the felt sense of being stuck right now?" These move the conversation from the cognitive layer where stuckness is unresolvable to the emotional layer where it can complete.

The 2024-03-18 session demonstrates this move precisely. The client said she felt stuck. Joe asked: "How does stuck feel?" The client described a mousetrap sensation, feet stuck to the ground. The conversation had moved to the layer where work could be done. Once on that layer, the amplification move and the entry move could surface what was being held, and the held material could complete.

The standard alternative — "what are the options you've been considering?" or "what would you need to take action?" — keeps the conversation on the layer where the work cannot happen. The conversation produces more words and no movement.

## Why Stuckness Lives in Emotion

The structural reason: a stuck-feeling is the felt sense of an emotion that has not completed. Emotions, when allowed to move, complete on their own timeline and leave the body. Emotions, when not allowed to move, persist as the chronic background sense of stuckness, heaviness, or constriction. The person experiences this as "being stuck" without realizing that what is stuck is the emotion, not the person.

The thinking activity that often accompanies stuckness is the system attempting to manage the unmoved emotion. The thinking is not solving the stuckness; it is the symptom of an emotion that needs to move and is being kept from moving by the constant cognitive activity.

This is also why "figuring it out" can make stuckness worse. The figuring-out is more cognitive activity, which is more holding of the emotion in place. The harder the person figures, the longer the emotion stays stuck, the longer the felt-sense of stuckness persists.

## The Operational Test

The principle provides a fast diagnostic: ask the client to drop into the felt sense of the stuckness for thirty seconds. If the stuckness moves or shifts during the drop-in, it was emotional. The right intervention is more felt contact. If the stuckness stays exactly the same during the drop-in, there may be a layer of avoidance in the way — the client is technically dropping in but not actually contacting the felt sense. In that case, the move is to address what is being avoided. See [[lets-go-there]].

In neither case is the right move "let me give you a planning framework." The principle reliably points away from cognitive interventions and toward felt interventions.

## What This Is Not

This is not a claim that thinking is bad. Thinking is appropriate for situations that are actually thinking problems (planning a project, debugging code, designing an experiment). The principle applies specifically to felt-stuckness — the experience a person reports when they say "I feel stuck." That experience is rarely a thinking problem.

It is also not a claim that all stuckness is emotional. There are situations where a person genuinely lacks information and the felt-stuckness will move once the information is acquired. The principle is operational: try the emotional move first, because it tends to be the right one. If the emotional move produces nothing, then look elsewhere.

It is also not the same as the catharsis model — "express your emotions and you will feel better." The principle is about contact and movement, not expression. A person can express emotion all day without contacting the held material, and the stuckness will persist. See [[acting-out-emotions-as-fluidity]] for the related anti-pattern.

## What Emotional Movement Looks Like

The form varies. It can look like:
- Crying that comes without a story attached
- Anger moving through the body as heat or shaking
- A long breath out as something releases
- A small laugh of recognition followed by relaxation
- A felt shift in the body — softening, settling, expanding
- A sensation that was present and then is not

What it does not look like: the person reaches a verbal conclusion about their situation. The verbal conclusion may follow the movement, but it is not the movement itself. If the person produces a verbal insight without a felt shift, the insight is on the wrong layer and the stuckness will probably return.

The marker that the emotional movement has happened: the body feels different in a way the person can describe. The "I feel stuck" report changes to something else — "I feel sad," "I feel relieved," "I feel surprised." The shift in the felt-state language is the diagnostic.

## The Direct Address to a Person Who Feels Stuck

The teaching, in its concentrated form, given as advice to listeners: "So that for anybody out there who's listening who feels stuck, it's a great thing to know, like move some emotion, see if you still feel stuck." — Joe Hudson, How Do I Get Unstuck? (Coaching Commentary)

The instruction is simple. Move some emotion. Then check. The check is empirical: is the stuck-feeling still there? If yes, more movement is needed, or a deeper layer needs to be touched. If no, the stuckness was the held emotion, and it has done what it needed to do.

The person does not need to know what the emotion was about. They do not need to be able to articulate why they were stuck. The mechanism does not require understanding. It requires movement.

## Tone

This principle is delivered with the slight relief of someone offering a corrective to a much harder-and-less-effective approach. The cognitive approach to stuckness exhausts people. Pointing them to a different layer is a kindness. The tone is gentle, almost confidential — "here's something it would be useful for you to know."

When the principle is enacted in a session, the tone is steady and direct. The coach does not announce "I am now redirecting you to the emotional layer." The coach simply asks the felt-sense question and works from there. The shift happens without commentary.
