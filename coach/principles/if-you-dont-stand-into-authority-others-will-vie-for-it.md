# If You Don't Stand Into Authority, Others Will Vie for It

## The Principle

A leader who refuses to inhabit the authority of their role does not create a freer or more egalitarian organization. They create a vacuum. The vacuum gets filled by people vying for the authority that the structure has not allocated. The vying produces chaos. The under-asserting leader's intention (not to be oppressive) produces the opposite of the intended outcome (a team that feels unsafe and politically distorted).

"If you don't come in and say... 'this is our process. This is our values. This is our principles. This is how we operate.' If you don't give enough of that to a team, they won't feel safe. So, it's better to say, 'this is exactly how I want you to do something,' which is the far side of one spectrum... When there's no authority, people start vying for that authority role. If you don't provide it, other people will vie for it, and that creates utter chaos." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## The Specific Trap This Names

The principle addresses a particular failure mode: the rebel-turned-leader who built or runs the organization partly to escape oppressive authority and who now cannot inhabit the authority role because they associate it with what was done to them. This is Position 3 in [[the-four-authority-positions]].

The trap has a specific shape:

1. The leader had a bad authority figure in their childhood (often a father).
2. The leader's rebellion against that authority figure became the engine of their entrepreneurial drive.
3. The leader is now in the authority position they spent their life avoiding being subjected to.
4. To avoid becoming the oppressor they hated, the leader under-asserts. They do not set process. They do not set principles. They tell people "do whatever you want, figure it out."
5. The team experiences this not as freedom but as abandonment. They do not know what to do, what is expected, what success looks like, what they will be judged on.
6. Inside the vacuum, people start vying for the authority that has not been provided. Political dynamics intensify. Decisions stall. The team feels unsafe.

Joe describes recognizing this in his own leadership history:

"And the middle path for me has been — I had like such a 'yeah, just do what you want, figure it out. Tell me what you want to do.' That doesn't work either." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## Why the Vacuum Produces Worse Outcomes Than the Original Authority

The under-asserting leader thinks they are avoiding the harm their original authority caused. They are actually producing a different harm.

The original authority caused harm by over-asserting: telling people what to do, what to think, who to be. The under-assertion does not produce the opposite of this. It produces unsafety. Without structure, people cannot orient. Without principles, they cannot make decisions confidently. Without process, every action becomes politically loaded.

The original authority's harm was felt by people who knew what was expected of them and resented it. The under-asserting leader's harm is felt by people who do not know what is expected of them and are anxious.

Both are forms of authority dysfunction. The under-assertion is not the cure for the over-assertion. It is the opposite imbalance.

## The Middle Path: Process and Principles, Not Directives

The corrective is not to swing back to telling people what to do. It is to inhabit the role differently. The leader provides the structure — process, principles, values, roles — without dictating the substance of the work.

"The middle path for me has been, this is, these are the principles, this is how we operate. This is the structure. This is the process. These are the roles. If you want to change them, I'm — it's open to discussion. But this is how we're doing things so that people feel safe." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The leader is the authority on:
- How decisions get made
- How meetings run
- How emails work
- How problems get solved
- What the values are
- What the principles are
- What the roles are

The leader is not the authority on:
- The substance of what each person does within their role
- The specific moves a team makes inside the agreed process
- The creative content of the work

This produces empowerment with safety. The team has agency inside a known container. The container is held by the leader. The team is held by the container.

See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for the broader organizational version of this principle.

## The Felt-Sense Diagnostic

The principle has a diagnostic in the body of the leader and the team:

- **If the leader feels "I am being too controlling":** they might be over-asserting on substance instead of holding structure.
- **If the team feels "I don't know what's expected":** the leader is under-asserting on structure.
- **If the team feels "I can't take any initiative":** the leader is over-asserting on substance.
- **If the team feels political dynamics intensifying:** the leader is under-asserting and the vacuum is being filled.

The same leader can be over-asserting on substance and under-asserting on structure simultaneously. The corrective is not "more authority" or "less authority" — it is to shift where the authority is being exercised. Hold the structure firm; release the substance.

## The Facilitator Version

Joe gives the same principle in a facilitation context. If he is facilitating a group and does not act as enough of an authority, the room feels unsafe. If he acts as too much of an authority, the room also feels unsafe. The balance is the work.

"If we're facilitating something, if I do not act like enough of an authority, the whole room feels unsafe. If I act too much like an authority, the room starts to feel unsafe. It's like it is this balance." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The same dynamics that play out in a company play out in a single session. The principle is the same: provide the container, do not dictate the contents.

## The Counterintuitive Implication

The principle has a counterintuitive implication for leaders who want their teams to feel empowered: empowerment requires more structure, not less. The under-asserting leader who is trying to maximize team freedom is producing the opposite of empowerment. The leader who is willing to inhabit the structural role releases the team to be empowered inside the structure.

This connects to [[structure-and-empowerment]] more broadly. Structure is not the opposite of empowerment; it is its substrate.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle as a correction to his own earlier instinct. The tone is rueful — he tried the "just do what you want" version, watched it fail, and now teaches the middle path. There is no shame in the rebel-turned-leader trap; it is a predictable consequence of a particular developmental trajectory. The work is to recognize it and find the middle.
