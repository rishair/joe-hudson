# Trying to Be Someone Other Than Yourself IS the Actual Imposter Act

## The Principle

The imposter feeling is correct. The person reporting "I feel like an imposter" is right. The mistake is in what the feeling is about. The person believes the feeling is reporting on their incompetence — "I feel like an imposter because I am not actually qualified." The feeling is actually reporting on something else: that they are trying to be someone they are not. The performance of competence is the imposter act. The not-knowing underneath is not.

"There's an interesting little like mind twist in there, which is that if you feel imposter syndrome, it's correct, except you're being an imposter in a different way than you might think. You think you need to be a certain type of confident, competent, confident, whatever that identity is. But in reality, the fact that you feel imposter syndrome is a sign that you are trying to be something other than yourself." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The implication: the feeling can be trusted as a signal, but the diagnosis it suggests cannot. The signal says "you are being inauthentic right now." The mistaken interpretation is "you are not skilled enough at the inauthentic role." Following the mistaken interpretation produces more inauthenticity (more skill at being the imagined someone else). Following the actual signal produces the move out: drop the performance, be yourself.

## Where the Misreading Comes From

The misreading is structural. The person grew up believing they had to be a certain way to be lovable. Some performance of value was required. So when the felt-sense of "I am not being myself right now" arrives, the only available interpretation is the one the conditional-worth installation gave them: "I am failing to perform the required version of myself." The feeling cannot be heard as a signal that the performance itself is the problem, because the performance is felt as a survival condition.

The person then tries to fix the feeling by getting better at the performance. They build more skill, accumulate more credentials, polish the act. The feeling does not go away because the act is exactly the thing the feeling is pointing at. The fixing-the-act strategy reinforces the very condition that produced the feeling.

See [[good-jobbed-as-conditional-validation]] for the installation of the conditional-worth structure and [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]] for the broader principle this is one application of.

## The Inversion

The standard logic: "If I feel like an imposter, I must not be qualified. The cure is to become qualified." This is the logic the person is running.

The actual logic: "If I feel like an imposter, I am performing a self that isn't mine. The cure is to drop the performance." The cure has the opposite shape from what the person is reaching for. They are reaching for more competence; what they need is less performance.

The inversion makes the cure counterintuitive. The person who is exhausted from trying to be confident has to do the opposite of what feels safer — admit what they don't know, ask for help, let the gap be visible. The vulnerability that feels like it would expose them as an imposter is actually the move out of being an imposter. The performance that feels like it would protect them is actually the imposter act itself.

"The only way to be confident in yourself is to be yourself. I'm going to pretend to be somebody else and now I'm confident being somebody else? No, like, it doesn't, it doesn't work like that." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

## The Only Place You're Not an Imposter

The complementary statement: there is exactly one position where the person is not an imposter — being themselves. Every other position is structurally imposter-status, by definition, because every other position is being-someone-other-than-themselves.

"Only you can be you. Like, that's the only place you're not an imposter, by the way. When you're being yourself. Everything else, you are being an imposter. So of course it feels like you're being an imposter." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The statement makes the structural fact explicit. The person trying to be a perfect leader, a perfect parent, a perfect anything is necessarily being an imposter in that role, because that role is not them. The same person being themselves while leading, parenting, doing anything is not being an imposter — they are being the only thing they can be without performing.

This reframes the work. The work is not to become the imagined competent person. The work is to be the actual person who happens to be doing this thing without fully knowing how. The first is the imposter act; the second is the exit.

## Connection to the Cognitive Cost

This principle pairs directly with [[maintaining-false-persona-costs-more-than-incompetence]]. The persona overhead and the imposter act are the same activity. Maintaining the false self consumes the bandwidth that the actual self would have used to do the work. The performance produces the very incompetence the performance is supposed to hide.

The combined picture: the imposter act both feels bad (the feeling correctly reports the inauthenticity) AND degrades performance (the bandwidth cost is real). Dropping the act resolves both. The feeling goes away because the act it was reporting on is gone. The performance improves because the bandwidth comes back. Two outcomes from one move.

## The Vulnerability Connection

This principle is also why vulnerability is the operative cure. Vulnerability — saying what you don't know, asking for help, letting the gap be visible — is exactly the dropping of the performance. The performance is the front; vulnerability is taking the front down. When the front comes down, the imposter act ends, and the feeling that was reporting on the imposter act has nothing to report.

"Brené Brown's work on this is pretty conclusive that if you're vulnerable, and you show that you don't know everything, the feeling of being an imposter starts dropping." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The mechanism is structural, not just emotional. Vulnerability is not "feeling brave enough" to admit weakness. It is the literal removal of the imposter act. The feeling drops because its source has been removed.

## In Coaching

When a client reports imposter syndrome and is asking how to convince themselves they are qualified:

1. Validate the feeling. It is correct.
2. Correct the diagnosis. The feeling is not about competence; it is about performance.
3. Locate the performance. What is the role they are trying to perform that is not them?
4. Name the inversion. The fix is to drop the performance, not to get better at it.
5. Move to the practical: what is one piece of the role they could be themselves in instead of performing? What gap could they name out loud?
6. Connect to [[ask-for-all-the-help-you-can-get]] and [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]] — the behavioral form of the principle.

When the client resists ("but I have to seem confident or they won't trust me"):

- The resistance is the conditional-worth installation operating. They cannot yet imagine being lovable / hireable / followable without the performance.
- Move to the structural fact: people trust the leader who can say "I don't know — what do you all think?" more than they trust the leader who has to pretend. The performance is not actually producing the trust the client thinks it is producing.
- See [[ceo-vulnerability-builds-team-alignment]] and related principles.

## Tone

Direct, with a small smile of relief built in. The "mind twist" framing Joe uses is doing real work — the principle is delivered as a structural surprise rather than a moral correction. The client has been working very hard at the wrong thing. They are not bad for it; the conditional-worth installation made the misreading nearly automatic. The reframe lets them stop working at the wrong thing.

Avoid moralizing the performance ("you shouldn't be performing"). The performance was protective once and is now in the way. The shift is from "stop performing because it's bad" to "stop performing because it's the source of the feeling you're trying to get rid of."
