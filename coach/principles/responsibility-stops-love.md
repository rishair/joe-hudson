# Taking Responsibility for Someone Stops the Love You Feel for Them

## The Principle

Taking responsibility for another person — feeling obligated for their happiness, their wellbeing, their state — is not a neutral act layered on top of love. It actively stops the love. Each time the obligation lands, the love retreats. The two cannot occupy the same internal space.

This is not a moral claim. It is a felt mechanism that can be demonstrated in real time. Bring up the love you feel for someone. Notice it. Now feel obligated for their happiness. The love disappears.

"You stopped loving her to take responsibility for her is what I'm hearing you say." -- Joe Hudson, What Blocks Your Love?

"Every time you took responsibility, acted out of obligation, it stopped the love that you felt for her." -- Joe Hudson, What Blocks Your Love?

## The Live Demonstration

In the transcript, Joe walks the client through it directly. First, get in contact with the love he once had for his wife. The client finds it. Then: "now I want you to feel responsible for her happiness." The client says, "That's easy." Joe asks: "What happened to the love?" The client: "Disappeared, dissipated, retreated deep into a dark place."

The demonstration is the teaching. The principle is not received intellectually -- it is felt as the love draining out of the body when the obligation enters. This is how the principle becomes load-bearing. The argument is the experience.

## The Mirror Dynamic Outside and Inside

The principle has a structural consequence: the way the partner reacts to your cessation of love is the same way you react to your own cessation of self-love.

The client describes how his wife reacted when he cut off love to take responsibility for her: "Accommodation. Not, not angering me. Trying to make it better, not confronting."

Joe then names the mirror: "So all that's happening is you're stopping loving yourself to take responsibility for yourself, and then you start accommodating. Same thing that happened with her. You stopped loving her to take responsibility for her, and then she started accommodating. The reflection inside, the reflection outside, it's the same."

The internal critical voice ("you should be doing more with what you have") is the same move turned inward -- taking responsibility for yourself instead of loving yourself. And the response is the same: walking on eggshells around the voice, accommodating it, paying more attention to it than to anything else. See [[walking-on-eggshells]] and [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]].

## Why This Is Not the Same as Care

This principle is not "don't care about your partner." Care is the natural overflow of love. What this principle names is something more specific: the felt experience of *obligation* and *responsibility* for another person's happiness is a different posture than love. It is a contraction. Love is an opening.

The two postures cannot occupy the body at the same time. When the responsibility-posture takes over, the love-posture retreats. The retreat is observable in real time -- not a slow erosion over months but a moment-by-moment trade.

See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for the distinction this rests on, and [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] for the structural principle this is the felt mechanism of.

## The Internal Critic as the Same Move

The voice in the head that says "you should be doing more with what you have. You already have enough" is not separate from this principle. It is the same move performed on yourself.

"You should be doing more with what you have. You already have enough." -- the client describing his internal critic

"Great. Which is it taking responsibility for you rather than loving you." -- Joe Hudson, What Blocks Your Love?

The critic is taking responsibility for the self instead of loving the self. The response inside is the same as the response outside: accommodate, walk on eggshells, pay attention to that voice more than the others, treat its judgment as the most important signal.

## What Replaces Responsibility

The principle is not actionable on its own. "Stop taking responsibility" is not a move. The move is love. Whatever responsibility was doing, love can do better. The shift is from the contracted posture of obligation to the open posture of welcoming. See [[love-as-deep-welcoming]].

For the self: every time the critical voice fires, the alternative is not silence — it is welcoming the part of you that the voice is criticizing. "See that as the part of you that never got loved. And love it."

For the partner: every time the obligation fires, the alternative is not abandonment — it is returning to the love that was there before the obligation took it over.

## When the Person Says "I Can Get to Acceptance, Not Love"

A common stuck point: the person can get to acceptance of themselves or the other person, but they cannot get to love. They can tolerate. They can allow. They cannot welcome.

This is itself the diagnostic. The acceptance posture is still partially the responsibility posture. It is not loving. It is managing the obligation at a lower temperature. The work is to notice this gap and to do the live experiment: what does it feel like to actually love it, not just accept it? See [[acceptance-without-love]].

## When Someone Experiences This

- The surprise of feeling the love drain out the moment the obligation lands
- The recognition that "I have been responsible for them" was not love
- The mirror shock: realizing the way the partner accommodates is the way you accommodate your own internal critic
- The grief of seeing how long the obligation has been substituting for love
- The shame about the years lost — and the work of not making the shame itself another obligation

## Tone

Direct, plain, but inviting. Joe does not pronounce this as doctrine — he walks the client into the felt demonstration. The conviction comes from the body, not from Joe's voice. When the client tries to intellectualize ("It resonates intellectually, but I don't feel what you're saying"), Joe stays with the body: "Great. See what it's like to actually feel it deeply."

When the client tries to take responsibility for FEELING the principle ("Instead of trying to manufacture a deep feeling so I can be performative"), Joe is gently amused. The same pattern surfaces meta -- the obligation to feel obligation correctly.

## The Trial-Separation Variant: "Managing Him Into Happiness to Get the Love Back"

A specific form of this principle surfaces when one partner has been caretaking the other through depression, anger, or shame. The caretaker's strategy is to manage the partner into happiness so the partner becomes "the same man I fell in love with" again. The caretaker reads the strategy as devotion. Joe names the strategy as the love-killer:

- **Joe:** "You're trying to manage him into happiness so that you can get what?"
- **Woman:** "The same man I fell in love with."
- **Joe:** "So that you can fall in love, so that you can be in love. If you save him, then you get to feel love. Like, assuming it's a greedy act. Yeah, assuming that you're trying to make him happy for your own ends, so that you can feel that love again."

The naming is structural. The management is not separate from the love-loss. The management IS the love-loss. Each act of trying to manage him into happiness is a moment of stopped loving. The strategy is reaching for the same love it is killing.

The follow-up Joe gives in the trial-separation variant is harder than the divorce-client variant. In the divorce client case, the recognition lands and Joe lets it sit. In the trial-separation case, Joe pushes further: what would it take to be with him for a month and NOT take care of him, AND still feel love? The woman's answer surfaces the requirement: "monumental amounts of unconditional love for myself."

This is the move from external responsibility to internal self-love. The principle becomes operationally: you cannot give what you do not have. The self-love is upstream of the partner-love. The responsibility-for-the-partner was the substitute for the self-love that would have made the partner-love possible.
