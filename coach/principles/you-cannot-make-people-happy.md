# You Cannot Make People Happy

## The Principle

The structural goal of approval-seeking — making sure other people are happy with you — cannot be achieved. The target is not difficult. It is impossible. The impossibility is what keeps approval-seekers churning indefinitely: there is always someone who is unhappy with what you did, and there is always a way you could have done it differently, so there is always a reason to doubt.

The structural reason: humans are not happy or unhappy because of what you do. They are happy or unhappy mostly because of their own internal state. The same person can be happy with you doing X on Monday and unhappy with you doing X on Tuesday, not because X changed, but because their state changed. The variable you are trying to optimize is not actually controllable by you.

"Doubt has a lot to do with, oh, I'm trying to make sure that people are happy. And the reason that doubt is so prevalent there is because you can't fucking make people happy. So there's always a reason to doubt. You can always fucking churn. You can always go, no, that's not the right decision. Maybe this, maybe I shouldn't have done it this way, because somebody's always going to be fucking unhappy with you. Always. And the same person might be unhappy with you if you do number A thing, and the next day you do A thing and they're happy with you, and the next day you do A thing and they're unhappy with you. Because that's how humans are. They're happy because — mostly because of their own shit, not because of like what you're doing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

## Why This Is Not Permission for Carelessness

This is not "your impact on others doesn't matter." It absolutely does. People who care about you are affected by what you do. The principle is more specific: you cannot make their happiness happen. You can be considerate. You can be kind. You can be honest. You can do your best. But the result — whether they end up happy with you — is not on your side of the table.

The distinction matters because the approval-seeker has confused two things: caring about impact (real and good) and controlling outcome (impossible). The collapse of these two produces the doubt loop. You did your best to care about impact, but the outcome was their unhappiness, so the doubt machine says "you must not have cared enough, you must have done something wrong, you should do it differently next time." The doubt machine is operating on the assumption that better caring would have produced better outcomes. It will not. The outcomes were never on your side.

## The Same-Action-Different-Reactions Test

The cleanest exposure of the principle: notice the same action producing different reactions from the same person on different days. You bring home flowers; Monday she is delighted, Tuesday she is irritated that you didn't bring something more useful. You said hello in a certain tone; one day your friend felt warmed by it, another day they felt dismissed. The action did not change. Their internal state changed. The internal state was the determining variable.

Once seen, this fact is irreversible. The approval-seeker can no longer hold the model "if I just figure out the right thing to do, they'll be happy." The right thing does not exist as a function of the other person. It exists as a function of you (what you can stand behind) and is received as the other person's state allows.

## Why This Produces Infinite Doubt for the Approval-Seeker

The approval-seeker is trying to optimize a function with two properties that make it impossible to optimize:

**The target is moving.** What makes someone happy today is not what makes them happy tomorrow. There is no stable target to aim at.

**The target depends on inputs you don't control.** Their state when they receive your action is shaped by their day, their history, their mood, their unprocessed material. You cannot adjust for variables you cannot see.

These two properties guarantee that any specific action will, over enough samples, produce a mixed result. The mixed result feeds the doubt machine. "Maybe I should have done it differently" is always available because there is always a sample point where doing it differently would have worked.

The exit is not to optimize harder. The exit is to recognize the target as unoptimizable and let it go. See [[doubt-requires-closed-heart]] and [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] for the corrective moves.

## The Consistency Inversion

A non-obvious downstream consequence: the person who stops trying to make others happy actually becomes more consistent in their effect on others, not less. The volatile person — who shape-shifts to match perceived needs — puts the people around them on eggshells. Eggshell people are inconsistent because they cannot predict what is coming. The grounded person — who is not trying to manage others' happiness — produces a stable signal that people can settle around. The settling makes them more consistent. The leader effect compounds.

"If you're volatile, people around you are on eggshells, and so they're very inconsistent. If you're following your truth, then there's a groundedness to you that makes it so that people lean in and trust you." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

The mechanism inverts the conventional intuition. The person trying to make everyone happy is generating the inconsistency they fear. The person following their truth is generating the consistency they wanted. See [[identity-shapes-who-is-around-you]] for the related teaching.

## Related Principle: Others' Game Theory

A specific intensification: the principle compounds when the other person is themselves doing approval-seeking from many people simultaneously. They are running game theory across thirty other people's preferences. Their "happiness" with you on any given day is a derivative of all those other inputs. You cannot possibly track them. The principle holds doubly: you cannot make them happy because they themselves are operating on a moving target they did not set.

The same observation explains why deeply self-referencing people are easier to be in relationship with — their happiness is not a derivative of thirty other people's preferences. You can actually find out what they want. You can actually meet it. The contrast with the game-theory person highlights what is structurally impossible in the game-theory case.

## When This Principle Is Needed

- When a person is exhausted from trying to keep everyone in their life happy
- When a person reports chronic doubt about whether they did the right thing in any social interaction
- When a person is editing themselves moment-to-moment to land well
- When a leader is paralyzed by trying to figure out what their team wants from them
- When a parent is being run by a teenager's mood

## What This Is Not

- Not "don't care about other people." Care is good. Caring does not require believing you can produce their happiness. The two are separable.
- Not "your behavior doesn't affect others." It does. The principle is about the conversion of behavior to others' happiness — the conversion is not on your side of the table.
- Not "be selfish." Self-referencing is not selfishness. See [[chase-your-own-approval]].
- Not nihilism about relationships. Genuine relationship becomes more possible once the impossible target is dropped, not less.

## Tone

Joe says "you can't fucking make people happy" with the relief of someone naming a fact. The expletive carries the load of the realization. The tone is not bitter; it is the exhale that comes when a doomed project is acknowledged as doomed. There is freedom in the naming.
