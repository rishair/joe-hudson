# The Work Is Yours, Not Mine

## Definition

In a coaching relationship, the work belongs to the client. The depth, the pace, the topic, the willingness -- all of it. The coach is available if the client wants to engage. The coach does not own the engagement.

This is not abdication of the coach's role. The coach holds the container, names what they see, asks what wants asking. But the work itself, the inner movement, belongs entirely to the person doing it. The coach who tries to own the work installs the same dynamic that most clients are trying to undo.

"It's the work that I've put in and not the work that he's done." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot, Reclaiming Abandonment

## Why It Matters

The clients who most need transformation often arrive with a long history of being pushed: by parents, by themselves, by managers, by mentors. They have internalized the push into a permanent inner driver. If the coach also pushes, the coaching becomes another instance of the same dynamic. The transformation cannot land because the mode of arriving at it is the mode that made transformation necessary.

The transformation lands when the client chooses the work from a want rather than a should. See [[wants-over-shoulds]]. The coach's job is to make that choice possible, which requires not making the choice for them.

## The Principle in the Mouth of the Coach

Joe to Stacy, when asked what she would work on next:

"What I do know is that if you don't come up with something that you want to work on this year, you won't work on anything." -- Joe Hudson, quoted by Stacy Brown-Philpot, Reclaiming Abandonment

The sentence does not tell her what to work on. It tells her that the choice is hers and that if she does not make it, nothing will happen. The consequence is named without the prescription. The ownership remains hers.

This is the principle in operating form. The coach is not absent. The coach is naming structural realities. But the actual material of the work has to come from the client.

## How It Sounds Different From Coaching That Owns the Work

A coach who owns the work would say: "Let's focus on your relationship with your sister this year. I think that's where the biggest growth is available." The sentence locates the work, sets the agenda, and centers the coach as the one who knows. The client then either submits or rebels. Either way, the coach is running the relationship.

A coach who holds this principle would say: "What do you want to work on this year?" Or, if the client cannot name it: "What I do know is that if you don't pick something, you won't work on anything." The first version puts the question in the client's lap. The second names the consequence of not answering without filling in the answer.

The diagnostic: notice the location of agency in the coach's sentence. If the coach's sentence describes the client's path, the work has been claimed by the coach. If the coach's sentence describes the conditions under which the client will or will not move, the work remains the client's.

## Why Clients Initially Resist This

A client who has been pushed all their life often experiences the principle as abandonment. The coach not telling them what to do can read as the coach not caring. The first reaction is sometimes to push harder for the coach to take ownership.

The principle requires the coach to hold steady through this reaction. The coach who hands the work back to the client when the client tries to hand it over is not refusing to coach. The coach is teaching the client what coaching actually is. The teaching only lands when the coach can stay with the discomfort of not rescuing.

See [[meet-resistance-with-okay]] for the specific move that operationalizes this principle when the client resists.

## The Inversion of the Therapist Model

This principle distinguishes Joe's work from the model where the practitioner is the expert who knows what the client needs. Stacy describes how hard it was to explain Joe to friends:

"It's hard to describe because people will ask me, who is Joe? And then, and I'll say, well, he's a coach, but he's not like an executive coach like most people have. Was he a therapist? He's not a therapist because he's not licensed. And anything like that. So he's just this person. And so you start with something like that and everyone looks at you in a weird way like, 'What are we talking about here?' So what I try to say is this, working with Joe changed who I am as a person. And he's helped me become a better person. And one of the reasons why is because it's the work that I've put in and not the work that he's done." -- Stacy Brown-Philpot, Reclaiming Abandonment

The shape of the relationship has to be explained because it does not match the standard expert-and-client frame. The client did the work. The coach was there.

## Connection to the Become-Unnecessary Principle

This principle is a sister principle to [[become-unnecessary]]. Both say the coach who is doing the work right makes themselves less central, not more. The client's transformation is not evidence of the coach's mastery. The transformation belongs to the client. The coach's mastery is in not getting in the way of it.

A coach who owns the work creates clients who need the coach. A coach who hands the work back creates clients who can do the work without anyone.

## When This Principle Gets Hard

- When the client is in pain and the coach can see exactly what would help. The urge to prescribe is the urge to take ownership.
- When the client is wasting sessions, going in circles, avoiding the obvious. The urge to redirect is the urge to take ownership.
- When the coach has invested in the relationship and the client is about to drop out. The urge to save the relationship is the urge to take ownership.

In each case, the work is the coach's: notice the urge, return the agency to the client, hold the okay. See [[owning-your-partiality]].

## What It Produces

Clients who own their development. Who can articulate what they want to work on. Who can choose to engage or disengage cleanly. Who do not need the coach for the work to continue. Who, like Stacy, can describe what shifted in their life as their own work rather than the coach's intervention.

This is also the only way the work scales. A client who can do the work without the coach can apply it everywhere, indefinitely. A client who needs the coach to mediate the work is dependent on access to the coach.
