# Speak to the Problem Without Solving It

## The Principle

When something does not feel right, name it immediately and stop. Do not solve it. Do not propose. Do not assign. Just speak to the problem and let the team take it from there.

The leadership move is two steps where most leaders do four. The standard four-step move: notice the problem, formulate the solution, assign the action, manage the execution. The principle collapses this to two: notice the problem, name it. The team does the rest.

"As quickly as you can, 'Oh, that doesn't feel right.' You don't have to do anything else. You think you do, you think you have to solve a problem, but you don't. And then somebody eventually is gonna say, 'Yeah, it doesn't feel right to me either.' Or they're gonna say, 'Oh, what doesn't feel right about it?' And a discussion is gonna ensue." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

## What This Solves

Most leaders carry an unspoken belief that their value is in the solutions they provide. The belief is partly accurate (leaders do need to produce results) and mostly counterproductive (the solutions the leader produces are usually worse than the solutions the team would produce if given the chance). The leader who solves every problem they name has trained the team to wait for solutions. The team's intelligence is suppressed in proportion to how reliably the leader supplies the answer.

"I can't tell you how many leaders I have worked with where we have dug in and really found out that one of the things that drives them is that they need to be valuable. Even the Taoists 2,000 years ago talked about leaders who nobody knew was there and how they were the best leaders. And basically what they're saying is that your ego's not in the game, not the ego of it has to be my way, not the ego of, oh, I'm necessary." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The principle is the structural alternative to the I-must-be-valuable engine. The leader who can name a problem without solving it has stopped trading on their solutions for their identity. The work-around for the missing solution is the team's intelligence, which was always there but was crowded out by the leader's reflex to solve.

## The Mechanism

When the leader names a problem and stops, three things happen that cannot happen when the leader names a problem and solves it:

**The team registers that the problem is real.** A problem that has been named and left open is more visible than a problem that has been named and immediately closed. The naming-without-closing creates an open question in the room that the team can engage with. The naming-with-closing creates a directive the team can only execute.

**The team's solutions become possible.** The leader's solution is one of many possible solutions. Once it is on the table, it tends to crowd out the others. The team's tacit knowledge — their direct contact with the work, their understanding of constraints the leader does not see — never gets translated into a proposal because there is already a proposal. The principle keeps the room open long enough for the team's solutions to surface.

**The solutions the team produces get the team's energy.** People execute on their own solutions with more conviction than on solutions handed to them. The same solution proposed by the leader and proposed by the team is implemented differently because the energy behind it is different. The naming-without-solving move recruits the energy.

"It's the solutions that people come up with that they're most likely to be aligned around, that they're most likely to put energy into, that they're gonna feel inspired to execute on." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

## What Happens If the Team Does Not Pick It Up

The standard objection: "But what if no one solves it? What if the problem just sits there?"

Two responses. First, the case is rarer than leaders imagine. The team that is not bringing solutions is usually a team that has been trained to wait for them. Give them a few cycles of you-naming-not-solving and the pattern usually breaks. The team starts to engage because the room is now structured for engagement.

Second, when the team does not pick up the problem, the right move is not to solve it. The right move is to ask "what doesn't feel right about it to you?" or to make the criteria of a good solution visible without specifying the solution itself. The principle is preserved by adjusting the question, not by abandoning the principle.

## The Criteria Move (When You Need to Steer Without Solving)

Sometimes the leader does have standards the solution has to meet. The principle does not require the leader to abandon those standards. It requires the leader to express them as criteria, not as the solution itself.

"Maybe you need to say, 'Oh, this is the criteria of the solution.' This is how we know we have a good solution. So you make sure that your standards are kept, but you don't actually ever have to solve the problem." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

This is the same structural move as [[give-parameters-of-winning]] applied to problems rather than tasks. The leader names what would count as a good solution. The team designs the solution. The standards are held; the method is delegated.

## The Parenting Form

The principle is identical with kids. The leader-of-the-family version of the move:

"With my children, for instance, whenever we have a problem like, I notice that I'm nagging, I'm like, 'Ah, that doesn't feel right.' And so I'll say to my kids, 'Hey, I'm nagging and that feels like crap. I don't wanna nag.' And they'll be like, 'Yeah, I don't want you to nag either.' I'm like, 'Okay, cool. How do we make sure that the house gets cleaned without me nagging?' And then they come up with a solution that works for them, that's easy for me 'cause they know I don't wanna nag, they know it doesn't feel good. We can all acknowledge the thing that doesn't feel good, and then I'm empowering my children to make the world that they want to make." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The structure is the same: name the thing that does not feel right (nagging), name the parent's own conflict (I do not want to nag), pose the question (how do we make sure X happens without the thing that does not feel good), let the kids design the solution. See [[bring-the-problem-to-your-teen]] for the dedicated parenting move.

The parenting form is structurally informative for the leadership form. If the move works with teenagers — among the populations most reflexively resistant to imposed solutions — it works with adults at work. The leader who hesitates with employees has a higher bar than the parent who is already doing it with children.

"If I can do that with teenagers, surely you can do it with people who you've employed or people that you work with." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

## The Ego Diagnostic

The reason most leaders cannot do the move is identifiable. The need to be valuable, the need to be necessary, the need to be the one with the answer — these are the engines that produce solving instead of naming.

The Taoist reference is not decoration. The leader whose ego is not in the game is the leader who can name and stop. The leader whose ego is in the game cannot stop, because stopping would be admitting that they do not have the solution, which they read as admitting they are not valuable. The principle, fully applied, requires the dissolution of the ego that produces the solving.

See [[become-unnecessary]] for the deeper structural form of the same teaching. The leader who has internalized that their job is to be unnecessary can name and stop with ease. The leader who has not internalized this will keep solving even when they consciously try to stop, because the engine is below the level of intention.

## What This Is Not

This is not abdication. The leader is still naming the problem. The naming is itself a leadership act, and arguably a more difficult one than solving, because it requires sustained presence with the problem without the relief of closing it.

This is not pretending not to have ideas. If the leader has a solution and the team has none and the situation requires speed, propose the solution. The principle is about the default move, not about every situation.

This is not delegation-by-vagueness. The named problem is specific. The leader says "this doesn't feel right" about a concrete thing, not "I have a vague feeling something is off." The specificity is part of what makes the move trustworthy.

This is not coercive consensus. The team's solutions are not subjected to the leader's veto. If the leader needs final say, the move requires the leader to make that explicit upfront ("I want us to come up with a solution; final call is mine") so the team is not deceived about the structure.

## The Inverse Cost

The cost of not doing this move is harder to see than the cost of doing it. The leader who keeps solving everything thinks they are being valuable. What they are actually doing:

- Communicating to the team that the team cannot solve the problem.
- Communicating to the team that they are not capable.
- Communicating to the team that the leader is necessary.
- Suppressing the team's intelligence about the work.
- Producing solutions that are usually worse than the team's solutions would have been.
- Ensuring that the leader must remain involved in every problem forever.

"If you move to solve the problem, then what you're doing is you're telling everybody that they can't solve the problem, that they're not capable, that you're necessary." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The cost compounds. Each problem the leader solves trains the team a little more that solving is not their job. After enough cycles, the team is structurally unable to solve problems on its own. The leader looks around at a team that cannot solve problems and concludes they need to keep solving. The system locks.

## When to Reach for This

- A leader who reports being overwhelmed by the number of problems they are personally solving.
- A leader whose team brings every problem to them rather than handling problems within the team.
- A leader who realizes their team's intelligence is being suppressed.
- A leader trying to scale out of the bottleneck position.
- A leader who has been doing [[state-the-want-not-the-action]] and is ready for the problem-side counterpart.
- A leader who has read [[become-unnecessary]] and is looking for a concrete behavioral expression of the principle.
- A parent in a recurring conflict where they keep imposing solutions and the solutions do not stick.

## When Not to Reach for This

- True emergencies where speed of execution overrides everything else. Solve and act.
- Problems where the leader has unique information the team does not have access to. The leader's solution may be the only viable one.
- Teams that have not been given enough structure to absorb the openness. See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for what needs to be in place before the move scales.
- Situations where the move is being used to avoid responsibility for an actual leadership call. The principle is for problems that the team can solve. It is not a permission slip to outsource decisions that belong to the leader.

## The Long-Term Effect

When this move becomes a leader's default, two cumulative effects emerge:

- The team develops the capacity to solve problems the leader could not have solved. The collective intelligence of the team, when given room, exceeds the leader's individual intelligence on most operational questions. The leader stops being the ceiling.
- The leader stops needing to be involved in every problem. The bottleneck dissolves. The leader's time is freed for the questions only the leader can address (strategy, culture, the few decisions that genuinely require the leader's authority). See [[become-unnecessary]].

## Tone

Plain and matter-of-fact. The naming is delivered without drama. "That doesn't feel right" is exactly the energy. Not "we have a serious problem." Not "I have grave concerns." Just the specific observation, plainly stated, with the implicit invitation to the team to engage with it.

The leader's calm in the naming is part of the move. A leader who names a problem with high anxiety pushes the team toward the leader's preferred solution by force of the felt-state. A leader who names a problem with low anxiety leaves the room actually open. See [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]] for the underlying mechanism.
