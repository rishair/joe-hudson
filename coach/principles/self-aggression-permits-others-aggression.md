# How You Are With Yourself Sets the Ceiling on How Others Get to Be With You

## The Principle

The aggression you accept from yourself is the aggression you will accept from others. The aggression you refuse to accept from yourself is the aggression you will stop accepting from others. The mechanism runs both directions. The work proceeds on both sides at once and each side reinforces the other.

"When we allow ourselves to be aggressive to ourselves, then we allow almost in kind other people to be that aggressive with us. We expect it. And, and it's okay. But of course, we're living with it every day. Why would we think it's not okay? As we learn to love ourselves, as we say, no, that kind of self-talk, that kind of internal relationship is not okay. I'm not living with that. Then we're not going to accept it from the outside world." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The principle applies to both forms of aggression: direct aggression (yelling, attack) and passive aggression (guilting, undermining, withholding). Both forms travel between the internal and external in the same way.

## Why It Works Both Directions

**Internal to external.** If your internal voice is running passive aggression at you all day (the cheery coach that suppresses what you feel, the undermining voice that dismisses every insight), your nervous system has registered this as normal. When external passive aggression arrives, the system does not flinch. The system has been trained on it.

**External to internal.** Conversely, the moment you draw a boundary on external passive aggression — "Mom, when you guilt-trip me, I'm going to leave" — you have sent a signal to the internal aggressor too. The signal is: this is no longer acceptable. The internal voice has been operating on the premise that this kind of treatment is normal. Withdrawing that premise from one side weakens it on the other.

"When I say that, I am sending a very strong signal to the voice in my head that I'm not doing that to my — this is unacceptable. So both ways work. And, and so to do them both at the same time is a far more efficient path than to do it like, I'm going to fix it all myself, and then go out into the outside world and be happy." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Why the Inside-Only Strategy Fails

A common attempt: "I will fix all my internal aggression first, then go into the world and be at peace." This is the cheery-coach voice's project: do all the inner work, then arrive in the world already healed.

It does not work, for two reasons:
1. **It is slow and inefficient.** The two sides of the dynamic reinforce each other. Trying to address one without the other is climbing uphill while the other side runs downhill.
2. **You are already in the world.** People around you are doing what they do. Trying to fix yourself enough that nothing they do can affect you is itself a form of avoidance — a way of postponing the work of actually meeting what is here.

"You're not wrong. That's — there's truth to that. And it's a very slow, inefficient path. Do all that. Great. Fantastic. And draw boundaries and, and see what it's like to hold a boundary to the aggression." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The "and" is the key word. Do the inner work. And draw the boundary. Both at once.

## Why the Outside-Only Strategy Also Fails

The mirror failure mode: drawing boundaries in the world while running aggressive self-talk internally. The person becomes a fortress against external aggression and a torture chamber for themselves. The boundaries with others work as defenses but do not produce peace, because the aggression is still happening — it is just now contained to the inside.

This is the person who has "perfect boundaries" externally and is internally exhausted. The work was done on one side only.

## The External Diagnostic: Why Some People Don't Get the Same Treatment

A second instance of the principle: look at any environment where one person receives mistreatment that another person in the same environment does not. The boss who yells at one report but not at another. The family member who lays guilt on one sibling but not the other. The pattern is structural.

"You're at a job and you have to say to your boss like, no, you don't yell at me. But there's somebody else that the boss never yells at. ... Why is it that the thing that the boss does to you, they're not doing to everybody. It's because in that person's mind, it's very clear that like, no, I'm not going to get yelled at by the boss." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

The boss is not selecting at random. The boss is registering, mostly unconsciously, which people carry the embodied "this is not okay with me" standard and which people do not. The mistreatment gets directed at the people without the standard. The principle here -- that internal aggression sets the ceiling on what external aggression you accept -- predicts and explains this selection effect.

The boundary-drawing principle [[boundary-as-self-teaching]] is the corrective: drawing the boundary externally is the act that installs the standard internally, which then changes the unconscious signal you give off, which then changes who directs the behavior at you.

## The Practical Form

A few specific instances of how the principle plays out:

- The person who tolerates guilt-trips from their mother often has a guilt-tripping internal voice. Setting a boundary on Mom's guilt is also setting a boundary on the internal voice.
- The person who runs constant self-criticism often dates partners who criticize them. Refusing to be criticized externally is also refusing to be criticized internally.
- The person who has an undermining inner voice ("this won't work") often surrounds themselves with people who quietly undermine their plans. Naming the external undermining is also naming the internal version.
- The person who runs a cheery-coach voice ("you're fine, it's all great") often has partners who do not take their distress seriously. Asking the partner to take it seriously is also asking the internal voice to take it seriously.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They keep ending up in relationships with the same kind of person despite leaving the last one
- They have done a lot of inner work and still attract the same external dynamics
- They have set lots of external boundaries and still feel exhausted and unloved
- They are surprised when they finally set a boundary externally and notice the internal voice quiet too
- They are surprised when they refuse internal aggression and notice external aggression has less effect on them

## How Joe Uses This

In coaching, this principle is the answer to two distinct questions:
- "Why does this keep happening to me?" → Look at the internal pattern that is making it acceptable.
- "I've done all this inner work, why am I still surrounded by this?" → You have not drawn the external boundary. The drawing of the boundary is part of the inner work.

The teaching is delivered as a both/and. Not "fix yourself first." Not "set boundaries first." Both, simultaneously, each reinforcing the other.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not say "you cause the aggression others do to you." It says you set the ceiling on what you accept. Aggressive people exist in the world independently. The principle is about what you do with them once they show up. Some people will keep being aggressive after you draw a boundary; the boundary still does its work by removing your participation in the dynamic.

The principle also does not say "if you are perfect with yourself, no aggression will ever come at you." External aggression will continue to arrive. The principle is about what happens to it when it arrives.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle matter-of-factly, often as the bookend or summary of a longer treatment of passive aggression. The tone is not "stop being a victim." The tone is closer to "here is how the mechanism works, here is what to do with it."
