# Love Surfaces the Unloved

## The Principle

When you start loving yourself, or feeling gratitude, or letting joy in, everything in you that has not been loved comes up to be loved too. This is not a side effect or a problem to solve. It is the mechanism. The unloved parts have been waiting. The arrival of love is the cue they have been waiting for. They surface because they want to be loved as well.

The common misreading: "I started loving myself and then grief came, so the love must not be working." The accurate reading: the love is working exactly as it works. The grief came *because* the love arrived. Grief is one of the unloved parts. Its showing up is the love already doing its job.

"When you start loving and feeling gratitude, all the things that you don't feel grateful for, that you don't love, come to the surface to get loved. It's like if you were feeding fish. All of a sudden, you feed one or two of them and all the fish show up at the pond surface to get fed. It's the same way. So it's a natural thing to happen that when love starts to show up, all the unloved parts of yourself come. And so oftentimes you're loving yourself and then anger or grief shows up and you think, 'What the heck happened?' Well, what happened is that part of you wants to be loved too." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

## The Feeding-Fish Metaphor

The image is precise. A still pond. A person drops food on the surface for one or two fish. Within seconds, all the fish in the pond come up. The fish were not invisible; they were below the surface. The food brought them up. Once they are at the surface, they can all be fed.

The person who started loving themselves is the person dropping the food. The fish at the surface are the parts that were already accessible. The fish underneath are the unloved parts. The arrival of food (love) brings them all to where they can be fed.

This is the operational reason that any sustained self-love practice ([[15-minutes-of-self-love-physical-therapy-for-the-heart]]) reliably produces tears, anger, grief, or other strong emotion. The practice is the food at the surface. The surfacing is the rest of the fish coming up.

The misunderstanding the metaphor corrects: people often interpret the surfacing as evidence that the love practice "doesn't work" or "made things worse." The metaphor makes visible that the surfacing IS the work. Stopping the practice because the unloved parts came up is throwing the food away as soon as the fish arrive to eat it.

## What This Implies for the Practice

The practice instruction follows directly: when love starts to flow and the unloved parts surface, love them too. Don't try to push the grief or anger back down. Don't conclude the love practice is failing. Don't switch to a different practice that doesn't surface this material. The work is to keep loving as the surfacing happens.

"If you start loving it, everything changes. Instead of saying, 'Oh, the grief is bad. I gotta get rid of the grief. Why is there so much grief?' None of that is loving the grief. But if you start to love the grief and you don't resist it, everything will change. And you'll start noticing that there's even love in grief." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The shift is from "the grief is the problem the love is supposed to solve" to "the grief is the next thing to love." Love does not eliminate grief. Love makes room for grief to be felt without resistance, which is what allows it to move.

The discovery that "there's even love in grief" is what arrives on the other side of the loving. The grief was never the absence of love. The grief was a form of love that had not been recognized. Loving the grief reveals what was always in it.

## The Generalization Across Emotions

The principle applies to any emotion that surfaces when love arrives:
- Love yourself, anger comes up. Love the anger.
- Love yourself, fear comes up. Love the fear.
- Love yourself, shame comes up. Love the shame.
- Love yourself, grief comes up. Love the grief.
- Feel gratitude, the parts of you that aren't grateful come up. Love them.
- Let in joy, the parts that don't think they deserve joy come up. Love them.

This is the practical engine behind [[loving-all-parts]]. The parts to be loved do not have to be hunted down. They will surface on their own when love is the field they are surfacing into.

## Why This Connects to the Joy Pattern

[[joy-as-matriarch]] describes a related but distinct mechanism: joy will not come into the house unless her children (anger, grief, fear) are welcome. The matriarch principle says joy requires the family to be welcome. This principle adds the other direction: when joy or love is welcomed first, it summons the family. They come because love is the field they need to surface into.

The two principles are complementary. Joy as matriarch describes why joy is missing when the family is exiled. Love-surfaces-the-unloved describes what happens when the love is turned on while the family is still exiled: they come to the door asking to be let in.

A person doing a self-love practice who finds themselves crying is the matriarch's children arriving at the door because the food finally appeared on the surface of the pond.

## The Failure Mode It Names

The most common reason self-love practices get abandoned: the practitioner does the practice, the unloved material surfaces, they interpret the surfacing as the practice failing or making things worse, and they stop. The cycle:

1. Person starts loving themselves.
2. Sadness or anger arises.
3. Person concludes: "This isn't working. I'm trying to feel good and I feel worse."
4. Person stops the practice.
5. The surfaced material gets pushed back down.

The principle interrupts the cycle at step 3. The accurate read of "I started loving myself and now I'm sad" is: "the love is working. The sadness was always there. It came to the surface because the love invited it." With that read, the person continues the practice through the surfacing rather than abandoning it.

This is why the PT-for-the-heart framing matters ([[15-minutes-of-self-love-physical-therapy-for-the-heart]]): the practice has to be daily and committed precisely because the surfacing is part of the work. A one-off attempt that gets abandoned at the first wave of grief never gets to the loving-the-grief phase, which is where the actual transformation happens.

## Deliver the Principle as Preparation for the Practice

When the principle is taught at the moment the person is about to begin loving themselves, the framing is forward-looking and slightly casual. The teaching is "here is what will happen, so you don't get confused when it does." The person needs the principle in advance so the inevitable surfacing does not get interpreted as failure.

The two-part version Joe delivered to an 18-year-old who had just done the first round of loving the worst-feeling body part:

"There's two concepts that are really helpful. The first is when you start to love yourself, all the unloved parts come up to be loved. And so you're like, wait, I'm loving myself and now this is here? Now, oh my God, I thought everything was great and now this thing has shown up. That's because it's coming up to be loved." — Joe Hudson, *This is How To Love Yourself*

The first concept is the principle itself. The second concept Joe pairs with it: there is no rush, the parts will be available to be loved for a long time, the practice can move at the most enjoyable pace.

The two together function as a kit for the person about to do the work alone. The first concept reframes any difficult emotion that arises in the practice as part of the work. The second concept gives the person permission to pace the work by their own enjoyment rather than by an imagined urgency. With both concepts in hand, the practice can be sustained — the surfacing is anticipated, and the pacing is internal.

The person in this session immediately recognized the first concept: "This is the fish example, right? Cuttlefish." (They had heard Joe's teaching of this principle on the podcast.) The recognition is one of the clearest examples of how the teaching, once internalized, becomes self-recognizing — the person had already understood the structural fact and could name it the moment Joe gestured at it. The recognition does not replace the experiential work, but it does shorten the time the person spends interpreting the surfacing as failure.

## The Coaching Implication

When a coachee reports that their attempt to love themselves "isn't working" because of what surfaced, the coaching move is to reframe the surfacing as the work. Not to comfort the discomfort. Not to switch to a different practice. To say: yes, that's the love working. The grief is what wanted to be loved next.

The reframe lands best when delivered without alarm or fixing energy. The surfacing is matter-of-fact. The fish came up. That's what fish do when food appears. Now love the fish.

## What This Is Not

- Not a license to dredge up emotion. The practice is to love. The surfacing happens on its own. Trying to produce the surfacing through effort is the opposite of love.
- Not a guarantee of catharsis. Some sessions of the practice produce no surfacing. The numbness is also data. Continue.
- Not a contradiction of [[joy-as-matriarch]]. That principle and this one operate at the same level and complement each other.
- Not a reason to skip the loving when it gets hard. The hard moments are the muscle being stretched. See [[15-minutes-of-self-love-physical-therapy-for-the-heart]] for the daily container.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They start a self-love practice and within minutes are crying for no clear reason.
- They begin a gratitude practice and feel a wave of ingratitude or resentment.
- They try to feel joy and immediately feel scared or sad.
- They open their heart to a loved one and old grief arises.
- They have done significant emotional work and notice that new unloved material keeps appearing as old material gets loved.
- They feel like loving themselves "makes things worse" and want to abandon the practice.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and warm. The principle is delivered as a structural fact, not as a moral instruction. The fish come up because food arrived. That's not good news or bad news. It's just news. The work is to keep feeding.
