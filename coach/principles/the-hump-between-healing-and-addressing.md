# The Hump Between Healing and Addressing (The Gym-Analogy Moment)

## The Principle

Between the impulse to address something hard and the actual act of addressing it, there is a structural moment of discomfort. The discomfort is not the work; the discomfort is the threshold between intention and action. Most attempts to address hard things die at this threshold, because the threshold feels exactly like the reason not to.

Joe gives this its operational name as he is explaining why so many elephants stay in rooms:

"There's a hump to climb over when you're dealing with something that you're ashamed of. There's a moment between healing and addressing it that feels like shit. And so it's a little bit like working out. There's a moment between the moment before you go to the gym to the end of the workout where you, where there's some struggle. And so doing that, jumping over that hump is something that's prevents people from doing it often." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The gym analogy is exact. The decision to go is easy. The being-at-the-gym-mid-workout is easy enough once you are there. The moment between the decision and the arrival — the putting on the shoes, the leaving the house, the driving over, the walking in — is where most people fail. The discomfort of crossing the threshold is structurally located between two states that, by themselves, are fine.

## What the Hump Feels Like

The hump has a specific somatic signature:

- A tightness in the chest as the words form
- A delay of seconds or minutes between "I am going to bring this up" and the actual words
- A sense that the timing is wrong — that a "better moment" is needed
- An impulse to drift into other topics to postpone
- A hyperawareness of the other person's mood as evidence that now is not the time
- A bargaining: "I'll bring it up tomorrow," "I'll bring it up after dinner," "I'll bring it up when they are in a better mood"

These signals are not data about the situation. They are the felt sense of the hump itself. The body interprets the threshold-crossing as a threat and starts producing reasons to retreat. The reasons feel like wisdom; they are actually the body protecting itself from the brief discomfort of crossing.

## Why the Hump Wins So Often

The structural advantage the hump has: nothing bad happens when you postpone. The discomfort goes away. The relief is immediate. The cost of not crossing the hump is invisible — it accrues over weeks, months, years, as a gradual deadening of the relationship or accumulation of unspoken material.

The cost of crossing the hump is sharp and immediate. The discomfort is brief but acute. The body remembers the discomfort. Next time, the hump feels even higher because last time it was uncomfortable.

This is why the standard advice "wait until you're ready" almost always fails. "Ready" is the state after the hump, which can only be reached by crossing the hump. Waiting for the readiness that arrives only after the crossing is waiting for something that cannot arrive without the action it is supposed to make possible.

## The Better-Time Trap

A specific failure mode: the person decides to bring up the elephant, gets to the moment, feels the discomfort, postpones to a "better time," and the better time never comes.

The better time does not arrive because the better time was never the issue. The issue is the hump itself. Every time the hump is met, the same postponement logic activates. Tomorrow always offers a more convenient version of the same evasion. The person can spend years preparing for the conversation they never have.

The diagnostic: if you have postponed the conversation more than twice citing "the right moment," you are in the better-time trap. The moment is never the obstacle. The hump is.

## What Makes the Hump Crossable

Three things shift the relationship to the hump:

**1. Recognizing it as the hump, not as wisdom.** When the body produces "now is not the time" with all its convincing somatic urgency, naming it — "this is the hump" — does not make it disappear, but it changes the relationship to it. You can be in the discomfort and aware that the discomfort is the threshold, not a real signal that the action is wrong.

**2. The gym analogy applied to the body.** Knowing that the discomfort is brief, that the relief is on the other side, that the hump shrinks with practice — these are true facts about the structure. The body that has crossed a few times learns that the crossing is survivable. The body that has never crossed believes the discomfort means catastrophe.

**3. Crossing the hump from open heart rather than from fighting it.** See [[speak-the-elephant-from-open-heart-not-fear]]. The crossing is easier when you are not also fighting the discomfort. The fight makes the hump higher. The acceptance of the discomfort — yes, this feels like shit, and I am crossing anyway — makes the hump cross-able.

## Why the Hump Is Higher With False Shame

The hump is taller when [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame|false shame]] is involved. False shame produces the conviction "I am uniquely defective for needing to address this," which makes the threshold not just uncomfortable but humiliating. The body anticipates not just discomfort but exposure.

The universalization test (see [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]]) is one tool for shrinking the hump in advance. Recognizing that thousands of others are crossing the same hump right now, with the same chest-tightness, dissolves some of the unique-to-me distortion. The hump is still there. But it is the ordinary human hump, not the uniquely-defective hump.

## The Hump in Other Domains

The principle is generalizable beyond elephant-naming:

- **The hump before crying** — the moment between the welling and the actual release. Most people stop here.
- **The hump before asking for help** — the chest-tightness before the request comes out.
- **The hump before apologizing** — the gap between deciding to apologize and the actual words.
- **The hump before the difficult creative act** — the discomfort between sitting down to write and actually writing.
- **The hump before reaching out to someone you have been avoiding** — the resistance between the impulse and the message.
- **The hump before feeling a feeling that has been pushed down** — the brief unpleasantness before the feeling actually arrives.

In each case, the structural pattern is the same: a brief discomfort between intention and action that produces the postponement that prevents the action.

The naming of the principle helps in each domain. "This is the hump" is portable. The crossing is the same move regardless of what is on the other side.

## What the Hump Is Not

- **Not a signal that the action is wrong.** The hump's felt urgency feels exactly like a signal that you should not do this. It is not. It is the threshold's signature.
- **Not your intuition.** Real intuition is usually quieter, more clear, less urgent. The hump is loud, somatic, and time-pressured.
- **Not a sign you need more preparation.** The "I need to figure out how to say this" loop is usually a sophisticated version of postponement. More preparation does not shrink the hump; crossing it does.
- **Not a personal flaw.** The hump is structural. Everyone has it. The person who crosses regularly has not eliminated the hump; they have learned that the hump is what crossing feels like.

## What Crossing Repeatedly Does

People who cross the hump regularly report that the hump shrinks. Not because the underlying material gets smaller, but because the relationship to the hump changes. The body learns:

- The discomfort is brief
- The relief is large
- The action on the other side is usually fine
- The catastrophes the hump predicts mostly do not happen
- The accumulated cost of not crossing is much larger than the immediate cost of crossing

Over time, the hump becomes a recognized phase rather than a barrier. The leader who runs [[whats-the-scary-thing-youre-not-saying]] in team meetings is, structurally, having their team practice crossing the hump weekly. The hump shrinks across the whole team.

This is also why Joe describes leaders who have built this culture as "looking forward to" elephants — the hump has shrunk so far that the crossing is no longer the salient event. The freedom on the other side is what is salient.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You decide to bring up something hard, get to the moment, feel a tightness in your chest, and tell yourself "later"
- You notice that you have been preparing for a conversation for weeks and have not had it
- You watch yourself talk yourself out of every available moment to bring up a topic
- You bring it up, feel the discomfort, and a few minutes later realize the conversation is actually going fine
- You realize the "right time" you have been waiting for is not arriving

## Anti-Patterns

- **Treating the hump as a real signal to abort.** The structural urgency of the hump feels exactly like reasonable doubt. It is not. The doubt is the threshold's signature, not data about the action.
- **Waiting for the "right moment."** The right moment is the one after you cross the hump. There is no other right moment.
- **Engineering elaborate preparation to avoid feeling the hump.** Preparation past a certain point is sophisticated postponement.
- **Trying to eliminate the hump rather than cross it.** The goal is not to no longer feel the discomfort. The goal is to cross while feeling it.

## Tone

The teaching is delivered with a kind of gentle clarity. The gym analogy is meant to make the principle feel ordinary — the hump is not a special spiritual obstacle, it is the same kind of structural resistance that keeps people from exercising. The ordinariness is part of how the teaching lands. The hump is not big. It just keeps winning because we treat it like data.
