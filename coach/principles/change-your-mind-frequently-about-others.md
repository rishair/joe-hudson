# People Change Their Minds About Each Other All the Time

## The Principle

When fear constructs the false end of a feared social consequence ("if I ask for the promotion my boss will think I'm presumptuous"), the principle that dissolves the false end is this: people change their minds about each other constantly. The boss who thought you were presumptuous on Tuesday has had eight other thoughts about you by Friday. The colleague who took a moment of offense at the meeting has had the moment supplanted by twenty other moments. The friend who was annoyed yesterday is not annoyed today. Social perception is not static. The fearful mind treats the worst predicted moment as if it were the permanent state of the relationship; the actual structure of social perception is constantly moving.

The principle is delivered as a question the listener already knows the answer to:

"How often do you change your mind about somebody? How often do you think, oh, that person's needy, and then you find out that they're not?" -- Joe Hudson, The More Stuck You Feel, The Closer You Are To Freedom

The question's force comes from its self-evidence. Every listener has had the experience of revising their view of someone many times. The boss they thought was scary turned out to be kind. The friend they thought was distant turned out to be shy. The colleague they thought was needy turned out to be going through something. The fluidity of social perception is part of every adult's lived experience -- it just gets dropped from the calculation when fear is constructing the worst-case consequence.

## What This Principle Does in the Stuckness Work

Within the [[fear-of-consequences-creates-stuck]] mechanism, the false end is the second of three cognitive distortions that fear produces. The mind freezes time at the moment of feared damage and treats that frozen moment as the permanent truth of the situation. The change-your-mind principle is the specific intervention for that distortion. It does not address the binary-thinking distortion or the safety-vs-risk inversion; it addresses the false end.

The intervention works by surfacing what the listener already knows. They have changed their mind about people many times. They have been wrong about people many times. They have updated their impressions many times. They have forgotten about minor offenses many times. They have re-evaluated based on new evidence many times. All of this happens to them. All of this happens through them, to other people. The system of social perception is in constant motion. The fear-construction was treating it as frozen because the frozen version is the version that supports the not-acting position.

Once the principle lands, the false end loses its grip. The person can still see that asking for the promotion might land badly on Tuesday. They can also see that Tuesday's bad landing is one moment in a relationship that has many other moments coming. The bad landing is not the verdict on the relationship; it is one data point in a long stream of data points the boss is integrating about them. The "permanent damage" frame was wrong. The actual structure is closer to "one bad day in a long working relationship," which is much more recoverable than the frozen-moment frame had been treating it.

## The Companion Observation

Joe pairs the change-your-mind question with another observation that does similar work:

"How often do you think, oh, that person's needy, and then you find out that they're not?" -- Joe Hudson, The More Stuck You Feel

The example is specific because it inverts the direction the fearful mind usually runs. The fearful mind is afraid of being perceived as needy / greedy / presumptuous. The companion observation reminds the listener that their own perceptions of others as needy / greedy / presumptuous have been wrong many times. The implicit conclusion: if my own snap judgments of others have been wrong this often, the other person's snap judgment of me is probably also wrong this often, and is probably also being revised over time.

This is the relational symmetry that the fear-frame strips out. The fear-frame treats the other person as a fixed judge holding a permanent verdict. The actual structure is that the other person is a fluid perceiver running the same updating-and-revising process the listener runs. Both directions of the social perception are in motion. Both are being revised. Neither is the static thing the fear was treating it as.

## The Broader Pattern This Sits Inside

The change-your-mind principle is a specific case of a broader pattern in Joe's work: the fear-construction strips away exactly the data that would dissolve the fear if it were included in the calculation. The fear-construction works by exclusion -- it shows the worst moment and hides the longer arc, shows the worst case and hides the typical case, shows the binary and hides the option-space.

The principle is the restoration of the excluded data. The listener already knows people change their minds. The listener already knows the boss's Tuesday verdict is not the boss's Friday verdict. The listener already knows their own perceptions have been wrong. None of this is new information. What the principle does is restore the information to the calculation that the fear-frame had been running without it.

See [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]] for the parallel empirical principle (the consequences the fear predicts usually do not arrive at all). The change-your-mind principle is the structural complement: even when the feared consequence does arrive, it is not the permanent thing the fear was treating it as.

## When This Principle Lands

The principle lands when:

- The listener has articulated a specific feared social consequence ("my boss will think I'm X")
- The listener has frozen time at the feared moment and is treating it as the permanent state
- The listener can be asked the question and has access to their own experience of having changed their mind about others
- The cognitive entry point is the right entry for this listener (some people need the felt work first)
- The feared consequence is, in fact, a social-perception consequence (the principle does not address material consequences like losing money or losing access)

The principle does not land when:

- The feared consequence is not social-perception but material (losing a job in a way that produces real financial hardship; ending a relationship in a way that has real children-and-housing implications)
- The listener is in the felt work and the cognitive intervention would interrupt the body's process
- The listener has the social-perception correctly calibrated and is not actually treating the verdict as permanent
- The listener cannot access their own experience of changing their mind (rare, but possible in dissociated or chronically-traumatized states)

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not the claim that other people's opinions don't matter. The principle is about the false-end structure of the fear-construction, not about whether opinions matter. Opinions matter; they also move. Both are true.

It is not the claim that there are no lasting consequences for bad behavior. The principle is about the typical case of feared social consequences from ordinary actions (asking for a promotion, having a difficult conversation, being more visible than usual). It is not a license for sustained behavior that would, in fact, produce lasting damage to a reputation.

It is not the claim that the listener should not care what people think. The principle is more modest: in the specific case of the false-end fear-construction, the long arc of social perception is fluid enough that the worst-moment frame is not accurate. The listener can care what people think and also recognize that what people think is not frozen at the moment of the feared consequence.

## Tone

Joe delivers the principle as a casual question to a friend, not as a teaching. The cadence is "you know this already; let me just remind you." The casualness is part of the move; it produces the recognition without the listener feeling lectured. The friend-cadence also models the relational ease the principle is pointing to: of course people change their minds; of course opinions move; this is how relationships work; let's not treat this moment as the permanent state.

The companion observation ("how often do you think, oh, that person's needy, and then you find out that they're not?") is delivered with the same casualness. It is offered as the obvious thing the listener has experienced many times, which is exactly what makes it land.
