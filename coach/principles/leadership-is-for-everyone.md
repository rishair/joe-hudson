# Leadership Is For Everyone

## The Principle

Everyone is already leading. The question is not whether you lead. The question is whether you have admitted to yourself that you are leading. The conscious recognition of that fact is itself the significant move.

"Everybody is leading. Everybody has an influence on their environment, and therefore they're leading. Now the question is, do they want to admit to it or not admit to it? Do they want to feel that responsibility or not feel that responsibility?" -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The principle separates two things that the culture has conflated: the title of leader (an assignment, a position, an authority that has been handed out) and the quality of leadership (a way of being that operates in every life regardless of title). When people say they are "not a leader," they usually mean the first. The second is operating in them whether they see it or not.

"It means literally that everybody is leading. A lot of the thing about leadership is that people don't admit it to themselves. So when we're talking about leadership here, we're talking about the quality that is in all of us. We are not talking about you've been given a title or you have some sort of control or apparent control over other people." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## The Way Of Being, Not The Assignment Of Authority

Two different referents share the word "leadership." Disambiguating them is the entry point.

- **Leadership as assigned authority.** The person is the CEO, the parent, the manager, the team captain. They have been given a role with formal power over others.
- **Leadership as a quality of being.** The person is influencing their environment by how they show up. They are setting an example. They are giving permission. They are establishing what is okay and what is not.

The first kind belongs to some. The second kind belongs to everyone.

The CEO who has the title but no quality of leadership is leading less than the housewife who has no title but is shaping every interaction in her household. The principle is not flattening the distinction between roles — it is locating leadership in the quality, not the role.

"So you're talking about the way of being that leadership is. Not the assignment of leadership. The assignment of authority." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## The Everyday Instances

The principle becomes concrete through specifics. Each of these is a moment of leadership:

- You smile at someone — they are more likely to smile back.
- You work out at the gym — your effort gives permission to others to work harder.
- You complain to your husband — you are leading the relationship in that direction.
- You wake up and say "I don't want to go to work today" — you are leading your own life in that posture.
- You don't say the thing in the meeting — you are leading by establishing what is acceptable to leave unsaid.
- You say the thing in the meeting — you are leading by establishing what gets named.
- You listen to a podcast about your own development — you are giving the example of change in the world.

"All this really is just to actually allow that shit in for a minute, to really go, oh, I am in leadership. When I complain to my husband, that's leadership. When I wake up and go, I don't wanna go to work today, that's leadership. When I don't say the thing in the meeting, that's leadership. When I say the thing in the meeting, that's leadership. To actually just recognize everything you are doing." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The specificity matters because it disarms the move of saying "well, I'm not a leader in a real way." The principle does not say "you have a leadership quality somewhere in you." It says "every action you are taking right now is leadership." The recognition is not aspirational. It is descriptive.

## It Is Natural, Not Imposed

The principle is not a moral demand. It is a description of how humans are wired. Leadership emerges naturally in groups. Kids playing in a field exhibit it constantly — leadership passes from one child to another situationally. Even three-year-olds show it.

"You'll just see that that leadership passes. It's like a natural quality in us to lead. And so the question isn't like, what gets us to lead? It's what kicks us out of it." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The reframe is important. The teaching is not "how do I learn to lead." The teaching is "how do I stop interfering with the leadership that is already operating." See [[leadership-emerges-from-confident-direction]] for the baboon study that demonstrates the same emergent leadership in animals.

## What Kicks Us Out Of Leadership

Since leadership is the natural baseline, the question is what stops it from operating consciously. Three main blockers:

1. **Fear of blame.** If I am leading, I am responsible. If I am responsible, I can be blamed. Better to disown the leading and avoid the exposure. See [[avoiding-leadership-still-leaves-you-as-the-leader]].
2. **Untrustworthy environment.** If the environment is unsafe — if abuse or harsh judgment has happened — the impulse to lead retreats. The retreat is protective and rational under the circumstances. But it persists past the circumstances.
3. **Traumatic conditioning.** If I have been told I am bad, the leading impulse is paired with the prediction of punishment. The leading does not feel safe to exhibit.

"If I can't trust the environment, I don't feel like I'm going to be able to lead. If I can't trust the people around me, I don't feel like I can lead. If I've been told I'm bad, I don't feel I can lead." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The implication: developmental work that increases trust in yourself and your environment is upstream of conscious leadership. As you heal from the traumas, leadership becomes naturally available again. The recognition that you have been leading the whole time becomes possible.

## The Move For The Person Listening Right Now

The principle has a single instruction: recognize it. That is the whole move.

"All they have to do is recognize it. Just recognize that when you smile at somebody, they're more likely to smile back. You're at the gym right now and how you're working out is giving permission to other people. Like that's all leadership." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The recognition is not a step toward leadership. The recognition IS the step. The person who recognizes that they are leading begins to lead more deliberately, more cleanly, with more attention to what they are setting up. Nothing in their external circumstances has changed. The role of leadership has not been conferred. But the leadership is now conscious, and conscious leadership behaves differently than unconscious leadership.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The surprise of seeing that the housewife is leading, the gym member is leading, the listener is leading
- The discomfort of realizing that "not stepping up" is itself a leadership move
- The relief of seeing that leadership is not exclusively the property of titles
- The unwanted recognition that the complaining and the avoidance are also leadership — and have been shaping the environment whether intended or not
- The opening of "if I am already leading, what do I want to be leading toward?"

## Tone

Joe delivers this with steady invitation rather than exhortation. The teaching is not "you should become a leader." The teaching is "you already are one — look." The tone is matter-of-fact, often with concrete everyday examples that make the recognition impossible to refuse without absurdity. The "really go, oh, I am in leadership" framing carries the texture of recognition rather than instruction.
