# Everybody Wants the Same Kind of Team (Own Your Wants Without Hesitation)

## The Principle

When you own and name what you want from the team, you can be assured everyone else wants the same kind of team. People may want different actions or strategies, but the underlying wants for the quality of the team itself are universal. Nobody actually wants a political team. Nobody actually wants a backstabbing team. Nobody actually wants a team where they cannot trust each other. The hesitation people feel about stating their wants comes from imagining their wants are idiosyncratic. They are not.

"If you own your wants and you start creating the vision of the way that you want the team to be, whether you are the head of the team or not head of the team, it doesn't matter. Everybody wants it." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

## The Universal Want Beneath the Question

The same observation applies to a related universal want. Everybody on every team wants to do great work. Nobody actively wants to do mediocre or shit work. The natural pull on a team is toward greatness; the leader's job is not to manufacture this pull, it is to avoid blocking it.

"Everybody on their team wants it. So what's going on there? What's happening that makes it that people want -- that there's this natural impulse, like as natural as a kid's impulse is to walk, is a team's impulse to do great work. I know everybody in this room right now, they want to do great work. Nobody's like, you know what would be great is if like I had a life where I just did mediocre shit work. For the rest of my life. Nobody's -- nobody says it. And yet somehow all this effort in -- into management to try to get the results when the, the natural pull is there for every team." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

This adds depth to the principle. The leader sometimes treats management as the task of "getting people to want to perform." The principle reframes this: the wanting is already there. The actual task is removing what blocks it. The whole construction of management as motivation-machine is built on the assumption that people don't naturally want greatness from their work. The assumption is false. The work that follows from the false assumption is wasted effort and (worse) blocks the natural pull it was designed to create.

## The Empirical Test

In every team Joe walks into, he runs a version of the same diagnostic. He asks each person what they want from a team.

"I'll do almost immediately in the team is I'll ask everybody what they want from a team. And they almost always agree on everything." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

The disagreements that remain are usually semantic. One person says "supportive," another says "has my back," another says "collaborative." Drill into what each means and they are pointing at the same thing.

"It's like one person will say, I want a team that supports each other. And I'm like, does anybody else want a team that supports each other? Everybody says yes. And so everybody really wants the same thing." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

The empirical regularity gives the principle its force. This is not a faith claim about humanity. It is a repeatable observation in actual rooms.

## What Nobody Actually Wants

The negative test makes the principle vivid. List the things people supposedly want and ask if anyone would name them as preferences:

"There's nobody's like, you know what? No, I want a fucking political team. I want a team where I don't feel safe and I have this backstabbing and there's no support. Like, nobody feels that way." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

No one says "I want a team I can't trust." No one says "I want a team where wants are hidden." No one says "I want a team that operates by politics rather than performance." The dysfunctional team is what people end up in by default, not what they wanted.

## The Defensive Variation Is Not the Same

There is a distinction the principle has to hold. People in dysfunctional teams sometimes act as if they want the dysfunction. The accurate frame is different.

"There's people who feel like that's the way all teams are and it's gonna be that way, so I need to protect myself and I need to win the game. But there's nobody who actually wants that team." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

The behavior of treating the team as adversarial is a defensive response to the experience of teams as adversarial. It is not the underlying want. The underlying want is for the team to be one in which the defense would not be necessary. The defense is what gets enacted when the underlying want is treated as unreachable.

This matters for the coaching path. The team member who has hardened into politics is not someone with different wants. They are someone whose wants are the same as everyone else's but who has stopped believing those wants are available. The opening is to make the wants speakable again, not to defeat the politics they have learned to play.

## Different Actions, Same Kind of Team

The principle does not say everyone wants the same plan. People disagree about strategies, priorities, decisions, and tactics. The principle says the wants about the quality of the team itself are shared.

"They might not want the same actions as you, but they definitely want the same kind of team as you do if you're on the same team." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

This distinction is important. Disagreement about actions is healthy and necessary in a functional team. Conflation of action-disagreement with wants-for-the-team-quality is how people end up suppressing the second to avoid the first. "I cannot say I want a trusting team because we disagree about the budget" makes no sense once the categories are separated.

## The Specific Universal Wants

Joe enumerates what everyone wants in a team:

"Everybody wants to be a part of a winning team. Everybody wants a supportive team. Everybody wants to be in a team that they can trust. Everybody wants a team where they know that people will tell them their problems instead of telling other people about the problems they're having with them. Like, everybody wants that." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

The list is not exhaustive but the structure is clear. The wants are about the felt quality of being on the team: winning, support, trust, direct communication. Each of these would be unanimously endorsed in a values exercise. The pattern of dysfunctional teams is that none of these things are happening despite everyone wanting them.

## Why This Frees the Want-Stater

The principle solves a specific problem. Most people hesitate to name their wants for the team because they imagine their wants are personal, perhaps inappropriate, perhaps an imposition. The principle reframes this: stating the want is making explicit what is already present in everyone in the room.

The person who names "I want a team where we can trust each other" is not introducing a new idea. They are giving voice to what everyone is already wishing for. The naming permissions the room.

This applies even when you are not the leader. The principle is position-independent. The team member at any level who names a want for the team's quality is offering what everyone else is silently wanting too.

## The Cascade

Even when the person you are speaking to doesn't immediately share the same actions, your wants give them something to orient around.

"Even if they don't want the same thing as you, they just you owning your wants helps them more clearly define and see the path to getting their needs met." -- Brett Kistler, Building A Functional Team

The owned want is data even for the person who doesn't endorse it. It shows them how someone is thinking, what is alive in the room, what they might agree with or want differently. Naming makes the field navigable.

## How to Apply It in the Moment

When you notice you want something for the team — more trust, less politics, more directness, fewer interruptions — the principle says: name it. The framing is not "I want this for myself." The framing is "I want this for the team, and I trust everyone else wants something similar."

"Oh, I notice I wanna hear you more, Lisa. I wanna hear you more, Ben. I wanna hear what you have to think about it. Or, oh, I notice that I want more trust in this group and it seems like there's politics that get in the way." -- Joe Hudson, Building A Functional Team

The first-person ownership of the want ("I notice I want") combined with the team-level content ("more trust in this group") makes the statement both personal and shared. The room will recognize itself in the want.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is about the quality of the team. It is not about specific plans, strategies, or actions. Two people on the same team can have legitimately different views about what to do; the principle does not collapse this into agreement.

The principle also presumes the team is actually a team — people working toward something together with some baseline of shared purpose. A "team" that is actually a competition (a sales floor where everyone is competing for individual commissions) does not have the same universal wants because the structure is not actually a team. The principle is about teams; it is not a claim about every group of people in a room together.

The principle also is not "everyone wants the same things in life." It is specifically about the quality of team being on. Don't extend it to "everyone wants the same career," "everyone wants the same relationships," "everyone wants the same lifestyle." Those are different questions.

## Tone

Joe says this with quiet conviction. He has tested it many times and observed the regularity. The conviction is not aspirational; it is empirical. The tone communicates: this is not a hope, this is what I see when I run the experiment.
