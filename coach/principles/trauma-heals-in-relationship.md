# Trauma Heals in Relationship

## Definition

Trauma that was created in relationship is best healed in relationship. This takes two forms: one-on-one work (coach/therapist and client) and group work where a container of love can be created among participants.

This is stated as a strong tendency, not an absolute rule. Joe qualifies: "I wouldn't say one thing, I would just say it's very helpful in it." -- Joe Hudson, A Discussion with Dr. Bessel van der Kolk & Joe Hudson

## In Practice

"The trauma that's created in relationship is best healed in relationship. And there's two forms of that that can happen. There's the one-on-one of a doctor-client, and then there's also a group of people. And then in that group, there can be a deep feeling of love and acceptance. If that's created, that can do a lot of the healing." -- Joe Hudson, A Discussion with Dr. Bessel van der Kolk & Joe Hudson

Group settings have a specific advantage: they provide the experience of being known and loved despite one's shame. When a person sees that 11 other people still love them after seeing their darkest self-judgments, that experiential evidence is hard to argue with. See [[container-of-love]].

## The Partnership as Specific Healing Container

The most consistent application of this principle is the long-term romantic partnership. The partner is, structurally, the person who will surface the original wounds and who -- if both partners can stay loving through it -- can become the loving witness the original caregiver could not be. This is the mechanism by which fights in a relationship are not the relationship failing but the relationship doing the work it was set up to do. See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] and [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]].

The specific move in a partnership is described directly:

"Oftentimes the way that we heal in the fights is by actually going into our trauma with somebody loving us. And that's the opportunity in almost every major fight. In almost every major fight, one of the biggest opportunities that you can have is somebody there loving you in your trauma, someone there giving you what you couldn't get when you, when you first received that trauma. That's the opportunity. That's why so much fucking growth is possible." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The structural claim: the original wound was created in a relationship where the loving witness was missing. The healing is the same wound met by the loving witness that is finally present. The relationship that is willing to do this work becomes the container where what could not be healed in childhood gets healed in adulthood.

The operational form in couples is [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] -- naming "I'm in my trauma right now" so the partner can shift from opponent to loving witness. The capacity to make this shift, on both sides, is what makes the relationship a healing container rather than a retraumatizing one.

## Why This Makes Hard Periods Productive Rather Than Failing

A relationship in a hard period -- where more trauma is surfacing, where fights are bigger, where things feel like they are getting worse -- is often a relationship doing this work at a deeper level. The pattern is described in [[feared-stage-of-relationship-deepening-can-feel-like-things-getting-worse]]. The hard period is what the loving partnership offers up so the deeper material can be met.

The example in long-form is Joe and Tara's [[tara-fights-that-destroyed-joes-narcissism]] -- a six-month period of vicious fighting that retrospectively turned out to be the period that destroyed a subtle narcissism Joe had been carrying that no other practice had been able to touch.

The implication: a couple in a hard period that wants to interpret the difficulty as the relationship failing is often misreading what is happening. The relationship-as-healing-container is doing its work; the work just feels like collapse from inside the process.

## Boundary Conditions

Not all healing requires relationship. Joe acknowledges this is one path, not the only path. Body-based modalities (yoga, Rolfing) can produce significant healing without relational intimacy. Bessel van der Kolk's personal experience of Rolfing illustrates this: "Somebody needs to just open up my goddamn body and allow my chest to open up."

Additionally, the earlier in life healing happens, the faster it occurs. Neuroplasticity in childhood means the same wounds resolve more quickly with younger people.
