# Obligation Cuts Off the Felt Experience of Love

## The Principle

In any given moment, if a person is acting out of obligation, they have stopped feeling love. The two are not simultaneous. The feeling of obligation literally constricts the emotional experience of love. This is not a moral claim about obligation. It is a mechanical observation about the felt sense of the two states.

A person can still love someone over the long term while acting from obligation in a particular moment. But in the specific moment of the obligated act, the felt love is not there. The obligation has taken its place.

"If you have a sense of obligation, then a requirement of that obligation is to stop the emotional experience of love. Doesn't mean you stop loving the person, but it means that your emotional experience of loving someone has to end. The obligation literally constricts the emotional experience of love." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

"In this moment, I cannot feel obligation for you to do something for you and feel love for you at the same time. It doesn't mean that I can't care for you. It doesn't mean that I can't be generous with you or a whole bunch of things, but I can't do it out of obligation and maintain this feeling of love." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Mechanism

Obligation and shame/guilt are closely related. Guilt is what makes you feel obligated to do something. Shame is what makes you feel obligated to be a certain way. Shame and guilt are blocker emotions that stagnate emotional flow. See [[blocker-emotions]] and [[shame-stagnates-emotion]]. Obligation inherits this stagnating quality — it sits on top of the love and stops it from flowing.

The trade is structural. You traded shame-relief for love. You agreed to feel obligated so that you would not feel shame, and in doing so you bought the obligation at the price of the love.

## The Strategy Behind the Obligation

Obligation, paradoxically, is often a strategy to *get* love. The person was taught in childhood that they have to do something to be lovable. Acting out of obligation is the adult version of performing for love. The catch: it occasionally produces an external approval (a thank you, a good mood from the partner) but it never delivers the actual felt love, because:

1. The person knows on some level they had to perform for it, so what was loved was the performance, not them.
2. The obligation they were running shut off their own love-feeling, so they could not receive what was offered anyway.

"It might get us somebody liking us or somebody approving of us or somebody maybe even loving us... However, there is something like that. It's always kind of a watered-down version of it or a surrogate of it. Because somewhere in the back of our mind, we know we had to perform for it, which means we're not getting loved for who we are. We're getting love for our performance." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

"So it never fully fulfills. It's never like a wholesome meal. At best, it's like a Pepsi." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## Long-Term Effect: The Love Erodes

A relationship organized around mutual obligation does not just lose love in individual moments. The accumulation of obligated moments slowly drains the love from the relationship over years. The eggshells get thicker. The performed kindness loses its taste. The partner senses what is and is not real and adjusts. The marriage flattens. The business partnership stales. See [[walking-on-eggshells]] and [[the-built-up-resentment-story]].

"If you look at the long haul of relationships, everybody's walking on eggshells, everybody feels obligated to do a certain thing all the time so that they can be loved and the marriage just the love erodes. So long-term, the love erodes. Short-term, the love isn't even available. It's just only in that medium frame of time that you might get lucky every once in a while." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Reframe That Is Not Being Made

This principle is not "don't act out of obligation." Joe explicitly does not make that prescription:

"I'm imagining somebody listening right now and they're thinking, oh my gosh, if I have to not feel obligation for somebody or I can't act out of obligation for — I'm not saying that. I'm like, feel free. Feel free to act out of obligation. Just notice that you have to cut off your love to do it. Just notice that you have to stop loving the person to do it." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

The work is not the elimination of obligation. The work is awareness of the trade being made. Once the person sees that obligation is paid for in love, the obligation often loses its appeal — not because they have decided to stop, but because the cost is now visible.

## The Self-Application

The same principle applies inward. When the inner critic tells you what to do and you comply out of obligation to the voice, you have just cut off self-love in the same moment. The four-layer should is the internal form of the external obligation. See [[should-as-threat]].

"You have to cut off love from yourself. You have to cut off self-love if you're acting out of obligation towards the self-abuse, the self-management." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Money/Work Application

The principle generalizes beyond people. Anything you relate to from obligation, you stop loving in the moment of the obligation. This applies to money, work, art, exercise, parenting, podcasting.

A person who feels obligated to make money cannot love money. Cannot love the work that makes it. Cannot love the company they built. The obligation constricts the love-feeling toward the thing, which is why the thing keeps slipping away. You do not pursue what you cannot love. See [[obligation-as-management]].

"If somebody feels like they have an obligation to make money, it doesn't allow you to love money... Since you can't love the money, then like, why do you want it in your life?" -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

The most successful entrepreneurs Joe observes do not relate to their business from obligation. They relate from "I get to, I want to." When things go sideways, they have the resilience to continue because they are in love with what they are doing. The obligated entrepreneur quits.

## The Coaching Demo

The principle was made viscerally clear in the coaching session that opened the episode. The client, in the middle of an unfinishable divorce, named patterns of obligation and responsibility. Joe asked him to find the love he once had for his wife, wait for it to be felt in the body, and then asked him to feel responsible for her happiness. The love disappeared. The client felt the trade in his own body, in real time.

"So you stopped loving her to take responsibility for her is what I'm hearing you say. Motherfucker." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation (to the divorce client)

"Every time you took responsibility, acted out of obligation it stopped the love that you felt for her." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

## The Distinction from Care, Generosity, and Action

The principle is not "don't take care of anyone." Care can be given without obligation. Generosity can flow without obligation. Action can be taken without obligation. The question is the felt state from which the action arises.

A person can take their partner to the airport because they want to, and feel love the whole time. The same person can take their partner to the airport because they feel obligated, and not feel love the whole time. The action is identical. The felt experience is opposite. The action only looks like love from the outside; from the inside, it is hollow.

The signature of clean care: the body relaxes during the action. The signature of obligation: the body braces.

## Why People Stay Confused

The reason this principle is unintuitive is that everyone has been told that love requires sacrifice, that good people put themselves last, that obligation IS love when it gets serious. The inherited definition fuses love with obligation. See [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]].

Brett named the inversion directly:

"When we're in the pattern of obligation, we see that the obligation is the only thing that's maintaining our connection. And we may even internally justify it as it's an intense form of love." -- Brett Kistler, Love and Obligation

Joe traces this to childhood: "We were taught as kids that we had to do something to be lovable... I think the other component of it is it's like this idea that we have that there's some sort of differentiation between doing what's best for me and doing what's best for you and that somehow or another I have to make a sacrifice to be a good friend or I have to make a sacrifice to be to do the right thing for you rather than finding the thing that says, oh, this is actually what I want. It's actually what's really deeply good for me. And it also corresponds to what's deeply good for you." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

The false binary between self-interest and other-care is one of the mental scaffolds that keeps the obligation-as-love confusion in place. See [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]] and [[what-is-truly-compassionate]].

## When Someone Experiences This

- The exhaustion of constantly doing things "for" people you supposedly love, with no felt warmth during the doing
- The hollow quality of a marriage where both partners are being good to each other and neither feels loved
- The post-favor resentment: you helped, you did the right thing, and now you feel a quiet anger you cannot name
- The moment of recognition that you stopped feeling love for your partner some time ago and you have been performing care instead
- The realization that every "I have to" you say to yourself drains you in a way that "I get to" does not
- The shock of feeling the trade happen in real time when you switch from felt love to felt obligation in a coaching moment

## Anti-Pattern Risk

Watch for the person turning this teaching into a new should: "I should never act from obligation." That is the should running again. The teaching is not a prohibition. It is an awareness. Acting from obligation is allowed. The awareness of what is being traded is the practice. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] for why prescribing the opposite of a should is still a should.

## Tone

When teaching the principle, matter-of-fact and clinical. The mechanism is described, not preached. When demonstrating the principle live (as with the divorce client), Joe is sharp, sometimes profane, willing to interrupt the client's performance to make them feel the trade in the body. The aliveness of "motherfucker" lands the recognition in a way that explanation cannot.
