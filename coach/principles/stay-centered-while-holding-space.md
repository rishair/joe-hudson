# Hold Space While Staying Centered in Yourself

## Definition

Holding space for another person and staying centered in your own knowing are not opposed. They are simultaneous requirements. The presence that helps another person is the presence offered from your own ground, not the presence of someone who has dissolved into theirs.

"You can hold space for people but you have to stay in yourself and you have to stay centered in your knowing, your truth, your wants." -- Jaime Waydo recounting Joe Hudson, Taking Care of Yourself First

The common misreading: to be with someone, I have to feel what they feel, agree with what they say, want what they want. The presence becomes a dissolving. The other person experiences not presence but a mirror, which is not actually a connection. It is merger.

The accurate reading: to be with someone, I have to be sufficiently here that there is a someone to be with them. The other person experiences another whole human attending to them. That is what they came for.

## What Goes Wrong Without It

When the person holding space loses their own center, several failures follow:

- The other person no longer has someone to push against. They are talking to a mirror. The mirror cannot challenge, cannot offer a different read, cannot say "I see this differently." The conversation produces no new information.

- The holder absorbs the other's emotional state and starts believing the other's story as if it were objective truth. See [[losing-yourself-in-others]]. The friend who absorbs the victim story becomes convinced the villain is who the friend is. The leader who absorbs the team's fear becomes afraid. The coach who absorbs the client's despair has nothing to offer.

- The holder cannot stay long. Empathic merger is exhausting. The holder retreats from the relationship or hardens against it, because the only alternative to merging is walling off. See [[empathy-vs-sympathy]].

- The holder loses access to their own wants, which removes the most useful thing they could offer: a real human in the room with the other one. See [[state-the-want-not-the-action]].

## The Leadership Version

In leadership, this principle is the corrective to [[chameleon-leadership]]. The leader who hears "meet people where they are" and morphs into each person they meet has confused presence with dissolution.

The leader who stays centered while holding space does both: attends fully to the report in front of them and remains identifiably the same leader from one meeting to the next. The team can find them. The team can predict them. The team can disagree with them. The team can be themselves around them.

The team does not actually want a leader who morphs to each person's preference. They want a leader who is reliably themselves and reliably present with whoever they are talking to.

## The Coaching Version

A coach who holds space without staying centered ends up taking on the client's story as their own. The coach starts believing the client's framing, advocating for the client's predetermined conclusion, or feeling the client's helplessness in their own body. None of this serves the client.

The coach who stays centered while holding space has the client's experience available to them and their own ground available simultaneously. The coach can feel the client's grief without becoming grief. The coach can feel the client's anger without taking the anger as orders to attack whoever the client is angry at. The coach can offer the client back something the client could not have generated alone.

This is the V and the E of [[view]] held together. Vulnerability requires being touched. Empathy requires staying with. Both require the coach remain a coach.

## How to Stay Centered

The mechanics are simple, the practice is lifelong:

- Stay in your body. See [[staying-in-your-body]]. Feet on the floor. Breath in the belly. Notice your own sensations while you attend to theirs.

- Notice when you are starting to morph. Ask: am I agreeing because I see it that way, or because they want me to? Am I feeling this, or am I picking it up?

- Have something you actually want and notice when you set it aside to manage the room.

- Use [[is-this-mine]] to check the boundary between your experience and theirs.

- When you notice you have lost yourself, return. The return is the practice. The losing is normal. The duration of the losing shortens with practice.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- When a leader presents with [[chameleon-leadership]] and needs a corrective framing.
- When a person says they are "too empathic" and gets lost in others.
- When [[i-caretake-people]] needs an alternative model of care.
- When someone is afraid that asserting themselves would mean abandoning the other.
- When the coach themselves is starting to lose ground with a client.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The chameleon leader who has been told to morph for each report.
- The caretaker who believes loving the other requires becoming the other.
- The new coach who has been taught "hold space" without the centering instruction.
- Anyone who confuses presence with agreement or merger.
