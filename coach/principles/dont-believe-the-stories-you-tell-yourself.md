# Don't Believe the Stories You Tell Yourself

## The Principle

The stories people tell about their lives — including the stories they tell about themselves to themselves — are often old stories that no longer describe what is actually happening. The story persists past the situation that originally generated it. The person continues to live inside the story as if it were current, without ever checking whether it still maps onto their actual life.

The foundational coaching stance that follows: do not take any presented story at face value. Not your own. Not anyone else's. Treat every stated problem as a hypothesis to be tested rather than as a fact to be accepted. The first move is almost always: is this actually true right now, or is this an old story being recited?

"There's a thing about all of our patterns where we tell ourselves we have this problem, but oftentimes that's an old story that we used to have, or it's a way that we tell ourselves, and we don't really look to see if it's true or not." — Joe Hudson, How Do I Get Unstuck? (Coaching Commentary)

"I don't believe any of the stories that I tell. And so I don't, to myself particularly, or I haven't in a long time. And, or maybe there's some left, don't get me wrong, but most of them I don't believe. And so I also don't believe the story that somebody else tells me. In this world, like, of, oh, this is the problem." — Joe Hudson, How Do I Get Unstuck? (Coaching Commentary)

## Why Stories Outlast Their Conditions

A story is generated in response to a particular set of circumstances. Once generated, the story tends to keep running even after the circumstances change. The reasons are structural:

- The story has become part of the person's identity. Identity has momentum; it does not update in real time.
- The story has become part of the person's social presentation. They have told it to others; updating it would require updating the presentation.
- The story is doing other work the person is unaware of (preventing a feared change, holding an old grievance, justifying a position).
- The story has not been challenged by direct contact with current reality. The person lives inside the story rather than checking it against what is actually happening.

The result: the story can be a decade or more old, describing a person who no longer exists in a situation that no longer applies, while still being lived as if it were a current report.

## The Diagnostic Question

The first move in working with any presented problem is to test whether the story matches the current reality. The question has many forms:

- "How long has this been true?"
- "What did your life look like a year ago?"
- "When did this story start?"
- "Is this really the problem, or is this the story you tell about the problem?"
- "What evidence do you have that this is currently true?"

The questions are not interrogative. They are diagnostic. They allow the person to surface their own counter-evidence, which is usually right there but has been obscured by the dominance of the story.

The 2024-03-18 session is the canonical demonstration. The person presented as stuck. Joe asked what her life had looked like a year ago. She said "very different." The "stuckness" story had been running while her actual life had been moving. Without the diagnostic question, the work would have proceeded on the wrong problem.

## The Stance Toward One's Own Stories

The principle applies inward first. Before disbelieving a client's story, the coach has to disbelieve their own. A coach who believes their own self-narrative will project that same credulity onto the client's narrative and will treat the client's stories as facts to be solved rather than as hypotheses to be tested.

The internal practice is ongoing. The coach notices a self-story arising ("I am the kind of person who...", "I always do this...", "I can't help X...") and does not automatically grant it credibility. The story is examined. Sometimes it is still true. Often it is not. The discrimination is the practice.

This does not mean the coach treats themselves as unknowable. The discrimination is between live experience (which the coach trusts) and ideational frames about live experience (which the coach holds lightly). The frame "I am insecure about my work" might be a story from a decade ago that no longer matches the current felt experience. The felt experience itself is the data; the story about it is one possible interpretation.

## The Stance Toward Others' Stories

When a client presents a problem, the coach's first move is not to engage with the problem as stated. The coach's first move is to test the framing. Three layers are being tested simultaneously:

1. **Is this currently true?** Is the situation the story describes still the situation the person is in?
2. **Is this the actual problem, or a cover story for a different problem?** Often the stated problem is a stand-in for something the person has not yet named.
3. **Is the person already aware of this in a way that the framing is hiding?** The very act of presenting the problem in this form often indicates more consciousness than the framing suggests. See [[awareness-of-pattern-is-halfway-through]].

The coach does not announce this skepticism as confrontation. The skepticism shows up as gentle questioning, as redirecting, as inviting the person to check rather than to accept. The person often finds the gap themselves once invited to look. They were never lying; they were repeating.

## What This Is Not

This principle is not a license to dismiss what people say. The opposite. The principle requires that the coach take the person's actual experience extremely seriously — which is precisely why the coach refuses to settle for the story version of that experience. The story is often what is in the way of the experience.

It is also not a stance of cynicism. The coach is not deciding in advance that the person is wrong. The coach is holding the story as one possible description of what is happening, with the working assumption that any specific story is more likely to be partial than complete. The completeness is not in the story; it is in the lived experience the story is gesturing at.

It is also not the same as [[the-questioning-stance]] in the philosophical sense. The principle is operational. It is about what the coach does in the first minutes of a session, not about epistemic position in general. The coach acts on the assumption that the presented problem is probably misframed and finds out by testing.

## Why This Is the Foundational First Move

If the coach accepts the stated problem as the problem, the entire session organizes around solving the wrong thing. The person can have years of coaching that addresses problems they no longer have, while the actual current dynamic goes unaddressed. The coaching feels like work and produces nothing because the work is being done at the wrong location.

If the coach tests the framing first, the session has a chance to find the actual current dynamic. The actual dynamic is often something the person is partially aware of but has not yet named. The coach's questioning helps the naming happen. Once the actual dynamic is on the table, the coaching can produce movement, because it is now operating on something that is actually present.

This is why the disbelief is the foundational stance. Without it, the rest of the coaching is downstream of an unverified premise. With it, the rest of the coaching is operating on what is actually happening.

## The Connection to "Already Halfway Through"

This principle pairs structurally with [[awareness-of-pattern-is-halfway-through]]. The two together describe Joe's foundational coaching stance:

- The client is more aware of what is happening than their story suggests
- The story they are telling is often older than the awareness
- The coach's job is to surface the gap between the story and the awareness
- Once the gap is surfaced, the work tends to complete on its own momentum

The disbelief of the story is the operational form of trusting the client's actual current intelligence. The coach refuses the story precisely because the client is more present than the story is letting them be.

## Tone

This principle is delivered with a quiet, almost amused matter-of-factness. Joe is not warning against stories with the urgency of a person who fears being deceived. He is reporting a structural feature of how stories work. The reporting tone communicates: this is just how it is, here is what to do about it.

When this principle is enacted in a session, the tone is even lighter. The coach does not announce "I don't believe your story" — that would be combative. The coach asks a small question that allows the story to be checked, then trusts the person to do the checking. The skepticism is in the questions, not in the manner.
