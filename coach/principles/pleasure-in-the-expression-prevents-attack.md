# Pleasure in the Expression Makes You Less Likely to Be Attacked

## The Principle

There is a way to ask for a want that is more likely to be attacked, and a way that is less. There is a way to draw a boundary that is more likely to be attacked, and a way that is less. There is even a way to get scared that is more likely to provoke attack, and a way that is not. The variable is whether pleasure is present in the act of the expression itself.

"If you're actually having pleasure in it, then you're in your body, and then you're not trying to manage the result of it and therefore less likely to be attacked." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The mechanism: pleasure in the expression is the somatic signature of being in your body. Being in your body is what makes the expression land as a clean expression rather than as a managed move with an agenda. Listeners track the agenda. A managed move with an agenda reads as something to defend against, even when the words are reasonable. An embodied expression without management reads as information about the speaker, not as a maneuver against the listener, and therefore does not trigger defense.

## The Three Domains

The principle is articulated across three different emotional acts, which makes clear it is not a special property of boundaries:

**Asking for a want.** A want can be asked for in a way that is contracted, managing how the listener will respond, scanning for permission, defended in advance against rejection. The same want can be asked for from the body, with the pleasure of the wanting present in the speaking, without an apparatus tracking the other person's response. The first version is more likely to be attacked. The second is more likely to be received.

**Drawing a boundary.** A boundary can be drawn in a way that is over-there (focused on what the other person is doing wrong, calculating their reaction, defended against pushback). The same boundary can be drawn in-here (from the felt experience of what the speaker needs, with the pleasure of the no-saying present in the act). The first reads as attack and provokes counter-attack. The second reads as a clean statement of position and does not.

**Getting scared.** Even fear can be expressed in a way that is more likely to be attacked (the contracted, defended, managing-the-response version) or in a way that is not (the embodied, here-is-what-I-feel version with the felt-aliveness of the fear present). The fear-with-pleasure-in-it is the fear that does not invite the predator. The fear that is being managed-against is the fear that does.

The pattern is the same across all three: the somatic configuration of the speaker — embodied versus managing — determines how the expression lands more than the words do.

## Why This Works

The result-managing apparatus is detectable to the listener even when neither speaker nor listener can name it. When a person is asking for a want while simultaneously trying to manage what the answer will be, the listener feels two things in the room: the want and the management. The management is the part that triggers defense. The listener does not consciously think "they are managing me," but the body picks up that there is an agenda being executed and responds accordingly.

The pleasure-in-the-expression is the felt evidence that no management is happening. The pleasure is not possible if the person is splitting attention between the expression and the monitoring of the response. The pleasure requires full presence in the body. The full presence in the body is what the listener registers as safety — there is no agenda being executed against them, just a person being a person.

This is why the same words, delivered by two different people, can land completely differently. The words are not the variable. The somatic configuration of the speaker is the variable. The pleasure-in-the-act is the marker of the configuration that does not provoke defense.

## What This Looks Like Practically

The person who has not trained this asks for a want and immediately their attention goes to the listener's face. Did they look annoyed? Are they about to say no? The attention-splitting is the management. The body of the speaker is partly in the want and partly in the monitoring. The listener feels the divided attention as pressure or as performance and responds to that, not to the want itself.

The person who has trained this asks for the want and stays in their body. The pleasure of the asking — yes, the asking itself has a pleasure to it, the embodied movement of expressing a desire — is allowed to be the dominant register. The listener does not have to defend against an agenda because there is no agenda being run. The listener can respond to the actual want with the actual answer, without the conversation being warped by the management apparatus.

The same applies to boundaries. The pleasure of drawing a boundary is the embodied feel of "this is my no" without the simultaneous apparatus of "and you need to accept it or else." When the apparatus is absent, the boundary lands as information. When the apparatus is present, the boundary lands as ultimatum, regardless of the words used. See [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums]] for the related distinction.

## The Brett Mapping

A specific articulation surfaces in the same exchange:

"My system went from the like angry at my mom and not like something's bad about it, and I'm like leaving myself and in her, which leads like more likely to be an attack, to, oh yeah, my — I get to have a boundary. And then like, I, I experienced that as coming back into my body, and from that place, it's less about the other person and more about what I need, which is far less likely to result in me taking a defensive action that's received as attack." -- Brett Kistler, Pleasure

The mapping is structural. "Leaving myself and in her" is the configuration that gets attacked — the speaker is over-there, focused on the other person's wrongness, defending the position. "Coming back into my body" is the configuration that does not get attacked — the speaker is in-here, focused on what they need, with the pleasure of the want present as the embodied register.

The pleasure-in-the-expression is the marker of the second configuration. The cultivation of pleasure-in-emotional-acts is the cultivation of the configuration that does not invite attack.

## Connection to What We Fear We Invite

This is a domain-specific form of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The person who is most defended against being attacked — who is most carefully managing how the expression will land — is the person whose configuration most reliably produces the attack. The avoidance of the attack-feeling is what generates the configuration that triggers attack. The willingness to be in the body with the pleasure of the expression is the willingness to not manage the outcome, which is what stops generating the attack.

The mechanism is precise. The fear of being attacked produces the management. The management produces the configuration that reads as attack to the listener. The listener's defense response is the attack the speaker was afraid of. The loop closes.

The exit from the loop is not better attack-prevention. The exit is the somatic shift to in-the-body-with-pleasure, which removes the management, which removes the configuration that was triggering the attack response.

## Tone

Joe presents this as a noticing, almost in passing. "I've never thought about it that way," he says when Brett maps out the embodiment-to-no-attack chain. The delivery is light, exploratory. The principle is being articulated for the first time in the conversation, and Joe is registering it as a real principle even as he is saying it.

The matter-of-factness matters. The principle is not a trick or a manipulation tool. It is a structural description of how embodied expression lands differently than managed expression. The naming of it does not weaponize it. A person who tried to use the principle as a technique — "I will perform pleasure in my boundary so the other person can't attack me" — would be running the management apparatus through a new vector and would produce the same attack-inviting configuration. The principle only works when the pleasure is actually present, which only happens when the management is actually absent.
