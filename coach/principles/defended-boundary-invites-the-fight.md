# A Defended Boundary Invites the Fight You Were Afraid Of

## The Principle

If you draw a boundary while internally ready for a fight, you will get a fight. The readiness leaks into the delivery. The delivery reads to the receiver as an attack. The receiver responds with their own attack. The fight you were braced for arrives -- as a direct consequence of the bracing.

"You're going to be ready for the fight, which invites the fight. Like, if I draw a boundary with you and I'm like, if you get angry at me, I'm going to leave. That is going to invite a fight where it's like, I'm not -- I don't want to be with your anger. So I'm happy to leave when you get angry and I'm happy to come back as soon as you're ready to have the conversation without yelling at me. So it's going to be a whole different interaction if you can draw it with that open heart." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

The same boundary, with the same words, lands differently depending on the internal state of the speaker. The defended version invites the attack. The open-hearted version invites the relationship.

## The Mechanism

The receiver does not respond primarily to the words. The receiver responds primarily to the energy and signal the words are carrying. A boundary delivered from bracing carries the signal "I expect you to attack me." The receiver picks up the signal, registers themselves as having been positioned as the attacker, and either accepts the position (and attacks) or rejects it (and counter-attacks the false positioning).

A boundary delivered from open heart carries a different signal: "this is what I am going to do, and it has nothing to do with controlling you, and I am still in relationship with you while doing it." The receiver picks up that signal too. The response is different because the field is different.

The receiver's nervous system is reading your nervous system. If yours is in fight-or-flight, theirs goes into fight-or-flight. If yours is in regulation, theirs has more room to regulate. The boundary words are the surface; the regulation state of the body delivering them is the substrate. See [[defense-feels-like-attack-feedback-loop]] for the broader pattern this is one instance of.

## The Specific Form for New Boundaries

The principle has a particular relevance for new boundaries, because new boundaries are exactly the boundaries you are not okay with internally. See [[new-boundary-always-feels-not-okay]]. The not-okay-ness produces the defensiveness, which produces the bracing, which produces the fight-inviting delivery, which produces the fight.

The compound failure mode: a person tries to draw a new boundary, the internal not-okay-ness produces a defended delivery, the defended delivery produces a fight, the fight reinforces the belief that drawing this boundary is bad, and the person withdraws the boundary. The whole sequence is then read as evidence that the boundary itself was wrong, when in fact the boundary was fine -- the delivery was the failure.

The corrective: do not wait for the internal not-okay-ness to dissolve before drawing the boundary. The not-okay-ness is structural for any new boundary. But also do not deliver the boundary while in the not-okay-ness. The order of operations: do the internal work that gets you to open-hearted delivery, then deliver. The work is not optional preparation; it is part of the act of drawing the boundary.

See [[speak-the-elephant-from-open-heart-not-fear]] for the parallel principle in the elephant-in-the-room context.

## The Two Examples Compared

Joe gives a direct comparison in the transcript:

**Defended version:** "If you get angry at me, I'm going to leave."

**Open-hearted version:** "I'm not -- I don't want to be with your anger. So I'm happy to leave when you get angry and I'm happy to come back as soon as you're ready to have the conversation without yelling at me."

The two versions have nearly the same propositional content. The differences are signal differences:

- The defended version contains a conditional threat. The open-hearted version contains a statement of what the speaker wants for themselves.
- The defended version positions the other person as the cause of the leaving. The open-hearted version positions the speaker's own discomfort as the cause.
- The defended version has no re-engagement plan. The open-hearted version explicitly includes return as a structure.
- The defended version contains nothing about caring for the relationship. The open-hearted version embeds the relationship-care as the framing of the limit.

The receiver hears different boundaries because the receiver is hearing different signals. Same propositional content; different field.

## The Connection to What-We-Fear-We-Invite

This principle is one specific instance of the broader pattern: what we are bracing against, we invite. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the full treatment.

The mechanism in this specific case: bracing for the fight communicates to the other person that you expect them to fight, which makes it more likely that they fight. The fear of the fight becomes the cause of the fight. The boundary that was supposed to prevent the fight becomes the trigger for the fight.

The release from this loop is the open-hearted delivery. The open-hearted delivery removes the invitation. The fight that was about to happen does not happen, because the conditions for it being invited have been removed.

## What This Refutes

This refutes the "be firm" approach to boundary delivery, which says you should deliver the boundary with strength and conviction so the other person knows you mean it. The "firmness" is often the bracing this principle warns against. The firm-sounding boundary is the boundary that invites the fight.

Real conviction sounds soft, not firm. The boundary delivered from a place where it is already true inside the speaker does not need to be enforced through tone, because the truth of it does not depend on the receiver's response. The firmness is a tell that the boundary is not yet held; the speaker is using tone to compensate for what is not yet stable internally.

See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] for the felt-sense distinction, and [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums]] for the related anti-pattern where the firmness tips into control.

## What This Asks of the Speaker

The work is on the internal state before the delivery. Specifically:

1. **Locate the fear or anger that is producing the bracing.** What are you afraid will happen when you draw this boundary? What anger is leaking into your sense of needing to draw it?
2. **Feel that emotion through to where it stops bracing.** The bracing is the avoidance of the emotion. Once the emotion is felt, the bracing releases.
3. **Notice whether the boundary still feels needed.** Sometimes, after the emotion is felt, the boundary turns out to have been the wrong tool -- the underlying need was for the emotion itself to be felt. Sometimes the boundary still feels needed; now it can be drawn cleanly.
4. **Run the [[does-saying-it-increase-your-love|love test]].** When you imagine saying it now, does your heart open or close? If it still closes, more internal work is needed before delivery.

This is what makes boundary-drawing slow at first. The internal work is the actual work. The verbal delivery is the cap on the work, not the work itself.

## The Delivery in the Body

The body signature of an open-hearted boundary differs from the body signature of a defended boundary. The chest is softer. The jaw is unclenched. The voice is lower-pitched and slower. The eyes are open. There is no edge in the energy.

This is why rehearsing the boundary in front of a mirror or with a coach is useful: you can see whether the delivery is in the body state you want. If the delivery comes out tense, no amount of word-choice editing will fix it. The body state has to shift first. See [[pleasure-in-the-expression-prevents-attack]] for the related principle about how the pleasure-bearing delivery does not get attacked.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The discovery that the same words, delivered from a different internal state, get a completely different response
- The recognition that fights they kept starting were being started by their own defensiveness before the other person did anything
- The relief of finding that an open-hearted boundary does not need to be defended because there is nothing to defend against
- The surprise of drawing a boundary that does not produce the expected fight
- The realization that "being firm" was the failure mode, not the safeguard
