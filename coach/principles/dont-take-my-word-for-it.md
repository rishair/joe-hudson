# Don't Take Anyone's Word for It — Run the Experiment

## The Principle

When you hear a teaching that resonates with you, the wrong move is to nod, agree, and incorporate it as a new belief. The wrong move is also to dismiss it because it does not fit your current model. Both reactions skip the only move that actually produces wisdom: design an experiment, run it for a defined period, and see what is true for you.

The principle is symmetric. It applies as much to teachings that feel right as to teachings that feel wrong. Resonance is not a signal that the teaching has integrated. Resistance is not a signal that the teaching is false. Both are signals to run the experiment.

"So you listen to that, you hear it, but instead of going, oh, that's right, and I'm gonna be grateful, fuck that, run an experiment. Say, oh, I'm gonna do gratitude practice 10 minutes a day for 2 weeks, and see what the hell happens. I'm gonna be grateful every time I remember and see what the hell happens. That's a far better way to do it than to just take my word for it." — Joe Hudson, 3 Mindsets That Transform Self-Development

## The Three Reasons

Joe gives three reasons the experiment beats the agreement:

**1. Active relationship beats passive reception.** "You learn it a lot better if you actually are in an active relationship with it." Information you have agreed with sits in the head as a belief. Information you have tested with your own life is in the body as a knowing. The difference is structural. The body knows what the head only thinks it knows.

**2. The teaching may not be right for you right now.** "Maybe gratitude doesn't work for you right now. Eh, let's find out." Teachings are not universal prescriptions. They are tools that work in certain conditions for certain people at certain times. The only way to know if a given teaching is the one for you in this moment is to test it. The teacher cannot know this from outside.

**3. Your version may be different from theirs.** "Maybe there's a way in which you can do gratitude that you're gonna discover in that experimentation that works better for you." The teacher's version was their experiment. Your experiment will produce your version. Sometimes the discovered version is better than the original. The experiment is also where innovation happens.

## The Symmetric Application

The principle applies to both poles of reaction. The two starting points and their respective experiments:

- If you hear something and think "oh, that resonates with me" — design an experiment to test it. The resonance is not enough.
- If you hear something and think "that does not resonate with me, that is triggering me" — design an experiment to test it. The trigger is information that the topic deserves attention, not that the teaching is wrong.

"If you hear something that you think, oh, that resonates with me, or that's something that doesn't resonate with me, ah, is it triggering me? And I want to experiment with it, then design an experiment where you get to play with it for a while and find out what's true for you." — Joe Hudson, 3 Mindsets That Transform Self-Development

The symmetric application is the test of whether the principle has been understood. A person who runs experiments only on teachings that already feel right is using experimentation to confirm what they already believe. The harder application — running an experiment on the teaching that feels wrong — is where the principle does the work.

## What Counts as an Experiment

An experiment in this sense has at minimum three components:

- **A specific behavior.** Not "I'll try to be more grateful" but "I'll do a 10-minute gratitude practice each morning, and I'll thank myself every time I remember during the day."
- **A time bound.** "For two weeks." A week is often too short. A month is fine but harder to commit to. Two weeks tends to be the right size for a personal experiment.
- **An observation.** What are you noticing? What is the day-to-day effect? Are you sleeping differently? Are decisions changing? Is the inner critic louder or quieter?

The experiment does not require predicting the outcome. It requires being willing to find out. The willingness to not know is what makes it an experiment rather than a confirmation. See [[enter-inquiry-with-soft-sense-of-self]].

## The Anti-Pattern: Half-Assing It

The experiment fails when it is performed without genuine engagement. The person sort of does the practice, doesn't really feel it, and then concludes the practice doesn't work for them. This is a false negative produced by unclean inputs.

If you are running the experiment, run it. If you cannot bring yourself to run it, the right move is to decline the experiment, not to half-perform it. The half-performance produces no information except about the half-performance.

See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]] for the structural problem with corrupted experiments and [[frame-it-as-an-experiment]] for the related team-context move.

## Why This Is the Author's Own Stance

Joe explicitly applies the principle to his own teachings. The teachings in his podcast and master class are not offered as truths to believe. They are offered as experiments to run. The listener's job is not to agree with Joe. The listener's job is to test what Joe says against their own experience.

This is structurally identical to [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]], but spoken from the other direction. That principle says: the coach does not transmit wisdom; the client discovers it. This principle says: the listener does not receive wisdom; the listener tests offerings and discovers what is true. Both principles point at the same architecture: wisdom comes from the inquiry, not from the transmission.

The implication: a listener who has absorbed Joe's teachings without ever having tested them in their own life has not really received the teachings. They have memorized a worldview. The worldview will be available to them as language and conceptual frame. It will not be available to them as embodied knowing. The difference shows up under pressure.

## What This Protects Against

**Spiritual ventriloquism.** The person who can quote teachings but has not lived them. The teaching is a costume rather than a substance. This is one of the more common failure modes of consuming personal-development content. The experiment frame protects against it because the experiment requires real engagement.

**The trap of resonance.** When a teaching feels right, the felt-rightness can be mistaken for the integration of the teaching. It is not. Resonance is the start. The experiment is the work. Many people skip from resonance directly to "I know this now" and miss the integration entirely.

**Premature dismissal.** When a teaching feels wrong, the dismissal can also be confused with discernment. Dismissal is data about the listener's current state, not about the teaching's accuracy. The experiment converts the trigger into information rather than letting it become a foreclosure.

## The Connection to Authenticity

The experiment principle is one of the [[improvement-vs-authenticity]] supports. Taking someone else's word for it is a form of importing an external standard — exactly what improvement-mode does. Running your own experiment is a form of finding out what is actually yours — exactly what authenticity-mode does.

"Looking for someone else to tell you your truth is, is never going to work as effectively as running experiments and finding out what your own truth is." — Joe Hudson, 3 Mindsets That Transform Self-Development

The structural parallel: every time you accept a teaching without testing it, you have outsourced your sense of what is true to the teacher. Every time you test a teaching, you reclaim the sense of what is true as a thing you arrive at through your own contact with reality. The reclaimed version is sturdier under pressure and more specific to your actual life.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a kind of irreverence. "Fuck that, run an experiment." The irreverence is structural — it is the same irreverence he is asking the listener to bring to all teachings, including his own. The tone makes it clear that the teaching does not deserve special protection. He would rather you test it than agree with it.

The irreverence is not dismissive. It is the right level of seriousness. Serious engagement with a teaching looks like testing it. Polite agreement looks like serious engagement but is not. The irreverence in Joe's voice is the signal that the polite-agreement response is what he is trying to prevent.

## When This Principle Is Most Relevant

- After any teaching episode, when the listener is deciding what to do with what they heard.
- When a person describes themselves as a "follower" of any teacher or method.
- When a person is dismissing a teaching without having tested it.
- When a person is accepting a teaching without having tested it.
- When the inner critic is using a teaching as a new should ("Joe says I should be grateful, so I'm bad for not being grateful").
- When a coach is at risk of becoming the holder of the client's truth instead of the facilitator of the client's experiments.
