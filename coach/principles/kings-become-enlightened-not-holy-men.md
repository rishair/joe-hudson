# Kings Become Enlightened, Not Holy Men

## The Principle

In the spiritual scriptures, the people who become enlightened are typically the kings -- the ones who had everything they could want -- not the holy men who renounced everything. The pattern is not accidental. It is structural.

A person who has not yet had the thing can always tell themselves that the thing will fulfill them. The hypothesis is untested. The chase has a future-located answer it is moving toward. The unfreedom of the chase is disguised by the felt-rightness of pursuing the future-located answer.

A person who has had the thing -- and discovered it does not fulfill -- has had the hypothesis tested. The future-located answer was acquired. The fulfillment did not arrive. The disguise drops. What is on the other side of the disguise becomes available to look at, because the strategy of "get the thing" no longer has somewhere to point.

The holy man who renounced from the start may have made the right move in the world, but they have not necessarily tested the chase-hypothesis. They have walked around it. The chase-hypothesis is still alive in them, just not being acted on. The king who got everything has tested it directly. The hypothesis has been falsified in their own life. The recognition has empirical grounding.

This is the structural reason that wealth, fame, beauty, and power, when actually attained, are sometimes more useful to a person's recognition than renunciation would have been. The thing is the door, not because having it is good, but because having it dissolves the cope that not having it is the problem.

## The Source of the Principle

The framing comes from a conversation Joe had with Dr. K (Alok Kanojia) about a pattern in the scriptures. The scriptures consistently feature kings -- people at the top of their domain -- as the ones who awaken. Not the priests. Not the renunciants. The kings.

The pattern is also visible empirically in Joe's coaching practice:

"I cannot tell you how many people I've met who have gotten their dreams, and then they're like, wait, I got everything I wanted, but it's not actually what I needed. And how many people have become $100 millionaires that I know who are miserable and they're saying, wait, I thought this was supposed to fix it." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 100s of Top Performers

The 100-millionaires-who-are-miserable are the modern kings. The pattern Joe describes is the same pattern the scriptures describe. The kings get everything. The everything does not fix it. The recognition begins.

## Why This Is Not a Recommendation to Chase Wealth

The principle has a clear failure mode if it is read as prescriptive: "I should become a king first, then I can become enlightened." This reading collapses the principle back into the chase it is supposed to dissolve. The new chase is "become a king so I can wake up," which is structurally the same as the old chase "become a king so I can be free."

The principle is descriptive of a pattern, not prescriptive of a path. Its usefulness is for the person who has *already* walked the chase to its end and discovered the falsification. For that person, the principle does two things:

1. **It contextualizes their position.** They are not alone in this pattern. Kings have been doing it for thousands of years. The disillusionment is not a personal failing. It is the structural reward for actually testing the hypothesis.
2. **It opens the next move.** The kings who became enlightened did not stay in the disillusionment. They turned toward what the chase had been organized to avoid. The principle gives them permission to do the same.

The principle is therefore primarily useful to clients in [[seen-through-the-disguise-of-success]] territory. It is rarely useful to clients still in the chase, because for them it functions either as discouragement ("you still have to walk this whole road") or as new ammunition for the chase ("see, I need to get to the top first").

## The Hole Is Not Filled

A specific feature of the king's recognition: the discovery is not that the thing was bad. The discovery is that the thing did not do the work it was supposed to do.

"You think something's going to fulfill you. You think that you're going to be fulfilled by money or by people loving you or by fame or by getting a lot of sex or whatever it is. And then you get all that stuff and you're like, whoa, the hole is not filled, the gap isn't filled." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 100s of Top Performers

The hole is the key word. The chase had been organized around filling a hole. The chase succeeded in producing the thing. The hole remained.

The hole is the felt-emptiness, the not-enough, the chronic ache the system has been running. The chase had been an attempt to fill the hole from the outside. The fill from the outside cannot land because the hole is not externally located. The hole is interior. External material does not have access to it.

The king's recognition is structurally the same as the recognition that all the work in [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]] points at. The hole is not the catastrophe; the hole is the doorway. But the king arrives at this recognition from a particular angle: through having tested the external strategy to completion and discovered its limit empirically.

## The Move That Follows

The king's recognition is the first half. The second half is the redirect: stop trying to fill the hole, and instead enter the hole.

"What is it like to go find out what that big empty space that I've been trying to fill, what is actually there? What's actually going on there? How do I actually fall in love with that? How do I enter into that? How do I stop avoiding that?" -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 100s of Top Performers

The move is operational. It is not a meditation technique or a renunciation. It is an entering and an interest. What is in this place I have been avoiding? What happens if I stop trying to escape it? What is there when I am no longer running?

The result of the move, when actually carried out, is the recognition that the hole was not the catastrophe. The hole was where the freedom was. The chase had been preventing the freedom from being available.

"That's where the transformational journey gets really good. That's where the whole identity can shift, and all of a sudden you can look up and you can say, oh right, the thing that I've been avoiding my whole life has been the most direct path to freedom that I've ever found." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 100s of Top Performers

This is the load-bearing claim of the principle, stated in Joe's own words. The avoided thing -- the hole, the empty space, the felt not-enough -- is the most direct path to freedom. The king's path differs from the holy man's path in that the king was forced to discover this by getting the thing and watching it fail to fill the hole. The holy man may or may not have done this. The king has.

## Why the King's Path Has a Specific Risk

The risk in the king's path is that the disillusionment, when it arrives, is rarely followed by the redirect. Most kings, having gotten everything and discovered it doesn't fulfill, install a new chase. The new chase is usually one of: more of the same (more money, more fame, more sex), philanthropy as the next chase, spirituality as the next chase, addiction as the avoidance, or depression as the collapse.

Each of these is a way of not entering the hole. The chase-for-more keeps the old strategy. The philanthropy installs a new chase wearing different clothes. The spirituality-as-chase converts the principle into a project. The addiction numbs the felt-emptiness. The depression collapses around the felt-emptiness instead of entering it.

The redirect -- actually entering the hole -- is rare. It requires the king to do the thing they have spent their life avoiding. The avoidance is the engine that built the kingdom. Stopping the avoidance means the engine stops. The system fears this stoppage as much as it fears anything.

This is why the moment after the recognition is the moment Joe is looking for. The window in which the redirect is possible is short. Pointing at the redirect before the next chase installs is the high-leverage coaching move at this stage. See [[seen-through-the-disguise-of-success]] for the read and the operational moves.

## How This Principle Differs From Renunciation

Some spiritual traditions teach renunciation as the path: give up the thing, become the holy man, find freedom in poverty. The kings-become-enlightened principle is not an argument against renunciation. It is a description of a different empirical pathway that the scriptures also describe.

The two pathways differ in where the test happens:

- **Renunciation path:** the test is not "does the thing fulfill" but "can I live without the thing." The chase is bypassed. The freedom is sought directly, by stripping away the substrate the chase was supposed to acquire.
- **King's path:** the test is "does the thing fulfill." The chase is walked to completion. The freedom is recognized through the falsification of the chase.

Both can produce the same destination. The king's path is more common in modern Western life because the cultural substrate (achievement, accumulation, capitalism) keeps people on the chase by default. Renunciation requires actively stepping off the conveyor belt. The king's path requires only walking the conveyor belt to its end and noticing what is there (or not there) at the destination.

The principle does not say one path is better. It says that the king's path is real, that it is well-documented in scripture and in modern experience, and that for the person who is on it, the work is the same: enter the hole the chase was supposed to fill.

## The Recognition Often Looks Like Disappointment

A subtle feature of the king's path: the moment of recognition often does not feel like a breakthrough. It feels like disappointment. The expectation was that the thing would fulfill. The thing was acquired. The fulfillment did not arrive. The felt-experience is closer to "what's the point?" than to "ah, now I see."

"You get there and all of a sudden you get to number one in the world and they're like, what's the point?" -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 100s of Top Performers

The disappointment is not the absence of the recognition. The disappointment *is* the recognition, in its raw, unintegrated form. The recognition does not yet know it is a recognition. It still feels like the failure of the strategy.

The coach's work is partly to name this. The disappointment is data. It is the signal that the strategy has actually been tested rather than continuously deferred. The "what's the point?" question is the entry to the redirect. The point is not on the other side of more chasing. The point is in the felt-emptiness that the chasing has been trying to fill.

When this is named, the disappointment often shifts. It becomes less heavy. The person realizes they have learned something rather than failed at something. The disappointment retains its sadness but loses its catastrophe.

## In Coaching

When a client is in the king's-recognition stage:

1. **Name what they have done.** They have actually tested the hypothesis most people defer testing their whole lives. This is rare and useful.
2. **Connect to the larger pattern.** The kings-become-enlightened framing puts them in good company. They are not alone in arriving here. The pattern is millennia-old.
3. **Resist the temptation to fix the disappointment.** The disappointment is the entry. Do not try to talk them out of it. Do not offer a new chase. Sit with the felt-experience.
4. **Point at the redirect.** Enter the hole. Stop trying to fill it. Get curious about what is there when nothing is being chased to fill it.
5. **Trust the building-joy if it is present.** Some clients in this stage still have intrinsic engagement with the activity that produced the kingdom (Scheffler's love of practice, for instance). The building-joy does not need to be coached against. The achievement-joy needs to be released. The two are different.
6. **Be patient.** The recognition lands fully over weeks to months, not minutes. The next chase will try to install several times. Each installation is the opportunity to point again at the redirect.

The principle's role in this work is to provide the contextualizing frame. It tells the client that their disillusionment is not a problem; it is the door. It tells them they are not alone in this stage. It tells them what move is next. It does not do the work for them.
