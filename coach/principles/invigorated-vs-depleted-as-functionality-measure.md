# Invigorated vs. Depleted at the End of a Meeting Is the Functionality Measure

## The Principle

The most direct measure of whether a team is dysfunctional: at the end of a meeting, do you feel invigorated or do you feel depleted? Not what was decided, not whether the agenda was completed, not whether things were "productive" in the conventional sense. The body's reading of the meeting is the most accurate diagnostic available.

"The best way to measure if a team is dysfunctional is at the end of a meeting, do you feel invigorated or do you feel depleted? How invigorated do you feel? How depleted do you feel? Is a great mark of the dysfunction. It just hits it right where it lives." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## Why the Body Is the Best Reader

A meeting that looked productive on paper can leave the team flattened. A meeting that produced no decisions can leave the team energized. The body integrates the totality of the dynamic -- the unspoken weight, the politics, the time wasted on display, the suppressed emotion -- in a way the meeting agenda cannot. The post-meeting energy is the team registering what actually happened, not what was scheduled to happen.

This is the same logic as [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] applied at the meeting scale. The human system runs on enjoyment. A team that consistently leaves meetings depleted is burning fuel faster than it is producing it, and the depletion will eventually show up as turnover, mistakes, or quiet quitting.

## What the Measure Picks Up

The invigoration/depletion read catches dysfunctions that the standard team-assessment instruments miss:

- The leader who runs technically efficient meetings but leaves the team feeling small
- The meeting that completed its agenda but spent its energy on display and politics
- The team that smiled through the meeting and exhaled in relief when it ended
- The conversation that solved nothing but left people more connected to each other and the work
- The leader who blames in a friendly tone but the body still registers as blame

## What the Measure Implies for Action

Once a team adopts this measure, the action becomes obvious: when a meeting leaves people depleted, something in the meeting needs to change. The question shifts from "was the meeting good?" to "what made it depleting, and what would have made it invigorating?" This is the foundation of [[five-star-meeting-commitment]].

The measure also extends to the question of which meetings should exist at all. A recurring meeting that consistently produces depletion is a meeting that should be redesigned or eliminated. The depletion is information, not an inevitability.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You walk out of a meeting and head straight for coffee, your laptop, anything to recover
- You realize you have been dreading a recurring meeting for months
- You finish a long day of meetings and cannot account for what you have produced
- The thought of one more meeting on the calendar makes your body sink
- Conversely: you walk out of a meeting wanting to start working on what was discussed

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader asks how to know if their team is functional
- Someone describes their team as "fine" while looking exhausted
- A leader is using output metrics that show success while the team is bleeding people
- Someone asks "what's a good measure of team health that's not lagging by six months?"
- The conversation about team functionality has been happening at the abstract level without a usable diagnostic

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the directness of someone naming the obvious that the listener has been working not to notice. The phrasing "it just hits it right where it lives" is matter-of-fact, almost casual. There is no drama in the delivery; the drama is in how quickly the measure shows what people have been avoiding.
