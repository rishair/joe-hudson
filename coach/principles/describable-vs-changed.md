# You Can Do the Work You Can Describe, Or the Work That Changes You — Not Both

## The Principle

Two paths exist in self-development work, and they trade off against each other. The first path produces a person who, after years of work, can describe in great detail what is wrong with them. Their patterns, their childhood imprints, their attachment style, their enneagram, their trauma map. They can give you a beautiful summary of their psyche. And nothing has actually changed. The second path produces a person who cannot articulate what happened to them. The work is in their muscles, their decisions, their relationships, their day-to-day way of being. They cannot describe any of it. And everything has changed.

These are not two stages of the same path. They are different paths. Articulability and actual change are inversely correlated in this work, not positively correlated, because the part of the system that produces clear descriptions is the same part that resists the kind of change that would be hardest to describe.

"You can either do the kind of work where you, after 10 years, can describe everything that's wrong with you, but nothing's changed. Or you can do the work that you say, what the fuck just happened? I can't describe any of it, but everything has changed." — Joe Hudson, What Changed After Master Class?

## Why Articulability Is Diagnostic

The capacity to describe what is wrong with yourself is a head-level skill. It involves observing the self, building a model of the self, and storing that model as a set of statements. The model is useful for a kind of social legibility (you can explain yourself to others, you can sound insightful in a therapy session, you can recognize your patterns when they show up). What it does not do is change them.

Real change happens at the levels below description: emotional fluidity changes, the nervous system recalibrates, the sense of self softens or expands. None of these movements produce a tidy verbal output. They produce a different daily life. The person making decisions differently, responding to triggers differently, holding their family differently. The articulation of those changes lags behind the changes themselves and is often impossible. The person literally does not remember what they did, what they saw, what shifted in them. They just notice the world is different.

The descriptive map of the psyche is a defense, not a transformation. The mind builds the map so it has something to do with the material that would otherwise have to be felt. Building maps is what the head does when it is being asked to feel something it would rather not feel. See [[feel-over-figure]] for the broader version of this dynamic.

## The Tradeoff Is Built Into the Mechanism

The two paths trade off because the work that changes a person requires bypassing the same descriptive apparatus that the first path is busy building. The transformative work happens in the body, in the heart, in the relational field, in the felt sense of being. These layers do not produce articulable summaries because they do not store experience as propositional content. They store it as patterned response. When the patterned response changes, the change is real but not narratable. The person can sometimes recall a moment, sometimes a sensation, sometimes a few words spoken in a group — but the integration itself is not a sentence.

The person doing the descriptive work is, by definition, keeping the material at the level where it can be described. That level is the level above where change happens. So the more describable the work remains, the less it is doing the structural thing it is supposed to do.

This is not a paradox to be cleverly resolved. It is a structural feature of how the layers of the system actually operate. See [[the-work-as-three-levels-of-change]] for the underlying layer model.

## The MasterClass Evidence

Joe references this principle when describing the design choices of the MasterClass and the in-person week-long. The deliberate decision: do not give the intellectual framing before the exercise. Run the exercise first. Let the body and the emotional system do their work. Then, if at all, talk about what happened.

The empirical observation that justifies the design: "When we do the week-long, like the in-person, nobody remembers what the fuck happened the entire time. They're just like, what the fuck happened? I only remember yesterday." This is reported as a sign that the work is landing, not as a sign that something has gone wrong. The non-recall is the integration signature.

The mechanism Joe gestures toward: brain wave state. Theta state, the state of deep absorption and pre-verbal learning. Information that lands in theta does not get filed in the propositional-memory system. It gets filed as patterned response and altered baseline. The person leaves the work with a changed nervous system and an unchanged biographical recall of the workshop.

"There's a theory about it that you're using, like your brain is in a different set of brain waves for that, theta, and so you don't remember in the same way. But all we know for sure is that people don't remember." — Joe Hudson, What Changed After Master Class?

The 3 / 2 / 1-year-out alumni confirm this in their own language. Eva on her post-MasterClass month: "It just, nothing made sense and made sense at the same time... I couldn't really tell what happened in August and September. It's funny, it's like I cannot even remember what happened in August, September, and a bit of October. But I was deeply like doing the work." Matthew on the post-MasterClass period: "August, that was a puddle." Caroline on the cumulative effect: "It built up skills of not getting hooked into drama even when challenging things happen."

## The Implication for Practice

For someone choosing how to do the work, the principle suggests several heuristics:

- **Be suspicious of any practice that primarily produces beautiful descriptions of your problems.** If you can summarize what you have learned in three years of work, you may have been doing the wrong work.
- **Be suspicious of any teacher who gives you the framework before the experience.** The framework will install itself in the same descriptive layer that produces unchanged lives. See [[experiential-before-intellectual]].
- **Trust the periods where you cannot describe what is happening.** The "what the fuck just happened" moment is the diagnostic signature of real movement. It feels unsettling because the descriptive apparatus is not in control.
- **Trust the behavioral evidence over the recall.** If you behave differently, you have changed, regardless of whether you can explain how.

Caroline's "not-toothache" observation captures this directly. The change is what is no longer there. You do not notice the absence of a toothache; you notice toothaches. You will not necessarily know what changed. You will just be able to do things you could not do before, like say "I'm really angry" and walk around the house and have it resolve in a day rather than fester for a month. See [[wisdom-is-when-you-dont-have-to-remember]].

## The Implication for Coaching

When someone presents in coaching as already very articulate about their patterns, the coaching task is often to move them out of description. The articulation is not a sign of integration; in many cases it is the substitute for it. The well-trained self-development consumer can describe their attachment style, their inner critic, their wound, their somatic experience. None of those descriptions necessarily indicate that the corresponding material has moved.

The move is to ask the person to feel rather than describe. To do an exercise without a frame. To notice the body. To follow what is happening now rather than narrate what has happened before. See [[not-sometimes-right-now]] and [[fuck-the-right-answer]] for two specific instruments that interrupt the descriptive impulse.

The opposite read is also useful: when someone cannot articulate what they have learned but their life looks different (relationships better, work more aligned, family less reactive, body more relaxed), do not push them to describe it. The non-description is the integration. Asking them to articulate it may pull the material back up into the layer where it can be described and stops being lived.

## The Pre-Existing Material

The principle has a long history in contemplative traditions, which Joe gestures toward indirectly. Zen has the formulation that the person who can describe the realization has not had it. Sufi tradition: "Those who know do not speak; those who speak do not know." The unique contribution of Joe's framing is the operational test: ask yourself which kind of work you are doing. Articulability is the diagnostic.

The principle also pairs with [[dont-make-family-dynamics-the-story]] — the trap of intellectualizing family patterns into a fixed explanation that prevents the actual feeling work from happening. The fixed explanation is descriptive work substituting for changed work.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a kind of finality. The line "I can't describe any of it, but everything has changed" lands as the description of the goal state. He is not equivocating about which path is better. The choice is presented as binary because the tradeoff is structural. The tone is intended to disrupt the implicit assumption many self-development consumers hold that more understanding equals more change.

There is also a slight irreverence in "what the fuck just happened." The expletive marks the territory: this is not the polished arrival at insight that a workshop pamphlet promises. This is the disorienting absence of being able to explain yourself, with the simultaneous evidence that you are different.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person describes years of therapy or self-work but reports no behavioral change.
- A person presents as highly articulate about their patterns and asks for more frameworks.
- A person evaluating which self-development modality to pursue is comparing them by how well-explained the system is.
- A person is using the descriptive map of their psyche as a way to relate to themselves instead of feeling themselves directly.
- A person is anxious that they cannot summarize what they just learned in a workshop or session.
