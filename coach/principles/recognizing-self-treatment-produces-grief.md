# Recognizing How You've Been Treating Yourself Produces Grief

## The Principle

When the work surfaces an accurate view of how the person has been treating themselves — and, often, how that mistreatment has been mirrored in how they have treated others — grief arrives. The grief is not a complication. It is part of the release. Shame's job was to stagnate this grief; when the shame lifts, the grief is what gets to come through.

The principle is structural. The pattern of self-criticism, hiding, and pre-emptive rejection of others has had a cost. The person has been doing harm — to themselves, and through the same operating mode, to the people they have refused contact with. The cost has been kept invisible by the shame layer. When the shame lifts, the cost becomes visible. The visibility produces grief.

"If I actually see how I've been treating myself and I see the way that I've been treating others, then I have to feel a fuck-ton of grief... That's right. Because that's what shame's job is, is to stagnate an emotional experience. And so when that shame leaves, there's going to be grief and other emotions that need to be felt that are going to be scary." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen

The grief is therefore not a sign that the work has gone wrong. It is the predictable consequence of the work going right. Anticipating it allows the work to proceed without the person interpreting their own grief as a setback.

## Why the Grief Is Often Scary

The grief is scary for several reasons:

**It implies the cost was real.** As long as the shame was running, the pattern could be treated as protective or necessary. When the shame lifts, the pattern becomes visible as costly. The person sees what they have lost — connection, intimacy, years of their own life — and the loss is grievable.

**It implies harm to others.** The fear-of-being-seen pattern is also a pre-emptive rejection of the other ("you will hurt me, you are bad"). When the person sees this clearly, they often see they have been treating people badly in their own minds, even while presenting as accommodating. The recognition of harm-to-others is its own grief.

**It usually arrives in a public moment.** Often the recognition happens in coaching — possibly in front of others. The grief that follows is then layered with the fear of being seen grieving. The person has to feel the grief while also feeling the spotlight, which is the very pattern the work was about. The double-bind is real and worth naming.

"Possibly in front of these three hundred people on this call... but in front of myself, especially." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen

The most exposing moment of the grief is in front of the self. Even when other people are present, the central exposure is to one's own witness. This is part of why the work is structured the way it is: building the capacity to be with oneself is the precondition for being able to bear seeing oneself.

## The Sequence Shame → Recognition → Grief → Release

The emotional sequence has a predictable shape:

**Phase 1: Shame running.** The pattern is operating. The cost is hidden. The person can name surface symptoms (the freezing, the hiding, the isolation) but cannot connect them to a coherent picture of what is happening.

**Phase 2: Recognition.** The pattern becomes visible. The person sees the shape — "oh, this is how I have been treating myself, this is how I have been treating them, this is the cost." The recognition is often quiet, almost matter-of-fact.

**Phase 3: Grief.** The cost lands as feeling. The grief is for the years of lost connection, for the version of the self that was kept hidden, for the people who never got the contact. Sometimes this is dramatic; often it is quiet and steady.

**Phase 4: Release.** When the grief is felt rather than blocked, it moves. The shame layer that was holding everything else still loses its grip. New emotion becomes available — sometimes love, sometimes anger that had been suppressed, sometimes a clear quiet that feels like rest.

The sequence cannot be rushed. Each phase has to actually happen for the next to be available. Trying to jump from recognition to release without the grief leaves the shame partially intact; the release does not stick.

## How Shame Stagnates Emotion

The principle sits inside a larger teaching: shame is an emotion that stops other emotions. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]]. As long as the shame is running, the other emotions cannot move. The grief that would naturally follow the recognition of cost is held still by the shame that prevents the recognition from arriving.

When the shame lifts — through the open-heart move, through the exposure, through the contact-with-counter-evidence — the held emotions come through. Grief is usually first because grief is what the shame was specifically blocking. Other emotions (anger, love, joy) often follow in sequence.

This is why a fear-of-being-seen client who is doing the work well often appears to "get sadder" in the middle of the work. The sadness is not new. It was always there, frozen behind the shame. The work has unfrozen it. The temporary increase in felt sadness is the marker of the work moving, not of the work failing.

## The Anti-Pattern: Re-Shaming the Grief

A common recursion: the person feels the grief, then feels shame about feeling the grief ("I shouldn't be this sad, this is embarrassing, why am I crying about this"). The re-shaming refreezes the grief. The work undoes itself.

The corrective: name the grief as part of the work. "This is the grief that was waiting under the shame. It is supposed to come through. You are doing the work right." The naming gives the grief permission to be present, which is what it needs to move through.

The [[the-eye-of-sauron]] is especially active in this moment. The same critical voice that was producing the fear of being seen will now produce the criticism of the grief. Recognizing the same voice in its new form interrupts the recursion.

## What This Principle Implies for the Coaching

The principle has several practical implications:

**Anticipate the grief.** When the work is approaching the recognition, the coach should expect grief and be ready to hold it. Not by pushing for it, but by not flinching when it arrives.

**Do not pull the person out of the grief.** The reflex to make the person feel better will short-circuit the release. The grief is the release. Stay with it.

**Name the grief structurally.** "This is what was underneath the shame. This is supposed to come through." Naming the grief as part of the work prevents the person from interpreting it as a sign they are broken.

**Hold the spotlight too.** If the grief arrives in front of others, the work is also to hold the meta-exposure of grieving while seen. Both can be held simultaneously.

**Trust the sequence.** Grief, when felt, moves. The temptation to intervene more is usually wrong. The body knows the sequence; the work is to allow it.
