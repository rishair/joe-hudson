# Searching for Purpose Pushes It Away

## The Principle

The reach for purpose is the obstruction to purpose. The mind that goes "let me find what I am supposed to do" implicitly holds the position that the answer is somewhere other than here, that the search is needed because the object is missing. The position is structural, not factual — and the act of holding it is what makes the position appear true.

Purpose is not a hidden object to be located. It is a felt orientation that arrives when the searching stops. The work is not to find it. The work is to stop preventing it from arriving.

"All that looking that you're doing to find your thing — that is a wall that prevents it from coming in." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Find My Purpose?

A related framing Joe uses in the teaching episode: the question assumes you are a dog that doesn't know it can bark.

"The question itself assumes that you are a dog that can't bark. How do I bark? It's like we're born with this thing, and the question is, are we living it in this moment?" -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The dog-asking-how-to-bark analogy is the same structure as the looking-for-the-thing-that-is-already-here: the question presupposes the absence of what is actually structurally present. The presupposition is the obstruction.

## The Mechanism

Searching presupposes absence. The search-state is the state of "this is not here, I have to find it." That state is the opposite of the receive-state. The receive-state is "it is here, and I am letting it in."

You cannot be in both states simultaneously. The search-state will not arrive at the receive-state by intensifying. It will arrive at the receive-state by stopping.

When someone has been searching for years, the structural problem compounds: the searching has become an identity, the suffering has become a story, the looking has become a fixed posture. The longer the search has run, the more invisible the searching-itself becomes as the problem. The person is no longer aware they are looking; they are aware only that they have not found.

"Instead of going out and looking, see what it's like to actually just assume it's coming towards you and your job is just to receive it." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Find My Purpose?

## Received vs. Acknowledged: Two Verbs for the Same Move

The opposite of "found" is sometimes "received" and sometimes "acknowledged." Both verbs point at the same anti-search move, with slightly different emphases.

- **Received** emphasizes the orientation: stop reaching out, let it land. The receive-state is open, available, oriented inward.
- **Acknowledged** emphasizes the recognition: it is already here, name what is present. The acknowledgment-state is honest, willing to see.

"What if the purpose isn't something that has to be found? It's something that has to be acknowledged." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

Joe describes a moment when this acknowledgment arrived for him: a stranger at a hot springs delivered an unexpected message — "we can't wait any longer; you know what you're supposed to do." The message did not introduce anything new. It demanded acknowledgment of what was already known. The acknowledgment was the work, not the discovery.

"And when it hit me, I was like, 'Oh shit, she's right.' It required that prompt. There was something that I'm supposed to be sharing — me being of service and helping people see themselves more clearly is what I'm supposed to be doing." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The information was already in him. The prompt forced the acknowledgment. Before the prompt, the information was being held without being acknowledged. After the prompt, the acknowledgment was made and the action followed.

This is the acknowledgment-frame: purpose is what you know but have not yet admitted you know. The search-frame imagines purpose is what you do not know. They are opposite framings. The first is workable; the second is not.

## The Always-Aligned-or-Less-Aligned Frame

A consequence of the receive-and-acknowledge frame: purpose is always partially present. There is no moment where the person is wholly out of their purpose; there are only degrees of alignment.

"There's been no moment that I can find where I don't know if I'm more aligned or less aligned with my purpose. How I'm being, how I'm thinking, how I am with you, how I am with myself, all of that, there's a purpose in that." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

This collapses the binary "I have my purpose" / "I haven't found my purpose" and replaces it with the gradient "I am more aligned right now" / "I am less aligned right now." The gradient is always readable. The reading produces immediate workable information: a small adjustment in this moment can produce more alignment.

See [[what-feels-more-in-purpose-right-now]] for the question that operationalizes this gradient frame in coaching.

## The Live Test

The principle can be demonstrated somatically in seconds. Joe runs the test by asking the person to do two things in immediate succession:

1. **Feel what it is to go look for your purpose.** Feel the search-state in the body.
2. **Feel what it is to receive it instead.** Without thinking, feel the opposite.

The contrast is the teaching. The body knows the difference immediately. The search-state is loaded, tense, externally oriented. The receive-state is open, available, internally oriented. The person discovers they have been carrying the wrong felt-state for years. See [[feel-the-searching-then-feel-the-opposite]].

When the receiving frame lands, intense emotion often arrives — tears, laughter, sweaty palms. The thing the person has been hunting has been there the whole time. The emotion is the recognition starting to register.

"It's very, it's, um, it's very— makes me want to cry, laugh, and my palms get sweaty all at the same time." -- guest, How Do I Find My Purpose?

## The Disproportion

Once the recognition lands, the disproportion becomes visible. The person has spent years searching for something that was always available. The years of trying, the experiments that caused pain and disruption, the despair at not having found it — all of that was the cost of the search frame.

This disproportion produces grief. Joe names it directly when the moment arrives:

"There's a shit ton of grief over the fact that that's been there the whole time and you've been wasting your energy struggling." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Find My Purpose?

The grief is part of the integration. It is not a problem to be fixed. The system is mourning the cost of having been organized around the wrong frame. See [[the-natural-integration-arc-after-breakthrough]].

The disproportion also produces the response "it feels too easy that that would be the answer." The system resists the proportion: years of suffering cannot have been about something so simple. But it was. The "too easy" reaction is itself a wall — a way of refusing to let the recognition in. The recognition lands anyway, slower, against the resistance, with grief.

## Why This Is Not "Just Stop Trying"

The recovery is not "stop searching" as an instruction. Trying to stop searching is another search — a search for the cessation of search. The mind that did not have access to the receive-state cannot produce the receive-state by issuing itself a command.

The actual move is the somatic A/B: feel the search-state, feel the receive-state, notice the difference, let the body know there are two options. Once the body knows the receive-state is available, it can be inhabited. Before the body knows, no amount of "stop searching" instruction reaches it.

This is why the principle has to be demonstrated, not preached. A lecture on "your search for purpose is the obstruction" produces another head-task. The person nods, agrees, and keeps searching, now with the added burden of the should "I should stop searching." The somatic demonstration bypasses this. See [[experiential-before-intellectual]].

## The How-To Question Is the Next Wall

When the person has felt the receive-state and the immediate emotion has moved through, a predictable question arrives: "How do I receive?" The question is the next wall. It re-installs the search frame, this time around the receive-state itself. "How do I do this thing I just did?"

Joe's response is to refuse the question and point at what just happened: "How did you just receive? How'd you do it?" The person was just receiving thirty seconds ago. The receiving has already happened, repeatedly. The how-to question is a way of saying "I do not want to actually have done that." See [[how-question-as-defense-against-having]].

The pattern is structural. Any teaching about a receiving move can be turned into a new how-to question, and the how-to is always the search frame re-installing itself. The teaching about purpose has the same vulnerability. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]] for the parallel pattern with listening.

## The Family of "Searching Pushes It Away" Principles

This principle is a specific instance of a broader pattern. Joe articulates the same structural insight across multiple domains:

- **Purpose:** searching for your thing pushes it away. (this article)
- **Love:** any strategy to get love cuts you off from love. See [[searching-for-love-cuts-off-love]].
- **Gratitude:** searching for gratitude pushes it away. See [[always-something-spectacular-happening]].
- **A partner:** love does not arrive when you are scared of losing yourself. See [[love-arrives-when-youre-not-scared-of-losing-yourself]].
- **Spiritual states:** chasing states pushes the state away. See [[state-seeking]].
- **Listening:** trying to listen better is the anti-listen. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]].

Each instance has the same mechanism. The seeker presupposes absence. The presupposition produces the absence. The seeker intensifies the search to fill the absence the search created. The loop runs until the seeker recognizes the structure.

The unifying principle: anything that lives in the receive-state cannot be acquired through the search-state. The search-state is what cuts off the thing being searched for. The exit is not a better search; it is dropping into the receive-state and noticing what is already available.

## What Purpose Actually Is, Once Received

The principle does not specify what purpose is. It only specifies how to stop preventing it. Once the searching stops and the receive-state is inhabited, what arrives is specific to each person — and often surprising, often quieter than the search had been promising, often already partially present in the person's life without their having noticed.

The recognition often takes the form "oh — this thing I have already been doing / loving / drawn to is what I was searching for." The searching had been obscuring the visible. With the searching released, the visible becomes apparent.

The recognition is not always specific work or a specific career. Sometimes it is an orientation, a way of being, a quality the person brings to whatever they are doing. The search frame insists purpose must be a specific external answer; the receive frame allows purpose to be whatever it actually is, including forms that the search frame would not have recognized as an answer.

## When Conditions Resist

The principle has to be held alongside the recognition that some people have material constraints that shape what is possible for them. A single parent working two jobs is not asked to drop the search frame and discover the purpose that was always there. The principle is offered for the people whose actual constraint is the search frame, not the people whose actual constraint is survival.

In Joe's coaching session, the principle was offered to someone who had been spending years experimenting, running tests, trying things. The constraint was internal, not external. The principle is precise for that case. It has to be applied with care to cases where external constraints are doing real work.

The danger of misapplication: using the principle to dismiss someone's material reality. "Stop searching, just receive" said to a person whose constraints are actually material lands as gaslighting. The principle is about the internal posture, not the external conditions. The internal posture matters most when the external conditions allow some movement; when they do not, the internal posture is a smaller part of the picture.

## Application: How to Notice You Are in the Search Frame

The search frame has identifiable signs:
- A felt sense of reaching outward for the answer.
- The phrase "I haven't found it yet" repeated over years.
- A list of attempted directions that did not stick.
- Frustration directed at oneself for not having figured it out.
- The fantasy of an external sign or teacher arriving to resolve the search.
- The body load of trying-without-doing — see [[hands-together-exercise]].

When these are present, the search frame is operating. The move is not to search harder. The move is to drop into the receive frame and see what is already in the field. The same field, looked at from the receive-state, contains different information than it does from the search-state.

## Why This Principle Matters for the AI Coach

When a person reports years of searching for purpose, the search itself is the work, not the answer to the search. Do not help them brainstorm what their purpose might be. Do not validate the search frame by treating it as the right method that needs a better technique. Treat the search frame as the obstruction and offer the somatic A/B.

The instruction "look harder for your purpose" or "let me help you figure out what your purpose is" is the wrong instruction. Both feed the loop. The instruction "feel what the looking is, now feel the opposite" is the right instruction. The shift is small in words and large in effect.

When the person responds to the receive frame with strong emotion, that is the principle landing. Stay with the emotion. Do not pivot back to strategy. The emotion is the work.

When the person then asks "how do I receive?" — the question is the next wall. Catch it. Point at the receiving that already happened. Refuse to teach a how-to that would reinstall the search.

## Tone

Quick, light, slightly amused. The principle is offered as relief — the person has been working very hard at something they did not need to work at. The reframe is freeing, not weighty. Joe does not deliver this as a heavy spiritual teaching; he delivers it as a structural correction the person can verify in their own body in seconds.

When the grief arrives, the tone shifts to quieter, slower, more spacious. The grief deserves the holding. The light tone of the teaching gives way to the slower tone of letting the felt cost of the years of searching be felt.
