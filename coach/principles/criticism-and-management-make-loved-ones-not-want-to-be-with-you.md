# Criticism and Management Are Why Your Loved Ones Don't Want to Be With You

## The Principle

The reason your loved ones do not want to be around you, when they do not, is mechanical. They do not stop wanting to be around you because love wore out. Love is wired in. The wanting to be in connection with the people we are bonded to is built into the species. Something has to be added to that wanting before it stops working. What gets added is criticism and management.

People do not want to come home to be told they are doing it wrong. People do not want to come home to be checked on. People do not want to come home to be the project of someone else's anxiety about how they should be. When the home runs on criticism and management, the people in that home stop wanting to come home, regardless of how much love is there.

"If you go into like the homes of most of my daughter's friends, the parents are telling them how to wash the dishes. They're telling them what to wear. Are you doing your homework? Are you — they're like getting checked on. They're getting managed. Every single one of those things is saying, I don't trust you to take care of yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

The principle is symmetric: it runs from parent to child, from child to parent, from partner to partner, from friend to friend. Wherever the relationship is conducted through correction and control, the wanting-to-be-there drops. The wanting was structural. The criticism and management are the structural reason for its departure.

## The Universal Want

The teaching rests on a claim about what people structurally want from intimate relationships:

"It is as inherent in us as eating, as breathing. It is part of our essence. It's our nature. And nobody wants that to come with a whole bunch of judgment and criticism and management." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

"We all really, really want the same thing. We all want to be in relationships where we are loved and supported, and we do our best work. We thrive the most when we are in loving, supportive relationships, not when we're in relationships that are criticizing us." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

This grounds the principle. The desire for connection with family is not negotiable; it is part of the wiring. What is variable is whether the connection is being delivered through love or through management. The first version draws people in. The second version drives them out. The wanting was never the problem.

## What Counts as Management

"Management" in this principle is not metaphorical. It is the steady stream of small corrections, instructions, check-ins, and adjustments that constitute most of the conversation in many homes:

- Telling the kid how to wash the dishes.
- Telling them what to wear.
- Asking if they did their homework.
- Reminding them to do the thing they were going to do anyway.
- Correcting their pronunciation, their posture, their tone.
- Asking the adult child why they have not gotten married yet, why they have not had kids, why they are still doing that job.
- The mother who will not tell anyone her daughter is pregnant because the daughter is not married and the mother feels it reflects poorly on her.

Each one of these, in isolation, looks small. The parent doing it does not register the impact. The cumulative message to the recipient is the same regardless of the surface variation: *I do not trust you to be who you are. I need you to be different. The way you are is not okay.*

The principle includes the structural reading of what management transmits: "Every single one of those things is saying, I don't trust you to take care of yourself."

## What Counts as Criticism

Criticism, in this principle, is not necessarily harsh or angry. The passive-aggressive variant counts. The "just a comment" comment counts. The disappointed sigh counts. The face you make when they tell you what they did counts. The silence that follows the announcement they were excited about counts.

The criticism the recipient registers is not the verbal content; it is the felt sense of being not-okay-as-you-are. Passive-aggressive criticism often does more damage than direct criticism because it cannot be addressed, only absorbed. See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] for what is happening on the criticizing side; this principle covers what the criticism does to the relationship.

## The Felt Experience on the Receiving End

The principle becomes useful when the felt experience on the receiving end is named:

- You dread the visit before it happens.
- You count the hours until you can leave.
- You feel yourself getting smaller as you walk in the door.
- You rehearse how to deflect the questions you know are coming.
- You decide what to wear based on what they will not criticize.
- You filter what you say about your life to minimize the openings for comment.
- You feel guilty for not wanting to come, and the guilt does not make you want to come more, it makes you want to come less.

The recipient often cannot name what is wrong because they have been receiving it their whole life. They register it as "I just don't want to go home" without locating the source. The principle gives them the source.

## Joe's Concrete Counter-Example: The Gay Son and the Catholic Dad

A demonstration of what the alternative produces:

"I had a friend, he was — I lived in LA at the time. He was like 24 years old, born and raised Catholic. Dad was a Catholic school teacher, and this man realized that he was gay, or admitted to himself he was gay. And I got to be there for the whole thing, and he finally decided he was going to go and tell his parents. So he tells his parents, and he comes back, and he's telling me this story about how his dad just started bawling when he told him that he was gay. And I was like, oh man, that's horrible. And he's like, no, it was fantastic. I was like, what? He's like, yeah, he cried, and then he looked at me and he said, 'I can't be Catholic anymore. Like, I know that you wouldn't choose to be gay, and I'm not allowed to — like, then you have to go to hell according to my religion, and I can't abide by a religion where you would go to hell. Like, there must be something wrong.' Guess what? That guy wants to go home, see his parents. He doesn't feel judged. He doesn't feel managed." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

See [[catholic-dad-gay-son-story]] for the example article.

The father in this story chose his actual son over his inherited framework for what the son should be. The son's experience of that choice is the principle in operation: the son wants to come home because the father is not managing or criticizing who he is. The Catholicism was the framework that would have done the criticizing; the father let go of the framework rather than let it criticize his son.

This is the available pole. Most parents do not realize they have a choice between criticizing their kid and being able to be with their kid. The story makes the choice visible.

## Joe's Concrete Pro-Example: The Pregnant Friend and the Hiding Mother

The opposite case:

"I'm just walking today with somebody who I know well, and she's pregnant. And she was telling me how her mom didn't share with any of the family that she was pregnant. And she asked many times, why aren't you sharing anything? And she had a whole bunch of excuses. But at the end of the day, most likely, it was that she's not married. She's been living with a beautiful man. They're great, but they've chosen not to get married. And the mom is not happy with that, feels like it reflects poorly on her. You can imagine this woman doesn't particularly want to go home because when she goes home, she's going to be criticized. She's going to be judged. She's going to be managed. I need you to be X, Y, and Z so that I look good, so that I can feel good, so that I get my way." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

See [[pregnant-daughter-hidden-by-unmarried-mother]] for the example article.

The pattern in this case: the daughter is doing her life. The mother cannot tolerate the version of her daughter's life that does not match what the mother needs the daughter to be (for the mother's social image, for the mother's comfort, for the mother's reputation). The mother manages the daughter's existence by trying to hide it. The daughter feels managed, judged, and unwelcome — so she does not want to come home. The mother's discomfort with her own social standing has become a force pushing her actual daughter away.

## The Children's Side: What Suffocation Produces in Teens

The principle has a specific operation in adolescence:

"When you suffocate a kid like that, then the only way that they can get a sense of self is to rebel against you." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

The teenager needs to become themselves. If the parent is constantly managing them — telling them how to be, correcting their attempts, instructing them on the basics — then any version of self the teenager can produce is going to be in contradiction to the parent. The rebellion is not the teenager's character. The rebellion is the structural consequence of being managed.

See [[suffocating-kids-forces-rebellion-as-only-self-route]] for the full treatment.

This produces the cultural script "teenagers will hate their parents." The script is downstream of the management. Parents who do not manage do not get the rebellion. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]] for the related principle.

## The Reverse Direction: When the Adult Child Draws the Line

The principle gives the adult child a name for what they have been experiencing. Once the name is available, the boundary becomes drawable.

Joe's own example: he called his father and named the pattern.

"I just, I saw it. I just recognized it. And I called up my dad and I said, 'You know, every time I come home, you criticize me. You tell me what I'm doing wrong. You tell me what I need to do to be good enough. And I wouldn't hang out with anybody who does that to me. So I'm just not going to hang out with you if you're going to do that. But I love you and I really want to hang out with you. So just let me know when you're prepared to hang out with me. And not judge me, not criticize me, not try to manage me.'" -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

"One year passed, two years passed, but he finally called and I came to his house and it was wonderful to be with him in a way where we could just be together. And he wasn't telling me I had to be different. He wasn't telling me that I had to change. It wasn't this constant state of criticism." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

See [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]] and [[joes-call-with-dad-about-criticism]] for the example articles. The boundary works because it operates on the principle directly: I am not going to participate in a relationship structured around being criticized. The boundary names what the relationship is doing and declines to participate in that version of it.

## The Self-Awareness Move: The Parent Names the Concern Without Managing

For parents who recognize they are doing this and want to stop, Joe offers the alternative move he uses with his own kids:

"With my girls, if there's something that I feel like, oh my gosh, I need to manage this thing, I'll talk to them about that. I'll just say, 'Hey, I'm concerned about this thing and I don't want to manage you because managing you sucks.'" -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

See [[name-your-concern-rather-than-manage-them]] for the move.

This is the structural alternative to management. The parent has the concern. The parent says they have the concern. The parent does not turn the concern into instructions, corrections, or rules for the kid. The concern is the parent's, named honestly. What the kid does with it is the kid's choice.

## The Mutual Recognition: "Nagging Sucks"

The principle is verified empirically in the relationship itself. When Joe named it to his daughters at 6-7 years old, they confirmed it:

"I sat down and I was like, 'I hate nagging you. It sucks. It feels like crap in my system to nag you.' And they were like, 'Yeah, it feels like crap to be nagged.' I was like, 'Yeah, I still have a memory of being nagged by my mom. I'm like, yeah.' And we'd literally, even at that young age, [say] like, 'How do we live this life together without me nagging you, but me still teaching you what I need to teach you?'" -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

See [[joe-and-daughters-on-nagging]] for the example.

Both ends of the management dynamic feel bad. The nagger does not enjoy nagging. The nagged does not enjoy being nagged. The dynamic persists because it has not been examined, not because anyone in it likes it. Once both sides name the felt cost, the dynamic can be redesigned together.

## What This Principle Is Not Saying

Not that parents should never give input. The teenage daughter still needed to be taught some things. The principle is about the difference between teaching and managing, between concern-honestly-named and constant-correction-of-the-person.

Not that the only reason adult children avoid family is because of criticism. There are many reasons. The principle is about the specific dynamic where the criticism and management are the operative cause, which is a substantial fraction of the cases.

Not that the answer to criticism in the family is to immediately cut off the family. The boundary Joe drew was a high-leverage move that worked in his case. It worked partly because he was willing to wait years. The principle is descriptive of the dynamic; the response to it depends on the specific situation. See [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]] for one downstream concern; see [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] for another.

Not that the criticizing parent is a bad person. The criticism is usually inherited, modeled, or driven by the parent's own unfelt material. See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]]. The principle names what the criticism does to the relationship; addressing the source is separate work.

## Tone

Direct, observational, slightly indignant on behalf of the people on the receiving end. Joe is naming a dynamic that is treated as normal in many homes and pointing out that it is the active cause of the relational distance people lament. The tone has the quality of "this isn't a mystery — here is what is happening." It carries the conviction of someone who has done the work to address it on both ends (with his own parents, with his own kids) and watched it work.
