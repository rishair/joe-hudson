# We Follow Leadership, We Do Not Follow Blame

## The Principle

In a healthy organization, the response to "something is wrong" is not validation of the complaint. The response is "great, go lead on it." The person who identifies the problem and proposes to fix it gets followed. The person who identifies the problem and waits for someone else to fix it does not. The structural rule is simple: leadership moves are rewarded; blame moves are not.

"In our organization, that generally just does not get rewarded at all. Because we follow leadership. We do not follow blame. So if that person was like, that's wrong and I'm going to go fix it, fantastic. Go, go fix. We'll all follow you." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The principle converts every problem-identification into a leadership opportunity. If the problem is real, the person who saw it is the natural candidate to lead on fixing it — they already have the diagnostic. If the person will not take the leadership move, the organization treats that as data about the problem (often the complaint was not really about fixing the thing) and does not allocate resources to the complaint.

## What The Rule Reorganizes

The rule has structural consequences for the culture:

1. **Problems get solved closer to where they are identified.** The person who notices the issue often has the cleanest read on it. Tying the reward to "noticing AND leading" means the diagnostic energy stays paired with the execution energy. The organization does not lose the leverage of the proximate noticer by routing the work elsewhere.

2. **Politics decreases.** Most workplace politics is fueled by blame that does not lead to action — complaints that build coalitions of grievance instead of producing change. The rule removes the social reward for blame-without-leadership. The political moves lose their fuel.

3. **The capable-but-frustrated person finds their lever.** People who see real problems but feel stuck waiting for permission discover that the permission was always available — they just needed to take the leadership move. Their frustration converts to agency.

4. **The complainer who is not actually trying to fix the thing gets surfaced.** The blame frame allows people to look productive by naming problems they have no intention of working on. The rule strips the productive appearance. Either the person leads or they let go of the complaint. Either is more honest than the chronic name-without-act pattern.

## The Conversion Mechanism

The rule operates as a conversion at the moment of complaint. When someone names a problem, the leader's response is to ask: "What do you want to do about it?" Not as a deflection. As an invitation. The follow-on is: if the person has a move, the organization backs it. If the person does not have a move, the leadership opportunity is open to whoever does want to take it.

This is structurally identical to [[turn-blame-into-wants]] at the individual level. The blame impulse is caught and converted to a leadership move (or a clearly-stated want). The conversion is the work. The rule provides the organizational scaffolding for the conversion to happen consistently.

## What This Is Not

**Not "don't complain."** Complaints are useful diagnostic data. The principle is about what happens after the complaint. The complaint is welcomed. The blame frame (this is wrong and someone should do something about it) is gently dissolved into a leadership frame (this is wrong and I am the candidate to lead on it, or I am not, and either is fine).

**Not punishment for blamers.** The rule does not require punishment. It just removes the social and structural reward. The blamer does not get the audience, the coalition, or the meeting time. They are not attacked — they are just not amplified.

**Not "every problem requires the noticer to fix it."** The noticer is the natural candidate. They are not the only candidate. If the noticer cannot lead on it for legitimate reasons (it is outside their domain, they lack the authority, the fix is genuinely outside their capacity), the leadership opportunity goes to whoever can take it. The rule is about converting the noticing into leadership-allocation, not about forcing the noticer to be the leader.

## The Connection To Responsibility-And-Authority

The rule works only when responsibility and authority are coupled in the organization's design. If the person who takes the leadership move gets the responsibility but not the authority to execute, the rule produces resentment instead of conversion. The person leads on a problem and then cannot get the resources, sign-offs, or backing to actually fix it.

"In our company, we even give the authority that way, which is rare in a company. We actually say if you're responsible, you get the authority." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The two principles are paired. Following leadership requires that the leadership move can actually be executed. Otherwise the rule installs cynicism: "they said go fix it but then I couldn't." See [[responsibility-and-authority-go-together]].

## The Connection To Distributed Leadership

The rule operationalizes [[leadership-emerges-from-confident-direction]] at the organizational level. The baboon study shows that any member of the group can lead the moment they move with directness, confidence, and a reasonable clip. The rule designs the organization to recognize and follow that emergent leadership rather than waiting for it to come from the formal hierarchy.

The result is a more decentralized organization that nonetheless has clear leadership — the leadership just rotates situationally to whoever earned it in the moment. The formal authority still holds the container (see [[lead-and-follow]] and [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]]), but the moment-to-moment leadership is allocated by the rule.

## The Joe Example: The Mun Live Podcast Decision

Joe's example of the rule operating: Mun, his producer, had conviction about doing live podcasts. Joe did not want to do it. Joe followed her anyway — because she had taken the leadership move and the organization follows leadership. The rule trumped Joe's preference because Joe's preference, in that moment, was just a preference, not a leadership move on the same question.

"I wanted to follow Mun. I saw her conviction. It was really important for me to show everybody in the company my leadership in that moment was to show everybody in the company that we're going to follow your conviction as long as it doesn't go against our principles, as long as it's within the context of what we can afford. We're going to follow your conviction." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The example shows what the rule looks like from the formal leader's side. Following Mun was itself a leadership move by Joe — it modeled the rule, it backed Mun's authority over her domain, and it demonstrated that leadership in the company was actually distributed rather than nominally distributed. The rule is upheld both by the people who take leadership moves and by the formal leaders who consistently follow those moves.

## The Constraints

The rule has constraints that the example articulates:
- The leadership move cannot violate the organization's principles
- The leadership move has to fit within the resources the organization can afford
- The leadership move has to be in a domain where the person has or can plausibly hold the authority

Within those constraints, the rule applies broadly. Outside those constraints, the constraint itself is the leadership move from the formal leader — naming the boundary that the proposed move is testing.

## When To Use This Principle In Coaching

For founders and leaders designing culture, the rule is one of the highest-leverage structural choices they can make. Once installed and consistently applied, it produces compounding effects: politics decreases, agency increases, problems get solved closer to source, the formal leader's bandwidth is preserved.

For organizational members who have been blaming and getting nowhere, the rule provides the diagnostic: "they are not following my complaints because complaints are not the move. The leadership move would be followed. What is the leadership move?"

## Tone

Joe describes this as a practiced design choice in his own company, not as universal prescription. The tone is operational rather than aspirational: this is how it actually runs, this is the consequence, this is the design that supports it. The teaching lands as a structural option that other organizations can adopt, not as a moral imperative that they must.
