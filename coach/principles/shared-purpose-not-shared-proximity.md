# Shared Purpose, Not Shared Proximity

## The Principle

A group becomes a [[support-system]] only when it has a shared, conscious, explicit purpose. Proximity alone does not produce a support system. The same coworkers, the same friend group, the same neighbors can be in each other's presence for years without ever forming one, because the purpose was never named.

"The most important thing that I'm aware of is being conscious of the purpose of the support system. So is this support system all designed to help us have great careers? Or is this support system designed to help us all become great parents?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Build A Great Support System

The diagnostic for whether you have a support system: can you state what it is for? If the answer is "we hang out" or "we're friends" or "we work together," you do not have a support system. You have a group of people in proximity, which is a different thing.

## Why Purpose Is the Filter

The purpose does three things at once:

**It selects.** People who share the purpose are drawn in. People who do not are filtered out. Without the filter, the group accumulates members for reasons unrelated to its function — convenience, social ambition, loneliness — and the function erodes.

**It coheres.** Members in the same purpose can read each other's struggles correctly. A leadership-development support system can hear what a member is wrestling with, because every member is in that wrestling. A general-purpose friend group cannot, because the members are working on different things or nothing.

**It permits hard things.** With a clear purpose, members can challenge each other, push each other, hold each other accountable, because there is a shared aim the challenge serves. Without the purpose, the same challenge feels intrusive.

## The Membership Implication: Decline High-Status Misfits

The hard test of this principle: a high-status person who does not share the purpose should not be in the group, regardless of who they are.

"I don't care if Elon Musk wanted to join that group, unless he had that purpose, he shouldn't be in the group." -- Joe Hudson

The pull to admit high-status members for reasons of prestige, access, or excitement is real. The cost of admitting them is the dilution of the purpose. Once the purpose drifts, the group drifts toward whatever the high-status member happens to want, or toward generic peer-group dynamics. The thing that made it work stops working.

The same logic applies to admitting friends, partners of members, or people the group otherwise likes. The question is not "do we like them?" The question is "are they here for this purpose?"

## Specificity, Not Generality

The purposes that work are specific enough to filter. "Leaders who want to be great leaders" is too general — every leader thinks they want this. "Leaders who want to lead in a way that helps them transform, who want to empower others to be more human inside their company, who treat leadership as a way to work on themselves rather than burn themselves out" is specific enough to filter.

The Council's purpose statement Joe articulates is dense by design:

"I wanna be a leader in a way that helps me transform, helps me on my journey of self-development. I wanna be a leader in a way that empowers other people. I wanna be a leader that helps people be more human, not less human by telling them to put half of their self in the door and not bring it into work. I want to be a leader that people feel like they're becoming better people because they're in a team that I'm leading."

Each clause filters. A leader who is reading the work as a personal-development practice will recognize themselves. A leader who is using leadership skills tactically will not.

## Implicit Purpose Is Still Purpose

Some support systems function with implicit rather than explicit purpose, and they still work because the implicit purpose is real and shared by the members.

Joe's Friday night hike with a group of guys: the implicit purpose is "we're gonna laugh and express our truth on this hike." Nobody wrote it down. Everyone knows it. The filter for new members is "do we like you, do you have the right consciousness?" Implicit, but functional.

This works when the group is small, the purpose is light, and the membership is reasonably stable. For larger groups, higher stakes, or groups that aspire to last, explicit purpose becomes necessary. The implicit version drifts as members change or as life pressures grow.

The boundary: do not assume because some support systems run on implicit purpose that all can. A business support system, a couples-and-parents support system, a leadership cohort — these need explicit purpose. The cost of leaving it implicit is that the drift happens slowly and is hard to see until the group is no longer functional.

## When the Purpose Stops Matching the Members

Purposes can outgrow groups. A founding purpose that fit the original members may no longer fit them five years later. The right response is not to ignore the drift; it is to renegotiate the purpose explicitly or to dissolve the group with honor.

A common failure mode: the group continues meeting because the friendships are now real, while the original purpose has eroded. The group becomes a friendship circle pretending to be a support system. Members feel a slow drain because their actual development needs are not being met, but they cannot name what is wrong because the relationships are warm. The way out is to make the drift visible: what is this group actually for now? Is that what we still want? See [[agreements-renegotiate-as-you-change]] for the broader pattern.

## The Question for an Existing Group

If you are inside a group and unsure whether it is a support system, the diagnostic question is direct: what is the purpose? If members give different answers, the group has not consciously named it. The naming itself is often the upgrade. Once the purpose is named, members who do not share it can leave with grace, members who do share it can engage more deeply, and new members can be evaluated against an actual standard.

The same question can be asked of every group in your life: family, work team, friend group, romantic partnership, neighborhood. Some have a purpose that nobody has named but that everyone is operating from; some have no purpose; some had a purpose that has eroded. The audit is useful because it surfaces which groups are doing what work in your life and which are not doing the work you thought they were.

## What This Implies About Building a Support System

The first design move is not to find people. It is to articulate the purpose. Until the purpose is articulable, the search for people is unanchored. With a clear purpose stated, the people who fit the purpose start to become recognizable, and they recognize you.

"It's gonna feel awkward, that's because you're not completely okay with it yet. So the more you're okay with it, the easier it is to say it. The easier it is to say it, the more likely you're going to find the people who want you to, who want to join you in that." -- Joe Hudson

The articulation work is internal first. Then external. Then structural.

- When someone is trying to build a community and skipping the purpose step
- When someone is admitting members to a support system based on liking them rather than the purpose
- When a group that used to work is no longer working and no one can name why

## Tone

Direct, structural. Joe states this principle matter-of-factly. The tone is not soft because the principle does soft work for the listener: it gives them a way to look at their existing groups and see clearly which are doing what they think they are doing.
