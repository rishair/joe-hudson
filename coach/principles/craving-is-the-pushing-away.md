# Craving Is the Pushing Away

## The Principle

Craving is not just attachment to something. Craving is the simultaneous pushing away of it. The two are the same nervous-system motion. The hand that grips also extends the thing being gripped beyond reach.

"Everything we crave, we push away at the same time. Craving is the pushing away." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Trust Myself?

This is sharper than the common Buddhist framing of craving as "wanting that produces suffering." The common framing treats craving and aversion as opposed motions: we crave good things and avoid bad things and both produce suffering. This principle says something more specific: craving and aversion are not separate. To crave is to push away. The gripping is itself the rejection.

## Why This Is True

When the system grips an object as needed -- this person, this outcome, this state -- it does so by separating itself from the object. "I need this" creates the I and the it as distinct, with a gap between them. The gap is the craving. Without the gap there is nothing to be wanted. The wanting is the gap.

So to crave peace is to create the gap that means peace is absent. The craving itself is what makes peace not available. The system that is grasping for peace cannot be at peace, because the grasping is the not-being-at-peace.

This is why the spiritual injunction "stop seeking and you will find" is structurally true rather than merely paradoxical. The seeking is the not-finding. The seeking establishes the gap that makes finding impossible. Dropping the seeking dissolves the gap, and what was being sought turns out to have been here.

## The Application to Peace

The most common live demonstration of this principle is around peace itself. A person craves peace. They meditate to find it. They notice an agenda for it. They try to drop the agenda. They notice an agenda about dropping the agenda. The hall of mirrors continues.

What's happening: the craving for peace is the active pushing-away of peace. Every move toward peace contains the move away from it. The person can sit for years in this loop, treating it as a meditation problem to solve.

The resolution is not better technique. The resolution is recognizing that the craving is the absence. When the craving relaxes -- when the person stops looking for peace -- peace is what was here the whole time. The craving was the only thing covering it.

"It's funny, it's like the thing I crave. And when I meditate, there's this -- everything we crave, we push away. There's a subtle agenda for peace, but knowing that if I orient toward peace, I won't find it because it'll be a want then." -- Person in How Do I Trust Myself?

The person in the session names the structure mid-realization. Joe doesn't add anything. He just confirms: "Right. Everything we crave, we're pushing away at the same time."

## The Application to Self-Trust

The principle also applies to the trust frame. The person who asks "how do I trust myself?" is craving self-trust. The craving creates the gap that means self-trust is not present. The grasping is the not-having. See [[how-do-i-trust-myself]] and [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]].

The same shape applies to enlightenment, awakening, love, healing, transformation. Any state that is craved is pushed away by the craving. The work is not to find better strategies for getting to the state. The work is to see that the seeking is the not-having and to relax the grasping. What remains is what was always present.

This connects to [[no-finish-line]]: the arrival fantasy is the structural form of craving. Believing there is a place to get to is the move that makes "here" not enough.

## The Application to Relationships

The same structure applies in relationships. If a person has a strong desire for something within their relationship -- their partner's attention, a certain kind of intimacy, a particular validation -- and keeps trying to get it but cannot, the inability to receive is structural rather than circumstantial.

"If you find yourself in a relationship where you have this strong desire for something and you keep on trying to get it and you can't get it, then it's really great to acknowledge that you're actually pushing it away, which means subconsciously you're not ready for it, don't want it, haven't admitted what it's going to do to your identity, some version of that." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The diagnostic is the failure-to-receive. The system says "I want this so badly and I cannot get it here." The diagnostic frame: you have not yet accepted your desire for it, or you have not yet admitted what receiving it would do to your identity. The wanting itself contains a not-wanting that holds the thing away.

The same is true of larger life cravings -- the partner, the recognition, the result -- that a person reports being unable to attain despite trying. The trying is what makes the attainment impossible. The work is not better trying; it is the acknowledgment of the subconscious half of the move.

This is also why "jealousy" often functions as a craving diagnostic. The intense desire that a partner not do X frequently reveals a desire for X that the person has not admitted to themselves. The craving for X (in a form the person cannot accept) is the same motion as the avoidance of X (in the partner's possible doing of it).

## The Resolution Is Not Indifference

The principle is not "stop wanting." Wanting and craving are different. Wanting is the felt direction of the system toward what it is alive to. Craving is wanting plus the contraction that "I have to have this or I'm not okay." See [[wanting-vs-craving]] for the full distinction.

The work is not to suppress desire. The work is to feel the wanting without adding the gripping. The wanting itself is pleasant. The craving is painful. Dropping the craving allows the wanting to be fully felt. The wanting then often pulls the person toward what they actually want, with less friction than the craving was producing.

This is also the distinction between [[the-want]] as a felt experience and craving as a held position. Wanting is alive. Craving is gripped.

## The Bidirectional Form

The principle has a less-noticed second half: everything we push away, we crave. The aversion is the grasping running in reverse. To push something away is to keep returning to it as something-to-be-pushed-away. The pushing-away keeps the object in the field as relevant.

This is the structural mechanism underneath [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The thing we fear we are subtly inviting, because the fear is the active relationship to the thing. The fear holds the thing in place as something-to-be-feared. The aversion is what gives the feared thing its grip on us.

So craving and aversion are not opposites. They are the same motion seen from two sides. The craved object and the avoided object are both being held in the field by the relationship the system has to them. Dropping the relationship -- letting the object be without the grip -- dissolves the dynamic.

## The Resistance Connection

The principle is a special case of [[resistance]]. Resistance is the active rejection of what is. Craving is resistance to the absence of what is wanted. Aversion is resistance to the presence of what is not wanted. Both are forms of resistance. The general principle holds: the resistance is the friction, not the object.

This is why [[love-is-the-best-change-agent]] is the resolution to craving. Loving what is -- including the absence of what is craved -- is what dissolves the craving. Fighting the craving with discipline produces more craving. Welcoming the craving as a feeling, and welcoming what is present in its absence, is what makes craving fluid.

## What This Looks Like in Coaching

When a person describes craving something -- a state, an outcome, a relationship, a feeling -- the coaching move is not to help them get it. It is to point out the structure: the craving is the pushing-away of the thing. The grasping is the gap.

This is often disorienting. The person believes they are working toward the thing. To be told the working-toward is the keeping-it-away seems contradictory at first. Then it lands. Then there is a small body relaxation and often a laugh. The grip releases. What was being sought turns out to have been close by all along, but obscured by the seeking.

The instruction is then: "What is here when you stop seeking it?" Usually what is here is closer to what they were seeking than they expected.

## Tone

Delivered with quiet certainty. The principle is not a paradox to puzzle over; it is a structural fact about the nervous system. Joe states it the way he would state any other observation about how the mechanism works. The slight smile is there because the trick is genuinely funny: the thing being sought is being kept away by the seeking.
