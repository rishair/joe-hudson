# It Is Expensive To Be Poor (When You Are Fighting Abundance)

## The Principle

Avoiding money does not save energy. It spends a different currency. When a person rejects money on principle, what they actually do is route their life through time- and energy-expensive workarounds: cajoling, scavenging, trading favors, taking long routes, doing work they do not want to do, in order to obtain what money would have obtained directly. The savings on the bank account ledger gets paid for by withdrawals on the life-force ledger.

"It's really expensive to be poor... I was really good at being poor. I figured out a way to do a whole bunch of things with no money. You know, I found my way down the Grand Canyon on a 14-day trip and I found a way to buy musical equipment when I wanted to do that. And I found ways to do stuff, but it took a lot of time. It took a lot of time." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

The principle is not "you should make money." It is that the rejection of money has a hidden price tag, and the price tag is usually paid in the form of life energy spent doing things the person does not actually want to do.

## What "Fighting Abundance" Means Here

This principle applies specifically when the poverty is a stance, not a structural circumstance. A person who has no money because of the structure of their life is in a different situation than a person who is actively rejecting money or refusing to engage with it. The principle addresses the second case.

The stance of fighting abundance produces a specific architecture: every needed thing requires a workaround because the direct route (money) is morally off-limits. The workarounds compound. Each one takes time, takes energy, takes attention. The person spends years doing work they do not love so that they can avoid earning money doing work they would love. The math gets visible only in retrospect.

"It was like, oh, I'm spending a lot of money or a lot of time to be poor. And so it was just an interesting recognition that same time that it was like, oh, it's expensive to be poor if you're fighting against abundance, which is what I was doing." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

## The Mechanism

Joe lived this for close to a decade. He worked one week per month to earn just enough to pay bills, went into debt to meditate, found elaborate ways to obtain what he needed (musical equipment, a 14-day Grand Canyon trip) without engaging with money directly. The workarounds worked. They also cost enormous time. He was spending his life energy in a way that was not aligned with who he was, in service of avoiding a thing (money) that he had decided was bad.

The currency exchange that was happening: time and energy being traded for the maintenance of a moral position about money. The position itself was the surrogate for an unresolved relationship with his father. See [[money-as-surrogate]] and [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]]. The bill for that surrogate was being paid in lived time.

"Instead of, oh, I'm gonna do shit that I really love, make money so that I can buy other stuff that I really want to do, I would do a whole bunch of shit to like cajole my way into a situation, none of which I wanted to do, none of which was creating the reality I wanted to see on Earth so that I could get down the Grand Canyon, you know. And so, so that's what I mean by expensive. It was like, it meant that my -- I was spending my energy by avoiding money. I was spending my energy in a way that wasn't fully aligned with who I was." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

## The Diagnostic Question

For a coachee who is in active rejection of money, the question that surfaces the principle: what are you spending instead? If the answer is time, energy, attention, work-you-do-not-love, relationships-of-convenience -- the cost is being paid. The bank account is not the only ledger.

The follow-up question: is the spend aligned with who you are? When the workarounds are themselves the things you would have chosen, the rejection of money is genuine alignment. When the workarounds are things you would not have chosen, the rejection of money has become a tax on your life that you are paying to maintain a stance.

## What the Principle Does Not Say

It does not say money is the answer. It does not say wanting money is virtuous. It does not say poverty is moral failure. It does not collapse into [[if-i-had-more-money]].

It also does not contradict the principle that some people do best when they live with less. The relevant variable is alignment. A person who has chosen a low-money life because that life is what they actually want is not fighting abundance. A person who has chosen a low-money life because money is morally suspect, while wanting things money would obtain, is fighting abundance and paying for it.

## What This Reveals About the Stance

When the principle lands for a person, it often surfaces that the stance against money was less about ethics and more about avoidance of an internal conflict. The person was using poverty as a way to not have to face what money meant to them. The cost of that avoidance, once seen, often dissolves the stance. Not because money becomes good but because money becomes neutral. See [[money-is-impersonal]].

The shift Joe describes after this recognition: he stopped using time and energy to be poor, and the workarounds stopped being needed. The alignment with what he actually wanted produced both money and the freedom to do the things he had been doing the hard way.

## Tone

Direct, slightly rueful. Joe is speaking from his own ten years of paying this cost. There is no judgment of the past stance, but there is clarity that it was costly in a way he did not see at the time. The principle is delivered as recognition, not as instruction.
