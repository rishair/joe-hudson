# You Can't Chase Someone Into Loving You

## The Principle

If the other person has decided they are done -- with the relationship, with the partnership, with the work -- no amount of chasing can convert that decision into a yes. The chasing does not produce love. It produces distance.

"If the person you're with doesn't want to work on the relationship, then grieve. Then you're not— nothing's going to happen. You can't chase somebody into loving you. It doesn't work that way." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The principle has two halves. The first half is mechanical: the chasing does not work as a mechanism. The second half is procedural: once the chasing has been tried and the answer is clear, the next move is grief. Not more chasing. Not more strategy. Grief.

## Why the Chasing Fails

Love that arrives in response to pursuit is not the love the chaser wants. The chaser wants to be chosen freely. What pursuit produces, at best, is reluctant continuation. The chosen-freely thing cannot be coerced into being. Pursuit is the wrong instrument for the thing being sought.

There is also a structural piece. The partner who is being chased experiences the chasing as pressure. Pressure activates the pull-away response. The harder the chaser chases, the more the chased pulls back. See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]] for the full pattern. The chaser, trying to close the gap, is the proximate cause of the gap widening.

## The Specific Trigger for the Teaching

Joe offers this principle in answer to a specific question: what to do when a marriage is at the brink of dying and one partner wants to save it. The answer includes the [[three-things-to-save-a-marriage-from-disdain]] framework (speak your truth, listen with VIEW, see them without trying to change them), and then this principle as the necessary completion of the framework.

The framework is offered to the partner who is willing to do the work. The principle is the limit on what the framework can accomplish. The work-the-relationship moves require both partners to be in the work. If only one partner is, the most the other partner can do is their internal work and then meet the situation as it is.

"Usually the person who would ask that question is the person who chases the love. And so you can't chase somebody into loving you. You can stop being reactive, but if they've given up, that's their business. You can respect them enough to just let them have that moment." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The line "respect them enough to just let them have that moment" is the soft form. The respect is the move. Allowing the partner to have their no -- without one more attempt to convert it into a yes -- is the unilateral move available when the chasing has run its course.

## The Co-Founder Example

The same principle works outside of romantic relationships. Joe offers a recent example.

"I had a phone call just yesterday from a person who's running a business with two co-founders, and at some point she was talking about how ways she could fight, ways that she could leave, all these things. And I just said to her, do your partners want to work on it? And she was, nope. I'm like, okay, so what does that tell you? And she was like, yeah, it means that like we have to not work together. Just that quickly." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The question "do they want to work on it?" cuts through the strategizing. The strategy options (fight harder, leave, restructure) only matter if the other party is in the work. If they are not, none of the strategy options will produce what is being sought. The question that organizes the situation is whether the other party is opting in, and if not, the situation has already concluded.

This generalizes. Marriages, business partnerships, friendships, working relationships -- the same principle applies. The other party's willingness to work on it is the precondition for any of the work-on-it moves. Absent that willingness, the work shifts from the relationship to the grief.

## Who This Principle Lands For

Joe is specific about who is most likely to need this principle. The person asking the question is usually the chaser. The chaser has, by the time they are asking, already tried many strategies. The strategies have not worked. The question they are asking ("how do I save this?") is itself a chasing move dressed as inquiry.

The principle is not an answer to the question they asked. It is a reframe of the question. The honest answer to "how do I make them love me again?" is: you can't, and the part of you that is still trying to needs to grieve so that you can stop being available to a no.

## Stop Being Reactive

The carve-out matters. The principle does not say "do nothing." It says "you can stop being reactive." The reactivity is the chasing. The non-reactivity is being present to your own state without spending the energy on producing a change in them.

"You can stop being reactive, but if they've given up, that's, that's their business." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The non-reactive presence sometimes shifts the dynamic, because the pressure has been removed. The shift is not the point. The point is that the chaser stops contorting themselves around the partner's decision. The partner is allowed to be where they are. The chaser stops trying to be different in order to produce a different result in the partner.

If the partner shifts in response to the non-reactivity, that is a real shift. If they do not, the chaser has at least returned to themselves, which is the only ground from which any further work becomes possible.

## What Grief Looks Like Here

Once the chasing has been recognized as chasing and the principle has landed, the next move is grief. The grief is for the relationship that was hoped for and is not arriving. It is also for the self that has been spent in the chasing. Often there is anger underneath the grief about the years of contortion. The anger needs to be felt before the grief can fully come.

See [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] for the related teaching where grief is offered as the move that paradoxically sometimes saves a relationship by freeing the griever from the chasing. See [[mourn-the-breakup-fully]] for the work after the relationship has ended. See [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] for the cost of skipping the grief.

The grief is not optional. The chaser who skips the grief carries the unmoved material into the next relationship and recreates the same dynamic with a different partner. The grief is what frees the chaser from the pattern.

## What This Is Not

This is not a license to give up at the first sign of friction. The principle applies when the work has already been offered -- the truth has been spoken, the listening has been offered, the chase has been honest -- and the other party has clearly opted out. It is not a permission to declare "they've given up" as a way to exit conflict early.

This is not a moral judgment of the chaser. The chasing is what the body does when the original wound that is firing in the relationship is the wound of having been left or unchosen. The chasing is the trauma's strategy. Recognizing it as trauma is the doorway to stopping. See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]] for the developmental context.

This is not a claim that the other person's no is final in some absolute sense. People change. The principle is about what the chaser can do in this moment, given this partner's current state. Future shifts are not the chaser's to produce.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A partner has stopped engaging and the chaser is escalating efforts to reach them
- The chaser has been trying strategies for months or years and none have worked
- The chaser is asking "what else can I try?" and the energy is increasingly desperate
- A business partner has emotionally checked out and the still-engaged partner is trying to revive them
- A child has gone no-contact and the parent is trying every angle to reopen the door

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the matter-of-factness of someone who has seen it many times. There is no sharpness in the delivery. The "that's their business" is offered as relief rather than rebuke. The co-founder story is told with quick warmth -- the speed at which the woman accepted the implication is part of the example. The principle is presented as a release, not a sentence.
