# Context Switching Drains Energy

## The Principle

The cost of switching between different kinds of work is not zero. It is also not small. Each switch requires the body and mind to drop one context, re-orient to another, and reestablish the state needed for the new work. The reestablishment takes time and energy. When the switches happen often, the cumulative tax across a day is large enough to be one of the dominant determinants of how depleted the person is at the end of it.

The fix is to structure the calendar so that similar work happens together. Coaching sessions on one day, not interspersed with operational meetings. Writing in one block, not broken up by message-answering. Teaching in one period, not alternated with one-on-ones. The work itself is the same. The cost of doing it is much lower when the mode does not have to keep shifting.

"I know context switching takes a lot of energy from me. So if I can do one whole day of coaching and then one day of podcasts and then one day of teaching, I have far more energy than if I am having a business meeting followed by a coaching meeting." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The principle is general but the implementation is personal. Each person has different modes that take different setup work and impose different switching costs. The principle is to notice your own modes and batch accordingly.

## Why Context Switching Costs So Much

Each kind of work requires a state. Coaching requires a state of warm presence, deep listening, and the capacity to be moved. Operational work requires a state of clear thinking, decisiveness, and efficient communication. Teaching requires a state of energetic transmission and pedagogical structure. Writing requires a state of solitary attention and verbal flow.

These states are not just mental. They are somatic. The body of someone in a coaching state is different from the body of someone in an operational state. The breath is different. The shoulders are different. The energy is different.

Switching states means:

- Closing down the previous state (which has its own residue)
- Re-opening to a new state (which requires the body to shift)
- Reestablishing the conditions for the new work (which often requires preparation, recall, or contextualization)
- Allowing the new state to actually settle in (which takes time)

When the switches happen rapidly, none of these phases complete. The body never fully settles into any state. Every block of work happens in a half-state -- not quite the prior mode, not yet the new mode. The work suffers. The body suffers more.

## What the Batched Day Looks Like

The batched day groups similar work into protected blocks. Variations of what this looks like in practice:

- A day of coaching: all sessions on one day, with the day shaped around the energy and recovery requirements of coaching.
- A day of writing: protected time for the writing, with messages, meetings, and other modes off-limits.
- A day of operations: leadership meetings, decisions, team check-ins, all on one day rather than scattered.
- A day of teaching or speaking: with the energy buildup and recovery the mode requires.
- A day of unstructured strategic thinking: with no scheduled meetings at all, just space to think and connect.

Not every kind of work has its own day. Some people batch by half-day. Some batch by morning-vs-afternoon. The principle is the batching, not the specific schedule.

Joe's own example -- one day coaching, one day podcasts, one day teaching -- is illustrative rather than prescriptive. The reason this configuration works for him is that each mode is distinct enough to impose a switching cost, and his role allows the calendar discretion to actually batch.

## What Counts as a Mode

A mode is a kind of work that requires a particular state to do well. Modes are not just topics. Two meetings on the same topic in different modes (one operational decision-making, one coaching the team through a challenge) require different states and impose a switching cost between them.

Common modes worth batching:

- Coaching / 1:1 deep work with another person
- Operational meetings / decisions / coordination
- Creative work / writing / making
- Teaching / speaking / public-facing transmission
- Strategic thinking / long-horizon planning
- Administrative work / email / messages

The line between modes is somewhat personal. For some people, two different kinds of coaching are different modes (executive coaching vs. emotional process work, say). For others, they are similar enough that the switching cost is low. The diagnostic is to notice your own depletion patterns and trace which switches actually cost.

## The Hidden Cost in a "Reasonable" Calendar

A calendar that looks reasonable on paper can be brutal in practice if the switches are frequent. A day with:

- 9am: operational meeting
- 10am: coaching session
- 11am: writing block
- 12pm: team lunch
- 1pm: strategic call
- 2pm: another coaching session
- 3pm: email block
- 4pm: speaking prep

is a day with seven mode switches. Each switch costs. The cumulative cost is severe even though no individual block is particularly demanding.

The same day reorganized:

- 9am-12pm: coaching block (two or three sessions, plus prep and recovery)
- 12pm: team lunch
- 1pm-4pm: writing block (deep, uninterrupted)
- 4pm: brief email and admin

has two modes for the working day. The depletion at the end is dramatically different. The output is also dramatically different.

The reorganization does not require less work to happen. It requires the work to be arranged differently. The reorganization is the high-leverage move on the calendar -- much higher leverage than trying to fit more in.

## Why This Is Hard to Implement

Three reasons people resist batching:

**1. Other people's needs.** Calendars often get filled by other people. Each request looks small in isolation. Saying yes to each looks reasonable. The cumulative effect of all the yeses is the chaos calendar. Batching requires the discipline to say no to the small requests in order to protect the larger structure.

**2. The fear of being unavailable.** A person who has built their reputation on being responsive often experiences batching as risk. What if someone needs them on a coaching day and they are deep in writing? The fear of unavailability prevents the batching. The cost of perpetual availability is the chaos calendar.

**3. The seduction of variety.** Doing seven different kinds of work in a day can feel stimulating. The dopamine of variety is real. The cost of the dopamine is the depletion. People often realize this only retrospectively, when they have tried batching and discovered their energy at the end of a batched day is much higher than at the end of a varied day.

The implementation is therefore not just calendar mechanics. It requires negotiating with the social expectations, with the self-image of being available, and with the appetite for variety. Each of these can be addressed, but the addressing is part of the work.

## When This Move Applies

The batching principle is the right intervention when:

- A person reports depletion from a calendar that has many small switches in it
- The day is structured around responsiveness rather than around any deliberate rhythm
- The person notices their best work happens in protected blocks and yet keeps scheduling against itself
- Burnout is showing up and the calendar review reveals frequent mode-switching
- A team's productivity is degraded and the team's typical day involves constant context shifting

## What This Is Not

This is not "always batch your time." Some work is genuinely better done in shorter blocks. Some people genuinely thrive on variety. The principle is to notice the cost of switching for you and to design accordingly, not to apply a single rule to everyone.

This is not anti-availability. It is in favor of deliberate availability. A person who batches well is more available within the batched blocks than a person whose attention is scattered across every block.

This is also not the same as time-blocking generally. Time-blocking is putting work on the calendar. Batching is grouping similar work together. You can time-block badly (lots of small blocks of different kinds) or well (large blocks of similar kinds). The grouping is the point.
