# Every Thought About Not Feeling an Emotion Is Itself an Emotion to Feel

## The Principle

When someone tries to feel an emotion all the way through, what often blocks the movement is not an absence of emotion. It is a thought *about* the emotion: "I shouldn't feel this." "This is too much." "If I let this happen I'll never stop." Each of those thoughts is itself a feeling in disguise. The thought is the surface; the emotion underneath it is the next thing to be felt.

Run the thought through the principle and the descent opens:

- "I should be able to cry" is shame. Feel the shame.
- "If I cry, I'll never stop crying" is fear. Feel the fear.
- "I shouldn't feel this way" is shame. Feel the shame.
- "Why am I not feeling anything" is fear (or anger). Feel that.

"Every thought that you have about not being able to feel an emotion all the way through is an emotion that you get to feel. That's like such a beautiful pointer. Every thought." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3—Ambition, Narcissistic Parents, Addiction to Stress, Parenting Emotional

## Why This Works

The thought is not the obstacle to feeling. The thought is the body's report of an emotion it has not yet metabolized. Anxiety produces the thought "this is too much." Shame produces the thought "I shouldn't be feeling this." Fear produces the thought "if I start I won't stop."

The thought feels like commentary on the feeling. It is actually the next layer of feeling, expressed in the only language the system has available in that moment. See [[thoughts-as-management]] for the broader principle that thoughts are usually doing emotional work, not delivering neutral information.

## Brett's Axiom-Tracing Articulation

A precise procedural version of the principle, focused on the case where the thought is "I don't feel anything." Trace the thought back to the axiom it depends on. The axiom is the next emotion.

"If you have the thought, oh, I don't feel anything, I'm not feeling anything, and you trace that thought back to where it comes from, how is it not fear, or there's like an assumption. Thoughts come from — they require assumptions to operate. We need to have certain axioms for which to do the logic with. And so what's the axiom there? The axiom is I should be feeling something that I'm not. And what is under that? The fear of not feeling, the fear of not being good enough." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

The procedure: when a not-feeling thought arrives, ask what axiom the thought depends on. The axiom is itself loaded with emotion (fear of inadequacy, shame about the deficit, anger at the failure of the practice). Feel the axiom's emotion. That is the next layer.

This gives the principle its operational form. The practitioner stops trying to argue with the not-feeling thought and starts tracing it to the axiom that produced it. The axiom is the door.

## The Descent

The descent moves through the layers in the order they were stacked. The top layer is usually shame, judgment, or guilt — the blocker emotions (see [[blocker-emotions]]). Underneath those is usually fear. Underneath the fear is often the original sadness, anger, or grief that the system was trying not to feel.

The descent often looks like:

1. The person tries to feel sadness. A thought arrives: "I should be over this by now."
2. The thought is named as shame. The shame is felt.
3. Underneath the shame: "If I really let this in, I won't be able to function." That is fear.
4. The fear is felt.
5. Underneath the fear: the sadness itself, finally available.

The peeling is not always this clean. Sometimes the layers reverse — anger on top of sadness, or shame about the shame. The principle holds regardless of order: whatever thought arrives about the not-feeling is itself the feelable.

See [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]] for the question form of this descent.

## The River Metaphor

The companion image. Trying to feel an emotion is like trying to float down a river. The instruction is to be a log: let the current carry you. The river decides which way to bend, which rocks to navigate, which rapids to hit. The mind that demands the river behave a certain way — "why isn't this a waterfall yet?" — is the thing that interrupts the floating.

"You're there because you love the emotion. The process can look a lot of different ways. So it's really just about following it. The question is kind of like asking, how do I float down the river? How do I fully float down the river? Or it's maybe more like, how does a log float down the river? That's what it does. It's what we do if we don't get in the way." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

Brett extends the image: "Sometimes the river goes around the bend, then through a little gentle Class 2 rapids, then to the right and over a waterfall and then a Class 4."

Joe's reply: "Right. Exactly. Why isn't it all waterfalls?"

The emotion knows the route. The thought "this isn't intense enough" or "I should be feeling more by now" is itself an emotion the body wants to feel — usually some form of impatience, frustration, or fear.

## The Practical Move

When someone is doing emotional inquiry and a thought arrives that interrupts the feeling, do not argue with the thought. Do not try to convince them the thought is wrong. Name the thought as the next emotion.

- "What's the emotion underneath that thought?"
- "If 'I shouldn't feel this' were a feeling, what feeling would it be?"
- "Notice the thought. What is it saying? Feel that."

The thought stops being an obstacle and becomes a pointer. The descent continues. Each thought is a step down.

## Why It Disarms Resistance

The most common resistance to emotional inquiry is the production of thoughts that explain why the inquiry will not work, should not work, is not working, or is dangerous. This principle pre-empts that resistance: every such thought, instead of being an objection to the practice, becomes part of the practice. There is no thought the body can produce that exits the practice. Every thought is more material for it.

This is the same structural insight that makes [[whats-stopping-you-from-enjoying-x]] so durable. The blocker is the next thing to look at, not the thing that ends the looking.

## Tone

Light, almost playful. Joe delivers this as a hack — "Yeah, it's really sweet, that thought process. I really dig that thought process." The energy is not "do not have these thoughts." The energy is "these thoughts are themselves the gold." The mind that was being treated as the enemy becomes a generous source of next steps.

## When to Use This

- When someone is doing emotional inquiry and getting blocked by thoughts about the inquiry.
- When someone reports they cannot feel an emotion they sense is there.
- When someone is in a meta-loop of analyzing their emotional state instead of feeling it.
- When the question "how do I fully feel this?" is itself the thing being asked.
- When a person says "I should be feeling X by now" or "I'm not feeling enough."

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone says "I can't feel my emotions" but the inquiry reveals thoughts about emotions, which are emotions wearing different costumes.
- When someone is trying to manufacture an emotion they think should be present (see [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]).
- When the felt experience is being interrupted by interpretive thoughts.
