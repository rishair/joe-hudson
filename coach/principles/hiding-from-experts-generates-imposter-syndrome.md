# Hiding From the People Who Could Help Generates More Imposter Syndrome

## The Principle

The imposter feeling is not just felt — it is generated, and the generator is the hiding behavior. When a person avoids the people who could most help them because admitting their gaps would expose them, the avoidance does not protect them from the imposter feeling. It manufactures more of it. Each act of hiding deepens the inner certainty of being a fraud, because the hiding itself is the fraudulent act.

"Which makes you more and more feel more and more like an imposter. The more you're hiding yourself, the more you're faking it, the more of an imposter you feel like you are. You are." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The mechanism: imposter syndrome is the feeling that one is performing a self that is not the real one. The hiding behavior is exactly that performance — pretending to know when you don't, organizing the work to keep the gap invisible, routing around the people who would see through the act. The hiding does not reduce the imposter act; it executes it. The feeling tracks the behavior. The more hiding, the more imposter feeling.

## The Self-Reinforcing Loop

The loop has four steps and runs continuously:

1. **The gap appears.** A piece of work, a meeting, a project where the person does not fully know what they are doing.
2. **The imposter feeling fires.** The body registers "I am supposed to know this; I do not; if they find out, something bad happens."
3. **The hiding behavior runs.** Avoid the expert, route around the help, organize the work to keep the gap invisible, perform competence in front of those who could see through it.
4. **The imposter feeling intensifies.** Because the person is now actively performing-a-self-that-is-not-the-real-one, the felt-sense of being an imposter strengthens. Return to step 1 at higher intensity.

The loop has no terminating condition from inside itself. The harder you hide, the more imposter feeling you generate. The more imposter feeling, the more you feel you need to hide. The structural exit is to break the loop by doing the opposite of what the imposter feeling tells you to do: ask for the help, expose the gap, name what you don't know.

## The Operational Signature

This principle gives the coach a precise behavioral diagnostic for imposter syndrome. Listen for:

- "I haven't reached out to X even though they would know" -- where X is the obvious expert
- "I'm trying to figure this out on my own" -- in a context where the figuring-out-alone is structurally a worse strategy
- "I don't want to bother them" -- as cover for "I don't want them to see what I don't know"
- Descriptions of organizing the work to avoid review by the people most qualified to review it
- Patterns of asking for help only from peers or juniors, never from people senior in the domain
- Tight, exhausting solo work where the obvious resource (the expert next door) goes untapped

The hiding behavior is often invisible to the person doing it. They experience it as preference, as autonomy, as efficiency. The coach's job is to make the structure visible: "you are routing around the people who could most help you. That routing is the imposter syndrome operating, and it is making it worse."

## The BASE Jumping Commercial as the Case

Joe's own example demonstrates the principle in concrete form. He got a contract to do a BASE jumping commercial in Switzerland — somewhere he had never been, for a discipline whose local experts he had never met. The production was funded, with a helicopter, cameras, full crew. When he showed up to organize the shoot, the imposter feeling fired. His response was to hide.

"When I showed up to organize the shoot, I avoided contacting the people who were the most experienced and most skilled who could help me because I would have felt like an imposter. And so the way that I organized the thing was trying to kind of hide it." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The result: the production value suffered. The avoidance of the people who would have made it better was the imposter syndrome operating. The hiding produced exactly the outcome the hiding was supposed to prevent — diminished quality of work, which in turn would have generated more imposter feeling on the next project.

The counterfactual: "What would it have been like if I just showed up and I was like, yep, I have literally never done this before. And that's, this particular thing we're doing is new to me. And I'm gonna do it... And I'm gonna ask for all the help that I can get." See [[base-jumping-commercial-as-imposter-syndrome-example]] for the full story and its use as a teaching example.

## The CEO Version

The same dynamic shows up at executive scale. The CEO with imposter syndrome who has to maintain the front of knowing cannot access the inputs from their team that would actually help them make better decisions. They become less competent through the hiding, which generates more imposter feeling, which requires more hiding, which further degrades the work.

"Which is what CEOs who do not have that sense of deep shame, who do not feel like they have to be of value to be worthy, are more likely to do. They're okay admitting the vulnerability, which of course takes away the imposter syndrome." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The CEO who can say "I don't know — what do you all think?" gets the inputs, gets the better decision, builds the team alignment, and does not have the imposter loop running. The hiding CEO gets none of these. The structural advantage of the vulnerable CEO is not character — it is that they have stopped running the self-defeating loop.

## The Exit: Ask For All the Help You Can Get

The intervention is the reverse of the hiding behavior. The same situations that triggered the hiding become opportunities to do the opposite: name what you don't know, contact the expert you have been avoiding, ask for all the help you can get. See [[ask-for-all-the-help-you-can-get]].

The first move feels counterintuitive because the imposter feeling is screaming that exposure will be catastrophic. The discovery: it is not. The expert almost always responds to a direct "I don't know this and I would love your help" with help. The relationship deepens. The work improves. The imposter feeling drops because the hiding behavior has stopped — the loop's input is gone.

This is also why [[maintaining-false-persona-costs-more-than-incompetence]] connects directly: the cognitive overhead of the hiding is the same overhead that the asking-for-help dissolves. The bandwidth comes back, the work gets better, and the imposter feeling has nothing to feed on.

## Tone

Direct, almost technical. The principle is delivered as a mechanism, not a moral. The hiding is not bad behavior; it is a strategy that produces the opposite of its intended result. The relief in the framing is the seeing of the loop -- the client realizes they have been working very hard to make their imposter syndrome worse.

The follow-up move (the recommendation to ask for help) should land with the same matter-of-factness. Not "be brave and ask for help." Just "the loop runs by hiding; it breaks by asking. The first ask is the harder one."
