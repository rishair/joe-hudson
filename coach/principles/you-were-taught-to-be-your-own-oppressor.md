# You Were Taught to Be Your Own Oppressor

## The Principle

The suppression that was done to you as a child by external forces (parents, family, culture, religion, school) doesn't end when the external forces are no longer in the room. The suppression continues, now performed by the part of you that was trained to suppress yourself. The external oppressor became the internal one. The work of breaking free is to recognize the internal oppressor as a learned operation and to begin doing the opposite — to begin telling your truth.

"You got taught how to be your own oppressor. And you're learning how to break free by telling your truth." -- Joe Hudson, What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?

The principle reframes ongoing self-suppression as a learned competence, not as a personal failing. The person has been performing the suppression for so long that it feels like just who they are. The principle names the suppression as inherited training, which makes it visible as something separate from the self, which makes it available to be unlearned.

## The Two Halves

The principle has two operative halves, and both matter:

**Half one: you were taught.** The suppression was installed. It was not chosen. It was probably installed in early childhood by people who themselves were taught the same suppression by their own caregivers. The chain goes back. The person did not invent their own self-suppression — they inherited it. This half matters because it removes the moral weight from the pattern. The person did not become their own oppressor through some defect; they became it through training. The training can be undone.

**Half two: you're learning to break free by telling your truth.** The undoing has a specific form. It is not self-improvement, not therapy, not insight, not awareness. It is the act of speaking the truth that the oppressor had been suppressing. The speaking is the un-suppression. Each act of speaking truth is one of the small operations that reverses the training.

The two halves together produce the operative frame: the suppression was learned, so it can be unlearned, and the way it gets unlearned is through doing the opposite of what was learned. Saying what wasn't allowed to be said is the structural reverse of the training that taught the not-saying.

## Why It's True

When a child is repeatedly told (or taught through consequences) that certain expressions are unacceptable — certain emotions, certain wants, certain opinions, certain truths — the child develops an internal monitoring system to prevent those expressions from occurring. The monitoring system protects the child from the consequences of being caught with the forbidden material. The system is adaptive: it works.

As the child grows up, the external monitoring (the parents, the family, the school) recedes. The internal monitoring stays. The adult continues to monitor and suppress the same material the child was originally trained to monitor and suppress, even when the external enforcement has been gone for decades. The training has been internalized. The adult is now their own enforcer.

This is structurally identical to other forms of internalized oppression — internalized racism, internalized misogyny, internalized homophobia — where the oppressive structure operates from inside the targeted person, no longer requiring the external apparatus. The personal-development version of the principle is just the individual-scale instance of a much larger pattern.

The freedom move is also structurally identical to its political counterpart. In each case, breaking free involves doing the thing that was trained to be impossible: speaking, taking up space, claiming the wanting, expressing the forbidden material. The doing is the freedom; the freedom is not a state achieved before the doing.

## The Connection to Holding-Back Truth

This principle is the structural complement of [[holding-back-truth-is-self-harm]]. The holding-back-is-harm principle names what the self-suppression does to the person (it harms them). This principle names where the self-suppression came from (it was taught) and what dissolves it (telling the truth).

The two together give the person:

- The cost of continuing (you are harming yourself)
- The origin of the pattern (you were trained to do this)
- The structural move out (tell the truth)
- The framing that makes it possible (this is breaking free from inherited oppression, not just personal-development work)

## What Was Suppressed Stays Underneath

The material the inner oppressor has been suppressing doesn't go away. It accumulates. See [[layers-of-unallowed-self]] for the layered structure of what stays underneath: the anger that wasn't allowed, the sadness that wasn't allowed underneath that, the joy that wasn't allowed underneath that.

When the person begins telling their truth, the layers come forward in order. The anger first, often. Then the sadness. Then sometimes the joy. The order matters because each layer is what the inner oppressor was protecting against the previous layer surfacing. The work of breaking free is to let each layer come.

In the source session, this dynamic was visible in real time. The woman let out her anger ("I'm pissed off about the bad advice"). Joe noticed the anger was "the thing that's your truth that you often don't speak." Then the sadness arrived ("the anger is turning into sadness really fast"). Joe named what was happening: "beneath the anger that wasn't allowed is a sadness that wasn't allowed, is a fucking joy that wasn't allowed. It's just layers of you that weren't allowed." See [[layers-of-unallowed-self]].

## The Despondent-Teenager Variant

A specific visible form of the inner oppressor is the despondent-teenager posture: the slumped, fuck-you-you're-not-going-to-pick-me, this-is-stupid energy of an adolescent whose willfulness has been crushed and has resigned into surly waiting. In the source session, Joe named this in the woman: "when you were waiting in line, like in the You had this kind of like, fuck you, you're not going to pick me. Like, this is stupid, the whole thing going on... Kind of just a little despondent teenager."

The despondent teenager is the visible signature of an inner oppressor that has succeeded. The willful, powerful, alive version of the person has been overruled. The remaining presentation is the resigned, half-angry, half-collapsed teenager who lost the war with the suppressing forces and now performs a kind of muted disgust. The presentation is the residue of the oppression — what's left after the original willfulness was crushed.

Joe's read in the source session was that beneath the despondent teenager was the powerful little girl who had been suppressed: "so much power you are holding back. Holy fuck, you must have been just like this willful, powerful little girl. Holy fuck. I can't imagine you. Did you play some sort of sport?" The despondent teenager is what's visible; the powerful kid is what was suppressed and is still alive underneath, just not allowed.

The work in this variant is to let the powerful kid come back through — not as performance of power, but as the resumption of what was always there.

## What This Principle Is NOT

- **It is not blame of the parents/family/culture.** The original oppressors were themselves trained in the same suppression. The chain of inheritance doesn't have a clean villain. The work is to recognize the pattern and unlearn it, not to spend the unlearning hating the people who taught it.

- **It is not victimhood.** The principle names a real structural dynamic, but it doesn't lock the person into a permanent victim role. The breaking-free-by-truth-telling is available. The person has agency in the present, even though they didn't have agency when the training was installed.

- **It is not the same as [[childhood-imprinting]].** That principle covers the broader phenomenon of childhood patterns becoming adult patterns. This principle is specifically about the suppression pattern — the internalization of an external oppressor as an internal one — and the specific move (truth-speaking) that dissolves it.

- **It is not the claim that all internal critique is oppression.** Some internal monitoring is functional — being aware of how one's behavior affects others, for example. The principle is specifically about the suppression of one's own truth, not about all forms of self-awareness. The diagnostic: if the internal voice is preventing you from speaking what is actually true for you, the inner oppressor is operating.

- **It is not solved by insight.** Recognizing the inner oppressor is necessary but not sufficient. The dissolving requires the actual speaking. Insight without speaking just produces an articulate inner oppressor that now has a vocabulary for what it's doing.

## When This Principle Is Operative

The principle has its strongest application when:

- The person is presenting a self-suppression pattern that has been operating for years and has become identity ("I just don't share that kind of thing").
- The person has visible signs of inner-oppressor activity: holding back, hedging, performing the acceptable version, the despondent-teenager posture, chronic anxiety around being seen.
- The person needs a frame that removes moral weight from the pattern. As long as the person is treating their suppression as a personal failing, the unlearning is blocked.
- The person needs a structural move (speaking truth) that is small enough to be doable but specific enough to actually be the work.
- The person is at the stage of recognizing the suppression but doesn't yet have a frame for what to do about it.

## What Joe Does With the Principle

- **Name the principle at the moment of recognition.** When the person realizes they've been suppressing themselves, Joe names the broader pattern. "You got taught how to be your own oppressor." The naming installs the frame.

- **Pair it with the breaking-free move.** The principle is incomplete without the second half. "And you're learning how to break free by telling your truth." The two halves together produce the operative frame.

- **Use it as the diagnostic for what's happening in real time.** When the person re-suppresses something in the room, the principle names it: the inner oppressor just ran. The recognition of the inner oppressor as a discrete operation makes it more visible each time it runs.

- **Hand it to the person as their long-arc practice frame.** The work of breaking free from the inner oppressor is not a single session's work. The principle gives the person a frame for the work that continues outside the session.

## Tone

Direct, grounded, slightly weighted. The principle has political resonance — "oppressor" is heavier language than "inner critic" or "self-suppression" — and the weight is intentional. The political framing makes the work feel like the breaking-of-a-real-structure rather than the polishing-of-a-personality-feature.

The directness matters because the principle has to land as a structural fact, not as a psychological theory. The person needs to feel that they are recognizing a real dynamic that has been operating their whole life. Hedging the language would undermine the recognition.

When the second half is delivered ("you're learning how to break free by telling your truth"), the tone softens slightly. The breaking-free is happening, even as Joe is naming the oppression. The softness conveys that the present moment is part of the freedom-work, not just a diagnostic of the unfreedom.
