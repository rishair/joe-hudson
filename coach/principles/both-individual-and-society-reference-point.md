# Hold Both the Individual and the Society as Reference Points

## The Principle

In a chaotic time, where the question becomes "what do I want to bring into the world?", the answer requires holding two reference points simultaneously: the individual (yourself, your direct life, your immediate people) and the society (the larger collective, your children's children, the planet, the institutions). Collapsing into either one alone produces a predictable failure mode.

**Pure self-focus produces fear.** If the only reference point is the individual self, the system's stakes are too narrow. The threats to the self loom large. The cortisol stays high. The horizon shrinks. The thinking narrows. The person becomes more reactive, not less.

**Pure collective-focus produces unworkable concepts.** If the only reference point is the larger collective, the resulting plans are abstract. They sound noble. They do not survive contact with how individuals actually live. The historical examples — Joe specifically names communism — are ideologies that worked beautifully at the concept level and failed at the individual level because nobody actually wanted to live the life the ideology required.

Both reference points are required for effective action in a chaotic time. Either alone fails.

"If you're really only focused on yourself, then what's gonna happen is you're far more prone to fear. But if you're thinking about it in a bigger way, oh, how do I help all of society? How do I help my children and my children's children? That allows it to not be personal and allows you to have a more calm state of mind. If you're only thinking about the other people, if you're only thinking about society and you're not thinking about you, you're not going to have the kind of solutions that are practical that are going to make a huge difference. Because if it doesn't apply to you, if you're not thinking about how it works for the individual, then most likely what's going to happen is you're going to think about a concept that is just a concept." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

## The Communism Example

Joe uses communism as the worked example of pure-collective-focus failing at the individual level. The idea was internally coherent at the conceptual level. The implementation collapsed because nobody actually wanted to live inside it. The individual response — "I do not want to do the same job for the rest of my life with no way to get ahead" — was not addressed by the concept.

"There have been transitions in our world where ideas come, and I'll use communism as an example. Great idea, but it didn't work on an individual basis because people weren't saying, oh, I want to be doing the same job for the rest of my life and not have any way to get ahead. No, that doesn't work for me." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

The example is structurally important because it is hard to argue with. A purely collective-oriented ideology produced massive human suffering precisely because it had no traction at the level of how individuals actually live. The concept's beauty at the abstract level was not protection against its incoherence at the individual level. The same pattern repeats across other purely collective frameworks.

## Why Pure Self-Focus Produces Fear

The mechanism is structural. A self that has no reference point outside itself has all the threats of life arrayed against a single small target — itself. The threats to its income, its body, its relationships, its survival, its status are the only stakes the system can compute. The cortisol load is therefore high. The horizon is short. The decisions are defensive.

A self that holds a larger reference point — children, children's children, society, the planet, a purpose larger than self-preservation — has the stakes distributed across a much larger surface. The threats to the self are still real, but they are not the only stakes. The system can be calmer because the system is not the only thing at stake.

"That allows it to not be personal and allows you to have a more calm state of mind." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

This is why people in service-of-something-larger orientations often report being calmer during chaotic times than people whose only reference point is their own situation. The larger orientation is not a denial of the personal stakes. It is a recontextualization of them within a bigger picture. The bigger picture does the calming.

## Why Pure Collective-Focus Produces Unworkable Concepts

The mechanism is also structural. A solution that has no reference point at the individual level has no friction-testing. It cannot be checked against the actual texture of how a person lives. It exists at the conceptual level only. The conceptual level is infinitely malleable; almost any concept can be made internally coherent.

When the concept meets actual individuals, the gap shows up. The individuals do not want what the concept requires. The individuals find workarounds. The workarounds become the actual operating system. The concept is preserved as the official frame and ignored in practice, or the concept is enforced against the individuals and produces the suffering Joe references in the communism case.

A solution that includes the individual reference point — "how does this work for me, for the people I know, for the daily texture of how we live?" — is testable. The testing is what makes the solution practical. The practicality is what makes the solution actually applicable to other people, because other people are also individuals whose lives have texture.

## The Both-And Required for Effective Action

The principle is therefore not "lean toward the collective" or "balance the two." It is "hold both at once as live reference points for the same decision."

For any move a person is considering in a chaotic time, the dual-reference test is:

- Does this work for me, my family, my immediate life? (individual check)
- Does this serve something larger than just me — my community, my children's children, the larger system I am part of? (collective check)

A move that passes only the individual test is narrow and likely to produce fear-based decisions over time. A move that passes only the collective test is abstract and likely to produce unworkable plans. A move that passes both is the move that actually has traction.

This is the implicit shape of every effective response to chaos that Joe has worked with. The CEOs who navigated COVID well were not just protecting their companies (pure self) or trying to save the world (pure collective). They were doing both: how do I keep this business alive in a way that also serves what the moment is asking of leadership? The personal-development practitioners who used the disruption well were not just optimizing themselves (pure self) or trying to be of service to humanity (pure collective). They were doing both: how do I become someone who, by being, contributes to what is being asked of this moment?

## When the Principle Is Most Useful

- When a person is in a fear-loop about their own situation and is missing the larger frame that would steady them
- When a person is in a noble-but-abstract frame about saving the world and is not addressing their own concrete situation
- When a leader is reaching for either "shareholder value" (pure self/firm) or "stakeholder value" (pure collective) and missing the both-and
- When a person is in a chaotic time and is collapsing into one of the two reference points as a way to manage the overwhelm
- When the conversation is about action in uncertain conditions and the action being considered is failing one of the two tests
- When the person is being pulled toward an ideology that has the elegance of pure collective-focus but lacks the individual-level traction

## What the Principle Does Not Say

It does not say the individual and the society are always aligned. They often are not. The principle says that both have to be live reference points, not that they always agree. When they disagree, the work is to hold both and find the move that addresses both as well as possible, not to collapse the tension by abandoning one.

It does not say self-care is selfishness. Self-care is one of the individual-reference moves the principle requires. The principle is critiquing the abandonment of the individual reference point, not the practice of taking care of oneself.

It does not say abstract thinking is bad. The principle is critiquing abstract thinking that is unmoored from individual-level reality, not abstract thinking as such. Good abstract thinking includes the individual-level check.

It does not say all collective ideologies fail. Some collective frameworks work because they include the individual-level reference point. The principle is critiquing the pure-collective-focus version, not collective concern itself.
