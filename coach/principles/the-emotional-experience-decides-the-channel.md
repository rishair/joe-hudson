# Whether You Liked the Felt Experience Decides Whether the Whole Life Channel Opens or Closes

## The Principle

A single emotional experience, depending on whether the person liked it or not, can open or close an entire channel of their life. Two people can have the same external event with the same external outcome. One liked the emotional residue; the channel opens; that whole domain of life becomes available. The other disliked the emotional residue; the channel closes; that whole domain shuts down, sometimes permanently.

The variable is not the event. The variable is the person's relationship to what they felt.

"You have an emotional experience that you don't like, like, like you just spoke about in speaking, and then you hit avoidance, and then like there's this whole channel of your life that's like shut down. Like speaking is this one example. This can be addiction, this can be I'm not going to date anymore. I'm not going to, you know, I'm not going to go after the woman because I had this one emotional experience. And these whole parts of your life are shut down because you didn't like the emotional experience. And it could have just as easily been, oh, I like the emotional experience. And then that whole part of your life opens up." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

## The Speaking Example

The cleanest example is in public speaking. Tristan describes two roads in childhood:

The **naturals** walk up on stage and may not perform any better than anyone else. But they love the experience. They love the adrenaline. They love the nerves. They love how alert they are. When they walk back down, they think: "I want more of that." They start seeking out opportunities to speak. They turn into the naturals through practice that they sought because they loved the feeling.

**Everyone else** walks up on stage and either feels actual humiliation or extreme anxiety. They hate the feeling leading up to it. They hate the feeling during it. From that moment on, they avoid. They say no to every opportunity. The avoidance compounds. They build filters and masks to make sure they never have to be in that feeling again. The speaking channel closes. Sometimes for life.

The external event was identical. What decided the trajectory was the relationship to the feeling. Joe affirms and generalizes the observation: this is one of the most consequential dynamics in being human, and it operates everywhere.

## The Dating Example

Joe extends the principle to dating:

"I tried to hit on a woman, a woman rejected me, and I was like, oh, that was kind of fun. Like, you'll be, you'll, you'll, you'll be dating lots of women if you, if that's what you want. You get that first rejection, you're like, oh, I'm horrible, I never want to experience that again. You're like playing video games and whacking off to porn for like the rest of your life, right? It's crazy how that works." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

Same event: an early rejection. One reaction: "that was kind of fun" -- the dating channel opens, and a life of romantic engagement becomes available. The other reaction: "I'm horrible, never again" -- the channel closes, and decades of avoidance, substitutes, and resentment follow. The pivot is not the rejection. The pivot is what the person did with the felt experience of the rejection.

## The Addiction Example

Joe names addiction as another instance of the same pattern. An emotional experience that the person could not be with -- typically pain, anxiety, shame, or emptiness -- produced an avoidance that organized around a substance, behavior, or distraction. The substance is the secondary phenomenon. The primary phenomenon is the rejection of the felt experience. The substance is what the person found to keep the channel closed.

This frame puts addiction in the same family as the dating-shutdown, the speaking-shutdown, the auditioning-shutdown, the dancing-shutdown. The mechanism is identical: a felt experience the person could not stay with became something to escape from, and the escape calcified into a life-shaped avoidance.

## The Channel That Closes Is Not Just the Domain

The cost of a closed channel is not only the specific domain that becomes inaccessible. The closure imposes a structural friction across the whole life:

- The channels that are closed have to be **avoided actively** -- which requires ongoing energy.
- The closed channels often hold what the person most wants (connection, expression, creativity, intimacy) -- so the avoidance is also the avoidance of what would have made them most alive.
- The closed channel is usually visible to others -- as the friend who never speaks up in groups, the colleague who never raises their hand for the high-stakes project, the partner who never initiates conversations about hard topics.
- The substitutes that fill the gap (porn for intimacy, video games for adventure, drinking for connection) are flatter than what the original channel would have produced, so the underlying hunger never resolves.

The shut-down channel is a structural cost that compounds over years.

## Why This Happens: The Avoidance Compounds

A single bad emotional experience does not automatically close a channel. What closes the channel is the avoidance that follows. The person avoids one situation; that avoidance feels like relief; the relief reinforces the avoidance; the next opportunity is avoided; the avoidance generalizes; the channel closes.

Each avoidance is a small choice. Tristan describes it as the person saying "no over and over and over to all these opportunities where I could test myself." Each "no" is small. The cumulative effect is the closure of a life domain.

This is why the early interventions matter so much. A person who has avoided speaking for one year can re-enter the channel relatively easily. A person who has avoided for thirty years has trained their entire identity around the avoidance. The mask has hardened. The persona has been built. The cost of re-opening is much higher.

## The Mask-Building Side Effect

Tristan adds an observation Joe affirms: the avoidance does not just close the channel. It produces masks. The person who hates how they felt while speaking does not just stop speaking; they start building filters and personas in every environment where speaking is required. They become a different person at work, with family, with each group of friends. The system gets overloaded. The person becomes trapped in a lie. Every utterance now has to pass through the filter of "does this fit the persona I am right now?"

The original avoidance produced not just a closed channel but a fragmented self. The cost of one disliked emotional experience compounds into a structural problem with identity itself.

## What Reverses It

The reversal of a closed channel is not "force yourself to do the avoided thing." That is just adding effort against the same closed door. The reversal is at the level of the original variable: the relationship to the felt experience.

If the felt experience can be met differently -- welcomed, allowed, even enjoyed -- the channel can re-open. This is why exposure therapy alone often fails: the exposure is forced, the felt experience is still hated, and the avoidance just relocates. What works is reintroducing the channel under conditions where the felt experience can be received differently.

This is also why [[fear-is-about-feeling]] is so foundational. The fear of the channel is the fear of the feeling. Working with the feeling works with the channel. Working with the surface behavior leaves the underlying mechanism intact.

The Ultra Speaking model Tristan describes operationalizes this: short reps in a supportive community, where the felt experience of speaking becomes pleasant rather than horrifying. The community is structured so the person experiences the speaking as fun. Once the felt experience changes, the channel re-opens. The thirty years of avoidance can unwind, sometimes quickly.

## The Inverse: The Channel That Stays Open

The principle works in the positive direction too. A person who liked their first speaking experience, their first rejection, their first creative risk, has a channel that stays open and expands. Joe notes this almost in passing -- "you'll be dating lots of women if you, if that's what you want" -- but it is the heart of the principle. The same emotional alchemy that closes channels opens them. The naturals are not gifted. They are people who happened to like the felt experience and acted on the liking.

## The "I Don't Have a Choice" Pivot

Joe's own speaking shift came from a moment of forced exposure: his presentation was lost at a venture capital conference, and he had to speak for 20 minutes off the cuff. He remembers being scared. He also remembers thinking: "I don't have a choice." He had to do it.

Afterward, his relationship to speaking had reorganized. "I will never prepare for a speech again. It was so much better than anything I'd ever done." The felt experience during the forced exposure was actually pleasant -- it was what speaking from the [[the-butler-metaphor]] feels like. The pleasantness shifted the channel.

The pattern of this kind of pivot:

1. Avoidance has been operating for years.
2. A circumstance arrives where the avoidance is impossible.
3. The person enters the avoided experience without their usual filters.
4. The felt experience turns out to be different than the avoidance was protecting against -- often better.
5. The channel re-opens, sometimes in a single instance.

The lesson is not "force yourself into the avoided thing." The lesson is that the felt experience the person has been avoiding is often not the experience they think they are avoiding. The actual felt experience, when met without the additional avoidance, is frequently fine -- sometimes wonderful. The avoidance is protecting against the imagined feeling, not the actual feeling.

## What Counts as Embracing (It Is Not Liking)

A specific clarification: embracing the emotional experience does not mean liking it from the start, performing positivity, or convincing yourself it is good. The first form of embrace is often simply not running. The willingness to feel the feeling without doing something about it. From there, the relationship to the feeling shifts gradually -- sometimes into enjoyment, sometimes into neutral familiarity, sometimes into a kind of recognized texture that no longer threatens.

The naturals describe liking the experience. This is not where most people start. Most people start with "I can be with this without fleeing." That is enough to begin the channel reopening. The fondness, if it comes, comes later.

## What "Channel" Means in This Principle

A channel is a domain of life that has its own characteristic emotional profile. Each channel contains feelings the participant has to be willing to have in order to be in the channel. A few examples:

- **Speaking publicly.** Adrenaline, exposure, the possibility of looking stupid, the risk of being pushed out.
- **Dating.** Vulnerability, the possibility of rejection, the discomfort of being seen wanting something.
- **Creative work.** The fear of producing something bad, the possibility of criticism, the discomfort of being seen having an opinion.
- **Leadership.** The exposure of being responsible for outcomes, the discomfort of being judged, the burden of decisions affecting others.
- **Intimacy.** The vulnerability of being known, the risk of being abandoned, the discomfort of seeing yourself through another's eyes.
- **Parenting.** The exposure of being inadequate, the fear of damaging your child, the helplessness of loving someone whose suffering you cannot prevent.
- **Money and business.** The fear of failure, the exposure of being measured, the discomfort of wanting something visible.

A person closing off any of these is closing off the whole channel. The cost is not the absence of one activity. The cost is the absence of a whole register of life. A person who has closed off intimacy is not just missing a relationship -- they are missing all the experiences a life of intimacy contains. The same for any other channel.

## Boundary Conditions

Not every closed channel is a sign of pathology. Some channels close because the person genuinely does not want them. A person who tried speaking, was clear-eyed about how much it cost them, and decided not to is not the same as a person who closed the channel out of fear of the feeling.

The diagnostic: is the closure a clean no, or is it a fear-based avoidance? A clean no comes with peace. A fear-based avoidance comes with tension, regret, fantasy about doing it someday, anger at people who do the thing, or a sense of disqualification. The first does not need to be reopened. The second is the channel-closing this principle describes.

## Why This Matters for Coaching

When a person reports avoidance of a life domain (speaking, dating, leading, creating, asking, expressing), the question is rarely "how do we get you to do the thing?" The question is "what felt experience does this domain produce that you have decided you cannot be with?"

The coaching path moves to the felt experience first. If the felt experience can be met -- if [[fear-is-about-feeling]] can be welcomed, if [[feared-stories-about-emotions-sense-something-real]] can be tested -- the channel often re-opens on its own. The behavior change is downstream.

The opposite move -- working on the behavior directly (techniques for speaking, scripts for dating, strategies for asking) -- can sometimes work, but it tends to produce a managed competence in a still-avoided domain. The person becomes the executive who gives a polished talk while still hating the felt experience. The mask is more elaborate; the underlying avoidance is intact. The channel is still closed; the person has just become better at performing in front of it.

## The "I Couldn't Have Just As Easily" Reframe

The most useful corollary for the AI coach is the symmetry Joe names. Whatever was disliked, the person could have liked. Whatever closed could have opened. This is not a moral judgment about people who shut down. It is a structural observation that the same event can route two ways. Once a person sees the route they took was not necessary -- they could have liked it, the route is not them, the channel is not closed because they are not built for it -- the possibility of re-opening becomes visible.

This is also a frame against shame. People often shame themselves for having closed channels. "I'm just bad at speaking. I'm just not a dater. I'm just not a creative person." The principle reframes: you are not bad at it. You are someone who had a disliked emotional experience and avoided. The channel is not who you are. It is what your avoidance has produced.

## Tone

Observed and slightly amused. Joe describes this principle with the affect of someone who has watched it operate hundreds of times in his own life and in clients' lives. The crazy thing about how it works ("It's crazy how that works") is observed without judgment of the people who got routed into the shutdown. The tone is closer to wonder at the mechanism than indictment of the response. The implicit invitation is to see the mechanism clearly enough that the person can recognize where they are inside it and choose differently.
