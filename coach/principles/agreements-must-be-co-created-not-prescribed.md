# Fight Agreements Must Be Co-Created

## The Principle

The agreements two people make about how to fight have to be theirs. Co-created. Both of them actually want them. Not prescribed by an outside teacher, not pushed through by one partner over the other, not adopted from a book or a podcast as the right list of fight rules.

"It's also important that those agreements are actually something that are creative and make you both feel good and that you both actually agree to. So it's not like a cajoling, like, okay, so this is how we're going to fight, right? Good. It's literally like, oh, when you do this, I can say this. And so Tara and I have many of those things." -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The co-creation is not a nicety. It is a structural requirement. Without it, the practice of [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]] does not work.

## What Co-Created Looks Like

Co-created agreements emerge from a specific dynamic between two specific people. They take the shape:

> Oh, when you do this, I can say this.

One partner names a thing that happens between them. The other recognizes the thing. Together they devise a response that helps. The response is specific to what they have noticed about each other, and it is specific to what each of them can actually do.

This shape — discovery and design — is different from rule-importing. Rule-importing brings a list (the Gottman list, the NVC protocol, the marriage workshop handout) and tries to apply it. Co-creation does not start with the list. It starts with the actual dynamic between these two people and works outward into agreements that fit.

## What Prescribed Looks Like

The failure mode Joe is calling out has a specific shape: cajoling.

"Okay, so this is how we're going to fight, right? Good."

In this shape, one partner has decided what the rules should be (often after reading or hearing something), is presenting the rules to the other partner, and is treating the other partner's assent as the goal. The rules become the right answer. The other partner's job is to agree.

This produces nominal agreements that do not hold under stress. The partner who was cajoled into agreeing has no investment in the rules. Mid-fight, the rules feel like the other partner's rules being weaponized against them. Defensiveness rises, the rules get violated as a way of pushing back against the cajoler, and the meta-fight begins.

This failure mode is structurally identical to the failure mode of [[new-age-fight]]: tools that one partner has imported and is using on the other partner rather than tools both partners are using together.

## Why "Yours" Matters

The agreements have to be yours for three reasons:

**Ownership produces durability.** An agreement you helped create is an agreement you actually have, in the sense that you can find it in yourself when it is needed. An agreement that was pushed on you is one you have to remember externally, and external memory under activation tends to fail.

**Co-creation surfaces what is actually needed.** The "what we want to agree on" conversation is itself a form of attunement. It produces information neither partner had before about what the other actually needs, what specifically lands wrong, what would help. Skipping the conversation skips the information.

**Mutual agreement removes the controller.** When the agreements are co-created, neither partner is the rule-setter. The rules sit between you. Mid-fight, the rules can be invoked by either party without it feeling like one partner is invoking authority over the other. When the rules are prescribed, the invoking partner is also implicitly invoking their authority as the one who set the rules, which itself becomes a fight move.

## "Make You Both Feel Good"

The "feel good" criterion is doing real work here. An agreement that one partner experiences as a constraint on themselves to make the other partner feel okay is not a co-created agreement. It is a sacrificed agreement.

The test is: when you both sit with the agreement, does something in both of you ease? Or does one of you feel a little squeezed?

The squeeze is the signal that the agreement is not co-created yet. Either the agreement needs to be different, or the conversation needs to continue, or one partner needs to surface what they actually need that this agreement is not meeting. The agreement is not finished until both partners can sit with it and feel something like relief.

## "Creative"

The "creative" requirement matters too. The agreements that work tend to be specific, particular to the two of you, sometimes weird, often inventive. The get-naked agreement Joe describes is creative — nobody would prescribe it; it works for that specific couple in a specific way. The "I see how much you care" phrase is creative — it is a specific phrase tuned to a specific trauma pattern, not a generic protocol.

Generic agreements (use I-statements, don't raise your voice) tend to fail because they are too abstract to attach to actual moments. Creative agreements (Tara says "I see how much you care" when Joe rushes; we get naked when we fight) tend to succeed because they have specific traction in specific situations.

The creativity comes from the co-creation itself. Two people thinking together about what would actually help them invent things neither would invent alone, and certainly things no outside expert would invent for them.

## "Oh, When You Do This, I Can Say This"

The shape of the working agreement, in its cleanest form:

> Oh, when you do this, I can say this.

This shape has three features:

1. **It names a specific behavior.** Not "when you're being defensive" but "when you do this thing I can recognize."
2. **It names a specific response.** Not "I'll be more loving" but "I can say this specific sentence."
3. **It treats both partners as collaborators on the dynamic.** Neither is positioned as the problem; both are positioned as people figuring out how to handle a recognizable pattern together.

This shape protects against several failure modes at once: it does not pathologize either partner, it gives the responding partner something specific to actually do, and it gives the partner being responded to something they have already agreed to receive.

## When This Principle Applies Beyond Fight Agreements

This is a particular case of a broader principle. See [[dont-take-my-word-for-it]] for the general form: the value of a teaching is in how you use it, not in your adoption of it as authority. Tools imported as authority become weapons. Tools co-created from your own experience become instruments.

It also connects to [[dont-position-yourself-as-your-partners-growth-guide]]: the failure mode of one partner being the rule-maker is structurally the same as one partner being the growth-coach. In both cases, one partner is occupying a position the relationship cannot afford to have occupied, because the position itself prevents the relationship from being collaborative.

The fight-agreements practice is one place where this broader principle is especially load-bearing, because the agreements are explicitly meant to be invoked under stress and have to be both partners' agreements to hold up to that stress.

## The Coaching Implication

When a coach (or any teacher) describes fight agreements, the right frame is: "here is the practice, the practice is for two of you to make agreements together, and the agreements have to be yours." Not: "here is the list of agreements you should make."

Joe is careful in the transcript to give examples but explicitly disclaim that these are his recommendations:

"It can be many kinds of agreements... There's all sorts of agreements you can have and they have to be yours. They have to be something not prescribed by me, but something that you and your partner sit down with and say, how do we want to fight?" -- Joe Hudson, The Best Trick For Having Great Fights In Relationship

The discipline of holding the practice without prescribing the content is itself part of what makes the practice transmissible. A coach who follows up "make fight agreements" with "and here are the right ones" has already broken the practice.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The discovery that an agreement borrowed from a book felt right intellectually but kept failing in actual fights
- The recognition that "we agreed to this" said about a rule one partner pushed through is actually fuel for resentment, not a stabilizer
- The relief of throwing out an imported rule and replacing it with something specific the two of you actually made together
- The first time a co-created repair phrase actually lands and dissolves a trauma loop, and the recognition that it lands because both of you built it

## Tone

Joe is firm on this one. The "they have to be yours" delivery is direct. He uses himself as the negative example — "not prescribed by me" — to make clear that even hearing the practice from him does not authorize him to set the content. The seriousness is about the structure of the practice, not about any moral weight.
