# Coaches Never Stop Growing and Need Supervision

## The Principle

The role of coach asks a particular thing of the person holding it: ongoing inner development that does not end at any point. There is no graduation. There is no completion. The coach who believes they have arrived is the coach who is becoming dangerous.

The corollary: coaches benefit from supervision the way therapists benefit from supervision. Working with clients triggers the coach. The triggers are data and are useful, but only if there is a structure for processing them outside the client relationship. Without that structure, the coach's unprocessed triggers contaminate the client work.

"I would build upon, I see this both as an opportunity and a little bit of a fear that I have. And that is, there is a belief that 'I've gone through coach training' or 'I have gone through my experience and I'm done.' As we all have experienced, being in conversation, being in partnership with a client, will trigger you. And that's a good thing if you know what you're doing, if you know how to respond to it. There's a reason why therapists by law must have a supervisor. And I would argue that coaches should have that as well." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

Joe affirms directly: "Absolutely."

## The "I'm Still Growing Up" Admission

Jerry continues with the personal admission that anchors the principle:

"And even at 61, I'm still growing up, man. I am still a wreck. And that's a good thing." -- Jerry Colonna, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The admission is the modeling. Jerry has done substantial work: decades of psychoanalysis, decades of coaching, executive roles, the founding of Reboot, the writing of a major book on leadership and coaching. By every conventional measure, he is what a finished coach looks like. His statement is that he is not finished. He is still growing. He is still, in his own framing, a wreck. And that is what makes the work continue to be good.

The framing protects against a specific failure mode: the coach who has accumulated enough credentials and external markers of authority to believe they have arrived. The arrival belief is itself the problem. It cements the current identity, stops the ongoing work, and produces a coach whose inner state is gradually diverging from the level of presence the role requires. The work-with-clients keeps triggering material; the unprocessed triggers accumulate; the coach's blind spots multiply; the client work degrades.

Jerry's "still a wreck" formulation is the inoculation. If even a 61-year-old coach with decades of work is still growing, the would-be coach who thinks they are done after training has a category problem. There is no done.

## Joe's Awakening-Specific Version

Joe adds the version of the principle that addresses the specific trap of having had a peak experience:

"I want to even put a bigger point on that. If you think that you have had an awakening experience, if you think that you're fully enlightened, you are not fucking done." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The version matters because the awakening trap is structurally different from the coach-training trap and is often more dangerous. A coach-training graduate at least knows they are still a student of the craft. A person who has had what they read as an awakening experience often believes they have transcended the need for ongoing work. The work itself becomes the proof that the awakening was incomplete. The person who is actually awake knows they are not done. The person who is sure they are done is the person who used the experience as the final identity to defend.

See [[dont-identify-as-awakened]] for the related anti-pattern and [[epiphany-becomes-rut]] for the structural mechanism. The awakening epiphany is structurally identical to every other epiphany: a real movement that becomes a future constraint if held as the end. The "you are not fucking done" is the protection.

## Why Client Work Triggers the Coach

The triggering is structural, not accidental. The client brings material that the coach has not fully metabolized in their own life. The coach feels it. The feeling is the trigger.

This is a feature, not a bug, if the structure is right. The triggers tell the coach where their own work is. The triggers point at the next areas of development. The triggers connect the coach to the client's experience in a way that makes the coaching possible. The triggers are the signal that the coach is in the work and not just performing it.

But the triggers require processing outside the client relationship. The client did not sign up to be the coach's therapy. The coach who processes triggers in the client room is using the client as personal-development material, which inverts the relationship and harms the client.

The structure that prevents this inversion is supervision: another practitioner who holds the space for the coach to process what came up. The supervisor does for the coach what the coach does for the client. The supervisor's existence allows the coach to be present with clients without the unprocessed triggers leaking into the work.

The supervision requirement is structural. It is not a credential thing or a guild thing. It is the design that allows the coach role to keep working over time. Without it, the coach's triggers accumulate and degrade the work. With it, the coach can keep going indefinitely.

## "Through Coach Training and Done" Is the Wrong Frame

The trap Jerry names is specific: the belief that completing coach training is completion of the development the role requires.

This is a category error. Coach training transmits skills, frames, distinctions, practices. These are foundational. They are not the work. The work is the ongoing inner development that allows the practitioner to actually use the skills, frames, distinctions, and practices in real time with real people without being captured by their own material.

A person can complete coach training and not be ready to coach. A person can be ready to coach without completing the formal training. The training is one piece of evidence about a structurally larger requirement.

The same applies to other forms of "completion." Having had an awakening. Having sold a business. Having raised children. Having survived a major hardship. None of these are completions. Each can be useful in the work. None of them removes the requirement of ongoing development.

## What This Implies for the Coach

Operational consequences:

- **Have a supervisor.** Not a peer-coach with whom you informally process. A supervisor: someone whose role with you is structurally parallel to your role with your clients.
- **Have your own coach or therapist.** Continue to be in the seat your clients are in. The view from that seat keeps you honest about what the work asks.
- **Treat triggers as data.** When a client triggers you, the trigger is information about your own next work. Do not suppress it. Do not act it out in the room. Note it and process it in the appropriate structure.
- **Refuse the done-belief.** When you notice the thought "I have this figured out," treat that thought as evidence that you do not. The done-belief is the structural marker that you are about to make the kind of mistake you would otherwise catch.
- **Expect to be a wreck in some way for the rest of your career.** Jerry's framing. The wreck is not the problem. The wreck is the substrate of the work. The work is to be a wreck who has structures for processing what the wreck-state surfaces.

## What This Implies for the Coach-Trainee

For someone considering coach training:

- The training is the beginning, not the completion. If you are expecting the training to make you a coach, you have a structural misunderstanding of what the role requires.
- The work you will need to do after the training is greater than the work you do during it. Plan for this. Build the structures (supervision, your own coach, peer community) before you start working with clients.
- If you cannot tolerate the continued inner work, the role is not the right one. The role is structurally incompatible with the avoidance of continued inner work. People who try to hold the role while avoiding the work get destroyed by it.

## Connection to the Other Principles in This Conversation

The principle stacks with the other vocational principles articulated in this conversation:

- [[dont-coach-if-you-have-a-choice]]: the call is required as the structural condition for holding the role
- [[transformational-coaching-vs-performance-coaching]]: the deeper version of coaching is what requires the ongoing development; the surface version can be done with less
- This principle: even if the call is present and the depth orientation is right, the ongoing development is required indefinitely

Together they form the picture of what the role asks for: the call, the depth orientation, and the lifelong continued work with the structures to support it. The person who has all three can hold the role. The person who has fewer than all three is doing damage in proportion to what they are missing.

## Tone

Jerry's tone is direct and personally exposed. The "still a wreck" framing is not a humble-brag. It is the actual report. The age (61) makes it specific. The role he has held (Reboot CEO, executive coach to major founders) makes it credible. The admission earns the principle.

Joe's tone is similarly direct, with the punctuation of the awakening-specific framing in stronger language ("you are not fucking done"). The stronger language is calibrated to the specific trap: people are more likely to believe the awakening conferred completion than they are to believe coach training did. The stronger language matches the stronger illusion.

Both are operating from the same underlying principle: the protection of the field requires that the practitioners hold themselves to the standard the role asks for, and the standard is that there is no completion.
