# Stress Comes From Not Doing the Thing

## Definition

The standard cultural picture of stress: too much to do. Overwhelm caused by the size of the load. The definition that actually fits the felt experience: stress comes from not doing the thing you want to be doing.

The thinking about it is the stress. The doing of it is the relief.

"The definition was beautiful in the fact that it wasn't overwhelmed and stressed based on everything you have to do, it was based on not doing it. And I find that to be very much the case that if I am not acting on the thing that I want to be acting on, that there's a lot of stress that shows up in my system. And I noticed that with other people as well, that oftentimes the stress is the thinking about doing it and it's relieved by the doing of it itself." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

## Why This Reframes Ambition

The common complaint: "ambition creates pressure." The reframe: ambition does not create pressure. Ambition is the impetus to move. The pressure comes from the stories attached to the ambition (identity tied to success, fear of the consequences of not achieving) and from sitting in the want without acting on it.

"I would say that ambition doesn't create pressure. I mean, yeah, needing to be of value, thinking that you're who you are is tied to your success and your ambition, that would all create a lot of pressure. But just the raw ambition, it's like, I want to get up and walk out of bed. That's what ambition is... The ambition itself is almost the relief of the pressure." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

See [[raw-ambition-vs-stories-on-ambition]] for the distinction Joe draws between the impetus itself and the stories that turn it into pressure.

## The Diagnostic

When the system is running stressed, the question is not "how do I have less to do?" The question is "what am I not doing that I want to be doing?" The body is sending a signal about the gap between the impulse and the action.

This connects to [[mental-task-inflation]]: the 15-minute task that takes a whole day mentally is the same mechanism on a smaller scale. The task itself takes 15 minutes. The not-doing of it eats the day. The stress is in the gap, not in the task.

It also connects to [[listening-to-impulse]]: the body produces impulses to move. When those impulses are ignored or postponed, the unspent energy becomes the felt experience of stress.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The week feels overwhelming, but a closer look shows that one specific thing the person has been postponing is what's actually charging the rest of the load.
- The morning feels stressful before any work has happened, because the work that wants to be done is being deferred.
- A long mental rehearsal of a conversation produces more discomfort than the conversation itself would.
- "I'm stressed about all this," followed by relief once any one of the items is actually completed.

## The Action Side

The implication is not "stop having things on your plate." The implication is that movement, not stillness, is the relief. The body running an action that it wants to be running discharges what otherwise sits as stress.

This is why [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]] often produces immediate relief: the person stops trying to manage the load mentally and engages the actual thing. The engagement itself is the medicine.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. Joe presents this as an observation, not a productivity hack. The reframe is small but precise: the stress is not what you think it is. Once you see it, you can stop trying to solve the wrong problem.
