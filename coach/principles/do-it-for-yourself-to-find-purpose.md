# Do It For Yourself to Find Purpose

## The Principle

The same activity, done for yourself, is purpose. Done for approval, it is not. The orientation of the doing — who the activity is being done for — determines whether the activity is in the purpose-thread or in the approval-thread. The activities can be identical; the orientation differentiates them.

This is why two musicians can play the same notes and one is in purpose while the other is not. The notes do not differ. The orientation of the playing differs. The musician playing for themselves is in purpose. The musician playing for the audience-as-approver is in the approval-thread, regardless of how skilled they are.

Joe describes his own discovery of this with music:

"When I made music, I was constantly terrified and wanting to know what people thought about it. And though that was living my purpose because it led me here, there is a way in which if I would have said, oh, how do I live my purpose in this music right now? What feels more in my purpose? Immediately it would have been, oh, what's more in my purpose is to write this music for me. To play it in the way that makes sense for me." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The deeper purpose was always available in the same activity. The version-of-the-activity that was in purpose was the version done for himself. The version that was constantly checking with the audience was not the version in purpose, even though it was the same activity.

## The Artist Test

"When you see great artists that are deeply in their purpose, they're doing it for them." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The mark of the artist in purpose is the for-themselves orientation. They are not making the work for the buyer, the critic, the audience, the market. They are making the work because the work has to be made. The audience is incidental; the work is primary.

This produces a recognizable quality in the work. The work has integrity that audience-driven work does not have. It is not optimized for reception. It is what it has to be. Audiences can feel the difference, even when they cannot name it.

The artist test is not about being self-absorbed or ignoring the audience. The audience may still be valued, the work may still be shared, the reception may still matter as feedback. What is different is that the audience is not the source of the doing. The source of the doing is internal. The work would still be made if no one were watching.

## Unassailability

A specific property of the for-yourself orientation: it makes the purpose unassailable.

"Yeah, and that way it becomes unassailable. So the doubt — there's no way to doubt it." -- Brett Kistler, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

If the activity is being done for approval, the absence of approval can collapse the whole structure. The person doubts whether to keep doing it. The validation from outside is needed to keep going. When the validation does not come, the activity becomes hollow.

If the activity is being done for the self, external response cannot collapse it. Approval is welcome; rejection is information. Neither one changes whether the activity is the right activity. The activity is the right activity because of what is happening on the inside, not what is happening on the outside.

This is the deep freedom of the for-yourself orientation. The person becomes independent of external response in a way that was not previously possible. The fear of rejection that comes with purpose (see [[purpose-cracks-you-open]]) still arises, but the rejection cannot actually take the purpose away. The purpose is sourced from a place rejection cannot reach.

## The Approval-Seeking Counterfeit

The approval-seeking version of purpose looks identical from the outside in the early stages. The person is doing an activity they care about. They are working hard. They have a sense of mission. The difference becomes visible at the moment of friction.

The for-yourself version, when faced with friction, doubles down on its own integrity. The work gets more itself. The person stops trying to please.

The approval-seeking version, when faced with friction, doubles down on pleasing. The work gets more diluted. The person tries harder to read what the audience wants.

Both versions are doing the same activity. The friction reveals which orientation is operating. The for-yourself orientation passes through friction. The approval-seeking orientation collapses under it.

See [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] for the diagnostic read on when a stated purpose is actually approval-seeking wearing purpose-language.

## The In-the-Moment Pivot

The for-yourself orientation can be brought into any activity in the present moment. The person does not have to leave the job to do this. They can ask, in the middle of the current task: how would I do this if I were doing it for me?

"If I would have said, oh, how do I live my purpose in this music right now? What feels more in my purpose? Immediately it would have been, oh, what's more in my purpose is to write this music for me." -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The pivot is small. The activity does not change form. The orientation shifts. The musician who was writing for the audience starts writing for themselves. The notes that emerge are different. The experience of writing them is different. The result is different. All from a small internal pivot.

This is also the operationalization of [[purpose-is-how-not-what]]. The how-of-purpose includes the for-yourself orientation. Bringing the how into the current activity means, in part, bringing the for-yourself orientation.

## Why People Default to the Approval Orientation

The approval orientation feels safer. The mind is doing something legible: producing a thing for a known audience whose response can be tracked. The for-yourself orientation requires the person to source their own validation, which means feeling whatever the work feels like to them, which means being present to themselves.

For many people, being present to themselves is what they have been avoiding. The approval orientation lets them stay over in the audience instead of being in themselves. See [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] for the broader pattern of using external orientation to avoid internal experience.

The approval orientation is therefore not just a strategy; it is a way of not being inside. Switching to the for-yourself orientation requires the person to come back into themselves. This is harder than it sounds. The person has to be willing to feel what they feel about the work, which is more vulnerable than reading what others feel about the work.

## What Counts as "For Yourself"

"For yourself" does not mean selfish. It does not mean ignoring others. It does not mean only doing what feels good. It means the source of the action is internal rather than external. The action arises from the person, not from the imagined audience.

A musician writing a song to comfort a friend can be doing it for themselves in this sense — if the comforting is what the musician needs to do, if the song wants to be written, if the action arises from inside rather than from external pressure.

A musician writing the same song because their manager said it would sell can be doing the same activity without doing it for themselves — the source is external, the action is performed for an external return.

The differentiator is not the content of the activity. It is the source of the action. Internal source: for yourself. External source: for approval.

## The Recognition Often Arrives Late

People often do not recognize they have been doing the activity for approval until much later, when they remember a moment of having done it for themselves and notice the difference.

Joe's reflection on his music is in retrospect. At the time, he was doing it for the audience. Looking back, he can see what the for-himself version would have looked like — and recognize that it would have been more in purpose. The recognition is not a judgment; it is a structural seeing-after-the-fact.

For the AI coach: the recognition does not require self-flagellation. The person did what they could do at the time. The recognition opens the option of doing the same activity differently going forward. The past versions are not failures; they are data that the for-yourself version is available and is the version with more aliveness.

## When the Work Lands With Others Anyway

A predictable phenomenon: the work that was done for the doer often lands with others better than the work that was done for the audience. The for-yourself work has integrity, specificity, presence. These qualities resonate.

This is not why the work should be done for the doer. If "do it for yourself because it will land better" becomes the new strategy, the strategy is the approval orientation wearing a different costume. The doer is back to optimizing for response, just through a longer path.

The for-yourself orientation has to be sourced from the for-yourself motive. The fact that it often lands better is a side effect, not the reason. If the reason becomes the landing, the orientation has already flipped back to approval-seeking.

- When someone presents [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] and is asking what their thing should be — the move may be to ask how they could do their current thing for themselves instead of for approval
- When someone describes feeling hollow inside an activity they "should" love — the activity may be being done for approval rather than for the self
- When someone says they want to be a [musician/writer/coach/artist] but is asking how to make it succeed — the success question may be the approval orientation surfacing
- When someone has been doing an activity for years with declining aliveness — the orientation may have drifted from for-self to for-approval over time
- When someone's purpose-question is "what would they want me to do" — the they is the tell
- When someone describes a successful career that feels empty — the success was for them, the orientation was not

## Tone

Light, slightly amused, freeing. The shift from approval-orientation to for-yourself-orientation is small in description and large in effect. Joe describes it as the obvious move once the question is asked — "immediately it would have been, oh, what's more in my purpose is to write this music for me." The tone matches that immediacy: not a heavy teaching, just a structural correction available the moment the question is asked.
