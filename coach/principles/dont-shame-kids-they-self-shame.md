# Don't Shame Kids - They Self-Shame When They Need To

## The Principle

The standard parenting frame: I have to shame my child when they do something wrong, or they will not develop a moral compass. The alternative: the moral compass is in them already, and starts showing up around 5-6 years old. If you do not impose shame, the child will still feel shame when they violate their own values. You do not have to add to it. You actually have to be careful not to.

Joe and his wife Tara raised their two children without punishment and without imposed shame. The result was not amorality. The result was children with a clear sense of when they had done something out of alignment with who they wanted to be, who would shame themselves in those moments, often more harshly than necessary.

"The way Tara and I have decided to raise our kids is that we don't shame them. We don't punish them and we don't shame them. And what I've noticed in them is that they will shame themselves. We don't have to do anything. When they do something that is not in alignment with their moral compass, man, they will shame themselves." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

## The Evidence the Compass Is Built In

The empirical claim: a child raised without imposed shame still self-shames when they violate their own values. The compass develops without needing to be installed. This is the parenting evidence for [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]] -- there is a natural layer, and it develops on its own.

"My experience with my daughters is that the moral compass is in them. You know, I wouldn't say right from the start, but it starts developing around 5, 6 years old, and there's this natural desire to be to be good." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

"They want to love and be loved. Humans want to love and be loved. And you traumatize them, you beat them up, you tell them that they're bad, and they will believe it, and then they will stop... being in touch with that. That desire to love and be loved, and they will start trying to make up with it for control or dominance or violence or whatever." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

The damage is not done by the absence of shaming. The damage is done by the imposition of shaming, which overrides the natural compass with installed conditioning.

## What the Parent Has to Do Instead

If the child will self-shame when they need to, what is the parent's job during those moments? The job is to be the loving witness. Sometimes the job is even to ask the child to feel less shame than they are giving themselves.

"Sometimes they'll even shame themselves when I'm like, 'That's ridiculous. Please don't.' And, I will literally say that to them. I'm like, 'I see that you're ashamed and I want you to know there's nothing in me that wants you to be ashamed. You're welcome to be ashamed, and I can be with you while you're ashamed, but I just want you to know there's nothing in me that wants you to be ashamed.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

See [[welcome-the-shame-without-extracting]] for the specific move. The role of the parent is the opposite of installing shame; it is communicating that no shame is needed from the parent's side, while allowing the child to feel whatever they are feeling.

## Why Imposing Shame Backfires

The shame the parent imposes does not function as the parent thinks it does. The parent imagines: "I shame the behavior, the child learns not to do the behavior." What actually happens is closer to:

- The imposed shame overrides the child's natural compass, which would have functioned better
- The shame locks the behavior in place rather than releasing it. See [[shame-drives-behavior]]
- The shame teaches the child that part of them is unacceptable, producing the architecture that will need years of adult work to undo. See [[childhood-imprinting]]
- The shame trains the child to seek the parent's reactions for self-evaluation, rather than developing the internal compass

The parent who shames does not realize they are competing with a built-in mechanism that would have done a better job. The imposition does not add to the natural compass. It replaces it with a worse one.

## What "Natural Compass" Is Pointing At

The natural shame in children, in Joe's observation, arises in response to things humans across cultures tend to find shame-worthy: hurting someone they love, betraying trust, deception that harms. It does not arise in response to the arbitrary tribal norms (farts, certain foods, certain forms of expression) that get pinned onto children by adults who have themselves been shaped by them.

This is why the natural compass is more trustworthy than the imposed one. The natural compass tracks human-scale ethics. The imposed compass tracks tribe-scale conformity. A child who follows the natural compass becomes an ethical adult. A child who follows the imposed compass becomes a conforming one, often at the cost of their own truth.

## The Self-Shaming Can Be Excessive

A complication: kids who self-shame may do so more harshly than necessary. Imposed shame has been overruled, but the natural-shame mechanism does not have a built-in volume control calibrated to the situation. Joe describes saying to his daughters "that's ridiculous, please don't" when their self-shame seems out of proportion to what happened.

The parent's job in those moments is the [[welcome-the-shame-without-extracting]] move: communicate that the parent does not want them to be in shame, while allowing the child to feel what they are feeling. Over time the child learns calibration.

## The Connection to Wants and Discipline

This principle is closely related to [[wants-over-shoulds]]. Both rest on [[inherent-goodness]]. Both observe that the child does not need to be controlled by external pressure; the child has internal mechanisms that, when not overridden, produce ethical behavior.

It is also related to [[dont-extract-apologies]]: do not extract apologies from kids. The apology produced under duress installs shame; the apology that arises naturally arises from the natural compass. See [[upright-apology]] for what an apology from inside the natural compass looks like.

## What This Looks Like Practically

- No punishment, no time-outs, no consequences imposed by the parent
- When the child does something wrong, the parent does not add a layer of disapproval; they witness
- The child often shames themselves; the parent communicates [[welcome-the-shame-without-extracting]]
- The parent draws boundaries about what they want or do not want, but does not load those boundaries with shame
- The relationship is the primary regulatory mechanism, not the parent's judgment

The result, in Joe's experience: children who develop a strong moral compass, who do not have an excessive inner critic, who can self-correct without self-brutalization. The compass works better when it is not buried under installed conditioning.
