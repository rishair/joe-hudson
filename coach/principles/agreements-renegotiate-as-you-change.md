# Renegotiate Commitments as You Change (The Only Commitment Is to the Relationship)

## The Principle

The fixed commitments in a long-term relationship are not roles, schedules, or specific agreements. They are: the commitment to be committed to the relationship, and the commitment to support each other's freedom. Everything else has to renegotiate as the people change.

Vows, schedules, sexual agreements, division of labor, parenting roles, time together, time apart, the boundary around the marriage -- all of these are working agreements at a point in time. The person you were when you made the agreement is not the person you will be in five years. The agreement that worked then will not necessarily work now. The work is to renegotiate, not to enforce.

"I don't really believe in commitments in the relationship outside of the commitment to be committed to the relationship... The way -- what Tara needed when she was 26 is not what Tara needs now. And what I needed when I was 26 is not -- when we got married -- is not what I need now." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## What This Refutes

The principle refutes the framing that a marriage is held together by the fixity of its agreements. The agreement was set; now we keep it; that is what marriage is. In this framing, the renegotiation of a commitment is a violation of the marriage.

In Joe's framing, the reverse is true. The unwillingness to renegotiate is what kills the marriage. The agreement made at 26 was made by people who do not exist anymore. The current people are pretending to be those people because the agreement was made by those people. The pretending corrodes both.

"I think that the agreement we have is like, how do we support each other's growth and be there for each other in that way? And, and that's our priority. And that's like, that's the commitment we have to the relationship. And so we both get to experience a lot of freedom, and we both get to experience, you know, support and tenderness and care. And but we don't feel like we have to be a certain way for the other person or have to maintain some way of being for the other person." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## What Has to Be Renegotiable

Joe is explicit that the list is long, and pointedly includes things normally treated as untouchable:

"This idea of commitment, even sexual commitment, or commitment to agreements or roles or ways of being or dishes, or like, all that needs to be renegotiated as our needs change." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The included list:
- Sexual exclusivity / non-exclusivity
- Roles in the household
- Ways of being together
- Schedules and time agreements
- How money is handled
- How decisions are made
- Dishes and other operational logistics
- The shape of physical intimacy
- The shape of emotional intimacy

Nothing on this list is sacred enough to remain fixed against the people's actual current needs. The marriage is sacred. The specific agreements are working tools.

## Less Rules, More Attunement

Brett's distillation: "Less rules, more attunement." Joe affirms this immediately.

The rules-based framing tries to substitute fixed agreements for ongoing attention. It is appealing because it offers certainty. It fails because the people change underneath the rules. The rules then either get enforced against the changed people (producing resentment) or quietly violated (producing dishonesty).

The attunement-based framing requires the harder thing: ongoing presence to what is actually happening between you and your partner, and ongoing willingness to renegotiate as that changes.

"It depends on like, do you have kids? Do you -- are you taking -- is somebody taking care of a baby, you know? You know, are you going through menopause? Are you, you know, like, right, all of those things have an effect and they're all biological changes that affect the agreement in the relationship if we're, if we're actually being attuned." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The biological reference is not incidental. The body changes. The needs that come from the body change. An agreement that was right when both partners were 26 and healthy may be wrong when one is pregnant, or recovering from surgery, or in menopause, or grieving a parent, or post-deployment. The work is to notice and renegotiate.

## The Non-Monogamy Example

Joe and Alexa both reference the polyamory frame because it makes the principle obvious. Couples who have been in ethical non-monogamy for 25 years have made huge transitions in their agreements: from open to slightly open to monogamous to open only together, and back. The agreements changed because the people changed. The relationship survived because the agreements moved.

"I've worked with clients that are, you know, have open relationships, those agreements always are changing and they have to. They like, they might have an agreement but it's always temporary, right? You know, and, and the couples that I know who have been doing that you know, having that lifestyle for now 25 years of marriage, um, their agreements have totally changed through that time frame." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The non-monogamy frame makes visible what is also true of monogamous marriages: agreements that survive are agreements that have been renegotiated. They just look like the same agreement because the words happen to still match.

## The "Rules to Make My Relationship Work" Trap

Alexa describes a common question she gets: "What rules will make my relationship work?" Her response is that this is not how she makes her relationships work. There are no rules that will make it work. There is only ongoing attunement and renegotiation.

The rules question is appealing because it offers a tractable problem. It promises that if you find the right rule, the work is done. But the relationship is not waiting for the right rule. The relationship is waiting for both people to be present and willing to renegotiate as the needs evolve.

This is consonant with [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]]: a fixed boundary that was right for one stage stops being right for the next stage. Boundaries refine; agreements refine; the relationship is the practice of letting them refine.

## What Stays Fixed

The two things that do not renegotiate:

**1. The commitment to be committed to the relationship.** This means: we are not exiting the relationship at the first sign of difficulty. We have agreed to do the work, including the work of renegotiation, before exit is on the table.

**2. The commitment to support each other's growth.** This means: my growth and your growth are the priorities, and we both treat the relationship as a vehicle for those, not as a constraint on them. See [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]].

These two commitments are what makes the rest of the principle workable. Without them, "renegotiation" can become "I'm changing the agreement to whatever I want unilaterally." With them, renegotiation is a collaborative process between two people committed to the underlying frame.

## How Renegotiation Happens

Renegotiation is operationalized through:

- [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]]: surfacing what is no longer working
- [[dont-compromise-find-both-needs-met]]: how to find a new agreement that works for both
- Periodic conversations that explicitly check whether current agreements still fit
- Permission to bring up something that "we already agreed on" without being accused of breaking the agreement
- Treating an unworkable agreement as data, not as a sign someone is failing

## When Someone Experiences This

- The relief of being given permission to want something different than what was agreed
- The discovery that an agreement made in good faith years ago no longer fits
- The fear of bringing up a renegotiation because the partner might experience it as betrayal
- The recognition, in a long marriage, that the original agreement is being silently violated by both people
- The aliveness that returns when the agreements are allowed to move

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A long-term couple presents with a specific agreement that no longer works
- A person asks "what rules should I have in my relationship?"
- A person is afraid to bring up a change they want because of the original commitment
- A couple has just been through a major life change (kids, illness, career shift) and the old agreements are creaking
- A person is considering whether to stay in a marriage they made when they were very different
- A person is in non-monogamy or considering it and asking about the right rules

## Tone

Joe is light and direct on this one. He acknowledges he does not even remember his own wedding vows -- the joke lands because the principle is real. The seriousness is in the structure, not in the delivery. The point is that the relationship is alive only when the agreements are allowed to be alive.
