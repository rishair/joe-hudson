# Manage Energy, Not Time

## Definition

A vast amount of cultural attention goes to time management. Energy management gets a fraction of the attention and is far higher leverage. A person who manages their time well but ignores their energy ends up burned out. A person who manages their energy well can do more in fewer hours than the person managing only their time.

"There's a huge amount of time and energy put towards time management, but I think energy management is critical. And you can spend a lot more time if you're really managing your energy well." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

The shift is from asking "how do I fit more into my hours" to asking "what is draining and what is replenishing my capacity to do anything at all."

## The Reasoning

Time is fixed. Every person has the same hours. Energy is variable. Two people doing the same task for the same hours can end the day with completely different amounts of remaining capacity, depending on how they did it.

The person who organizes around energy can do an eighty-hour week and end it with more aliveness than they started. The person who organizes only around time can do a forty-hour week and end it depleted. The difference is not the work. It is what was tracked.

This is the operational version of [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]]. Enjoyment is one of the signals that energy is being managed well. Depletion is the signal that it is not.

## The Audit

The practice that follows from the principle: pay attention to what is producing adrenaline and what is producing replenishment.

"That's another thing is to just pay attention to when you're running on adrenaline and what's creating that and what's a way to do it where you're not running on adrenaline to get that same job done. And if you problem solve there, then you have so much more energy." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

For each draining task: what would have to be different for this to give energy rather than take it? See [[how-could-you-do-this-and-gain-energy]] for the coaching move version of this question.

## The Anchor Examples

The on-time pattern: a person who must always be on time will run from meeting to meeting on adrenaline. The cost is paid by the body. The cause is usually a parent who got angry when the person was late as a child. The undoing of the childhood pattern -- recognizing that being five minutes late is not a threat anymore -- frees up the adrenaline that was being spent on the constant micro-rushing.

"If you always have to be on time, which is something that I have struggled with, then usually that means there's a parent who you got in trouble with if you weren't on time, and therefore you're like running from meeting to meeting. That's a lot of adrenaline use." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

The same logic applies to every "I have to" that is structural in someone's life. Each one is an adrenaline cost. Each one points back to a psychological source that, when seen, frees the energy.

## The Productivity Inversion

The person who manages energy well does not work fewer hours. They work the same hours or more. The difference is they have energy left at the end. They sustain. They do not collapse.

"That's another thing is to just pay attention to when you're running on adrenaline and what's creating that and what's a way to do it where you're not running on adrenaline to get that same job done. And if you problem solve there, then you have so much more energy, which is why I've done a lot of that work and it's why I can work the way I work at the speed in which I work and not burn out." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

The claim is concrete: Joe attributes his ability to work the way he works without burning out to having done the energy-management work. The hours are similar to other people's. The internal cost is much lower.

## When Someone Experiences This

- When they have optimized their calendar to the millimeter and are still exhausted.
- When they read time-management books and feel temporarily better and then collapse again.
- When the question "how do I fit more in?" has reached its limits.
- When they notice that two days with the same calendar can leave them in completely different states depending on how they showed up.

## In Practice

The shift is from quantity (hours, tasks completed) to quality (what is the energy state at the end of the activity). The diagnostic question after any activity: am I more energized or more depleted? See [[invigorated-vs-depleted-as-functionality-measure]] for the team version of this same diagnostic.

The action that follows from the diagnostic: for each depleting activity, find a way to do it that energizes or stop doing it. For each energizing activity, do more of it. Over a year, the cumulative effect is dramatic.
