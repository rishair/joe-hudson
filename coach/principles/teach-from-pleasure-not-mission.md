# Teach From Pleasure, Not From Mission

## The Principle

The most honest teaching comes from a teacher whose answer to "why are you doing this?" is some version of "I love it." Not "the world needs this." Not "to save lives." Not "to fix something." Just: I find this deeply enjoyable, and doing it with other people enriches the enjoyment. The teaching that runs on this motive lasts. The teaching that runs on mission, savior energy, or duty distorts itself and the people who receive it.

Joe articulates this directly in response to Brett naming that his retreat is not about saving anyone:

"I love the idea that you're teaching for your own pleasure. I think that's so critical. Not to be there to save the world, not to be there because the world needs you, not to be there to save a single person. It feels to me like the most honest teachings are the ones where the teacher's like, I'm here because I love it." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The principle is held in the word "honest." Teaching from pleasure is honest because it accurately names what the teacher is getting from the act. Teaching from mission obscures the same exchange and adds a layer of obligation that the recipient ends up paying for.

## Why Mission-Teaching Distorts

The teacher who is teaching to save people positions the people as in need of saving. The framing seeps into every interaction. The students are receivers, the teacher is the giver. The students are broken, the teacher is the source of the fix. The students owe the teacher gratitude, the teacher owes the students patience. The structure is set up before any actual teaching happens, and the structure shapes what is possible inside it.

The student in this structure has a hard time becoming their own authority. The implicit message is that without the teacher, the student is still in danger. The teacher's job becomes self-perpetuating. The student's growth threatens the teacher's purpose.

The teacher in this structure is also stuck. Their motive depends on the students continuing to need them. They cannot fully celebrate the students' independence because independence undermines the savior premise. The savior teacher is structurally compromised in the work of [[become-unnecessary]].

Worse, the savior motive is usually a transfer from earlier life. Often the teacher was the kid who managed the difficult parent's emotions, who held the family together, who was responsible for making the dysfunctional adults okay. That role generated a particular set of skills and a particular sense of self-worth tied to others' recovery. Teaching becomes the adult continuation of the childhood role. The teaching becomes a vehicle for managing the teacher's own un-felt childhood material rather than for serving the students. See [[coaching-as-savior-role]] for the specific application to coaching.

## Why Pleasure-Teaching Cleans This Up

The teacher who teaches because they love it has no equivalent distortion. They are not getting their unmet childhood needs met through the teaching. They are getting pleasure -- a direct, present-tense, non-extractive exchange of enjoyment. The students are not positioned as needing them. The students are positioned as fellow participants in something the teacher finds wonderful.

This produces a different structure of relating. The teacher is genuinely interested in the students because the students are part of what the teacher enjoys. The students are not in danger if they grow past the teacher; the teacher is happy to watch them grow because the watching is also pleasurable. The teaching is sustainable because the motive is renewable -- pleasure is generated in the act itself, not extracted from the students' need.

Brett's example carries the form:

"I don't need somebody to be like, oh cool, I did the welcoming fear trip and now I want to go jump off of cliffs. And I'm like, great, I saved you. You're going to be safer. It's really just like, I love being in that exploration. That's what I've loved about jumping from the very beginning. And I love being with people in it." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The teaching has an outcome -- some students will be safer, some students will have epiphanies -- but the outcome is not what motivates the teaching. The pleasure of being in the exploration is what motivates the teaching. The outcomes follow.

## What This Does Not Mean

The principle does not mean teaching is selfish in the small sense. The pleasure that motivates the teacher is genuine, deep, and includes the joy of being with the students. The teacher is not exploiting anyone; they are sharing something they love with people who want what they have.

This is structurally aligned with [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]]: the deepest forms of selfishness and altruism converge. The teacher who is fully in their own enjoyment is also the teacher who is most genuinely available to others. The teacher who has suppressed their own enjoyment in favor of mission is less available, even though they look more dedicated on the surface.

The principle also does not mean missions are illegitimate. A person can have a mission. They can care deeply about an outcome in the world. What matters is that the teaching itself runs on pleasure rather than on the mission. If the missionary motive is what gets the teacher into the room, the pleasure motive is what should be running once the room is full. The motive that drives the room is the motive the students absorb.

## The "Saving Lives" Inversion

The specific inversion this transcript reveals: Brett describes how he used to think a lot of what he was doing was about saving lives. As his practice matured, that fell away. What replaced it was the recognition that he loved being in the exploration with people. The shift did not make his work less impactful. It probably made it more impactful, because the savior energy that had been adding distortion was no longer in the way.

"A few years ago, I would have said a lot of what I'm doing here is about saving lives. And interestingly, I feel like that's kind of fallen away. It's like, I don't believe that I -- that anyone needs me to save them." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The fall-away is the maturation. The early-stage teacher often runs on mission because mission is the available legitimating story. The later-stage teacher has done enough work on themselves to recognize that the mission was a cover for something else (the savior pattern, the wanting to be seen as important, the unmet childhood needs). The cover comes off; what remains underneath is the pleasure of the work itself. The pleasure is what the teaching actually rests on, and the teaching is better for the recognition.

## What Joe Listens For

When evaluating a teacher, facilitator, or coach -- whether for training, for hiring, for following, for trusting -- Joe listens for the answer to "why are you doing this." The answer is diagnostic:

- "I love it" or "I find this fascinating" or "I can't not do it": pleasure-motivated. Trust the teaching.
- "Because the world needs this": mission-motivated. The teaching may still have value, but ask what the teacher is getting out of the mission. The savior pattern is usually somewhere underneath.
- "I want to help people who are suffering": well-meaning, but at risk of [[coaching-as-savior-role]]. The student becomes the suffering one in need of the helper.
- "I feel called to do this": ambiguous; could be either. Ask what the call feels like in the body. Pleasure-calls feel expansive. Duty-calls feel constricting.
- "Because I have important things to say": the wanting-to-be-seen-as-the-knower variant. See [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]].

The diagnostic is not about the words but about what is underneath them. A teacher who says "I love this" because they have learned the principle, but who is actually running on savior energy, will eventually show the savior energy in how they teach. The structural motive is more honest than the stated one.

## What This Asks of the Teacher

The principle requires the teacher to do the inner work that lets pleasure be the actual motive. Teachers who have not yet done this work cannot simply decide to be pleasure-motivated; the savior or mission or knowing motives are operating in them whether or not they want them to. The work is to feel what the savior motive is covering (often childhood material about responsibility for others' affect), let it through, and find what is underneath. What is underneath is often the simple pleasure that was the original draw to the work.

This is the same work that the four foundational ingredients in [[training-coaches-and-facilitators]] are organized around. The teacher who has done this work can teach from pleasure. The teacher who has not done this work is still teaching from the patterns the work would have released.

## In Coaching

When a coachee says they want to teach, lead, coach, or facilitate, the diagnostic question is some version of "why." The answer reveals which motive is operating. The coaching path from there:

- If the answer is pleasure-driven, the coaching is about building the capacity to do the teaching well. The motive does not need work; the skills do.
- If the answer is mission-driven, the coaching is about surfacing what the mission is covering. The mission itself may be real, but what it is doing for the teacher's psyche is the work. Until that is surfaced, the mission will be the actual content of the teaching, regardless of the explicit curriculum.
- If the answer is unclear ("I just feel called"), the coaching is about getting curious about the call. What does it feel like in the body? Does it feel expansive or duty-laden? Where does it come from? What gets fed by following it?

The point is not to disqualify mission-driven teachers. The point is to make the motive visible so it can be worked. A mission-driven teacher who knows they are mission-driven, and is doing the work to integrate the underlying material, can teach well. A mission-driven teacher who insists the motive is pure pleasure, while continuing to run on savior energy, distorts everything they teach.

## In Leadership

The same principle applies to leaders. The CEO who is running the company because they love it differs from the CEO running it to prove something, to escape something, or to save something. The love-of-it motive produces a different company culture than the proving, escaping, or saving motives.

This is why succession in mission-driven organizations is so hard. The founder's mission was the engine. When the founder leaves, the engine leaves with them. An organization that ran on the founder's love-of-the-work has a chance of sustaining the love-of-the-work in the next generation, because the love can be transmitted as a felt experience rather than as an inherited duty.

See [[business-as-spiritual-practice]] for the broader application.

## What This Produces Externally

Teachers who teach from pleasure are reliably the teachers students return to over decades. The pleasure is contagious in a way that mission is not. The students find themselves enjoying the work because the teacher is enjoying it. The pleasure shows up in the room, the room becomes a place students want to be, and the students return because the room continues to be that.

Teachers who teach from mission produce shorter relationships. The students often complete their initial reason for studying with the teacher and move on. The mission energy is heavy; the heaviness is what eventually causes the student to need a break. The students may return for a refresher; they do not stay in the room year after year because the room is not a place anyone wants to be year after year.

This is the structural reason for the durability of pleasure-motivated teachers. The room they create is one people want to be in. The room mission-motivated teachers create is one people pass through.

## Tone

Joe offers this with warmth and a kind of relief. The frame is liberating, especially for teachers who have been carrying the weight of mission. The tone communicates that the lighter motive is the more legitimate one, which inverts the conventional intuition that the heavier motive is the more dedicated one. The lightness of the principle's content is part of why it lands. A heavy delivery of "teach from pleasure not mission" would contradict itself.
