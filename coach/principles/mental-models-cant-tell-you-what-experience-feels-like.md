# Mental Models Can't Tell You What an Experience Feels Like

## The Principle

The search for purpose conducted in the head is a sequence of mental models — thoughts about what each possible thing would be like, evaluated for whether it might be the answer. The fatal flaw of this search method: a thought about doing a thing carries no information about what doing the thing actually feels like. The model can be perfectly constructed and produce no useful data, because the model and the experience operate in different registers.

You cannot know whether being an artist will fulfill you by thinking about being an artist. You cannot know whether running a company will fulfill you by imagining running a company. The only thing that knows is the doing. Until the doing has happened, the model is the only available data, and the model is not the data.

"What you're doing when you're searching for your purpose is you're taking it outside of the moment. You're not actually experimenting with what's going on in your world. Instead, you're just running mental model after mental model. And the problem with each one of those mental models is you have no idea what it feels like to do the thing. You only have a thought of what it would be like to do the thing. You don't actually know what it feels like to do the thing." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

## The Five-Year-Old Swimmers Analogy

Joe's daughter, at fifteen, told him she had been searching her whole life for the special thing that would get her into a great college. Her search method was to think about something, wonder if that was the thing, conclude it was not, and start over.

Joe gave her the analogy:

"That's like looking at 10 5-year-old boys and saying, which one is gonna be the world champion swimmer? You can't know until they get in the pool, until you see how they swim, until they have a drive, until you see how, if they like water, if they're scared of water, if they have any passion about swimming. You actually have to go and investigate the thing to understand what the outcome is gonna be." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

Five-year-old boys standing on the deck contain no information about which one will become the world champion. The boys have to get in the pool. The pool reveals what the dry-land observation cannot reveal. The same boys, after a year in the pool, contain large amounts of information — about technique, about drive, about love of water, about fear, about all the variables that determine whether a person becomes a champion swimmer.

Purpose works the same way. The thinking-about-it is the dry-land observation. The doing is the pool. No amount of dry-land observation will produce what one session in the pool produces.

## What Mental Modeling Actually Produces

When the person runs a mental model of a possible purpose, what they generate is:

- **Their idea of how the activity would feel.** This is constructed from media, from secondhand reports, from imagined scenarios, from projections of current emotional states onto future situations.
- **A comparison between this idea and other ideas.** The mental model gets weighed against other mental models, all of which suffer from the same construction problem.
- **A felt-response to the idea, not to the activity.** The body produces real feelings, but they are feelings about the imagined version, not about the actual version. The person can feel excited about an imagined career and then enter the actual career and find it does not contain what the imagination promised.

What mental modeling does not produce: the actual data. The actual data comes only from the actual experience. The mental modeling consumes attention without delivering what the search frame promised it would deliver.

## The Investment Banking Example

Joe's own arc demonstrates the cost of mental modeling. He hated investment banking. He spent seven years chasing the mental model of being an artist. He arrived at being an artist — observing director on a TV show — and discovered the actual structure was the structural duplicate of investment banking. One person in charge, everyone working long hours, everyone unhappy, everyone wanting to be in the next place.

"I have been running for 7 years towards an idea of what I want, only to find out that I was running towards the thing I was running away from." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

The seven years of running were premised on a mental model of art. The mental model did not contain the information about the actual structure of working in TV. The structural information was only available inside the doing. The seven years were the cost of mistaking the model for the territory.

See [[creative-career-story]] for the full arc and [[running-toward-the-thing-youre-running-from]] for the pattern this exemplifies.

## What to Do Instead

If mental modeling does not work, the alternative is direct investigation. Two operational forms:

1. **Say yes to what is presented.** Whatever opportunity arrives, take it. The doing produces information that the not-doing cannot. After a while, enough yeses produce enough data that the narrowing becomes possible — say yes only to the things that are actually exciting. See [[creative-career-story]] for the full operational arc.

2. **Run the in-the-moment check.** In any given moment, the felt-sense can be queried: what would feel more in purpose right now? The answer arrives from the body, not from a constructed model. See [[what-feels-more-in-purpose-right-now]] and [[what-is-my-purpose-asking-me-to-do-right-now]].

Both forms share the same structure: get out of the head, into the experience, and let the experience produce the data.

## Why This Is Hard to Accept

The mental-model approach is comfortable. It happens entirely inside the head. It does not require risk, action, or vulnerability. The person can spend years generating models without ever exposing themselves to the consequences of being wrong about any of them.

The experience-only approach is uncomfortable. It requires saying yes to things the model has not pre-approved. It requires the willingness to enter a situation that may turn out to be wrong. It requires updating the search based on data the head did not predict.

For a mind heavily identified with planning and prediction, the experience-only approach feels like abandoning the search. It is not abandoning the search; it is changing the unit-of-investigation from thought to action. The investigation continues. It just stops being conducted in the wrong register.

## The Special Case: Mental Modeling About People

A common application of this principle: trying to figure out whether a person is the right partner, friend, or collaborator by running mental models of being with them. The model produces a guess. The guess is built from limited data and shaped by current emotional state. The actual relationship can only be known by being in it.

The same structural error: substituting the model for the experience. The same structural cost: years lost to the wrong evaluation method. The same structural exit: actual engagement, with attention to what the body reports in real time, not to what the head predicts in advance.

## The Limit of the Principle

The principle is not "never think about anything." It is "thinking does not produce the experiential knowledge that thinking is being asked to produce." Some thinking is useful — strategic, logistical, evaluative thinking about constraints. The thinking that fails is the thinking that imagines it can substitute for direct contact with a domain it cannot reach.

The diagnostic: is the thinking generating useful operational steps, or is the thinking generating models of how-it-would-feel? The first is fine. The second is the mode this principle is naming as the trap.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] and is describing a years-long search conducted primarily in the head
- When someone is evaluating a possible career, relationship, or move by running scenarios in their head and weighing the imagined outcomes
- When someone is asking "what would it be like if I did X?" expecting the question to be answerable from current information
- When someone has not yet noticed that all their data about possible purposes is constructed, not observed
- When someone is in the [[running-toward-the-thing-youre-running-from]] pattern — the running is always sponsored by a mental model of the destination

## Tone

Practical, light. The principle is delivered as a structural correction, not as a critique of thinking. Joe's swimmers analogy is delivered with affection — the daughter is not wrong for trying; she is using the wrong method, which everyone in her stage uses, and the correction is available. The reframe is meant to relieve, not to shame.
