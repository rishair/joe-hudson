# Self-Blame Abdicates the Other Person's Agency

## The Principle

Taking all the blame in a relationship is not the generous move it appears to be. When one person says "this is all my fault, I screwed up, it's on me," they are also implicitly saying that the other person is not accountable -- not strong enough, not capable enough, not adult enough to hold their part of the situation. The self-blame removes the other person from the situation as an agent.

"If I'm in a relationship and I'm saying, I screwed up, I did it wrong, like this is all my fault in this relationship. One thing I'm doing is abdicating any kind of accountability for the other person. So that's going to be... like I'm basically telling them they're weak, they're not accountable, they're -- I'm better than them because I'm responsible for everything. And they're just like along for the ride, as if they're not like -- they don't have agency. They're not their own human being. That's the -- that's demeaning." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

This is a counter-intuitive principle because the cultural script reads self-blame as humility and accountability as virtue. The principle locates the cost: the partner is being treated as a passenger in their own life.

## The Covert Superiority Inside Self-Blame

The principle reveals a structural feature of "I'll take all the blame": it is a position of covert superiority. The person taking all the blame is implicitly placing themselves above the partner -- as the one with agency, the one who could have done differently, the one whose actions matter. The partner is placed below -- as the one who is just along for the ride.

"I'm better than them because I'm responsible for everything. And they're just like along for the ride." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

This is not an obvious move. The person doing it usually experiences themselves as humble, responsible, and self-critical. The covert side does not surface in self-experience. It surfaces in the impact on the partner, who often feels diminished, infantilized, or condescended to without being able to say why.

The principle does not say the self-blamer is consciously asserting superiority. It says the structure of taking all the blame produces this effect regardless of the self-blamer's experience. The structural move is the relevant unit, not the intention.

## Why This Is Different From Healthy Ownership

Healthy ownership of one's part in a situation is not self-blame. The difference is the totality and the structural exclusion of the other person.

- **Healthy ownership:** "I see how I contributed to this. I see where I want to do differently." The partner remains as an agent in the situation. The work is on the speaker's part. The partner's part is the partner's to address.
- **Self-blame:** "This is all my fault. I'm responsible for everything. You're just along for the ride." The partner is removed as an agent. The speaker has claimed the whole situation as theirs to fix.

The first version respects the partner's adulthood. The second version takes the partner's adulthood from them.

The diagnostic: does the framing leave the partner with anything to own, or has the speaker claimed everything? If everything is claimed, the move is self-blame and the cost is the partner's agency.

## The Caretaking Connection

The principle is a special case of the dynamic in [[caretaking]]. The caretaker takes care of the other person's pain, responsibility, or growth in ways that abdicate the other person's capacity to do those things for themselves. The self-blamer does the same with responsibility for the relationship: they take all of it, and in taking all of it, they communicate that the partner does not have the capacity to hold any of it.

The intention is usually love. The effect is usually demeaning. The pattern shows up most clearly in long-term partnerships where one person has been "taking responsibility for the whole relationship" for years and the partner has receded into passivity that the self-blamer then resents.

The path out is the same as in caretaking generally: stop carrying the part that is not yours to carry. Trust that the other person has their own capacity to engage. The trust is what gives them back their agency.

## The Self-Blame Path of Healing Doesn't Heal

The principle has a second dimension beyond the demeaning effect: self-blame also doesn't work for the self-blamer.

"That's the -- that's demeaning. But the other thing that's happening is that I am just doing that so I don't have to feel something underneath. So I don't actually have to feel the thing that's underneath the blame, which is where my healing happens. My healing doesn't happen by saying, 'I'm bad. I'm fucked up. I need to improve.'" -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The self-blame is doing the same shame-installation move as other-blame, only with the target turned inward. It prevents the feeling of whatever is underneath the blame, which is where the actual healing would happen. See [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]] for the developmental arc and [[shame-drives-behavior]] for why self-criticism does not produce change.

So the principle has two costs:
1. **External cost:** the partner is treated as not having agency, which damages them and the relationship.
2. **Internal cost:** the self-blamer is using the self-blame to avoid feeling the thing underneath, which prevents their own healing.

Both costs are paid simultaneously. The self-blame is functioning as a defense on both sides.

## What Makes the Principle Hard to See

The self-blame mode is socially rewarded. People who say "it's all my fault" are read as humble, responsible, accountable. The cultural script does not surface the demeaning effect on the partner. The self-blamer's friends often validate the framing ("at least you're taking responsibility"), which reinforces the move.

The demeaning effect tends to be felt only by the partner, and the partner often cannot name what is wrong with being on the receiving end of "I'll take all the blame." It feels off. It feels like something is being taken from them. They can't quite say what.

What is being taken is their agency. The naming of it is the relief.

## The Coaching Move

When a person presents with the framing "I'm taking responsibility, it's all my fault, I'm the one who needs to fix this," the coaching move is not to validate or to argue with the self-blame directly. The move is to surface the abdication.

The question Joe is implicitly asking: "What about your partner's responsibility? What about the parts of this that are theirs to hold? What is it doing to them when you take all of it?"

The reframe: ownership of your part, not ownership of all of it. Healthy ownership leaves the partner intact as an adult. Self-blame removes them from the room.

## When This Connects to the Three Stages

This principle is the specific cost of stage two in [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]]. Stage one (blaming the other) has the cost of installing shame in the partner. Stage two (blaming the self) has the cost of removing the partner's agency. Both are corrosive. The cost of stage two is the one that is harder to see, because the move looks like accountability.

The recognition of this cost is part of what makes stage three available. Once a person sees that self-blame is not the destination -- not just because it doesn't heal them but because it demeans the partner -- the path forward becomes about neither blaming the other nor blaming the self. It becomes about [[how-do-we-want-to-be-together]] as the forward-looking question that does not run on blame in any direction.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a slightly surprised note, as if he is making a point that he expects the listener will not have considered. The frame is not "you are a bad person for self-blaming." The frame is "look at what this is doing to the other person, which you probably haven't seen." The tone is matter-of-fact discovery, not accusation.
