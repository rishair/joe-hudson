# Encouraging Someone Who Has Negative Self-Talk Agrees With Their Self-Attack

## The Principle

When a partner has an internal voice that says "I'm not good enough, my work isn't good enough, I should be more, I should do more," the well-intentioned encouragement that says "you can do it, just put it out there, everyone's going to love it, don't be afraid" agrees with the structure of the internal critic. The encouragement only makes sense if the deficit being asserted by the critic is real. The cheerleader and the inner critic are saying the same thing: "you currently are not what you should be, and here is what would close the gap."

The person being encouraged receives both the encouragement and the structural confirmation of the deficit. The encouragement and the critic are pointing at the same target. The encourager is unwittingly the critic's collaborator.

"So the fact that you're taking care of him and you're agreeing that he's somewhat of a victim in this situation and that he needs some caretaking is ensuring his position as a victim. That fucking sucks. That sucks." -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

## The Mechanism

The encouragement assumes a position the receiver does not currently hold. The act of asserting "you can do it" encodes "I see that you currently doubt you can do it." See [[pep-talk-encodes-the-deficit]] for the structural argument about why this happens whenever a positive affirmation runs on top of a contrary belief.

This principle extends the mechanism into the relationship. When the encouragement is being delivered by a partner to address the partner's negative self-talk, two additional dynamics activate:

1. **The partner is being positioned as needing the encouragement.** This is structurally identical to being positioned as a victim of their own self-talk. The encourager has agreed that the self-talk has a legitimate target. The target is the partner-as-deficient.

2. **The encouragement perpetuates the deficit by treating it as real.** The cheerleader has stepped into the position of "I am the one who knows you are more than you think." The implicit content is "you are not yet what you should be, and you need someone to tell you otherwise to behave like you are."

Both dynamics keep the partner inside the deficit frame. The encouragement is the wrong-direction intervention. It treats the symptom (the partner's perception that they are not enough) as if increasing the volume of the opposite signal will dissolve the perception. The opposite signal only confirms the perception by responding to it.

## The Live Example

A wife describes encouraging her husband to publish his book: "You can, you're going to be a New York Times bestseller before me. He's written screenplays and he's written a book, and I'm like, just put it out there. Like, everyone's going to love it. Don't be afraid. Just do it."

The wife also notes that her husband has "such negative self-talk." Joe's response names the structural problem:

"Either way, I'm just perpetuating his negative self-talk." -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

He clarifies the mechanism with a test question: "Why? Who would you say that? Would you say that to me? No. Right."

The test is diagnostic. You would not say "you can do it, you're going to be a New York Times bestseller" to a peer whose competence you respect. You only deliver that kind of encouragement to someone you have, however subtly, positioned as needing it. The form of the encouragement reveals the positioning.

## What This Anti-Pattern Often Hides

The encouragement-of-the-partner pattern frequently disguises one of these:

- **Avoidance of being alone in the want.** The encourager wants the partner to do the thing because the encourager does not want to feel alone in their own version of it. The encouragement is the encourager's loneliness wearing a generous face. The session source surfaces this directly: "Am I selfishly doing that because I'm afraid to do it alone?"

- **Control via care.** The encouragement positions the encourager as the helper, the seer, the one who knows the partner's potential. This is a soft form of control that does not look like control because it is wrapped in care. See [[making-your-partner-a-project]] for the broader pattern.

- **A want the encourager cannot speak directly.** The encourager wants something specific from the partner (the book published, the business launched, the weight lost, the job changed) and has converted the want into the architecture of encouragement because asking directly feels too vulnerable. The encouragement is the want disguised as service. See [[own-your-want-instead-of-encouraging-them]] for the corrective move.

- **An avoidance of seeing the partner as whole.** If the partner were whole, no encouragement would be needed. The encouragement requires that the partner be deficient. The encourager has subtly chosen the deficiency-frame because the wholeness-frame would leave them without a role.

## The Test

The cleanest test for whether your encouragement is the well-intentioned trap or the legitimate naming of what you see:

**Would you say it to someone you considered an equal or a superior in the relevant domain?** If yes, the statement is a recognition. If no, the statement is a corrective that assumes their deficit. The asymmetry of who would receive that statement from you is the diagnostic.

A second test: **what does it cost you to take the statement back?** If your partner said "please stop encouraging me about my book," would you feel relieved or threatened? If threatened, the encouragement was doing work for you that is not about them. The encouragement was the architecture of your own avoidance.

## What Joe Says Instead

The replacement is not silence. The replacement is the truth of what you actually want.

In the source session, Joe offers the alternative directly:

"And then the other piece, which I really liked you saying, which is like, would be such a lovely thing to say, is like, I would -- you can do whatever you do, and I would love you to put this book out in the world because I don't want to be alone in this. And I feel alone in this. And you can do what you got to do. Like, I just have to own my want." -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

The replacement statement has four moves:

1. **Affirm them as whole.** "You can do whatever you do." Their wholeness is the starting position.
2. **Name your own want directly.** "I would love you to put this book out in the world." The want is yours, not theirs.
3. **Name what the want is about for you.** "Because I don't want to be alone in this. I feel alone in this." The vulnerability under the want.
4. **Hand them back their agency.** "You can do what you got to do." The choice is theirs.

The structural difference: every piece of the new statement is the encourager's own truth. None of it asserts what the partner should be or do. The partner is not being positioned as deficient. The encourager is being seen.

See [[own-your-want-instead-of-encouraging-them]] for the move article that operationalizes this replacement.

## Distinguished From Honest Recognition

The principle does not forbid telling a partner "I think your work is good" or "I see how much you have grown." Genuine recognition of what is already true is not the same as encouragement that asserts a position the receiver does not hold.

The distinction:

- **Recognition:** "I am noticing that you have done this thing, and I want to name what I see." The content is observational. The receiver can either receive it or not. Nothing is being asked of them.

- **Encouragement-as-anti-pattern:** "You can do this thing, you should not be afraid, just put it out there." The content is corrective. The receiver is being asked to be different from how they are showing up. The encourager has positioned themselves as the seer of what should be.

The line is whether you are naming what is or prescribing what should be. The first does not perpetuate the deficit. The second does, no matter how warmly delivered.

## When the Encouragement Is Already Established

If the pattern has been running for years -- if you have been encouraging your partner about a specific thing for a long time and the encouragement is part of the relationship's texture -- the corrective is not just to stop. The corrective involves naming what you have been doing.

The session source models the recognition: "I think I've been like hindering him... I'm like literally picturing him as a little boy." The recognition is allowed to land. The naming of what the pattern has been is part of the work, not just the change to a new behavior.

The followup move once the recognition has landed is described in [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]] and the broader self-reliance corrective work: the partner has been positioned by the encouragement, and that positioning is worth being seen and grieved before the new behavior installs cleanly.

## In Practice for the Coach

When a coachee describes encouraging their partner who has negative self-talk:

1. **Name the structural problem.** The encouragement and the partner's internal critic are pointing at the same deficit. You are agreeing with the critic.
2. **Run the test.** "Would you say that to someone you considered a peer in this domain?" Let them feel the asymmetry.
3. **Find the want underneath.** What does the encourager want? Why is the encouragement easier than the direct want?
4. **Find the avoidance underneath.** What is the encourager not wanting to feel? Often loneliness, neediness, the helplessness of not being able to control the outcome.
5. **Offer the replacement.** Help them construct the four-move statement above using their actual want and their actual vulnerability.
6. **Do not weaponize the principle.** The encourager has been doing this out of love. The naming of the structural problem is information, not condemnation.

## Tone

Direct but warm. The principle lands hard the first time someone hears it because it inverts the moral valence of behavior they were proud of. Joe delivers it with directness ("that fucking sucks") and immediately follows with the alternative. The directness is permitted because the warmth is already established. The warmth is required because the recognition is painful.
