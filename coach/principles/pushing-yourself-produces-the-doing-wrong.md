# Pushing Yourself Is What Produces the Doing Wrong

## The Principle

The pushing that is meant to prevent doing wrong is what produces the doing wrong. The protective device is the harm.

When the system pushes hard to get it right, it dissociates. The dissociation is what makes the actions land badly. The pushing is the dissociation. The wrongs the person remembers — the ones they push to prevent — all happen in the pushing state.

The fear-of-doing-wrong drives the pushing. The pushing is offered to the self as the solution. The person never tests whether the pushing actually prevents wrong because they have only ever experienced the fear and the pushing, never the absence of both. When the test is run — "name one thing you did wrong when you weren't pushing" — the answer is usually that no such example exists. All the wrongs happened in the pushing state.

## Why This Is True

The pushing is a tightening. It contracts attention, restricts the body, narrows perception. In that state, the person cannot receive the input they need to act well. They cannot feel the other person. They cannot sense the room. They cannot perceive their own actual desire. They act from a constricted, partial, mis-calibrated read of the situation.

The actions that come out of this state are the actions the person later judges as wrong. They were short with their partner. They missed the obvious feeling in the other person. They said the thing they didn't mean. They worked harder than the situation called for. They abandoned themselves. They contradicted what they actually wanted.

The system then interprets these "wrongs" as evidence that more pushing is required — if the pushing was producing this much wrong, imagine how much wrong there would be without it. The interpretation is exactly backwards. The pushing produced the wrong. Less pushing would have produced less wrong.

This is the same structure as [[trying-prevents-the-feeling]], applied to action rather than feeling. The trying-to-feel is what prevents the feeling. The trying-to-do-right is what produces the doing-wrong. The same mechanism is at work in both: effort applied to the self is dissociating, and dissociation is what's actually generating the problem.

## What This Implies for the Work

The corrective is not "stop pushing." That is more pushing, in a new costume. The corrective is to see the structure clearly — to run the inversion, to test the underlying claim, to discover in the body that the wrongs all live in the pushing.

Once the structure is seen, the pushing loses its justification. The system can still push by default; the wiring takes time to update. But the intellectual claim ("if I don't push, I'll mess up") is no longer available. The next time the pushing arrives, the person can notice it and recall that the pushing is the wrong-maker, not the wrong-preventer.

The further work is somatic. The pushing has a specific body signature — tension, breath-hold, narrowed attention, leaning forward. Learning to notice the signature and release the tension is the operational form. The release is not a doing; it's an undoing. See [[undoing-not-doing]].

## The Live Coaching Application

The move that surfaces this principle in a session is [[tell-me-one-thing-youve-done-wrong-when-you-werent-pushing-yourself]]. The setup is [[what-makes-you-push-yourself]]. The full sequence: identify the pushing, ask what drives it, invert the answer to expose that the pushing causes what it's trying to prevent.

The principle works because it can be tested live. The person cannot name a non-pushing wrong. The evidence is in their own memory. The structure is not abstract — it's a specific fact about how they've been operating.

## Joe's Words

The compressed form of the principle as it appears in the source session:

- **Joe:** "Tell me one thing you've done wrong when you aren't pushing yourself. You're pushing yourself right now."
- **Client:** "Feels like I haven't done anything wrong and I haven't pushed myself."
- **Joe:** "Now think about all the things that you think you've done wrong and how many of them are happening while you're pushing yourself."
- **Client:** "It's me, all of them."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Stop Trying So Hard)*

The principle is not stated as a teaching. It is produced by the client through the inversion. Joe does not say "pushing produces wrong." He sets up the conditions for the client to discover it. The discovery is more durable than the teaching would be.

## What This Principle Is Not

- It is not "effort is bad." Effort applied skillfully toward something concrete is fine. The principle is about effort applied to the self — pushing yourself to be different than you are. That specific kind of effort is dissociating.
- It is not "you should never try." Trying is fine when the trying is congruent with the body. The pushing-that-produces-wrong is a specific kind of trying — the kind that requires the self to be other than it is in this moment. See [[trying-vs-doing]] for the distinction.
- It is not "everything would be fine without any effort." The principle is structural. It points at why a specific category of effort produces the opposite of what it intends. Other kinds of action remain available.
