# Trust Requires Conflict (and the Overcoming of It Together)

## The Principle

Trust is not the absence of conflict. Trust is the presence of conflict that has been moved through together. The least offensive person in your life is also the least trustworthy: you do not know what they actually think, where their boundaries are, or whether they will stay when things get hard.

"Trust is built by having conflict and overcoming it together." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The relational mechanism: every conflict survived shows both parties that the relationship can hold weight. Each successful overcoming raises the ceiling on what the relationship can carry. A relationship that has never had conflict has no proof of capacity.

## The Diagnostic

"Think about the least offensive person you know."

The least offensive person is usually someone with no observable boundaries, no clear positions, no resistance to your preferences. Asking yourself whether you trust them produces an interesting answer: not really. You do not know what they would do under pressure because you have never seen them under pressure.

"Where's your boundaries? Where's [the rest of you]?" -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The trustworthy people in your life are not the easy ones. They are the ones who will tell you what they actually think, who will hold their ground when you push, who will stay through conflict rather than smoothing it away. They are the ones who have been willing to risk the relationship by being honest in it.

"The best CEOs that you would trust to put your money in are [not] conflict avoidant. The best social activists aren't conflict avoidant. The best doctors aren't conflict avoidant. The best CPAs, whatever it is. The best friends. People who tell you what they actually think instead of trying to make you happy. Those are the ones we value." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

## The Inverse Form

The principle has an inverse form that hits harder for some people. If you are conflict-avoidant, you are not building trust. You are preventing it. The people in your life cannot fully trust you because you have not given them the data: where do you draw the line, what do you actually want, will you stay when it gets uncomfortable.

The person worried about being trusted often performs trustworthiness through agreeableness. The mechanism is reversed. Trust is generated through honest difference held in connection, not through the absence of difference.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You have relationships that look good from the outside but feel hollow.
- You realize you do not actually know what your partner or coworker thinks, only what they say to keep things smooth.
- You catch yourself realizing you do not fully trust someone who has never disagreed with you.
- You notice that the relationships in your life that have weight are also the ones where you have had hard conversations.
- You become aware that your own conflict avoidance is preventing the people you love from being able to fully trust you.

## Joe's Synthesis

"People who tell you what they actually think instead of trying to make you happy. Those are the ones we value." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The principle is presented as observation, not aspiration. It is how trust empirically forms in human relationships. The person who has been organizing their life around avoiding conflict has been organizing their life around producing relationships that cannot fully trust them.

## How Trust Gets Built More Specifically

In the team-design context, the principle gets a fuller articulation of the mechanisms. Trust is built across all its dimensions through similar moves:

"Trust is built in similar ways. It's having the difficult conversations. It's not avoiding them. Any conflict-avoidant manager, if you are listening to this and you are a conflict-avoidant manager, your team does not trust you." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

Specifically:

- **Having the difficult conversations** rather than avoiding them. The conflict is the through-line.
- **Doing what you say you will do.** Reliability over time.
- **Not allowing the unaccountable.** Dealing with the dirty laundry, including the laundry between people, the failing product, the personal struggles the leader has been hiding.
- **Bringing things up.** "Bringing it up is a way to create trust. It might be hard, but it's a way to create trust."

The reverse list is the destroy-trust list. Conflict avoidance, breaking commitments, allowing non-accountability, not naming the unaddressed. Each of these reads as kindness or pragmatism in the moment and as betrayal across time.

## The Multifaceted Trust Connection

Trust is not a single thing. See [[trust-as-multifaceted]]. The principle that trust requires conflict applies across all dimensions, but the specific conflicts that build each dimension differ.

- Communicative trust gets built by saying the thing that was not yet said and surviving it together.
- Financial trust gets built by working through a money disagreement.
- Reliability trust gets built when something falls through and the parties repair it.
- Conflict trust itself gets built by having conflicts and the relationship surviving them.

Each dimension has its own form of conflict, its own form of overcoming, and its own consequence for the relationship. The blanket statement "we have trust" hides which dimensions have been tested and which have not.

## Tone

Observational, almost commonsense. Joe presents this as something the listener already knows but has not yet applied to themselves. The persuasion is in the recognition, not in the argument.
