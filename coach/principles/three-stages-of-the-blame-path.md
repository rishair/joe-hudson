# The Three Stages of the Blame Path

## The Principle

There is a developmental arc with blame. It has three stages, not two. People who notice they are blaming others often think the work is to stop blaming the other person, and that the destination is to "take responsibility" by blaming themselves instead. That is the middle of the path, not the end. The end is no blame at all.

"At the beginning of the path, you blame somebody else. In the middle of the path, you blame yourself. And at the end of the path, you don't blame anybody." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The mistake the principle corrects: treating the second stage as the goal. When someone stops blaming their partner and starts saying "it's all my fault," they have not finished the work. They have switched the target of the blame. The blame itself is still in motion.

## Why the Middle Stage Is Not the Destination

Self-blame and other-blame are the same mechanism with the target rotated. Both install shame. Both stagnate the emotional flow that would actually produce change. Both prevent the work that lives underneath the blame.

"Blaming you or blaming me in a relationship, both of those things are corrosive." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The path is often described as if "stopping blame of the other" is the work, with self-blame as the implicit upgrade. That framing keeps the blame engine running. The person feels morally improved (they are no longer being unfair to their partner) while continuing to do the same shame-installing move internally. The relationship does not improve. The internal climate gets worse.

Self-blame also has a specific cost the first stage does not: it demeans the partner by abdicating their accountability. See [[self-blame-abdicates-others-agency]] for the mechanism.

## The Substitution Pattern

The principle predicts a specific substitution pattern between the stages. People who try to stop blaming their partner often discover they have started blaming themselves. People who try to stop blaming themselves often discover they have started blaming their partner. The blame circulates between targets without dissolving.

"Usually what happens is if somebody stops blaming somebody else, in a relationship, then they start blaming themselves. Or if they're blaming themselves, they're not blaming the other person." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The substitution happens because the underlying mechanism has not been addressed. The blame is a defense against something underneath -- often shame, often helplessness, often grief. As long as the underneath remains unfelt, the blame has to land somewhere. The targeting is interchangeable. Only the relinquishing is final.

## What "No Blame" Means

The third stage is not absolution of anyone. It is not "nobody did anything wrong." It is the removal of the *blame frame itself* as the way the situation is processed.

In the third stage, the question is not "whose fault was this." The question is "what is happening here and what do we do about it." The fault frame is recognized as a frame that does not produce movement, and the energy that was going into fault-finding is redirected toward something that does.

This is the same orientation as [[fault-vs-responsibility]] at the level of a single situation. The third stage of the blame path is the steady-state version: not just declining to assign fault in one moment, but having moved past the use of fault as a mental motion.

The forward-looking question that opens at the third stage: [[how-do-we-want-to-be-together]]. See also [[patient-on-the-table]] for the depersonalization that supports the third stage in single situations.

## Why This Is a Developmental Arc, Not a Choice

The stages tend to come in order because each one is a real shift. The person at stage one is unaware that they are blaming -- they experience themselves as observing what the other person is doing wrong. Recognition of the blame is a real shift; that recognition often produces stage two. The person now sees the blame and turns it on themselves as the "responsible" move.

Stage two is also a real shift, in that the person has stopped externalizing. They are looking inward. But they have not yet recognized that the blame itself, regardless of target, is the issue. That recognition is stage three.

The principle is offered as a way to skip the middle stage where possible -- to recognize at the moment of catching the other-blame that the work is not to redirect the blame inward, but to stop installing blame anywhere.

## The Diagnostic

Joe uses the principle as a diagnostic in coaching. If a person reports that they have "taken responsibility" for the problem in their marriage by accepting that it is all their fault, the diagnostic flag is up. They are likely in stage two and treating it as the destination. The next move is to surface that the self-blame is doing the same damage as the original other-blame -- and that the work goes further.

The diagnostic also runs in reverse. If a person has been doing the work for a while and is no longer blaming their partner, the check is: where did the blame go? If it went to themselves, the work is not finished. If it went nowhere -- if the person can describe the situation without locating fault anywhere -- they are at stage three.

## What Lives Underneath Blame at Any Stage

The blame at any stage is doing one job: preventing contact with whatever is underneath. Usually shame. Sometimes grief, sometimes helplessness, sometimes hurt. The blame is a way to not feel the thing. As long as the thing remains unfelt, blame will recur, switching targets when forced.

"What's actually happening there is what we're saying is we don't want to feel the shame. So if I blame you, I cannot feel the shame." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The third stage opens when the underneath has been felt enough that the blame is no longer needed to keep it at bay. The relinquishing of blame is the natural consequence of the underneath becoming feel-able. It is not a discipline imposed from above.

This connects to [[shame-drives-behavior]] (the lock is the shame, the blame installs the shame), [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] (the avoided emotion is what produces the loop), and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] (the actual mechanism by which the third stage becomes available).

## The Healing Note

The principle has a corollary about where healing happens: not in either of the first two stages.

"My healing doesn't happen by saying, 'I'm bad. I'm fucked up. I need to improve.' Because I can tell you every addict out there right now who knows they're an addict is telling themselves that, and it's not changing shit." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The self-blame stage produces the same anti-healing effect as the other-blame stage. The healing happens when the blame is set down and the emotion underneath can be met. This is why the principle is more than a developmental observation -- it is a practical map. The healing is not in stage one (no movement possible while blaming the other), not in stage two (no movement possible while blaming the self), only in stage three (the blame is set down and the actual work begins).

## The Arc and the Development of Leadership

The same three-stage arc maps onto the development of leadership capacity. The person at stage one is handing their leadership to whoever they are blaming (see [[blame-is-the-abandonment-of-leadership]]). The person at stage two is handing their leadership to a punitive internal voice that does the blame thing in the guise of authority (see [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]]). The person at stage three has the leadership available to use, because they are no longer transferring it through the blame frame.

"At the beginning of the journey, you blame others. In the middle of the journey, you blame yourself. At the end, there is no blame. And definitely the more you move in that direction, the better your leadership becomes." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The connection is structural: leadership requires the agency that blame keeps externalizing. The leadership-development arc and the blame-path arc are the same arc viewed from two angles. Moving from stage one to stage three is the same motion as moving from disowned leadership to owned leadership. See [[leadership-is-for-everyone]] for the broader frame in which this development takes place.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with matter-of-fact precision. The frame is structural, not aspirational. The third stage is not described as enlightened; it is described as the place where the work becomes possible. The middle stage is not condemned; it is just located accurately so it is not mistaken for the end.
