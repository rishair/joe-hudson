# Once Tasted, the Homing Beacon Is On

## The Principle

Once a person has had a taste of the recognition that some traditions call awakening -- even a brief glimpse, even a few seconds -- a persistent understanding of it becomes nearly inevitable in their lifetime. Their life will arrange itself, often without their conscious effort, toward the conditions in which the recognition can land more fully.

This is not the same as state-seeking. State-seeking is the contracted chase -- the person tries to recreate the taste through effort. The principle here is descriptive of what tends to happen anyway. The system has registered that the recognition exists. From that point forward, the orientation has shifted at a level deeper than choice.

"When somebody has a taste of it, there's almost an inevitability of a persistent understanding of it because it's just like when you see the light at the end of the cave, you know which way to walk and you're just gonna get to it eventually. Like, it's like that homing beacon has been turned on, you're gonna get there, you know, unless you pass suddenly. It just seems to be like that's the experience that I notice. Once people taste this, they're just like -- their life arranges to find it." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

## What "Taste" Means Here

A taste is any direct experience -- however brief -- of the underlying recognition. It might be:

- A few seconds of complete dissolution during meditation.
- An unexpected moment in nature where the boundary between self and surroundings disappeared.
- A breakthrough during emotional work where the I was momentarily transparent.
- A near-death experience.
- A psychedelic state that included a moment of complete oneness.
- A moment in deep love where the lover and the loved temporarily had no boundary.
- A moment in extreme flow (athletic, creative, sexual) where the doer disappeared.

The taste does not have to be long. Joe's own first taste was eight seconds. The criterion is not duration but quality -- a direct experience of the underlying recognition rather than a thought about it.

## Why the Homing Beacon Turns On

The mechanism: the recognition is not an idea the person is now seeking. It is a glimpse of something that has always been there and is now known to be there. The knowing creates a different kind of orientation than the seeking does.

Seeking is forward-looking and contracted -- trying to get to something. The homing-beacon orientation is more like the way a body that has stretched once knows it can stretch again. There is a different relationship to the territory. The territory is now familiar at a felt level, even if briefly.

The life-arranging happens at multiple scales. Some of it is conscious (the person seeks out practice, teachers, community). Some of it is below choice (the person finds themselves making decisions that loosen identifications, releasing relationships that depend on the bounded self, encountering experiences that prepare the system for the next taste).

## Why This Is Different From State-Seeking

The distinction matters because the principle can be misread as endorsement of [[state-seeking]]. It is not.

State-seeking is the bounded self trying to engineer the recognition through effort. The effort itself reinforces the bounded self, which is the structure the recognition is supposed to dissolve. State-seeking does not produce the recognition; it produces more state-seeking.

The homing-beacon principle is descriptive of what tends to happen even when the person is not in the state-seeking mode. The life arranges itself. The person does not have to push.

Joe lived both. After his first eight-second taste (see [[joes-first-meditation-retreat]]), he spent 7-8 years in pure state-seeking. The state-seeking did not work. The recognition eventually landed (see [[joes-bathroom-realization]]) after the state-seeking had quieted -- but it landed in the same person whose system had been arranging toward it the whole time. The arrangement was happening underneath the failed chase.

The teaching from inside this: trust the arrangement. The taste did its work. The forward movement is happening whether you push or not. Pushing slows it down. Allowing it to happen lets it happen on its actual timeline.

## What the Person Can Do

Given the principle, what is the person's role?

- Do the work that loosens identifications (the work they would have been doing anyway).
- Welcome the experiences that arise without grasping at them. See [[savor-the-moments-its-here]].
- Stop chasing the specific state. The state arrived once because the conditions were available. The next landing will happen on its own timeline. The chase is in the way.
- Trust the arrangement. If the homing beacon is on, the system is doing more than the conscious mind can see.

The instruction is gentler than the typical spiritual prescription. The principle does not require more effort. It often requires less.

## The Counter-Principle: It Can Still Take Years

The principle says inevitable. It does not say fast. The arrangement can take decades. The person who had a taste at 25 may have the full landing at 55, or at 75, or on their deathbed. The homing beacon is on; the arrival timeline is not specified.

Joe's own arc: 8-second taste at 28 or so. Recognition landing in his early-to-mid 30s. The recognition becoming background -- like hair color -- a decade or more after that. The full arc was decades long. The homing beacon was on the whole time.

This is also why the principle is not an excuse to drop the work. The work shapes the arrival. Without the work, the arrival can still happen, but it can arrive into a less-prepared system and produce the [[fog-walker-vs-belt-over-the-head|belt-over-the-head]] shape more violently, or arrive into someone who has no framework for what is happening and gets diagnosed with depersonalization disorder (see [[zen-sickness]]).

## Why This Matters for Coaching

This principle has specific coaching applications:

**When someone has had a glimpse and is now in despair that they cannot recreate it:** the principle is reassurance. The taste was not a one-time event. The arrangement is happening. The savoring of the glimpse is what supports the eventual landing. See [[savor-the-moments-its-here]].

**When someone is exhausted from years of chasing:** name that the chasing is in the way. The homing beacon was on after the first taste. The chasing has been blocking the arrival, not producing it.

**When someone is afraid they "missed their chance":** there is no chance to miss. The taste turned on the beacon. The chance has been ongoing since then.

**When someone is asking what they should do with a glimpse they just had:** less than they think. Welcome it. Don't grasp at it. Trust the arrangement. Continue the work that has nothing to do with the recognition.

**When someone is comparing themselves to teachers or peers who seem further along:** the principle disarms the comparison. They are also on the same arrangement. The timeline is not the measure of the arrangement. See [[awakening-as-natural-development]].

## What This Doesn't Say

It does not say the recognition will land in everyone. The principle says the homing beacon turns on after the taste. People who have never had the taste are not subject to this principle directly -- though see [[awakening-as-natural-development]] for the broader principle that the recognition is structurally available to any nervous system that keeps evolving.

It does not say the recognition will land in this lifetime. The principle says the arrangement is ongoing. The arrangement does not guarantee a specific endpoint within a specific window.

It does not say no work is required. The work is part of the arrangement. The arrangement is more than the work, but the work is not skippable.

It does not say the taste itself was caused by the person. The taste was received. See [[awakening-is-received-not-achieved]]. The homing beacon is a downstream effect of the receiving, not evidence that the person produced it.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with quiet conviction. The metaphor of the light at the end of the cave is offered as something he has observed across many people, not as a doctrine. There is something tender in the delivery -- the principle is reassurance for the person who has had a glimpse and is now afraid they lost it. The reassurance is structural, not sentimental: you didn't lose it; the beacon is on; trust the arrangement.
