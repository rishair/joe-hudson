# The Body Lies Just as Much as the Mind

## Definition

A corrective principle, especially important for people newly discovering body wisdom or gut wisdom. The body is not the irrevocable truth. The gut is not the direct line to God. The heart is not infallible. Every intelligence captures a partial slice of reality. None captures all of it. Treating any one of them as absolute creates a new kind of error.

"The body lies just as much as the mind." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

This is not a rejection of body intelligence. It is a refusal to let the corrective become the new tyranny.

## The Photograph Metaphor

Joe softens the word "lie" with a metaphor.

"If I take a picture of you and then I walk two feet to the right and take another picture of you, both of those pictures are true. They're actually moments in time that are there, but they're both lies because they're going to capture different things. They're not going to be the entirety of the truth." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

Every intelligence is a photograph. Each one is true at its angle. None is the whole. The thought is true at its angle and a lie because it is not all the truth. The body sensation is true at its angle and a lie because it is not all the truth. The heart's read is true at its angle and a lie because it is not all the truth.

## Why This Principle Matters

A predictable arc: a head-dominant person discovers the body, has a breakthrough, and now treats every gut sensation as gospel. The over-correction is sometimes worse than the original imbalance. The head-dominant person at least had the habit of questioning their thoughts. The newly body-dominant person does not yet have the habit of questioning their sensations.

"I see people who are really intellectual and all of a sudden they understand like, holy crap, my body has all this wisdom. And they start using the body wisdom and then the body becomes the irrevocable truth and direct connection to God and intelligence and wisdom, which is not true either. The body can get confused as well, or the heart does, and the heart can get confused as well." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The principle keeps the discovery from becoming a new prison.

## The Limits Are Sensory and Cognitive

The principle is not just about errors of individual organs. It is about the fundamental limits of human sensing. The mantis shrimp sees twelve color spectra; humans see three. A dog smells what humans never will. The senses themselves are partial. The mind is partial. The body is partial. The heart is partial. The whole human is partial.

"We are limited to the capacity of our senses and our mind. There are things that we will never be able to understand because of that. It does not mean that we don't know what's right for us right now. It doesn't make life purposeless. It's just the humility of the fact that there is always going to be a mystery." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The principle is a doorway to humility, not despair. The person still knows what is right for them right now. They simply stop pretending they have access to the totality.

## In Coaching: When to Reach for This

This principle is the antidote to a particular kind of confidence: the certainty that "my gut said so" or "I felt it in my body" therefore it is true. The certainty often shows up in the second year of someone's emotional work, after the initial body-discovery has taken hold.

Signs the principle is needed:
- The person uses the body or the gut to justify decisions they cannot logically support and are uninterested in examining.
- The person dismisses their partner's or colleague's logic as "just intellectualizing" and elevates their own sensation as the deciding evidence.
- The person treats their intuition as immune to projection or trauma.
- The person resists questioning their gut as if questioning were an attack on the body itself.

## What It Sets Up

When the body, the heart, and the head are all known to be partial, the person can stop seeking the master organ. The question becomes: what is each one saying, and how do they integrate? See [[head-heart-gut]].

This pairs with [[wonder]]. The acknowledgment that no single intelligence has the whole truth opens the field for genuine inquiry. The certainty that "my gut knows" forecloses wonder. The willingness to hold each intelligence as partial keeps the inquiry alive.

It also pairs with [[judgment-vs-discernment]]. Judgment is the head, the heart, or the gut acting as if its partial view were total. Discernment is the integrated read across all three, held with awareness of its limits.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with a kind of friendly correction. He has watched the over-correction happen many times. The point is not to take anything away. It is to keep the discovery proportionate.

When he first said it to Brett, Brett found it "really fascinating." The principle does its work by reframing rather than by warning. The photograph metaphor is the load-bearing image.
