# Loving Attention Beats Consistency

## The Principle

The standard parenting advice puts consistency at the center. Be consistent with your boundaries, your bedtimes, your responses, your discipline. The kid needs to know what to expect. Consistency is the variable that produces the well-adjusted child.

This principle inverts the priority. The variable that actually matters is whether the parent is in loving attention when they are with the kid. A parent who is in loving attention can be highly inconsistent and still produce great results. A parent who is technically consistent but not in loving attention is offering the kid a consistent absence — which is its own kind of damage, not a substitute for the loving attention the kid actually needs.

"I was surprised by how inconsistent I could be and still get great results. Meaning, the job is to be patient with them as they're having a big emotion. I would say it's to sit in loving attention. Like, if you can't be in loving attention, then get your wife, have her be in loving attention. And if you just can't do it at all, then it's also okay to say to a kid, like, I just can't be in loving attention with you right now, we're just gotta leave the store. So for me, it was more important to be in loving attention than it was to be consistent." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

## The Three-Option Decision Tree

The principle gives the parent a clean decision tree when something is going wrong with the kid.

**Option 1: Be in loving attention.** This is the default if the parent has the capacity. The kid is having a big emotion; the parent stays present, patient, available. The emotion moves through. The connection reconvenes. This is the practice of [[hand-in-hand-parenting]] in operational form.

**Option 2: Hand off to the partner.** If the parent can feel they do not have it in them, hand the situation to the partner who does. This is not a failure. It is recognition of the actual state of the parent's nervous system and a deployment of the more capable resource. The kid gets loving attention from the available source.

**Option 3: Honestly name the absence and exit the situation.** If neither parent is available to be in loving attention, the parent says to the kid, "I cannot be in loving attention with you right now. We have to leave." This is preferable to the alternative — staying in the situation while pretending to be present, which the kid will read as the felt absence it is, and which will install whatever the parent is actually feeling underneath the performance.

The third option preserves the principle. The kid does not get loving attention in that moment, but the kid also does not get the falsified version of loving attention that would otherwise be installed.

## Why Consistency Is Not the Bottleneck

The standard consistency frame assumes the kid is calibrating to the parent's rules. The kid learns what the parent will do; the predictability of the rules produces the kid's stability.

The actual mechanism is different. The kid is calibrating to the parent's nervous system, not the parent's rules. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]]. The kid's stability comes from the parent's felt state being stable enough to land in, not from the rules being applied identically every time.

A parent who is in loving attention on Tuesday and exhausted on Wednesday is offering the kid a felt state on each day. If both days have a real version of the parent — present on Tuesday, honestly absent on Wednesday with a clean handoff or exit — the kid is reading both as honest. The kid's nervous system is calibrating to a real signal, even if the rule about bedtime varied.

A parent who is technically consistent but operating from frustration, distraction, or chronic resentment is offering a stable rule and an unstable felt state. The kid is reading the felt state. The consistency does not compensate for what is being transmitted underneath it.

## What the Principle Permits the Parent

The principle relieves the parent of a specific load: the load of trying to maintain consistency when the parent's actual capacity is variable. Many parents drive themselves into chronic depletion trying to be the consistent figure they were taught to be, only to find that the consistency is being delivered through an increasingly thin and resentful version of themselves.

The permission: the parent can be variable. The parent can have bad days. The parent can hand off. The parent can leave. The thing the parent cannot do — without cost to the kid — is fake the loving attention. The fake version is the failure mode. The honest variability is not.

This also relieves the parent of a kind of shame. Many parents carry the felt sense that they should always be patient, always available, always emotionally regulated. When they fail at this, they blame themselves for not being good parents. The principle reframes the failure: the failure was not the variability. The failure would have been pretending the variability was not there.

## What the Principle Asks of the Parent

The principle requires the parent to have an honest relationship with their own state. The parent has to be able to tell, in the moment, whether they are in loving attention or not. This is not always obvious — many parents have been trained to perform calm while actually being dysregulated, and the performance can fool the parent themselves.

The diagnostic question: am I actually present with this kid right now, or am I performing the appearance of presence while my system is somewhere else? The body usually knows. The body is tight; the breath is short; there is a felt distance behind the eyes. These are the signals that loving attention is not actually online.

When the diagnostic is honest, the principle becomes practical. The parent does not have to manufacture loving attention. The parent has to recognize whether it is there, and if it is not, choose one of the other two options.

## The Practice Side: Building Loving Attention as a Capacity

The principle implies that loving attention is something the parent has access to, at least some of the time. For parents whose own nervous systems were not regulated in childhood, the access can be limited. The parent who finds they cannot be in loving attention very often is not failing the principle; they are encountering the actual condition of their own capacity.

The work in such cases is the parent's own inner work. The capacity to be in loving attention with a kid is downstream of the parent's capacity to be in loving attention with themselves. See [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]]. The parent who is developing self-attunement is building the same capacity they will then have available for the kid.

In the meantime, the three-option decision tree gives the parent something honest to do. The partner-handoff and the honest-exit are not stopgaps. They are valid moves that produce better outcomes than the faked-presence alternative.

## The Distinction From [[dont-use-loving-presence-as-disassociation]]

This principle is the positive form; the anti-pattern article names the failure mode. Loving attention is not vacancy with a soft expression. It is actual presence with the kid, including the parent's full somatic engagement. The parent who has gone into "I'm being loving" while internally checked out is the failure mode the anti-pattern addresses. The principle here is what real loving attention looks like — and the recognition that when it is not online, the right move is to name that honestly, not to perform it.

## When This Principle Surfaces

- A parent describing chronic guilt about not being consistent enough
- A parent driving themselves into depletion trying to be the consistent figure
- A parent who is technically consistent but feels the relationship with the kid going cold
- A parent asking what to do when they cannot be patient and have to keep parenting
- A parent who is in a public situation (store, restaurant, airport) and feels they are failing
- A parent navigating a co-parenting handoff and wondering if it counts as a failure of consistency
- A coaching topic where the parent is comparing themselves to an idealized standard of always-on patience

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not say all forms of inconsistency are fine. Inconsistency about safety (different rules about car seats on different days, different rules about hitting on different days) does damage the kid, because it removes the structural ground the kid needs to function. The principle is about emotional availability and patience, not about safety-critical structure.

The principle also does not give a parent license to be chronically absent. If the parent is finding they cannot be in loving attention most of the time, the work is not to keep exiting — it is to look at why the capacity is so limited and do the upstream work. The honest exit is a valid in-the-moment move, not a sustainable parenting strategy.

The principle also assumes a partnership where the partner-handoff is actually available. Single parents do not have this option in the same way. For them, the decision tree is option 1 or option 3, and the honest-exit move becomes more central — sometimes a 5-minute time-out for the parent in the bathroom, sometimes naming "I need a minute" and using that minute, sometimes recognizing the structural need for outside support to make option 1 more available.

## Tone

Direct, warm, relieving. The principle takes pressure off the parent who has been trying too hard to be consistent. It does not give license for absence; it gives license for honesty about the parent's actual state. The relief is the recognition that the kid is calibrating to something real about the parent, not to the parent's performance of an ideal.
