# The Most Efficient Way to Change Your Consciousness Is to Change Your Behavior

## The Principle

There are two directions in which the consciousness-behavior relationship can be worked. The more obvious one — change your consciousness and your behavior will follow — is what most transformation work emphasizes. The less-obvious one is more efficient: change your behavior, repeat it, and your consciousness reorganizes around the new behavior.

"You start to recognize when you really start living by your principles, that the most efficient way to change your consciousness is to change your behavior. You just say, oh, I'm going to do something different 10 times and see what happens. That will change your consciousness." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The claim is specifically about *efficiency*. Other paths to consciousness change exist — insight, contemplation, deep emotional work, ritual. They work. They are not always the fastest path. The behavior-first path is often faster because it does not require the consciousness to be ready before the change starts. The body does a new thing ten times, and the consciousness updates to match what the body has been doing.

## The Bidirectional Frame

The principle assumes a specific frame about the consciousness-behavior relationship: it runs in both directions.

"You realize, oh wait, I can change behavior to make decisions, or I can make decisions to change behavior." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The standard frame is unidirectional: consciousness changes first, then behavior follows. The principle adds the reverse direction: behavior can be changed deliberately, and consciousness reorganizes to support what the body is now doing. Neither direction is wrong. Both are available. The point is that the consciousness-first approach is not the only approach, and is sometimes the slower one.

The bidirectional frame matters because it removes a specific stuck-point: the person who is waiting to "feel ready" to change a behavior. The waiting can take years. The principle says: do not wait for the consciousness shift. Change the behavior. The consciousness will catch up. The waiting was a category error — it assumed a sequence that does not have to operate that way.

## The 10-Times Experiment

The operational form: pick a new behavior, do it ten times, see what happens. The number is not magic; it is just enough repetitions that the consciousness has had a chance to register the new pattern. Single instances of a new behavior tend to get filed as anomalies. Repeated instances start to organize a new sense of what is possible, what is wanted, and who one is.

"You just say, oh, I'm going to do something different 10 times and see what happens. That will change your consciousness. It's really, really simple when you do it this way." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The "see what happens" framing is important. The instruction is not "do the new thing ten times and you will reach the consciousness state you wanted." That framing converts the experiment into a management strategy, which usually defeats it (see [[when-experiments-become-management]]). The instruction is to do the thing ten times and observe — let the consciousness report what it found in the new behavior, rather than dictating what it should find.

The experimental orientation makes the principle compatible with [[wrestle-with-your-principles]]: each ten-times experiment is itself an iteration on a principle. If the behavior produces a consciousness shift the person wants, the principle gets strengthened. If it doesn't, the experiment is data and the principle gets revised. Either way, the wrestling is moving.

## What Principles Replace

The specific application Joe gives is the replacement of old principle-behaviors with new ones. The old unconscious principles produced specific behaviors ("get angry when you don't get what you want"). The new conscious principles produce different behaviors that the person can deliberately install via the 10-times experiment.

"This is a place where my principles used to say, oh, I'm gonna get angry. But now my principles say, I'm gonna ask a how, what question. Now my principles say, oh, what is it that I'm not understanding here? Now my principles are gonna say, I'm gonna embrace that intensity of anger and I'm going to feel it in my whole body. And then something different happens." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

Three replacement behaviors named in this single passage, all replacing the same unconscious-anger response:
- Ask a how/what question (see [[how-what-questions]])
- Ask "what is it that I'm not understanding here?"
- Embrace the intensity of anger and feel it in the whole body (see [[embrace-intensity]])

Each of these is an alternative behavior that, repeated ten times, would change the consciousness around what to do when one does not get what one wants. The angry-when-blocked principle would be replaced by the curious-when-blocked principle, the inquiring-when-blocked principle, or the felt-when-blocked principle, depending on which substitute behavior the person installs. Each ten-times experiment installs a different principle.

## Why This Works (The Body-Learning Mechanism)

The mechanism is the same one that makes principles eventually feel effortless (see [[principles-free-you]]). The body learns through repetition. When a new behavior is repeated, the body files it as "this is what we do here." The consciousness updates to match what the body has been doing, because consciousness is partly a story the system tells itself about what it is and what it does. When the doing has changed, the story has to update.

This is also why insight without behavior change tends to fade. The insight names a new possibility. The body keeps doing the old thing. The story the consciousness tells reverts to the body-evidence: "I keep doing X, so apparently I am someone who does X." The behavior-first path inverts this: the body does the new thing, and the consciousness has to account for the new evidence. The story updates.

The principle is therefore not magical. It is the use of the body as a teacher of the consciousness. Most consciousness work tries to use the consciousness as a teacher of the body. Both directions work. The body-as-teacher direction is often faster because the body has less to overcome — it just has to do the new thing, not first believe in it.

## The Boundary With Willpower

This principle is not "white-knuckle a behavior change for ten reps." That conversion defeats it. The 10-times experiment is exploratory, not forced. The instruction is to try the new behavior and observe what changes, not to grimly enforce it.

The boundary is the same as the boundary in [[awareness-of-pain-stops-the-behavior]]: when the practice becomes effortful, the voice has captured it. The functional version of the principle has the quality of curiosity ("I wonder what happens if I do this ten times") rather than the quality of obligation ("I have to do this ten times to fix myself"). The curious version produces consciousness change. The obligated version reinstalls the same patterns the experiment was trying to escape.

This is why the principle is most useful in combination with [[awareness-of-pain-stops-the-behavior]] and [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]]. Together they form a more complete picture: feel the cost of the old behavior, feel through the avoidance driving it, and try the alternative behavior ten times to let the consciousness reorganize. The behavior-change leg is doing the consciousness-update work; the feeling legs are doing the avoidance-dissolving work. Both are needed for the change to take.

## The Pairing With "Figuring Out How to Do It"

Joe is careful to note that knowing the principle is not the same as doing it. The 10-times experiment sounds simple. The hard part is figuring out which new behavior to try in a given case, having the support to actually try it, and noticing what the consciousness does in response.

"It's really, really simple when you do it this way. Figuring out how to do it is a whole nother trick, which is why we created the decisions course." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The honest disclosure matters: the principle is simple in form; the application requires real support and structure for most people to install. This is also why the principle is paired with [[wrestle-with-your-principles]] — the wrestling is the ongoing structure that keeps the 10-times experiments running and visible. Without structure, the principle remains an idea. With structure, it becomes the operational lever.

## The Whole-Life Implication

When applied across a person's full domain of decisions and behaviors, the principle compounds. Each 10-times experiment shifts a small region of consciousness. Many such shifts, across many domains, restructure what the person experiences as "who they are." This is part of why people who do sustained transformation work appear to become different people: their behavior has been changing in many places at once, and the consciousness has been updating to match.

"It has been an amazing process to watch people have such a radical change in such a short period of time. And it's not just like, oh, I'm making decisions easier now. It's that the whole life improves." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The radical-change-in-short-time observation is consistent with the bidirectional mechanism: when the behavior side is being deliberately changed in many places at once, the consciousness has many new behaviors to update around, and the update produces what looks like total life redesign. The redesign is not happening through one big shift in consciousness. It is happening through many small behavioral experiments whose consciousness-effects are compounding.

## When To Reach For This Principle

- When a person has been "trying to feel ready" to change a behavior for months or years.
- When insight work has produced understanding without behavior change.
- When the consciousness-first approach has been tried and stalled.
- When the person knows what they want to change but cannot find a way in via feeling or thinking.
- When the principle being installed needs to be installed in the body, not just held in the head.
- When the person is asking for a faster path than the standard inside-out work.

In each case, the principle adds the outside-in path as a complement to the inside-out work the person has been doing. It does not replace the feeling work or the awareness work. It runs in parallel and often unlocks places those approaches cannot reach.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is not universal. Behavior change without any consciousness contact produces compliant behavior with no underlying shift, which tends to revert under stress. The 10-times experiment works when the consciousness is actually being allowed to notice what changes — when the experiment is observational, not forced.

The principle also does not apply to behaviors driven by trauma in the absence of trauma-appropriate support. White-knuckling a behavior change through a trauma response is a different situation requiring different support.

For ordinary behavior change in a person with reasonable access to their interior, the principle works as stated.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The first time a new behavior produces a surprising emotional shift that the person did not expect.
- The dawning recognition that "I am no longer the person who does X" after enough repetitions of an alternative.
- The release of the wait for "feeling ready" — the recognition that the readiness can follow the behavior rather than precede it.
- The simplification of transformation work from a lifelong project into a series of 10-times experiments.

## Tone

Joe presents this matter-of-factly, as a piece of practical operational guidance rather than a profound insight. The tone is something like "here's a thing that works — try it." The lightness matters because the principle stops working when it becomes a heavy obligation. It is offered as an experiment, not as a discipline.
