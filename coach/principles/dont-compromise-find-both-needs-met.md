# Don't Compromise -- Find a Way Both Needs Get Met

## The Principle

In a healthy partnership, the move is not "we both give up something to meet in the middle." The move is "we both commit to finding a solution where both of our needs get fully met." Compromise as Joe defines it -- neglecting some aspect of yourself to make the other person happy -- builds resentment. The creative work of finding a both-needs-met solution does not.

"What we're going to do now is we're going to figure out a way that you get your needs met and I get my needs met. And we're going to be smart enough to do that. And we're going to commit to doing that." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

## What Joe Means by "Compromise"

The word compromise gets used in two ways. Joe is being specific:

"I'm going to use that word really specifically, like to compromise, meaning that I am going to neglect some aspect of myself to make you happy. I highly recommend never doing that. Highly recommend never doing that because that always builds up resentment." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

So "compromise" in this principle means self-abandonment in service of the other. It is the move where you decide "I'll stop being this part of myself so we can stay together." That move always produces resentment, eggshells, and slow corrosion. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] and the way [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]] manifests.

The other meaning of compromise -- finding a creative solution that involves new constraints for both -- is not what Joe is warning against. That is precisely the work he is recommending. It is closer to negotiation than to sacrifice.

## What Compromise Costs

The cost of compromise is not just the unmet need. It is the friction that follows the suppression:

"Now you're walking around the house going, 'I can show these 3 parts of myself but not these 2 parts of myself. Can't say this thing. I got to walk on eggshells.' All this other weight and friction comes into the relationship rather than saying, okay, like What we're going to do now is we're going to figure out a way that you get your needs met and I get my needs met." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The hidden parts do not stop existing. They go underground. They show up as resentment, as passive-aggression, as the slow drift in intimacy, as the relationship that is technically still together but functionally cold. The cost compounds.

## The Implicit Faith Underneath

The principle requires faith that the both-needs-met solution exists. Joe's experience is that it does, every time, if both people are committed to finding it.

"If for whatever reason that can't be done, which in my experience never can't be done, then okay, then maybe the relationship's not right for us and let's admit that. Let's get there. But neither of us are sacrificing. We're both going to get our needs met and we'll find a way to do it. There's always a way." -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The dishes example: "We're going to get someone to come in and do the dishes, or we can do the dishes at like 7 o'clock at night together, or we're using -- I would hate to say this, but disposable, recyclable, and put it all in the compost." There are a thousand solutions if both people are not in a power struggle.

## Why It Usually Doesn't Get Tried

Two reasons the creative solution doesn't get found:

**1. The power struggle is the actual content.** When the apparent disagreement is the surface and the underlying dynamic is "who is going to win," no creative solution is available because the goal is not solving the problem. See [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]]. The fight stays unresolved because resolution would interrupt the dynamic both people are unconsciously running.

**2. The stated need is not the actual need.** Brett names this: stated needs point to deeper needs (safety, autonomy, connection). When a couple stays at the surface need ("I want the dishes done now"), the creative solution space is small. When they go to the underlying need (safety, autonomy, being met), the solution space expands dramatically.

"I think a part of that is seeing the other's needs or the other's stated needs as not threats, but as something that points to a deeper need. That's right. Because then you can kind of climb down the ladder of apparent needs into what's actually needed, which tends to become sort of the same thing. The need for safety, the need for autonomy, the need for connection." -- Brett Kistler / Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The deeper needs are usually compatible. The surface needs often appear incompatible.

## The Big-Stakes Version

The principle still applies to the high-stakes versions: monogamy vs non-monogamy, having children vs not, one partner wanting to build a billion-dollar company while the other does not. Here is where the principle is tested.

Joe distinguishes two stances toward the big-stakes mismatch:

**The closed stance:** "No, I will not do that." Armored, defensive, treating the other's need as the attack.

**The open-hearted stance:** "Oh, I really hear that you're sexually unsatisfied in the marriage, and I want you to be sexually satisfied in the marriage, and I'm not okay with us having relationships outside, or I'm not okay with us not having relationships outside of the marriage as long as we're not connected, or whatever the boundary is."

"It's so much different than saying, 'I won't do that.' 'I won't do that' is this armored defensive than saying, 'Oh, I can really acknowledge and see your needs and I really want you to get your needs met. And oh, by the way, this scares the fuck out of me because I think we're about to get a divorce because you have a need that I'm not gonna be able to fulfill.'" -- Joe Hudson, How Relationships Reveal Us

The open-hearted stance includes the possibility that the both-needs-met solution does not exist for this configuration -- and that the next step is an honest conversation about what to do next, not a forced compromise.

## What the Open-Hearted Stance Sounds Like

The structure has three components:

1. **Real acknowledgment of the other's need.** Not strategic listening, not "I hear you" as a deflection. Actual reception of what they are saying they need.
2. **Stated boundary or need of your own.** Not as attack, not as ultimatum, as your own truth about what you can and cannot do.
3. **Naming the vulnerability that comes with this.** Often the vulnerability is "I am scared this means we're not going to work."

The third component is the one most people skip. Without it, the conversation is two stated positions with no body to it. With it, both people are in the room as humans rather than as positions.

## The Relationship to Boundaries

This principle does not contradict [[boundaries]]. A boundary is not a compromise. The boundary is your own truth about what you can and cannot accept. The principle is that you do not abandon your truth to keep the peace, and you do not require the other person to abandon theirs.

The healthy boundary said clearly often opens the both-needs-met conversation rather than closing it. The unhealthy "no, I will not" closes it.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The relief of finding a solution neither person had imagined
- The recognition of how much you have been compromising and what it has cost
- The fear of stating your actual need because the relationship may not survive it
- The discovery that the underlying needs were compatible all along
- The quiet ache of seeing a long marriage in which both have been compromising for decades

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A couple presents with an apparent zero-sum disagreement
- A person describes "always being the one to give in"
- A person describes resentment that has built up over years
- A person presents a non-negotiable position (kids/no kids, monogamy/non-monogamy, etc.)
- A person says "we had to compromise" and is in a flat state about it
- A person describes walking on eggshells in their relationship

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward the underlying needs: what does each person actually need underneath the stated position
- Toward [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]]: the larger agreement that supports the creative work
- Toward [[boundaries]]: the difference between a boundary and a compromise
- Toward [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]]: the diagnostic for unspoken compromise
- Toward [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]]: when the compromise has been long-standing
- Toward [[agreements-renegotiate-as-you-change]]: the larger principle that working agreements (and the workarounds they require) update over time

## Tone

Direct and practical. Joe is not gentle on this one. The "highly recommend never doing that. Highly recommend never doing that" is repeated with emphasis. The doubling is the tone. The cost of compromise is treated as obvious and large, and the creative alternative is treated as available, not aspirational.
