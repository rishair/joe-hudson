# Stress Comes Mostly From the Rejection of the Stress (Not the Stress Itself)

## Definition

The activation in the body that we call "stress" is not, by itself, the problem. The same activation that feels like stress when rejected feels like aliveness when received. The variable is the relationship to the activation, not the activation itself.

Two people in identical conditions: one is having a great time, one is suffering. The conditions are not the variable. The relationship to the conditions is the variable.

## The Woods Story

The teaching's anchor example. A 26 or 27 year old goes out into the woods to fast and be alone. Sitting in the woods, the body produces stress — just naturally, because of the unfamiliarity, the hunger, the silence. The first thought: "oh my God, I'm stressed out." The next thought: "what's wrong with me that I'm stressed out?" The system becomes more stressed.

The recognition: the stress in the woods was not the problem. The story about the stress was the problem. The thought "I shouldn't be stressed" generated more stress than the original activation. The thought "what's wrong with me?" added shame on top of the stress.

"It wasn't like there was, oh, this is too much stress and this is not enough stress. It was, am I thinking about my stress in a way that is, oh, this is useful? Is it like in my system? Does it feel — do my thoughts show, oh hey, this is useful? Or am I rejecting the stress? Am I saying, no, this isn't good, this is bad, I need to stop that? And that is actually what made the stress good for me or bad for me." -- Joe Hudson's co-teacher, 4 Causes of Stress

## The Basketball Comparison

The same activation that produces suffering in one context produces enjoyment in another. Playing basketball: there is massive activation in the body, a stress to the effort of trying to get to the basket. That stress can feel really good. The same level of activation showing up before going to work — "fuck, I gotta go to work again, this sucks" — feels terrible.

"Let's say you're playing a game like basketball. There's a massive activation, there's a stress to it. You're trying to get to the place, you're trying to get the basket. There's the stress to it. It can feel really, really good. Or you can be, fuck, I gotta go to work again, this sucks, and I'm thinking about it all the time." -- Joe Hudson's co-teacher, 4 Causes of Stress

The body cannot tell the difference between the basketball-stress and the work-stress on the activation level. The difference is in whether the system is receiving the activation or rejecting it.

## The Mechanism

The rejection of an experience adds a second layer on top of the first. The first layer is the original experience (stress, sadness, fear, whatever). The second layer is the resistance to the first layer. The second layer is typically larger than the first.

A useful framing: "what I resist, persists." The activation that is rejected has nowhere to discharge — the system is busy fighting it, not metabolizing it. The activation that is received moves through and clears.

This is the same mechanism as in [[meaning-of-overwhelm-determines-direction]]: the meaning made of the overwhelm determines whether it builds the person or breaks them. The overwhelm itself is the same; the meaning is the variable.

It is also why [[dont-add-the-overwhelm-of-creating-the-overwhelm]] is a coaching anti-pattern. Telling someone "you are creating your own stress" too early adds shame on top of the stress, which is itself more stress. The teaching is true, but the timing matters because the act of teaching can itself produce more rejection-of-stress.

## The Diagnostic Question

When stress is operating, ask: am I in relationship to this stress as something useful, or am I rejecting it?

If the answer is "rejecting": the rejection itself is most of the load. Softening the rejection often reduces the felt stress by 70-90 percent, even when nothing about the external situation has changed. The remaining 10-30 percent is the actual activation, which can be felt and which moves.

If the answer is "this is useful, this is what I'd expect, this is fine": the activation is already moving. There is little to do. The system is metabolizing.

## Why "Just Have a Positive Attitude" Is Incomplete

The natural conclusion from this principle is: "so I should have a positive attitude about my stress and the problem will go away." That conclusion is half right and half wrong. See [[pep-talk-cant-override-the-body]] for the full treatment.

The short version: cognitive reframe can shift the relationship to stress somewhat. There is also a layer underneath the thought — the nervous-system level, the muscular level — where the body's actual rejection lives. If the body is still tensing against the experience, no amount of positive self-talk will change the felt stress. The work has to happen at multiple layers.

But — and this matters — the cognitive reframe is not nothing. It opens the door. The thought "this stress is useful, I can enjoy this" is the first step. The body's softening follows or doesn't follow. If it doesn't, deeper work is needed, but the door has been opened.

## Application: When Stress Is the Presenting Concern

When someone presents "I'm so stressed out," the first thing to investigate is not the to-do list. It is the relationship to the stress.

- "What does the stress feel like?" (open the body to it)
- "What thought are you having about the stress?" (find the rejection)
- "What if this activation was useful?" (offer the cognitive shift)
- "Where in your body is the rejection?" (locate the muscular layer)

This is upstream of removing items from the calendar. The calendar might still need to change. But changing the calendar without softening the rejection just produces a quieter, more managed version of the same loop.

See [[four-causes-of-stress]] for the broader framework. This principle is the thought-level entry point of cause 1.

## Tone

Practical. The teaching is delivered without mysticism. The same activation can be enjoyed or suffered — that is a mechanical observation, not a spiritual one. The work is on the relationship, which is trainable.
