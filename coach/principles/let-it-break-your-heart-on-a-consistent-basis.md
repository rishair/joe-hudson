# Let the Impact Break Your Heart on a Consistent Basis

## The Principle

As influence grows — as more people are touched by your work, your leadership, your decisions — the structural risk is empathy loss. The studies are clear: power tends to dull empathy. The mechanism is simple. The impact becomes abstract. Decisions get made about people who have receded into statistics. The leader is no longer affected by the people the leadership affects.

The corrective is not to limit the influence. The corrective is to let the impact, when it arrives, actually land in the body. To let the heart be broken by the lives changed by the work. To not abstract the impact into "the work has reach" but to feel each specific encounter with someone whose life the work has touched. Consistently. As an ongoing practice.

This is what permeability looks like operationally. See [[dissolution-of-self-vs-permeability-of-self]] for the distinction this principle sits inside.

## Joe's Naming

Joe describes this as the next phase of his own work, as the platform around him grows:

"Nowadays, it's at least daily now. It's maybe even getting twice a day that I get a DM or a handwritten note. The other day, typed notes of people not knowing me, showing gratitude, just needing to express gratitude for what the work has done in their lives. That's like constantly happening now. Like, I constantly am seeing and being told about somebody whose life has changed." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The data — the changed lives — is already arriving daily. The question is what to do with it. The temptation is to abstract it into "the work has reach" and let the daily arrivals become background. The alternative:

"To be intimate with that experience is incredibly personal. To allow that to break my heart, I know that, that, that to allow that in on a personal level, on an intimate level, is gonna be required for me not to become dismissive of people, to not — there's this whole thing about power that like, as you have more power, you lose empathy. There's like a whole bunch of studies on it. For me not to lose that empathy, actually requires me to allow that to break my heart on a consistent basis, like this guy in this hot spring." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The reference to "this guy in this hot spring" is the man Joe met who started weeping while telling a single story about helping a woman at Walmart interpret a dream. The story matters because the man's own weeping demonstrated what Joe was describing: he could not have told the story that way if it had not been personal for him. The personal-ness was what made the impact transmissible. Joe's recognition: "You can't do that if it's not personal."

The same applies on the other side. The teacher cannot stay actually connected to the changed lives if the changed lives are abstracted out of body-level contact.

## Why the Practice Has to Be Consistent

A one-time encounter with someone whose life shifted from the work might move the teacher. The teacher might cry. The teacher might feel the weight of it. Then the encounter passes and the abstraction returns. The next changed life shows up as a DM. The teacher reads it, feels a vague good, moves on. Within a week, the structure is back: "the work has reach" without the body-level contact with what reach means.

The practice has to be consistent because the abstraction returns faster than the heartbreak does. The empathy-preserving move is not a one-time heart-opening. It is the daily allowance of impact to actually land. The cadence matches the cadence of arrival. If the changed lives are showing up daily, the heartbreak has to be available daily.

This is structurally the same principle as [[letting-judgment-in]] — allowing what would otherwise be deflected to actually touch the body. It is also the same principle as not building up immunity to gratitude. The shield against being touched, once installed, generalizes. The teacher who has built the shield against praise has also built the shield against being moved.

## What "Break Your Heart" Means

The phrase is precise. It does not mean be devastated. It means allow the heart to break open. The break is the move from a closed structure to a porous one. The structure that was containing the impact at arm's length yields, and the impact enters.

This is the language Joe uses elsewhere for vulnerability and for love. See [[letting-judgment-in]] ("let it break your heart") and [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] (the parallel practice for letting compliments and recognition land rather than deflecting them). The heart-break is the felt-shift from "this is about them, I am observing" to "this is also about me, I am affected." The teacher remains the teacher. The teacher is also a person, in a body, who can be moved.

The "break" in "break your heart" is not a tragedy. It is the structural softening that makes contact possible. Without it, the contact is performed but not felt. The student senses the difference even when they cannot name it.

## The Specific Failure Mode This Prevents

The failure mode is the slow conversion of the practitioner into someone for whom the people they are working with have become abstractions. This is the audience-capture mechanism named in the negative ([[audience-capture]]): the leader is shaped by the energy of the crowd, but the crowd itself is undifferentiated. The leader is responsive to "the audience" without being touched by any specific person inside the audience.

The practitioner who lets the heart be broken on a consistent basis is structurally protected from this. Each specific person who reports the impact of the work is a specific person, not an audience-member. The leader who can be moved by individual encounters cannot fully convert their crowd into an abstraction. The crowd retains its members. The members retain their specificity.

This is why the move from dissolution to permeability matters at the developmental level Joe is describing. Dissolution can be the cover for the abstraction. "I'm just a channel; the work is not mine to take personally" can become "the people the work touches are not mine to feel either." The permeability frame restores the personal that the dissolution frame removed.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The operational form:

- When a message of gratitude arrives, do not just read it. Pause. Let it land in the body. Allow the feeling that wants to arise.
- When you encounter a person whose life has been changed by the work, do not abstract them into a success story. Hold them as a person. Let yourself be affected by what they have actually been through.
- When the volume of changed-life reports grows to the point that any individual one is statistically small, hold at least some of them as concrete. Pick one each day. Stay with it.
- When tears arrive in the encounter with someone changed by the work, let them arrive. Crying is not unprofessional. The blockage of crying is the start of the abstraction.
- Notice when "the work is touching a lot of people" is becoming the cognitive substitute for being touched by specific people. The substitution is the failure mode.
- If you cannot remember the faces of the people who have reported changes recently, the abstraction is winning. Slow down. Re-encounter the recent ones.

## The Both-And With Dissolution

This principle is not the abandonment of the channel-frame. The channel-frame remains true: the wisdom is not the practitioner's, the work is moving through. AND the impact is personal, the practitioner is real, the heart can be broken by what the work is doing in people's lives.

The both-and is the work. The teacher who only has dissolution loses the empathy. The teacher who only has permeability claims the wisdom and loses the structural humility that made the work clean. The integrated form holds both.

Joe is explicit about this:

"So on one level, it's like, yeah, it's me doing this. It's like, it's not not me. It's like, it's me doing this. And there's like, and, and, but the other way, the most profound way that I felt it recently is..." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The "it's me AND it's not not me" is the held both-and. The permeability is the new addition that makes the both-and complete. See [[the-hollowing-out-process]] for the developmental arc this principle is one move inside.

- A practitioner whose work is producing visible impact but who has stopped being moved by the impact
- A leader confronting the studies on power and empathy and asking what the corrective is
- A person whose audience is growing and who is wondering how to hold it without becoming what audiences turn most people into
