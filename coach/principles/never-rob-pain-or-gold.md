# Never Rob a Person of Their Pain or Their Gold (Both Serve Them Equally)

## The Principle

A person's pain belongs to them. So does their gold. The instinct to remove someone's pain -- to spare them, to fix it, to skip them ahead to the lesson -- robs them of something that was theirs. The instinct to give them gold they did not earn -- to install your wisdom in their consciousness, to give them the answer that took you decades, to short-circuit their own discovery -- robs them of something equally important.

Both directions are robbery. Both are typically motivated by love. Both still rob.

The phrasing comes from Aaron Taylor, in the parenting context:

"I've got to value their walk, right? Never rob a man of his pain or his gold because both serve him equally well." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The principle applies far beyond parenting. It applies to coaching, mentoring, leadership, friendship, marriage, any relationship where one person has the temptation and the capacity to install their hard-won understanding into someone else's process.

## The Pain Side

The pain side is more familiar. Most discussions of caretaking, codependence, and enabling cover the basic insight: removing someone's pain (paying off their debt, hiding the consequences of their behavior, doing for them what they need to do for themselves) prevents the growth that the pain was producing. See [[caretaking-prevents-the-bottom]] for the structural treatment.

The pain that a person feels in a developmental moment is doing work. It is calibrating something. It is reorganizing what they understand. It is producing the felt urgency that will make change possible. When that pain is removed by an outside party, the calibration does not complete. The person stays in the original configuration with the additional debt of having been bailed out.

Aaron's autobiographical instance: his mother's response when, at 15, he came home from his first day of full-pads practice at De La Salle in tears, certain he could not continue. She did not remove the pain. She did not insist that he try again. She did not offer reassurance that it would get better.

"She said to me, 'You got to figure out if what you want is worth the price that you may have to pay to get it. Doesn't matter to me whether or not you play football or not. But tomorrow morning I'll have breakfast ready for you, and if you get up, I'll know the decision you made. And if you sleep in, I also know the decision that you made. But either way, I won't say a word to you.'" -- Aaron Taylor's recollection, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The pain stayed Aaron's pain. The decision stayed Aaron's decision. He showed up the next morning. The career that followed (Notre Dame, Green Bay Packers, Super Bowl, Hall of Fame) rested on a moment that would not have happened if his mother had spared him the pain.

## The Gold Side

The gold side is less familiar but equally important. A person's *gold* is the wisdom, the realization, the insight that they earned through their own process. The temptation to give someone else your gold -- to install your hard-won understanding in their consciousness before they have done the work to discover it themselves -- is just as much a robbery as removing their pain.

The reason it is robbery: the gold matters not as content but as the artifact of the process that produced it. Your gold delivered to me as a statement is a clever idea. The same gold discovered by me through my own process is integrated knowing. The form is the same. The function is completely different.

Aaron's autobiographical instance: his impulse with his 13-year-old water polo player son to insert his viewpoint, his TED Talks, his lessons about discipline and details and resilience. The frustration that they are not absorbing the content. The recognition:

"I have to laugh at myself in those moments because it's like, what are you doing, dude? Like, they are perfect. My mom did -- she didn't even play hopscotch, let alone play sports, and I got exactly what I needed at the right time in the right way. How about just listening, accepting, and admiring, and cheering your kids on, and stop trying to be what you wished you had when you were freaking 13?" -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The gold he is trying to install in his son is real. It is genuinely useful gold. The problem is that his son cannot receive it as gold -- only as instruction. The instruction installs as an external should, not as integrated knowing. The son will either rebel against it or comply with it. Either way, it is not his.

## Why Both Pain and Gold Are Specifically the Person's

The principle frames pain and gold together because they are the same structure viewed from two angles. The pain is the felt friction of a developmental edge. The gold is the integrated understanding that follows when the friction has done its work. They are continuous. Removing one prevents the other.

A parent who spares the child the pain also prevents the gold. A coach who installs the gold also robs the pain (because the pain was about discovering the gold the hard way). The two are bound together.

The hardest version of the principle is when you can see exactly what gold the person is about to discover. The temptation is enormous: I know what they are going to learn, why not just tell them and save them the months of suffering? The principle says: the months of suffering ARE the gold. The content delivered without the suffering is not gold. It is a clever quote.

## What This Permits

The principle does not say to stand by silently. It permits much. It permits:

- Witnessing. Naming what you see without prescribing what to do about it.
- Asking. Inviting reflection through questions whose answers belong to the person.
- Modeling. Living the way you live and letting the other person decide what to draw from it.
- Loving. Holding the person with affection through their pain without trying to remove it.
- Sharing your own story. Not as a lesson for them but as a piece of your honest disclosure that they can use as they wish.

What it does not permit:

- Lecturing. Installing the conclusion.
- Bailing out. Removing the pain because it is uncomfortable for you to witness.
- Pre-empting the discovery. Telling them the answer because you cannot bear watching them search.
- Inserting your TED Talks into their teenage water polo career.

## Aaron's Realization About His Own Children

The transcript captures the realization in real time:

- **Aaron, in jest at first:** Back in my day, I didn't have a father to tell me to brush my teeth. [The reverse joke: my kids have what I didn't, why don't they appreciate it?]
- **Aaron, going deeper:** It crushes me that they don't listen to all the TED Talks on success and resilience and teamwork and the win one for the Gipper speeches that I try to fire hose them with. Like, I didn't have a dad, and you guys don't let -- I get paid to talk, and you guys don't want to listen to me.
- **Aaron, the turn:** And the reality is, like, you don't know what is going to work and what's going to be there. And I have to laugh at myself in those moments [...] My mom did -- she didn't even play hopscotch, let alone play sports, and I got exactly what I needed at the right time in the right way.

The turn happens when Aaron applies the principle to himself. His own mother was specifically *not* an expert in football. She did not have gold to install. The thing that worked for him was the thing he is robbing his children of: the chance to discover their own gold through their own process.

The realization extends into the principle. The gold he is trying to give his sons is not gold to them. The gold for them is the gold they will earn themselves. The most loving thing he can do is the thing his mother did: get out of the way, witness, love, and let their gold be theirs.

## The Coaching Application

The principle is foundational to coaching as Joe practices it. See [[the-work-is-yours-not-mine]] and [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]]. The coach who installs answers into the client robs the client's gold. The coach who removes the client's pain robs the discovery. The coach's job is to be present, to ask, to witness, and to refuse to do for the client what the client must do for themselves.

This is why Joe does not push, does not prescribe, and does not save people from quitting. The pain of considering quitting is doing work. The gold of staying or leaving belongs to the client. See [[meet-resistance-with-okay]].

## The Leadership Application

The principle applies to leadership. A leader who has accumulated decades of insight is constantly tempted to install that insight into employees. The faster path looks like: tell them what I know, save them the months of mistakes. The actual effect: the employees develop in a particular shape that depends on the leader's gold, not their own. When the leader is no longer present, the gold does not generalize.

The leader who applies this principle states their wants (see [[state-the-want-not-the-action]]), holds the structure, asks questions, and lets the employees discover what they discover. The team that develops this way is more capable than the team developed under the install-the-gold model, because the gold belongs to the people who have it.

## When the Principle Is Hard

The principle is hardest:

- When you can see the pain about to happen and feel responsible for warning the person.
- When you love the person and the pain looks unnecessary.
- When the gold you have is genuinely useful and would save the person years of work.
- When the person is asking you for the answer and you have it.
- When the cultural pressure (especially in parenting) is that the loving thing is to spare them and give them.

The principle does not say to never speak. It says to speak in ways that do not rob. Sometimes that means saying nothing. Sometimes that means saying something that points without prescribing. The honest test: am I trying to give them their gold or skip them past their pain? If yes, hold the impulse and ask what would honor their walk.
