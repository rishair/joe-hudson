# When You Feel the Emotion Fully, the Story Changes (Every Time)

## The Principle

If you allow an emotion to move fully -- not just observe it, not just name it, but let it actually move through the body -- the story attached to it will change. Not sometimes. Every time.

"If you allow the emotions to move, your stories change, period. Every time." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

This is one of the most reliable mechanisms in the work. It explains why somatic discharge produces cognitive shifts. It explains why a person walks out of a session seeing a relationship differently after a big cry. The change is not coincidental. It is structural.

## The Mechanism

A story holds an emotional charge. The emotion holds the story in place. When the emotion moves, the structural support for the story dissolves. What remains is either a clarified version of the story, a different story altogether, or no story at all.

The mind cannot accomplish this by itself. Cognitive deconstruction can change which story is in awareness, but it cannot release the emotional charge that locks a particular story in. That release requires the body to actually move the emotion.

See [[emotions-and-stories-dance]] for the bidirectional framing.

## Concrete Example: The Rental House

Joe and his wife Tara were renting a house and got kicked out after a conflict with the owner-neighbors over their daughter. Every time Joe drove past the house, he felt "a kick in the stomach." He would speed up, try not to feel it.

One day he decided to do the opposite. Every time he drove past, he stopped the car and let himself feel the kick fully. He invited it. He wanted to "milk this thing for everything."

Two or three weeks later, driving past the house produced nothing but love. The story had been "these people are assholes who screwed us." After the emotion had been fully felt, the story had changed to: "I could see their pain, I could see their anger, I could see all of them. It wasn't just one aspect of them anymore that I was looking at."

"My story changed from these people are assholes who screwed us to I could see their pain, I could see their anger, I could see, I could see all of them. It wasn't just one aspect of them anymore that I was looking at. And so that's how feeling your emotions can change your stories." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

See [[joes-rental-house-anger]] for the full example.

## Why "Every Time"

Joe rarely uses absolute language. "Every time" is one of the rare absolutes. The reason it can be absolute is that the story-emotion link is structural, not coincidental.

If a story is locked in place by an emotion, and the emotion moves, the lock releases. There is no scenario where this would not be true except one: where the emotion did not actually move. In that case, the test would be the lack of change in the story.

This makes the principle into a diagnostic. If the emotion moved and the story did not change, the emotion did not actually move. The release was performance, naming, or observation -- not actual movement. See [[dont-name-emotion-to-avoid-feeling]].

## The Two Outcomes

When the emotion moves fully, one of two things happens to the story:

**The story dissolves or expands.** What was "they screwed us" becomes "I see all of them." What was "I'm worthless" becomes a more complicated and less burdensome picture. The story does not necessarily become positive. It becomes accurate, or it becomes irrelevant.

**A deeper story is revealed.** Sometimes the surface story drops away and what is underneath is another story, one that was being protected by the first. The work continues at this new layer. See [[emotions-and-stories-dance]] on how this layered revelation works.

Either way, the original story does not survive the actual movement of the emotion.

## What This Does Not Mean

This is not a method for changing your feelings about someone in order to keep tolerating their behavior. The story changing is not the goal. The story changing is the byproduct of the emotion moving. Trying to feel an emotion in order to change a story is [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]] in new clothes -- and that motivation prevents the actual movement.

Sometimes after the emotion moves, the new story is "I want even less to do with this person than before." The clarity that arrives is its own clarity, not the clarity you predicted. See [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]].

## The Coaching Implication

When a person is stuck in a story (a fixed interpretation, a grievance that recurs, a self-concept that will not shift), the question is not "how can we change the story." The question is "what emotion is keeping this story in place." Find the emotion. Help it move. The story will take care of itself.

This is also why repeatedly cognitively deconstructing a stuck story often fails. The story is not held by reasoning. It is held by an unfelt emotion. Reasoning the story away leaves the emotion in place, which reloads the story. The reverse approach succeeds where the cognitive approach plateaus.

## Tone

Joe says this with quiet certainty. Not as a sales pitch. He has watched it happen in himself and in clients enough times that the absolute is earned. When he says "every time," there is no qualifier. It is a description of how the system actually works.
