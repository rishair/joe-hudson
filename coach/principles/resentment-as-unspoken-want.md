# Resentment Means You Are Not Following Your Wants

## The Principle

Resentment is information. Specifically, it is the information that you are not following your wants, not holding your boundaries, not saying what you want, or not getting your needs met. The feeling is not a problem to manage. It is a signal to be read.

"If someone feels resentful, it means that they're not going -- they're not following what they want. They're not holding their boundaries. They're not saying the things that they want. They're not getting their needs met. So resentfulness is a really good way to find that indicator." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## What Resentment Is Pointing At

The resentment usually frames itself as being about the other person. They are demanding too much. They are inconsiderate. They do not appreciate me. They make it impossible to say no.

The principle redirects. The other person's behavior is the occasion for the resentment. The cause is the unfollowed want inside the resentful person. The other could not produce resentment if there were not an unspoken want for them to bump up against.

This is uncomfortable to receive. The resenter would prefer the resentment to be about the other person, because then the corrective is on the other person. The principle puts the corrective back on the resenter: what do you want that you are not following or saying?

## The Two Directions of the Same Signal

The other side of the principle: if you are asking for what you want and someone is dismissing you or getting angry at you, that is also data.

"Then the other side of it is you'll get dismissed. If you're starting to really ask what you want and people get resentful, you'll see their anger or dismissal from them, and that's another good indicator." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The resentment-in-the-other surfaces when their unowned wants conflict with your owned wants. The signal flows in both directions.

## How This Sits Alongside the Caretaking Diagnostic

[[resentment-as-caretaking-diagnostic]] is a specific case of this broader principle. Caretaking produces resentment because caretaking is one of the most common ways people refuse to follow their own wants and refuse to set their own boundaries. The caretaker is paying with truth-suppression and self-abandonment. The unspoken want underneath the caretaking is the source of the resentment.

But the broader principle covers cases beyond caretaking:

- The employee resenting their boss is usually not asking for what they want at work
- The friend resenting a friend group is usually not setting the boundaries they want
- The spouse resenting their partner is usually not naming what they want from the relationship
- The artist resenting their job is usually not honoring the want to make art
- The citizen resenting the system is usually not acting on what they want to build

In every case, the resentment is doing work that owned wants and clear boundaries would otherwise do.

## Why This Is More Useful Than "Process the Resentment"

The common move with resentment is to "work on it." Therapy the resentment. Forgive the person. Reframe the situation. Find compassion.

Joe's framing makes this work secondary. The primary move is to ask: what want am I not following? What boundary am I not holding? What ask am I not making?

Once those questions are answered and acted on, the resentment usually relaxes on its own. Without the underlying unfollowed want, there is nothing for the resentment to be fed by.

## The Body Signal

Resentment has a specific somatic signature: a tight, sour, low-grade aversion that builds over time. It is distinct from acute anger (which is sharp and fast) and from disappointment (which is more sad than aversive). Once the body signature is recognized, it becomes a reliable internal alarm.

The alarm asks the same question every time: where am I not following my want?

## Why Resentment Is So Common

Most people were taught as children that their wants were bad. The training did not eliminate the wants; it eliminated the willingness to follow or name them. The wants stayed, the inhibition compounded, and resentment became the chronic background of much of adult life.

See [[childhood-imprinting]] for the underlying mechanism. The current resentment is fed by old training. The current corrective is to start following the current want.

## The Practice

When resentment surfaces:

1. Notice it as a signal, not a problem.
2. Ask: what do I want here that I am not saying or following?
3. Locate the want. It will usually be specific (I want to leave this conversation, I want my colleague to stop interrupting me, I want time alone this weekend).
4. Decide what action would honor the want (state it, set the boundary, change the schedule).
5. Take the action.
6. Notice the resentment relaxing as the want is honored.

The practice is repeatable. Most resentments fold quickly once the underlying want is named and acted on.

## What This Is Not

- Not a license to use "I'm resentful" as a weapon. The signal points back at you. It does not authorize blaming the other person.
- Not a denial that the other person did anything. Sometimes the other person genuinely transgressed and the resentment is also about that. The principle is that even in that case, your resentment-now is being fed by your unfollowed want-now.
- Not a quick fix. Recognizing the want is often easier than acting on it. The principle does not eliminate the fear or shame of acting; it only relocates the work.
