# Enjoyment Attracts

## The Principle

People who enjoy their life start doing things they enjoy more and more. People who endure their life end up doing things they endure more and more. The choice in any given moment is the choice that compounds.

"People who enjoy their life, they start doing things that they enjoy more and more. And people who endure their life end up doing things that they endure more and more." -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work

This is the trajectory principle: enjoyment and endurance are both attractors, both compounding, both self-reinforcing over time. The life you have a few years from now will be made of the moments you currently reinforce.

## The Two Mechanisms

The compounding works through two distinct pathways. Both are operating at all times.

**The external pathway: enjoyment attracts what you enjoy.** When you do things in a way you enjoy, you attract people who also want to be in enjoyment. You attract work that you will enjoy. The opportunities that arrive are filtered by the state from which you do the work. Doing it joyfully attracts joyful opportunities; doing it grimly attracts grim opportunities.

"When you are doing things in a way that you enjoy them, what you do is you attract people. You attract people who also want to be in enjoyment. You attract work that you will enjoy." -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work

**The internal pathway: enjoyment expands the capacity for enjoyment.** Each enjoyed moment trains the nervous system to enjoy. Each endured moment trains the nervous system to endure. The trajectory is built one moment at a time, and each moment locks in the next moment's default.

The two pathways reinforce each other. The enjoyed work attracts enjoyable opportunities, which then are received by a nervous system more capable of enjoying them. The endured work attracts more endurable obligations, which are received by a nervous system increasingly trained in endurance.

## Why Enjoyment Is Readable

The attractor mechanism is not magic. Enjoyment is somatically detectable. When a person is in enjoyment, others can feel it. When a person is enduring, others can feel that too. The transmission is direct, before any words are exchanged.

This is the same mechanism described in [[consciousness-is-the-product]]: whatever state you are in while doing the work becomes part of what the work delivers. The state radiates from the work into the people who encounter the work. They reciprocate the state they receive.

A salesperson who is enjoying the sale produces a buyer who is more open to buying. A teacher who is enjoying the teaching produces students who are more open to learning. A leader who is enjoying the leading produces a team that wants to be on that team. The reverse is also true: the enduring salesperson produces buyers who feel sold-to; the enduring teacher produces students who tune out; the enduring leader produces a team that disengages.

"It's not authentic to you. And so everybody can read and feel that." -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work (on why the un-enjoyed marketing campaign did not work)

The marketing case illustrates the principle at the operational level. See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] and [[authenticity-in-business]] for the worked example.

## The Fork in Any Moment

The principle's coaching value is that it converts an abstract life-trajectory question into a present-tense choice. The question is not "what kind of life do I want?" The question is "what am I reinforcing right now?"

Every action is being done either enjoyably or endurably. There is no neutral. Even the most ordinary task — emails, dishes, the commute — is reinforcing one of the two trajectories. The compound interest runs on small repeated decisions, not on dramatic choices.

This is also why the answer to "how do I build a life I enjoy?" is not "find activities I will enjoy." It is "enjoy the activity I am currently doing." See [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]] for the move. The find-the-right-activity strategy works against the principle: it postpones enjoyment to a future activity while reinforcing endurance in the present one.

## The Endurance Trap

The mirror image of the enjoyment trajectory deserves naming, because most people are running it without recognizing it. The endurance trajectory feels responsible. It feels grown-up. It feels like the path to eventual freedom. It is none of those things. It is a self-reinforcing loop that produces more of itself.

The person who endures their job to get to retirement is not landing in enjoyment at retirement. They are landing in a more deeply trained endurance, applied now to retirement. The structure that converted work into endurance does not stop operating when work stops.

The same logic applies to the person who endures the dating they hate to find the partner, endures the work they hate to make the money, endures the obligations they have to earn the freedom. The endurance is the trajectory. The arrival point inherits the trajectory's shape.

This is also why [[i-make-money-only-when-i-hate-what-im-doing]] is a self-perpetuating concern. The endurance is being reinforced; the enjoyment is being deferred; the wiring is being installed for the next round of the same pattern.

## When Joe Reaches for This Principle

- When someone describes a long-term plan that requires enduring the present for a future payoff.
- When someone is grinding through work they do not enjoy and asking how to be more productive.
- When [[i-keep-postponing-enjoyment]] needs the long-arc framing: not just "you cannot postpone enjoyment in this moment" but "the moments you postpone are training a life of postponement."
- When the question is "how do I build something I love?" and the answer is "by doing what you are currently doing in a way you love."
- When a leader is asking how to attract better employees, better customers, better partners — and the answer is at the level of how the work currently feels.

## The Self-Test

A person can apply the principle to themselves with one observation: look at what they are doing more of, over the past year. The things they are doing more of are the things they were enjoying. The things they are doing less of, or only out of obligation, are the things they were enduring. The pattern is visible in their calendar, in their relationships, in their work output. The trajectory has been operating whether they noticed it or not.

This is also a coaching diagnostic. When a client asks "what should I do next?" Joe can sometimes invert the question: "what have you been doing more and more of? what have you been doing less and less of?" The pattern names the trajectory. The trajectory names what is being trained.

## The Reframe for "I Have to Get Things Done"

A common objection: "I do not have time to enjoy the work. I have to get things done." The principle reframes the objection structurally. The work being done from endurance is also producing the next batch of work. If the next batch is going to be more endurance, that is the trajectory. If the next batch is going to be enjoyable, the present work is what produces that.

The argument is not that enjoyment is more productive in the moment (though [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] also makes that case). The argument is that endurance is producing more endurance. The time saved by not enjoying is being spent on more of the same. The compounding works against the endure-now-enjoy-later plan.

"All that's required is that you just focus on the enjoyment the way that you would focus on getting a job done. And the job gets done. It just gets done better. It just gets done more efficiently. And you start having a life that you just enjoy." -- Joe Hudson, How To Enjoy Your Work
