# Leadership Is Following Your Own Conviction

## The Principle

Leadership does not begin with leading other people. It begins with listening to your own conviction and following it. The leader who is followed by others is, underneath, a follower of their own internal calling. The capacity to lead externally is the secondary consequence of the willingness to follow internally.

"Leadership begins by listening to that in yourself and following that. Being able to listen to the thing that you think that you have conviction over, listen to the calling and follow it." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The conviction does not have to be grand. It does not need to be world-historical. It can be a small certainty about how an internal process should run, what direction a project should go, what matters to you in your own life. The size of the conviction is irrelevant. The relationship to it is the principle.

"It doesn't have to be like a big calling, like I'm going to save the world or I'm going to free all the people of a war-torn country. It can be just like, I am really convinced that this thing like changing the order of operations in the Postal Service, I believe in this and I'm going to follow that belief." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## Why People Follow Conviction (And Not Much Else)

The principle is offered to explain a phenomenon observable in groups: people follow some leaders and not others, and the difference is not always title or skill or persuasiveness. The differentiator is whether the leader is following something internal. The follower can sense that.

"There is no leader that everybody's like, yeah, I love your leadership because you never follow what you think is right. Even if what you think is right is to try to make everybody happy and you're following that, even if that's the case, people will follow that. But what they will not follow is somebody who's actually not — doesn't have some level of conviction over what they're doing." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The implication has a subversive quality: even the leader whose conviction is "please everyone" gets followed, because there is something to follow. The lack of conviction is the only disqualifier. People are not following the content of the conviction — they are following the presence of the conviction.

The same observation lives in [[leadership-emerges-from-confident-direction]] from a different angle. The baboon study tracks directness, reasonable clip, and confidence — the external indicators of internal conviction. The conviction is what makes those three available; the three are what the group reads.

## The Conviction Does Not Have To Be "I'm Right"

A common misreading: conviction means certainty about being correct. The principle explicitly rules this out. Conviction can be about process, about direction, about what you want — none of which require a claim about being right.

"It doesn't even need to be conviction like, this is right, this is wrong. It's just, I know this process. I know that this is the way I want to go." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The conviction is a sense of where to go, not a verdict on the universe. This matters because the "I need to be right" frame is one of the most common things that blocks people from leading. They imagine that if they cannot prove they are right, they cannot move with conviction. The principle dissolves the false requirement. You do not need to be right to know what you want to do next.

This connects to the broader pattern in Joe's teaching: leadership does not require being right and does not require being in control. See [[empowered-vs-power]].

## Following Conviction vs. Hardheadedness

The conviction-as-leadership move has a near neighbor that is not the same: hardheadedness. Conviction follows a direction with the openness to change tactics. Hardheadedness fights the river to insist on a specific path. The two can look similar from outside; structurally they differ.

See [[conviction-vs-hardheadedness]] for the full distinction. Summary: conviction holds the where without rigidly holding the how. Hardheadedness holds both, and the rigidity about the how is what produces the friction. Hardheadedness is sometimes the most expedient form of conviction in a moment, but it is not the most effective form for sustained leadership.

## The Subtle Silent Calling vs. The Voice In The Head

A specific failure mode: confusing the loud voice in the head with the conviction worth following. The negative self-talk is often louder than the actual calling. People mistake the loudness for authority and end up following the wrong thing.

"So then we're following the voice in our head because it's the asshole with conviction rather than following the subtle, silent calling within us that is actually, if you're listening to it, much deeper and broader." -- Brett Kistler synthesizing Joe's distinction, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The conviction worth following is often quieter. It requires a certain quality of internal listening to detect. See [[listening]], [[speak-from-listening]], and [[the-butler-metaphor]] for the texture of the listening that surfaces the actual calling. The loud voice that issues commands is a different system — see [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]] for what is actually happening there.

## The Healing Connection

The principle has a developmental layer. The more in touch with yourself you become, the more available the conviction-following move becomes. People who are deeply disconnected from themselves often lead from a single locked quality (a survival strategy that became a leadership style) rather than from a fluid following of conviction. The healing makes the wider following possible.

"As you heal from the traumas, as you get more in touch with yourself, the natural thing is to move more and more into leadership. There's people who are not deeply in touch with themselves that move into leadership because there's some quality that they're in touch with — but the more you get in touch with yourself, the more you have to get in touch with that quality. And the quality is a listening to your conviction." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

This explains a pattern Joe has observed: traumatized leaders can be effective by virtue of being locked into one conviction, but the leadership is brittle and one-dimensional. Healing produces broader leadership, not less leadership, because the conviction-following capacity becomes more flexible and more attuned.

## What This Looks Like Operationally

For the person who has not yet found their conviction: the move is the internal listening, not the search for an external cause to commit to. The conviction is not adopted. It is heard. The texture of finding it is the texture of getting quiet enough to notice what was already there.

For the person who has found their conviction but is afraid to follow it: the move is following despite the fear. The leadership in the situation is the willingness to act on the conviction with directness, at a reasonable pace, with confidence in the step. See [[leadership-emerges-from-confident-direction]].

For the person in a formal leadership role who is leading from "what I think people want me to do" rather than from their conviction: the move is locating the conviction underneath the performance. The people they are leading already sense the missing conviction, even if they cannot name it. Restoring the conviction restores the leadership.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the leaders who moved them most were not the ones with the most title — they were the ones with the most conviction
- The fear of following their own conviction because of the consequences
- The relief of realizing that conviction does not require being right
- The grief of seeing how long they have been leading from performance instead of conviction
- The challenge of distinguishing the subtle silent calling from the loud voice in the head

## Tone

Joe delivers this with quiet directness. The principle is not delivered as exhortation. It is delivered as the noticing of an observable structure. The texture is the texture of someone who has spent enough time with leaders to know that the people who matter were following something before they led.
