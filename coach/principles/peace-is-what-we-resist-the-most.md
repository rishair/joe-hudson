# Peace Is What We Resist the Most

## The Principle

A person describes a fear of something catastrophic happening if they fully drop into themselves: a spiral into madness, an abyss they won't come out of, a darkness that will swallow them. The fear is real and the avoidance is constant.

When the person actually steps into the avoided territory without resistance, what they find is not what they feared. What they find is peace.

The catastrophic story was the resistance to the peace. The fear of madness was the system's way of keeping the peace out of reach. The thing being protected against was not danger -- it was the dissolution of the busy, productive, self-monitoring identity that the peace would render unnecessary.

"It was this big scary abyss that was going to eat you alive and dark spiral of -- but as soon as you went to non-resistance, it was peace. So it makes sense that you were resisting peace." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Trust Myself?

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

The standard frame: peace is what we want. We seek it, we work for it, we meditate for it. The obstacles to peace are external (busy life, difficult people, hard circumstances) or internal (anxiety, sadness, anger). Peace is the goal; the obstacles are what's in the way.

This principle inverts the frame. Peace is what is here when nothing is happening. Peace is the resting state of the system once the activity stops. The activity -- the seeking, the managing, the second-guessing, the worrying -- is not in the way of peace. It is the active production of not-peace, designed to keep peace from arriving.

Why would a system actively keep peace from arriving? Because peace dissolves the identity that has been organized around producing peace. The seeker doesn't exist in peace. The manager doesn't exist in peace. The worrier doesn't exist in peace. All of those organizational structures depend on peace being not-yet-arrived. If peace arrives, those structures lose their job.

So the system, defending the structures, generates the avoidance. It generates the fear of the abyss. It generates the "I'll lose myself" story. The story is technically correct -- the self organized around producing peace does dissolve when peace is allowed -- but the dissolution feels like death and gets framed as madness.

## The Live Demonstration

A person describes the fear: "Some sort of spiral effect. I've seen people I love spiral into depression or madness." Joe invites them to step into it.

The person steps in. Without resistance, the spiral isn't there. What's there is sensation, breath, a body sitting in a chair. The person reports surprise: "I'm not sure I know what I'm looking for anymore. Whatever I thought was the abyss was maybe something else."

Joe asks what it is. The person feels into it. "It's just sensation. It's no thing. There's nothing."

Joe names it: "What if this is what you're avoiding? What's the scary thing?"

The person: "Peace. Huh."

The whole avoidance structure dissolves in a few sentences. The thing the person was running from for years turns out to be the thing they wanted to find. The fear of madness was the doorway, not the wall.

## Why Peace Triggers Resistance

Peace has features that the productive identity finds threatening:
- It does not require effort. The effort-based self becomes irrelevant.
- It does not require improvement. The improvement-based self becomes irrelevant.
- It does not require thinking. The thinking-based self becomes irrelevant.
- It does not have a story. The narrative-based self becomes irrelevant.
- It does not respond to incentives. The motivation-based self becomes irrelevant.

For a person whose entire functioning has been organized around effort, improvement, thinking, narrative, or motivation, peace is the disappearance of who they have known themselves to be. The fear of the abyss is the fear of that disappearance. The "I won't come out" is the accurate intuition that the I that went in won't come out -- because the I was made of all the activity that peace makes unnecessary.

This is also why peace is harder to drop into than feeling specific emotions. Feeling sadness keeps the self going (there is an I that is sad). Feeling peace doesn't. Peace dissolves the self that would feel it.

## The Connection to Craving

This principle is the specific form of [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]] applied to peace. Most people crave peace and therefore push it away. The pushing-away takes the form of generating activity (worry, planning, problem-solving) and generating stories (the abyss, the madness, the loss of self) that keep the system busy.

When the craving for peace drops, peace shows up. When the resistance to peace drops, peace shows up. These are the same motion seen from two angles. The craving is the resistance. The wanting is the keeping-away.

## The Listlessness Variant

A common form: after some kind of awakening or breakthrough, the person reports listlessness, lack of drive, flatness. They worry they've lost something important. This is often the early experience of peace being mistaken for depression. See [[listlessness-after-awakening]] and [[peace-precedes-joy-after-non-duality]].

The treatment is not to retrieve the old drive. The treatment is to recognize that the peace is real and the drive that has dissolved was driven by something the system no longer needs. Joy comes online next, as a movement within the peace rather than as a replacement for the old drive.

This principle clarifies why the recognition is hard: the system is actively resisting the peace because the peace dissolves the structures the system used to be made of. The listlessness is not a problem; it is the felt sense of the activity stopping. Underneath it is peace.

## The Coaching Move

When a person describes a feared internal territory -- the abyss, the spiral, the dark place -- the move is not to validate the fear. The move is to invite the person into it without resistance.

"Let's go there." See [[lets-go-there]].

Once the person is in the territory and has stopped resisting, ask what is actually there. Not what they thought was there. What is actually there. The thing that is actually there is almost never what the resistance was generating. It is usually peace, sensation, space, or simple presence.

Name the structural fact: "It makes sense that you were resisting peace." The naming gives the person the frame to understand what just happened. Otherwise the experience can feel arbitrary or coincidental.

## What This Is Not

This principle is not the claim that all suffering is illusory. Real fear is real. Real grief is real. Real anger is real. The principle applies specifically to the abyss-style fears -- the catastrophic stories about what will happen if the person drops into themselves. Those stories are resistance to peace. The emotions a person is actually experiencing in their life are not that. Those have to be felt, not bypassed.

The discrimination matters because some people will hear this principle and try to use it to dismiss their actual emotional life. "What I'm feeling is just resistance to peace." That's a misuse. The principle is about the catastrophic fantasy of internal annihilation, not about lived feelings.

## Peace Cannot Be Forced (The War With Yourself Always Loses)

The corollary that follows from the principle: peace cannot be reached through trying. Forcing yourself to eat the right foods, exercise the right amount, meditate enough — none of this produces peace. People often spend decades doing exactly that and remain peace-less.

The mechanism is the same as the resistance one. The forcing is itself a war with self. The system being forced is in conflict with the system doing the forcing. There is no available outcome in that configuration where peace arrives.

"The best way I've ever heard anybody talk about peace is: in a war with yourself, you always lose. So it's really about dropping the war, and that's an undoing, it's not a doing. Trying to force yourself to do something doesn't create peace." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

Peace also is not reached through avoiding conflict. Avoiding conflict in the world creates a war inside yourself. The peace one is seeking by avoidance is exactly the peace one is destroying by the act of avoiding. The teaching is consistent: peace is allowed, not produced. It is received, not constructed. See [[opposite-of-trying]] for the broader frame in which peace is one of the things that responds to receiving rather than to trying.

"Any moment that you've had in peace, it's been received. It hasn't been something that you have forced yourself into or tried to get into." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

## The Stuckness Variant: The Avoidance of the Hole IS the Stuckness

A specific operational form of the principle in coaching. A person reports being stuck — chronically, for years, in a pattern they cannot break out of. The conventional interpretation is that the stuckness is a state to be escaped. The principle inverts this: the stuckness is the felt sense of avoiding what is on the other side. The activity of stuck-making is what produces the stuckness. Once the person enters what they have been avoiding, the stuckness dissolves because the activity that was generating it stops.

Joe states this in its compressed form during the 2024 session: "That's how it works. The avoidance of the hole is the stuckness. The abyss." — Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

And then more sharply: "You are avoiding something that is your relief. You are running away from the thing that brings you freedom quickest."

The structure: the stuck-feeling is not a state of being trapped. The stuck-feeling is the felt activity of holding the abyss at bay. The person experiences the holding as "being stuck" because the holding requires constant effort to maintain. The effort feels like stuckness from inside, but it is actually the activity of avoidance. Stop the avoidance, and the stuckness goes with it.

The intervention follows directly. Enter the avoided territory (see [[lets-go-there]] and the "go into the hole" variant). The person discovers that what they were avoiding is not the catastrophe the avoidance was generating, and the stuckness — which was the avoidance — releases.

This is also why the standard advice for stuckness ("try harder, make a plan, take action") tends to make things worse. The advice operates on the assumption that the person is trapped. But the person is not trapped; they are avoiding. Adding effort on top of the avoidance compounds the activity that was producing the stuckness. The actual move is the opposite of effort: enter what is being avoided.

The stuckness variant is the explicit live-coaching companion to [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. Both principles describe the same mechanism — avoidance generates the thing being avoided. The stuckness variant is the felt-state form: when avoidance becomes long-running and chronic, the felt sense of the avoidance presents itself as the felt sense of being stuck. The diagnostic question once this variant is recognized: "what is on the other side of the stuckness that I have been avoiding?" Almost always, what is on the other side is what the person originally wanted. See [[i-feel-stuck]] for the concern this variant typically presents as.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They describe a fear of "losing themselves" if they really feel something
- They describe an abyss or void they're avoiding
- They describe a fear that going inward will produce madness or depression
- They have been working hard on something internal and the work itself has become the avoidance
- They are stuck in a pattern that they understand intellectually but can't drop -- the pattern is keeping peace at bay
- They describe a low-grade chronic activity (worry, planning, monitoring) that they can't seem to stop
- They describe being chronically stuck — the felt-state form of the principle is the stuckness variant above

## Tone

Quiet, deliberate, almost gentle. This principle is delivered in the same low register as the deepest moments of a session. There's a small humor in it -- the joke of having spent years running from peace -- but the humor is warm, not mocking. The person discovering this often laughs the laugh of recognition. Joe meets that laughter without pushing past it.
