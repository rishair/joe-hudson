# Public Failure Models More Than Performed Competence

## The Principle

A leader who lets their team watch them struggle in real time teaches more about the work than a leader who arrives with the answer. The struggle, witnessed, transmits something the polished version cannot.

The principle is counterintuitive. Most leaders are trained to demonstrate mastery: prepare the answer, deliver the conclusion, model what good looks like. The hidden cost: the team learns to perform mastery too. The team never sees the process by which someone actually figures something out. They only see the result. When they need to figure something out themselves, they lack the model.

Public failure inverts this. The team watches the leader fumble, get stuck, try again, miss, and eventually find the thing. They learn what the actual process looks like. They internalize permission to do the same.

## The Modeling Mechanism

Ant Taylor, CEO of Light, describes a moment where his executive team watched him fail for three hours on the Harrison Assessment exercise:

"This would have been a mortifying discovery for me, greater than 12 months ago, to realize that I was the problem in the room. But now, 12 months on, it actually became a super intense moment where my team got to watch me fail over and over and over again for 3 hours until I had that breakthrough. And there's no way I can simulate that for them. There's no way that they're going to get it better than to watch me struggle and find the thing, whatever the thing is, right in front of their eyes." -- Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

The phrase "there's no way I can simulate that for them" is the principle. The leader who has already done the work can describe the work, but cannot transmit it. The leader who does the work in real time, in front of the team, transmits it directly. The team's mirror neurons do the rest.

## What Performed Competence Trains

A team led by a leader who only shows competence learns:

- Mistakes should be hidden.
- The right answer is supposed to be obvious.
- Confusion is a sign of inadequacy.
- The space for not-knowing is private, not shared.
- Innovation requires being smart enough to arrive at it alone.

These lessons quietly shut down the team's actual creative work, which requires the opposite of all five.

## What Public Failure Trains

A team led by a leader who shows their struggle learns:

- Confusion is a normal phase of the work, not a sign of failure.
- The right answer is the one you arrive at, not the one you walk in with.
- Mistakes get debugged in public.
- The space for not-knowing is shared.
- Innovation comes from being willing to be wrong out loud.

These lessons are the actual conditions of creative work. The team becomes able to do what the leader has been doing.

## The Permission Effect

The deeper mechanism: the leader's public struggle permissions the team to struggle publicly too. Without the permission, the team carries the same suppressed struggle the leader used to carry. The compounding compartmentalization is what produces the burned-out, brittle, surprise-prone organization.

Ant on the daisy chain effect:

"It cracked me open, and what flowed out of that was a deeper state of vulnerability that I think then allowed the team to show up and go deeper in their own vulnerability. And I think getting a couple levels down into the work in community execs that are working 12, 15, 16, 17-hour days together. It meant that everybody could go -- it was sort of a self-fulfilling prophecy, like, I'm going deeper, so you're going deeper, you're going deeper, so I'm going deeper. And the deeper we go, the more benefit we get from the work." -- Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

The principle works in both directions: the leader's depth permissions the team's depth, the team's depth permissions the leader's depth. The container deepens through the mutual permissioning.

## The Leadership Style Shift

Ant describes the felt-sense shift in his leadership style after the first big public-failure moment:

"It triggered a different kind of, I guess, leadership style that was a little bit more like, I'm gonna go jump into that pit over there, guys. I'm pretty sure it's filled with snakes. I'm pretty sure I'm gonna get my ass handed to me. It's gonna be at least funny, possibly dangerous, but if I live, come with me." -- Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

The contrast Brett offers is sharp: the alternative is walking through a snake-filled jungle and saying "don't worry guys, there's no snakes here, just come along."

"One I can do really well, one I can do authentically, one is authentically me. The other one, like, nobody believed there were no snakes in the fucking jungle. They never believed that. They became burnt out by it. Some of them left." -- Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

The "no snakes" leader produces burnout and attrition. The team can feel the gap between the projected confidence and the felt reality. The gap is exhausting to be around. The leader who names the snakes -- and walks toward them -- produces the opposite. The team trusts that they are seeing what the leader sees. They follow.

## What This Requires of the Leader

Public failure as a leadership stance requires:

- The leader has done enough internal work to not need to look competent. The need for the appearance of competence will sabotage every attempt to be publicly fallible.
- The leader trusts the team enough to let them see the process. This is not blind trust. It is earned over time.
- The leader has a relationship with their own embarrassment that allows them to keep working through it. The first instinct is to escape the visible struggle. The leader has to stay.
- The leader holds the line between modeling struggle and dumping struggle. The first is generative. The second is a different problem -- the leader using the team as emotional support, which the team will read and resent.

The distinction between modeling and dumping is the same as the distinction between [[vulnerability]] and [[vulnerability-as-control]]. The modeling version is being seen in the work. The dumping version is using the team to manage one's own emotional load.

## When the Leader Cannot Yet Do This

Not every leader is yet in the condition to do this well. The reps come from doing it badly first. The first attempts will be over-performed, under-performed, or awkward. The team will sometimes feel the leader is performing vulnerability rather than embodying it. This is the development arc.

The path is to do it imperfectly and keep going. The leader who waits until they are good at public failure to start practicing public failure will never start. See [[connection-over-perfection]].

## The Connection to Authenticity

This principle is one expression of the broader [[improvement-vs-authenticity]] distinction. The performed-competence leader is improving their image. The publicly-failing leader is being themselves. The improvement frame produces a leader who looks better than they are. The authenticity frame produces a leader who is what they are, which is what the team can actually work with.

See [[consciousness-is-the-product]]: whatever state the leader is in becomes part of what the team produces. A leader running on the performance of competence produces a team running on the performance of competence. A leader running on the public reality of their actual process produces a team that can work with their actual process.

## Tone

The principle is delivered without heroism. The leader is not being brave by failing in public. They are being functional. The dramatization of public failure as a virtue is itself a form of performance and should be avoided. The point is not that the failure is admirable. The point is that letting it be visible teaches something the hidden version cannot teach.
