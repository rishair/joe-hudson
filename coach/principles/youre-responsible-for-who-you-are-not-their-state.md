# You're Responsible for Who You Are, Not for Their State

## The Principle

The line that distinguishes care from caretaking sits exactly here: you are responsible for how you show up. You are not responsible for the other person's state. These are two different operations performed by two different people, and conflating them produces the entire caretaking dysfunction.

"I'm responsible for who I am with my children and with my wife, but I'm not responsible for who they are. So I'm responsible for, am I showing up loving? I'm responsible for, do I own my triggers? I'm responsible for if I apologize, if I've not been the person that I wanna be, that's what I'm responsible for. But what I'm not responsible for is getting my wife in a good mood. I can't, it's like, it's not within my realm of control." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The principle gives the clean operational split. It names what you are responsible for in concrete terms: how you are showing up, owning your triggers, apologizing when you have not been who you want to be. And it names what you are not responsible for: producing a state in the other person.

## What's Inside the Line of Your Responsibility

Your responsibilities are all operations performed by you, on yourself:

- **How you are showing up.** Loving, present, available, honest. Are you bringing what you want to bring? This is yours.
- **Owning your triggers.** When something hits you, you recognize it as your trigger rather than locating the cause in them. The recognition is yours.
- **Apologizing when you have missed the mark.** When you have not been who you want to be, the repair is yours to make.
- **Naming your own wants and saying what you want.** This is the active form of taking care of you (and of the relationship, since named wants are easier for others to navigate than hidden ones).

All four operations have the same structural property: you can do them. They are inside your reach. They are operations on your own self, conduct, and stance.

## What's Outside the Line of Your Responsibility

What you are not responsible for has a different structural property — it is not inside your reach:

- **Their mood.** Your wife in a good mood. Your child in a happy state. Your friend not anxious. None of these are operations you can perform.
- **Their happiness.** A state. Not produceable from outside.
- **Their healing.** Their work to do, in their time.
- **Their decisions.** Their authority, their consequences.

"My job is to be with her sadness. It is not to take responsibility for her sadness." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The parental example is clean. When the daughter is sad, the father has a job — to be with the sadness. He does not have the job of fixing the sadness or making it not be there. Being with it is inside his reach. Eliminating it is not.

## Why the Conflation Is Catastrophic

When the two operations are conflated, the operation that is inside your reach (showing up loving) gets confused with the operation that is not (producing their state). The person attempts the impossible task and fails, because the task was never possible. The failure is then interpreted as personal inadequacy.

Two specific costs result:

1. **The actual responsibility is neglected.** The person trying to produce the partner's good mood is not focused on whether they themselves are showing up loving. They are focused on whether their attempts to fix the mood are working. The conflation diverts attention from the operation that is inside the reach to the one that is not.

2. **The other person is structurally disempowered.** Taking responsibility for their state tells them that their state is not theirs to be with. This is the deeper damage. See [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] for the full treatment. The "I'll take care of your sadness" stance trains the other person out of their own relationship with their sadness.

"And if I do it, then I'm deeply disempowering her. I'm saying, 'Oh, you need help to be happy, so I am gonna help you.' You know, there's, besides hubris in it, it's also just like deeply disempowering." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The hubris is structural — assuming you have a control you do not have. The disempowerment is relational — communicating to the other that they do not have a control they do have. Both costs accrue every time the line is crossed.

## The Operational Test

When you find yourself trying to produce a state in another person, the test is: is this an operation I can actually perform? The answer is almost always no. Their state is theirs to produce. The operations you can perform are on your own showing up.

The redirect: come back to the inside-the-line operations. Are you showing up loving? Are you owning your triggers? Have you apologized for where you missed the mark? Have you named what you want? Those are the questions. The answer to "is the other person happy" is not a question you can answer through your action; it is a question whose answer is whatever they produce.

## The Wanting Version of the Principle

The principle has a specific application in the want domain. Owning your wants is part of "showing up" — it is how the relationship gets the information it needs to run. Producing the partner's satisfaction with your wants is not your job. The partner's response is theirs.

"Part of caring is to tell somebody what you want." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

This connects the responsibility-line to the want-line directly. Naming what you want is inside your responsibility (it is information about you that the other person needs). Their response to your want is outside your responsibility (it is their information about them).

The conflation in the want domain looks like: "I shouldn't say what I want because she might not like it." The principle redirects: saying what you want is your job; how she responds is hers. Both jobs are performed cleanly when the line is held.

## The Parental Application

The parental version of the principle has a specific clarity:

"My responsibility is who I am, how I'm showing up. My responsibility is not making somebody else happier, taking responsibility for them." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The parent's job, when the child is sad, is to be with the child in the sadness. Not to remove the sadness. The parent who tries to remove the sadness is doing the same disempowerment to the child that the spouse does to the spouse — communicating that the child's emotional experience is not theirs to be with.

The healthy parental version: "I see that you're sad. I'm right here." See [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] for the contrast with "Don't be sad" and the related teaching on the 4 keys move.

## What This Is Not

- Not "don't care about your partner or child." Care is required. The principle is about responsibility, which is different from care. Care without responsibility-for-their-state is the integrated form.
- Not "don't be moved by their state." You can be present with their state, attuned to it, available to it, while still not being responsible for producing or fixing it.
- Not "you're not part of the system." You are. Your conduct affects their state. But your conduct affects their state; your conduct does not produce their state. The agency for what they do with what arrives is theirs.
- Not "you can't help." You can offer help. They can take it or not. The offer is yours; the taking is theirs. The clean offer is the offer made without the implicit demand that they accept.

## The Hubris Frame

A useful diagnostic: the moment you start taking responsibility for someone else's state, notice the hubris underneath it. The stance assumes a level of control over their interior that does not exist for anyone, including them. The "I can make her happy" stance is structurally arrogant, regardless of how kind it looks on the surface. Recognizing the arrogance is sometimes what allows the stance to drop.

The corollary: when you are at the receiving end of someone trying to take responsibility for your state, notice that what looks like care is also a small form of disempowerment. The caretaker is telling you they can do something with your state that you cannot do for yourself. The implicit message is unflattering, even when it is wrapped in concern.
