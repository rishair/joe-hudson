# Don't Search for the Truth, Search for Their Truth

## The Principle

In a fight, the move that ends it is not searching for THE truth (who is right, what really happened, whose interpretation is correct). The move that ends it is searching for THEIR truth (what is true for them, how they are seeing it, what is real in their experience).

The truth-of-the-fight is a category error. There is no single truth waiting to be discovered that, once found, will dissolve the conflict. The very act of searching for it keeps the conflict alive, because the search itself is the structure both partners are caught inside.

"If you're looking for like what the truth is instead of trying to understand the other person's truth and what their reality is, then you miss each other. There's no connection there. Nobody has ever said, once I find the truth of this relationship, then the relationship will be happy. That's not how it works." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The corollary is the operational shift: stop adjudicating. Start understanding. The first dissolves the fight by removing its engine. The second creates the connection that the adjudication was supposed to produce but never could.

## The Two Versions of the Same Question

The fight runs on the wrong question. Most people, inside a fight, are asking some version of "what really happened?" The form is variable:

- "What did you actually say?"
- "What did you mean by that?"
- "Did you or did you not do X?"
- "Is what they're saying about me fair?"
- "Am I being unreasonable here, or are they?"

All of these are courtroom questions. They presume that there is a single state-of-affairs that, once correctly described, will resolve the dispute. The dispute is conceived as an evidence problem.

The principle says: this is the wrong question. The right question is "what is true for them?" — not as a debate move, not as a strategy, but as a sincere orientation. Their truth is not the truth-of-the-fight. It is the truth of how they experience this. That is a different kind of object entirely, and it is the only object actually available to be seen.

## The Courtroom Metaphor

Joe and Tara developed an explicit phrase for the trap:

"We used to have this phrase and it was like, hey, let's not go into the courtroom. Courtroom was to figure out who had done what and what the truth was." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The courtroom is the specific shape the fight takes when both partners have agreed (without saying so) to treat the disagreement as a thing to be adjudicated. In the courtroom:

- Each partner is the prosecutor of the other and the defender of themselves
- Evidence is presented (what was said, what was meant, what the other person did three weeks ago)
- A verdict is sought (who was wrong)
- The verdict is supposed to bring resolution
- The resolution never arrives because the structure cannot produce it

The naming move — "let's not go into the courtroom" — is the in-the-moment exit. It does not require either partner to surrender their position. It only requires both to opt out of the structure that the positions were being defended inside.

This becomes a candidate [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight|fight agreement]]: agree, when calm, that "let's not go into the courtroom" is a phrase either of you can use mid-fight to remind both of you of the agreement to step out of the adjudicating frame.

## The Internal Recognition That Has to Happen First

The verbal move ("let's not go into the courtroom") is downstream of an internal recognition. Without the internal recognition, the verbal move is technique and the courtroom continues.

The recognition Joe names from his own marriage:

"This is true for her. Like this is how she sees the world. It doesn't have to be mine. It's hers." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

Three components:

1. **"This is true for her."** Her experience is real. Not "her interpretation of what happened is correct" — but "her experience of what happened is what she actually has."
2. **"It doesn't have to be mine."** I do not have to adopt her view in order for her view to be real for her. Her view's reality does not impose itself on mine.
3. **"It's hers."** The view belongs to her. My job, if I want to see her, is to see what she has. Not to debate it, not to verify it, not to refute it.

The recognition lives inside the listener's body. When it is actually present, the listening that follows lands as receiving. When it is not present, the listening that follows lands as preparation-for-rebuttal, no matter what words come out.

## Why "Finding the Truth" Cannot End the Fight

The principle includes a structural argument for why the courtroom never works:

"Nobody has ever said, once I find the truth of this relationship, then the relationship will be happy. That's not how it works. But everybody says, I would love to be seen. I would love to be understood. I would love to be connected with as I am." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The argument: the desire underneath the fight is not for the truth to be established. The desire is to be seen. The truth-search is a proxy that the wanting-to-be-seen has been routed through, because the culture has made the truth-search a legible activity and the wanting-to-be-seen has been shamed. The proxy fails because it is not the actual thing.

Even if the truth were found, the seeing would not have happened. The fight would not end. A different fight would start. The seeing-need would still be unmet, and it would now have less material to work with because both partners would feel they had been adjudicated.

See [[the-desire-to-be-seen]] and [[being-heard]] for the underlying need that the truth-search misroutes.

## The Mechanism That Makes It Work

The move ends fights for a structurally simple reason: when one person stops searching for the truth and starts searching for the other's truth, the other person almost immediately wants to do the same.

"As soon as someone feels seen, almost immediately they want to see the other person. And so the fight just almost instantaneously resolves." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

This is the asymmetric exit that breaks the symmetric loop. The [[defense-feels-like-attack-feedback-loop]] keeps running because both partners are defending. When one partner stops defending and starts seeing, the other partner's defense loses its trigger. The body that was bracing for attack starts to settle. The seeing that the other partner was trying to extract through the fight is now arriving without the fight needing to continue.

One person can do this unilaterally. The fight does not require mutual agreement to end. It requires one person to step out of the courtroom. The other person, deprived of the input that was generating the loop, follows.

## What This Principle Is Not

This is not "agree with everything they say." Searching for their truth does not require you to adopt their truth as yours. Joe is explicit:

"Finding the other person's truth, understanding it, it doesn't mean you compromise yourself. It doesn't mean that you change your actions. It's just saying, I understand you. I get how that's the case." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Relationship Fights in 10 Minutes

The seeing is independent of the agreement. You can fully see how something is true for them without that seeing dictating your action.

This is not "the truth doesn't matter." In domains where there is a stake of fact (a math problem, an engineering decision, a legal question), the truth matters and can be searched for. The principle applies specifically to the psychological/relational domain where the fight is about meaning, experience, and interpretation. See [[truth-is-perspective-with-context]] for the boundary the principle sits inside.

This is not a technique. The internal recognition has to be actually present. If the searching-for-their-truth is just a wrapper around the searching-for-the-truth-that-will-vindicate-me, the partner will feel the inversion and the fight will continue. The principle works as an orientation, not a procedure.

This is not "never name what you saw or experienced." The partners can still describe what they experienced. The principle is not about silence; it is about what register the descriptions are delivered in. "This is what I experienced" delivered as my-truth-to-be-seen is one register. "This is what actually happened" delivered as the-truth-you-must-accept is another. The first is compatible with the principle. The second is the courtroom.

## What It Produces

When held, the principle produces:

- **Fight duration collapses.** What used to take hours or days takes minutes. See [[joe-and-tara-la-show-fight]] for the canonical illustration of the day-to-ten-minutes compression.
- **Both partners feel seen.** The first who steps out of the courtroom sees the second. The second, feeling seen, almost always sees back.
- **The disagreement may still exist.** Stepping out of the courtroom does not require dissolving the difference of view. It only requires removing the structure that was forcing one view to win.
- **The relationship moves from adjudication to co-creation.** "How do we want to see reality together? How do we want to be together? Even if you continue to see it this way and I continue to see it that way." This is the question that becomes available once the courtroom is empty.

## What This Principle Lives Alongside

This principle and [[truth-is-perspective-with-context]] are companions, not duplicates.

- **Truth-is-perspective-with-context** is the meta-claim: in the psychological domain, there is no single truth; perspectives are partial and context-dependent. This is the *epistemological* argument.
- **Don't-find-the-truth-find-their-truth** is the operational pivot: in a fight, stop searching for THE truth and start searching for THEIR truth. This is the *behavioral* application.

The first is the reason the second works. The second is what the first looks like in live action.

They can be used independently. Some people get traction faster on the epistemological argument (the Bay Bridge analogy lands and the truth-search loosens). Others get traction faster on the operational pivot (the courtroom metaphor lands and the orientation shifts).

## The Operational Recipe

When inside a fight, the move has three steps that compress to seconds when practiced:

1. **Notice the courtroom.** The internal sign: you are trying to figure out what's true, what really happened, whether they have grounds for what they are saying. The trying is the courtroom.
2. **Make the internal recognition.** "This is what is real for them. It is theirs. It does not have to be mine." Hold it long enough for the body to register.
3. **Listen with an open heart.** Not to gather evidence. Not to formulate a rebuttal. To receive what is true for them as theirs.

The verbal moves that come after — apology, reflection, naming — only land if these three have already happened. The verbal moves are downstream of the orientation. Reverse the order and the moves become technique.

- The recognition, after a fight that took the whole day, that you spent it trying to establish what really happened
- The discovery that you can stop trying to figure out whether your partner is being fair and just see what they are experiencing

- The realization that "let's not go into the courtroom" is a thing you can say to yourself
- The moment of seeing that the question "but who was actually right?" is the question that is keeping the fight alive
