# Real Giving Requires Desire

## The Principle

Giving from guilt, shame, or obligation does not produce the happiness that giving is supposed to produce. The structure of "I am giving this because I have to, because they need it, because I should" creates disempowerment and fear in the giver, not the felt-sense expansion that real giving produces.

The kind of giving that actually generates happiness for the giver requires that the giver authentically wants to give. The desire to give is what makes giving give back. Without the desire, the act of giving becomes another form of self-abandonment, another way of leaving the self to manage someone else's situation.

"There are some folks out there where giving is from guilt or obligation or because they feel like they have to. And I don't see that kind of giving actually make people happy. I see that kind of giving actually create a disempowerment and a fear. It's because unless the giving actually is something that — so to me, it's like to give is useful because it creates the desire to give. But the real happiness comes from giving through the desire to give, not just giving, that there's an actual intent." -- Joe Hudson, Happiness, the New Way

## What Makes Giving Real

Real giving has a specific signature:

- **It comes from desire.** The giver wants to give. The giving expresses something the giver is, rather than something the giver is supposed to do.
- **It is self-expansion, not self-sacrifice.** The giver grows through the giving. The giving is part of who the giver is becoming, not a subtraction from who the giver is.
- **It is self-determined.** No one forced it. No guilt manufactured it. No social pressure produced it. The giver could just as easily have not given, and is giving because they want to.
- **It is specific to the giver.** The giver brings what is authentically theirs — their unique capacity, their particular love, their actual gift. Not a generic donation of effort.

Stephanie Harrison, in the same conversation, names this from her own framing:

"Service is not self-sacrifice. It's self-expansion." -- Stephanie Harrison, Happiness, the New Way

Joe agrees with this directly. Self-sacrifice giving — giving that costs the giver — is not the giving that produces happiness. Self-expansion giving — giving that grows the giver — is.

## The Common Failure Mode

The common failure mode is the over-giver. The person who has been taught that being good means giving, who experiences any need they could meet as an obligation to meet it, who feels guilty when they consider stopping. This person gives constantly and is exhausted, resentful, and quietly disempowered by the giving.

The conventional advice to this person — "give less" or "set better boundaries" — addresses the symptom but not the cause. The cause is that the giving is being done from obligation rather than from desire. The same person, giving from a desire-based stance, could give a lot and not be depleted. The same person, giving from obligation, could give very little and still be depleted by what they did give.

The leverage is in the orientation of the giving, not the quantity.

See [[obligation-cuts-off-love]] and [[responsibility-stops-love]] for the specific mechanisms by which obligation in close relationships interrupts the love that obligation is supposed to be expressing.

## The Other Side: Receiving

The complement to this principle is that the same people who give from obligation usually cannot receive. The self-reliant, hyper-independent over-giver has spent years training themselves to be the one who gives. When someone tries to give to them, they deflect, decline, or feel uncomfortable. The capacity to receive has atrophied.

"A lot of people need more help learning how to receive, especially those self-reliant folks. Actually learning how to receive is more of a challenge for them than giving." -- Joe Hudson, Happiness, the New Way

The implication: working on giving-from-desire often requires working on receiving as well. The two are paired. A person who is unable to receive will continue to give from obligation because they cannot let in the giving that would refill them. The work on receiving is the work that makes desire-based giving sustainable.

This connects to the broader pattern of [[self-reliance-pushes-people-away]] — the person who cannot receive is also the person who pushes love away while complaining that love is not arriving.

Stephanie names the structural extension: in a system organized around interconnectedness, refusing to receive denies the giver the chance to give. If giving is what produces happiness, then refusing to be helped is denying someone else the chance to be happy. The two-way flow is required for either side to work.

"If you actually — if you want to serve others, if you want to be somebody who's a giver, you have to let them help you at the same time." -- Stephanie Harrison, Happiness, the New Way

## Giving Generates the Desire to Give (The Bootstrap Subtlety)

Joe makes a subtle distinction. He notes that "to give is useful because it creates the desire to give." There is a bootstrap process in which the act of giving can generate the desire to give that then makes the next round of giving real. Without any giving, the desire may not develop. The first round of giving may have to happen on faith, before the desire is fully there.

The danger is that this bootstrap observation can be misread as a license for obligation-giving. "I should give even when I don't want to, because the giving will create the desire to give." This is the trap. The bootstrap works when the giving is offered as an experiment, with attention to whether desire is actually emerging. The bootstrap fails when the giving is forced, because the forcing itself prevents the desire from emerging.

The diagnostic: does the giving leave the giver feeling expanded or depleted? If expanded, the bootstrap is working — the next round of giving will be more wanted. If depleted, the bootstrap is not working — the giving is becoming obligation, and more giving will deepen the obligation rather than develop the desire.

## The Connection to Wants

This principle is one instance of the broader [[wants-over-shoulds]] principle, specifically applied to the domain of service and giving. The same logic operates here: a should-driven action produces depletion; a want-driven action produces aliveness. Giving is not exempt from this logic. The same act, performed from should or from want, produces different effects in the giver.

This is why coaching on burnout often does not work if it only addresses how much the person is giving. The actual variable is whether the giving is wanted. Reducing the quantity of obligated giving may help, but the underlying problem remains: the person is doing things they do not want to do. The deeper work is to find the giving that is actually wanted and to expand that.

See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] for the coaching move.

## Why Obligation-Giving Creates Fear

Joe specifically names that obligation-giving creates fear, not just disempowerment. The fear comes from the implicit logic of obligation: the giver is doing this because something bad will happen if they do not. The fear of that bad outcome is encoded in every obligation-giving act. The giver is acting from fear rather than from love.

This is structurally identical to [[shame-drives-behavior]]: the action is being driven by what the person is trying to avoid, not by what they want. Fear-driven action does not produce expansion. It produces more fear, because the fear is what kept the action going.

By contrast, desire-driven giving has no fear in it. The giver gives because they want to. If they did not give, nothing bad would happen, because the giving was not addressing a feared outcome. The absence of the feared outcome in the structure is part of what makes the giving feel expansive rather than depleting.

## In Coaching

When someone presents with "I should give more" or "I feel guilty about not helping," the move is not to validate the guilt and encourage more giving. The move is to question the structure of the obligation:

- What makes you think you should be giving this?
- What would it be like to ask: do I want to give this?
- What would it be like to give something else that you actually want to give?
- What are you not giving — to yourself, to others — by giving this thing you do not want to give?

The reframe is from "I am not giving enough" to "I am not giving from desire." The work that follows is locating the desire — the actual giving the person wants to do — and reorganizing around that. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] for the broader move.

For the self-reliant over-giver, the coaching often has to address the receiving side first. The work to develop the capacity to receive precedes the work to give from desire, because the desire to give cannot be sustained when the giver is not being refilled.

## Tone

The principle is delivered without judgment of people who are currently giving from obligation. The pattern is usually installed in childhood, often as a survival strategy in a family where the giver was rewarded for caretaking. The point is not that the person should feel bad for doing it; the point is that it does not produce what they think it is producing. The work is to find the giving that actually works for them, not to add another should about how to give correctly.
