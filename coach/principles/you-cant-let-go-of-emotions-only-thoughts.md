# You Can't Let Go of an Emotion. You Can Let Go of a Thought.

## The Principle

The instruction "just let it go" makes a category error when applied to emotions. You cannot let go of an emotion. The emotion you are having is the emotion you are having. You do not get a choice about what emotion is currently flowing through your system. What you can let go of is a thought -- and when the thought changes, the emotion that was being held in place by that thought may then move on its own.

The reverse is also true. When you fully feel an emotion, the thought that was attached to it often changes on its own. Both directions exist. Neither involves letting go of the emotion directly.

"You don't get a choice on what emotions you're feeling. So to just say let go is like, the emotional experience is the emotional experience you have. What you might let go of actually is a thought pattern... that could hold an emotional experience in place for a long period of time. So there's definitely thoughts that you can let go of that you can see through, and then the emotional experience will change." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Fluidity

## Emotion Is Like Breath

Emotion is constantly happening. There is no moment in which a person is not having an emotional experience, in the same way that there is no waking moment in which they are not breathing. Asking someone to let go of their emotion is asking them to let go of something that is always currently occurring.

"Emotion is like breath. You're always having an experience of one. And you don't get to choose what it is. So you can't let go of an emotion. You can let go of a thought that changes your emotional experience." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Fluidity

The same principle stated in the public-facing video, as the setup for the four-tactical-ways framework. The framing here serves a different audience -- the beginner who has not yet accepted that there is always something to feel:

"There is no time when you're awake when a feeling isn't happening. You might not note it, you might not register it, but there is always an emotional sensation happening in your body at all times." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The "you might not note it" qualifier addresses the most common objection: "I don't feel anything." The principle does not say the person is currently in touch with their feeling; it says the feeling is currently happening regardless of whether they have access to it. This reframe is what makes the framework's first tactical way ("recognize the emotion when it is happening") sensible. The emotion does not need to be summoned. It needs to be detected.

The implication: any practice or instruction framed as "let go of the anger / fear / sadness" is mislocating the work. The work is upstream (a thought that holds the emotion in place) or downstream (allowing the emotion to move). It is not on the emotion itself.

## Both Directions: Thought Change and Emotion Movement

The two directions of the mechanism are symmetric:

**Direction 1: Thought changes, emotion shifts.** A person has been holding an emotion in place because of a story: "they hurt me and therefore they are the bad one and I am the good one." When the story softens -- not because of suppression but because of seeing through it -- the emotion that the story was holding in place often moves on its own.

**Direction 2: Emotion moves, thought changes.** A person fully feels the underlying anger, sadness, or fear. Once the emotion has moved, what was previously a strongly-held belief becomes clearly seen as a thought. Clarity arrives that was not available before.

"I would say that you can fully feel an emotion and then the thought changes. That happens all the time. I see people finally release anger and all of a sudden they can see clearly so many things, particularly around the things that they were clear about or the things that they hadn't drawn boundaries around." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Fluidity

Both routes work. They are not in competition. They are two doors into the same loosening of the thought-emotion knot. See [[stronger-story-stuckier-emotion]] for the underlying coupling.

## Why "Just Let It Go" Fails

The phrase as commonly used implies that the listener can, by an act of will, choose not to have the emotion they are currently having. This is not how the emotional system works. The attempts to comply produce one of two failure modes:

- **Suppression dressed as release.** The person clamps the emotion physically, calls the clamping "letting go," and walks away with the emotion still held -- now with an additional layer of self-deception that it has been handled. This is repression with a spiritual vocabulary.
- **Frustration and self-blame.** The person tries to let go, finds they cannot, and concludes there is something wrong with them. The work they think they are failing at is impossible. Their inability is data about the instruction, not about them.

The correct instruction is more precise. If a thought is holding the emotion in place, work the thought (see it through, question it, drop it). If the emotion needs to move, allow it to move. Do not ask the person to perform an operation directly on the emotion that the system does not support.

## The Relationship to Letting Go in General

Not all "letting go" instructions are wrong. The principle is specific: emotions cannot be let go of. Letting go can be a real practice when applied to:

- A thought pattern (see above)
- An attachment to an outcome (see [[surrender-the-outcome-of-a-decision]])
- A gripping of experience (see [[letting-go-vs-doing-nothing]])
- A held image or identity

The category error is to extend "let go" from these to "let go of the sadness I am currently feeling." The first set is operational; the last is not.

## In Coaching

When a client says "I just need to let this go," the response is to slow down and ask what they mean. The phrase often hides whichever of the following the person is actually trying to do:

- Suppress an emotion they do not want to feel (in which case, see [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]])
- Stop a recurring thought they cannot stop (in which case, work the thought, possibly through [[voice-in-the-head]] or [[inquiry-on-limiting-beliefs]])
- Reach a posture of acceptance about a circumstance they cannot change (in which case, the work is on the resistance, not on the circumstance)

The "let it go" framing collapses all three. Disentangling them is part of what makes the coaching useful.

When the client believes they have already let an emotion go but the same situation keeps producing the same reaction, the diagnosis is usually that the emotion was suppressed rather than felt. The "let it go" was a successful management move and not a movement of the emotion. The emotion is still there, waiting to be felt. See [[unwelcomed-emotions-return-until-felt]].

## Tone

Plain and clarifying. The point is to dissolve a confusion that produces real harm in people's practice. Joe states it directly: "yeah, no, you didn't" when Brett offers the common framing of "I just let go of my sadness." The correction is short and unmistakable.
