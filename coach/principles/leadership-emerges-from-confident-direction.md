# Leadership Emerges From Confident Direction at a Reasonable Clip

## The Principle

In any group, leadership in a given moment goes to whoever moves with confidence and clarity at a reasonable pace. The leader of the moment is not necessarily the person in the formal authority position. It can be anyone in the group, and it rotates situationally. The recognition of this fact, by a person in the formal authority position, is one of the most generative shifts available to them.

The principle comes from a baboon-tracking study Joe uses repeatedly. See [[baboon-leadership-study]] for the full story of the example and its specific framings. The summary:

"They put all these tracking collars on baboons, and I love this metaphor. And every morning the baboons would come down off the tree or whatever, and they would decide which way to go. And so they were trying to figure out how the fuck they decided which way to go. Who led them? Was it the alpha male? And it turned out it was anybody. Sometimes the least dominant female would lead the way, and sometimes the most dominant male would lead the way. And so they would sit there, and what would happen is like some people would start going this way and the troop wouldn't listen, and then somebody would go this way, the troop wouldn't listen, then somebody would go. And what they literally found was the chimp, uh, the baboon that did it with the most directness, because that's what you could track. They were just like, yep, this is what we're doing. And they went at a reasonable clip and they moved with confidence. Then the whole troop followed." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## The Three Components That Actually Produce the Following

The baboon study isolates three components, observable enough to be tracked with collars:

1. **Directness.** The leader is not equivocating. The signal is unambiguous: this is what we are doing.
2. **A reasonable clip.** Not a frantic dash. Not a wandering stroll. A pace that the group can match and that does not seem panicked or uncertain.
3. **Confidence in the movement.** Not certainty about being right. Confidence about taking the step. The body of the leader is committed to the direction.

When these three are present in any group member, the group follows. When they are absent in the formal authority, the formal authority does not lead — even though they hold the role.

## Why This Matters for Authority Issues

The principle resolves a common confusion in the [[authority-issues]] frame. People with authority issues often imagine that "the leader" is fixed and external — the boss, the CEO, the parent, the teacher. The baboon observation reveals that leadership is a function the group is constantly assigning to whoever meets the criteria in the moment. The role and the function are separable.

This has two implications:

- **For the person in the formal authority role:** the role does not guarantee the function. You can hold the title and not be the leader the group is following. The group is following someone — sometimes a different team member in each meeting, sometimes a particular subordinate by default, sometimes a culture or a process. Recognizing this is the entry point to actually inhabiting leadership rather than just occupying the position.
- **For the person who feels powerless under authority:** leadership is not the property of the role. You can lead from any position by meeting the three criteria. This is also why [[compliment-people-to-become-the-authority]] works: the complimenter is operating from a confident, direct, paced position that the room registers as leadership regardless of their formal title.

## The Wisdom Is in the Group, Not in One Person

The deeper observation underneath the baboon study: the group itself contains wisdom that no single member holds. The alpha baboon is still the alpha baboon — they still hold the structural position. But the directional wisdom of where to go on a given morning is distributed. Sometimes the least dominant female has the read. The group's intelligence is realized through whoever has the read this time, expressed through the three components.

"That alpha male baboon is still the alpha male baboon, but the leadership came as much from him as anybody else in the group. And you see that happen all the time. A CEO wants something to be this way, but it's not that way. And they're like, why isn't it that way? Because the culture is doing something different. And to recognize that is really — it's quite lovely when you really see, like, oh, if you harness that instead of fight it as a leader, it's a really amazing — it's magical. There's a wisdom greater than any one person's in a group." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The mistake the over-asserting leader makes is to fight this wisdom — to insist the group go where the leader wants regardless of where the group's read is pointing. The mistake the under-asserting leader makes is to mistake the existence of distributed wisdom for the absence of any need to provide structural leadership. Neither is the move. The move is to provide the structural container (see [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]]) while also recognizing that the moment-to-moment leadership of direction belongs to whoever has the clearest read.

## What This Looks Like Operationally

In a meeting, the person who synthesizes the discussion and names the next step with directness and confidence becomes the leader of the moment — regardless of their title. Watch any healthy team and the leadership rotates through whoever has the clearest read on the topic at hand.

In a crisis, the person who can act with confidence at a reasonable pace becomes the leader, even if they have no formal authority. The formal authority who freezes or panics loses the function even though they hold the title.

In a long-term project, the structural authority holds the container, but the moment-to-moment direction is held by whoever has the clearest read on the present step. The team is constantly assigning the leadership function to whoever earns it through directness and clarity.

## The Inverse: Why Hesitation Costs the Role

The principle also explains why a formal leader who hesitates loses the room. Hesitation violates all three criteria. The signal is unclear (no directness). The pace breaks (no reasonable clip — either frantic or stalled). The confidence is gone (no commitment to the direction).

The team, sensing the violation, looks elsewhere for whoever does meet the criteria. Sometimes this is constructive — another team member steps in. Often it is destructive — the team starts vying for the authority that has been vacated. See [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]].

This is why "fake it till you make it" has a real referent for leaders. The fakeability is not about pretending to know. It is about being willing to move with directness, clarity, and confidence in a direction even while internally uncertain. The internal uncertainty is fine. The external hesitation is not.

## The Counterintuitive Implication for Leaders

The leader who treats their team as a source of leadership rather than as followers becomes more of a leader, not less. The capacity to follow the team's read when the team has the read is itself a form of leadership. The team registers this capacity as a higher-order directness: the leader is direct about who has the read in any given moment, and the group reorganizes around the actual signal rather than around the role.

This is the [[lead-and-follow]] distinction expressed at the level of moment-to-moment direction. The leader leads by going first in the direction the read points; the leader follows by recognizing when the read is in someone else's hands and reorganizing around them.

## What This Is Not

**Not abdication.** The principle does not say "anyone leads, so the formal leader does not need to." The formal leader still holds the structural container. The principle is about who holds the moment-to-moment directional read, not about who holds the role.

**Not consensus-seeking.** The principle does not say "let the group decide everything." The baboons do not vote. One baboon moves with directness; the troop follows. The mechanism is emergent, not procedural.

**Not flat hierarchy as ideology.** The alpha baboon is still the alpha baboon. The roles are real. What the principle adds is that the function of leadership in any given moment can be distributed across the roles.
