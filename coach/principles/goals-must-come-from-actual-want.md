# Goals Must Come From Actual Want Somewhere in the System

## The Principle

For a goal to function -- to organize action, to produce energy, to actually be pursued -- a real want must be located somewhere in the system that is going to pursue it. The want has to belong to a person or to a subsystem of a person. If no one actually has the want, the goal does not run, regardless of how clearly it has been written, how prominently it has been displayed, or how many metrics have been attached to it.

"The alignment is really, really important. The want is also really, really important, and the want has to come from somewhere. ... When the want isn't there, when everybody's doing it for somebody else, I'm doing it for the board, and then I'm doing it for the CEO, and there's nobody who actually has that want, it's very difficult." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The "very difficult" is structural. The work cannot be powered by a want that does not exist. The system can be forced through some quantity of work by willpower, threat, or duty, but the work degrades, the people leak out of it, and eventually the goal fails because the energy it requires was never there to spend.

## Why This Is a Principle and Not a Preference

A want is the fuel. A goal is a structure designed to direct the fuel. A goal with no fuel is a structure with nothing moving through it. The structure may look identical to a goal that has fuel -- it has a number, a deadline, a metric, an owner on a slide -- but it does not function.

The conventional approach treats goal-setting as a writing exercise: write the right SMART goal and the goal will operate. The principle here says no. Goal-setting is fuel-locating. Until the want is located, the writing is decorative.

This is why the principle is structural. It is not a recommendation that goals work better with wants. It is the recognition that goals are formal structures that route the energy of wants, and without the underlying want they do nothing.

## The Two Failure Modes

Two specific ways the want-less goal fails:

**Organizational want-less goal.** The board wants the CEO to want it; the CEO has installed it as a goal because the board wants it; the executives are pursuing it because the CEO has installed it; the operators are executing because the executives told them to. At no point in the chain does an actual want exist. The goal is real on paper; the work is real on calendars; the energy is missing. The org grinds. People leave. The goal misses.

**Personal want-less goal.** The voice in the head ("the board" inside the person) issues a goal because some authority (cultural script, parental voice, peer comparison) says it should. The rest of the system -- body, heart, underlying self -- does not endorse. The person attempts the goal. Willpower carries it for a while. Willpower runs out. The goal abandons or is pursued joylessly. The person concludes they are bad at goals. The actual problem was that no part of them ever wanted it.

The second failure mode is what [[the-inner-board]] describes. Both failure modes share the same structure: the goal exists, the want does not, the goal does not function.

## What "Somewhere in the System" Means

The want does not have to come from a particular place. It just has to come from somewhere real.

- In a company, it can come bottom-up from the teams (they want to build the thing) or top-down from the CEO (they want to build the thing and others align around it). Both work. See [[bottom-up-vs-top-down-goals]].
- In a person, it can come from the head, from the heart, from the body, from the underlying self. What matters is that some subsystem actually has the pull, not where the pull is located.

The principle disqualifies one specific source: pure obligation. A goal that exists only because someone-else's want exists, without any subsystem in the executing person actually wanting it, will not function. The chain of "I'm doing it for X who is doing it for Y" eventually has to terminate in a real want, or the whole structure collapses.

## The Diagnostic for a Want-less Goal

The diagnostic is simple. Ask the system: who actually wants this?

In a company: name the goal. Ask each person at the table whether they want this, in the felt-pull sense rather than the "yes I support the strategy" sense. If everyone reports that they support the goal because someone else wants it, the goal is want-less. Find the real want or revise the goal.

In a person: name the goal. Check the body. Did anything light up when the goal was named? Did the breath move? Did energy come into the room? If the system is silent and only the head's voice is saying yes, the goal is want-less. See [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]] for the move that surfaces this.

The diagnostic does not require deep introspection or extended inquiry. The body is usually direct about whether it has a want or not. The work is just listening to the body's signal instead of trusting the head's report.

## What to Do With a Want-less Goal

Three options once the want-less status is recognized:

1. **Locate the real want underneath.** Sometimes the want-less goal is covering a real want that has not been named. The $10M revenue goal turns out to be a wanting-to-want; underneath is the want for security, which is real. The goal gets revised to reflect the actual want. See [[need-behind-the-want]] and [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

2. **Find the want elsewhere in the system.** Sometimes the goal is the right goal but the want is misallocated. The CEO doesn't want the new product line; the head of product does. Move the ownership of the goal to where the want lives. The product head sets it; the CEO supports rather than mandates.

3. **Drop the goal.** Sometimes the goal is purely borrowed and there is no real want anywhere in the system. The right move is to release the goal. The relief of dropping a borrowed want is often substantial. The person can then notice what they actually want, which is usually different from what they were trying to make themselves want.

## The "Just Because Voice Says So" Trap

The most common form of want-less goal in personal life: the voice in the head says "this is what your goal should be," and the person installs it as a goal without checking whether any other part of them agrees.

"Just because the voice in the head said, this is what your goal should be, doesn't mean it's what we want." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The voice in the head has the authority of long association and constant presence, but the voice is one input. It can issue goals that the rest of the system would never have set. The system is not obligated to comply just because the voice said so. The principle here is the structural reason why: goals from the voice alone are want-less unless the rest of the system also wants what the voice issued. Usually it doesn't, and the goal will fail or grind. See [[the-inner-board]].

## What This Implies for Coaching

When a person brings a goal into coaching, the move is not to take the goal at face value. The first work is locating the want. Where is it? In what part of the system? Is it real?

If the want is real and located, the work moves to clearing obstacles, refining the goal, finding the right metrics. The goal can be pursued.

If the want is not real or not located, the work is the opposite. Investigate where the goal came from. Surface the source. Find out what is actually wanted. Sometimes the actual want is a smaller, more immediate, more felt version of the goal that was presented. Sometimes the actual want is something completely different. Sometimes there is no actual want and the recognition of that fact is the entire work.

The trap to avoid: helping the person pursue a want-less goal more efficiently. This is the most common failure of conventional coaching. The coach takes the stated goal as the work and helps the person execute on it. The execution proceeds. The collapse is delayed. The collapse arrives. The coaching is judged by the collapse, but the original error was at the goal-locating layer.

## What This Implies for Self-Directed Work

For someone setting their own goals, the practice that follows from this principle:

1. Before writing the goal, ask what is actually wanted. Not what should be wanted, not what would be impressive to want, not what a strategy would suggest wanting. What is actually wanted, located in the body and the heart.

2. If the answer is unclear, do not generate a goal. Stay in the unclarity. The actual want will surface if given room. The pressure to generate the goal before the want is clear is itself one of the things that produces want-less goals.

3. Once a real want is located, the goal that emerges is usually different from what the head would have written. It may be smaller, larger, in a different domain, or shaped differently. Trust the want's version, not the head's version.

4. When the want shifts (and wants shift over time), let the goal shift too. The goal is downstream of the want. The want is the authority. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]].

## What This Principle Is Not

- It is not a recommendation against ambition. Real wants can be ambitious. The principle is about source, not size.
- It is not "follow your bliss" without discipline. The want once located still requires discipline to execute. The principle is about the relationship between the want and the goal, not about whether work is required.
- It is not "if you don't immediately want it, don't do it." Sometimes the want is buried under fear, shame, or other blocks. Investigating the blocks can surface the want. The principle is about ensuring a real want is there before the goal is operationalized, not about refusing work that requires emotional investigation first.
- It is not a license for refusing collective goals. Sometimes the want is located in the team, the family, or the organization rather than in the individual. Participation in a collective want is real participation. The principle is satisfied as long as the want is real somewhere.
