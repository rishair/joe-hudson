# You Create the Support System, the Support System Creates You

## The Principle

You create a [[support-system]] so that it can create you. The doing flows one way at first — the design, the curation, the agreements — and then the doing flows the other way for the rest of your life. The person who exists ten years into a real support system is not the person who built it. The system has been making them the whole time.

This is the structural answer to the I-have-to-do-it-all-myself orientation that drives so much striving. The relief of a real support system is not just that you get help with this or that problem. It is that the basic premise — that you are the sole agent of your becoming — turns out to have been false.

"I'm gonna create my support group only for my support group to create me. And that second part, there's a relief because what I noticed most people are doing in the world and what I used to do is like, what do I have to do to fix it? What do I have to do? How am I? What do I buy? I, I, I, I, I. And then when there's this support group and you look up and you're still thinking I, I, I, I, but you're looking around and you go, Yeah, except it never would have happened without them." -- Joe Hudson, How To Build A Great Support System

## What the Relief Actually Is

The shift is not from "I do everything" to "they do everything for me." The shift is from "I am the sole author" to "we are co-creating each other and my life."

The I-I-I orientation puts the entire burden of becoming on a single agent. Every move is a move I must make. Every direction is a direction I must choose. Every change is a change I must engineer. The orientation is exhausting in a way that is easy to mistake for normal, because it has been the default since adolescence for most people in achievement cultures.

A real support system disconfirms the orientation. The person looks back over a decade and sees that the marriage was held by the couples group, the leadership growth was held by the men's group, the work was held by the community of practice, the resilience after losses was held by friends who knew how to be in grief. Without those holdings, none of the development that the person attributes to themselves would have happened.

This is not false modesty. It is structural accuracy. "It never would have happened without them."

## Humility and Gratitude as the Felt Form

When the structural truth is felt rather than just acknowledged, two things show up:

**Humility.** Not the performance of humility. The actual recognition that the work was not yours alone, which dissolves the inflation that comes from thinking it was. See [[humility]] for the broader concept.

**Gratitude.** Not the gratitude practice. The actual sense of having been carried by people who did not have to carry you, in ways you can never fully repay, who are still carrying you now.

"There's a humility and a gratitude, which is nice. But what's even nicer about it is to realize that like, it's not fucking about you. You know, like you're part of something that's greater and it's a gift and it's a blessing." -- Joe Hudson

The humility and gratitude are not virtuous additions to a life that was otherwise the same. They are the felt form of a different relationship to one's own becoming. The shift is from "I did this" to "this happened, and I was part of it." The relief is structural.

## You Might Be Dead Without Yours

Joe states the implication starkly. The support systems are not a nice-to-have. For many people, they are the difference between the life they have and a much smaller, more brittle, more lonely, possibly shorter life.

"I guarantee you I wouldn't be where I am in my life without my support systems, right? So you might be dead without yours. Yeah, yeah, definitely, right? I know that like, it was the men's group and the tussle that happened in the men's group. And it's that couples group that I'm still a part of. And it's the community of practice that allowed me to have the life that I had, that allowed this like level of happiness that I get to exist in." -- Joe Hudson

The claim is testable in your own life. The exercise: imagine your life over the last decade without each of your support systems. What happens? Some of them you can remove and your life is roughly the same. Some of them you cannot. The ones you cannot are the ones that have been creating you.

## What This Implies About Identity

There is an identity move that becomes possible only when the principle is felt: stop being the person who does it all yourself. The shift is hard not because the work of letting others in is hard (though it is). It is hard because the identity of being the one who does it all is sticky. It has been the source of pride, of self-worth, of legibility in achievement cultures. Letting it go requires letting go of something the system has been organized around.

The trade is favorable. What is released is exhausting and isolating. What is received is a relationship to one's own life as something that is happening with other people rather than to one. See [[identity-shapes-who-is-around-you]] for the related principle about how identity organizes the social field. The hyper-independent identity organizes a social field of solo strivers and admiring distant figures. A different identity organizes a social field of peers in mutual support.

The island experiment Joe runs (see [[island-experiment]]) makes the felt case for what the conditions do over time. Imagine being on the island with twelve people who unconditionally love you for ten years. Who would you be at the end? The same question applied here: imagine being in three or four real support systems for ten years. Who would you be at the end? The answer is the felt evidence for this principle.

## The Average-of-Five Frame

Brett offers the popular frame about becoming the average of the five people closest to you, with the self-compassionate implication: whatever you might be proud of about yourself is partly a product of your environment, not a singular achievement of yours.

"In the way that we are a product of our environment, you know, it's been said that you become something like the average of the 5 people you're closest to. And there's something self-compassionate in that recognition where, whatever it is that I could have an ego about, about myself is actually a product of my environment as much as it is a product of any intention of myself." -- Brett Kistler

The compassion runs in both directions. The qualities you wish you had are not entirely within you to create through willpower; you can also work on the environment, which will produce them more reliably than effort. And the qualities you have are not entirely within you to take credit for; they were produced by the people around you. The relief is the same: you do not have to be doing it all.

This is the practical encouragement implicit in the principle. If you want to become something, you can design the support systems that will produce it, rather than trying to engineer it from the inside alone. The design work is real work. The thing the design produces over time is not something you produced. It is something the design produced.

## What This Implies for Coaching

When someone presents with [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]], the work is often not to encourage them to stop being so hard on themselves. It is to surface the structural error in the I-I-I premise. Ask what would change if they were genuinely in a support system that was creating them. Most people in this pattern have never imagined this clearly, and the imagining itself starts to dissolve the premise.

The principle is also useful as the closing frame on a long arc of coaching about leadership, marriage, or any high-effort domain. The person who has worked hard on becoming someone often arrives at a relief when they see that the becoming was not all on them; the people, structures, and containers in their life were creating them all along. The recognition is not deflating. It is liberating.

## Tone

Quiet, grateful. This is one of the warmer principles in the methodology. Joe states it from his own felt experience of being created by his support systems. The tone is not promotional; it is the tone of someone naming a structural feature of a good life that most people miss.
