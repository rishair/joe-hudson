# The Two Things a CEO Must Get Right

## The Principle

There are only two things a CEO has to get right to build a successful company: build great relationships, and make great decisions. Everything else is downstream of these two. If both are operating well, the company will succeed. If either is failing, no amount of work on anything else will close the gap.

"You need to get two things right in building a company, which is building great relationships and making great decisions. If you get those two things right, you will be successful." -- Johannes Langgraf quoting Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

The principle compresses the CEO job into two leverage variables. The work of leadership is to develop the capacity for both at the same time, because they are not independent -- the quality of one constrains the quality of the other.

## Why These Two

**Decisions are the CEO's actual output.** A CEO does not produce code, write copy, or close deals (or if they do, they are bottlenecking the company). The CEO produces decisions. The quality of those decisions, over a portfolio, determines what the company becomes. See [[decisions-are-emotional]] and [[good-decision-is-how-you-make-it-not-the-outcome]] for what decision quality actually means -- it is not measured by outcomes but by how the decision is made.

**Relationships are the substrate for everything.** Every decision touches people: the team that has to execute it, the board that has to fund it, the customers that have to adopt it, the partners that have to commit to it. The quality of those relationships determines whether a good decision can actually be executed. A great decision communicated through bad relationships becomes a worse decision in practice than a mediocre decision communicated through great relationships.

The two variables also interact. Great relationships produce the data that enables great decisions (people will only bring you the truth if they trust you). Great decisions, made repeatedly, build the trust that produces great relationships. Each compounds the other.

## How These Two Interact

The interaction is what makes this principle compressive rather than reductive. The two things are not separate competencies. They are facets of the same underlying capacity.

**The trust-data loop.** A CEO with great relationships gets more data. People bring them the truth, including the difficult truth. The CEO with poor relationships gets the truth filtered, the bad news softened, the dissent invisible. The decisions made with full data are structurally better than the decisions made with filtered data. See [[trust-as-foundation-of-team-dysfunction]].

**The execution-quality loop.** A decision is only as good as its execution. A great decision executed by a misaligned team produces mediocre results. The relationships are what produce the alignment that makes the execution match the decision. See [[alignment-beats-effort]] for the operational form of this.

**The boardroom loop.** Joe's anchor story for the principle (the M&A board call -- see [[principled-board-decision]]) illustrates both variables operating at once. The mentor had a great relationship with the board, which gave the room the trust to receive the principled decision without panic. He made the principled decision. The combination produced an outcome a year later that vindicated both.

**The conflict-tolerance loop.** Great relationships permit the conflict that produces great decisions. See [[trust-requires-conflict]]. A team that cannot disagree is a team that cannot make decisions worth executing. The trust capacity and the decision capacity scale together.

## What This Principle Excludes (And Why That Matters)

The principle does not say "be a great strategist." It does not say "be a great operator." It does not say "be a great fundraiser" or "be a great salesperson" or "be a great recruiter." All of those are legitimate executive activities, but they are downstream of the two leverage variables. A CEO who is mediocre at strategy but great at relationships will recruit a great strategist and trust them. A CEO who is great at strategy but bad at relationships will fail to recruit anyone better than themselves and will bottleneck the company at their own strategic ceiling.

The compression also means that a CEO who is exhausted from trying to be excellent at twelve things can simplify. Drop the things that are not these two. Hire the things that are not these two. Focus the remaining attention on the two variables that compound.

## What Builds These Two Capacities

The same internal work serves both. The CEO who has done their own work -- on shame, on defensiveness, on the patterns inherited from family of origin -- becomes better at relationships and better at decisions simultaneously. The work is not two separate developmental tracks; it is one.

Specifically:

**Embracing intensity.** See [[embrace-intensity]]. The CEO willing to feel the difficult emotions makes better decisions (because the decisions are no longer organized around what the CEO does not want to feel) and builds better relationships (because the CEO can stay present in difficult conversations without collapse).

**Living by principles.** See [[principles-free-you]]. Pre-handled principles dissolve 80% of the deciding (see [[80-percent-of-decisions-are-automatic-via-principles]]), which frees attention for the genuinely novel decisions. The principles also produce predictability that becomes the substrate for trust in the relationships.

**Connection as a primary commitment.** See [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]]. The CEO who starts in connection, stays in connection, and ends in connection is producing the relationship capacity continuously. The connection orientation also tends to produce better decisions because it surfaces more data.

**Working on self-awareness.** See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]]. The patterns the CEO has not seen in themselves will show up in both their decisions and their relationships. The self-awareness work is the upstream lever on both downstream variables.

## When Joe Reaches for This Principle

- A founder asks "what do I most need to develop as a CEO?"
- A leader is overwhelmed by the number of things they think they need to be excellent at
- A CEO is asking what to delegate and what to keep
- A leader who is great at one of the two variables is asking why their company is still struggling
- A leader is being told they need to develop ten different competencies and is collapsing under the load
- A founder is asking what separates successful CEOs from unsuccessful ones over the long arc

## How This Principle Lands With Founders

The principle tends to land easily because most founders intuit it but have not had it stated. The work that follows is the developmental work on the two variables. Most founders enter the role strong on one variable and weak on the other.

The technical founder is often great at decisions (logical, data-driven, willing to make hard calls) and weak at relationships (introverted, conflict-avoidant, uncomfortable with emotional content). The work is to develop the relationship capacity without losing the decision capacity. See [[do-the-known-skip-the-unknown]] for the structural temptation to skip the unknown side.

The relational founder is often great at relationships (charismatic, trust-building, energy-generating) and weak at decisions (avoidant of hard calls, indecisive, prone to deciding what is popular rather than what is right). The work is to develop the decision capacity without losing the relationship capacity.

The founder who develops both becomes the kind of CEO whose company can navigate hard transitions (the AI pivot, the layoff, the architectural rewrite) without rupturing. The founder who develops only one becomes the kind of CEO whose company is structurally bounded by what the missing variable cannot produce.

## What This Principle Does Not Say

It does not say the two are sufficient. There are companies that fail despite great relationships and great decisions because the market shifted, the timing was wrong, the capital was insufficient, or chance simply did not favor them. The principle is about necessity, not sufficiency. The two variables are what the CEO can actually control. The principle says: control these, and the rest is exogenous.

It also does not say the two are equally weighted in every moment. There are seasons when one demands more attention than the other. A company in a major decision cycle needs more decision capacity. A company in a major integration or growth cycle needs more relationship capacity. The principle says: develop both, deploy them as the situation demands.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle as a compression, not as a slogan. The compression is the message. The relief in the room when a founder hears it is the marker that it has landed. Most founders are carrying a list of fifteen competencies they think they need; the principle puts that list down and replaces it with two.
