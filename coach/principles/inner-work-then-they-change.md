# Do Your Own Work, Stop Trying to Fix Them, Then They Change

## The Principle

There is a specific three-step sequence by which the people around you actually change. You cannot reverse the order, you cannot skip a step, and you cannot do step three directly. The sequence:

1. **Do the work on yourself.** Whatever pattern you see in them, find its analogue in you and work with that.
2. **Stop trying to fix them.** Hold the pattern you've worked with internally. Stop offering them tools, stop pointing out their patterns, stop trying to facilitate their transformation.
3. **They change.** Not always, not on your timeline, not in the way you would have prescribed — but the shift you wanted often shows up, sometimes faster than seemed possible.

The order is structural. Step one without step two means you are still leveraging the work as a way to be right about them. Step two without step one means you have simply checked out emotionally. Both steps together create the conditions in which step three can happen. Step three cannot be made to happen; it can only be allowed to happen by getting out of the way.

"You did the work on yourself, then you stopped trying to fix other people, and then they changed. I mean, like, I'm not focusing on other people, I'm gonna focus on myself, and then the people change. Like, if people are going to change, it's after you've stopped trying to change them and you focus on yourself." — Joe Hudson, What Changed After Master Class?

## The Failure Modes Each Step Prevents

**Without step one (do your own work):** You go directly to "I won't try to change them." This looks like detachment, but it is usually withdrawal or contempt. The other person can feel that you have given up on them. The relational field is colder than it was when you were actively trying to fix them. They do not change because the underlying signal you are sending is "you are hopeless."

**Without step two (stop trying to fix them):** You do the inner work and now you have the tools, the language, the framework. You become a missionary. You explain to them what their pattern is, what they should do about it, what worked for you. See [[dont-evangelize-the-work]]. The other person experiences this as pressure, as condescension, as a relationship in which you are the one who knows and they are the one who needs to catch up. They do not change. They often regress.

**Skipping to step three (managing their change directly):** You try to engineer their transformation. You schedule them for the workshop, you give them the book, you arrange the therapist. This produces the predictable rebellion. The person now associates the change with your agenda for them, which makes the change something to resist on principle.

The structural feature of the sequence is that the only thing you can actually do is step one. Step two is a release, not an action. Step three is a consequence, not an action. The sequence works because it correctly identifies the one place where your effort is useful and removes effort from the places where effort would backfire.

## The Mechanism: Why They Change When You Stop

Several mechanisms operate simultaneously:

**The pattern was held in the relationship, not just in them.** What you saw as "their" pattern was usually a pattern of the system you were both in. Your move in the system held their move in place. When your move changes, theirs has nothing to interlock with. The pattern collapses without anyone consciously dismantling it. See [[every-trigger-with-your-kid-is-projection]] for the parenting version of this.

**You stop sending the "you need to be different" signal.** Even when unspoken, the signal is felt. The person you are trying to change can sense the disapproval underneath the offered tools. When you stop trying, the disapproval lifts. The person can now breathe. From the regulated state they can sometimes access the change you were trying to install but failing to install.

**You become a model of what you were trying to teach.** Instead of teaching them the work, you embody the work. Embodiment is communicable in a way teaching is not. They watch you handle the same trigger differently and the watching does what the teaching could not. See [[dont-evangelize-the-work]] on "show up with more unconditional love" as the alternative to evangelism.

**Roles redistribute.** When you drop a role you have been playing (the worried one, the responsible one, the one who manages everyone's feelings), the role becomes available for others to step into in their own way. See [[dropping-roles-makes-room]].

## Eva's Two-Year Account

The principle was articulated by Eva, a MasterClass alumna two years out, describing what happened with her family:

"I struggled with that so much because I got to a point where maybe I could see patterns in other people and having the strength to bring it back to me instead of trying to fix others and bringing it back to me, my experience, my everything. And enduring the suffering and the pain in myself and seeing others not, not having what I have thanks to the Masterclass. And still then I can see how, especially in the past few months, things unraveled in a way in my family that I could not be happier. And I was able to show up in a way that would've been impossible for me like a couple of years ago." — Eva, What Changed After Master Class?

Three features of her account are diagnostic:

**The seeing came first.** She could see the patterns in others. This is usually the first move people make: gain the framework, recognize it in the people around them. This is also where most people stop and become missionaries.

**The hard middle: bringing it back to herself.** The phrase "having the strength to bring it back to me instead of trying to fix others" is the second step. Note the word "strength." It takes more discipline to redirect the work inward than to redirect it outward. The outward direction is easier because it lets the other person be the problem. The inward direction asks you to find your version of the same pattern.

**The suffering of waiting.** She names "enduring the suffering and the pain in myself and seeing others not having what I have." This is the relational cost of step two. You hold the work inside while watching the people you love continue to suffer in the old way. The temptation to violate step two is constant. The discipline is to keep holding.

**The eventual unraveling.** "Things unraveled in a way in my family that I could not be happier." Years of inner work, the discipline of not fixing, and then the family shifts. The unraveling is hers in the sense that it would not have happened without her work. It is not hers in the sense that she did not do it to them.

## What This Is Not

**This is not "if you do the work, they will eventually change."** Sometimes they do not. Sometimes the inner work produces a different outcome: you become clearer about whether to stay in the relationship at all, you become less reactive to their unchanging behavior, you find that the relationship can continue with the pattern intact because you no longer need it to be different. The principle does not promise that all relationships shift. It describes the only path by which they sometimes do.

**This is not "withhold love until you have done the work."** The work and the loving are happening simultaneously. The principle does not gate loving them behind some milestone of inner work completion. The point is that the loving is not the same as the fixing. You can love them while doing the work and while not fixing them.

**This is not a manipulation strategy.** If you adopt the sequence as a strategy to get them to change, you are violating step two from the inside. The sequence works because step one is genuinely about you, not because step one is secretly the most clever way to engineer step three. The moment you do the work as a strategy, the work stops doing the work. See [[loving-to-transform]] for the related anti-pattern.

**This is not bypassing the actual problem.** If there is a behavioral pattern in the other person that is harming you or others, the principle does not say to wait silently for years. It says: do your own work on your relationship to the harm, get clear about what you want and what your boundary is, communicate cleanly, and stop trying to change their internal state. The sequence does not preclude boundaries or honest conversations. It precludes the project of internal-state-modification.

## The Order of Operations

A useful way to hold the sequence:

- Step one is something you do for yourself, regardless of whether they ever change.
- Step two is the release of the project of changing them, also done for yourself, because the project itself is a constriction.
- Step three is what happens when steps one and two have been in place long enough for the field to reorganize.

If you find yourself anxious about step three not happening, you are back in step two violation. The anxiety is itself the fix-them-energy returning under a more sophisticated disguise. The instruction at that point is to notice the anxiety and return to step one.

## What Someone Experiences

For someone in this principle, the typical experience is:

**Early:** Frustration. You can see the pattern clearly. They cannot see it. You want them to see it. You try various ways to point at it. None work. They get defensive or distant.

**Middle:** Grief. You realize you cannot make them see it. You begin to do your own work on it. You notice the analogue in yourself. You start to feel the pain of having focused on them instead of yourself for so long. There is sometimes grief about all the years of unsuccessful fixing.

**Discipline phase:** Hard. You catch yourself reaching for the old fix-them moves. The reaches get more subtle: a leading question, a recommendation, a gentle observation. You learn to notice them and not act on them. You stay with your own work. You let them be where they are.

**Letting-be:** Spacious. The relationship feels different even if their pattern has not changed. You are not pushing. They are not defending. The field is less charged.

**Sometimes (not always):** Their shift. They start doing the thing you stopped trying to make them do. Sometimes weeks later. Sometimes years later. Sometimes not at all. When it happens, it has the quality of being theirs, not yours. They did it. You held the space.

## Tone

Joe's affirmation of this in the episode is direct and warm. "That was beautifully said." He repeats the structure back to mark it: "You did the work on yourself, then you stopped trying to fix other people, and then they changed." The tone is recognition, not invention — he is naming a pattern Eva already lived and articulated. The teaching is in the recognition that this is the order.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is frustrated that the person they love is not changing.
- When someone is using the work as a tool to fix their partner, parent, or child.
- When someone has done years of inner work and is wondering why the family system has not shifted.
- When someone is grieving the years they spent trying to fix others before learning to do their own work.
- When someone is afraid that if they stop trying to fix the other person, the other person will get worse.
- When a parent is trying to install transformation in a child or teen.
- When someone in a long marriage is wondering whether the marriage can change.
