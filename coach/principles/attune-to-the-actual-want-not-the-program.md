# Attune to the Actual Want, Not the Program You Run

## The Principle

When someone you love comes to you with something, you almost always run a program. The program is whatever response your conditioning installed for situations of that shape. "Someone comes with a problem" → solve the problem. "Someone says they're hungry" → cook the food. "Someone is upset" → make them feel better. The program is automatic, fast, and feels like care. It is also frequently not what the person actually wants.

Attunement is the alternative. Attunement is asking — explicitly or by reading — what the specific person in front of you, in this specific moment, actually wants from you. The attuned response and the programmed response may sometimes overlap. Often they do not. The cost of running the program when attunement was called for is most of what corrodes long relationships.

"We have a program that we run when somebody comes to us with X, Y, or Z, but it's not particularly always what the person who's coming to us wants, generally. So many fights happen because somebody is basically thinking that somebody wants something when they don't want it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

## The Structure of the Problem

The program is invisible to the person running it. The fixer cannot see that the fix-impulse is a program, because from inside, the impulse feels like the obvious correct response. The "obvious" is what programming feels like from the inside.

The receiver feels the program operating but often cannot name what is happening. They feel managed, unheard, talked-down-to, undermined. They get cranky. The crankiness is the data the program-runner needs but the program-runner usually reads the crankiness as the receiver being difficult.

Over years, the receiver's accumulated experience of being responded-to by program rather than attended-to as a person becomes the structural resentment that flattens the relationship. Each individual instance was small; the cumulative effect is large.

"So many of my fights with Tara were because I was just not attuned to the thing that she wanted in that moment. And so, and vice versa." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

## The Gender-Coded Version

A particularly common configuration Joe names: husbands defaulting to solve/help, wives wanting to be heard. The husband responds to the wife's problem-description with solutions. The wife feels belittled and disempowered — the implicit message is "you cannot handle this, let me handle it for you." She gets resentful. The husband, who experiences himself as actively trying to help, cannot understand why his help produces resentment. He does more of what he thinks help looks like. The cycle deepens.

"I see this in such a broad way. Where so many times I see, particularly husbands, but not always, particularly husbands are constantly trying to help their wife solve a problem, which makes the wife feel undermined and belittled, like, I don't need your fucking help. And then they get resentful and this whole thing like gets — and then like years of this goes on and then the wife feels like belittled and disempowered and the husband is like, 'Wait, I do everything I can to make her happy and she's still not happy.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

The same configuration runs the other way too. The man asking for help and getting empathy when he wanted advice. The man saying "I just want a little fucking help" and being told "yeah, it's really hard when your boss does that." The mismatch is symmetric. The gender-default is statistical, not absolute.

"It works both ways." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

The point of naming the gendered pattern is not to assign blame to husbands. It is to make the program visible by giving it a recognizable shape. Once a husband can see "I run the solve-it program," he can see when he is running it and choose attunement instead. The same recognition is available to anyone running the empathy program when advice was wanted.

## The Operational Move

The principle becomes practical through [[ask-what-they-want-before-responding]]. The three-option ask — "do you want me to listen, do you want some questions, or do you want advice?" — is the practical interrupt of the program. Asking the question installs the attunement where the program would have run.

The principle is broader than the specific move. The principle is "attune," and the three-option ask is one operational form of attunement. Other forms exist:

- Reading the body of the person who came to you. Are they leaning toward you (wanting contact) or pulling away (wanting space)?
- Reading the energy of what they're saying. Are they processing (want listening) or genuinely stuck (might want help)?
- Reading the relational moment. Has the day been hard? They probably want presence first, problem-solving later.
- Asking implicitly through your first response. A soft "tell me more" allows the speaker to keep going if they want to. A direct "have you tried X?" closes the listening. The first response sets the channel.

The ask is the most explicit form. It is the right move when reading is failing or when the relationship has not yet built shared attunement. The reading is the move once enough attunement has accumulated that the three-option ask becomes unnecessary.

## What Drops When the Principle Lands

Two specific configurations drop when this principle is taken in:

**The responsibility-for-their-happiness configuration.** The program-runner is usually running the program because they have implicitly accepted responsibility for the speaker's emotional outcome. They have to solve, fix, cook, advise — because if they don't, the speaker will stay unhappy and that will be their fault. Attunement reveals that the speaker is not asking for the outcome to be fixed. They are asking for one specific thing. Delivering that one specific thing is the entire job. The responsibility for the outcome lands back where it belongs — with the speaker.

"I had this feeling of like, I'm responsible for her happiness. And now it's like, oh no, like I can just be with her. It's just that easy." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

See [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] for the full principle and [[responsibility-stops-love]] for the felt mechanism.

**The "I have to do so much" exhaustion.** Running the program is expensive. The fixer is generating solutions, the cook is preparing food, the advisor is curating advice. Attunement is cheaper. Most of the time the request is "be with me" or "listen" or "ask me a question," none of which require much production. The fixer who switches to attunement discovers their daily energy expenditure on the relationship drops dramatically while the relationship gets better. The expended energy was being wasted on outputs the partner did not want.

"I used to think I had to do so much... Now it's like, oh no, like I can just be with her. It's just that easy." -- Joe Hudson, How To Attune To Your Partner's Needs

## When Someone Experiences This

- When you feel exhausted from the work of taking care of someone you love, and the relationship is not getting better.
- When you keep being told you "didn't listen" even though you offered great advice.
- When your partner gets cranky in conversations and you cannot tell why.
- When you notice you have a default response to your partner's distress and you cannot remember the last time you checked whether the default was what they wanted.
- When you feel resentful that all your help is not appreciated.
- When you feel managed by your partner's responses to your distress, even though they seem to be trying to be supportive.

The corresponding internal recognition: "Oh, I am not actually responding to this person. I am running my conditioning."
