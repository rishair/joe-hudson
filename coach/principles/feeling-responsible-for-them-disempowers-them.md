# Feeling Responsible For Them Disempowers Them

## The Principle

When you feel responsible for another person — even from a position of legitimate leadership over them — you are disempowering them. The over-responsibility move assumes that they are not responsible for their own experience. The assumption is the disempowerment. The felt sense of "I let them down" is doing the work, regardless of whether it produces any external behavior change. The internal frame of responsibility-for-them strips them of authorship over their own life.

The principle is counterintuitive for the conscientious leader, who has been taught that good leadership means taking responsibility. The principle reveals that there are two kinds of responsibility-taking, and the wrong one corrodes the people being led.

## The Joe Realization

Joe describes the moment he learned this from a person he was leading:

"I remember this moment of I'm sitting with somebody and I'm like, oh, I feel like I've let you down. And they were like, that's super disempowering. I was like, what? Like, I'm this leader, you paid the money. He's like, what? Like, you don't think that I'm responsible for my own experience?" -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The recognition landed: by saying "I let you down," Joe was implicitly claiming that he was the responsible party for the other person's experience — and therefore that they were not the responsible party. The framing positioned them as the passive recipient of his leadership rather than as the author of their own engagement with it. The framing felt like generosity to Joe; it felt like erasure to the person.

"It just hit me like a ton of bricks. Holy, you know, it was, oh, right, I am disempowering this person. I am assuming that they are not responsible for themselves." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## The Two Things This Distinguishes

The principle distinguishes two moves that look similar:

**Move A: Taking responsibility for your own actions and choices in the relationship.** "I made this decision and it had this consequence and I am accountable for that decision." This is leadership.

**Move B: Taking responsibility for the other person's experience of those actions.** "I am responsible for how you felt about what I did. I let you down." This is over-responsibility, and it disempowers the person whose experience is being taken over.

The leader can do A without doing B. The trap is the assumption that A requires B — that taking responsibility for your actions means taking responsibility for the felt-impact on the other person. The actions are yours. The felt-impact is theirs. The mix-up of the two is the disempowerment.

## Why It Disempowers

The mechanism is structural. When you feel responsible for another person's experience, you are operating from the assumption that their experience is yours to manage. The assumption produces specific consequences:

1. **You make choices designed to manage their experience rather than to act from your truth.** This makes you a worse leader — your actions are now optimized for someone else's emotional regulation rather than for the actual leadership move.

2. **They get treated as a managed entity rather than as an agent.** They sense this. The relationship becomes managed-by-you rather than co-created. Their experience of themselves as authoring their own life weakens in the relationship.

3. **They get the implicit message that their experience is not theirs.** If your goal is to manage their experience, their experience is the object — not the subject. They are not the one having it; you are the one shaping it. The framing strips them of being the author of their own experience.

4. **They lose the developmental opportunity.** Their experience is theirs to work with. The disappointment in you, the frustration with the situation, the grief about what didn't happen — all of these are theirs to feel and make meaning from. Your taking responsibility for it preempts the work that would have grown them.

## The Inverse Failure: Shirking Responsibility

The principle has a near-neighbor that needs to be distinguished. The opposite of over-responsibility is not "I'm not responsible at all" — which is its own failure mode. The over-responsible leader who recognizes the disempowerment can swing to the under-responsible position, claiming they are not responsible for anything. This is just a different form of not owning the leadership.

"There's a different thing that is correct — 'I'm not responsible' — which is just not owning the authority in a different way." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The accurate position is the middle: I am responsible for what I am responsible for (my actions, my choices, my conduct in the relationship). I am not responsible for what is theirs to be responsible for (their experience, their reactions, their meaning-making). The distinction is fine-grained and lives in the relationship moment-by-moment.

## The Connection To The Coaching Frame

The principle is foundational for coaches. The coach who feels responsible for the client's progress is disempowering the client. The coach who recognizes the work as the client's is preserving the client's authorship. See [[the-work-is-yours-not-mine]] for the coaching expression of the principle. The same applies to therapists, teachers, parents, and any other leadership role where the developmental work of the other person is at stake.

The texture of the correctly-bounded coaching presence: the coach is fully present, fully cares, fully brings what they bring — and does not feel responsible for whether the client uses it. The client's use of it is the client's responsibility. The coach's job is to bring it; the client's job is to take it. Holding the line cleanly is what makes the coaching available rather than oppressive.

## The Connection To Love And Responsibility

The principle has a relational dimension. Taking responsibility FOR someone (in the over-responsibility sense) tends to kill the love. See [[responsibility-stops-love]] for the mechanism. The over-responsibility move pulls the relating into a manager-and-managed dynamic, which is incompatible with the felt sense of love between two agents. The disempowerment is the same; the loss of love is the relational symptom.

This is why the leadership move and the love move converge here. The leader who is loving from the open heart is also the leader who is not feeling responsible for the other person's experience. The two are different angles on the same stance.

## The Connection To Blame

The principle clarifies the other side of [[blame-is-the-abandonment-of-leadership]]. Blame transfers leadership from the blamer to the blamed. Over-responsibility transfers leadership from the led to the leader. Both are leadership-transfers. Both produce disempowerment for one party — but in opposite directions. Blame disempowers the blamer. Over-responsibility disempowers the led.

The clean stance is neither: hold your own leadership, recognize their leadership over their experience. The transfer (in either direction) is the failure.

## The Mun Variant: Responsibility For The Team You Lead

The same principle applies in a more diffuse way to large-group leadership. The leader who feels responsible for all the people they are leading is, by the same mechanism, disempowering them. The people on the team made their own choices to join, to engage, to follow. They are the authors of their own participation. The leader's over-responsibility for them strips that authorship.

"There's another side of it, which is like, I am now responsible for all these people that I'm leading. Yeah, no, they made a choice. They led themselves to you too, and they led themselves to follow you." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The reframe: the team led themselves to follow you. Their following is itself a leadership move (see [[first-follower-creates-the-movement]]). Treating the team as your responsibility strips their agency in the act of having followed. The accurate framing: they are leading-by-following, you are leading-by-leading, and the leadership is shared.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The conscientious leader who is exhausted from feeling responsible for everyone
- The coach or therapist who is feeling the weight of the client's progress
- The parent who feels responsible for their adult child's life
- The partner who feels responsible for the other partner's happiness
- The recognition that "I let you down" has been doing the work of disempowering the other person
- The relief of seeing that letting go of the over-responsibility is not the same as not caring
