# Shift the Environment, Not the Actor

## The Principle

When a system produces a recurrent pattern that the participants want to change, the standard reflex is to identify the actor inside the pattern as the problem and attack the actor. The wolf is eating the deer; shoot the wolf. The conflict-prone employee is causing problems; fire the employee. The child is throwing tantrums; punish the child. The anger keeps coming up; suppress the anger.

The principle inverts this reflex. The actor is not the problem. The environment that allows the actor is the leverage point. Shift the environment, and the actor's behavior changes — sometimes by the actor changing, sometimes by the actor no longer being there, sometimes by the same actor functioning differently in the changed conditions. Either way, the work is at the environment level, not the actor level.

"The opportunity is in shifting the environment. It's not in attacking the wolf and saying the wolf is bad." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

## The Underlying Frame

The principle rests on a recognition: living systems organize themselves around conditions. An actor — a wolf, a person, an emotion, a behavior — is the system's response to the conditions, not the cause of them. The actor would not exist without the conditions. The actor's behavior is what the conditions invite.

"All of them are holding wisdom. The wolf holds wisdom, the deer holds wisdom, the forest fire holds wisdom. The environment allows for wolves, the environment allows for deer." -- Joe Hudson, The Forest Fire Theory of Personal Growth

This is not a denial of agency or responsibility. The wolf chose to eat the deer. The employee chose to start the conflict. The child chose to throw the tantrum. The principle is about where leverage lives, not about where blame lives. The actor's choice is real and the actor is accountable. The conditions that made that choice the likely one are where the change actually happens.

## What the Principle Replaces

It replaces the blame-and-eliminate reflex. The blame-and-eliminate logic: there is a bad actor; the system is bad because of the actor; remove the actor; the system gets good. This logic feels intuitive and is reliably wrong in interconnected systems. Removing the targeted actor often crashes the system in worse ways than the actor was producing. See [[kaibab-deer-mountain-lion-balance]] for the canonical illustration.

It also replaces the more sophisticated version of the same error: behavior-modification at the actor level without conditions-change. The actor is told to stop the behavior. The actor tries. The conditions that produced the behavior continue. The behavior comes back, or a worse version takes its place, or the actor breaks down. The intervention failed because it was aimed at the actor instead of the conditions.

## Applications

**Personal psychology.** A person hates their anger and tries to eliminate it. The anger was a response to the environment they had built (no boundaries, chronic over-extension, suppressed wants). Without addressing the environment, the anger keeps returning or gets replaced with something worse (depression, illness, breakdown). Shift the environment — restore the boundaries, reduce the over-extension, surface the wants — and the anger's role in the system changes. The anger may persist, but its meaning and its frequency change because the conditions are no longer demanding it as the alarm signal.

**Family systems.** The "problem child" is rarely the problem. The child is the symptom of the family system. Punishing the child, medicating the child, sending the child away — none of these address the system. The system reorganizes around a new "problem child" or the original child breaks down further. Shift the family system — change how the parents are with each other, change how the family responds to feeling, change the implicit rules — and the original child's behavior often changes without direct intervention.

**Organizations.** The toxic employee is often the employee who is reacting to a toxic configuration that everyone else is silently absorbing. Firing the employee removes the surface symptom and leaves the configuration. The next employee absorbs the toxicity or becomes the next "toxic employee." Shift the configuration — change what is being implicitly demanded, change how feedback flows, change the operating model — and the role disappears because the conditions that produced it are no longer there.

**Cultures and societies.** A culture that has a recurrent "problem group" is a culture whose configuration is producing the problem-group dynamic. Eliminating the targeted group does not solve the dynamic; the system finds a new targeted group. Shift the cultural configuration that requires a targeted group, and the dynamic dissolves.

**Coaching.** A client who keeps falling back into the same pattern is in a life-environment that keeps producing the conditions for the pattern. Coaching at the pattern level (try harder, use this technique, practice this skill) often fails because the environment keeps demanding the pattern. Coaching at the environment level — what relationships, what work, what daily structure, what implicit demands — produces shift the pattern-level work cannot.

## Why This Is Hard

The principle is hard because it requires the practitioner to release the satisfaction of identifying the bad actor. Identifying the bad actor is gratifying. It locates the problem outside oneself. It creates a clear target. It produces the brief relief of "I know what is wrong."

Shifting the environment, by contrast, requires acknowledging that the conditions one has built (or accepted, or participated in) are producing the unwanted result. The leverage is at home, not over there. This is harder to face and slower to act on. The reward is that the actual work is being done. The cost is the loss of the bad-actor satisfaction.

This connects to [[fault-vs-responsibility]]: the actor may be at fault for the specific behavior, but the responsibility for the environment is rarely the actor's alone. Holding both is harder than collapsing into either pure blame or pure self-blame.

## The "Environment" at Different Scales

The principle's "environment" is scale-relative.

- **For an individual:** the environment is their relationships, their schedule, their internal narratives, their sleep, their body, their daily inputs, the people they spend time with.
- **For a couple:** the environment is the patterns they have co-created, the implicit rules, what each is allowed and not allowed to express, the larger life structure they have built.
- **For a team:** the environment is the operating cadence, the implicit norms about feedback, the leadership stance, the incentive structures, the meeting patterns.
- **For an organization:** the environment includes the org chart, the incentive design, the cultural narratives, the customer-facing pressures, the financial constraints.
- **For a culture:** the environment includes the implicit narratives, the economic structure, the media patterns, the institutional configurations.

The principle is the same across scales. The specific work of "shifting the environment" varies by scale, but the leverage logic is constant: shift the conditions that produced the actor's behavior, do not attack the actor.

## When the Principle Is Most Useful

- When a person is about to fire / divorce / cut off / punish someone and has not yet looked at the conditions
- When a person has been trying for years to eliminate a behavior in themselves and the behavior keeps coming back
- When a leader is about to perform a single-target intervention that will leave the producing conditions untouched
- When a parent is at the end of their patience with a child and is about to escalate the consequences
- When the conversation is stuck in "who is to blame for this" rather than "what is the configuration that keeps producing this"
- When [[forest-fire-as-system-reset]] or [[kaibab-deer-mountain-lion-balance]] has been introduced and the listener is ready for the operational principle

## What the Principle Does Not Say

It does not say actors are not responsible. Individuals are responsible for their choices. The principle is about where the leverage for systemic change lives, not about whether individual choices matter.

It does not say no actor should ever be removed. Sometimes the actor's removal is the right move (the abusive partner, the unsalvageable hire, the actively harmful element). The principle is about the default reflex, not about the rule. The default reflex of "the actor is the problem; eliminate the actor" is what the principle is correcting.

It does not say all environments can be shifted. Sometimes the environment is fixed (a chronic health condition, a market reality, a family-of-origin history) and the work is to be different inside it. The principle is about identifying the actual leverage point, which is sometimes the environment and sometimes the relationship to an unchangeable environment.

It does not say environment work is faster than actor work. Environment work is often slower. It is more often the work that actually produces change.

## Tone

Direct and quietly insistent. The principle is delivered as a structural observation rather than as a moral one. Joe is not telling the listener to be more compassionate toward the bad actor. He is pointing out that attacking the bad actor does not work as a strategy, and that the strategy that does work happens at a different level than the listener has been operating at. The tone is the tone of someone explaining a mechanism, not the tone of someone advocating for a value.
