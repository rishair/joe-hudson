# Avoided Conflict Goes Internal

## The Principle

The conflict you avoid does not disappear. It moves inside. The stories you tell to justify the avoidance ("I should just take care of myself," "It's my fault," "It's pointless, they won't get it," "I'm not living the life I want to live and I can't") are not absence of conflict. They are the same conflict relocated from the relationship into your interior.

"A common thread that I hear in all of these is that the conflict doesn't go away, it just goes internal. Like, I should take care of myself is an internal conflict. It's my fault is an internal conflict. It's pointless, they won't get it, not worth it. Still kind of a conflict. There's like a suppression." -- Brett Kistler, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

"Yes. Because I'm not living the life I want to live and I can't. That's an internal conflict." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The recognition lands because of how exact the equivalence is. Every story used to avoid an external conflict produces an internal conflict of identical structure. The choice is not between conflict and no conflict. The choice is between conflict in the room (where it can be metabolized) and conflict in the interior (where it cycles).

## The Stories Joe Names

The transcript surfaces several recurring stories. Each one looks like a reason to avoid the external conflict. Each one is a complete internal conflict of its own.

- **"I should just take care of myself."** External tension is denied; internal tension between the desire to address the situation and the should not to becomes the lived experience.
- **"It's my fault. What did I do wrong? How do I fix myself?"** The conflict that would have been about the other person becomes a conflict about your own brokenness.
- **"It's pointless, they're gonna always be an asshole, so why even handle it?"** The external conflict becomes the internal conflict of judging the other person and the meta-judgment about whether your judgment is fair.
- **"They won't get it, it's not worth it."** The external conflict becomes the internal conflict of contempt held silently.
- **"People who fight are idiots / lesser than me."** The external conflict becomes the internal conflict of maintaining superiority while feeling the unspoken resentment.
- **"If I were to fight, I'd be bad / weak / wrong."** The external conflict becomes the internal conflict of holding the self-judgment about even wanting to address the situation.

Each story has the same shape. There is something to be said externally that is not being said. The energy of the unsaid thing has to go somewhere. It goes into the interior, where it manifests as the story.

"Yeah, those are other examples. I think sometimes we judge others, sometimes we put ourselves above, sometimes we do it by saying that it's our problem. There's lots of ways that we avoid the conflict." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## Why This Matters

The conflict-avoidant person often experiences themselves as someone who has chosen peace. The structural fact is that they have not chosen peace. They have chosen the internal version of the conflict over the external version. The interior is now full of the unprocessed material.

The cost shows up as:

- Persistent self-criticism (the conflict relocated as self-blame)
- The looping voice in the head (the unsaid argument continuing internally) -- see [[voice-in-the-head]]
- Resentment that has no clear target
- Exhaustion that does not match the visible workload (the internal conflict is taking the energy)
- Anxiety that has no specific source
- The sense of not being able to live the life they want (the internal conflict has displaced their wants)

The peace they thought they were producing was the surface. The interior is where the war moved.

## The Diagnostic Move

The principle inverts the usual question. The conflict-avoidant person asks "how do I avoid the conflict?" The diagnostic question is the reverse: "what is the internal conflict that is currently running because I am not having the external conflict?"

Once that question is in view, the trade gets visible. Most people will find that the external conflict, fully metabolized, is significantly less costly than the internal conflict that has been running for months or years in its place.

This connects to the broader principle that [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]]. The internal conflict cannot heal because it is not the actual conflict; it is the displaced form. The healing requires the external conflict be brought back into the room where it can be addressed.

## The Sequence Out

The sequence is not "have the fight." The sequence is closer to:

1. Notice the internal conflict that is currently running
2. Identify the external conflict it is the displacement of
3. Process the emotions that have been accumulating internally (this is where work like [[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[emotional-inquiry]], and similar practices fit)
4. Bring the actual want or truth into the external conversation, kindly and clearly

The third step is what makes the fourth step possible without it becoming the kind of leaning-in that is just defendedness in disguise.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A persistent self-blaming story about a relationship situation
- The realization that the energy you spend judging the other person is a conflict you have not had with them
- Catching the loop of "they won't get it anyway" and noticing the loop is itself a conflict
- The exhaustion that comes from running an argument in your head that you have not had aloud
- The sudden recognition, sometimes years late, that what you thought was your inner work was actually the displaced version of a conversation you have been avoiding

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents persistent internal conflict (self-criticism, looping thoughts, chronic guilt) and is doing inner work that is not landing
