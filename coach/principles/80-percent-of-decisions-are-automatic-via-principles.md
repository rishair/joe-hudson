# Good Operating Principles Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

## The Principle

A well-built set of operating principles, lived through and consciously refined, removes 80% of the deciding from the leader's daily work. The decisions that would otherwise have required deliberation get made automatically because the principles have pre-handled them. The 80% that gets automated also gets made well -- consistently well -- because the principles encode the considered version of the decision rather than the in-the-moment version.

The principle compresses the value proposition of operating principles into a measurable claim. The remaining 20% of decisions are the genuinely novel ones that the principles cannot cover -- the strategic pivot, the unprecedented crisis, the decision that surfaces a gap in the principles themselves. The 20% gets the attention. The 80% gets the consistency.

"If you have a good set of operating principles, 80% of your decisions get made almost automatically and they get made well, consistently. It's just an amazing efficiency." -- Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

The figure is approximate but the claim is structural: most of the decisions in a leader's day are versions of decisions that have been made before. Well-built principles capture the considered answer to those decisions once, then automate the answer for every subsequent instance.

## How the Automation Actually Works

A decision feels like a decision when the brain is weighing options and trying to predict the emotional consequences of each. The deliberation is what produces the experience of "deciding." A principle that has been internalized removes the deliberation from decisions that fall under it. The brain still notices the situation, but the answer is already there -- not as a rule to follow, but as the obvious next move.

This is structurally identical to the way a skilled driver makes lane-change decisions. The novice driver weighs each lane change carefully (deliberation). The skilled driver makes the same decision in a tenth of a second (automation). The skilled driver's automated decision is not worse than the novice's deliberated decision -- it is usually better, because it integrates more information faster.

The same applies to leadership decisions. A principle like "I don't work with assholes" (see [[principles-free-you]]) automates the entire class of decisions about whether to take on a difficult client, work with a problematic partner, or accept a project that comes with a person the leader cannot stand. The deliberation that would have happened in each instance is pre-handled. The leader still notices the situation; the answer is already in the room.

## What This Looks Like in a Company

In the Johannes Langgraf example, the operating principles installed at his company (Ona, formerly Gitpod) handle the bulk of the decisions the company makes. Not "guide" the decisions. Make them.

"A lot of your decisions get made based on your operating principles, meaning they're not really getting made by you. They're not really getting made by the CTO. It's we make our decisions based on these operating principles." -- Joe Hudson observing Johannes's company, 6 Operating Principles

The principles become the decision-maker. The CEO and the executive team become the maintainers and refiners of the principles, not the deciders of every instance. This is the operational state that makes a company able to navigate rapid change without bottlenecking at the leader's deciding capacity.

The decisions that get automated include:
- Whether to take on a customer
- Whether to fire a problem employee
- Whether to ship a feature that violates a principle
- Whether to delay a launch to address a quality issue
- Whether to have the hard conversation now or defer it
- How to handle the conflict between two teams
- What the response is to a missed quarter
- Whether to invest in a new line of business

Each of these would otherwise require executive deliberation. Each, with the right principles, gets made automatically and consistently. The leader's attention is freed for the genuinely novel decisions.

## The Training-the-Dog Metaphor

Operating principles, like training a dog, require substantial upfront investment that pays back over the lifetime of the dog. The first months of training are intensive. After they are done, the rest of the dog's life is much easier for both the dog and the trainer.

"It's a lot like training a dog. If you don't spend the time up front, it's a lot of time up front, but if you do it, the rest of your dog's life is a lot easier. But there is upfront cost to it." -- Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles

The same applies to principles. The bottom-up experimentation process (see [[rollout-principles-in-organization]]) takes months. The quarterly re-wrestling (see [[wrestle-with-your-principles]]) is an ongoing investment. The principles do not install themselves. The investment is real.

The return on that investment is the 80% of decisions that no longer require deliberation. Over the lifetime of a company, this compounds into enormous time savings. The CEO who has the principles installed is making 80% fewer decisions and getting better outcomes than the CEO who is deciding everything from scratch.

The leader who refuses the upfront cost ("we don't have time to do this principles exercise; we need to ship") ends up paying the back-end cost forever. Every recurring decision keeps requiring fresh deliberation. The team keeps re-litigating the same questions. The CEO becomes the bottleneck on every decision because no decision can be made without their attention.

## The Time-ROI Calculation

The investment in operating principles, measured in hours, is much smaller than most leaders fear once the principles are stable.

"Would you say 40 hours every 2 years? What would you say just generally?" -- Joe Hudson asking Johannes
"I mean, we have kind of a recap at every offsite, which happens twice a year. As part of the leadership of -- but right now they're very stable." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles

A few hours per offsite, twice a year, to re-evaluate principles that are stable. Compared to the time saved on the 80% of automated decisions, the investment is trivial. The math favors the rollout aggressively.

The catch is in the upfront phase, which is more intensive (see [[rollout-principles-in-organization]] for the multi-step method). After the initial rollout, the maintenance cost is small. The leader who survives the upfront phase gets compounding returns; the leader who skips it gets no returns at all.

## What Distinguishes the 80% from the 20%

The 80% that gets automated:
- Recurring kinds of decisions (hiring, firing, customer issues, internal conflicts, prioritization between known options)
- Decisions where the principle has been tested before
- Decisions where the leader's first instinct already aligns with the principle
- Decisions that scale (the same principle applies to many instances)

The 20% that remains:
- Genuinely novel situations the principles have not been tested against
- Decisions where the principles are in tension with each other and have to be re-balanced
- Decisions that surface a gap in the principles themselves (which then become the input to the next round of wrestling)
- Strategic-level decisions that operate above the level of the principles (what business to be in, when to pivot, when to sell)
- Decisions that require the leader's actual judgment because the situation has no precedent

The 80%/20% split is not a fixed ratio; it depends on how mature the principles are and how stable the company's environment is. A young company with new principles may operate closer to 50%/50%. A mature company with stable principles may operate closer to 90%/10%. The trajectory is from less automation to more automation as the principles get refined through use.

## What Goes Wrong When This Doesn't Happen

A company without operating principles -- or with values posters that do not change behavior -- pays the 80% cost in time forever. Several specific dysfunctions follow:

**Bottlenecking at the CEO.** Every decision routes to the CEO because no other decision-maker has the context to make it consistently. The CEO becomes the limiting factor on the company's pace.

**Inconsistent decisions across teams.** The same kind of decision gets made differently by different people because there is no shared framework. The company runs on local optimizations that conflict with each other.

**Decision fatigue.** The leader who is making 100 deliberated decisions a day burns out. The leader who is making 20 deliberated decisions and letting 80 happen via principles has bandwidth for the next thing.

**Re-litigation.** The same decision keeps being made over and over because each instance feels novel without the principles to handle them. The team spends meeting time on questions that should have been answered structurally.

**Political decision-making.** Without principles, decisions get made based on who has power, who is loudest, who is in the room. The principle-less company is the political company. The principled company replaces politics with shared standards.

## Boundary Conditions

The 80% claim depends on the principles being good. Bad principles, or principles that nobody actually follows, automate nothing. The claim assumes the principles have been built and lived through the way [[wrestle-with-your-principles]] and [[rollout-principles-in-organization]] describe. A poster on the wall does not produce the 80%. A principle that has been tested, refined, and integrated into how the team actually decides does.

The claim also depends on the principles being current. Principles that were right five years ago but have not been wrestled with since may automate the wrong answer. The maintenance cost (quarterly re-wrestling) is what keeps the 80% claim true over time. Without the maintenance, the automation degrades into rigidity.

## Tone

Direct, almost engineering. The principle is offered as a measurable benefit with a specific number attached, not as soft advice. The number invites disagreement; the structural claim survives the disagreement. Even if the actual ratio is 70% or 60% rather than 80%, the leverage argument is the same. The principles handle the bulk; the leader handles the edge.
