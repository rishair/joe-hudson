# Marriage Is Built Up the Mountain, Not Fallen Down From the Top

## The Principle

When a couple feels they have found "the perfect partner" and starts at the top of the mountain, the only direction the relationship can move is down. Falling in love is, structurally, a descent -- the high state is the starting state and the trajectory bends back toward earth as the projections collapse and the actual partner becomes visible.

When a couple does not start at the top -- when they have to figure each other out, build the structure of attachment, strengthen the trust through use -- the trajectory bends upward. The relationship gets stronger over time because it is being built, not found.

The principle does not actually require arranged marriages. It uses the arranged-marriage example to make the structural point visible. Any marriage that is approached as a building project rather than as the discovery of a pre-existing match takes the upward-trajectory shape.

## Tara's Naming of the Teaching

Tara attributes the teaching to Sibonfu Somé, an African teacher whose tradition included arranged marriages.

"There's a teacher, beloved teacher, a teacher I loved, Sibonfu Somé, who came from, um, a part of Africa where they had arranged marriages. And not that I'm encouraging arranged marriages, but she talked about how when you fall in love with your perfect partner, you start at the top of the mountain and the only place to go is down, like falling down a mountain. Whereas with an arranged marriage, with putting together with someone, you have to build and muscle strength and strengthen the marriage to get up the mountain. So it's a building, building, building. And creating structure to get to the top of the mountain. And so I think that marriage is about building and creating structures and attachments." -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The teaching's two-trajectory contrast is what carries the principle:

**Top-of-the-mountain start (the "perfect partner" found):**
- Begins at peak felt state
- Each subsequent moment is at best a maintenance of, more typically a decline from, the peak
- The projections that produced the peak collapse
- The actual partner becomes visible and disappoints the projection
- The arc is downward
- The end state, in 25 years, is often the dead marriage Joe describes (see [[snickers-and-mars-marriage-pattern]])

**Build-up-the-mountain start (the partnership undertaken as a project):**
- Begins at no peak; just two people in a configuration
- Each subsequent moment is an opportunity to build
- The structure of the relationship is created in the doing
- The attachment strengthens through use
- The trust grows through demonstration
- The arc is upward
- The end state, in 25 years, is something the couple built, not something they found

## What This Refutes

The principle directly refutes the dominant romantic ideology: that the work of partnership is finding the right person and that, once found, the partnership will then function on the strength of the rightness. The ideology produces:

- Endless dating as a search for the peak-feeling
- Quick exits when a relationship moves past the peak
- The "we just grew apart" narrative for marriages that were never built in the first place
- The mid-life crisis in long marriages where the peak has worn off and no building project was undertaken to replace it
- The chronic dissatisfaction in partnerships that arrived through chemistry alone and were never strengthened through deliberate work

The principle says the chemistry that produces "fell in love" is a starting condition, not a sustaining condition. The sustaining condition is the building. The building can happen whether the chemistry was there or not. The chemistry alone does not produce the building.

This also closes the loop with [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]]. The willingness-to-do-the-work is the willingness to engage the building project. The "is this the right person" question, properly understood, is the question of whether the two people can build together. The mountain is climbed by building; whether the starting point felt like the top or the bottom is mostly irrelevant.

## The Joe-Specific Empirical Confirmation

Joe gives the inverse-pattern empirical observation that backs up the principle:

"I notice a dynamic that typically people who are like, it's just a great fit and here we are and we don't have a lot of issues, usually have a pretty— in 25 years, most of them seem to have a pretty dead marriage or one of them looks up one day and says, you know, you're Snickers and I've just decided I like Mars. So like I see a lot of, a lot of relationships end that way that seemed like, oh look, we have a great relationship. And just follow that. It all builds up underneath. Um, so that's the pattern I've seen." -- Joe Hudson, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The empirical claim is unidirectional in Joe's observation: relationships that started at the top of the mountain (great fit, no issues) tend, at 25 years, to either be dead or to suffer the Snickers-and-Mars exit. The implication is not that the no-issues start guarantees the bad end. The implication is that the no-issues start often masks the absence of a building project, and the absence of the building project shows up at the long-arc mark.

The building-project marriages, by contrast, tend to be uneven in the first years and stronger in the later years. The work that was done early shows up as structure that holds in the harder periods. The chemistry that may or may not have been present at the start is not load-bearing for the long arc; the building is.

## The Operational Consequence

For someone in early partnership, the principle's operational consequence is to ask "what am I building" rather than "is this the right person." The building question is answerable. The right-person question is not.

For someone in a long marriage that feels off, the principle's operational consequence is to investigate whether building has been happening. Many long marriages that feel dead are dead because nothing has been deliberately built in many years. The peak that started the marriage worn off; nothing was constructed to replace it; the relationship coasted on the residual until the residual ran out.

For someone evaluating whether to commit, the principle dissolves the "do I feel the spark intensely enough" question. The spark intensity is not the load-bearing variable. The willingness to build is. A relationship with moderate spark and high willingness-to-build will, in 25 years, be a stronger relationship than one with high spark and low willingness-to-build.

This is also why arranged marriages, in cultures that take them seriously, often produce satisfaction reports that surprise observers from chemistry-first cultures. The arranged couples were never given the option to confuse chemistry with sustainability. They had to build from the start. The structure they built holds, and the felt-sense of the relationship at 25 years is often higher than the chemistry-first comparison group.

## What This Is Not

This is not the claim that chemistry-first marriages are doomed. Some chemistry-first couples also do the building work and produce the long-arc structure. The principle does not predict outcomes; it identifies which variable is load-bearing.

This is not an endorsement of arranged marriages. Tara is explicit: "not that I'm encouraging arranged marriages." The example is being used to make the structural point visible, not to advocate for the practice.

This is not the claim that the peak feeling of falling in love is wrong or to be avoided. Falling in love is fine. The principle is about what happens after. The post-falling-in-love phase is when the building work either does or does not happen. The peak itself is not the failure mode; the failure mode is treating the peak as the achievement rather than as the starting condition.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- A person is in an early relationship and asking whether the chemistry is enough to predict whether it will last
- A person is in a long marriage that feels dead and is asking what happened
- A person is dating and oscillating between partners on the basis of who produced the most chemistry
- A person is considering leaving a relationship that does not produce intense chemistry but where both partners are willing to do the work
- A person is in a midlife crisis and looking back at their marriage and wondering if they picked wrong
- A person is romanticizing the idea of finding "the one" and the work-frame needs to be introduced
- A person had a strong start to a relationship and is now disappointed that it has plateaued; the building-work needs to be named as what is missing

## Coach's Move

The principle can be delivered nearly verbatim. The mountain metaphor is compact, sticky, and self-explanatory. The arranged-marriage attribution to Sibonfu Somé is a useful sourcing because it locates the teaching in a tradition that took partnership-as-building seriously, which gives the metaphor authority that pure assertion would lack.

The follow-up question is usually some form of: "What are you building in your relationship right now?" The question forces the abstract teaching into concrete reality. The answers tend to be illuminating. Most couples who feel dead are not building anything in particular. Most couples who feel alive can name what they are building, even if the building is not labeled that way.

## Tone

Tara delivers this with the warmth of someone passing on a teaching from a teacher she loved. The "beloved teacher, a teacher I loved" is the marker that Sibonfu Somé occupies a particular place for Tara. Use the same tone when delivering the principle -- this is a teaching with lineage, not a piece of self-help originality.

The "not that I'm encouraging arranged marriages" caveat is part of how the teaching delivers. Without the caveat, the listener gets stuck on whether arranged marriages are good or bad. With the caveat, the listener can hear the structural point. Use the same caveat when teaching the principle.
