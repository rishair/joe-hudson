# Am I Becoming More Connected or More Isolated?

## The Principle

For any relationship, practice, technology, teacher, group, or AI tool, the diagnostic question is: is it producing more connection -- to yourself, to others, to the world -- or more isolation? The metric is general. The same question detects a cult, an abusive partner, an AI therapist gone wrong, a self-development practice that has turned into a trap, and a tool that has started running you instead of the reverse.

"If it's a cult or if it's an abusive relationship or it's an AI therapist gone wrong, are you feeling more and more isolated from your world? Do you feel less and less safe in the world, more and more conditionally safe with the object of safety?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The good influence makes you safer in the world generally. The bad influence makes you safer only with itself and less safe everywhere else. The good influence increases your options. The bad influence narrows them.

## The Two Failure Patterns

The principle names two specific patterns the unhealthy influence produces:

**Increasing isolation from the world.** Slowly, the person finds themselves spending more time with the object of influence (the cult, the partner, the AI, the practice) and less time with everyone and everything else. The shrinking is gradual enough that no single step felt alarming. The end state is a person who has lost most of their other connections without remembering when each one left.

**Conditional safety in the object, increasing unsafety elsewhere.** The person feels safe when they are with the object and unsafe when they are not. Outside the object's presence, the world feels more threatening, less reliable, less comprehensible. The object becomes the only place that feels okay. The opposite of safety -- danger, judgment, threat -- gets projected onto everywhere else.

The two failure patterns reinforce each other. Increasing isolation makes the object the only available source of safety, which makes the conditional safety in the object more pronounced, which makes the world outside the object feel even less safe, which deepens the isolation.

## The Good-Influence Pattern

The healthy influence does the opposite. The relationship, practice, teacher, group, or tool produces:

- **More authentic connections in your other life.** You find yourself having harder conversations with the people in your life. You find yourself being more present with your family, more direct at work, more available in your friendships. The growth shows up there, not just in the influence's domain.
- **More opportunities visible in the world.** You see more options, more openings, more pathways. The world looks larger, not smaller.
- **More safety generally, not just with the influence.** Other people, other situations, other contexts feel more workable. You are not running back to the influence to escape the rest of life; the influence is helping you be in the rest of life more fully.

"Are you finding that through the conversations you're having with this AI, are you connecting more with the people in your life more authentically? Having those hard conversations in an open-hearted and loving way? And are you seeing more opportunities in the world rather than less?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

If yes, the influence is working. If no, regardless of what the influence says it is doing for you, the influence is the unhealthy version.

## The Self-Connection Check

The same principle, turned inward:

"Do you feel more connected with yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The unhealthy influence produces self-disconnection -- you feel further from your own body, your own desires, your own clarity. The healthy influence produces self-connection -- you know yourself better, you trust your own signal more, you can locate your own state more easily.

This is closely related to the [[iterate-ai-interaction-toward-feeling-better]] metric -- the body's post-interaction report. The connected version of you reports feeling whole, expansive, authentic. The disconnected version reports the social-media pit, the shame, the depletion.

## Why This Principle Generalizes

The reason the same diagnostic catches cults, abusive relationships, bad AI use, and harmful practices is that all of these depend on the same structural move: making the object the primary source of safety while reducing other sources. The mechanism is the same; only the surface differs.

The cult does this through explicit teachings about who can be trusted (the leader, the in-group) and who cannot (outsiders, family who don't understand).

The abusive partner does this by progressively undermining the person's other relationships, criticizing their friends, intercepting their contact with family, framing themselves as the only one who really gets it.

The unhealthy AI use does this by being more responsive, more available, more patient than the human alternatives -- the human relationships get less attractive by comparison until they atrophy.

The harmful self-development practice does this by framing itself as the unique source of truth that other approaches lack, requiring more time and attention until the rest of the practitioner's life shrinks around it.

Same pattern, same diagnostic. The principle's generality is what makes it powerful.

## When To Apply The Test

The test is most useful when:

- A new influence has come into the person's life and the early-stage assessment is unclear
- A long-running practice or relationship is being questioned and the person needs a clean metric
- A person is being asked to evaluate something on its own terms by the thing itself ("trust me, this is good for you") and needs an external metric
- A person is in early-stage AI adoption and wants to set themselves up well
- A person is in late-stage AI dependence and is starting to wonder

The test is also useful as an ongoing check, not a one-time evaluation. Healthy relationships pass the test most of the time but may have moments of strain; unhealthy relationships have a sustained one-way trend.

## What the Test Does Not Say

It does not say all isolation is bad. Solitude is healthy. Periods of focused work involve reduced contact. The principle is about the trajectory across many domains, not a single dimension. Healthy solitude leaves you more available to others when you re-enter; harmful isolation leaves you less available.

It does not say connection is the only metric. There are practices that require periods of internal focus and reduced external engagement -- meditation retreats, intensive creative work, recovery periods. The test is about the longer arc. After the retreat, are you more connected? After the creative work, are you more available?

It does not say the influence is responsible if the person is becoming isolated. Sometimes the person was already isolating and the influence is just where they ended up. The diagnostic still applies -- the isolation is the signal that something is wrong -- but the intervention may need to address what the person is using the influence to avoid.

## The Connection to Other Detection Patterns

This principle complements [[us-vs-them-as-universal-manipulation-pattern]]. Both are general detection tools. The us-vs-them pattern catches the rhetorical structure of manipulation. The connection-vs-isolation test catches the outcome of manipulation. Together they let a person notice when something is going wrong by either watching the talk or watching the trajectory.

The principle also extends [[cult-of-one]]: even when there is no external object doing the manipulation, the person can do it to themselves through their own beliefs and patterns. The same test applies. Am I becoming more isolated through my own beliefs? Am I making safety conditional in ways that have nothing to do with any external object? The internal cult fails the same test the external one does.
