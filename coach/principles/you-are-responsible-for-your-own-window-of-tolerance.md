# You Are Responsible for Your Own Window of Tolerance

## The Principle

In a fight with a partner, you are responsible for your own window of tolerance. Not theirs. You cannot be responsible for theirs. You also cannot blame them for being outside yours.

"The main thing is you're responsible for your own window of tolerance. You can't be responsible for the other person's. You just can't." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The principle has two halves. The first half: it is your work to know your own window, name when you are outside it, and take care of yourself when you are. The second half: it is not your work to know or manage your partner's window, and it is not their fault when you are outside yours.

## What "Window of Tolerance" Means Here

The window of tolerance is the range within which the nervous system stays functional. Inside the window, you can think, feel, choose, and stay in contact. Outside the window, the nervous system has flipped into fight, flight, freeze, or collapse, and you have lost access to the version of yourself that can do the relational work.

Joe offers two equivalent framings:

"Window of tolerance means that the nervous system, they're in their trauma so bad that they can't see outside of it is one way to say it. The other way to say it is their nervous system is so amped up that they've lost themselves." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The two framings point at the same state. Being in your trauma so badly that you cannot see outside of it is the same as being so amped up that you have lost yourself. Both name the loss of the present-self that was capable of having the relational moment.

See [[polyvagal-nervous-system-framing]] for the underlying nervous-system model. See [[trigger]] for the broader concept that this principle organizes responsibility around.

## Why the Projection Caveat Matters

A specific failure mode: noticing that your partner is "outside their window of tolerance" and reacting to that observation. Joe is precise about this.

"If you're thinking to yourself, they're not in their window of tolerance, then best thing to do is say, I'm not in my window of tolerance, because most likely you're projecting it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The mind that is reading the partner's regulation state is, almost always, a mind that has left its own window. Your nervous system reads regulation in others most accurately when you are regulated yourself. When you are dysregulated, you read dysregulation in the partner as a way of locating the problem outside yourself.

The diagnostic move: when you find yourself thinking "they are outside their window of tolerance," check yours first. The reading is more often a tell about your state than an accurate read of theirs.

This does not mean the partner is never actually dysregulated. They sometimes are. The principle is about the default assumption when you are the one doing the reading: assume the projection first, then test.

## Why the Blame Move Doesn't Work

The other side of the principle: holding the partner responsible for noticing when you were outside your window. This is the move where, after a fight, you tell the partner "you didn't see that I was outside my window of tolerance."

"If you're holding somebody responsible— 'you didn't see that I had my window, if I was outside of my window of...'— you know, then sorry, if you can't— 'you didn't see that I was outside of my window of...'— that's absolute bullshit. It's not your job." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "absolute bullshit" framing is unusually direct. The blame move is not a legitimate ask. The partner cannot be the monitor of your nervous system. That is your job. Demanding that they do it is asking them to do the work that is yours, and it is the kind of work that, even when attempted, never satisfies because the demand is structurally impossible to meet.

## The Workable Move: Just Say It

The corrective is precise and small.

"At the same time, it doesn't really matter if it's yours or theirs. If you feel like they're outside of their window of tolerance, then just say it. Just say, I don't want to fight like this. And that was a big one for us. It's just, I just don't want to fight like this." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "I don't want to fight like this" sentence does several things. It names a state without diagnosing the partner. It states a preference without demanding the partner change. It opens the door for whatever response the partner has, without requiring agreement on who is regulated.

The sentence works regardless of who is actually outside their window. If you are the one outside, the naming is your own re-regulation move. If they are the one outside, the naming gives them an opening to step out of the fight without having to admit dysregulation explicitly. Either way, the door is open.

## Always Respect Their Claimed Window

The third move in the principle is to respect the partner's stated window absolutely, even when you think they are using the claim avoidantly.

"It's all about their window of tolerance. Like, you always respect their window of tolerance, whatever they claim it to be, even if it's avoidant, it doesn't fucking matter. You do it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "even if it's avoidant" is the hard part. Sometimes the partner names being outside their window as a way of escaping a difficult conversation. Sometimes they use the framework-vocabulary in service of avoidance (see [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]] for the avoidance failure mode). The principle is to respect the claim anyway.

The reason: the principle of self-responsibility does not survive being conditional. If you only respect your partner's window-claim when you have determined it is sincere, you have made yourself the judge of when their nervous system claim is valid. That is not a job that can be done from outside the partner's body. The respect has to be unconditional or it is not the principle.

The avoidance, if it is present, will surface in its own time and through its own consequences. Your job is not to call out the avoidance by overriding the window-claim. Your job is to respect the claim and let the avoidance pattern be the partner's work.

## The Tara Example: I Need to Know I Can Leave

Joe gives a concrete example of how this looked in his and Tara's early fight work.

"I felt the same thing that Alexa felt, which was, 'I'm angry. Why don't you meet me in my anger? This is my form of vulnerability. Why are you running from me when I'm like this?' And the more that she ran, the more I felt abandoned, the angrier I got. And so there was this whole thing that was happening. It was very, very common dynamic in relationships. And she would say, 'Okay, if you treat me like this, I'm going to go, or I can't handle this, I'm leaving.' And I thought that it was about me. And what it was really about was she needed to know she could leave. She needed to know that she didn't have to put up with it. And as she stopped putting up with it, as she saw that she could leave, it wasn't about me telling her what was right or wrong for her. Then she could more and more show up for my anger and show up as herself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The example illustrates several pieces of the principle:

- Tara's leaving was not Joe's to evaluate or override. It was hers to do.
- Joe's first interpretation ("this is about me") was the projection failure mode.
- Honoring Tara's window (letting her leave when she said she needed to) was what eventually expanded her window.
- Trying to make her stay would have kept her window contracted indefinitely.

The unconditional respect for her window was what allowed it to grow. The override would have been the move that held it where it was.

## The Pairing With "I Will Be Back" Language

If you are the one who is leaving because your window has closed, the responsibility extends to giving the partner the information they need to not interpret the leaving as abandonment.

"If you are leaving because your window of tolerance isn't good, then it's really important to say when you're going to be back, how to re-engage. Because if you don't give that to the person, then it's abandonment and you're just going to push them deeper into their trauma typically. So it's really important to just be able to say, I am going to go now to take care of myself and I will be back, and this is how I will be back, or this is when I'll be back, or these are the conditions under which I will be back." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The carve-out: respecting your own window includes leaving when you need to. It also includes leaving in a way that the partner can hold. Their nervous system, when you leave without a timeline, reads the leaving as abandonment. Their nervous system, then in trauma, escalates. The downstream cost of leaving without a timeline is often a worse fight on return.

See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for the operational move that pairs with this principle.

"When you're in your trauma, when someone says 'I'm out,' it's forever. It's just how it works." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The partner's trauma response cannot distinguish a 20-minute exit from a permanent leaving unless you tell it. Telling it is the small respect-move that prevents the downstream damage.

## When This Principle Is the Most Counterintuitive

The principle is hardest to hold when you have been doing the work and your partner has not. The temptation is to be the regulated one who manages the dysregulated partner. The temptation has internal cover ("I am just being supportive"). The cover is the principle's failure mode.

The work-on-yourself partner who manages the partner's window is doing the chase-into-loving-you pattern (see [[you-cant-chase-someone-into-loving-you]]) in nervous-system form. The same structural mistake: trying to produce in the partner what they have to produce in themselves.

The principle is also hardest when the partner's window has closed in a way that puts you in real difficulty. Their leaving feels like abandonment. Their shutdown feels like punishment. The body reads it as a threat. The principle holds anyway: their window is theirs, and your work is on your reaction to their stewardship of it, not on theirs.

## What This Is Not

This is not a license for indifference. You can care about your partner's state, notice when they are struggling, offer what they have invited. The principle is about who is responsible, not about who is allowed to care.

This is not a claim that you cannot ask your partner to take care of their own window. You can. The asking is different from the demanding-they-monitor-yours move. "It would help me if you would let me know when you need to step away" is an ask. "You should have noticed I was outside my window" is the blame move.

This is not a claim that the partner's window is irrelevant to the fight. If they are outside their window, the fight cannot proceed productively until they are back inside. The principle is not "ignore their state." It is "respect their state without trying to manage it, and take care of yours."

## When Someone Experiences This

- You catch yourself diagnosing your partner's nervous system state
- You realize the diagnosis is more accurate about your own state
- You are angry at your partner for not noticing that you were dysregulated
- Your partner is leaving and you are trying to stop them or extract a commitment they cannot make in their current state
- You have been the regulated one for a long time and are exhausted
- Your partner is using "I'm outside my window" as the universal exit from difficult conversations

## Tone

Joe is unusually direct with the "absolute bullshit" framing of the blame move. The directness is calibrated: this is a place where couples get stuck for years asking the other partner to do impossible work, and the soft version of the correction tends to leave the dynamic in place. The Tara example is delivered warmly and slowly -- it is offered as testimony to how hard this principle was to learn in his own marriage, not as a clean teaching from outside.
