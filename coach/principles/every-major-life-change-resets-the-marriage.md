# Every Major Life Change Resets the Marriage

## The Principle

A long marriage does not progress in a smooth line. It progresses in cycles. The cycles correspond to major life changes -- not to fixed time periods. At each major life change, the marriage enters a hard period of re-formation, because the people in the marriage have just changed and the marriage configuration has to update to the new selves.

Couples who do not know this take the hard periods as evidence that the marriage is broken. Couples who know this take the hard periods as the expected re-formation work and meet them as such. The principle's value is mostly predictive: knowing the resets are coming makes them survivable. Not knowing makes them feel like the end.

## The Pattern Joe and Tara Describe

In the Modern Relationships interview, Eric asks about Joe's earlier mention of cycles. Joe and Tara describe the actual pattern in their marriage. The cycles do not arrive on a fixed clock. They arrive at every major life change.

"Our, our big boom came with any kind of big life change. Like when we went from being two single people to being married was one boom. Then when we went to bringing children in, that was another kind of reassessment because we really had to change who we were, who we were was changing. Like it or not. And so the marriage had to change. And then job career shifts. That's another one. Or working together, starting a company together. That was another big one for us. One kid leaving hasn't been, but I imagine when two kids leave, that might be another." -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The list of marriage-reset triggers from Joe and Tara's specific case:
1. Two single people to a married couple
2. Married couple to parents
3. Career shifts
4. Working together / starting a company together
5. Eventually: kids leaving (forecasted, not yet experienced)
6. Eventually: aging, retirement (forecasted)

Each triggers a re-formation because the people involved have changed in ways that the previous marriage configuration cannot hold. The re-formation is the work the cycle is for.

Joe adds the biological dimension that often accompanies the life changes:

"Hormones are real, male and female. Male and female hormones are real. That changes shit. Kids, kids change hormones. That changes shit." -- Joe Hudson, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The hormones are a confounding variable in the timing. A marriage reset that coincides with a pregnancy, a perimenopause, an andropause, has the additional load of a hormonal shift. The reset is not "just" about the life change; it is also about the biological state of the people in the change.

## Why This Pattern Is Predictable

The principle is structural, not mystical. The mechanism: the marriage configuration was sized for the people who started it. The people change at every major life change. The configuration that fit the previous selves does not fit the new selves. The mismatch produces friction. The friction is the work the reset is for.

The hard periods are therefore not failures of the marriage. They are the marriage doing what it has to do to update. The couples who survive long marriages are the ones who can do the update. The couples who do not survive long marriages either could not do the update or did not know they were supposed to.

This is structurally the same teaching as [[agreements-renegotiate-as-you-change]] applied to the relationship as a whole. The agreement to be in this specific configuration of marriage is one of the agreements that has to update as both partners change. Major life changes are when the people change most. Major life changes are therefore when the marriage configuration most needs to renegotiate.

This is also why [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] is a recurring rather than one-time practice. Each reset is a small death of the old configuration. The grieving practice is the medium of the death. Without the grief, the old configuration stays in place, the new configuration cannot emerge, and the marriage stays frozen at a stage that fits neither partner anymore.

## The Cycles Joe Specifically Identifies

Joe and Tara's specific cycle history, as named in the interview:

- **Pre-marriage to marriage:** the first big boom. The legal and ritual entry into marriage triggered a reset. Tara specifically describes a difficult year after marriage when "all of my shit could come out" because the safety of the vows made the deeper material accessible.
- **Marriage to parents:** another reset. Roles had to be redefined. Joe and Tara had planned for Tara to be the breadwinner and Joe to be the stay-at-home dad; the hormones at pregnancy changed Tara's relationship to leaving the kids and the planned roles flipped. See [[parenting-as-deep-tissue-massage]] for what this kind of identity shift does.
- **Career shifts:** another reset. Each major change in work-life pattern produced a renegotiation.
- **Starting a company together (Art of Accomplishment):** another major reset. Two people who had been working separately now had to work together in shared professional stakes.
- **First kid leaving:** they expected this to be hard. It was not. They reported "we fell in love with each other again in that way that pre-kids." See [[joe-and-tara-weekend-reset]] for the related dynamic of the marriage being starved by the kid demands.
- **Eventually, aging and retirement:** forecasted but not yet experienced.

The forecast is the operational gift of the principle. Knowing that aging, retirement, the kids leaving home, the next career shift will produce a reset means the couple is ready to meet it as a reset rather than as a crisis.

Tara puts the operational use directly: "So you could just know if you're going into a big life change, like you're going to ever retire."

## The Cycles That Were Not Cycles

A useful detail: the first kid leaving did not produce the expected boom. Joe and Tara were braced for it. It was not bad; it was "delicious." The marriage came alive in a way they had not had since pre-kids.

"We had both kids leave for the first time last summer and we were like, this is, this is going to get dicey. And then we were like, nope, that was just fucking awesome." -- Joe Hudson, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The principle does not require that every life change be hard. Some life changes produce easy transitions because the previous configuration had been holding the marriage in a constrained shape and the change releases the constraint. The principle says to be ready for the boom at each life change; it does not say every boom will materialize.

The forecast is therefore: "this might be a boom; be ready." Some are; some are not. The readiness is the variable. The actual experience of the change is what it is.

## What This Pattern Means for the Coach

The most useful application of this principle in coaching is the normalization of the hard period. A couple in a difficult patch after the first kid arrives is not in a failing marriage. They are in the marriage doing its first major reset. A couple struggling after one spouse takes a major new job is not necessarily broken; they are in a reset. A couple finding the empty-nest difficult is doing the reset that the kids-leaving triggers.

The coach's move when this principle applies:

1. **Name what changed.** Identify the specific life change that triggered the difficulty. Often the couple has not connected the dots. They are presenting marriage trouble; the marriage trouble is downstream of a specific recent change.
2. **Normalize the reset.** The hard period is what should be happening. The previous marriage configuration was sized for previous selves. The new configuration has to be constructed.
3. **Direct toward the work.** The work is to update the marriage to the new selves. See [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] for the practice that enables the update.
4. **Hold the long arc.** This is not the end; it is the work of a phase. Phases pass. The couple who can do the reset work emerges with a marriage that fits their new lives.

## The Counterpoint to the 7-Year Itch

The pop-psychology framing of the 7-year itch makes the cycle sound like a malfunction of the marriage or of the people in it. The reset principle reframes the same observation. The 7-year mark in many marriages happens to coincide with a kid arriving or with a major career shift. The "itch" is the reset doing its work. The "itch" is not a failure of love; it is the marriage being asked to update.

The reset principle is therefore not 7-year-cyclical. It is life-change-cyclical. The 7-year statistic is a side effect of the typical pacing of life changes in modern Western marriages. The underlying mechanism is the life change, not the calendar.

## What This Principle Does Not Promise

The principle does not predict that every reset will be navigated successfully. Some couples reach a reset and cannot do the update work; the relationship ends in form. The principle does not contradict that. It says the reset is what is happening; the work the reset requires is a separate question from what the couple does with it.

The principle also does not name a specific timeline for each reset. Some last weeks; some last years. The Joe-and-Tara post-kids-arriving period was about a year. The 7-or-8-year mark vicious fights (see [[tara-fights-that-destroyed-joes-narcissism]]) were a 6-month period. The actual duration depends on the size of the life change and the couple's capacity to do the reset work.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- A couple presents marriage trouble that coincides with a specific recent life change
- A long-married couple is in a hard period and is using "we just don't have what we used to have" as the frame
- A couple in their first year of marriage is in trouble and treating the trouble as a sign of fundamental incompatibility
- A new parent is questioning whether they should have married this person
- A couple working together professionally is having a hard time and not seeing the connection to the work-together transition
- An empty-nest couple is in trouble and presenting it as the relationship being over
- A retired couple is struggling and not seeing the retirement-reset connection

## Tone

Casual, almost matter-of-fact. Joe and Tara deliver this with the lived knowledge of a couple who has been through several resets and is now in a position to forecast the next ones. The "you could just know" of Tara's framing has the tone of a practical heads-up. Use the same tone when teaching the principle. This is operational information, not weighty teaching. The reset is coming; the listener gets to be ready.

The "fucking awesome" tone marker on the empty-nest experience is also part of the teaching. The principle does not predict every reset will be hard. The forecast is "be ready"; the actual experience can range from "this is going to get dicey" to "this is delicious."
