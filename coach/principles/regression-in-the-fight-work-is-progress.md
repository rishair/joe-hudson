# When the Fight Work Feels Like Regression, You're on Mile 24

## The Principle

In long-term fight work in a relationship, periods that feel like regression are usually progress. The fight that feels worst is often the fight that produces the biggest shift. The 6-month wave of bad fights after years of good fights is not the relationship breaking. It is the next layer of the work surfacing.

The diagnostic is not how the fight feels in the moment. The diagnostic is what comes online after the wave has been worked through.

"It is very much as if somebody is running a long marathon race and they're saying, 'I am tired.' It's like, yeah, that's what happens when you're running a marathon race. That's good. That means you're on mile 24. It means you're making progress. You're there. So it's a very similar thing that there's certain things that are challenging in the fighting that are progress and you can't see it until afterwards." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "you can't see it until afterwards" is the key constraint. The principle is a retrospective frame, not an in-the-moment reassurance. Inside the wave, it feels bad. Inside the wave, you are not sure if the marriage is going to survive. The principle does not promise that it will. The principle says that what you are inside is, structurally, more often the breakthrough than the breakdown.

## Joe and Tara's Example: The 6-Month Wave

Joe gives a concrete example.

"I don't know, seven, eight years ago now, we had a really bad moment in our relationship. And we had had these really amazingly healing fights for years. I thought it was all behind us. I hardly had a memory of our like vicious fights. And all of a sudden they came back with vengeance for the 6-month period. And I was like, what the— and we both for years, oh, I wish that hadn't happened. Not quite that— it wasn't 'I wish that hadn't happened' but it was that thing, 'that incident happened, we could have handled it differently' blah, blah, blah, blah." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The 6 months of vicious fights happened years after the early-relationship vicious fights had been worked through. The new wave was unexpected. It looked from the inside like a return of the worst patterns. Both Joe and Tara, in the moment, treated it as evidence of a problem.

Years later, Joe could name what the wave actually accomplished:

"I think that series of fights is what destroyed my narcissism. And it wasn't— I mean, there's no narcissism that isn't all completely destroyed ever, but there was a way in which I was subtly holding myself above Tara. And it was that set of fights that destroyed that. My brain can't conceive comparing myself to Tara anymore." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The wave was the work that dismantled a layer of subtle narcissism that the previous years of fight work had been unable to reach. The new layer required the bigger fights. Without the wave, the layer would have stayed. See [[tara-fights-that-destroyed-joes-narcissism]] for the example in its full form.

## The Sign That the Wave Is Working

Inside the wave, there is one marker that tells you the work is still working, even when the fights look like the worst version of themselves.

"It was in that moment, I remember in those fights, there came a place where I, I learned to just— or we learned how to just sit there in loving attention with each other, just in the face of absolute bullshit from one another. Yeah, I got, I gotcha. I'm right here. I gotcha." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The new move that emerged inside the wave was the capacity to sit in loving attention while the partner was in their full trauma response. That capacity did not exist before the wave. The wave produced it.

The diagnostic for whether a wave of bad fights is breakthrough or breakdown:
- Is something new becoming available in the fights? A new capacity, a new naming, a new way of staying close?
- Are the fights producing material that gets worked, even if slowly?
- Is the wave bounded in time, or has it become the permanent state of the relationship?
- After each fight in the wave, is there contact or only distance?

The wave-as-progress version has all four of these. The wave-as-collapse version has none.

## The Counterintuitive Frame: I Cannot Tell You Because I See the Progress

The principle has a working form for the coach. When a couple presents a recent bad fight and is distraught, the coach who sees the structure can hold the bad-fight-as-good-news frame even when the couple cannot.

"I'm working with a couple, or I, I know more than one person in a couple, and they tell me something that happened in the relationship and they're so distraught. And I'm like, oh good. I cannot tell you, because I see the progress that they're making even though it feels like shit in that moment." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "I cannot tell you" gestures at the delicacy. The coach cannot deliver the frame in the moment because the moment is too raw. The coach holds the frame internally and waits. The frame becomes accessible to the couple weeks or months later, after the wave has settled and the new capacity has come online.

The withholding is part of the coaching skill. Naming "you're on mile 24" to a couple who is in mile 24 lands as dismissive. The naming has to wait until the marathon has been finished and the couple can see for themselves.

## What This Is Not

This is not a license to dismiss bad fights as "just progress" without doing the in-the-moment work. The wave is doing its work because both partners are showing up to the wave with everything they have learned. The principle is not "ignore the fight." The principle is "do the fight work and trust that the work is metabolizing material that was not previously reachable."

This is not a claim that every wave of bad fights is progress. Some waves are the relationship ending. The diagnostic above (is something new becoming available?) is how to tell. The principle is the default frame for waves in relationships that have been doing the work. It is not the default frame for relationships that have not been doing the work, where a wave of bad fights is often simply a wave of bad fights.

This is not a principle that the fight should be celebrated in the moment. Inside the wave, the wave is hard. Both partners often question the relationship. Joe is explicit:

"I thought at the time, I was like, fuck, maybe our marriage is over. Maybe I, I fucking don't know shit about what I'm talking about. Maybe like I, I questioned everything during that." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The questioning is part of the wave. The principle does not eliminate it. The principle is what the wave looks like in retrospect, available to the couple after the wave has done its work.

## The Connection to the Larger Fight Work

This principle is one of the structural reasons [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] is a destination, not a starting point. The destination is reached through waves like this one. Each wave dismantles a layer that the previous wave couldn't reach. The couple's capacity to fight well grows through the waves, not despite them.

The wave-as-progress frame is also a corollary of [[hold-back-from-relationship-is-what-needs-healing]]. The waves often arrive because something has been being held back at a deeper layer than the previous work could surface. The wave is the surfacing.

See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] for the structural mechanism. The trauma being worked in the wave is older or deeper trauma than the wave-before-this-one worked. The progression is not linear; each wave brings up the next layer of material that the relationship is capable of meeting.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A couple who had been doing well has suddenly entered a hard period and is questioning the relationship
- The fights have escalated to a level not seen in years
- One or both partners are wondering if everything they thought they had built is real
- The same patterns that were supposedly worked are showing up again with new intensity
- Inside the fights, there is still some new capacity emerging (a new way of staying close, a new naming)
- The wave has a felt-sense of being big enough to dismantle something, not just to recycle

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the conviction of someone who has been through several waves and watched them produce something each time. The "I cannot tell you" line is a precise piece of coaching honesty: the structure is visible to him in the moment, but the couple needs the structure not to be named to them yet. The marathon metaphor is delivered warmly, almost as encouragement. The "I thought our marriage might be over" admission is offered without self-judgment as part of the principle: even Joe, with the framework, has the inside-the-wave experience of questioning everything.
