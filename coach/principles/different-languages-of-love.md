# We Often Cannot See the Love Coming At Us (We're Listening on the Wrong Channel)

## The Principle

A person's perception of how much love is present in their life is filtered by their imprinted definition of love. When love arrives in a form that does not match the imprint, the person often does not recognize it as love at all. They report feeling loveless or lacking in love. What is actually happening is that love is being broadcast on one channel and they are listening on another.

The corollary: chasing the form that matches the imprint usually produces something that is not really love (fear-management, control, caretaking dressed as love), while the real love that is already around the person goes unmetabolized.

"Different languages of love." -- Joe Hudson, How Love Gets Confused

"We're listening on the wrong channel." -- Brett Kistler, How Love Gets Confused

## How This Functions

The imprint installs a specific set of behaviors as the recognizable shape of love. A direct, honest sentence ("we know you're lying and we'd like you better if you didn't") qualifies as love only if the imprint can read directness-with-care as love. If the imprint says love is nice and indirect, the same sentence reads as cruelty.

The same dynamic works in reverse with the imprinted form. A person who was loved through being criticized may experience criticism from a partner as evidence of love — the imprint matches and the body responds with the felt sense of being loved — even though the criticism is doing actual harm. The match is not evidence of the love being real. It is evidence that the imprint and the behavior are the same shape.

The cumulative effect over a lifetime is that people often experience their lives as more loveless than they are. Love is happening in forms the imprint cannot read. The unread forms are still doing their work in some respects — the person is still benefiting from the actual care being offered — but the person does not get the felt experience of being loved, because the felt experience requires recognition.

## The Example: The Direct Woman

Joe describes a moment with one of his clients and a person the client was working with. The other person was describing the difficulties she was having with the client, and kept apologizing: "I know that sounds harsh."

"And I kept on saying, 'no, that sounds like compassion.' Now, my client couldn't hear it because of their version of what love is. And she, this woman I was talking to, she was in a situation where so many people in the world had told her that's not what love is. But she was just very direct, very straightforward, and very clear about how to be with somebody. And it was deeply compassionate. It wasn't refined, it wasn't skillful, but there was compassion behind it. And that's the same thing that happens is that we have this idea of what love should be and therefore we can't even see the love that's coming at us." -- Joe Hudson, How Love Gets Confused

Two failures of recognition in the example, on opposite sides:

1. The client could not recognize the directness as love because their imprint did not include directness as a love-shape.
2. The direct woman had also been trained to doubt her own delivery because the surrounding culture had told her directness was not love. She was apologizing for the very behavior that was carrying her care.

Both people were missing the love that was happening. The Joe move in the example is to name it out loud — "no, that sounds like compassion" — for both parties at once, so each can begin to see what the other has been doing.

## The Cost of the Mismatch

The cost is double:

- The person does not feel loved when they actually are. They chase a form of love that matches the imprint and find only its surrogates.
- The person who is loving them feels unloved in return, because their love is not being received. Over time the love withers from the rejection.

This shows up in long marriages where one partner says "she never tells me she loves me" and the partner is showing love through actions the other does not register. It shows up in workplaces where a leader's care is delivered through directness and the team experiences it as harshness. It shows up in friendships where the friend who shows up reliably in crisis is not registered as the loving friend because the friend who says effusive things is the one who matches the cultural imprint.

## What the Coaching Move Is

When a person reports feeling alone, unloved, or starved of love, one of the first inquiries is whether love is present and going unread. Not as an attempt to talk them out of their experience, but as an honest question: who in your life is loving you in a form you might be missing?

The questions:
- Who shows up for you reliably, even if they don't talk about it?
- Who tells you the hard truth when no one else will?
- Who keeps inviting you to things even when you've declined many times?
- Who pays attention to what you need without you having to ask?
- Whose presence feels easeful even though nothing dramatic is happening?

The answers often surface love that has been operating outside the imprint's channel. Naming it allows the person to begin receiving it. Receiving it is the work; the naming is the door.

## The Symmetric Application: Who Are You Loving in Forms They Cannot See?

The same mismatch operates in the giving direction. A person who loves through directness may be giving care that a particular other person cannot receive because that other person's imprint reads directness as cruelty. A person who loves through reliability may be giving care that someone whose imprint requires effusive demonstration cannot register.

The work here is twofold:

1. Recognize that the form of love being given may not be the form the receiver can take in. This is information, not a verdict on either side.
2. Sometimes expand the channels available, so love can be transmitted in more forms. This is not about abandoning your authentic mode of loving. It is about adding a few more languages so the love can land.

The asymmetric version — giving love in your channel and expecting the other person to learn your language — is sometimes appropriate. Often it is appropriate. But knowing that the gap exists is what allows it to be navigated consciously rather than producing endless mutual hurt.

## Connection to Love's Felt Ubiquity

When the imprint loosens and the person becomes able to recognize love in more forms, an interesting thing happens to the felt experience of love. It begins to seem ubiquitous — present in many more places than the person had been able to see.

"The experience is, when you actually find the real love, the experience is that's everywhere all the time. And it's like the — it almost feels like it's creating the entire reality. It's an amazing thing when you're like, oh right, it's that — it's the thing that's animating everything on some level, is the experience. It's a felt experience of it. And it's like, wait, how did I ever chase this when it's just there?" -- Joe Hudson, How Love Gets Confused

The shift is not that more love arrived. The shift is that the channels widened. The same world that previously seemed mostly loveless now seems mostly loving. The change is in the perception, and the perception was being narrowed by the imprint.

This is the same mechanism as [[negative-bias-of-trauma-imprinting]] applied to love specifically. The imprint filtered the available evidence to confirm itself. Removing the filter does not change the underlying world. It changes what the person can see in the world.

## When Someone Experiences This

When a person says "I just don't feel loved" while surrounded by people who appear to be loving them — the channels mismatch.

When a person reports a chronic sense of loneliness despite a full social life — they may be unable to register the connections they have because the connections do not match the form they were taught to expect.

When a person rejects offers of help or care and then complains that no one helps them — the offers were happening in a form the person could not metabolize.

When a person reads a partner's actions as hostile or distant while the partner is reporting being attentive and caring — both descriptions may be accurate. The actions are caring; the imprint reads them as something else.

When a person works the love-imprint material and reports a sudden expansion in how much love they perceive in their daily life — the channels have widened.
