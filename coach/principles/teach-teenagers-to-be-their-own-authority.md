# Teach Teenagers to Be Their Own Authority

## The Principle

The parenting work changes shape with age. When children are young, the parent is the authority and should be — teaching them to sleep, to eat, to be with their emotions. As children grow, the work shifts. The main thing to teach a teenager is how to be their own authority. The parent's job becomes not to be the third opinion their child checks before doing something, but to support the child in checking with themselves first.

The structure: a young child has not yet developed the internal mechanisms to know what is right for them. They need external authority to scaffold their development. A teenager has the developing capacity. If the parent continues to be the external authority, the parent prevents the development of the internal authority. The teenager learns to seek the parent's approval rather than to listen to themselves.

By the time the child is 17 or 18, they need to be in charge of their choices. They get to make mistakes. They get to call in advice when they want it, including from the parent, but as one source among many that they evaluate against their own felt sense.

"As they get older, the main thing you want to do is teach them how to be their own authority." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## The No-Compliments Move

A specific practice Joe describes: he largely stopped giving compliments to his daughters as they got older, because he did not want to install himself as the third party in their internal experience.

The mechanism: when a child finishes their homework and a parent says "good job," the child learns to do homework for the parent's approval. The parent has become part of the feedback loop the child uses to decide whether what they did was worth doing. The next time, the child is partly working to earn the parent's "good job" — even when the parent is not in the room, the parent's voice has been installed.

Joe's alternative: instead of evaluating, notice and reflect what the child is already feeling. "I see how excited you are." The child's own felt response is what gets reflected back, not the parent's evaluation of the action. The child's own response becomes the trusted source.

"I often just didn't even give compliments to kids. There's some good research on this. So with the kids, kids, they get their homework done and I'm not like, 'good job.' I see that they're excited. I'm like, 'oh, I see how excited you are.' I'll be excited with them because I don't want them to do their homework because I said to them, 'that's a good job.' I don't want to be their third. I want them to learn to listen to themselves." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The result Joe reports: his daughters do their work without any nagging because the felt sense of doing the work is what they are oriented to, not the parent's reaction. The motivation is internal.

The cost Joe also reports: at one point, one of his daughters said, "I've gotten straight A's and you've never congratulated me. What the fuck?" The non-compliment approach can be felt by the child as withholding. The corrective: at certain moments, the parent can name the pride and the appreciation explicitly, while still not making the child's primary feedback loop run through the parent.

"I told her how proud I was of her and how I realized the work that was put into it. But it's so important to me. So maybe I overcorrected there, but it was so important for me to have them be able to listen to themselves and not tell them what to do." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The principle is not "never affirm your child." The principle is: do not install yourself as the third party in their internal evaluation. There is room for direct acknowledgment of pride and care. The risk is in the habitual, automatic praise that subtly reorients the child's motivation away from themselves.

## The Move Applies From Earliest Childhood, Not Only Teens

A clarification surfaced in the Nathan Baschez interview: the reflection-instead-of-evaluation move is not reserved for teenagers. It is the family's operating practice from the kid's earliest expressive moments. Tara introduced the move when the kids were small; it became one of the structural commitments of how Joe and Tara raised the daughters across the developmental span.

"And it took weird things to do. And this was Tara who first introduced the idea of — so for instance, if they do a great thing, we don't say good job. We'll say, oh, how does that feel? Oh, I see that that makes you feel great inside. That's cool. Constantly reflecting back to them their internal state so that they listen." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The framing is precise: the move is teaching the kid to listen — to themselves. "So that they listen" is the operative phrase. The reflection-of-internal-state is the felt template the kid is being given for the developmental task of self-listening. The kid who has thousands of repetitions of "I see how excited you are" eventually has the internal apparatus to notice their own excitement without external mirroring. The adult who can do this is the adult who can self-attune; the adult who cannot is the adult who is still looking outward for the felt confirmation of what they feel.

See [[have-them-receive-your-listening]] for the analogous adult coaching move that uses the same mechanism. The "how does that feel?" question is the early-childhood form of teaching self-listening; the [[have-them-receive-your-listening]] move is the adult-coaching form; both are deploying the same mechanism — using the parent or coach's reflection as the felt template for the receiver's self-attunement.

The broader implication: the work of supporting another person's internal authority is the same work whether the other person is 2, 17, or 47. The reflection-instead-of-evaluation move is the operational form. The teenage-version names the developmental stakes; the early-childhood version names the foundational installation; the adult-coaching version names how to repair what was not installed. The same mechanism, deployed across the developmental arc.

## The Inverse Problem: When the Child Needs You to Tell Them What to Do

The same principle has an inverse failure mode. A parent so committed to not telling the child what to do can fail the child when the child legitimately needs instruction.

Joe describes this with his daughter Esme, who wanted to do a project but it was not getting done. Joe's first instinct was to back off — he did not want to support her if the project was not happening. Esme then asked directly: "What I need you to do is just tell me exactly what to do." She had never done this before. She had no template. She needed instruction.

Joe's response: surprise that this was what she needed, then full engagement with the instruction. They walked through the pipeline together step by step. Joe gave feedback on the email. He walked through who to send it to, how to send it, what to say. After the first cycle, Esme was running the process on her own.

"She literally needed me, and I was completely, I'm like, 'I don't want to tell you how to do it, I want you to be your own authority, right?' And she was like, 'the authority in me says this is what I want, this is what I want.'" -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority? (paraphrasing the moment)

The principle in this version: respect the child's authority by listening to what they are actually asking for. Sometimes what they are asking for is instruction. The work is not to refuse to instruct — that is the opposite extreme — but to follow the child's asking. Esme's asking is itself the authority in operation. The parent who refuses to instruct when asked is overriding the child's authority just as much as the parent who instructs without being asked.

See [[esme-step-by-step-project]] for the full example.

## The Two Failure Modes

The principle has two opposite failure modes parents can fall into.

**Failure 1: Continuing to be the authority too long.** The parent treats the 17-year-old like the 7-year-old. The parent gives directions, evaluates choices, provides constant feedback. The child either rebels (the only way to develop internal authority is to fight the external authority) or capitulates (the child learns to please rather than to listen). Either response prevents the internal authority from developing in a clean form.

**Failure 2: Withholding instruction even when asked.** The parent so commits to not being the authority that they refuse to offer guidance even when the child is explicitly asking. The child interprets this as abandonment or as not being taken seriously. The internal authority does not get to develop through legitimate request-and-response with the parent.

The middle path: the parent withholds default authority assertions, while remaining available for what the child actually requests, and reflects the child's own felt sense back to them rather than installing the parent's evaluation as the criterion.

## The Symmetry With Teacher-Student Work

This principle is the parenting version of [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] and [[community-over-teacher]]. The structural problem is the same: an authority figure who continues to function as the source of evaluation prevents the development of the other party's own evaluative capacity.

The corrective is the same: point the authority back to the person. Reflect what they already know rather than supplying what they should know. Be available when they request you. Resist the urge to provide certainty when they could be developing their own.

A teenager raised this way arrives at the same place a healthy coaching client arrives: capable of seeking good advice, choosing among sources, integrating feedback, but oriented to their own felt sense as the final ground.

"That's what I watch my girls do as they walk around and they look for good advice. Which was always my hope because I was so ignorant that way because I had such a crappy relationship with authority. I thought all authority was bad. So I never got really great advice through my life. And so I'm really happy that they have that capacity to do that. And at the same time, know what's right for them." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The result is not anti-authority. It is the capacity to use authority well: seek it when useful, evaluate it against your own knowing, integrate what serves, leave what does not.

## What the Inverse Looks Like in Coaching

A common coaching topic: a parent whose teenager is rebelling, or who has stopped communicating, or who seems to have no internal motivation. The principle suggests where to look. Is the parent still operating as the authority in places where the teenager has the capacity to be their own authority? Is the teenager rebelling because rebellion is the only way to develop the internal voice the parent has not made room for? Is the teenager pleasing because pleasing has become the way to keep the parent calm?

The work is often on the parent's letting go of the authority role, while staying available as a resource when the teenager seeks them.

A complementary topic: an adult who reports having had no internal compass growing up, who is now working in their 30s or 40s to learn to listen to themselves. Often the story includes parents who continued to be the authority too long, or whose praise/disapproval shaped the child's choices into adulthood. The healing work has structural parallels to the work Joe is doing as a parent — installing the internal authority that did not get to develop earlier.

The other shape this can take in adulthood: an authority issue installed by a dysfunctional childhood authority figure that the person did not get past. Joe's own arc is one version. His relationship with his father was non-functional, and the consequence was an internal authority issue that he ran across all of life:

"That relationship was not functional at all. So I went through life not trusting any authority. The idea of having mentorship, I couldn't even conceive of it until I was well into my 30s that somebody would mentor me. So I lost that. With teachers, I just didn't trust any teacher. I could read the book, but I wouldn't actually go and hang out with any kind of spiritual teacher because I couldn't trust them." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The cost in Joe's case was decades of inaccessibility to mentorship, decades of reinventing wheels, and a near-miss with one of his most important teachers (see [[joe-and-adyashanti-rebellion]]). The benefit was the experimental capacity that built this work. The point is that the cost and the benefit both compound from the same imprint, and the parenting work is partly about choosing what to install knowing both will come.

This is also why the teenager-version of the work matters so much. A teenager who is helped to install their own internal authority — neither rebelling against parental authority nor capitulating to it — is a teenager who arrives at adulthood with the capacity to engage authorities (teachers, mentors, bosses, partners) as humans rather than as figures the rebellion or approval-seeking patterns automatically activate around. See [[authority-issues]] and [[the-four-authority-positions]] for the adult patterns this parenting work is upstream of.

## Tone

Joe describes this with the warmth of a parent who has experimented, made mistakes, watched the results, and adjusted. He is not prescriptive. He names that the no-compliments approach can overcorrect. He names the moment his daughter called him on it. The principle is held provisionally — the right amount of authority withdrawal depends on the child and the moment. The orientation is toward installing the child's internal authority, with the recognition that this is an ongoing calibration, not a fixed rule.
