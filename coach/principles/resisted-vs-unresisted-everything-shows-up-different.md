# Resisted vs. Unresisted: Everything Shows Up Differently Depending on How It's Met

## The Principle

Anything that is being resisted shows up differently than the same thing being received. This is true of conversations, ideas, requests, people -- and emotions are no exception. The same emotion met with resistance and met with welcoming is not the same emotional experience. The body of it is different, the duration of it is different, the available signal in it is different.

This is not a metaphor or a frame. It is a literal claim about how experience changes based on the receiving end of it.

## The Pitch Metaphor

The clearest illustration uses a domain everyone has experienced: trying to be heard.

"I'm going and talking to someone, let's say I'm trying to pitch somebody on raising $40 million for my company as an example. And the person is resisting me the whole way. I'm going to show up differently than if the person I'm pitching is not resisting. They're like open and they're curious and I'm going to show up differently. So we all, everything shows up differently when it's resisted and when it's not resisted." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Inquiry

The same pitch, the same words, the same person delivering it, lands as two different events depending on whether the receiver is open or closed. The pitch's content is constant; the pitch's experience is not. The variable is the reception.

The corollary, which is the structurally important move: emotions work the same way. The same anger arriving in a body that is bracing against it is not the same anger arriving in a body that is welcoming it. The anger doesn't change identity; the experience of it transforms.

"Emotions are no different. Emotions show up differently when they're resisted and when they're not resisted." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Inquiry

## Why This Matters

The principle is the mechanism that makes [[emotional-inquiry]] work. Without it, the practice looks like wishful thinking ("if I just welcome my fear it will go away?"). With it, the practice is a description of how the system actually functions: change the reception, change the experience.

It also disposes of the most common objection: "but the emotion is still there, what good does welcoming it do?" The principle's answer is that the emotion you welcome is not the same emotion you resist. The resisted version is the one that destroys things, lasts forever, freezes action. The unresisted version is the one that moves through, carries signal, and leaves the body when it is done. See [[kinked-emotions]] for the named form of the difference.

## What the Unresisted Version Looks Like

When emotions are met without resistance, they tend to:

- Become more comfortable. Not pleasant in every case, but tolerable -- often surprisingly so.
- Become something one can invite. The body stops bracing for them.
- Become appreciated. The savor that was always there but obscured by struggle becomes accessible. See [[each-emotion-has-its-own-positive-flavor]].
- Become readable as signal. The information the emotion was carrying is no longer drowned by noise. See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]].
- Move on their own timeline rather than at the rate effort can push them.

"It shows up differently. And when it shows up differently, as it turns out, usually more comfortable, usually something you can invite and welcome, something that you start appreciating, something that you can start seeing the signal through the noise of those emotions. And so it allows us to stop avoiding emotion." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Inquiry

The "signal through the noise" phrase carries weight. The unresisted emotion has both signal and noise. The resisted emotion has so much noise (resistance, secondary feelings about feeling, attempts to suppress) that the signal cannot be heard. Drop the resistance and the signal becomes audible.

## The Generalization

The principle does not stop at emotions. It applies to anything that meets a receiver:

- A child's behavior shows up differently to a parent who is open vs. one who is bracing.
- A piece of feedback shows up differently to a person who can hear it vs. one defending against it.
- A piece of news shows up differently to a mind that is curious vs. one that is fighting it.
- A sensation in the body shows up differently to attention that welcomes it vs. attention that wants it gone.

The same is true going the other direction: how a person shows up to others is shaped by whether they are being met with resistance or reception. The fundraiser meeting open investors makes a different pitch than the same fundraiser meeting closed ones, even with the same intent.

This makes the principle a general truth about experience, not a special claim about emotions. Emotions are one application. The mechanism is universal.

## The Test for Whether the Principle Has Landed

The internal test: does the question "how am I meeting this?" become as natural as "what is this?" If yes, the principle is operating. If "what is this?" still dominates, the orientation is still on the object-side, and the lever of how-it-is-being-met is going unused.

See [[approach-to-emotion-matters-as-much-as-emotion]] for the specific application of this principle to the relationship with one's own emotions. The two-sided principle is the operational form; this principle is the underlying mechanism.

## Tone

Plain, explanatory, slightly emphatic on "everything." Joe delivers this as a piece of straightforward observation -- the kind of thing that becomes obvious once stated. The pitch example is given lightly. The teaching weight lands in the move from "everything shows up differently" to "emotions are no different."
