# A Leader's Wants Are the Organization's Wants

## The Principle

A leader's individual wants and needs are not private. They are organizational data. The leader's resentment, discomfort, satisfaction, and desire are signals about what the organization itself wants and needs. Suppressing them suppresses information the organization requires to function.

The reframe inverts the usual model. The leader's wants are typically treated as personal preferences that must be subordinated to organizational requirements. The actual relationship is the reverse: the leader is positioned in the organization in a way that makes their internal state a sampling of the organization's state. Suppressing the sampling does not make the organization not want the thing. It just removes the leader's ability to see what the organization wants.

"I've come to learn that my wants and needs are the wants and needs of the organization. So I feel that it's my responsibility to express them, whereas before I pushed them down." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

## Why This Is True

The leader's position in the organization aggregates signals from across the system. The leader hears from many functions, sees patterns across teams, and absorbs the cumulative felt sense of how the organization is doing. The leader's body is processing organizational data whether the leader recognizes it or not.

A want that arises in the leader is often a want that has been arising across the organization. The leader is the first or most articulate place it surfaces because of position. Suppressing it as "just my preference" misreads the signal. The leader is the antenna.

This is also true downward. A team member's wants are often the team's wants. The leader's job is to surface these and to model the surfacing of their own.

"When my team is expressing their wants and needs, and sometimes I have to pull it out of them, I literally validate them by saying, hey, if you are feeling a certain way, it's because your team or your organization feels that way. And you owe it not only to yourself, but to them to say it out loud." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

## The Reframe of Duty

The standard frame: stating personal wants at work is self-indulgent or unprofessional. The reframe: stating personal wants at work is a duty. The leader who hides their wants is failing the team.

This inverts the morality of want-suppression. Most leaders have been trained that suppressing wants is mature, responsible, and team-oriented. The actual effect of the suppression is the opposite: it deprives the team of information they need, generates politics where information should flow, and breeds resentment in the leader.

See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] for the mechanism by which suppressed wants surface sideways. The principle here is the moral inversion: stating wants is not selfishness; it is service.

## The Bodily Diagnostic Implication

The principle extends to bodily discomfort. If something in the leader's system is resistant or in friction, that is information about a thing the organization needs to address. The body is not generating noise. The body is reading the field.

"How cool is it that your body shines a light on the very thing that you need to deal with? So if there's discomfort in your system, if you literally don't feel good about something, if there's resistance or friction in you, that's a thing we need to go deal with. Let's go unlock that." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, quoting an L12 colleague, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The leader's discomfort is the organization's diagnostic surfacing. Ignoring it is not stoicism. Ignoring it is closing the diagnostic.

See [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]] for the related principle that anxiety is information about an unspoken want or boundary.

## The Coaching Reframe

When a leader presents a want and asks whether to express it, the question reveals the suppression frame is still operating. The reframe is not "should you express this want?" but "what would it take to recognize that not expressing it is depriving your team?"

The shift is from permission-seeking ("am I allowed to want this?") to responsibility-taking ("the team needs to know what I want, and I have been withholding it").

## What This Is Not

This is not license for the leader to dump all internal states onto the team. The principle is that wants are organizational signals. The principle is not that every fleeting emotion is an organizational mandate. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] and the discipline of releasing emotions before processing them with others.

This is also not "the leader's preferences override democratic input." See [[wants-vs-partiality]]. Owning a want is not the same as imposing it. The expression of the want opens a conversation; it does not close one.

This is also not "leadership wants are objectively right." The principle is that the wants are signals worth surfacing. The signals are then interpreted, debated, and integrated with other signals. The leader who treats their own wants as final has misread the principle.

## The Cascading Permission

When the leader models naming wants as duty, the team learns it is permitted. The naming becomes contagious. The organization develops the capacity to surface what it actually wants rather than what people think they should want.

"That gives permission for those around you that you care about to articulate their wants and needs. And it's one of the best ways to drive a true meaningful relationship with someone, whether it's a work colleague or for a friend or family member." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The cascade is the principle scaling. The leader's naming makes the team's naming possible. The team's naming makes the organization's naming possible. The organization develops a vocabulary for wants where previously there was only the vocabulary of strategy and goals.

## When to Reach for This

- A leader is sitting on a want and treating it as personal preference.
- A team member is suppressing a want and the suppression is generating dysfunction.
- An organization has high politics and low directness.
- A leader is feeling resentment toward their team and the resentment is being held as a personal failing.
- A leader is in a coaching session asking whether to bring up something that bothers them.

## When Not to Reach for This

- When the want is actually a fleeting emotion that has not been processed. Process first; then check whether the want is still there.
- When the want is a craving rather than a want. See [[wanting-vs-craving]]. Cravings dressed as organizational signals create dysfunction.
- When the suppression has a structural basis the leader is trying to override. If the role does not have the authority to enact the want, the move may be to surface the structural issue rather than to enact the want unilaterally.

## Tone

Direct and serious. The reframe is a significant inversion of how most leaders have been trained. Delivered lightly, the reframe lands as a tip. Delivered with weight, the reframe lands as the structural shift it is.
