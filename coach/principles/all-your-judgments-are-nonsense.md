# All Your Judgments Are Nonsense

## The Principle

Every judgment is nonsense in one of exactly two ways. Either the judgment itself is not true (the claim about reality the judgment is making does not hold up under examination), or the judgment is true but the consequences attached to it are not true (the judgment is accurate as a claim but the catastrophic implications loaded onto it are false).

This is the structural hack for working with one's own judgments. The two-way analysis exhausts the space. There is no third flavor where the judgment is true and the consequences are also true and the judgment is therefore deserving of the suffering it produces.

"All of your judgments are like this. All of your judgments are nonsense on some level. They're either nonsense in they're not true, or they're nonsense in the fact that they're true, but the consequences are, the consequences that you think they are are not true." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

## The Two Flavors

**Flavor one: the judgment is not true.** This is the standard cognitive reframe. The person believes "I'm not as good as I thought I would be" or "I'm going to fuck this up." Examination shows the belief is not supported. The person is in fact at least as good as they thought. The fuck-up is not imminent. The judgment dissolves under inspection.

**Flavor two: the judgment is true, but the consequences are not.** This is the more useful and less obvious flavor. The person believes "I won't be the top of my class in this." Examination shows that yes, in fact, they will not be the top of their class. The judgment is accurate. But the consequences the person has attached to the judgment ("therefore I cannot do this," "therefore I am worthless," "therefore there is no point") do not follow. The judgment is true and entirely consequence-free in any meaningful sense.

The second flavor is harder to see because the truth of the judgment feels like it validates the suffering. The person assumes that if the judgment is true, the suffering is justified. The principle says no — even if the judgment is true, the consequences-claim attached to it almost always is not, and the consequences-claim is what produces the suffering.

## The Two Flavors in the Source Session

A client presented with stuckness around creating what she wanted in her life. Underneath: the judgment "I'm not going to be as good as I thought I was going to be. I'm not as good as I thought I would be." Joe's move:

"You're not the top of your class in this. Like, no, there's no possible way, whatever it is, the project you're going to want to do next, you won't be the top." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

Joe accepted the judgment as true. The client will not be the top. He did not argue with the content. He flipped the consequences-claim. The implicit consequence the client was loading onto the judgment was "and therefore I cannot move forward." Joe demonstrated that the judgment can be true and the consequence does not have to follow.

"You're, you won't be the top. Sit, sit. Feel. Yeah." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The instruction is: hold the judgment as true and feel it. Notice that the catastrophic consequence does not arrive. The world does not end. The work can still happen. The contribution can still be amazing. The not-being-the-top has nothing to do with whether the work is worth doing.

"It doesn't mean that what you're going to contribute isn't amazing. So the thing that I'm doing in that moment is really talking to the judgment." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

## The Hack: Talk to the Judgment

The principle yields an operational move: when a judgment is running, talk to it. Run the two-flavor test. Ask: is this judgment actually true? If not, the first flavor applies; the judgment dissolves. If yes, ask: and so what? What does that have to do with anything? The consequence-claim almost always evaporates under direct examination.

"What I'm doing is I am turning the whole idea of the judgment around. So oftentimes the way a person allows themselves to judge themselves is they say, it's gonna come back. The other shoe is gonna fall. I'm not gonna be the best. And it's true, but what does that have to do with anything?" -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The "what does that have to do with anything?" is the lever for flavor two. The person has been treating the judgment-plus-consequence as a single package. The principle breaks the package into two pieces. The judgment can stay as true. The consequence can be dropped because it was a free-floating attachment, not a logical entailment.

## Why This Is Different From "Reframing"

Standard cognitive reframing tries to argue judgments out of existence. The judger believes "I'm not good enough." The reframer suggests "you are good enough." The argument can succeed at the level of the head and fail at the level of the body — the person agrees intellectually but the felt sense of not-good-enough persists.

The two-flavor principle does not require agreement at the level of the content. The judgment can be true. The work happens at the consequences-claim level. The person does not need to convince themselves of anything they do not believe. They need only notice that the consequences they have been treating as inevitable are not in fact attached to the judgment by anything other than habit.

This makes the principle more robust against the smart client. A smart client can defeat a "you are good enough" reframe by producing evidence. They cannot as easily defeat "okay, you're not, and so what?" because there is no claim about reality to contradict. The argument has moved to a different ground that the body can actually walk on.

## The Universal Scope

The principle's claim is universal: ALL judgments are nonsense in one of these two ways. Not most. Not some. All. This is a strong claim and Joe makes it as such because the strength of the claim is part of the work. Once the principle is held universally, every judgment that arises has a known structure. The question is not "is this judgment one of the nonsense ones?" The question is "which kind of nonsense is this one?"

The universality also blocks the escape route where the person claims their particular judgment is the special exception. There are no special exceptions. Every judgment fits one of the two slots. The work is identifying which slot, not arguing whether the slot applies.

## When This Principle Is Useful

- When the client is running a self-judgment that they treat as inarguable
- When the client agrees the judgment may be true but cannot move past it
- When the client has tried to argue themselves out of the judgment and the argument has failed
- When the consequences-claim attached to the judgment is producing the suffering (not the judgment itself)
- When the client needs a portable hack they can run on their own judgments later
- When the work has been at the level of the judgment's content and is not landing — the consequences-claim is where the work belongs

## The In-Session Form

The operational form in a live session:

1. Hear the judgment ("I'm not going to be as good as I thought").
2. Accept the judgment as possibly true. Do not argue the content.
3. Surface the consequence the client is loading onto the judgment ("and therefore... what?").
4. Examine the consequence directly. Does it follow? What does the judgment actually have to do with the implied outcome?
5. Demonstrate that the judgment can be held as true while the consequence is dropped. The body feels the relief of the dropped weight without having to deny the content.

This is the move Joe executes in the source session. The client's judgment ("I'm not going to be as good as I thought") stays intact. The consequence ("and therefore I am stuck and cannot move forward") gets disconnected. The relief is visible on her face within seconds. The judgment did not need to be argued out of existence. It needed to be separated from the consequence it had been wedded to.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not a license to deny obvious facts. The principle does not say judgments are always false. It says they are always nonsense in one of two ways, one of which is that they are TRUE but consequence-free.

It is not a way to bypass actual problems. If the consequence really does follow from the judgment (you took the wrong action and now there is a real-world result to handle), the principle does not apply at the consequences level — the consequence is real and needs to be dealt with. The principle applies to the consequences-claims the mind loads onto judgments that do not actually carry those consequences.

It is not a denial of standards. A person can have standards, want to improve, hold accountability, without using judgment as the engine. The principle is about the suffering layer of judgment, not about the discernment layer. See [[judgment-vs-discernment]] for the related distinction.

## Tone

Plain and slightly dismissive of the judgment's apparent authority. The principle is delivered as a structural fact, not as a sympathetic reframe. The dismissiveness is functional: the judgment has been treated with too much weight; the principle's tone restores proportion. "What does that have to do with anything?" lands because it sounds like a real question, and once asked, the answer is genuinely "not much."
