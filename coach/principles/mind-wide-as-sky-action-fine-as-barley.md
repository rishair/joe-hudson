# Mind Wide as the Sky, Action Fine as Barley Flour

## The Principle

A Tibetan Buddhist teaching that names the integrated end state of deep self-inquiry: the mind becomes able to hold every perspective without identification, and the action becomes extremely precise — narrowed by what is felt rather than by what is thought.

"There's this phrase that I only learned recently, and it's a Tibetan Buddhist phrase, and it says, 'mind is wide as the sky, action is fine as barley flour.'" -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

Barley flour is very fine. The image is precise: the mind is vast and open; the action that emerges from it is narrow and exact.

## What It Means

The principle resolves a tension that appears at the depth of [[questioning-assumptions]] and similar self-inquiry practices. At a certain depth, the practitioner can see every perspective as having some truth and no perspective as having the whole truth. The mind becomes wide as the sky. There is no right and no wrong at the level of thought.

This produces a worry: if there is no right and wrong, how do I act? What guides me? Won't I be paralyzed, or amoral, or unable to make a decision?

The Tibetan principle answers: when the thinking-layer stops generating the rules, the body and the felt sense start. And what the body generates is much more precise than what thought generated.

"Your mind can see everything, can see all the perspectives, and it's as wide as the sky. It's vast. There is no truth or there's no right and wrong on that level. But the rest of your system, it is incredibly painful to act in a way that is not kind, not compassionate, not the — what we might call right, the right action." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The thought-layer holds many perspectives equally; the body-layer holds the actual cost of moving away from love, kindness, openness. The body knows that closing the heart hurts. Knowing this means the action that does not close the heart is the only available action — not because of a rule, but because the alternative is felt to be too painful.

## The Mechanism

Three things happen as the practice deepens:

1. The thoughts that previously produced morality ("this is right, that is wrong") become transparent. You can see through them. They have some truth and they don't have whole truth.
2. With the thoughts no longer running the morality, attention falls more naturally into the body. There is nothing else trustworthy enough to organize action.
3. The body's signals become legible. Closing the heart hurts. Lying produces constriction. Self-betrayal is felt as pain. These signals were always there but were overridden by thought.

The action that emerges is fine — narrow, precise — because there is only one move that does not produce that body-pain. The wide mind sees the options; the body chooses the only liveable one.

## What This Is Not

This is not a sophisticated form of nihilism. The principle does not say "nothing is true so do whatever." It says "no thought is the whole truth, and the body is a reliable guide for action."

This is not a morality of thought. There is no longer a rule like "you should be kind." Instead, there is the felt experience that not being kind produces pain. The discernment has moved layers, not vanished.

This is not amorality. Joe is explicit: "my behavior became far, far more moral, but not moral like a sense of morality, moral as in like it is painful to move away from myself, to leave my center, to do something like lie or close my heart or be self-serving in a way that didn't consider other people."

This is not "I know what is right for you." The principle is first-person. Each system has its own felt cost of leaving itself. The practice produces clarity about one's own action, not authority over anyone else's.

## How This Resolves the Right-and-Wrong Worry

When [[questioning-assumptions]] is practiced deeply, the destabilizing question arises: how do I make decisions if everything can be questioned? The principle is the structural answer.

The right-and-wrong of thought ("this person is wrong, this action is right") goes away. The right-and-wrong of action ("there is only one move I can make") gets more commanding, not less.

"On one level, right and wrong is completely gone in the head, but the action is very clearly just — it's very fine because you have no choice but to act in a way that is compassionate." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The freedom and the precision arrive together. The freedom is at the level of thought (nothing to defend, no perspective to maintain). The precision is at the level of action (there is one move, and it is the move that does not betray the body).

## Why This Is Not Available Without the Practice

The principle describes an end state, not an entry condition. Trying to act from "mind wide as the sky" without having done the inquiry produces a fake openness — affecting equanimity while still being controlled by unexamined assumptions. The mind being actually wide requires that the assumptions have been seen through, repeatedly, over time.

Trying to "let the body guide me" before the thought-layer has been transparent produces noise. The body is not yet legible because it is still being overridden by thought-rules. The principle requires the inquiry to have done its work.

This is why the principle appears at the depth of the practice rather than at the beginning. It is what is available on the far side of the [[disorienting-middle-of-self-inquiry]], not before.

## Use in Coaching

When a person worries that questioning their assumptions will leave them paralyzed, amoral, or unable to act, the principle is the answer. The worry assumes that morality lives in the head and that removing thought-rules removes morality. The principle reframes: morality moves to the body, and what was diffuse becomes precise.

The coaching move is not to argue the worry away but to point at the structural shift the practice produces. The person is worried about a state ("no rules") that is not actually the destination ("very fine action with no rule-defense needed"). Naming the principle lets the person continue the inquiry without the worry pulling them back.

The coach can also use the principle as a diagnostic. If a person who claims to be "post-morality" or "beyond right and wrong" is acting in ways that betray love, kindness, or others, the principle is being misused. The mind wide as the sky does not produce a person who closes their heart. It produces a person for whom closing the heart is no longer available because the cost is too felt.

## Related Framings

This is the integrated form of several adjacent teachings:

- [[Impartiality]]: the wide-mind capacity to hold every perspective with equal welcome.
- [[Empathy]]: the body-felt sense of another's experience that informs the precise action.
- [[Judgment-vs-discernment]]: judgment lives in the head; discernment lives in the body.
- [[Humility]]: structurally, the absence of the defended self that the inquiry has dissolved.
- [[Comparative-mind]]: what the practice releases at the thought level.

The principle is the umbrella under which these gather as the practice matures.

## Joe's Own Description of the End State

"As the thoughts dissipated, as I couldn't believe them anymore, the reality became so apparent. All of a sudden, it is painful to move away from myself, to leave my center." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The thoughts dissipating did not produce a flat or unmoored state. It produced the apparent reality of the body's actual signals. The morality was always there. It had been covered by thought.

## Tone

Joe carries this teaching with a certain reverence — he attributes it to the tradition rather than claiming it as his own. The tone when invoking it is quiet, almost still. The principle is large and does not want to be made small by enthusiasm.
