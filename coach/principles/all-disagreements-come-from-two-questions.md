# All Disagreements Come From Two Questions

## The Principle

Every disagreement, every argument, every cultural misunderstanding inside an organization (or anywhere else) is operating from one or both of two underlying questions:

1. Who's in charge?
2. How much do you love me?

The first is about authority, decision-making, structure, role clarity. The second is about being seen, recognized, valued, understood. When the surface debate is about something else (the budget, the strategy, the meeting format, the org chart), the live energy underneath is almost always one of these two.

"All disagreements, all arguments come from two questions. Who's in charge and how much do you love me? And the base of all cultural misunderstanding comes from those two things. It's people feeling unseen and it's people trying to figure out how, like, who's in charge? How do we get it done? What's the mechanism?" -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

## Why This Is the Base of Culture Work

Every list of "what makes a great culture" -- great communication, clearly defined roles, psychological safety, accountability, transparency, recognition -- can be re-read as an answer to one of these two questions. Clear roles answers who's in charge. Recognition answers how much do you love me. Great communication answers both. The list items are not wrong. They are downstream of the two underlying questions.

"Everybody was like, great communication, easily defined roles, clearly defined roles... They're not wrong by any stretch. Those are really important things. But all of those things, everything that was listed is, is really trying to answer those two core questions." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

This makes culture work tractable. Instead of running through a list of best practices, ask: in this specific organization, which of these two questions is unanswered? Where is the answer unclear? Then design the answer.

## The Decision-Making Diagnostic

The first question -- who's in charge -- has a sharp diagnostic. Walk into a typical business and ask: how do you make decisions here? Most cannot answer. The decision-making process is invisible to the people inside it, which means it is operating by default rather than by design.

"And what's fascinating to me is if I go into a typical business, and by typical I mean 95% of the businesses I've been in, and I say, okay, how do you make decisions here? They can't tell you the answer, which is fascinating when you think that all a company is really at the atomic level of a company, it's people's relationships and people's decisions." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

A company is, at the atomic level, people's relationships and people's decisions. If nobody can describe how decisions get made, the first underlying question is live everywhere. Politics expands to fill the vacuum. People are spending energy trying to figure out who's actually in charge of any given thing because no one made it explicit.

## The Meeting Diagnostic

The second question -- how much do you love me -- has its own diagnostic, and it is the felt quality of meetings. If meetings produce dread ("fuck, I have to go to a meeting"), the second question is unanswered. People are not feeling seen, recognized, or valued in the room. The meeting is the daily venue where the answer to "how much do you love me" gets reported.

The leverage runs the other way too. Move meetings from dread to neutral to good to great, and the second question starts being answered every day. The cumulative effect on the company is enormous because the question is being asked and answered hundreds of times per week across the organization.

"Most companies have a meeting culture, which is a great measurement of relationship. How good the meetings are. And they have meeting cultures where everyone's like, fuck, I have to go to a meeting. So just think about that. If that's all that a company is, is just the relationships people are having and the decisions, and all of a sudden you find a 10% better way to make decisions, or your meetings jump by, ugh, I don't want to go to the meeting, to, oh, that was a pretty good meeting, to, oh, I love my meetings. I can't wait to go to these meetings. Like, what does that do to performance of a company? It's, it's like a ridiculous multiplier." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

See [[invigorated-vs-depleted-as-functionality-measure]] for the diagnostic frame this points to.

## The Same Two Questions in Relationships and Families

The principle generalizes beyond companies. The fight in the kitchen, the recurring argument with a teenager, the breakdown in a friendship -- almost all of it traces to the same two questions. Who's in charge of this decision? Do you actually love me? Once you see the substrate, the surface content of the argument matters less. The work is to address the underlying question rather than to win the surface debate.

This is also why the same person who cannot create a great company culture often cannot create a great marriage. The substrate questions are the same. The leader who hasn't figured out how to answer them at home will struggle to answer them at work. See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]].

## How to Use the Principle in Diagnosis

When a team has chronic friction, when a couple is in a recurring fight, when a family meeting always derails, the move is to ask: which of the two questions is live underneath this? Sometimes it is both. The surface content will distract.

A few specific tells:

- **Recurring arguments about process** ("we never decide anything," "decisions get made without me," "I don't know who actually owns this") are usually the first question.
- **Recurring arguments about being heard** ("you don't listen," "my contributions aren't recognized," "I don't matter here") are usually the second.
- **Politics, gossip, and side conversations** are almost always the second question leaking through informal channels because the formal channels are not answering it. See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]].
- **Over-preparation for the boss, over-polished deliverables, deference patterns** are usually the first question warped into the answer "the person above me is in charge in ways that are not explicit." See [[spit-at-the-top-is-a-waterfall-at-the-bottom]].

Once the operative question is identified, the response can be designed for it. Vague decision processes are addressed structurally. Unmet need to be seen is addressed by [[make-everyone-feel-seen-when-things-go-wrong]] and by leaders naming their own wants so the team's wants become speakable. See [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]].

## Why the Two Questions Are Universal

The two questions are the structural form of two human universals: agency and belonging. Every person needs to know what they are in charge of and what someone else is in charge of (agency). Every person needs to know they matter to the group (belonging). Cultures that fail to answer either question produce predictable forms of suffering at scale.

The principle is therefore not arbitrary. It maps to the two things every human is asking from any social context they are inside.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is about disagreements within a shared context -- a team, a relationship, a family. It is not a universal claim about every conflict ever. Conflicts about external constraints (resource scarcity, market conditions, physics) are not reducible to the two questions. The principle is about the relational substrate of disagreements that recur inside a stable group.

It also is not a claim that every disagreement is "really" about feelings. The first question -- who's in charge -- is structural and substantive. Sometimes the structural answer needs to be designed, and the design is the work. The principle does not collapse all conflict into emotion. It points at the substrate that is live underneath when the surface content does not resolve.

## What This Means for Culture Design

If the two questions are the substrate, then culture design becomes the explicit project of answering them. Not by edict, but by structure: how decisions actually get made (the first question), and how people get seen and recognized inside the daily mechanics of the work (the second question). The structural answers are more durable than the personality-based ones. See [[structure-and-empowerment]] and [[require-an-action-at-the-end-of-every-email]] for examples of structural answers to the first question. See [[make-everyone-feel-seen-when-things-go-wrong]] for the move-form of answering the second.

Joe delivers this principle with the conviction of someone who has tested it against thousands of organizational dynamics. He attributes the quote to a source he refuses to name on the podcast ("I hate where this quote comes from. I, I, but, but I love this quote. So I'm not gonna say where it comes from"). The self-consciousness about the attribution does not soften the principle; he is clear that it is operationally true regardless of where it came from.
