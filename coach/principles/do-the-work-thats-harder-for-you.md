# Do the Work That's Harder for You (When Two Paths Are Available)

## The Principle

When a piece of coaching work has two available paths, the more productive path is almost always the one you are less drawn to. If you find yourself ready and willing to do path A, the work for you is usually path B. The appealing option is usually the one your existing patterns are already comfortable with. The unappealing option is where your growth lives.

This is not a rule against doing the appealing work. Both paths usually need to be walked. But when time and energy are limited and a choice has to be made, the path that produces the most movement is the one the system is least already practiced at.

"The one of those two things that you're most likely to do, the most productive path is the other one. So if it's appealing for you to like, oh, I'm just gonna be with their emotional state, then probably the better work for you is to do the thing, to say the thing. And if you're more likely to be like, yeah, I'll just say that thing, but to actually be with them emotionally is the hard part, then that's probably being with them emotionally is the better work for you. And just do both. Like, there's, you don't have to choose. But I think that that's a good point." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered!

## The Underlying Logic

The system that is already in place is what produced the current configuration. Whatever work the system can do easily is work the system has already been doing. More of that work will not change the configuration. It will reinforce it.

The work the system resists is the work the system has not been doing. Doing it is what changes the configuration. The resistance itself is the diagnostic that this is the unworked material.

A speaker who already says everything they think but cannot be present with another's emotion will become a louder, clearer speaker if they keep working on speaking. The relationship will not change. The relationship changes when the speaker develops the capacity to sit with what the other person is producing.

A sitter who can hold space for anyone's pain but cannot say what they need will become a more saintly sitter if they keep working on sitting. The relationship will not change. The relationship changes when the sitter learns to say the thing.

## The Two-Path Pattern Shows Up Everywhere

The principle is not specific to triggers in relationships, though that is where Joe articulates it. It applies anywhere coaching offers two adjacent moves:

- **Speak the thing vs. sit with the emotion.** The trigger-response pair. See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] and [[being-with-anothers-emotion-is-being-with-your-own-pain]]. The person who easily speaks needs to develop sitting. The person who easily sits needs to develop speaking.

- **Feel the emotion vs. understand the pattern.** The person who already understands every pattern they are in needs to feel the emotion. The person who has been feeling the emotion for years without insight needs to understand what is producing it.

- **Boundary vs. opening.** The person who easily says no needs to practice opening. The person who easily opens needs to practice the boundary. See [[boundaries-expand-as-capacity-grows]].

- **Action vs. allowing.** The person who acts compulsively needs to practice [[making-vs-allowing]]'s second half. The person who allows everything passively needs to practice action.

- **Connection vs. solitude.** The hyper-relational person needs to practice being alone. The isolated person needs to practice connection. The work is in the direction they are not already going.

## Why People Resist This Reading

Two reasons people prefer the easier path:

**1. The easier path looks like growth because it produces effort.** Doing more of what you already do produces fatigue and the felt sense of working hard. The fatigue is mistaken for growth. The actual growth-marker is not fatigue; it is the development of new capacity.

**2. The harder path looks like incompetence.** When you do the work you are bad at, you produce bad results at first. The bad results read internally as failure. The system retreats back to the work it is good at, where the results are competent. Competence is not growth.

The reframe required: the felt sense of "I am bad at this" is the felt sense of doing the work that will actually move you. The felt sense of "I am good at this" is the felt sense of doing the work that has already happened.

## How to Apply It

When coaching offers two paths:

1. **Notice which one you reach for.** The one that feels natural, the one you are explaining how you would do, the one you already have plans for.

2. **Notice which one produces resistance.** The one you have a reason to skip, the one you are dismissing as "not my thing," the one that feels uncomfortable.

3. **Do both, but weight the harder one.** Joe is explicit: this is not "skip the easy one." It is "do not let the easy one absorb all the work." The harder one is where the actual movement is.

4. **Notice when the harder one starts becoming easier.** That is the signal the work is happening. At that point a new harder path will become visible. The cycle continues. See [[development-as-corkscrew]].

## What This Is Not

This principle is not "do whatever scares you." Some things that scare you are scaring you because they are actually dangerous. The principle is specifically about two adjacent paths within a single piece of coaching work, where both are constructive and one is more well-trodden for you than the other.

It is also not a permanent assignment to the uncomfortable path. The work is to develop the capacity that was missing. Once the capacity is developed, the path is no longer the work. A new edge becomes the edge.

It also does not mean "the uncomfortable feeling is always growth." Discomfort that comes from doing work that genuinely is not yours -- absorbing other people's emotions, performing roles that violate your truth, taking on responsibility that belongs to someone else -- is not growth. It is self-abandonment. See [[losing-yourself-in-others]] and [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]].

The principle requires accurate diagnosis of which work is yours to do. Once that is clear, the harder of the two available paths is usually the productive one.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the work they have been doing for years has not produced change because it was the wrong work
- The discomfort of trying the path they have always avoided
- The surprise of finding that the resisted path is the one that actually moves things
- The relief of being given permission to stop overdoing the comfortable work
- The new edge that becomes visible once the previous edge has been worked

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person presents work they have been doing for a long time without movement
- A coach offers two paths and the person immediately commits to one
- A person says "I've already done that work" about the path that would actually move them
- A person describes their natural strength and treats it as their only available move
- A couple where each is already doing what comes naturally and the relationship is not changing

## Tone

Joe delivers this as a quiet diagnostic, not as a confrontation. The point is not to shame the person for the work they are already doing. The point is to redirect attention to where the actual movement is available. The tone is "yes, and also..." rather than "no, do this instead."
