# Channeling vs. Authorship

## The Principle

When a teacher, coach, leader, or creator owns what comes through them — claims authorship of the wisdom, takes personal credit for the work — a predictable cascade begins. Responsibility becomes obligation. Obligation becomes resentment. Resentment cannot be left because the rewards (the income, the attention, the prestige) are too attractive. The trapped resentment finds release through addiction, control, or both.

The alternative is to recognize that the work is being channeled. The teacher is not the source. The wisdom is moving through, not being generated. The teacher is an archaeologist unearthing what has always been there, in a packaging suited to the current moment. Once the work is held this way, the cascade does not begin. The teacher remains free.

"I channel— when I see the people who have done it the other way, where they get, oh, it's me, then with that, oh, it's me, there's a responsibility that becomes an obligation that turns into a resentment, but they can't leave it because the girlfriend's too sexy. It's like making them lots of money and everybody's looking at them and... And then it leads to like some crazy addiction typically and like need for control." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically with Tristan de Montebello

## The Cascade

The cascade Joe describes is specific and worth tracing step by step.

**Step 1: Authorship.** The teacher claims the work as theirs. "I figured this out. I built this. This is my teaching, my methodology, my insight." The work becomes a possession.

**Step 2: Responsibility.** Possession comes with responsibility. The teacher is now responsible for the outcomes — for keeping students engaged, for keeping the institution running, for keeping the wisdom available. Responsibility is natural; this step is not the problem yet.

**Step 3: Obligation.** Responsibility hardens into obligation when the teacher feels they cannot stop. The students are counting on them. The income depends on them. The reputation requires them. The freedom to walk away is lost. See [[obligation-as-management]] for what obligation does in any relationship.

**Step 4: Resentment.** Obligation produces resentment. The teacher is no longer doing the work for love; they are doing it because they cannot stop. The students start to feel the resentment underneath the teaching. The institution starts to feel the resentment underneath the leadership. The relationships start to corrode.

**Step 5: Inability to leave.** The teacher could resolve the resentment by stopping — but the rewards are too attractive. "The girlfriend's too sexy." The money is too good, the attention is too rich, the identity is too established. Walking away would mean losing all of it. So the teacher stays.

**Step 6: Addiction or control (often both).** The resentment has to go somewhere. It finds release through substances, through compulsive behaviors, through tightening control over students or institutions. The famous teacher who develops an addiction. The famous leader who develops a need for total control. The pattern is the same. The cascade is the cause.

## What Channeling Prevents

When the teacher recognizes channeling, the cascade does not start. Each step is preempted.

**No authorship to defend.** The wisdom is not the teacher's possession. It came through them; it could come through anyone. There is nothing to defend.

**Responsibility is held lightly.** The teacher is responsible for showing up well, for being a clean channel. They are not responsible for the wisdom itself. The wisdom is taking care of itself; they are just one delivery vehicle.

**No obligation.** The teacher could stop at any time. The work would continue without them, through someone else, in another form. The freedom to walk away is preserved.

**No resentment.** Without obligation, there is no resentment. The teaching continues because the teacher loves it. See [[teach-from-pleasure-not-mission]].

**No need for addiction or control.** The cascade does not arrive at this point. The teacher remains free, the work remains alive, the students remain met without manipulation.

This is one of the structural reasons Joe consistently points away from himself when describing his work. He is not modeling humility as a virtue. He is preserving his freedom by refusing to enter the cascade at step one.

## The Archaeologist Frame

Tristan offers a parallel image. The teacher is an archaeologist — not inventing the wisdom but unearthing it from where it has always been buried. The work is to find it, brush off the dust, and package it for the present moment.

"It feels like we're just unearthing something that already exists like an archaeologist and that we're just unearthing the truth, sharing it, packaging and sharing it in a way that can be understood in our current zeitgeist. But really it's a, we're not inventing anything. I'm just bringing this stuff back up and presenting it to people." -- Tristan de Montebello

The archaeologist does not claim credit for the artifact. The artifact was there before them. Their work is the digging, the cleaning, the contextualizing. The artifact itself is older than they are. The same is true of any wisdom that comes through teaching: it has been around for thousands of years, in different traditions, in different languages, finding different teachers in different eras to bring it forward.

Joe applies this directly to his own work. He had a moment of recognition during a conversation with masterclass students from years past, when someone said "it wasn't what— it didn't change my life, it changed generations." Joe felt the weight of that and immediately recognized: that wasn't actually his work.

"It wasn't me doing that. It was like the 20 people who had a huge influence and taught me and it was the 20 people and some of them 2,000 years old and the people who taught them and the people who taught them. And it's like, it's such this interesting thing... that the butler's for everybody, that there's a faithing that's been going on for You know, for eons, 200,000 years, people have been pausing and seeing what the fuck comes outta their mouth." -- Joe Hudson

## Why This Is Not False Humility

A natural objection: "Isn't this just performative humility? Of course the teacher built something. Saying 'it's not me' is just modesty theater."

The objection misses the point. The principle is not about how the teacher *presents* themselves. The principle is about how the teacher *holds* the work internally. The internal holding determines whether the cascade begins. A teacher who internally claims authorship while externally performing humility is still in the cascade. A teacher who internally recognizes channeling and externally takes appropriate ownership (signing their name, getting paid, accepting feedback) is not.

The diagnostic is not what the teacher says. The diagnostic is whether the teacher can walk away. A teacher who internally recognizes channeling can stop teaching tomorrow and feel relief. A teacher who internally claims authorship cannot. The trapped-ness is the symptom.

See [[own-your-narcissism]] for the related principle that points at the same dynamic from the other side. You can own that you wanted to be heard, you can own the role, you can own the income — and still recognize that the wisdom itself is not yours. Both moves are happening at once.

## What Makes the Cascade So Tempting

The reason teachers fall into the authorship trap is not malice. The reason is that authorship is reinforced by everyone around the teacher.

Early-stage teachers and entrepreneurs are particularly vulnerable. Tristan names this directly:

"It's almost like you have no choice but to pump up your chest and say, what I'm doing is so important and so amazing and I'm changing lives. And you can, you can get caught into that so easily." -- Tristan de Montebello

The marketing requires it. The funding requires it. The identity-building of the early phase requires it. The teacher gets pulled into authorship without consciously choosing it. Each step of the cascade then follows naturally because the first step was rewarded.

The reversal of the cascade requires conscious work. It requires the teacher to internally pull themselves out of authorship even when externally the role demands a degree of authorship-performance. The internal work is harder than the external work. But the internal work is what determines whether the cascade gets installed.

## The Connection to Burnout in Helpers

This principle is closely related to the burnout patterns Joe sees in helping professions. Therapists, coaches, doctors, pastors, founders, leaders all show similar cascades. The mechanism is the same: claim ownership of the work → become responsible → become obligated → become resentful → cannot leave → addiction or control.

The relevant fix is not "set better boundaries" — that addresses the symptom. The relevant fix is to revisit the original framing of the work. Was the work being claimed as personal authorship? If so, the cascade was inevitable. If the framing can shift to channeling, the obligation can release. From there, the burnout has somewhere to go.

This is why Joe often points clients in helping professions back to the question: "Whose work is this? Are you generating it, or is it moving through you?" The diagnostic surfaces the trap.

## The Connection to the Downward Spiral Between Teacher and Students

When a teacher is in the cascade, the students typically participate in maintaining it. The students project authority onto the teacher; the teacher receives the projection; the teacher's identity inflates; the students notice the inflation and start taking the teacher down; the teacher becomes defensive; the relationship corrodes. See [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] for the full pattern.

The channeling stance protects against this. A teacher who is genuinely in the channeling stance does not receive the projection in a way that inflates the identity. The projection lands and moves through; the teacher does not absorb it; the inflation does not occur; the students cannot find an inflated identity to take down. The downward spiral does not get the foothold it needs.

This is one of the practical reasons the channeling stance matters. It is not just about the teacher's personal freedom. It is about preserving the conditions under which the work can continue without poisoning the relationships it is built on.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A teacher in the channeling stance:

- Refuses to defend the teaching when challenged. See [[agree-when-challenged-on-the-teaching]].
- Names the lineage. Talks about who they learned from, how the wisdom has been carried, what is borrowed and what is integrated.
- Holds the work loosely enough that it can be updated, revised, abandoned, or transformed.
- Encourages students to find their own teachers, their own versions, their own integrations.
- Treats the work as a service to those it helps, not as a possession to be protected.
- Notices if income or fame becomes the binding constraint and works to keep the option to walk away alive.

A teacher in the authorship trap:

- Defends the teaching against criticism, often with subtle aggression.
- Erases the lineage and presents the work as original.
- Holds the work rigidly. Any deviation from the original framing feels like betrayal.
- Discourages students from learning from anyone else.
- Treats the work as a possession to be protected. Becomes territorial about students, ideas, methods.
- Cannot imagine walking away. The work is the identity. Loss of work would be loss of self.

## The Connection to Speaking and the Butler

This principle is the meta-frame around speaking from flow, channeling content, and letting the butler arrive. See [[the-butler-metaphor]] and [[speaking-authentically]].

A speaker who feels the words are their personal product is in the authorship frame. The pressure is on the speaker to generate good words. The butler retreats. The state breaks. The speech becomes performance.

A speaker who recognizes the words as channeled — coming through them, not from them — is in the channeling frame. The pressure releases. The butler arrives. The state holds. The speech flows.

The same shift applies to coaching, writing, leading, parenting, and any other domain where output is being produced under pressure. The release of authorship is the release of the pressure. The pressure was always being generated by the authorship claim. Drop the claim, drop the pressure.

## Tone

Joe describes the cascade with a tone of having watched it happen many times. There is no contempt for the teachers who fall into it — the cascade is structural, not a personal failing. The point is that the cascade is preventable if the channeling stance is recognized early. The tone is more "here is how this works so you can avoid it" than "shame on those who have not avoided it."
