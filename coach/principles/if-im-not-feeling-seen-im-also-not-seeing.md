# If I'm Not Feeling Seen, I'm Also Not Seeing

## The Principle

When you are feeling unseen by someone, you are also -- at the same time -- not seeing them. The two states travel together. The complaint "you don't see me" rarely arrives without a corresponding "I am not letting myself see you," even if the second part is unconscious.

This is a specific case of the general principle in [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] applied to the seeing dimension. The work of seeing is bidirectional. The block on either direction tends to be the same block expressed in two ways.

The operational consequence: when you notice you are not feeling seen, the highest-leverage move is not to demand more seeing from the other. It is to ask what you are not seeing about them. The reverse-engineer hack collapses the defense that was holding the unseen story in place.

## Tara's Naming of the Hack

In the Modern Relationships interview Tara names this as her personal hack, in the context of Joe and Tara's marriage practice for the see-me-don't-see-me dynamic.

"There's a second part too, which is when I'm saying I don't feel seen, most of the time I'm also not seeing. So a hack for me is, um, if I'm not feeling seen to say, okay, hold on, what am I not seeing here? What am I missing? Tell me, tell me all about your experience. And in doing so, something in my defenses drops and I actually get curious. And then Joe will share all sorts of things that, and I'm like, oh, I didn't know that. And then he's like, yeah, what do you, what do you, what, what, what don't I see? And like, but that asking, oh, especially if I have a story, I'm not being seen here. If I can reverse engineer it, like, okay, what am I not seeing? It's just a hack and it helps me drop my defenses and then I can go to, what am I not seeing? If I'm not being seen, I'm also not seeing. And I can find my way through that way." -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The operational sequence:
1. **Notice the not-feeling-seen.** The "I don't feel seen here" thought or feeling arises.
2. **Reverse-engineer.** Instead of pursuing the not-being-seen story, ask: "What am I not seeing here? What am I missing?"
3. **Ask the partner.** "Tell me about your experience." Open the channel for their interior to land.
4. **Watch the defenses drop.** The asking itself dissolves the defensive posture that was holding the not-being-seen story.
5. **Receive what they share.** "Oh, I didn't know that." The information that was being blocked starts arriving.
6. **The reciprocal seeing returns.** Once you are seeing them, the openness for them to see you tends to follow.

The reciprocal step (6) is structural, not magical. The defensiveness that was blocking the seeing-of-them was usually also signaling unsafety to them. When the defensiveness drops because you are now curious about them, the unsafety drops on their side, and the seeing-of-you they were also holding back becomes available.

## Why This Works

The mechanism is the same mechanism that makes [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] work. The "you don't see me" story is held by a part of the self that is also not doing the seeing. The story protects that part by keeping the attention on what the other is failing to do rather than on what the self is failing to do.

When you redirect the attention from the partner's failure-to-see to your own failure-to-see, the protected part loses its protection. The defensive posture that was organizing around the story relaxes. The relaxation is felt in the body as the willingness to be curious about the partner's actual experience.

This is also why the move is described as a hack rather than a discipline. The shift is not produced by deciding to feel seen. It is produced by the structural redirect from one frame ("they aren't seeing me") to another frame ("what am I not seeing about them"). The new frame requires different cognitive work, and the different cognitive work produces a different body state.

## What This Is Not

This is not a denial that the partner has any responsibility for the seeing. The partner may genuinely not be seeing the person. The principle does not contradict that. It says the not-being-seen state is paired with a not-seeing state in the person who is noticing the unseenness, and that the more workable entry point is the side they control.

This is not a unilateral rule that the person who feels unseen is always the one at fault. The fault frame is not what the principle is operating in. The principle is operating in the more useful "where can I move from" frame. The person who feels unseen can either demand more from the other (high friction, low reliability) or work the side they have access to (low friction, high reliability).

This is not always the right first move. Some situations -- abuse, manipulation, gaslighting -- require the person to assert what they are seeing rather than to soften into curiosity about the other. The principle does not say "always ask what you are missing." It says that in the configuration where two people are in a stuck unseenness pattern with no immediate safety threat, the asking move tends to unlock what the demanding move cannot.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- A person describes feeling chronically unseen by their partner and the partner is similarly describing being unseen
- A person is stuck in the "you don't see me" loop and the demanding-more-seeing approach is producing no movement
- A person has been to therapy multiple times on the same not-being-seen complaint with no resolution
- The seeing-asymmetry is operating in a long marriage and both partners feel righteous
- The not-being-seen story is being held against someone who, on observation, is also working hard to be seen and not getting through
- A person says "I just want to feel seen" and the conversation needs to redirect from demand to inquiry

## How to Coach This

The instruction can be delivered nearly verbatim:

"Try this. The next time you notice you are not feeling seen by [partner/person], instead of pursuing the not-seen story, ask 'what am I not seeing here? What am I missing?' Then ask them: 'Tell me about your experience.' Let it land. Notice what happens to your defenses. Notice what they say. Notice whether the seeing-of-you that you were waiting for becomes more available once you have done the seeing-of-them first."

The instruction is small enough to be tried in a single conversation. The verification is immediate -- the person can tell within one attempt whether their defenses dropped and whether the partner's sharing landed differently. The verification builds trust in the principle; once trusted, the move becomes available reliably.

## The Connection to See-Yourself-And-Acknowledge-Others

This principle is the relational complement to [[see-yourself-and-acknowledge-others]]. The acknowledgment practice is daily and bidirectional (see yourself, see them). This principle is the in-the-moment repair when the bidirectionality has collapsed and one direction is being noticed as missing.

The two practices stack. The daily practice keeps the seeing flow open in both directions in ordinary times. The in-the-moment hack restores the flow when one direction has gone quiet and the not-being-seen story has started to organize the experience.

## Tone

Conversational, almost casual. Tara introduces it as "a hack" -- not as a teaching, not as a principle, just as something she does. The tone is the tone of someone sharing a practical trick that works. The lack of weight is part of what makes it transmissible -- the listener can try it without feeling like they have to commit to a framework first.

The "I can find my way through that way" closing has the rhythm of someone who has used the move many times. The confidence is earned. The instruction is offered as something that has worked in Tara's actual marriage, not as something theoretically sound.
