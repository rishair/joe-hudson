# It's Just Not a Match

## The Principle

When a team member is not working out, the right frame is not "they failed" or "they are a bad person" or "they are in trouble." The right frame is: "this isn't a match." The team has a specific shape. Some people fit that shape. Some people don't. The mismatch is information about fit, not a verdict about the person.

"It's just like, this isn't a match. We, our team is very diverse, but it's not diverse in that. Yeah. We're not defensive. We're not a defensive team. We're not a dumb team. There's some diversity that we just don't have. And one of those things that we do are not diverse in, and this is, I think, really important for managing and delegating, is to be really clear on what you are willing to accept and what you are not willing to accept." -- Joe Hudson

The frame allows the leader to remove someone from the team cleanly. The leader is not yelling. The leader is not labeling the person as bad. The leader is naming a structural reality: this team has a specific shape, and this person does not fit that shape.

"You're not going to yell at, you're not a bad person. It's just not a match." -- Joe Hudson

## Why This Frame Matters

The default frame around firing or separating from a team member is loaded with moral content. Either the person is bad (and the leader is righteous for removing them) or the leader is bad (and is failing the person by removing them). Both framings are interpretive overlays on a structural reality.

The matter-of-fact frame removes the moral overlay. The team has a shape. The person has a shape. The two shapes are not compatible. The right move is to acknowledge the incompatibility and act on it, without making either party wrong.

The frame is kinder to the person being released. They are not being labeled as deficient. They are being told that this particular environment is not for them. Some other environment might be a perfect fit. The mismatch is contextual, not personal.

The frame is also more accurate. Most team incompatibilities are actually about fit, not about absolute quality. A person who is dysfunctional on one team would thrive on another. The mismatch is real, but it is about the specific configuration, not about the person's worth.

## The Sort-Of Trouble Distinction

There is still a "kind of trouble" in being told you're not a match. The conversation has real consequences. The person is leaving the team. The interviewer surfaces this nuance:

"Right. Which is a sort of a kind of trouble, but it's not, you're in trouble." -- the dialogue

Joe agrees. The two things are distinct. "You're in trouble" carries the moral charge — you did something wrong, you are being punished. "This isn't a match" carries the structural recognition — the fit isn't right, the relationship is ending, but no one is being condemned.

The team member can still feel disappointment, loss, or fear. The principle doesn't make those feelings go away. What the principle does is keep the leader from adding the moral overlay that would compound those feelings with shame about being the kind of person who got fired.

## How This Connects to Hiring

The principle is the downstream consequence of the hiring being intentional. The leader who has been clear about what the team is — its shape, its standards, its non-negotiables — can also be clear about what the team is not. When someone is found to be a mismatch, the mismatch is verifiable against the explicit shape, not against a moving target.

"This is, I think, really important for managing and delegating, is to be really clear on what you are willing to accept and what you are not willing to accept." -- Joe Hudson

The leader who has not done this work cannot make the matter-of-fact call. The mismatch claim requires the prior work of knowing what the team actually is. Without it, every separation feels like a personal judgment because the leader has nothing structural to point at.

## The Conditions Under Which the Frame Works

The frame works when:

- The leader has been clear about what the team is and is not (the shape is explicit)
- The leader has actually given the person a fair chance to operate inside that shape
- The leader can deliver the conversation without contempt or anger
- The leader is genuinely seeing fit, not just convenient labeling of a person they don't like
- The team's shape is actually being maintained for the team's actual purpose (not as cover for the leader's preferences)

The frame fails when:

- The leader uses "not a match" as a euphemism for personal dislike
- The leader has not been transparent about the team's shape and the person did not have a real chance to fit it
- The leader's contempt for the person leaks through the matter-of-fact words
- The "shape" the person doesn't fit is invented in the moment to justify removing them

When the frame is misused, the team detects the misuse. The principle relies on its own integrity to function.

## Why This Pairs With No Yelling

The principle's specific phrasing — "you're not going to yell at" — is operational, not stylistic. The leader who is yelling is in the moral frame. The yelling itself signals to the team that "not a match" is being used as cover for personal anger.

The matter-of-fact tone is part of how the team verifies that the principle is being used in good faith. The same words delivered with contempt mean the opposite of what they say. The same words delivered with neutrality mean what they say. The tone is part of the principle.

The principle is also kinder to the leader. The leader who has internalized "this isn't a match" does not have to generate the emotional energy of being angry at someone. The leader can deliver the conversation from a place of clear-eyed structural recognition rather than from a place of fueled-up indignation. The lower emotional cost makes the conversation easier to have, which makes it more likely to actually happen.

## Where the Frame Comes From

The frame comes from a more fundamental recognition: the leader is not trying to be everything to everyone. The team has a shape, and the shape excludes some patterns. The exclusion is not a judgment about the patterns being bad. It is a judgment that those patterns don't belong on this team.

"Our team is very diverse, but it's not diverse in that." -- Joe Hudson

The team can be diverse in many dimensions and still maintain non-negotiable shape on others. The team is not "anti-diversity" by maintaining the shape; the team is being intentional about what the shape is. The intentionality is part of what makes the team work.

## What This Is Not

**Not callousness.** The frame is matter-of-fact, not cold. The leader can still care about the person, wish them well, support their transition to a different environment. The frame is about how the leader holds the structural reality, not about whether the leader has feelings about it.

**Not a justification for unilateral removal.** The frame requires the prior work of having given the person a real chance, of having had the hard conversations, of having tried to address the pattern before deciding it's a mismatch. The principle is not a shortcut to firing people.

**Not a denial of stakes.** The person being told they're not a match is losing their job. That is a real loss. The frame doesn't make the loss not happen. It makes the loss happen without an additional layer of moral condemnation.

**Not a refusal of structural diversity.** The team can be intentionally non-diverse on some dimensions (defensiveness, dishonesty, refusal to grow) while being intentionally diverse on others (background, perspective, approach to work). The "not diverse in that" claim is specific, not blanket.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and clear. The principle's defining feature is the absence of the moral charge that usually accompanies firing conversations. The leader delivers the words with the same neutrality they would use to describe any operational fact. The neutrality is part of the message: this is not a moral event, it is a fit reality.
