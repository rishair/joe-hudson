# Putting Someone Above (or Below) You Protects You From Your Emotions

## The Principle

Placing another person above you ("they're better than me") or below you ("I'm better than them") is structurally an emotion-avoidance move. The hierarchy installs a relationship between you and the other person that displaces the relationship between you and your own emotional experience. As long as the hierarchy is operating, you do not have to feel what you are feeling.

The reason: feeling your emotions requires being in yourself. The hierarchy puts you "in them" — concerned with their position, their assessment, their state — and out of you. Approval-seeking is one form this takes. Judgment is another. Both are the same structural move.

"This is why putting somebody above us protects us from our emotions, because if we feel our emotions, then all we can do is really listen to ourselves. So when you say, 'Somebody's better than me,' then you don't have to listen to your emotional experience, and so then you're really concerned about how they think about you." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

## The Mechanism

The structure runs like this:

1. There is an emotion the person does not want to feel — usually something in the shame/smallness/not-enough family, sometimes anger or grief.
2. Feeling that emotion would require being in the body, in the self.
3. To not feel it, the person needs somewhere else to put their attention.
4. Placing someone above (the person whose approval is needed) or below (the person whose judgment is dismissed) creates an attention-target outside the self.
5. The attention now lives in the comparison and in their imagined response to the person. The original emotion is no longer in the field.

"It's really hard to be in yourself if there's an emotion you don't wanna feel. So it goes like this, 'Oh, there's an emotion I don't wanna feel, so I can't be in myself, so I'm gonna go be in them, and now I care what they think.'" -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The sequence reveals that approval-seeking is not really about the approval. It is about needing somewhere to be that is not in the felt-sense of the unwanted emotion. The other person's approval is the destination only because the destination has to be outside of self. Any sufficiently externalizing strategy would do the job.

## Why This Generates Approval-Seeking Specifically

Putting someone above you, in the form of "they are better than me / they know better / they have the answer," makes their judgment of you the central thing in the field. You now have to care what they think — because they have been positioned as the authority whose verdict matters. The caring-what-they-think is downstream of the placing-them-above. Stop the placing, and the caring loses its anchor.

This is also why the inversion — placing someone below you, "I'm better than them" — does not actually break the cycle. It still requires you to be over in them, ranking, comparing, assessing. The cost in self-listening is the same. The judgment is the externalizing strategy in its dismissive form. See [[comparative-mind]] for the general pattern.

## The Resolution

The instruction is simple and operational. Every time you notice the approval-seeking (or the judgment), feel the emotion underneath. Even briefly.

"All you need to do is every time you notice that you're seeking out approval from somebody else, feel the emotion that you're not allowing yourself to feel. Right there, right in the spot, even if it's just for 2, 3, 4 seconds, allow yourself to feel it. And slowly but surely, you'll start paying attention to your truth rather than other people's truth." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The duration is small on purpose. The body has been avoiding the emotion for years. Asking it to feel the emotion for hours immediately is asking too much. Two to four seconds is enough to reverse the externalizing motion. The reversal is what does the work. Repeated, the capacity grows. Eventually the emotion is felt fully and the externalizing strategy becomes unnecessary.

The result is the relocation of "truth" from outside to inside. The person who has been listening to other people's truth (their assessments, their approval, their judgment) starts listening to their own (their actual experience, their actual want, their actual response). The change is not motivational. It is structural. Feeling the emotion makes the externalizing impossible, which makes the self-listening inevitable.

## The Universal Form: Every Judgment Is Emotion-Avoidance

The principle generalizes beyond approval-seeking. Every act of judging anyone, including yourself, is the cognitive method of avoiding an emotion. This is the universal form.

"Whenever we're judging anybody, including ourselves, it's because we don't want to feel a certain emotion." -- Joe Hudson, The Price Of Being The Logical One In Love

The implication is structural and operational. Wherever you find yourself running a judgment — of a partner, a coworker, a stranger, yourself — there is an emotion you are using the judgment to avoid feeling. The judgment is not separate from the avoidance; it is the avoidance, in cognitive form. Stop the judgment without dealing with the emotion and the emotion finds another judgment to ride. Find the emotion and feel it briefly and the judgment loses its reason to run.

This unifies several previously-distinct frames:
- Approval-seeking (placing the other above to avoid the inadequacy emotion)
- Judgment-as-superiority (placing the other below to avoid an emotion that the superiority hides)
- Self-judgment (judging the self to avoid an emotion that would be more painful than the self-attack)
- In-fight superiority (judging the partner from a rational position to avoid the emotion the fight is trying to deliver)

All are the same structure with different content. The instruction is the same in each: feel the avoided emotion briefly, the judgment relaxes.

## The Fight-Specific Application

The judgment-as-emotion-avoidance principle has a specific operational form inside a relationship fight. The intellectual partner's superiority over the emotional partner is doing the same emotion-avoidance work, but with the additional consequence that the partner is being treated as the object the avoidance is being conducted against.

This makes the fight unproductive. See [[control-of-the-other-makes-fights-unproductive]] for the structural account: when judgment-as-avoidance is happening inside a fight, it functions as a control move on the partner, which the partner naturally resists, which prevents the fight from delivering its developmental work. The principle here is the cause; that principle is the relational consequence.

The mid-fight self-check that interrupts the chain is [[what-am-i-not-feeling-right-now]]. The cue is the noticing of the superiority. The question redirects from the judgment (which is the symptom) to the avoided feeling (which is the cause). The feeling, briefly felt, dissolves the need for the judgment, which dissolves the control attempt, which lets the fight return to its productive shape.

## The Connection to Self-Approval

Once the emotion can be felt, the move to [[chase-your-own-approval]] becomes available. The person can ask "do I approve?" rather than "do they approve?" because they are now in themselves enough to have an answer. Before, the externalizing strategy meant there was no "I" in the room to do the approving. After, the I is here and can answer its own question.

## What This Connects To

The principle is the structural core underneath several familiar patterns:

- **Approval-seeking:** placing others above creates the field where their approval becomes the currency.
- **People-pleasing:** the placing-above generalized to everyone in the field.
- **Judgment:** the placing-below as the inverted form of the same emotion-avoidance.
- **Imposter syndrome:** placing peers above and yourself below as the persistent shape that prevents the self from feeling its own actual experience. See [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]].
- **Authority-freeze:** placing the authority figure above and freezing as a result of the displacement. See [[i-freeze-around-authority]].
- **Comparative mind:** the principle generalized to all comparison. See [[comparative-mind]].
- **The narcissistic configuration:** placing self above (to avoid the inadequacy underneath) is the same move as placing self below. The structure is the same; only the polarity flips. See [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]].

The shared mechanism: a hierarchy is being used to keep the person outside of themselves so that an avoided emotion does not have to be felt.

## What This Looks Like in Coaching

When a client describes caring what someone thinks of them, the move is not to argue the person should care less. The move is to find the emotion the caring is covering.

A typical sequence:

1. The client describes the approval-seeking or the worry about someone's opinion.
2. Joe asks: "What's the feeling underneath this that you don't want to feel?"
3. Often there is a long pause. The client has not asked the question of themselves before.
4. Something surfaces — usually a small-self felt sense, a sense of not-enough-ness, sometimes a specific shame.
5. Joe has them stay with the feeling, briefly. Two to four seconds is enough.
6. The client notices that, while feeling the emotion, they are no longer worried about the other person's opinion. The structure has reorganized itself.
7. The new structural location ("in myself, feeling this") is what self-approval can grow from.

The work is in the feeling, not in the conversation about the feeling. The talking about the principle is the setup. The brief in-body feeling is the move.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not say all care for others' opinions is bad. The capacity to receive feedback, to notice how you are landing, to consider another person's perspective is part of being in relationship. The principle is specifically about the *structural displacement* — when caring what someone thinks of you has become the operating system that keeps you out of yourself.

The diagnostic question: is the caring producing genuine attunement to the other person, or is it producing absence from yourself? Real attunement does not require self-displacement. The dysfunctional version does. The principle addresses the dysfunctional version.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle as a structural observation, not a moral teaching. The tone is matter-of-fact: this is how the mechanism runs. He is not arguing that the person should stop caring what others think; he is showing them what is actually happening when the caring runs.

The slight smile when he says "I'm gonna go be in them" is the tone marker. He is naming a familiar move with a small fondness for its absurdity. The instruction (feel the emotion for 2-4 seconds) is delivered as practical, doable, small. He does not ask for heroics. The principle is undone by tiny reps.
