# Address What's In the Way, Not the Surface Problem

## The Principle

When someone wants to change something specific (quit smoking, start a business, lose weight, write the book, have the conversation), working directly on that specific thing is the least effective intervention available. The specific thing is the symptom. What is in the way of the specific thing is the actual leverage.

"To do these exercises, it was important that they weren't solving the problems that people think they had. They were solving the things that were getting in the way from getting what they wanted." -- Joe Hudson, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

A person can plan their business launch in detail, follow every step on the checklist, and still not launch. The plan is not the problem. The thing that is stopping the execution of the plan is the problem. That thing is usually internal, often emotional, frequently shared across many domains.

## The Convergence Phenomenon

The same block usually shows up across many surface goals. The person who cannot start the business often cannot quit smoking either. Not because the business and the smoking are connected, but because the same internal pattern is preventing both. Fix the underlying block and many things move at once.

"When the big components get undone, 10, 12 things in a person's life change." -- Joe Hudson, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

Brett observes the same thing in the same exchange:

"Oftentimes it's the same thing that's stopping somebody from quitting smoking and starting a business. Yes, something deep in there that if you just address the starting a business thing, you could come up with a to-do list of what you need to do to start a business, but it'll still be blocked by the psychological component." -- Brett Kistler, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

This is the leverage argument. Working on the surface goal moves the surface goal (maybe). Working on the underlying block moves the surface goal AND ten others. The return on the same effort is an order of magnitude different.

## Why the Direct Approach Fails

The direct approach assumes the person has access to the action and is choosing not to take it. The assumption is wrong. The person does not have access to the action. Something is blocking it. Pushing harder on the blocked thing produces struggle, shame, and relapse. The block is not pierced; it is reinforced.

The pattern of trying harder and failing again is itself diagnostic. A person who has tried to quit smoking twenty times and failed is not failing for lack of effort. They are working on the wrong thing. The smoking is the symptom of a pattern that has not been addressed. Until the pattern is addressed, the next attempt at quitting will fail the same way.

This is also why advice does not work for these problems. Advice operates at the level of the surface goal: "have you tried nicotine patches?" "have you tried setting smaller milestones for the business?" The advice may be technically correct and still useless. The person is not stuck for lack of correct technique. They are stuck because the technique cannot run while the block is in place.

## The Diagnostic Question

The diagnostic for whether you are working on a symptom or a cause: "If this specific problem went away tomorrow, would the underlying pattern still produce the same kind of problem somewhere else?" If yes, you are working on a symptom. The block will resurface in another domain. If no, you have probably actually hit the cause.

The same diagnostic in the reverse direction: "If this block were dissolved, how many other things in my life would change?" If only the immediate goal would change, you are working on a symptom of the block, not the block itself. If many things would change, you have located the actual leverage point.

## The Methodology Implication

This principle reorganizes the entire methodology around indirection. Joe's exercises do not target the symptoms people come in with. They target the things that block the symptoms from resolving on their own. See [[seven-limiting-beliefs]] for the structural map of the seven things Joe identifies as most commonly in the way.

"I built [the course] around the 7 things that I saw my clients and any students that I'd worked with really getting in the way of whatever it is that they wanted to accomplish. And so I look for exercises around those 7 things and just look for the most powerful ones." -- Joe Hudson, How to Tell if the Master Class Isn't For You

The 7 things are not 7 problems. They are 7 categories of block. The smoking, the business, the book, the relationship, the weight are not the focus. The blocks that keep all of those stuck are the focus.

## The Coaching Posture This Produces

The coach following this principle does not validate the surface problem as the problem. The coach helps the client locate the block underneath. This can be disorienting for clients who arrived with a specific request ("help me quit smoking"). The coach is not refusing the request. The coach is locating the actual mechanism by which the request will be answered.

The reframe usually has to be explained. "We are going to spend time on this thing that seems unrelated to your goal. The reason is that the thing in the way of your goal is also in the way of many other things. When it shifts, your goal will shift with it. Working directly on the goal will not work; you have already tried that and it has not worked. We are going to work on what is in the way."

The reframe also requires the client to trust the indirection. Many clients want the direct path. The direct path is exactly what has not worked. Holding steady through this resistance is part of the coaching.

## What This Principle Asks of the Client

The client has to be willing to work on what does not look like their problem. They came in asking about smoking. They will end up working on something about their relationship with their father, or with shame, or with control, or with the want underneath the should. The connection between the original ask and the work being done will not be obvious in the moment.

This requires [[sincerity-is-the-prerequisite]]: the client has to be willing to admit there is something to work on that is not the surface thing. The client who cannot admit this will keep redirecting back to the surface thing and the work cannot land.

## Connection to the Stinky House Experiment

[[joes-stinky-house-experiment]] is the personal-scale demonstration of this principle. Joe did not work directly on taking out the trash. He worked on the relationship between want and should that was preventing the trash from being taken out. When that relationship shifted, the trash got taken out without struggle. The same logic applies at all scales.
