# Triggers Dissolve When You Can Feel the Emotion Others Are Afraid Of

## The Principle

When a person has built the capacity to feel emotions they were previously avoiding, a downstream effect appears that is not the work's main goal but is one of its most useful side-effects: their ability to take offense at other people's behavior drops dramatically. The behaviors that previously triggered them — being judged, being guilt-tripped, encountering chronic indecision in others, watching someone dissociate — become readable as fear-of-an-emotion rather than as attacks. Once the behavior is read as fear, the trigger does not fire. There is nothing personal to take offense at. There is only a person who is scared to feel something.

This is the "no harm, no foul" position. The other person's behavior is still happening. The receiver still notices it. But the receiver's nervous system no longer treats the behavior as an attack requiring defense. Instead, the nervous system reads the behavior accurately: that person is managing an unfelt emotion. The accurate read produces compassion automatically. The defense that the trigger would have mobilized is no longer necessary because there is no attack to defend against.

"The cool part about all this, like the added benefit, is that all of a sudden your ability to take offense at other people is going to go way down. And the way this works is so cool. So for instance, so many people are like, oh my gosh, somebody might be judging me. I can't do that because somebody might be judging me. But when you go through this process, you can go, oh, they're not actually judging me. What they're doing is just scared of feeling an emotion." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

## Why It Works This Way

The trigger fires when the other person's behavior gets read as a moral attack against the receiver — "they think I am bad," "they are coercing me into compliance," "they are abandoning me." Each of these reads positions the receiver as the target of a will directed at them. The defense the trigger mobilizes is appropriate to this read: protect against the will being directed at me.

The reframe changes the read. The other person's judgment is not actually a will directed at the receiver; it is a defense the other person is running against an emotion the other person does not want to feel. The judgment is incidental to the receiver. It is about the other person's interior, not about the receiver's worth or character. The same logic applies to guilt-tripping (the guilter is scared to feel their fear), to indecision (the indecisive person is scared to feel the consequence of either branch), and to dissociation (the dissociator is scared they will be overwhelmed by what they would feel).

Once the read shifts, the defense becomes structurally inappropriate. There is no will-directed-at-me to defend against. The behavior continues but its meaning has changed. The trigger has nothing to fire on.

"They're trying to make me scared of that emotion. Like, no harm, no foul. It's just fear happening of an emotion. I don't have to feel guilty." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

## Why This Requires Having Done the Work First

The reframe only lands when the receiver has built the capacity to feel the emotions that the other person is avoiding. The reason is structural: a person who is also avoiding the emotion the other person is avoiding cannot read the other person's behavior as fear-of-the-emotion, because that read would require admitting that the emotion exists and is feelable. The avoider's defense system is invested in the emotion not being a thing that anyone has to feel. So the avoider reads the other person's behavior as attack rather than as fear, because the attack reading does not require any contact with the emotion.

This is why "just don't take it personally" advice fails. The receiver cannot just not take it personally. The capacity to not take it personally is downstream of the capacity to feel the emotion the other person is avoiding. Without that capacity, the offense-taking is structurally inevitable. With that capacity, the offense-taking disappears with no effort.

The implication for the work: the receiver does not work on "not taking it personally." The receiver works on feeling the emotions they themselves have been avoiding. The not-taking-it-personally emerges as a side effect once the capacity is there. The fundamental work is the same as the rest of the methodology. The downstream relational benefit is the bonus.

## The Reframe Applied to Each Signal

The principle works the same way for each of the [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]] (and the public-facing expansion that includes guilt and dissociation).

**When someone is being judgmental toward you:** They are not actually judging you. They are scared of feeling an emotion that your existence or behavior is touching in them. The judgment is the way they are managing the emotion. No harm, no foul.

**When someone is guilt-tripping you:** They are scared to feel a fear, usually the fear of losing you or losing control of the situation. The guilt is the form the fear takes because no other form is available to them. They are not actually demanding anything of you in the deeper sense; they are trying to relieve their own discomfort. See [[guilt-is-aggression-not-poor-me]] for the complementary read on what the guilt-trip is structurally doing. Both reads are true. The fear-underneath read produces compassion; the force-on-the-receiver read protects the receiver from absorbing the directive. Hold both.

**When someone is chronically indecisive in a way that affects you:** They are scared of feeling the consequence-emotion on each side of the decision. The indecision is bothersome to you; the bothersomeness is real. But it is not a personal attack. They are managing their own avoidance.

**When someone is dissociating in your presence:** They are scared they will be overwhelmed by something. They are leaving. The leaving is not a rejection of you; it is the only move their nervous system knows. Treat the leaving as the fear it is, not as the abandonment it looks like.

"Indecision. Oh, right, they're indecisive. That's really bothersome. But really what it is is they're just scared to feel an emotion. And it allows you to have so much more compassion for the people that might trigger you when they're judgmental or indecisive or dissociating." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 17 Minutes And You'll Stop Avoiding Your Emotions

## What This Is Not

This is not a justification for the other person's behavior. The behavior may still be problematic. The receiver may still need to set limits, decline requests, or leave the situation. The principle does not eliminate the receiver's right to respond to the behavior; it changes the felt-quality of the response. The response from compassion is different from the response from triggered defense. The action may be the same; the relational substrate is different.

This is not a denial of the receiver's experience. The judgment, the guilt-trip, the indecision, the dissociation are all real and have real effects on the receiver. The principle does not say "you are not affected." It says "your reading of why you are affected was inaccurate, and the more accurate reading produces a different internal response."

This is not a one-time achievement. The capacity is dynamic. The receiver who has built the capacity for one emotion may still be hooked by triggers involving an emotion they have not yet worked through. The principle becomes more complete as the emotional capacity broadens. In the meantime, the principle applies wherever the capacity has been built.

This is not a substitute for the other person's work. The other person's chronic emotional avoidance is theirs to handle. The principle helps the receiver, not the avoider. The avoider may continue avoiding indefinitely. The receiver's freedom from the trigger does not depend on the avoider changing.

## The Relational Effect

When the receiver stops being triggered by the avoider's behavior, the relational dynamic often changes on its own. The avoider has been receiving a reaction that reinforced their behavior (the receiver's defensive response provided the avoider with evidence that emotional content is dangerous and must be managed through the avoidance behavior). When the reaction stops, the loop breaks. The avoider's behavior loses some of its reinforcement.

This does not always change the avoider's behavior — they may continue running the pattern with someone else who still gets triggered. But it sometimes does. The receiver, by not being triggered, has held a different field around the avoider. The avoider sometimes notices, over time, that they do not have to defend in the same way around this person. Some of the avoidance behavior softens. The relationship becomes more available to both.

The receiver, regardless of whether the avoider changes, has the benefit immediately. The triggered defense was costing them. Without it, more of their attention and energy is free for what they actually want to do.

## The Connection to Doing Your Own Work

This principle is one of the most direct demonstrations of why the inner work is also the relational work. The receiver does not work on the relationship. The receiver works on their own capacity to feel. The relationship changes as a side effect.

This is also why "you can't change other people, you can only change yourself" is more than a platitude. The change in the self produces a change in the field around the self, which produces different responses from the people in the field, which produces different relationships. The change does not need to be marketed to the other person. The change shows up in how the receiver shows up. The other people in the receiver's life encounter a different person and respond differently.

The principle also closes the loop with [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The receiver who is afraid of being judged tends to invite judgment through the avoidance-behaviors that fear-of-being-judged produces. The receiver who has built the capacity to feel the emotions that being-judged would touch stops inviting judgment in the same way. Both the field and the receiver have changed; the triggers genuinely happen less often, and the ones that still happen are read more accurately.

## When This Principle Is Useful for the Coach to Offer

The principle is useful when a client is doing the emotional work and is starting to notice the side-effect but does not have a name for it. The client may report something like: "I noticed my mother's guilt-tripping last week and I just felt... not affected by it the way I used to be. I don't know what changed." The principle is the name for what changed.

The principle is also useful when a client is asking why they should bother doing the difficult emotional work. The relational benefits are often more motivating than the abstract promise of "you will feel better." Telling them "the people who trigger you will stop being able to trigger you in the same way, and you will be able to feel compassion for them where you used to feel attack" is a tangible and verifiable promise.

The principle is not useful as instruction during the moment of being triggered. A person currently being triggered cannot use the reframe to talk themselves out of the trigger; the trigger is a structural failure of the capacity, not a thinking error. The instruction during the trigger is to feel what is arising. The reframe applies to the next time the same situation occurs, after the capacity has been built.

## Boundary Conditions

- The principle does not apply to active abuse or imminent harm. A person facing genuine threat needs the threat read as threat. The principle is about chronic relational dynamics with people whose behavior is not actually dangerous but feels threatening because of unfelt-emotion patterns.
- The principle does not require the receiver to remain in the relationship. Sometimes the accurate read of "this person is scared to feel an emotion and runs this pattern continuously" supports the decision to leave the relationship. Compassion for the other person's interior does not obligate continued exposure to the other person's behavior. See [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]] for the related territory.
- The principle does not eliminate appropriate anger. Sometimes the accurate read also produces the felt-sense that the behavior is unacceptable. The anger that arises from the accurate read is different from the anger that arises from the triggered defense; it has clarity rather than reactivity. Both can co-exist with the compassion.

## Tone

Light, almost relieved. Joe presents this principle as a "cool added benefit" — a bonus that comes with the work. The tone matches: the principle is the gift the work hands back to the person who did the work. The "no harm, no foul" framing carries the lightness of the position the receiver finds themselves in once the trigger no longer fires.
