# Your Issue Is the Community's Issue (Not Yours Alone)

## The Principle

In a functional [[community]], a problem that arises in one member's life is treated as the community's problem, not as that member's private burden. Not because the community is going to solve it. Because the trouble is read as a reflection of something happening in the community, and the act of holding it together heals the whole, including the person at the apparent center.

"If there's a marriage in trouble inside of the community, that the community supports that marriage. Because if a marriage is having trouble inside of a community, it is a reflection of something else happening inside of the community." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

The principle inverts the standard frame, which says: your problems are yours; bring them to me if you want help and I'll see if I have time. The community frame says: the appearance of trouble in a member is information about the community itself, and the response to that information is collective.

## What This Is Not

Three nearby things this is not, each of which destroys community when confused with it.

**It is not the community fixing the member's problem.** The community does not solve the marriage. The community holds space while the couple does their work, with the recognition that the work is also the community's work. See [[fixing-people]] and [[send-the-message-that-youre-whole]].

**It is not collective shame or blame.** "There's a problem in the community" is not a verdict of failure. It is an observation about what is being expressed through one of its members. The response is presence and witnessing, not finger-pointing at the group or at the member.

**It is not absolving the member of responsibility.** The paradox: the principle works only when each member is also fully responsible for their own life. A community of victims who expect rescue collapses. The two halves — full self-responsibility and shared ownership of the whole — are both required.

## The Reflection Logic

The trouble in one member is read as data about the whole. Not in a literal sense (the community didn't cause your divorce), but in the sense that what is unfelt or unspeakable in the community will tend to express itself through whichever member is closest to it. The struggling marriage often surfaces something the whole community has been avoiding speaking about.

"How do I, you know, like in my case it was, hey, let's do a morning ceremony. And like, hey, can the community come together and support you and your wife? How do we do that?" -- Joe Hudson

The response to seeing the reflection is twofold: support the member as they go through what they're going through, and let what surfaces in them also surface in everyone else. The witnessing is mutual. The healing is collective.

## Why This Is the Healing Frame

The standard alternative is to treat each problem as the individual's responsibility and to offer help only on request, with rules about when help becomes intrusion. This is the model most adults learn. It produces isolation precisely when isolation is most damaging — in the middle of a hard thing.

When the community holds the problem as its own, three things change for the person in the middle. They don't have to ask. They don't have to perform okayness so others don't feel burdened. They don't have to manage everyone else's discomfort with their situation. The energy they would have spent on these is freed for the actual work.

See [[community-grief-ceremony]] for a worked example: the bereaved couple was not the object of the gathering, they were members of a community feeling something together. The frame is exactly this principle in action.

## How It Shows Up in Practice

In Joe's community, this shows up as the immediate move to gather when someone is in crisis. Not after they ask, not after the trouble is solved, not as a check-in call. The community comes together to be in it with them.

It also shows up in the way conflicts inside the community are handled. Disputes between two members are not their private problem to figure out alone. Witnesses are present. The community supports the working-through. The premise is that the conflict is information about the whole and that working it out heals more than just the dyad.

It also shows up in the absence of certain moves. Nobody says "well, that's their issue." Nobody outsources the trouble to a professional with the framing of "they need help, I'm not the right person." Nobody puts a calendar limit on showing up for a member in crisis. The frame is in the air, not in any one person's behavior.

## The Discipline This Requires

This is high-demand. It requires that members notice their own pull to make someone else's trouble "their thing" rather than ours. It requires noticing the pull to advise, fix, or rescue, because all three are forms of putting the problem back on the other person. It requires the willingness to feel one's own feelings during another person's hard thing, rather than managing them away.

It also requires that the community has the underlying capacities — [[vulnerability]], [[impartiality]], [[empathy]], [[wonder]] — that make holding the trouble possible without it collapsing into chaos or rescue. Without [[view]] as the substrate, the principle becomes either a heavy moral imperative or a license for enmeshment.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not apply outside a functional community. A friend network that has not built the depth cannot operate this way without harm. Trying to enforce "your problem is our problem" on a community that hasn't earned it produces obligation, performance, and the same dynamics that drive people away from community. The principle is downstream of [[community]], not a method to install one.

The principle also does not override a member's discernment about what they want shared and how. The community treats the trouble as its own, but the member still chooses what they bring forward and when. The "your issue is the community's issue" is about the receiving end, not about forcing disclosure.
