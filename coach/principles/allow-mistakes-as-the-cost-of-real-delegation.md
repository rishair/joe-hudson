# Allow Mistakes as the Cost of Real Delegation

## The Principle

Real delegation requires the leader to be okay with people making mistakes. There is no version of delegation where the work always goes correctly the first time. If the leader cannot tolerate mistakes — if they punish people for making them, or visibly tense up when they happen, or re-do the work themselves — the team learns the lesson and stops taking real ownership of delegated work.

"You have to be good with people making mistakes. If you're going to do great delegation, people will make mistakes. Like, yeah. And if you're not good with that and you start punishing people for making mistakes, then they — you will never be a good delegator." -- Joe Hudson

The principle has two directions. The leader has to allow mistakes from the team. The leader also has to allow their own mistakes — in vision, in strategy, in direction-setting, in the delegating itself. The delegation chain breaks at either end if mistakes are not allowed.

"And you also have to be willing to make the mistakes in the delegation. You need to be willing to make mistakes in the vision and the direction and the strategy and allow people to make mistakes." -- Joe Hudson

The principle is the structural counterweight to perfectionism in leaders. The perfectionist leader cannot delegate because delegation is a mistake-allowance act. The work moves through other people, and other people produce different results than the leader would have. Some of those results will be worse, some better. The leader has to be willing to live in the system that produces both.

## Why Punishing Mistakes Ends Delegation

The mechanism is simple. When the leader punishes mistakes — visibly, audibly, or through subtle withdrawal — the team registers the punishment. The next time a delegated piece of work has any uncertainty, the team behaves in one or both of two ways:

1. **They escalate every decision back to the leader.** The team protects itself from making the mistake-that-will-be-punished by not making any decisions at all. The leader is now the decision-maker on every detail, which is the opposite of delegation. The team has become a decision-routing layer rather than a doing layer.

2. **They hide mistakes.** The team continues to make decisions but stops surfacing the ones that go wrong. The leader loses visibility into the actual state of the work. Failures accumulate underground until they erupt as crises. The leader's punishment has produced the very condition (lack of clean information) that the punishment was supposed to prevent.

Either way, the punishment ends delegation. The leader who wants real delegation has to stop punishing the mistakes that real delegation produces. The two are incompatible.

## Why the Leader Also Has to Make Mistakes

The second direction of the principle is harder for many leaders to absorb. The leader's own mistakes in vision, strategy, and direction are part of the delegation system. The leader sets a direction; the direction turns out to be wrong; the team has been working on it; the work has to be redirected. The leader has to be willing to live in this.

The leader who cannot make their own mistakes cannot delegate because they cannot afford to set a direction. They become paralyzed at the level of vision. They want to wait until they are certain before delegating, but certainty is not available at that level. The leader's perfectionism about their own decisions becomes the upstream block on the team's ability to act.

"You need to be willing to make mistakes in the vision and the direction and the strategy and allow people to make mistakes." -- Joe Hudson

Both ends of the chain require the same capacity. The leader's tolerance for their own imperfect direction-setting and the leader's tolerance for the team's imperfect execution are the same psychological capacity expressed at two scales.

## Why This Is the Thing That Stops People From Becoming CEOs

The principle connects to a broader observation: many competent people never become CEOs because they do not want the ultimate responsibility that comes with making decisions that might be wrong. The decision-mistake is what they cannot tolerate.

"I see a lot of really competent people never become CEO because they don't want to have that ultimate responsibility." -- Joe Hudson

The principle is the same. The leader has to be willing to be the one whose vision was wrong, whose direction needed to be redirected, whose delegation produced a result that was not what was wanted. The willingness is the price of the role. The competent people who never become CEOs are the ones who could not pay it.

## How This Connects to Identity

The frame that makes the principle hard is the frame of mistake-as-identity-claim. If a mistake means "I am the kind of leader who makes mistakes" — and that identity is intolerable — then mistakes have to be prevented at all costs, which means delegation has to be prevented (because it produces mistakes the leader cannot prevent).

The principle requires the leader to disentangle mistakes from identity. Mistakes are events that happen in systems. The leader who runs a system that produces some mistakes is not "a leader who makes mistakes" in the identity sense; they are a leader running a real system in the real world, which always produces mistakes. The identity claim has to be released for the system to function.

See [[iterative-mindset]] for the broader frame this connects to. Mistakes are iterations. The system that runs iterations produces faster learning than the system that prevents mistakes. The leader who has internalized iteration as the operational mode does not experience mistakes as identity threats; they experience them as data.

## The Empowering Inversion

The principle has a counterintuitive payoff. When the leader can hold mistakes without reactivity, the team becomes more empowered, not less.

"If you're delegating the way that I suggest, where you're coming up with a solution criteria and you're getting other people to solve the problem and you're doing the stuff on how you do accountability, all that stuff actually is deeply empowering for people. They feel empowered. And if they feel empowered, then they don't feel like you're responsible for their happiness. They don't feel like you're responsible. Like, we won, we lost, not you won, you lost." -- Joe Hudson

The empowerment comes from the team experiencing themselves as the actual agents of the work. Mistakes included. The team that knows the leader will not absorb their failures into the leader's identity is the team that takes the failures as their own and responds to them. The team that knows the leader will absorb every failure is the team that abdicates ownership preemptively.

## What This Pairs With

The principle pairs with [[hire-people-who-can-do-the-job-better-than-you]]. Even the right hire makes mistakes. The hiring principle gets the right person; the mistake-tolerance principle keeps them in the role.

The principle pairs with [[ask-the-three-accountability-questions]]. The accountability conversation is the leader's way of addressing mistakes without punishment. The questions surface what happened, what's next, and what's needed — none of which requires the leader to react punitively.

The principle pairs with [[iterative-mindset]]. Mistakes are iterations. The leader who has the iteration frame at the personal level can extend it to the organizational level.

## What This Is Not

**Not unconditional tolerance.** The principle is about mistakes, not about pattern incompetence. A team member who makes the same mistake repeatedly, after the accountability conversations have surfaced what should have changed, has a different issue. The principle is about being okay with mistakes as a feature of delegation, not about being okay with chronic underperformance.

**Not a license for sloppiness.** The leader who tolerates mistakes can still hold high standards. The two are not in tension. The standards are the criteria; the tolerance is for the mistakes that occur in pursuing the criteria. The team that knows the standards and is supported through the mistakes operates at a higher standard than the team that is punished for falling short.

**Not the absence of accountability.** Mistakes get addressed through the accountability conversation. They get named, the next move gets clarified, support gets offered. What does not happen is punishment, withdrawal, or rumination.

**Not a permission for the leader to be careless.** The leader is supposed to do their best work in setting vision and direction. The principle is that even doing their best work, the leader will make mistakes, and they have to be willing to live in the system that surfaces those mistakes. The permission is for the inevitability, not for laziness.

## Tone

Direct and matter-of-fact. Joe presents this as an operational requirement of the role. Leaders who cannot tolerate mistakes cannot delegate. Leaders who cannot delegate hit ceilings on what they can build. The principle is the math, not a moral position.
