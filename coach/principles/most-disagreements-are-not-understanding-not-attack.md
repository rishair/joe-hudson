# Most Disagreement Is Not-Understanding, Not Attack

## The Principle

When two people are stuck in conflict, the felt experience on both sides is usually "you are attacking me / you don't care about me / you are rejecting what I am." The actual mechanism, in most cases, is something much smaller: each person is running the other person's words through their own filters, and neither person is hearing what the other is actually saying. The disagreement that feels like rejection is, in most cases, simple mutual incomprehension.

The reframe is operationally useful because of an asymmetry in the body: anger toward incomprehension is hard to sustain, while anger toward attack is easy to sustain. The same conflict, reframed from "attack" to "ignorance" (in the soft sense — not knowing what you mean), loses most of its charge.

"It's really hard to be mad at ignorance. Not ignorance like you're ignorant, but ignorance like not understanding. It's really hard to be mad at not understanding. Really easy to be mad at 'you're attacking me.'" -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 5 Minutes And I'll Transform How You Listen Forever

## Why the Reframe Works

The anger response is calibrated to threat. When the body reads incoming words as attack, it mobilizes for defense. The defense produces escalation. The escalation reads as further attack to the other person, whose body then mobilizes too. Both nervous systems are now operating under the assumption that the other one is dangerous.

The body's threat-response does not calibrate to incomprehension in the same way. Someone not understanding you is not a threat — they are a limitation, a gap, a confusion. The nervous system does not flood for confusion the way it does for attack. The energy that was being spent on defense becomes available for the simpler work of clarifying.

The principle does not deny that there are real attacks. It points out that most of what is *felt* as attack inside a long-running relationship is not actually attack — it is two people whose interpretive filters are colliding. Once the comprehension lands, what looked like a fight becomes a small clarification.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The practice of [[mirror-until-they-confirm]] is the operational form of this principle. The mirroring practice slows the conflict down enough to reveal that most of the "disagreement" was misunderstanding. After enough fights resolve this way — where the shouting collapses the moment one person says "oh, I thought you meant X but you meant Y" — the reframe starts living in the body. The next fight no longer feels like attack by default. It feels like a comprehension gap by default.

"There's a way in which all of a sudden when you're listening like this, when you start working on the issue... you're like, oh, we're just looking at this problem like it's a patient on the table. It's not personal. It doesn't mean anybody's bad. We can just listen to each other and like understand each other and then everything's going to resolve itself." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 5 Minutes And I'll Transform How You Listen Forever

See [[patient-on-the-table]] for the related move at the team/work level. The two principles are the same insight applied to different domains: the moment the problem is treated as impersonal — whether that is a body's symptoms or a couple's miscommunication — the energy that was being spent on blame becomes available for solving.

## The Two Layers of the Reframe

The principle has two layers. The first is operational; the second is relational.

**Layer one (operational):** Most disagreement is comprehension gap, not actual disagreement. Once the comprehension lands, the disagreement often dissolves or shrinks dramatically. The work of conflict, in most cases, is the work of comprehension.

**Layer two (relational):** What you have been experiencing as "they don't care about me" or "they reject me" is, in most cases, "they don't yet see what I am saying." That is a very different felt experience. The relational temperature drops when the reframe lands.

"This isn't personal. This isn't them rejecting me. This is them not understanding me, right? This isn't like they think I'm bad. This is they don't get where I'm coming from. They like, they just, they can't comprehend it." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 5 Minutes And I'll Transform How You Listen Forever

The relational layer is what makes the principle so useful in long marriages. The accumulated heat in a long-running relationship is rarely about any single instance of misunderstanding. It is about the meaning each partner has assigned to the chain of misunderstandings: "they don't get me, therefore they don't love me, therefore I am alone in this." The principle interrupts that chain at the first link.

## The Trauma-Filter Mechanism

The reason both partners keep mishearing each other is not laziness or bad faith. Each partner has trauma-shaped filters that load specific meanings onto words. Words about money carry one meaning if one partner grew up poor and another if they grew up with money. Words about time carry one meaning if one partner was rushed by a parent and another if they were neglected. The two partners are running different decoders on the same sentences.

"We just had different ways of looking at the world and different ways of communicating. And our trauma filtered things in different ways." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 5 Minutes And I'll Transform How You Listen Forever

This is the same mechanism named in [[different-languages-of-love]] applied to conflict. In love, the imprint causes one partner's care to be unrecognized by the other. In conflict, the imprint causes one partner's words to be misread as attack by the other. Both are channel mismatches. Neither is bad faith.

The implication: there is no version of the work where you fix the partner's filters. You can only do two things:
1. Make your own filters more visible to yourself.
2. Use practices like [[mirror-until-they-confirm]] to keep checking whether the meaning landed.

The reframe to "not-understanding rather than attack" is the result of doing this work long enough that you can no longer maintain the attack story. You have seen too many examples of "oh, that wasn't what they meant" to keep assuming attack as the default reading.

## When This Principle Applies

- Recurring fights with a partner in a long relationship
- Conflict with a coworker, sibling, or parent where the same fight keeps showing up
- Cross-cultural or cross-generational disagreements where filter-mismatch is structurally guaranteed
- Negotiations where each side is operating in different vocabularies
- Any conflict where one or both parties report "I keep saying this and they just won't hear me"

## When This Principle Does Not Apply

The principle is not a denial of real attack. There are people who do attack — verbally, emotionally, or otherwise. There are cultures of contempt. There is real malice. The principle says that *most* disagreement inside a stable relationship is misunderstanding, not that all conflict is. The discernment between misunderstanding and actual attack is its own work.

The principle also does not work as a one-sided absolution. "It's just that you don't understand me" can become a sophisticated way of avoiding the substance of a real disagreement. The principle requires both partners to mirror, to be corrected, to update. Used unilaterally as cover, it becomes another form of [[chasing-understanding-as-control]].

## The Patient-on-the-Table Connection

Joe links this principle directly to [[patient-on-the-table]] — the impersonalize-the-problem move taught by Jaime Waydo. Both moves do the same thing at different scales: they take the personal heat out of the problem so the problem can be worked on as a problem rather than as a referendum on either person's character.

In a couple, the heat is "you are rejecting me." The reframe is "you are not yet understanding me." In a team, the heat is "whose fault is this." The reframe is "what are the symptoms, what's the next step." Both reframes move the energy from defense to work.

"It's like looking at the problem like it's a patient on the table. It's not personal. Like we just look at it like, oh, this is the problem. And then all of a sudden that's what happens... It's not personal. It doesn't mean anybody's bad. We can just listen to each other and like understand each other and then everything's going to resolve itself." -- Joe Hudson, Give Me 5 Minutes And I'll Transform How You Listen Forever

## When Someone Experiences This

- The fight is heating up; you remember the principle; you ask "wait, are we actually disagreeing or are we misunderstanding?" and the heat drops
- You catch yourself reading a sentence from your partner as rejection; you pause; you ask what else it could mean
- A repeating fight you have had for years suddenly resolves in 10 minutes because one of you finally heard what the other was actually saying
- The relief of realizing the person you love is not actually attacking you, even though it felt that way for years
- The accumulated weight of "they don't care about me" lifts when you start seeing how often "they didn't get me" was the more accurate read

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle as relief, not as instruction. The story comes after a year of fighting and mirroring with Tara. The reframe is presented as a discovery — the kind of thing that, once you see it, changes how the body responds to conflict. The tone is matter-of-fact about the mechanism ("really hard to be mad at not understanding") and warm about the relational consequence ("we can just listen to each other and like understand each other").
