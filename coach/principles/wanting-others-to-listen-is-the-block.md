# If You Want Them to Listen, Start Listening Yourself

## The Principle

The complaint "the people in my life don't listen to me well" has only one effective response: become a great listener yourself. Trying to get others to listen better is the very move that prevents them from listening.

"You don't. Stop it. Stop it. Stop it. Stop it. Stop it. If you really want everybody in your life to start listening to you better, start listening better." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## Why Trying Doesn't Work

Two mechanisms run in parallel.

**One: trying to make someone listen is a form of control, and people resist control.** The more you push, hint, complain, or shape your speech to get the other person to listen, the more they feel managed. The more they feel managed, the less they listen. The push produces the very thing you are pushing against. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the broader pattern.

**Two: concerning yourself with how they listen stops you from listening.** Your attention is on how they are receiving you. You are not in your own body. You are not in their body. You are in a third place — a meta-position monitoring the reception of your speech — which is a kind of management. The listening drops out of you while you are demanding it from them.

"If you concern yourself with how people are listening to you, then you are not listening to yourself and you're not listening to them. And it's not within your control. And the more you do it, the more you try to get someone to listen to you, the more controlled they feel and the less likely they are to listen to you." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The trap is recursive: your inability to be heard makes you try harder to be heard, which makes you less heard, which makes you try harder.

## The Way Out: Listen Better Yourself

The move is to drop the demand and become the listener. The reasons:

**It works directly.** The listening you give shapes the reality that arrives. See [[listening-shapes-the-reality-that-comes-back]]. The people around you will become better listeners *to you* because you are now better listeners *to them*. People reciprocate the listening they receive.

**It works indirectly.** The relief you experience from no longer trying to make them listen changes the field between you. They feel the absence of the pressure. The conditions for them to actually listen become available.

**It stops mattering as much.** A person who can listen to themselves does not need other people to listen to them in the same way. The hunger that was driving the original complaint diminishes. The need has moved inside.

"What we really want is to listen to ourselves. If we're listening to ourselves, what other people do doesn't matter so much." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

## The Underlying Want

Often the surface complaint "they don't listen to me" is a more specific buried complaint: "I'm not listening to myself, so I'm desperately trying to get someone else to do it for me." The other person is being asked to fill a hole that they cannot fill, because nobody can listen to you for you. They can listen with you; they cannot do the listening that needs to happen between you and yourself.

A person who has begun to listen to themselves stops outsourcing this need. The remaining wish for others to listen is less desperate, less demanding, less easily disappointed. It becomes a preference rather than a hunger. From that posture, paradoxically, it is more often met.

## The Same Principle Inside Being Misunderstood

The same algorithm runs when the complaint is "I keep being misunderstood." The variation does not matter — being-heard and being-understood are the same hunger with slightly different framing. The chasing produces the very absence it is trying to fill.

"There's this weird thing. You want to be understood, but the more you chase it, the less it happens." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

What the listener experiences when the speaker chases understanding is identical to what the listener experiences when the speaker chases listening: a sense of being controlled. The body of the listener reads the install attempt and pulls back. The pulling-back registers in the speaker as further misunderstanding. The speaker pushes harder. The loop tightens.

The speaker's own report of what happens when they are on the other side of such pushing is diagnostic. From the same session:

- **Joe:** "And when someone controls you, how do you respond?"
- **Savannah:** "Well, I hate that. ... I listen differently when someone's trying to control me. I listen in ways to figure out what they're actually trying to get rather than what is actually happening."
- *(Source: 2024-04-12 How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?)*

The speaker can describe the listener's defensive response to being chased. They have done it themselves. They have never connected the description to what they are doing on the other side of the same dynamic. Naming the connection is part of the unwinding.

The path out is the same as for the listening variant: stop chasing, return to self-listening. See [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] and [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]] for the practical moves. See [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]] for the structural reason the chasing cannot succeed.

The deeper wound underneath the chasing is often a second layer of hurt the speaker has not named: not just that they are not understood, but that the *goodness they are bringing* is not being recognized. See [[two-levels-of-hurt-in-being-misunderstood]].

## What This Looks Like in a Coaching Conversation

Someone says: "My partner just doesn't listen to me. I've tried everything. I've told them. I've cried. I've gone to therapy. They still don't listen."

The intervention is not advice on better communication. It is the inversion: how have *you* been listening to *them*? How have you been listening to *yourself*?

Often the person has been so focused on not being heard that they have stopped hearing — themselves, their partner, what their body is telling them in the conversation. The work is to bring attention back to those two places. The partner's listening will follow as a downstream effect, but the work is here, not over there.

This is the same move Joe makes with "how do I get my kids to listen?" — same answer. With "my employees don't listen" — same answer. With "my parents never listened" — the answer is still about your listening now, not theirs then.

## The Cascade That Often Follows

When someone actually does the inversion — stops trying to get others to listen and starts listening to themselves and then to the people around them — the cascade is often larger than they expected.

A specific client of Joe's had a relationship causing them so much pain it was constraining their work performance; they were focused on the relational suffering all day. One week they tried the move: listen to the partner with unconditional acceptance. Just that. They came to the next session asking, with some incredulity, whether it could be that simple — did the partner just need to be listened to?

The realization did not stay in the relationship:

"In that came this huge epiphany for them that just was realization that, oh wait, probably my employees don't feel listened to, my direct reports don't feel listened to. And they just start opening up and listening. And all of a sudden they understood the problems of their business, they understood the problems of their marriage. People felt closer to them, they shared more deeply, and in that sharing, and as they talked about their problems, the solutions started forming. Because as people talk about their problems, eventually they run into their own solutions." -- Joe Hudson, How To Listen Better

The pattern shows what the principle predicts. The client had been trying to fix one painful relationship by getting the partner to listen. The move that worked was the inversion: become the listener. Once the capacity was developed, it transferred to every relationship the client had — the marriage, the team, the direct reports. Problems that had been intractable became visible because the people around the client started telling them what was happening. Solutions formed in the speakers themselves, because people working out their problems out loud, to a real listener, often arrive at their own answers.

The lever was on the client's side the whole time. Pulling it changed the marriage *and* the business *and* the relationships with the team — none of which was the original presenting problem.

## The Forbidden Direction

What you should not do:

- Repeat yourself with more emphasis until they "get it."
- Stage a confrontation to force them to acknowledge that they don't listen.
- Use the language of listening (or even of Joe's teachings) as a weapon to demand listening.
- Withdraw to punish the other person for not listening.
- Read articles on listening with the intent to show your partner.

All of these are versions of the same trying-to-control move. All of them put the energy in the wrong place. All of them produce a small worsening of the very thing they are trying to fix.

## The Frustration

It is normal to feel frustrated by this principle when you first hear it. The hidden hope inside the complaint "they don't listen to me" is often: tell me how to make them listen. The answer "stop trying to make them listen" can land as dismissive — *but my pain is real, my partner really is a bad listener, why am I being told to do more work*?

The frustration is fair. The principle is not that the other person's listening is fine. It is that the lever you have is not on their side. You can spend years pulling on a lever that does not exist (their listening) and never move anything. Or you can pull on the lever that does exist (your listening) and watch the field rearrange. The latter is the only path that actually moves the situation.

## Tone

Direct, almost playful. The "stop it stop it stop it" repetition is the tone marker — Joe does not soften this one. The bluntness is the medicine; softening it would let the listener keep negotiating with the principle. The follow-through is more spacious — *if you want people to listen, become a great listener* — delivered as the simple obvious move that was available the whole time.
