# Make the Practice Juicy (Not Another Should)

## The Principle

Any practice that is undertaken as a should fails. The body knows the difference between a thing being done because it is required and a thing being done because it is enjoyed. The same body-awareness practice can produce two completely different outcomes depending on whether it is approached as obligation or as exploration. The practice that lasts is the one the practitioner makes pleasurable. The practice that does not last is the one the practitioner forces.

The instruction to anyone starting a practice — somatic awareness, emotional inquiry, daily emotional yoga, any of them — is to ask: how do you make this juicy? How do you make it exciting? How do you inspire yourself to do it? The motivation has to come from pleasure, not from compliance.

"So I'd say, take your time, have a lot of patience with yourself, and how would you make it exciting? It's like, oh, I'm gonna go to Italy in my gut. Like, how is it exciting to be an internal explorer. How do you inspire yourself to do it? It's not another thing I have to do 'cause that's what AOA says. How do you make it juicy?" -- Tara Hawley

## Why Should-Based Practice Fails

A should is the configuration of forcing the body to do what the body does not want to do. The body registers this as a small assault and protects against it. Over the first few days the practitioner can override the protection through willpower. Over the first few weeks the protection wins. The practitioner stops doing the practice and adds a layer of self-judgment for not doing it.

This is the standard arc of new-year-resolution practices. Strong start, weak middle, abandoned end, residue of self-attack. The arc is not a personal failure. It is what should-based practice produces structurally.

The body cannot distinguish between an external task-master and an internal task-master. Either way it is being forced. The internal task-master with a copy of the AOA newsletter is no more welcome to the body than an external one with a clipboard. The body responds to both the same way: by going dead to the request.

For more on this mechanism, see [[should-as-threat]] and [[the-trying-itself-becomes-the-obstacle]].

## What Juicy Looks Like

Juicy means the body is interested in the practice. Juicy means the practitioner is curious, playful, slightly anticipating the next session. Juicy means the practice has the flavor of going on a small adventure, not the flavor of going to the dentist.

Concrete reframes Tara offers:

- "I'm gonna go to Italy in my gut." Reframe the gut as a destination to be visited. What is it like in there today? What is the weather? What is the texture? Body awareness as travel.
- "How is it exciting to be an internal explorer?" Reframe the practitioner as an explorer of unknown internal terrain. The gut, the heart, the throat, the shoulders — all of these are territories that have never been mapped from the inside.
- "Take little baby bites, go down, check it out." Reframe the practice as snacking on small experiences rather than performing a sustained discipline.
- "Yummy self-care." For practices of restoration after intense feeling work, the word is "yummy." The hot bath. The walk. The meal. The nap. These have to be enjoyable, not earned.

The vocabulary itself does work. "Juicy" and "yummy" and "exciting" and "explorer" and "baby bites" all carry the energy of a child playing rather than an adult complying. The vocabulary signals to the body what configuration the practice is being held in.

## The Same Practice Performed Two Ways

This principle is the same teaching as [[presence-is-pleasure-not-trying]] applied at the level of practice design rather than at the level of presence itself. Joe describes the same insight from the other direction in the dishes-and-eating story: doing the dishes slowly with pleasure produced presence; eating slowly without pleasure produced rigid performance. Same external behavior, opposite internal configuration.

Tara's instruction is the prescriptive form of that insight: do not just notice that pleasure produces the result. Design the practice so that pleasure is the entry. Choose the framing that makes the body excited to participate. The framing is part of the practice, not a separate matter of attitude.

## Why This Matters Beyond Body Awareness

Tara names this in the context of building somatic awareness, but the principle generalizes to every practice in the methodology. [[emotional-inquiry]] performed as a should produces management; performed with the wonder of a child finding a turtle, it produces inquiry. [[daily-emotional-yoga]] performed as a discipline produces strain; performed as the body's permission to release, it produces release. [[staying-in-your-body]] performed as an attention requirement produces a clenched feeling-watcher; performed as a delight in the body's sensation, it produces flow.

Every practice has a juicy version and a should version. The same external behavior. Completely different outcomes. The principle applies to whichever practice the AI coach is supporting.

The coaching corollary: when a person reports they are not sticking with a practice, the diagnostic is rarely "they lack discipline." The diagnostic is usually "they are doing it as a should." The intervention is not more willpower. The intervention is to find what would make it juicy for them.

## The "How Would You Inspire Yourself" Question

The diagnostic question to ask the practitioner who is not sticking with the practice:

"How do you inspire yourself to do it?"

The question rejects the standard motivators (compliance, fear, guilt, achievement) and asks for the practitioner's actual inspiration. The answer is the practice's anchor. Without an answer to this question, the practice does not have an anchor and will be abandoned the moment the willpower runs out.

The question is also useful preventively. Before the practitioner starts the practice, ask: what would make this juicy for you? What is your version of going to Italy in the gut? Build the answer into the design of the practice from the start.

## What This Looks Like as Coaching

- When a client describes a practice they are doing as obligation, name it. "That sounds like a should. What would make that juicy for you?"
- When a client reports they keep starting and stopping a practice, do not prescribe more discipline. Ask what would make the practice something they look forward to.
- When prescribing a new practice, do not deliver it as instruction. Deliver it as invitation, and ask the client to design their own juicy frame for it.
- When a client uses heavy or burdensome language about the work ("I have to," "I need to," "I should"), notice the language. The configuration of the practice is already failing in the description.

The reframe is short and the work is in finding the version of the practice that fits the person. Some people are explorers. Some are artists. Some are scientists. Some are playful. Some are sensual. The juicy frame has to fit the person's actual aesthetic, not Tara's image of Italy.

## What This Differs From

- From [[presence-is-pleasure-not-trying]]: that principle is about the structure of presence itself. This principle is about the design of practice. The two are the same mechanism applied at different levels — pleasure is the operational ground of both the state and the practice that cultivates the state.
- From [[pleasure-tells-system-its-safe]]: that principle is about why pleasure builds resilience at the nervous-system level. This principle is the application: design practices to use pleasure as the entry rather than reaching for it as a reward.
- From [[joy-as-natural-state]]: that principle is the metaphysical claim that joy is the baseline. This principle is the practical instruction for designing practice consistent with that claim.

## Tone

Tara delivers this with playfulness. The word "juicy" itself is part of the teaching — it is irreverent toward the seriousness most practitioners bring to spiritual work. The framing is gentle and slightly cheeky. The body should be approached the way a kid approaches a new playground, not the way an adult approaches their tax return.
