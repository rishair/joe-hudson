# If You Don't Defend Yourself, Nobody Can Attack You

## The Principle

Attack requires a defense to attack against. Remove the defense and the attack has nothing to land on. The attacker tires itself out swinging at water.

"If you don't defend yourself, nobody can attack you. It's like the Zen — if you're being attacked by a sword, be the ocean. Like there's no defense in the ocean. Like they can whack away at it. They'll just tire themselves out." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

This is the operational principle behind dropping the [[shame-hot-potato]]: the throws of the potato are attacks that work only because the throwee defends. Without the defense, there is no purchase. The attack still happens, but it does not connect.

## Why It Feels Like a Trap Before Doing It

The intuition before trying it: "if I don't defend myself, I will be destroyed." This intuition is wrong about what gets destroyed but correct that something does. The defended sense of self is what gets destroyed. The actual self does not.

"It's scary to do at the moment because you're like, oh my God, I'm going to give, someone's going to take advantage of me. But when you do it, you realize, oh, like nobody, if you don't defend yourself, nobody can attack you." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The fear is correct that the defended self is at risk in the move. The fear is wrong that the person is at risk. What is at risk is the construct that has been organized to defend. That construct is not what the person actually is. See [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]].

## The Zen Sword Image

Joe attributes the metaphor to Zen. A sword attacks; the ocean does not parry. The sword strikes water; water does not push back; the sword has nothing to hit. The swordsman swings until they tire. The water remains water.

The principle is not about being passive or formless. The ocean is not nothing. It is everything in that volume. The swordsman fails not because the ocean is weak but because the ocean is not the kind of thing the sword is built to defeat. The same is true of a non-defended human: the human is fully present, just not configured as the kind of target the attack assumes.

## The Twitter Example

Joe uses Twitter as a live laboratory for this. Periodically he gets attacked. The practice he describes:

"It's one of my favorite parts about Twitter is that occasionally I get crazy attacked and I'm like, oh wow. And then like, I'm like, oh, how, how do I be undefended here? How do I like, and oftentimes I just, I can like laugh. There was some guy named like Lurker McLurkface or something like that. He was like trolling me and I was like, oh wow, I get, and then I could just like laugh. And so it's just like, there's something, there's something really sweet about that for me in social media. It's like this perpetual exercise about how to be non-defended in my approach with people." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The platform delivers a near-continuous supply of attacks that mean almost nothing personally. This makes it ideal for practicing non-defense. The stakes are low enough that the body can stay open. The frequency is high enough that the practice can compound.

## The Quality of the Laugh Matters

Brett raises an important caveat: the laugh can be defensive. The laugh can be over-the-top, can be a power move, can be the act of being above the attacker. If the laugh has any of those qualities, the move has slid back into defense.

The diagnostic is the body. If the body is invigorated and undefended, the move worked. If there is still tension, still need to show the attacker something, still need to win, the shame is still operating.

"If you don't feel empowered and invigorated, then you're still in it. You're still in the shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The same applies to non-defense in any setting. The signature of the genuine move is empowered invigoration. The counterfeits are dismissal, smugness, performed indifference. All of these are defense at a higher abstraction layer.

## The Two-Part Stance

The non-defense stance has a specific shape that distinguishes it from collapse.

"I had to come to a couple stages in my life where the only thing I could do was be non-defended. The only thing that I could do is just every day show up and say, 'I'm sorry,' or, you know, like, 'I accept full responsibility. I'll take on all of the blame and shame from you. I won't take it on. I won't give it to myself, but I'll take all of that on.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The two parts:

**1. Receive the blame.** Take it on. Let it land. Do not deflect. Do not defend.

**2. Do not take it on.** Do not internalize it as shame. Do not let it become a story about being bad. Receive it without consuming it.

The combination is what non-defense actually is. Without the first part, the stance is just managed distance. Without the second part, the stance is collapse. With both parts, the stance becomes the ocean: receiving everything, becoming none of it.

## Why This Erodes Narcissism

The stance, practiced repeatedly, dissolves the structures of self that need defending. Narcissism on Joe's reading is largely a project of putting oneself above others quietly or overtly, and not feeling certain feelings. Non-defense requires feeling all of the feelings the defense was preventing. So it strips the substrate that narcissism runs on.

"It like, it erodes away — we're all narcissistic on some level, but it erodes away our narcissism so, so quickly because it, it requires us to feel. If we do that, we have to feel all the stuff. And narcissism, to a large part, is just not feeling feelings on the emotional level. On the intellectual level, it's very much about our — each of our narcissism is the way that we put ourselves above others quietly or outright." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The stance is the practice and the practice is the work. Each cycle of undefended reception strips a piece of the defended self that was organized to avoid feeling.

## The Jesus-on-the-Cross Image

Joe uses Jesus on the cross as a metaphor for what the non-defense practice actually requires: not just being non-defended, but feeling all the pain of being attacked. The death of personality happens through the feeling, not through a stoic refusal to feel.

"I love the metaphor of Jesus in this particular way because it's like he didn't just get on the cross in the metaphor. He, 'why have you forsaken me?' He felt the pain. Like it wasn't just like, 'I'm great. This is no problem. We're all going to be in kingdom of God today.' Like it was, it was like he felt all of that struggle. And the result in that metaphor, the result is that like there is a death of the personality. And you're right, like the sense of self dies through that process. That undefended process is like one of the quickest ways I know to burn through our false sense of self." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The metaphor matters because it corrects a common misreading of "be the ocean" as "stay above it all." The ocean is not above. The ocean is in it, fully. The water that takes the sword takes it. The sword cuts through nothing because the water flows around it. But the water is touched by everything.

## The Long-Arc Result: Immunity to Shame

The practice, sustained over time, produces a structural change in how shame can land on the practitioner. The shame the throws were designed to install stops sticking. The practitioner becomes — in a specific operational sense — immune to shame.

"So the more that you learn not to defend yourself, the more you can start to notice your inherent goodness. You start to become immune to shame because when you see people shame you, it's like, oh, you can just see they don't want to feel bad about themselves or don't want to feel out of control." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The mechanism: defense is the act of treating the accusation as something that might be true and might harm you. Without defense, repeated enough, the accusation stops being treated that way. The accusation becomes information about the accuser's state, not data about the practitioner's worth. The shame the accusation was meant to install has nowhere to land because the receiver has stopped agreeing with the premise that there is something there to be ashamed of.

This is the same shape as the connection to [[inherent-goodness]]: dropping defense reveals the goodness underneath, and the visible goodness then prevents new shame from installing. The two effects compound. The more the goodness is visible, the less the shame installs. The less the shame installs, the more the goodness is visible.

See [[immune-to-shame-through-non-defense]] for the principle as its own article. The relationship between non-defense and inherent goodness is bidirectional: each strengthens the other through the practice.

## What This Principle Is Not

**It is not letting yourself be abused.** Non-defense does not mean staying in situations where harm is happening. [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] still applies. You can be undefended in front of someone and also choose to leave. The boundary and the defense are separate operations.

**It is not stoic suppression.** The pain of the attack is felt. The non-defense is not the absence of feeling. It is the absence of the wall the defense would have built. The water is wet through.

**It is not above it all.** Anything that has the felt-sense of being above the attacker is defense in a sophisticated form. See [[spiritual-narcissism]]. The genuine move has no above. The ocean is not above the sword.

**It is not a strategy.** The moment "be the ocean" becomes a technique deployed to win, it becomes another move in the attack. The principle has to be lived from the inside, not performed.

## The Daily-Apology Variant

A specific practice that runs on this principle: every day show up and say "I'm sorry, I accept full responsibility." Not as a martyrdom move. As a daily practice of dropping defense at the very moment the defense would normally fire. The receiver of the apology may not even understand what you are apologizing for. The apology is for the practitioner, not for them.

The mechanism: the daily upright apology forces the practitioner to feel what the defense was preventing them from feeling. Over time, the defense pattern weakens. Over more time, it dissolves. See [[apologize-as-daily-practice-for-bad-habits]] and [[upright-apology]].

## Why This Works Where Defense Fails

The defended fighter wants to win. The winning requires the opponent to lose. The opponent's losing requires their continued engagement. The defended stance therefore guarantees prolonged conflict.

The undefended fighter has no need to win. There is no opponent in the same sense. The encounter can end the moment the other person tires. There is nothing in the undefended person that requires the other person to stay engaged.

This is why an opponent with an open heart who is still determined to win is more effective than a defended opponent. The Bhagavad Gita's instruction to Arjuna: fight the war, do not close your heart. See [[joes-board-member]] for an extended illustration.

## The Diagnostic Question

When you have done the work and are checking whether you actually dropped the defense or just relocated it:

> "Do I feel empowered and invigorated, or do I feel tension somewhere?"

If empowered and invigorated, the defense was actually dropped. If tense or above-it-all or smug, the defense moved to a higher layer. The body is the diagnostic. The mind will lie about whether the defense is still operating; the body will not.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You are being attacked and choose not to defend. Something settles. The attacker swings at nothing.
- You apologize fully without performing self-flagellation. You feel cleaner, not smaller.
- You let a partner's anger land without defending. The anger has nowhere to go. It resolves.
- You read a critical comment on the internet. You laugh in a way that does not have an above-it quality. The comment loses force.
- You are in a meeting and someone attacks your idea. You let it in. You consider it. You respond from the consideration, not from the defense. The meeting shifts.
