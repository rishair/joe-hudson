# Connection Does Not Depend on Anyone Else

## The Principle

Connection with yourself and with the moment is not contingent on what another person does. The other person can refuse to engage, leave, attack, or stay disconnected themselves. None of that breaks your connection unless you agree to let it.

"You being in connection with yourself and others is not dependent on anybody else. Because being in connection with what is, to be in contact with what is, doesn't require anybody else." -- Joe Hudson, Connection: A State Beyond States

The principle inverts the common frame: "I'm out of connection with this person because they did X." The correct frame: "I have allowed what they did to pull me out of connection with myself." The agency is internal. The cost of misplacing it is permanent dependency on others' behavior for one's own state.

## Why This Matters

If connection is the bottom-line principle for life (see [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]]), and connection depends on what other people do, then the bottom line is hostage to other people. Every interaction becomes a negotiation in which the other party can withdraw connection and impose disconnection on you.

The internal-locus version makes the bottom line stable. Other people's behavior may be painful, infuriating, or grievous. None of it can stop you from being in connection with what is, including with the felt reality of what they are doing.

This also resolves the apparent paradox of how to love people who are hurting you. You can. The love does not require their cooperation. It does require them to stop having a particular external relationship with you (the business does not continue, the contact does not continue) -- but the love itself is not theirs to revoke.

## The Story Joe Tells

A long-term client did something intentional to hurt Joe. The trigger was that the love was scary -- it reminded her of love she had received in childhood that came alongside abuse, and she had a trauma response.

"I expressed some anger, I expressed sadness, I expressed hurt. And at some point I said, I'm not gonna let you stop me from feeling connected and love for you. I'm not gonna do that because that hurts me. So you're doing all this stuff, that's fine. And I'm not gonna stop loving you. I'm gonna stop doing business with you. 'Cause I'm not gonna get her -- I'm not gonna allow that damage to be done again, but I'm not gonna stop loving you." -- Joe Hudson

Two things happened in that sentence. First, the relationship structure (the business) ended. Second, the love did not. The two are separable. Withdrawing from contact does not require withdrawing love. Continuing to love does not require continuing to subject oneself to harm.

This is also a working example of [[upright-undefended-love]]: love that does not submit and does not harden. The boundary protects the body. The love stays open.

## Two Caveats Joe Names

**1. Sometimes the person is already gone.** This kind of statement requires that the person is still around to hear it. Sometimes someone has already left and there is no opportunity to say it. That does not change the principle. You can continue to love them silently. You do not need their permission or even their presence.

**2. It can sound creepy or stalkery if the energy is wrong.** Brett raises the worry: in a romantic context, "you can leave but you can't stop me from loving you" delivered with the wrong energy is stalking. Joe agrees. The principle is internal. The expression is not always required. Sometimes the felt sense is shared silently and that is enough.

"There's other cases -- I'm in a grocery store, I see somebody, I feel like a deep sense of connection. I don't have to say anything about it. You know, I often walk around the world feeling like a tremendous amount of love for people I don't know. And I'm not like walking around going, 'I love you, I love you, I love you.' It's not happening." -- Joe Hudson

The specific case (the long-term business relationship) called for explicit expression. Most cases do not.

## The Limit Case: Someone Refuses to See It

The principle does not promise that the other person will receive it. Their capacity to receive love is limited by their own state. Some will project that the love is needy. Some will project that it is stalkery. Some will project that it is a manipulation.

"That's their reaction to being -- their own presence and their own love. It's their own sense of connection. 'Cause what you'll notice is that any person has kind of the capacity to get to a certain level of connection, and then that's kind of their, okay, now it's getting more intense. Now I'm gonna lift up, I'm gonna go get into the phone, I'm gonna go watch television, I'm gonna go do heroin, whatever it is, there's some kind of some level of it that is, within their normal range, and then they're gonna react to it. And that's why it can't depend on anybody else." -- Joe Hudson

This is why the principle has to hold even when the other person rejects what is being offered. The connection is yours. Their reception is theirs. They are not the same transaction.

## The Metta Parallel

Brett offers a useful analogy: metta meditation. The practitioner sends love to all beings, including those they have never met and those they would not enjoy meeting. Kim Jong-un cannot stop someone from doing metta. The recipient does not have to know, agree, or reciprocate. The practice still produces the effect.

"Nobody in the world, Kim Jong-un can't stop you from doing a metta meditation in which you are sending love to all beings. And that's your own internal experience. And that is beneficial for you and has impact on all those that you have contact with in some sense due to the impact on your consciousness." -- Brett Kistler, summarized

This is the secular form of the religious principle "love thy neighbor." The instruction does not specify that the neighbor will be lovable, will reciprocate, or will know they are being loved. The instruction is about your relationship to the act of loving, not about the neighbor's response.

## The Subtle Trap

The principle can be misused as a way to override grief, anger, or appropriate withdrawal. "I will not let them stop me from loving them" can become a spiritual bypass that prevents the person from feeling the hurt of what was done.

In Joe's example, the love was preserved alongside the anger, the sadness, and the hurt. He expressed all of them. The withholding of business was a real consequence. The love was not used to dissolve the appropriate response.

See [[loving-to-transform]] for the related anti-pattern: using love as a strategy to change someone. The internal love is not a strategy. It is a state. If the love is being used to make the other person come back, behave differently, or apologize, it has been corrupted into a tool. See [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]] for the analogous trap with compassion.

## Locus of Control as Partial Truth

There is a counter-balance to the principle that is worth naming. Locating connection entirely in the self is true to a point and then untrue. There is also a level at which connection arrives as a gift, through surrender, through the grace of being held by something larger than oneself.

"There's the locus of control being yourself. That's true to a point and then very not true to a point. And the not true to the point is where it's not where it's dependent on somebody else, but you can see that it's a gift. Like there's a deeper level of connection that occurs when there's surrender in you. And so to think that you're in the locus of control often makes it so that, so that you forget that you're here by the grace of God is a way to say it, but that you're here by the grace of the laws of physics and the universe and nature and existence." -- Joe Hudson

The principle is therefore: connection does not depend on anyone else, AND there is a level of connection that requires surrender to what is beyond the self. The "not requiring others" liberates one from interpersonal hostage-taking. The "requiring surrender" prevents the principle from collapsing into ego inflation. Both are operating.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The discovery that hurt and love can coexist; the love does not require the hurt to be over.
- The relief of not needing the other person to behave a certain way before being able to love them.
- The recognition that withdrawal of contact is a separate decision from withdrawal of love.
- The first time saying "I will not let you stop me from loving you" with no expectation that the other person will change because of it.
- The settling realization that one's own state is no longer dictated by what other people do.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone says "I lost connection with X because they did Y" and is locating their state in the other's behavior.
- When someone is preparing to set a boundary and is conflating leaving the relationship with stopping loving.
- When [[connection]] has been introduced and the person is asking how it works when the other party refuses.
- When [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] has surfaced and the person is wondering how to draw a hard boundary without hardening their heart.
- When trauma material has come up and the person is reactive to a behavior they used to be open to.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[connection]] as the underlying concept.
- Toward [[upright-undefended-love]]: the operational stance.
- Toward [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]: drawing a hard boundary while staying in connection.
- Toward [[loving-to-transform]]: the anti-pattern of using the love to change the other person.
- Toward [[surrender-to-ineffable-vs-person]] when the gift dimension of connection is the next layer.
- Toward gratitude as the simplest practice that surfaces the always-available connection.
