# The Chaser and the Love-Remover Both Experience the Other's Move as Death

## The Principle

In most established couples, both partners sort into one of two stances around how love and conflict are handled. One partner is the chaser — the person who pursues, reaches, asks for the connection. The other partner is the love-remover — the person who pulls back, takes space, withdraws when overwhelmed. The configuration is not symmetrical in surface behavior, but it is exactly symmetrical in trauma: each partner's natural move is the precise trigger of the other's deepest trauma.

"One person is usually like the chaser and one person is usually like the, yeah, okay, like, and they kind of -- they're more like the love remover. So one's like chasing the love and one's like removing the love typically. So for the person who's the chaser, it is like death, internal death to like be abandoned. And so oftentimes the person who's the abandoner, it's like death to be chased. Like, oh, just like, give me a fucking break. Give me a moment. Like, leave me alone for a second. I need to find myself. And so both of them feel that way. They feel like, like a little bit of death for the person, especially if it's like a big trauma in their life." -- Joe Hudson, The Healthy Way To Leave A Fight

The chaser experiences the partner leaving as death-by-abandonment. The love-remover experiences the partner refusing to let them leave as death-by-engulfment. Both are real trauma responses. Neither is more legitimate than the other. The structure is symmetrical even when the surface behaviors look opposite.

## Why This Symmetry Matters in Fights

Without recognizing this structure, each partner reads the other as the problem:
- The chaser sees the love-remover as cold, distant, unwilling to engage.
- The love-remover sees the chaser as clingy, suffocating, unable to give space.

Both readings are accurate to what the partner is experiencing. Both miss the trauma underneath. The chaser's pursuit is not personality; it is the chaser's nervous system fighting the death of abandonment. The love-remover's withdrawal is not personality; it is the love-remover's nervous system fighting the death of engulfment.

When this symmetry is not seen, every fight becomes a contest about whose trauma response is correct. The chaser argues that the love-remover should not need so much space. The love-remover argues that the chaser should not need so much closeness. The argument has no resolution because each partner is trying to argue the other out of a trauma response.

## The Configuration in Joe's Own Marriage

Joe was the chaser in his early relationship with Tara. Her natural move — saying "I just need a moment for this to take a break" — was producing the exact death his trauma was organized around avoiding.

"Every time she was like, oh, I just need a moment for this to take a break. I would like -- my whole system would like freak the fuck out. I'd be like, what the hell is going on here? And I would lose -- I would totally lose myself. And, and I didn't understand it." -- Joe Hudson, The Healthy Way To Leave A Fight

The "freak the fuck out" and "totally lose myself" are the chaser's trauma firing. Tara was not doing anything wrong. She was making a self-care move. But the move had no frame around it, and so Joe's nervous system read it as the abandonment it had been organized to expect.

The fix was not for Tara to stop needing space. The fix was for the leaving to carry the [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline|timeline]] that would let Joe's nervous system register: this is bounded, this is not forever, the love is not being removed.

## The Same Instrument Resolves Both Sides

The remarkable feature of this configuration is that one practice addresses both partners' trauma at once. The timeline-bound leaving is the single instrument:
- For the chaser, the timeline says: the abandonment is not happening. The love is not being removed. The partner is coming back, and by a specific time.
- For the love-remover, the timeline says: you are allowed to leave. The chaser will receive the leaving as care because it is framed. You can take what you need without triggering the death of the person you love.

"All of a sudden, these breaks became like really great ways to have a fight. It wasn't a death anymore. And she could do it with an open heart... She could feel safe because she didn't have to feel like she was being yelled at. I could feel safe because I didn't -- wasn't going to get abandoned." -- Joe Hudson, The Healthy Way To Leave A Fight

Both partners' worst trauma is addressed by the same sentence. The chaser is held by the explicit "I am coming back." The love-remover is held by the explicit permission to leave that comes with the partner being able to receive the leaving.

## Relationship to the Broader Anxious-Avoidant Pattern

This is the in-relationship form of [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]]. The anxious-avoidant frame in dating shows up as chasing the unavailable person — the chaser wires love and pursuit together; the avoidant wires closeness and threat together. Once these two end up in a committed relationship, the same configuration shows up not in who they pick but in how they handle moments of friction.

The crucial distinguishing feature in established couplehood: both partners have already chosen each other. The question is no longer "will I find an available partner" or "will I learn to want availability." The question is "how do we move through the fights without each of our trauma responses producing the other's worst nightmare." The answer is a small set of practices, of which the timeline-bound leaving is the most concrete.

## The Symmetry Is What Makes the Configuration Stable

This configuration is one of the cleanest examples of [[complementary-trauma-surfaces]]. The chaser's trauma response and the love-remover's trauma response interlock. They are not just compatible; they are the precise pieces that fit each other's shape. This is why these couples find each other. The chaser's body recognizes the love-remover as "the person whose distance I will pursue, the way I learned to pursue love." The love-remover's body recognizes the chaser as "the person whose pursuit I can withdraw from, the way I learned to find self by leaving."

The symmetry is part of what makes the work possible. The work is not for one partner to change while the other stays the same. Both partners are doing exactly equivalent work. See [[your-trauma-is-equally-matched-with-your-partner]] for the broader principle: in any couple that has been together long enough, the trauma is matched. Neither partner is more advanced. Neither has less work to do.

## When Both Partners See It

When both partners can see the configuration, the fights change quality completely. The chaser can hear "I need a break" and not collapse, because the framing is there. The love-remover can take the break and not feel guilty, because they know the framing protects the chaser. Both partners can then bring more honesty and more heat to the actual content of the fight, because the meta-fear of losing the relationship has been turned down.

"That, like, level of safety and trust allowed for the conflict to just be so much more civil, so much more loving, so much more open-hearted, and yet still have the conflict that we needed. So that we could take our relationship to the next level." -- Joe Hudson, The Healthy Way To Leave A Fight

The configuration does not disappear. Joe and Tara still had a chaser and a love-remover. What changed was that both knew it, both had language for it, and both had the practice of the timeline-bound leaving. The fights then served the relationship rather than threatening it.

## Tone

Joe describes this with self-recognition and humor. He names himself as the chaser. He names the experience as "death." He is not pathologizing either side — he is reporting what his own nervous system did, what Tara's nervous system did, and how they figured out the move that worked for both. The tone is warm and matter-of-fact about a trauma structure that most couples are operating inside without seeing.

## What This Is Not

This is not a frame for assigning blame. The chaser is not the broken partner who needs to "stop being needy." The love-remover is not the broken partner who needs to "stop pulling away." Both are doing what their respective trauma installed. The work is not to flip the configuration but to build the practices that let both partners' trauma responses coexist without producing the other's death.

This is not a permanent identity. The same person can be the chaser in one relationship and the love-remover in another, depending on what the other partner activates. The configuration is relational. It describes the role this person is currently playing in this dynamic, not their fixed character.

This is not always cleanly one-or-the-other. Some couples have both partners running both moves at different times. The principle still holds: each move has its corresponding death on the other side, and the work is to make the moves with the frame that keeps the other person's nervous system out of the death state.
