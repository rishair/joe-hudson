# Anxiously Pleasing Is Leaving, Just Like Anger Is Leaving

## The Principle

When someone is in a difficult moment with another person — the other is upset, angry, in pain — the felt menu has three options that pretend to be different from each other: anxiously try to please the other, get angry at the other, get angry at the self. All three are forms of leaving. None of the three is being present with what is actually happening.

The principle disrupts the felt sense that the pleasing option is qualitatively different from the angry options. The pleasing option feels safer, more loving, more caring. The body's automatic reach for it is built on the implicit story that pleasing is the form of staying-with that the other two options are not. The principle names this story as wrong. Pleasing is a form of leaving. The leaving has different aesthetics — softer surface, more apparent care, less visible aggression — but at the level of presence, the pleasing person is no more there than the angry person.

"You're not there in either way. Anxiously trying to please him, you've left. Angry at him, you're a little more there if you're angry at him or yourself, I guess, to some degree, but you're still, you still left." -- Joe Hudson, Watch Her 2-year Relationship Paralysis Dissipate

The asymmetry Joe names matters. The angry options are *slightly* more present than the pleasing option — there is at least a charge in the body, a contact with one's own feeling, a willingness to take up some space. The pleasing option has none of that. The pleasing person has vacated entirely. But the difference is degrees of leaving, not the difference between leaving and staying. The staying option is not on the menu at all.

## The Mechanism

Each of the three options is a way to avoid being present with the difficult thing happening between two people. The difficult thing is usually the pain in the other person and the speaker's felt response to that pain.

**Anxiously pleasing** vacates the self by relocating the speaker's attention entirely to the other person's state. The speaker is no longer experiencing themselves; they are experiencing the other person and adjusting their behavior to manage the other's state. The body's center of gravity moves outside the body. Whatever the speaker would feel if they were inside themselves is offstage; what is onstage is the management.

**Anger at the other** vacates the self by pushing the energy outward in a way that targets the other rather than meeting what is actually happening. The anger is not nothing — it is a real charge in the body, a real movement — but it is functioning as an exit from being with the other person's pain. The anger says: I cannot bear that you are in pain, so I am going to be angry at you for being in pain, and now I do not have to be with the pain.

**Anger at the self** vacates the self by turning the same energy inward. The structural equivalence between this option and the previous one is the [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] mechanism. The anger has the same shape; only the direction has changed. The inward direction shows up as shame and self-blame, which then occupies the speaker's attention in the same way that outward anger would have occupied attention with the other. Either way, the speaker is not available to what is actually happening.

The staying option that is not on the menu would be: feel the heartbreak of the other person's pain, while remaining present in one's own body, without managing them, without pushing them away, without making it about one's own wrongness. The staying option is hard precisely because all three of the menu options have been the survival strategies the person learned in childhood. The body knows how to do the three; it does not yet know how to do the staying.

## Why the Felt Sense Makes Pleasing Feel Different

The body has been trained — usually in childhood, by a caregiver whose state was unpredictable and consequential — to read the pleasing option as the safe option. The training is functional in the original conditions. A child who pleases a volatile parent does sometimes reduce the immediate risk. The body remembers this and reaches for the same move in adult relationships where the original conditions no longer apply.

The felt sense that pleasing is the safe option also picks up moral packaging over time. The pleasing person tells themselves they are being kind, being loving, being a good partner. The angry options are framed as failures of love. The pleasing option is framed as the loving option. This framing is part of what makes the configuration so durable: the speaker cannot give up the pleasing without giving up the self-image of being a loving person.

The principle is designed to disrupt the framing at the level of presence. Whatever you are doing when you anxiously please, you are not actually there with the other person. You are with the management of the other person. The other person is alone in the room with their pain, just as they would be if you were yelling at them. The aesthetic of the leaving is different. The leaving is the same.

## What the Principle Produces in a Session

Naming the principle takes the pleasing option off the menu as the obvious right answer. The speaker can no longer call the pleasing the loving thing to do. The three options are now visible as three forms of the same absence. The question shifts from "how do I do the pleasing better?" to "what is the staying that is not any of these three?"

In the source session, the question Joe asks after naming the trilemma drives this directly:

"So what makes anxiously trying to please more acceptable than anger at him or yourself?"

The question forces the speaker to defend the asymmetry that they have been operating on. The defense usually fails. The speaker says some version of "I don't know" or "it just feels like the right thing to do" or, in the source session, "they're both really bad." The "they're both really bad" is the recognition landing. Once both are bad in the same way, the speaker can stop choosing between them and start looking at what is being avoided by the whole menu.

The next move is to name the staying option that has been off the menu. The staying is being-with the partner's pain — the heartbreak of witnessing pain you cannot fix. See [[your-job-is-to-be-with-the-pain-or-leave]]. The principle prepares the ground for that staying to become visible as the actual unit of work.

## How the Principle Relates to "Don't Caretake"

The principle is a specific operational form of the broader prescription in [[caring-vs-caretaking]] and [[i-caretake-people]]. The caretaking-vs-care distinction names the difference between two stances. This principle names the *structural reality* that the caretaking option, even when chosen consciously and skillfully, is still a form of leaving. It is not a softer form of presence; it is presence's opposite, with better PR.

This matters because many speakers, once they have learned the caretaking-vs-care distinction, treat the caretaking as the wrong choice and beat themselves up for making it. The beating-themselves-up is the inward-anger version of the same configuration. They have moved from the pleasing option to the anger-at-self option without acquiring the staying option. The principle helps them see that both moves are still in the same trilemma; the staying that would actually be presence is a different move entirely, not visible from inside any of the three.

The principle also helps speakers see why their attempts to "stop caretaking" tend to fail. They are trying to stop the pleasing option without having any other move available. The body cannot stop doing one of the three without having a fourth move to do instead. The work is not to subtract the pleasing; it is to add the staying.

## The Asymmetry That Makes the Angry Options Slightly Better

Joe is careful to acknowledge the asymmetry: "Angry at him, you're a little more there if you're angry at him or yourself, I guess, to some degree, but you're still, you still left." The angry options are degraded forms of presence; the pleasing option has no presence at all.

This asymmetry has a clinical consequence. For a speaker who has been deeply in the pleasing pattern for years, moving to the anger options can be a meaningful first step — not because the anger is the destination, but because the anger restores the felt experience of having a body, having a charge, taking up space. The speaker who has been entirely vacated can sometimes find their way back to themselves through anger before they can find their way to the staying. See [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] for the related mechanism by which moving anger outward releases the inward-anger pattern.

But the anger is not the destination. The destination is the staying. The principle names anger as a slightly-better leaving precisely so the speaker does not mistake the partial recovery of presence (through anger) for the full recovery of presence (through being-with). The full recovery requires the staying that is not any of the three menu options.

## Why This Principle Is Easy to Forget

The principle is counterintuitive in a way that does not stay counterintuitive once it lands. Once you have felt the equivalence — anxiously pleasing and angry are both forms of leaving — the equivalence is obvious. But the body's automatic reach for pleasing is built on a story that does not require conscious belief to operate. The story can be cognitively dissolved many times before the body stops automatically reaching.

This is why the principle is best taught experientially rather than intellectually. Joe's move in the source session is to ask the speaker to *literally feel both options for a minute*: "feel that feeling of, oh my God, is he gonna be upset? What did I do wrong? Fuck you. What did I do wrong? Fuck you. Like what, what makes one more difficult than the other?" The body comparison is what produces the lasting recognition. The conceptual understanding is the post-hoc name for what the body has already noticed.

The principle is also easy to weaponize against oneself if it is held only conceptually. The speaker who has the principle but not the body experience can use it to beat themselves up: "I'm doing the pleasing again, I'm such a bad person." This is the same trilemma operating one layer up. The new pleasing is the conformity to the principle; the failure of conformity becomes the new anger-at-self. The body has not actually moved into the staying.

The corrective is to keep the work in the body. The principle is the orientation. The work is the practice of returning into oneself in the moment when the management was about to start. See [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] and the practices [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] and [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]] for the body-level moves that actually shift the configuration over time.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle mid-session, after the trilemma has been surfaced through the avoided-feeling question. The delivery is matter-of-fact, almost diagrammatic. He is not making an argument; he is naming a structural reality the speaker can verify by checking their own body.

The "I guess, to some degree" qualifier in the source delivery matters. Joe is acknowledging the asymmetry between the angry options and the pleasing option rather than papering over it. The honesty about the partial difference is what makes the equivalence land. If Joe collapsed all three options as identical, the speaker would resist the framing because the asymmetry is real to their body. The acknowledgment of the partial difference makes the larger equivalence credible.

The principle is best delivered after the body has already done some of the comparison work. Asked cold at the start of a session, it lands as an abstract claim. Asked after the speaker has felt the three options in their body, it lands as the name for what they have just noticed.
