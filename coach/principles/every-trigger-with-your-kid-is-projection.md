# Every Trigger With Your Kid Is a Projection (And You're Making the Child Into It)

## The Principle

When you are triggered by your child, you are projecting. The thing you want to call them is the thing you are. The instruction you want to give them is the instruction you have not taken yourself. The label is a mirror.

Worse than that — and this is the part most parents do not see — by labeling them with it, you are installing it. You are not just projecting onto the child. You are using your label as the mechanism to make the child into what you projected.

"Every time I was triggered, I was projecting onto my kid. If I wanted to go, 'You're selfish,' it meant I was being selfish. If I wanted to go, 'Why don't you just listen?' it meant I wasn't listening. If I was saying, 'You need to calm down,' it meant I needed to calm down. To see that every single time I was triggered, I was making that child into my trigger. I wasn't just like projecting it onto them, I was making it them." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

## The Two Layers

This is a stronger version of the general principle [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]]. There are two specific layers operating in the parent-child relationship.

**Layer 1: The trigger reveals your own pattern.** Same as in any relationship. The thing that triggers you is the thing you have not yet seen in yourself. The label you want to apply is your own.

**Layer 2: The label installs the trait in the child.** This is what makes parenting different from other relationships. The child is forming. The child does not yet have a stable self-concept. The parent's labels are formative.

"You need to calm down tells a kid that they're not calm. You're naughty tells a kid they're naughty, right? So you're defining them, and, and so you're — it's like literally the mechanism to make them into you. Holy cow, to hand over your patterns." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The parent who labels the child as the trait the parent is triggered by uses the labeling to hand the trait to the child. The child accepts the label (because children believe what their parents say about them) and develops the trait. The pattern transmits.

## The Diagnostic the Trigger Provides

If the principle is true — and Joe is emphatic that it is — then every trigger with your kid is also useful data about you. The trigger names, in the words you want to use, the thing in you that needs attention.

"It's such a cool thing because it's like every time you get triggered, you're like, that's me, that's me, that's me." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The list of common parenting triggers, read as a self-diagnostic:

- "You're being selfish" → you have not been letting yourself want what you want, or you have been suppressing your own needs to the point that the kid's needs feel threatening
- "Why don't you just listen?" → you have not been listening (to yourself, to your partner, to your kid)
- "You need to calm down" → you need to calm down
- "Stop being so dramatic" → you are not letting yourself feel
- "Why are you being so mean?" → you are being mean to yourself or someone else
- "You're being lazy" → you are being lazy somewhere you have not been admitting
- "Why can't you just do what I ask?" → you have a pattern of not doing what you actually want, and the child's refusal triggers your own suppressed refusal

Each is the parent's pattern. The parent who reads the trigger as data rather than as truth about the child has access to a self-diagnostic instrument that runs all day.

## Why This Is "Such a Cool Thing"

Joe calls this "such a cool thing" because the alternative — believing the labels are about the child — produces a parent who is both wrong and harmful. The reframe is gift on two sides. The parent gets self-diagnostic information they would not otherwise have. The child gets to not be defined by the parent's projections.

The mechanism by which the parent stops handing over their patterns is the recognition itself. The parent who sees "that's me" in the moment of the trigger has the option of not labeling the child with it. The trigger is still there. The pattern is still there. But the child does not get installed with it.

This is the practical mechanism through which inner work translates into the next generation. The parent who does the work to see their projections does not pass the patterns forward. The parent who does not do the work passes the patterns forward.

## Why The Mechanism Works Specifically Through Labeling

A child is forming their sense of self. Their primary source of information about who they are is what their parents say and how their parents react. When a parent says "you are X" with conviction and emotional weight, the child takes the data as information about who they are. The label, repeated, becomes the self-concept.

This is also why [[dont-shame-kids-they-self-shame]] is so important. Shame attached to the labels accelerates the installation. The child not only takes on the label but takes on it as a moral defect that needs hiding. The trait becomes secret. The trait persists.

The parent who refrains from labeling — who, in the moment of triggered impulse to say "you're being selfish," instead says "I am noticing my own selfishness right now, I need a minute" — interrupts the transmission. The trait does not get installed because the trait does not get named onto the child.

## What This Does Not Mean

The principle does not mean parents should never give children feedback about their behavior. It means that feedback delivered from a triggered state, in the form of a label about who the child is, is mostly a projection. There is a difference between "you are being selfish" (a label about the child's character) and "I need you to share with your sister right now" (a request about the behavior). The first is the projection. The second is the request.

The principle also does not mean every parent observation about their kid is a projection. A parent can correctly notice that the child is tired, hungry, scared, or angry without it being a projection. The diagnostic is whether you are triggered. If you are triggered, you are projecting. If you are calm and observing, you are usually seeing what is there.

## Joe's Self-Application

Joe identifies this as one of the coolest things parenting taught him. The way he reports it is in the present tense — this is still useful information to him. The pattern of using the trigger as data did not turn off after the kids were small.

"To notice that every time I was triggered, I was projecting onto my kid." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This connects to [[joe-apologizes-to-esme-for-the-3-year-old-imprint]] in a particular way. The 3-year-old moment Joe apologized for was the kind of moment this principle describes. He was triggered then. He labeled or reacted in some way. The pattern transmitted. Years later, he saw the pattern running in her. The apology was the recognition working in reverse: he was now able to see what he had projected at the time and to take it back.

## When To Use This Principle

- A parent reporting that their child has a pattern that frustrates them
- A parent who has been labeling their child in a particular way
- A parent who is triggered repeatedly by a specific behavior
- A parent asking how their own patterns are showing up in their kids
- A coachee who is the kid in the story — who absorbed a label from their parent and is now trying to release it
- A discussion of [[trauma-mapping]] specifically in parent-child transmission
- A parent who is doing the work and wants the most direct application of self-awareness to parenting

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent in the middle of yelling at their kid about something they themselves do

- A coachee unpacking what their parent kept calling them as a child and realizing it was the parent's pattern, not theirs
- A parent doing emotional work and starting to see how often their child shows them their own un-met material
