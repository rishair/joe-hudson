# You'll Never Get the Confirmation From Others That You're Seeking (Nobody Ever Has)

## The Principle

The confirmation a person is seeking from others — the steady, unanimous, settling verdict that they are okay, that they are valuable, that they are doing it right — is not available. No one has ever gotten it. The configuration of reality that would deliver it does not exist.

For every person who confirms, there is a person who does not. For every person who thinks you are amazing, there is a person who thinks you are an asshole. The confirmation is unanimous only in fantasy. In any actual audience, the verdict splits — and the part of the verdict the seeker wants (the unanimous yes) is never the verdict that arrives.

"You're never going to get the confirmation from others that you're seeking. No, nobody ever has, right?... I'm on a call with whatever it was, 500 and something people. I could consider this confirmation. There's another 500 who think that I'm an asshole or think that I'm inappropriate or think that I swear too much or I'm never going to get the confirmation of others. It never comes. It's not how humans work." -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

## Why It Is Structural, Not Personal

The unavailability is not because the seeker is unlovable, unimpressive, or doing the wrong thing. It is because of how humans work. Reception splits across any audience. The signal one person finds compelling, another finds repellent. The same words land as profound for some and as obnoxious for others. There is no version of the speaker that gets unanimous reception, because the reception is produced by the receivers, and the receivers are not aligned.

This connects to [[simultaneous-reception-is-uncontrollable]]: every spoken moment lands as epiphany for some and as bored-irritation for others, simultaneously. The wish to be only-confirmed is the wish for an unavailable configuration of reality.

It also connects to [[you-cannot-make-people-happy]]: the seeker's strategy of being-good-enough-to-be-confirmed cannot succeed because the target is moving (the same action is approved one day and rejected the next) and depends on inputs they do not control (the receiver's state, history, current mood).

The structural unavailability is what makes the seeking unbounded. The seeker keeps seeking because each piece of confirmation that arrives can be revoked, and because every piece of confirmation that arrives is matched by a piece of disconfirmation somewhere in the audience. The seeking has no completion criterion. The pursuit is set up to run forever.

## Why the Principle Lands as Relief

When the seeker realizes that no one has ever gotten the thing they have been seeking, the position they have been holding becomes audibly absurd. They have been waiting for a delivery that does not exist. They have organized their life around earning a verdict that no human has ever earned. The futility of the strategy becomes visible.

The relief is not in being told they cannot have what they want. The relief is in being told that what they have been wanting is not on the menu — and that they can stop trying to be good enough to earn it, because no one is good enough to earn it, because it does not exist.

The client in the coaching session received this and said "wow" and "no way" in the same breath. The recognition was simultaneously a loss (the thing he had been pursuing was not a real thing) and a release (he could stop pursuing).

## What This Reveals About the Seeking

If the confirmation cannot be delivered, the question becomes: what is the seeking actually doing? It is not doing what it claims to do (securing the confirmation). It is doing something else.

The diagnostic question Joe asks next:

"What makes you look for confirmation from others when you can never get it?" -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The answer almost always surfaces the actual mechanism. The client in the canonical session: "Because I'm not good enough to do it, anything, myself. Because I can't confirm myself."

The seeking-from-others is the alternative strategy for a person who cannot confirm themselves. The outsourcing happens because the internal channel is broken or has never been opened. The fix is not to keep seeking from outside (which structurally cannot deliver) but to repair the internal channel.

This routes the work toward [[chase-your-own-approval]] — the practice of asking "do I approve?" as the central question rather than "do they approve?" The internal source can deliver. The external source cannot.

## Why Confirmation Cannot Cure What It's Being Asked to Cure

Even when individual pieces of confirmation arrive, they do not stop the seeking. The seeker receives the compliment, the recognition, the contract, the praise — and within hours or days the hunger is back. The piece of confirmation did not satisfy. It was eaten and the hunger returned.

This is because the confirmation is being asked to do what only the internal verdict can do. The structure is identical to [[money-as-surrogate]]: a thing is being recruited to deliver what that thing cannot deliver, and the more of the thing arrives, the more visible it becomes that the thing was never the actual want.

The actual want underneath external confirmation is the felt sense of being okay as you are. Other people cannot deliver that. Only you can. The confirmation strategy is the wrong strategy for the want. No amount of correct execution of the wrong strategy produces the wanted outcome.

## The Two Inversions

The principle produces two inversions that are the operational form of the work.

**First inversion: from "do they approve?" to "do I approve?"** See [[chase-your-own-approval]]. The verdict that can actually be delivered is the internal one. Relocating the central question to inside is the practice that makes the principle livable.

**Second inversion: from "I will be myself when I am confirmed" to "I am being myself, and the audience splits as it splits."** The confirmation cannot be the prerequisite for being yourself, because the confirmation will never arrive. The being-yourself has to happen now, under conditions of split reception, because there are no other conditions ever available.

See [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] for the structural mechanism: speaking your truth out loud is the act that approves of it, because voicing the perception is treating it as valuable. The speaking is the self-confirmation made operational. The split audience that hears it cannot remove that.

## What This Does Not Mean

The principle does not mean that others' input is worthless. It means that others' input is information, not verdict. Feedback can be useful. Critique can be useful. Recognition can be enjoyed. None of these is the same as the confirmation-from-others that the seeker is chasing. The seeker is chasing a unanimous, settling, never-revocable verdict. That is what does not exist. Information from others continues to be available and useful.

The principle also does not mean isolation. The person who stops chasing confirmation does not stop relating. They relate more, because they are no longer extracting from each interaction. See [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]] — the chasing was itself the thing preventing the contact. Stopping the chase opens the contact.

## How Joe Delivers This in Session

The principle is delivered as direct teaching, with the speaker's own life as the demonstration. Joe uses the call he is on as the live data: "I'm on a call with 500 people. There's another 500 who think I'm an asshole." The example is not abstract. It is happening right now, in the room where the teaching is being delivered. The seeker can see that even the person teaching does not have what the seeker is trying to get.

The use of self as the live demonstration is structurally important. Telling the seeker "no one gets this" while implying that the speaker is the exception would leave the seeker still hoping. Naming "I don't get it either, here, in this moment, on this call" closes that escape route. If the person teaching the principle does not have the thing being sought, no one has it.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly amused. The "no way" energy from the seeker is met with a calm "right" from Joe. The bluntness is required — softening it would let the seeker continue believing the confirmation is theoretically achievable if they just got better. The bluntness closes that door.

The amusement is in the absurdity of the configuration. Joe lets the seeker hear that the structure they have been operating inside is funny once seen. The 500-against-500 split is delivered with the energy of "look at this, it's hilarious that anyone ever expected unanimous reception."

After the principle lands, the tone moves to warmer, more practical. The work shifts to "what makes you look for confirmation from others when you can never get it?" — and the answer that surfaces is met with the gentleness of someone who knows the answer is going to be a tender thing.
