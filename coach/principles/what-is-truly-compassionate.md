# What Is Truly Compassionate for You Is Truly Compassionate for Them

## The Principle

The deepest version of self-care is also the deepest version of care for the people in your life. The apparent conflict between caring for yourself and caring for others is a function of staying at shallow levels of both. Go deep enough and they converge.

"What I see is that the thing that is true in us, that's the compassionate treatment of ourselves, is also the compassionate treatment of the person across from us." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

This is the principle that resolves the caretaker's central dilemma. The caretaker believes they have to choose between honoring themselves and honoring the other. Joe's frame: the choice is false. Anything actually compassionate for one is compassionate for both.

## The Masochist God Argument

Joe arrives at the principle through a friend's question: "I have a hard time knowing when I am supposed to take care of myself and when I am supposed to take care of others."

Joe's response: "Wow, so your God's a masochist."

The reasoning: if the world is structured so that you must choose between self-love and the love of another, the designer of the world has set up a torture. A coherent worldview cannot include a true conflict between the deepest goods.

"If your God has set up a world where it's binary, where it's either self-love or the love of another, then something is the design that this God has created is one that's masochistic. It's one that is to torture." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The alternative: the apparent binary is the result of staying at the level of surface wants and surface care. At depth, the binary dissolves.

## Why the Apparent Conflict Arises

The caretaker's experienced conflict is real at one level. The wife wants peace; the husband wants to keep yelling. The mother wants rest; the child wants attention. The friend wants to leave the party; the host wants them to stay.

But the conflict appears at the level of immediate preference, not at the level of deep want.

"Everyone says, 'Well, what's best for me is, I just wanna go out and drink, but I gotta take care of my kid.' Like, is you going out and drinking or partying, is that really what's best for you?" -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

What is best for the parent at the level of impulse (drink, escape) is different from what is best at the level of deep want (be present in a meaningful life). Once the question is asked at the deep level, the answer often converges with what is best for the child.

The inverse holds:

"Somebody says, 'Well, what's really best for them is if I'm just really nice and I take care of them.' Is that really best for them? Or is what's really best for them, for them to learn how to be independent, for them to be told the truth?" -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

What looks best for the other at surface level (be nice, take care of them) is different from what is best at the deep level (let them grow, tell them the truth). At depth, the deep want and the deep good for the other converge.

## The Depth Question

The operational form of the principle: when the apparent conflict between self and other arises, ask the question at the deeper level. Not "what do I want right now?" but "what do I actually want here, all the way down?" Not "what is nice for them?" but "what is actually best for them?"

The conflict typically dissolves at the third or fourth iteration. The deep want, fully felt, turns out to be the deep good for the other.

"How deep are you gonna go down that want hole? How deep are you gonna go down that compassion hole?" -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

This connects to [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]]. The first want is rarely the actual want. The actual want is several layers down. Once located, it has a different character: it is not in opposition to the wellbeing of the people around you. It is congruent with it.

## The Limit Case

Joe states the principle in its hardest form: even when the deep want requires sacrificing the relationship, it can be congruent with love for the other.

"If you go down to the deepest level of compassion, which is, I am willing to sacrifice our relationship for my love for you. I'm willing to sacrifice what you think of me. I am willing to sacrifice this person, you, who I love, because I know it's what's best for you. Then the law works. Then God isn't masochistic." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The caretaker who finally tells the addict brother they will no longer pay for his housing may be doing the most loving act available, even though it looks like withdrawal of love at the surface. The deep love for the brother includes letting him face the bottom that his recovery requires.

This is the same logic as [[love-vs-nice]]. Loving the other is sometimes saying the thing that risks the relationship. The willingness to risk is the depth of the love.

## The Question Joe Asks

When a person finally breaks through into "this is what I want," Joe almost always asks the follow-up:

"And so what about that is not the most compassionate thing for the person who you've been caretaking?" -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The question forces the person to test the apparent conflict. They typically find that the want they have just located is, in fact, the most compassionate thing for the other person. The caretaking was preventing both wellbeings simultaneously.

This is the move that closes the loop. The person started believing they had to choose between themselves and the other. They end seeing that the choice was the problem. The deep version of self-care is the deep version of other-care.

## When the Convergence Does Not Hold

There are cases where the principle does not appear to hold. A person enacts what they call their deep want and the other person genuinely suffers. The principle suggests checking whether the want was actually deep.

The shallow want, dressed in deep-want language, often produces this outcome. "I'm following my truth" used as cover for impulse, escape, or harm to others is not the principle in action. It is the principle being weaponized. See [[spiritual-narcissism]].

The check: a truly deep want carries a felt sense of rightness about everyone in the situation, including the people who will be hurt by the action. The hurt is acknowledged, mourned, and taken responsibility for. The action is taken anyway because the alternative would be a deeper harm to multiple people. This is different from "I want this and I do not care about the impact."

If the action feels good to the actor and harmful to everyone else, the want was probably shallow. Go deeper.

## Connection to Selfishness as Development

This principle is closely related to [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]]. Both make the same structural claim: deep selfishness is not in opposition to care for others; it is the engine of it. The deep selfish desire of a developed person is congruent with the wellbeing of everyone around them. The shallow selfish desire of an undeveloped person is in apparent conflict with everyone.

The work is to develop the selfishness, not to suppress it.

## When Someone Experiences This

When someone is caught between "what I want" and "what they need."

When someone is caretaking and feels guilty about the resentment that has built up.

When someone has just broken through into a clear want and is afraid of what it will do to the people around them.

When someone is using "I have to take care of them" as justification for not honoring their own truth.

The principle delivered cleanly often produces immediate relief. The person has been operating as if the choice were real. The reframe lifts the impossible burden.
