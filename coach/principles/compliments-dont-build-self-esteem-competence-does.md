# Compliments Don't Build Self-Esteem (Seeing Your Own Competence Does)

## The Principle

Telling someone "good job, I really see that" does not reliably increase their self-esteem. The structural reason: the self-esteem deficit is an internal verdict, and external statements are filtered through that verdict. The person who believes they are not enough hears the compliment, runs it through the not-enough filter, and either deflects it or accepts it without letting it land (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]).

What does reliably increase self-esteem is the person's own experience of their competence. A demonstration to the self by the self. The doing produces the seeing, and the seeing produces the esteem. The substrate has been changed by direct evidence the person generated themselves rather than evidence delivered from outside.

"It's why giving people compliments often doesn't actually increase their self-esteem. Them doing things that allow them to see their own competence often increases self-esteem, is the most likely thing to increase self-esteem, not somebody else telling them, 'good job, I really see that.'" -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Most Want

The principle has the form of a corrective: the standard intuition about how to help someone with low self-esteem (give them more compliments, tell them they are doing well) is structurally backwards. The thing that works is the opposite move — set up the conditions where they will see their own competence operating, and let them register the seeing for themselves.

## Why This Is True

The mechanism is the same one named in [[chase-your-own-approval]] and [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]]. The verdict about self that determines whether self-esteem is high or low is an internal verdict. External evidence cannot directly change an internal verdict — it has to pass through the verdict to be received, and the verdict can refuse to update. The deflection is structurally automatic for the person whose verdict is "I am not enough."

Competence-seen-by-self bypasses the verdict because it is not external evidence. It is the person's own direct perception of their own functioning. The verdict cannot deflect what the body is directly registering as a fact about itself. The "I am not enough" verdict gets contradicted by the felt sense of "I just did that thing well." The contradiction lands because the source of the evidence is the same self whose verdict is in question.

The compliment, by contrast, is data from outside the self. The verdict-holder can dismiss the data as ignorant, manipulative, or polite (see the three deflection forms in [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]]). The dismissal preserves the verdict. The competence-seen-by-self cannot be dismissed because the dismisser would be calling themselves a liar about what their own body just did.

## The Receiving Caveat

The principle is not an argument against compliments. It is an argument against compliments as the primary mechanism for building self-esteem in someone whose receiving channel is closed. For the person whose receiving channel is open — see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] — compliments do contribute, because the verdict has been loosened enough that contrary evidence from outside can land.

The integrated form: open the receiving channel first (or in parallel), so external compliments can begin to land. Set up the conditions for competence to be demonstrated and seen. Both routes feed into the same updated verdict. The internal demonstration is doing the heavier lift; the received compliments are reinforcement that can land once the channel is open.

The corollary: the person who has already received the compliment fully has often already integrated some piece of the corresponding competence. The receiving and the seeing are coupled — a compliment that lands is often landing because the body has already glimpsed the competence the compliment is naming. The compliment is the external articulation of what the inside has been beginning to see.

## What This Means for Parenting and Leadership

The principle has direct implications anywhere one person is trying to help another build self-esteem.

**For parenting:** the standard "good job, you're so smart" approach has a known failure mode (the kids told they are smart try less hard at the impossible test, because failing would prove they are not smart — see [[identity]]). The principle suggests the correction: instead of telling the kid they are smart, set up situations where the kid will see their own competence operating. Let them struggle through something and succeed. Let them notice they did it. The kid's own seeing of the doing is what builds the durable self-esteem. The "good job" comment is not the engine.

**For leadership:** the manager who is trying to fix an under-confident report by praising them more is using the wrong tool. The praise gets deflected (see [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]] for the structural reason). The intervention that works is structurally different: set the report up to do things that will produce a competence-recognition in them. Let them complete the project. Let them solve the problem. Let them notice they handled the meeting. The praise can be present as ratification, but the engine is the experience itself.

**For coaching:** the coach trying to help a client build self-trust by complimenting their progress is leaning on the weaker tool. The stronger move is to construct experiences inside or outside the session where the client will encounter their own capacity directly. Compliments from the coach can ratify the recognition but cannot substitute for it.

## The Compliment's Other Use (Identity-Dissolution, Not Esteem-Building)

A clarifying distinction: the let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel work with compliments is doing different work from self-esteem-building. The compliment-receiving practice dissolves the identity that says "I am not enough" by letting contrary evidence move through the body. The dissolution is the work, not the addition of positive evidence to an identity that stays in place.

"What it's actually doing is taking the part of you that feels limited and it's challenging it. It's making the part of you that says, 'No, I'm not enough.' And it's like, wow, there's evidence to the contrary. And you have to deal with that... It really shows you who you are instead of what you think you are." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval (cited in [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]])

So compliments do work — but through the dissolution route, not the addition route. The compliment dissolves the deflective identity. The dissolved identity is what reveals the actual person, who turns out to have more capacity than the identity allowed for. The "self-esteem" that arrives after is less of an addition and more of a recognition of what was always there under the deflection.

This is why the principle is not "compliments are useless" but "compliments don't build self-esteem in the additive way the standard model assumes." They work in a different direction. The standard model is wrong about the mechanism, which is why the standard model's interventions often fail to produce the predicted effect.

## When Joe Uses This

- When a client is asking how to build self-esteem in themselves or in someone they love
- When a parent is asking how to praise their kid effectively
- When a leader is asking how to build confidence in an under-confident report
- When the standard "give more compliments" prescription has been tried and is not working
- When a client is hoping that enough external validation will eventually fix the internal deficit (see [[chase-your-own-approval]])
- When the conversation about [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] needs the structural reason that external compliments alone do not reliably do the job

## Tone

Joe states this as a structural observation, not as a critique of compliments. The tone is matter-of-fact. The implication carries the corrective weight without the delivery having to emphasize it.

The principle is delivered in a teaching-monologue context, which gives it room to be stated as a principle rather than as a coaching move. When it shows up in coaching, it usually shows up as a redirect — "before we go to who can tell you you're doing well, let's look at what you can see about your own functioning."
