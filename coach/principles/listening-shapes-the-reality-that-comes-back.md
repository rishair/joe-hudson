# Listening Shapes the Reality That Comes Back to You

## The Principle

The quality of your listening is one of the most direct levers you have on the reality that arrives at you. People do not give the same speech to a listener-of-this-quality and a listener-of-that-quality. The information you receive — from a partner, a child, an employee, a customer, your own body — is shaped by how you are receiving. Change the listening; the data changes.

This is not metaphor. It is structural. The speaker is adjusting in real time to the receiver. The receiver who is open gets one set of disclosures. The receiver who is evaluating gets a different set. The receiver who is preparing to respond gets a third. The same speaker, given a different listener, becomes a different speaker.

"The quality of the listener does directly back-channel and impact the way that we share." -- Brett Kistler, The Opposite of Trying

## Why It Is True

People feel the listener. The body of the speaker registers the body of the listener. When the listener is open, the speaker's nervous system relaxes and more is available to be said. When the listener is closed, evaluating, or preparing a response, the speaker's nervous system contracts and the speech contracts with it.

This happens whether the participants are conscious of it or not. A child running to show a mud pie can read the mother's stance instantly; the mud pie deflates before any word is spoken. Adults do the same thing more subtly. The conscious mind narrates "I'm just having a normal conversation"; the body knows whether to open or close.

The implication for leverage: trying to "get more honesty" from someone by asking better questions, while listening the same way, is the small lever. Changing how you listen, while leaving the questions the same, is the larger lever. Doing both is bigger still.

## The Two-Day Experiment

The clearest demonstration is the two-day listening experiment. See [[listen-as-the-wisest-person]].

Day one: spend the entire day listening to everyone with skepticism. Day two: spend the entire day listening to everyone as if they are the wisest sage you have ever encountered. Notice what gets shared, the texture of the conversations, the world that arrives back.

"Spend one day going around listening to everybody with skepticism and then take the next day and listen to everybody like they are the most wise sage speaking to you and see how your world changes in a day." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The world changes in a day. Not the world out there. The world that is offered to you. People volunteer different things; conversations have different content; relationships look different through different listening.

## Specific Domains Where This Shows Up

**Business.** The quality of your listening affects how good your product is, how happy your customers are, how heard your employees feel, how many of your best people stay, what intelligence your team is able to surface. As a venture capitalist Joe noticed that the CEOs who got picked were the ones who asked questions and listened. The ones who answered questions usually did not get the job. The same pattern repeats with customers, investors, partners.

"Listening basically is one of the most profound ways to make your business accelerate quicker than coming up with an idea." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

**Teams.** Google's Project Aristotle found that the variable that distinguished functional teams was that everyone felt free to express themselves and everyone actually did. That only happens when the listening is in place. See [[aristotle-project]].

**Family.** The people in your life who listen to you well are almost always the people you love most. There is no version of "I love that person but they're a terrible listener of me." Become that listener and the people around you reciprocate. They also unfold faster.

**Self.** When you listen to yourself well, you evolve faster. What needs to come to the surface comes to the surface. The system unfolds the way a deeply-attuned child develops — holistically, quicker, better. This is what meditation is at its best: a listening to yourself, not a management of yourself. See [[listening]].

## What This Replaces

The default theory of communication leverage is that the way to get more from people is to ask better questions, deploy better techniques, find better words. That is a real lever, but it is downstream of the listening lever. A great question asked by a poor listener lands as a survey. The same question asked by a deep listener becomes an invitation. The technique amplifies what the listening is already doing. It cannot substitute for it.

The default theory of leadership influence is that you influence by speaking. Joe inverts this:

"Listening is one of the deepest ways of affecting your reality." -- Joe Hudson, Listening

The reach for influence-by-speaking comes from a model where the speaker is the one who shapes the encounter. The reality is more bidirectional. The listener shapes the encounter at least as much, often more. The leader who has learned to listen has more influence than the leader who has learned to speak well, because the listening sets the conditions under which the speaking lands.

## The Two-Way Cost

The principle cuts both ways. If your listening is shaped by management, by preparing responses, by skepticism, by performing care — that is the world you live in. People give you guarded versions of themselves. Your information is filtered through their guard. Your business runs on filtered data. Your relationships run on filtered intimacy. The cost is the diminished life that arrives.

The cost compounds. Over months and years of skeptical or distracted listening, the people around you stop offering certain things. They learn what is welcomed and what is not. The reality contracts to fit the listening. The listener does not know what has been withheld because the withholding has never been visible.

## What the Coach Does With This

When someone is in a hard situation with a partner, employee, child, or team, and the strategy has been to find better words, a useful question is: how have you been listening? Often the better-words intervention has been masking the listening problem and producing only marginal change. Shifting the listening tends to open up things that no amount of language could have opened.

When someone says "they never tell me anything," "they shut down on me," "they don't trust me," "I can't seem to reach them," the first lens to apply is the listening lens. Not "what should I say to them?" but "what is my listening doing to what they are willing to bring?"

## What This Is Not

This is not the claim that you are responsible for what other people say. People still have agency. They still have their own histories, fears, and patterns. A great listener does not make a closed person open. The principle is that the listening is the lever the listener has. It is the half of the conversation the listener can change. It is, in fact, the half of the conversation the listener can change *most*.

This is also not a trick. Performing the receptive stance while internally still skeptical or managing or evaluating produces only a slightly worse version of skeptical listening. The body of the speaker reads the body of the listener, not the performance. See [[listen-as-the-wisest-person]] for the warning against treating this as manipulation.
