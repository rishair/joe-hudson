# Go to What You Know Least About, Do the Simplest Experiment to Learn the Most

## The Principle

When you are building something you do not know how to build, the highest-leverage move is to identify the part you know least about and design the simplest possible experiment to learn the most about that part. Do that experiment first. Then build the rest around what you learned.

The principle inverts what most people do, which is to build the parts they already know how to build first, leaving the unknown parts for last. The problem with that approach is that the unknown parts, once finally addressed, often force a redesign of everything that was already built. The known parts have to be torn down and redone because they were sized to the wrong assumptions.

"In MIT Media Lab, as I understand it, they have this principle: if you're going to build something that you don't know how to build, go to the thing you know the least amount about, do the simplest experiment to learn the most about it." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## Why It Works

The principle solves a structural problem in iteration: each iteration produces learning, but the learning is most valuable when it forces the least amount of rework. Learning early about the unknown part means everything you build afterward is sized correctly. Learning late about the unknown part means everything you built before has to be redone.

"If I say I was going to build a car, I've never done it before. I'm going to build everything that I know, and then I'm going to build the thing that I don't know, let's say the chassis. And then once I've built the chassis, I've learned some stuff that will make it so I have to rebuild the whole car to fit to the chassis." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

The chassis is the unknown. The chassis sized to a wrong assumption is the cost of learning about the chassis last. The principle says: build the chassis first, learn what it requires, then build the rest of the car around what you now know.

## The Simplest Experiment Part

The instruction is not "build the unknown thing first." It is "design the simplest experiment that produces the most learning about the unknown thing." The two are different.

The simplest experiment is small enough to be cheap. It is not the finished product; it is a test that produces information. A cardboard chassis. A paper prototype. A two-week pilot. A single conversation. The point is to extract the learning at the lowest cost.

This is the structural form of the iterative mindset applied to a specific decision: where to spend the next iteration. The answer is: where the learning per unit of effort is highest. That is almost always the unknown part.

## What This Replaces

The default approach is the opposite of this principle. People build what they know first because building what they know is comfortable, fast, and looks like progress. The unknown gets deferred because the unknown is uncomfortable and slow.

"And people do the things that they know. That experimentation process teaches you a lot about the thing you don't know, which makes it the quickest way to build the car or build the self-awareness or build the self-discovery or build the life that you want." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

The deferral feels efficient (we are getting stuff done!) and is structurally inefficient (we are getting the wrong stuff done because the unknown will force the rework). The MIT principle re-routes the effort toward the place where each iteration produces the most learning.

## The Application to Self-Awareness

Joe extends the principle from engineering to personal development. The same logic operates. The person who already understands their relationship to their work and does not understand their relationship to their body will benefit more from experiments on their relationship to their body than from more experiments on their relationship to their work. The unknown is where the learning is.

The application: look at your life. What is the part you know the least about? What is the place where you have not yet experimented? That is where the next experiment goes. The areas you already understand can wait. The areas you do not understand will, if left alone, eventually force a reorganization of everything else.

## The Application to High-Stakes Decisions

When a decision is high-stakes and there is genuine fear of getting it wrong (a country in a war, a company facing a market threat), the principle says: do not run the experiment on the high-stakes part. Do what you know works on the high-stakes part. Run the experiments on the lower-stakes parts that you know least about, and let the learning from those experiments inform the high-stakes part.

"If I was running the US government and I'm dealing with, say, like a war in Ukraine, I would want to run a series of lesser experiments to see what I can find out without, you know, committing $10 billion to this weapon or whatever it is. So I would look for, just like we talked about with the MIT Media Lab, I would look for the thing I know the least about and what's the simplest experiment where I can learn the most." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

The principle does not say "experiment everywhere." It says experiment at the highest-information-per-cost location, which is rarely the highest-stakes location. The high-stakes location is where you deploy what you have already learned. The low-stakes location near the unknown is where you run the experiment.

See [[experiments-must-stay-in-window-of-tolerance]] for the related principle: experiments designed to stay inside the window of tolerance produce learning; experiments outside the window collapse into fear and the experimental mindset is lost.

## Reasoning Joe Gives

The deeper reason: most learning is in the nuance. The unknowns hold the nuance that the knowns do not. By going to what is most unknown, the experimenter exposes themselves to the highest density of nuance. By going to what is most known, the experimenter exposes themselves only to confirmation of what they already had.

This is also why iteration works structurally. Iteration is the conversion of unknowns into knowns. The fastest way to perform that conversion is to go where the unknowns are densest. The MIT principle is the operational form of that observation.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the energy of someone who has watched it work in his own business. He cites the MIT Media Lab not as authority but as a name for an obvious-once-stated structural principle. The delivery is matter-of-fact: this is how engineers learn to build things efficiently, and the same logic operates on lives and businesses.
