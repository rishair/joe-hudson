# Capitalism Can Be a Force for Human Healing

## The Principle

The widespread assumption that consciousness work or healing work and the structures of business are incompatible is a structural mistake. The same mistake was widely held about environmental work and business in the 1970s — until enough people demonstrated through their lives that you could be both an environmentalist and a businessman, that the same person could pursue both, and that the integration was sometimes more powerful than either pursuit alone. The same collapse is available with consciousness work. You can evolve consciousness and make money. Capitalism, properly held, can be a force for helping humanity heal, understand itself, become itself.

This principle does not say that capitalism in any form is healing. It says that the structural separation between capitalism and consciousness work is artificial, and that practitioners who work inside both can produce something that practitioners working only inside one cannot.

## Joe's Naming

Joe describes this principle as the conclusion of the 12 years he spent running consciousness nonprofits and environmental venture capital in parallel (see [[joes-transition-from-art-to-venture-capital]]).

"I think there was something that, you know, what I saw in the environmental world was that people used to think in the '70s you could be an environmentalist or you could be a businessman and you couldn't be both. But somewhere in the '90s, people were like, oh yeah, you can be both. And still to this day, you can be both. And I was like, that same thing is gonna happen with consciousness, that you can become a conscious, like you can evolve consciousness and make money at the same time, or capitalism can be a force for helping humanity heal, become themselves, understand themselves." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The structural claim has two parts. First: the dichotomy was real-feeling but never structurally necessary. In the 1970s, environmentalists treated business as the enemy and businesspeople treated environmentalism as a fantasy. By the 1990s, neither group held the dichotomy as tightly. The change was not a softening of either side; it was the recognition that the two could be done together. Second: the same recognition is currently available with consciousness work, and Joe's life is one of the demonstrations of the integration.

## The Environmental Precedent in Detail

The environmental story carries the structural lesson. In the 1970s, environmental work was largely opposed to business. Activists were anti-corporate. Corporations were largely indifferent or hostile to environmental considerations. The space of action was small, and the assumed roles were antagonistic.

By the 1990s, this had begun to shift. Some businesspeople started taking environmental considerations seriously not despite their business orientation but because of it — recognizing efficiencies, regulatory tailwinds, brand value, supply-chain stability. Some environmentalists started taking business seriously not despite their environmental orientation but because of it — recognizing that environmental work without economic engines behind it would never reach the scale required for the problems.

The integration produced new forms of work that neither original group could have produced alone: sustainable supply chains, energy-efficient retail (see [[walmart-environmental-profitability]] for the canonical example Joe uses), regenerative agriculture, environmental venture investing. The new work is now more economically significant than either the 1970s environmental movement or 1970s business-as-usual could have anticipated.

The integration did not require either side to abandon its core orientation. It required both sides to drop the assumption that they were opposites. Once the assumption was dropped, the structures emerged.

## The Same Structure for Consciousness Work

The consciousness-work-versus-business assumption follows the same shape. Many people doing consciousness work assume business is the obstacle, a contamination, a domain incompatible with the depth of the work. Many people doing business assume consciousness work is irrelevant, soft, possibly useful as personnel development but not load-bearing for the actual operations.

Both assumptions are wrong in the same way the 1970s environmental-business assumptions were wrong. They feel real because the cultures of the two domains are different. They are not structurally necessary.

The integration is already starting. Coaching has become a significant business in part because business leaders have noticed that the inner work of the leader is load-bearing for the company. Consciousness practices — meditation, somatic work, emotional regulation — have moved out of the spiritual subculture into mainstream corporate training. Investment frameworks have begun to include human-development metrics. The integration is in its early phase, in roughly the position environmental-business integration was in the early 1990s.

Joe's bet, by living both threads for 12 years and then becoming a coach to leaders, is that the integration will continue and deepen the same way environmental-business integration did. The work that exists at the seam of business and consciousness will become more, not less, central.

## Why The Integration Produces Different Work

The work that comes from inside the integration has a different quality than work from either side alone.

Consciousness work without business pressure can become beautiful, slow, and self-indulgent. The practitioner has all the time in the world to refine a single insight. The work can drift toward [[dont-make-self-discovery-entertainment]]. The lack of pressure does not always produce depth; sometimes it produces a kind of permanent meditation-retreat orientation that never quite engages the world.

Business without consciousness work can become mechanical, instrumental, and corrosive. The work is responsive to short-term metrics and not responsive to the longer-arc questions of meaning, contribution, ethics, and human flourishing. The practitioner becomes more efficient at producing things that may not be worth producing.

Business-with-consciousness work produces practitioners who are forced to make their inner work load-bearing for actual outcomes (so they cannot fool themselves about whether the inner work is real) AND who are oriented by considerations larger than next-quarter metrics (so they do not become instrumentalized). The integration disciplines both sides.

This is the same structure as [[business-as-spiritual-practice]]: the practice happens in the friction with the actual conditions of doing business, and the business benefits from the depth that consciousness work brings. The two reinforce each other rather than competing for the practitioner's attention.

## The Domain Where The Integration Is Particularly Important

Joe is specific about one domain where the integration is especially important: environmental work itself. He spent 12 years in environmental venture and observed that the bottleneck in environmental progress was not technological. The technology already existed to solve most environmental problems. The bottleneck was human consciousness — specifically, human ego.

"What I noticed is in the environmental space that the number one problem was human ego. It wasn't, there was plenty of technology. Technology that could — whatever environmental problems we have, we have the technology to solve them by far, easily. All we need is humanity to be human. Like, all we need is, like, egos to, to, to stop doing what egos do." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

This observation collapsed the apparent separation between Joe's two threads. The environmental work could not advance further without the consciousness work, because the egos blocking environmental progress were the same egos consciousness work addresses. The integration was structurally inevitable, not optional.

"So I realized that the root of the problem that I wanted to work on was, was helping people understand themselves, to love themselves, because that was what's probably going to solve all the rest of the problems. That's, that's the core. All these problems came out of human consciousness. So human consciousness shifts." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The implication is general. Wherever the bottleneck of a domain is human consciousness, business-with-consciousness work is the only viable path through. And the bottleneck is human consciousness in more domains than the conventional separation between business and consciousness work would suggest.

## What This Principle Asks Of A Practitioner

For a consciousness-work practitioner: stop assuming business is contamination. The friction with actual conditions disciplines the work in ways that pure-practice cannot. The pressure of having to produce results reveals what is real in the practice and what is performance.

For a business practitioner: stop assuming consciousness work is soft. The inner work of the leader is load-bearing for the company in measurable ways. The practitioner who has done the consciousness work makes different decisions, attracts different collaborators, sustains different cultures, and is more resilient to the failure modes that destroy other leaders.

For someone considering which of the two threads to follow: consider running both. Joe's 12 years of running both in parallel was not a detour from his eventual work; it was the preparation for it. The substrate he coaches from is built out of having been inside both worlds simultaneously.
