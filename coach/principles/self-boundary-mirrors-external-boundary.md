# The Only Times You Can't Draw a Boundary With Others Is When You Can't Draw the Boundary With Yourself

## The Principle

The capacity to draw a boundary with another person is downstream of the capacity to draw the same boundary with yourself. Whatever behavior you tolerate from your own internal voice you will tolerate from others, because the standard you have set inside is the standard you can enforce outside.

"If we yell at ourselves, it's hard for us to see that it's not okay for somebody else to yell at us. If we criticize ourselves all the time, then it's hard for us to draw a boundary with somebody who's constantly criticizing us." -- Joe Hudson, How to Draw HEALTHY Boundaries

The principle is structural, not motivational. It is not "work on self-love and then you will magically be able to draw boundaries." It is: the internal standard is the external standard. They are one standard expressed in two directions.

## The Diagnostic

When someone cannot draw a boundary with a particular behavior in their life -- yelling, criticism, contempt, manipulation -- the question is not "why can't you draw the boundary?" The question is: "where are you doing that same thing to yourself?"

If a person yells at themselves internally, they cannot register external yelling as not-okay. The treatment matches. If a person criticizes themselves all the time, external criticism reads as the normal acoustics of life. If a person treats their own wants as unworthy, they cannot draw a boundary against someone treating their wants as unworthy.

This is the inverse mirror of [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]]. The two principles are the same structural fact expressed in two domains: love and protection. The capacity for external love equals the capacity for internal love. The capacity for external boundaries equals the capacity for internal boundaries.

## The Mechanism

The mind cannot tolerate two different standards for the same behavior. If yelling is okay when I do it to myself, the registration of external yelling as not-okay creates an unbearable contradiction. The mind resolves the contradiction by accepting the external yelling. The internal permission generalizes outward.

The reverse also holds. When the internal permission is withdrawn -- when the person stops yelling at themselves, stops criticizing themselves, stops treating their wants as unworthy -- the external behavior suddenly becomes registerable as not-okay. The boundary becomes drawable because the standard is now consistent.

This is why boundary work that ignores the internal relationship fails. The person learns the words, learns the diagnostic, learns the love test, and still cannot draw the boundary in the moment. The mouth cannot speak what the inner relationship contradicts.

## What This Means for the Work

When a person reports difficulty drawing a specific boundary, the move is to look at the internal version of the same behavior.

- "I can't tell my partner to stop criticizing me." Where is the internal criticism running?
- "I can't ask my boss to stop yelling at me." Where is the internal yelling running?
- "I can't refuse my mother's contempt." Where is the internal contempt running?

The work is on the internal standard. As the internal standard refines, the external boundary becomes available. The person does not need to be coached into firmer language. They need to stop tolerating from themselves what they are also unable to refuse from others.

See [[extend-the-no-asshole-rule-inward]] for the operational form of this move. The person who has built a strict external no-asshole rule but tolerates the internal asshole has the asymmetry the principle describes. The work is making the rule symmetric.

## The Fear-of-Attack Inversion

The principle has a specific tactical form Joe gives in the boundary teaching: the fear that you will be attacked when you draw a boundary is not really fear of the attack. It is the recognition that you are not yet okay with the boundary yourself.

"The thing about a boundary is you only care about the response when you aren't firm on your own boundary, when you're not okay with your own boundary. If you can't overcome your fear of the attack, if you can't get over the idea that they're gonna run over your boundary or they're gonna attack you, then the real work is to be okay with the boundary yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How to Draw HEALTHY Boundaries

The mechanism: when the internal standard is "I am allowed to leave when someone is yelling," the external attack on the boundary cannot land. The person leaves anyway. The attack becomes irrelevant because the boundary is held internally first. When the internal standard is "I am not really allowed to leave," the external attack lands because it is reinforcing the internal doubt. The boundary collapses.

This relocates the boundary work. The boundary is not drawn at the moment of stating it. The boundary is drawn in the internal relationship, and the statement is the announcement of what is already true inside.

## What This Predicts

The principle predicts the populations that struggle with external boundaries:

- People with strong inner critics struggle to draw boundaries with critical people in their lives.
- People who repress their anger struggle to draw boundaries with angry people.
- People who treat their own wants as selfish struggle to draw boundaries with people who treat their wants as selfish.
- People who abandon themselves struggle to draw boundaries with people who abandon them.
- People who manipulate themselves into compliance internally struggle to draw boundaries with people who manipulate them externally.

The match is almost always exact. The external boundary problem is a readout of the internal boundary problem. The reading-out is precise enough to use diagnostically: tell me what kind of behavior you cannot draw a boundary against, and I will tell you what kind of internal behavior you are currently allowing.

## What This Refutes

The principle refutes the "just learn the right words" approach to boundary work. Books about boundaries that focus on phrasing -- "use I-statements," "be assertive," "don't apologize for your needs" -- are addressing the wrong layer. The phrasing is not the bottleneck. The phrasing arrives effortlessly once the internal standard is in place. Until the internal standard is in place, no phrasing will produce the boundary.

The principle also refutes the assumption that boundary work is interpersonal work. Boundary work is intrapersonal work whose results manifest interpersonally. The hours that get spent rehearsing what to say to the partner, the boss, the parent, are usually displaced from the hours that should be spent examining the internal voice that authorizes the same treatment.

## What This Does Not Mean

It does not mean a person has to be perfectly self-loving before they can draw any external boundaries. The capacities grow together. Often the act of drawing a boundary -- even imperfectly -- is part of how the internal standard begins to shift. The external move and the internal move reinforce each other.

It also does not mean external behavior is never the problem. There are abusive people, manipulative bosses, narcissistic parents. The principle is not "your boundary problem is your fault." The principle is: the place the work has the most leverage is internal. Even when the external behavior is unambiguously the problem, the path through to the boundary runs through the internal standard.

## Connection to the Asshole Principle

The principle is the explicit extension of the [[definition-of-asshole]] and the [[no-asshole-rule]] inward. The classic no-asshole rule says: do not tolerate behavior in your life that takes emotion out without apology, without recognition, without making amends. The principle here says: that rule must also apply to the voice in your head. The voice that calls you stupid, that ruminates on your failures, that contemptuously dismisses your wants is an asshole by Joe's definition. The protection from external assholes does not work if there is no protection from the internal one.

See [[extend-the-no-asshole-rule-inward]] for the live coaching move that uses this principle as the diagnostic.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The frustration of knowing all the boundary tools and still not being able to use them
- The discovery that the person you cannot say no to has the same voice as your own inner critic
- The recognition that your tolerance of someone else's behavior matches your tolerance of your own self-treatment
- The realization that the "fear of attack" when drawing a boundary is really uncertainty about your own deserving
- The shift when softening the internal voice suddenly makes the external boundary drawable

## Tone

Joe delivers this almost matter-of-factly at the end of the boundary teaching, after the tactical pieces have been covered. The principle is the deepest layer -- the one that explains why all the tactical work fails when the internal layer is not addressed. The directness is the teaching: "if you start really looking at that, the only times it's not okay for us to draw a boundary is when we can't draw the boundary to ourself." No qualification. No softening. The principle is held as structural.
