# Become Unnecessary

## The Principle

The measure of a good teacher, coach, or transformative organization is how unnecessary it becomes. The less involved the teacher is in the student's ongoing growth, the better the teaching has been. The less the organization is required for the development of its participants, the healthier the organization. Any drift toward "you need us" is the early signal of corruption.

"If someone's teaching you math, you don't want them to have to be there every time you're doing math. That would be a pretty shitty teacher." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

## The Diagnostic

A teacher's relationship to their own necessity is a diagnostic for what is driving the work. The pull to be needed, to be central, to be special, to be the one who saves -- these are the engines that produce dependency-creating organizations. The same teaching delivered without the need to be needed produces empowered students. The same teaching delivered with the need to be needed produces dependents.

"The need to be special or the need to be needed or the need to save, any of those kind of needs are what create those organizations." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

## The Mechanism

The teacher's internal stance about their own necessity becomes embedded in the structure of the work. A teacher who believes they are necessary builds programs that require their continued presence, develops content that only they can deliver, and creates dependence in the student under the guise of helping. A teacher who believes they are not necessary builds programs that increase student capacity, develops content that students can carry forward, and points the student back to themselves at every turn.

This is one specific expression of [[consciousness-is-the-product]]: the teacher's stance toward their own necessity becomes the structure of the organization, which becomes the relationship the students have to themselves.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The online course exposed the necessary-teacher illusion for Joe. The in-person work felt like it required him. Coronavirus forced an experiment in online delivery. The discovery: the work could be delivered without his constant presence, often more powerfully. That discovery was the unlock.

"I saw that the online version needed less of me, and that became really exciting for me." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

The principle is fractal. At each scale, the question is: where am I being more central than I need to be? Where is the design pulling people toward me rather than toward themselves?

## The Cult Dynamic This Prevents

The "your existence is necessary for the development of humanity" stance is the foundation of cult dynamics. The community decides the teacher is special. The teacher accepts. The teacher becomes the bottleneck through which transformation must pass. The organization becomes the gatekeeper. People become dependent on continuing access. Dependency creates the conditions for exploitation.

The corrective is not humility-as-performance. It is genuinely placing the work in service of something larger than the teacher. The organization is here to serve human development. The organization is not itself necessary for human development.

"The less we have a risk of becoming one of those organizations [is] the less necessary that I view myself to be in the work or that I view the organization to be, or that our existence alone isn't necessary for the development of humanity, that we're just here to serve it." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

## What This Requires of the Teacher

The teacher has to be willing to let the work succeed without them. Has to be willing for students to outgrow their tools. Has to be willing for the methodology to be taken in directions the teacher would not take it. Has to be willing for the contribution to become invisible.

"Everything that I do is all about pointing people back to themselves. And the less involved I can be, the less involved the organization can be, the better, so that we don't create an organization where people need us." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

For a teacher whose ego is hooked into being central, this is uncomfortable. For a teacher who has done the inner work, this is freeing. The release from being needed is itself a part of the work.

## In Coaching

When a coachee is the leader of an organization that creates transformation (a coach, a therapist, a workshop facilitator, a course creator), this principle is directly relevant. The questions to surface:

- Where is the work designed around your continued presence?
- What would change if a student finished and never came back?
- What part of you would resist their independence?
- Where is "needing you" producing your sense of worth?

The work is often at the level of the personal need to be needed, not at the level of the business structure. The structure follows from the stance.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a teacher or coach reports feeling burned out by the demand to be central
- When an organization is showing dependency patterns and the leader is asking why
- When a leader of transformative work is grappling with how to scale without distortion
- When the question of intellectual property and gatekeeping becomes pressing (see [[dont-evangelize-the-work]])
- When the [[fixing-people]] pattern is showing up at organizational scale

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[community-over-teacher]]: the structural alternative to teacher-centric design
- Toward [[dont-evangelize-the-work]]: the missionary impulse is the personal-scale version of the necessary-organization impulse
- Toward [[consciousness-is-the-product]]: the teacher's stance is what gets transmitted
- Toward [[integration]]: the design goal is the student's independence, not their continued dependence
- Toward asking what the organization is actually here to serve, and whether continued reliance on the organization is part of that service

## Tone

Plain, structural. Joe states this without moralizing. It is presented as a design principle for transformative work, not as a virtue. The framing is: this is what produces healthy organizations. The alternative produces unhealthy ones. The choice is the leader's.
