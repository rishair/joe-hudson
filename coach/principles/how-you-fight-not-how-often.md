# How You Fight, Not How Often

## The Principle

The standard metric people use for a healthy relationship is "how often do we fight?" Less fighting is better; more fighting is worse. The metric is wrong. The right metric is "how do we fight?"

The fight-count metric makes the goal of the relationship the suppression of friction. The fight-quality metric makes the goal of the relationship the maturation of how friction gets handled. The two metrics produce opposite trajectories. Couples optimizing for fewer fights tend to stagnate, suppress, build resentment, and eventually rupture. Couples optimizing for better fights tend to grow, deepen, and let the relationship become developmental work.

"Nowadays that idea is like ludicrous to us. It's not like we don't judge it by how much we fight, we judge it by how we fight. That's how we're judging our relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Why Fighting Is Important

## The Trajectory Joe and Tara Walked

Joe and Tara originally used the wrong metric. After a period of severely abusive fights, they stopped fighting and started tracking the relationship by the count: fewer fights this week, doing well; more fights this week, doing worse. The metric served them at the time because it gave them a way out of the worst-form fighting.

But the metric did not stay useful. Eventually it became, in Joe's words, "ludicrous." The relationship moved into a stage where fighting itself was not the problem; the form of the fighting was the only thing that mattered. See [[joe-and-tara-retreat-prep-fight]] for the example that illustrates the new metric in operation: a 3-4 minute fight during retreat prep that ended with "it's really hard to fight with you, and I'm glad we resolved that, I love you" -- and two bystanders who had not even registered that a fight had happened.

The transition from the wrong metric to the right one is itself a stage on [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]]. Couples coming out of abusive fighting often need the count metric for a while. Couples in the destination orientation do not.

## The Replacement Metric: How Are You Fighting?

The "how" metric is not one question; it is a cluster of diagnostics about the form of the fight. Joe names them as a list:

- Are you fighting with love?
- Are you fighting with an open heart?
- Are you fighting with respect?
- Have you completely lost your nervous system?
- Are you completely out of control?
- Are you being abusive?
- Are you being mean?
- Have you lost yourself?
- Are you being who you want to be in that fight?

"Are you fighting with love? Are you fighting with an open heart? Are you fighting with respect? Or have you completely lost your nervous system? Are you completely out of control? Are you being abusive? Are you being mean? Have you lost yourself? Are you being who you want to be in that fight?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Fighting Is Important

The list works as a self-evaluation pass. After a fight, the questions are run through and the form of the fight is assessed. The answers tell the couple whether the fighting is producing growth or producing damage. None of the questions ask whether the fight happened.

The list also works in real time. A person who notices mid-fight that they have lost their nervous system has information that lets them choose the next move. A person who is only tracking whether the fight is happening has no information at all -- they are already losing on their metric just by being in the fight.

## Why the Count Metric Fails

The count metric fails for three reasons.

**It treats friction as the problem.** It rewards the suppression of friction and penalizes the surfacing of it. But friction is not the problem; the form of how friction is handled is the problem. See [[friction-is-required-for-life]] for the underlying claim that friction cannot be eliminated from any living system. The count metric tells the couple to do the impossible: take the tension out of the relationship.

**It rewards bad strategies.** A couple optimizing for fewer fights will start avoiding the topics that cause fights. The topics do not go away. The fights about them get bigger and rarer, not smaller. See [[avoided-conflict-goes-internal]] and the compounding-cost argument in [[conflict-as-evolutionary-tension]]. The same dynamic appears at the system level in [[black-swan-small-collapses-prevent-big-ones]]: the suppression of small disruptions guarantees large ones.

**It punishes the couple for being alive.** Two people who are growing, changing, and being honest with each other will have friction. The count metric tells them that the natural friction of two living people is evidence that the relationship is broken. The couple ends up either suppressing themselves to lower the count or feeling like failures for not being able to.

## Why the Quality Metric Works

The quality metric works for three reasons.

**It is achievable.** A couple cannot eliminate fights, but they can change how they fight. The metric is something the couple can actually move on.

**It rewards the right strategies.** A couple optimizing for fighting with love will work on noticing their own nervous system, dropping defenses, naming their trauma, repairing quickly. These are the skills that produce the [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] orientation.

**It scales with development.** A new couple just starting the work will be doing well to keep a fight from becoming abusive. A couple deep in the work will be doing well to look back on a fight with pride. The quality metric scales with where the couple is. The count metric does not.

## When the Old Metric Is Still Useful (Transitionally)

The count metric is not wrong at every stage. Couples coming out of severely abusive fighting often need it. The first work is to stop the abuse. Tracking the count is one way to stop the abuse, because it gives the couple a clear signal that the abuse is recurring.

"And so we had like stopped fighting. And we started tracking our relationship by like how often we fought, right? So it was like we were doing good if we weren't fighting as much and we were doing worse if we were fighting more." -- Joe Hudson, Why Fighting Is Important

The trap is keeping the count metric past the point where it is useful. Once the abusive form has been stopped, the count metric starts producing the suppression problem above. The couple is then stuck in the [[we-never-fight]] configuration, with no path to the next stage.

The move is to graduate from the count metric to the quality metric once the abusive form has been interrupted. The graduation is not automatic. Many couples never make it; they stay in low-friction-low-aliveness for the rest of the relationship.

## The Public-Fight Marker

A specific marker of the quality metric in action: the couple stops hiding the fights. Joe and Tara have fights in front of the people they are facilitating with. The fight is wherever it needs to be. This is not exhibitionism; it is the absence of shame about fighting.

The shame about fighting is downstream of the count metric. If having a fight is evidence the relationship is failing, then having a fight in public is evidence on top of evidence -- everyone now knows the relationship is failing. The couple hides the fight to protect the appearance of the relationship.

Once the count metric has been dropped, the shame about fighting drops with it. The fight is just another piece of the relationship being handled. There is nothing to hide because there is nothing the fight indicates about the health of the relationship beyond the form in which it is handled.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You realize you have been tracking your relationship by the count and the count has been masking real problems
- You notice that the relationships in your life that have the most depth are not the ones with the fewest fights
- You catch yourself feeling proud of "not fighting" and wonder if the pride is hiding something
- You have a hard fight and realize, on reflection, that you handled yourself well -- and feel a quality of pride that the no-fight stance never produced
- You notice that the topics that cause fights are exactly the topics that are not being addressed, and the count is what is keeping them buried

## Tone

Plain, declarative. Joe states this as a simple correction of a common mistake. He uses the word "ludicrous" to describe his own old metric, with self-deprecating humor. The list of "how" questions is delivered as a rapid-fire diagnostic, almost a checklist. The whole teaching has the quality of a fix that took Joe and Tara years to find and that he wishes more couples had access to earlier.
