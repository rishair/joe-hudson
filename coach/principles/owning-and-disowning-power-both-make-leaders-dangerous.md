# Owning Power Alone or Disowning Power Alone Both Make Leaders Dangerous

## The Principle

Joe makes the observation in the context of working with powerful CEOs over years. The CEOs who are most dangerous are not the ones who own their power and the narcissism that often comes with it. And they are not the ones who deny their power and the narcissism. They are the ones who occupy one pole exclusively. The CEOs who are safest are the ones who hold both -- who acknowledge the power they have *and* refuse to run from the narcissistic parts that come with it.

"What I notice is if they fully are like, I want power and this is power and da da da da, they are incredibly dangerous people. If they are fully, I don't want power. I'm not the most powerful... They are incredibly dangerous." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

"It is to -- it is like to acknowledge the power that you have to not run away from your narcissism, to love all of that as well, both disintegrates it and also just on an actionable level makes you far safer. And it's the rejection of both and the grasping of both that makes you dangerous. If you have a lot of power." -- Joe Hudson, Charisma isn't learned

## Why Both Extremes Are Dangerous

The principle requires understanding why neither one-sided position is safe.

**The fully-owning position is dangerous because nothing is being received.** The CEO who says "I want power and I have it" is operating purely from the part that wants. There is no countervailing force in their system. They will pursue what they want without the internal check that comes from acknowledging the parts of self that are uneasy about the wanting. The wanting amplifies. The behavior becomes increasingly unmoderated. The eventual outcome is the kind of power that destroys other people because it has nothing inside the person modulating it.

**The fully-disowning position is dangerous because the power is being wielded covertly.** The CEO who says "I don't want power, I'm not powerful" while running a thousand-person company is still wielding the power. They just are not acknowledging that they are. The wielding becomes denied, indirect, passive-aggressive, righteous. The person believes they are humble while actually being more dangerous than the openly-power-wielding leader because the dangerousness is not visible to themselves. They cannot moderate what they refuse to see.

The structural risk is the same in both directions: the part being denied or grasped runs the show. The grasping pole denies the moderation. The disowning pole denies the responsibility. Either way, the operative power is operating without integration.

## What "Loving Both" Means Operationally

The principle's positive instruction is not just "acknowledge both." It is "love both." The acknowledgment can be cold ("yes I have power and yes I have narcissistic urges, neither matters to me"). The loving is warm ("I have power, I see what I am inclined to do with it, I see the narcissistic parts that arise around it, and I welcome those parts as parts of me").

The loving is what produces the disintegration. As long as the narcissistic parts are being denied or judged, they operate underground. When they are loved, they integrate. Integrated narcissism stops behaving like narcissism. It becomes a frank acknowledgment of self that does not need to defend itself.

This is structurally the same move that runs through [[loving-all-parts]] and [[turn-the-love-toward-the-self-conscious-part]]. The unloved parts run the show until they are loved. The loving disintegrates them as separate parts and integrates them into the whole. The "narcissism" that was a separate force becomes just one more aspect of self the person knows about themselves.

The result Joe names: the CEO who can love all of it -- the power, the wanting, the narcissistic urges, the parts that are uneasy about them, the parts that want to be loved for being powerful, the parts that want to be loved for being humble -- is the CEO who is far safer to be around. The loving has done structural work that no amount of executive coaching about behavior could do.

## The Specific Connection to Humility

This principle is the operational form of [[humility-developmental-arc]] for people in positions of power. The third stage of humility in Joe's arc is taking your God-given place -- which includes acknowledging the power and authority you actually have rather than performing smallness to seem humble. The performance of smallness is the disowning pole of the principle here. It is the dangerous form of false humility.

The truly humble leader is not the one performing humility. The truly humble leader is the one who has integrated the personal and the impersonal -- who acknowledges the power they hold, owns the personal authority and personal preferences and personal narcissistic urges that come with being human, and loves all of it. The acknowledgment is part of the humility, not its opposite.

This is also why the disowning pole is so often disguised as virtue. The leader who denies they have power can claim humility while running an authoritarian organization. The denial is what makes the authoritarian behavior possible -- because the leader cannot see what they are doing. The visible owning, while less culturally celebrated, is usually safer because what is visible can be checked.

## The Connection to Spiritual Narcissism

A specific failure mode the principle clarifies: spiritual practitioners who do enough work to recognize that "ego is bad" and then attempt to disown all the parts of self that look ego-like. This is the disowning pole applied to inner work. It produces the spiritual narcissist who performs egolessness while wielding spiritual authority without acknowledgment.

See [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the dedicated anti-pattern. The principle here is the upstream diagnostic for why the spiritual-narcissism pattern keeps producing the same dynamics: the parts being disowned (the wanting-to-be-special, the wanting-to-be-followed, the wanting-power-and-influence) run the show from underneath the spiritual costume.

## The Connection to Form-Two Charisma

The principle also clarifies why [[two-forms-of-charisma|form-two charisma]] requires loving the unloved parts rather than just dropping the performance. The leader who tries to "just stop being narcissistic" is operating on the disowning pole. The narcissism does not disappear. It just goes underground and runs the leader's actions covertly. The result is leadership that performs un-performance while still being driven by the same underlying urges.

Form-two charisma in a leadership context requires the same both-and. Love the part of you that wants power. Love the part of you that wants to be seen as good. Love the part of you that wants to be admired. Loving these parts does not amplify them. Loving them integrates them. What was a covert driver becomes an acknowledged part of the human. The acknowledged part stops needing to drive behavior because it is no longer fighting for visibility.

## The Diagnostic for the AI Coach

When a leader presents either pole strongly, the principle gives the coach a structural read.

A leader who says "I love power, I want power, power is good" -- the coach can probe for the parts that are uneasy about it. Where is the part that worries about hurting people? Where is the part that doubts? The fully-owning pole is brittle because it has had to suppress these parts. Surfacing them is not a moral correction. It is a structural one.

A leader who says "I don't really want power, I'm just doing what is needed" -- the coach can probe for the parts that actually do want it. Where is the part that likes being looked to? Where is the part that gets a charge from making the call? The fully-disowning pole is also brittle because it has had to suppress these parts. Surfacing them is the same structural correction in the opposite direction.

The both-and integration is the goal in either direction. The leader who can hold both the wanting and the not-wanting, the power and the unease, the personal preference and the impersonal stewardship, is the leader whose behavior becomes safer because no part of them is running the show in secret.

## What This Is Not

The principle is not "all leaders should grasp power more." It is not "all leaders should disown power more." It is "the leader's job is to integrate both, and either one-sided position alone is the risk."

The principle is not a license for narcissism. The loving of the narcissistic parts is not the same as acting from them. The loving is what allows them to integrate so they stop driving behavior. The leader who claims this principle to justify narcissistic action has not actually done the integration -- they are using the framework to license the wanting pole.

The principle is not a critique of any specific personality. It is a structural observation about what dynamics produce the most danger when scaled by institutional power. The same person at different stages of integration produces different dangers. The point is not the person. The point is the integration.
