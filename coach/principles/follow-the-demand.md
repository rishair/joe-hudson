# Follow the Demand, Don't Impose the Agenda

## The Principle

In both coaching and business, you start where the other person is and follow their agenda, not yours. You do not tell people what they should want. You do not bring a pre-formed vision and try to fit them into it. You ask what they want, you find out what is being asked for, and if you can be of service to that in alignment with your own integrity, you serve it.

This applies in two places simultaneously: in a coaching session, where the coach starts with the coachee's stated concern rather than the coach's diagnosis; and in a business, where the offering responds to what people are actually requesting rather than what the founder thinks they should want.

"I feel like it's the same when you're coaching somebody, you start with where they are. You don't tell them what you think their agenda should be. You follow their agenda. I think business is the same way. You don't ask people to be different. You don't give them an agenda. You find out what their agenda is. And if you can serve that, if you can be of service to that, and it is aligned internally, then by all means, go ahead." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

## The Mechanism

When you impose an agenda, the other person feels the imposition before they feel the content. The imposition itself becomes the obstacle to receiving the content. Even good content delivered as imposition produces resistance, because resistance is the natural response to being told what to want.

When you follow the demand, the person experiences being met. The being-met itself is part of the value. The content delivered after the meeting can be received because the relationship has been established as one of service rather than one of correction.

This is the same dynamic as [[impartiality]] in [[view]]. Wanting the person to want a particular thing is partiality. Starting with what the person actually wants is impartiality. The first produces resistance. The second produces movement.

## Joe's Own Business Story

The path of Joe's business is the principle in operation. He did not set out to build a coaching business. He went into venture capital. He brought self-awareness to that work. The portfolio companies he invested in benefited. The founders started telling other people. The line got long. He created a course because there was no other way to meet the demand.

"I was really just following the demand. I was just following people's requests for the most part. I have a vision for sure, but it's incredibly informed by what people have asked for and what they want." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

The vision exists. The vision is informed by the demand. The vision is not imposed on the demand.

This is different from "build it and they will come." That stance starts with the founder's vision and assumes the market will appear. The principle here starts with the market and lets the offering grow into the demand that is already present.

## What This Rules Out

This principle rules out several common business and coaching patterns:

**The visionary founder who knows what people need before they know.** This stance occasionally succeeds (when the founder happens to be right) but more often produces products no one wants. The pattern is also fragile because the founder cannot iterate based on feedback when the feedback contradicts the vision.

**The savior coach who has the answer the coachee does not yet know they need.** This stance often produces an initial sense of insight that does not translate into change, because the coachee's actual concern never got addressed. The coach was answering the question the coach wanted to answer.

**The teacher who tells the student what their growth edge is.** This stance occasionally produces breakthroughs (when the teacher is right and the student is open) but more often produces compliance or rebellion. The growth edge belongs to the student. The teacher can offer reflection, but the diagnosis belongs to the one being diagnosed.

## What This Does Not Mean

This principle does not mean abandoning your own integrity. Joe is explicit: "if you can be of service to that, and it is aligned internally." If the demand is for something that violates your alignment, you do not serve it because the demand exists. The demand is the starting point. Your integrity is the gate.

This also does not mean having no vision. Joe has a vision. The vision is informed by demand, not imposed on demand. The two work together. The vision provides direction. The demand provides the specific path.

## The Wonder Connection

This principle is the business and coaching expression of [[wonder]]. Wonder is the state of being open to what is actually there, without prescribing what should be there. The coach who follows the demand is in wonder about what the coachee is bringing. The founder who follows the demand is in wonder about what the market is asking for.

Without wonder, both the coach and the founder are imposing their own answer onto a question they have not yet heard. With wonder, the question can be heard, and the response can be calibrated to the actual question.

## What This Looks Like in a Coaching Session

The coachee arrives with a presenting concern. The standard temptation is to listen briefly and then deliver the framework the coach thinks is most relevant. The principle here is to stay with what the coachee is actually presenting until the coach genuinely understands what the coachee is asking.

This includes the surface request ("help me with my marriage") and the underlying ask ("help me feel less alone") and the immediate ask ("just hear me right now"). The coach who jumps to the underlying ask before the coachee has been heard at the surface is imposing an agenda. The coach who stays with the surface forever is also failing, because the coachee may need help locating the underlying request.

The work is to follow the actual movement of what the coachee is bringing, rather than to advance the coach's preferred path.

## What This Looks Like in Business

The standard business pattern is to build what you think people will buy. The principle here is to find out what people are already asking for, and either serve that or decline. The "build it and they will come" approach is replaced with "they are already coming for this, let me serve it well."

This requires actual conversations with actual potential customers. It requires the founder to be willing to be wrong about what the market wants. It requires the willingness to hear that the demand is for something different than the founder imagined.

The result is offerings that fit demand rather than offerings that fight for demand. The marketing becomes easier, the product-market fit becomes natural, and the customer feels met from first contact.

- When a coach is wondering how to start with a difficult client
- When a founder is asking what to build next
- When a leader is imposing a vision and getting resistance
- When the question is whether to scale a vision or follow demand
- When a coachee feels unheard and the standard frameworks are not landing

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[impartiality]]: the state that allows actual hearing
- Toward [[wonder]]: the practice of staying open to what is actually being asked
- Toward [[marketing-as-the-beginning]]: the marketing that emerges from following demand is qualitatively different
- Toward [[authenticity-in-business]]: serving actual human drives rather than imposing structures
- Away from [[fixing-people]]: fixing requires an imposed agenda about what is broken
- Away from [[dont-evangelize-the-work]]: evangelism is imposition by definition

## Tone

Joe says this matter-of-factly, as obvious. The framing is "this is what works," not "this is virtuous." The principle is offered as a practical instrument for both coaching and business, with the implication that ignoring it produces predictable failure modes.
