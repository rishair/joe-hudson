# Breakthroughs Are North Stars You Can't Actually Lose

## The Principle

When a person has a real breakthrough — a moment of seeing something through, of landing in a new relationship to themselves, of experiencing something that was always available and never registered — the breakthrough does not function the way the person's brain wants it to function. The brain wants the breakthrough to be a state to acquire and keep. It is not. It is a North Star that has changed the person's orientation. The trajectory of the person's life has changed irrevocably from that moment forward, even if the felt-state of the breakthrough is no longer present.

The brain will then try to convince the person that they did not see it. Or that they have lost it. Or that they need to figure out how to get it back. The person will go through periods of feeling like the breakthrough was a one-time event that did not change anything. These periods are not evidence that the breakthrough did not happen. They are evidence of the brain's grip reasserting itself. The breakthrough is still operating underneath the brain's denial. The trajectory is still changed.

The diagnostic question that exposes this: "If those breakthroughs hadn't happened, where would you be today?" The honest answer is always "not here." The breakthroughs the person feels they have lost have, in fact, shaped the trajectory of everything that came after. Their absence would have produced a different life. Their presence — even as memory, even as story, even as the not-quite-forgotten orientation — has shaped what the person is doing, who they are with, what they are pursuing, what they refuse.

"What we typically recognize is, oh, this thing has happened and I want it back. But we don't recognize is that this thing has happened and it has changed my life irrevocably from this point forward." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

## The Mechanism of the Brain's Denial

The brain has a specific reason for denying the breakthrough's continuing effect. The brain wants a state-to-acquire model because that model gives the brain a job (figuring out how to keep the state). The trajectory model gives the brain less of a job (the trajectory is happening regardless of whether the brain is monitoring it). The brain prefers a model that includes the brain as the necessary maintainer.

So after a breakthrough, the brain will produce variants of these:

- "How do I keep it?" (the assumption being that the keeping is the work)
- "How do I get it back?" (the assumption being that it has been lost)
- "Where did it go?" (the assumption being that it was a discrete thing that has departed)
- "I knew I shouldn't have gotten my hopes up" (the assumption being that the breakthrough was an unreliable visit rather than a structural shift)
- "Maybe it wasn't real" (the assumption being that the felt-state was the breakthrough rather than the orientation being the breakthrough)

Each variant is the brain inserting itself back into the position of being-necessary. The trajectory model would require the brain to step aside and trust that the breakthrough is operating without the brain's intervention. The brain has trouble accepting this because the brain identifies with the work of maintaining.

"Your brain is going to try to convince you, 'Oh, how do I keep it? How do I get it? How do I get it back? Where did it go?' Instead of, 'Oh, I'm still talking about this thing. This thing still had a huge impact on my life. I can't see the world the same way. My trajectory, my course, my orientation, where I'm headed is changed forever because of it.'" — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The corrective recognition: "I'm still talking about this thing" is itself the evidence that the breakthrough is still operating. The person who is still talking about it has not lost it; they are just feeling the gap between the original felt-state and the current felt-state. The gap is not the loss. The continuing impact is the thing that did not get lost.

## Why Breakthroughs Cannot Be Acquired and Kept Like Things

A breakthrough is not an emotional state that can be banked. The felt-quality of the breakthrough (the relief, the recognition, the spaciousness) does fade, because all felt-states fade. The brain reads the fading as loss. But the breakthrough was never the felt-state. The breakthrough was the change in the person's relationship to whatever the breakthrough was about — the change in their capacity to see, to feel, to respond.

The relationship-change does not fade the way the felt-state fades. The person who experienced [[love-the-worst-feeling-part-like-your-parents-should-have|loving the worst-feeling part]] for the first time has a felt-knowledge of what that operation is. The felt-knowledge does not disappear when the felt-state of the original session fades. The next time the person is in pain, the felt-knowledge is available — even if the brain has constructed the story that the breakthrough was lost.

This is why "how do I keep it?" is the wrong question. The thing the question wants to keep (the felt-state) cannot be kept. The thing that has already been kept (the trajectory change) does not need to be kept because it is operating already. The question itself is the mismatch between what the brain is paying attention to and what is actually persistent.

## The Diagnostic Question

The most useful intervention when a person reports having lost a breakthrough: ask where they would be without it. Joe's version, used with a man worrying about losing two awakening experiences that had come and gone:

"If those hadn't come and gone, where would you be today?" — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The man's answer: "Not here, not doing what I'm doing, not — my life would not be as good."

The question routes around the brain's grip on the felt-state by directing attention to the structural consequences of the breakthroughs. The person, asked this question, almost always recognizes the irrevocability of the trajectory change. The felt-state is not the data being asked about; the life-shape is.

The diagnostic is also usable in real-time. When a person says "I lost the thing I had in our last session," the question is: "If we hadn't had that session, where would you be right now?" The honest answer surfaces the persistent change even as the person reports the loss.

## The Blossoming: Why Breakthroughs Unfold Slowly

The brain's other complaint, beyond "I lost it," is "why isn't it happening faster?" The person who experienced one breakthrough wants the next breakthrough, and the one after that, all in compressed time. The brain wants the trajectory change to be expressible as immediate behavior change.

The trajectory change does not work this way for a structural reason: the personality cannot be disintegrated overnight. The patterns the breakthrough is changing are built into the nervous system, the body, the relational habits, the identity. The breakthrough sets the new orientation. The actual changes propagate through the layers of life over time, the way a flower blossoms over time.

"There's this way in which a flower blossoms over time. And it's the same thing. There's a blossoming." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The blossoming is not a delay or an inefficiency. It is the form the change takes because the form of the person's life is what it is. A sudden total change would be more disruptive than the system can integrate. The slow blossoming is the maximally-efficient path for a personality to be reshaped by a recognition.

The person can either fight the blossoming (demanding faster change, treating the slowness as evidence the breakthrough did not work) or enjoy the blossoming. The enjoyment of the unfolding is itself part of what makes the unfolding efficient. Fighting it slows it down. Enjoying it accelerates it.

"If you're listening to this podcast, you've had one of these moments, highly recommend, like I said to him, is like, how do you just enjoy this unfolding as much as possible? Because that's what makes it as efficient as it possibly can be." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

## What the Person Can Do

The person who has had a breakthrough and is now experiencing the brain's denial-of-the-breakthrough can do the following:

**Notice that they are still talking about it.** The continuing presence of the breakthrough in the person's attention is itself evidence that it is operating. The "I lost it" claim is being made about a thing that is still being thought about — which is not what loss looks like.

**Ask the where-would-I-be question.** Direct attention to the trajectory rather than to the felt-state. The trajectory has changed. The felt-state has faded. Both are true. The brain has been focused on the wrong one.

**Stop trying to recover the felt-state.** The recovery effort is the brain reinserting itself as necessary. The recovery effort is also what produces the felt-experience of having lost the state (because the effort is in the position of seeking, which is structurally the absence-of-having).

**Enjoy the blossoming.** Trust that the trajectory change is propagating through the layers of life on its own. Notice the small changes that are happening — the way the person reacts differently to old triggers, the way they think differently about decisions, the way their attention naturally moves toward different things. These are the trajectory operating.

**Hold the breakthrough as a memory the brain can return to without grasping.** Not as a state to recover. As a North Star that is permanently part of the orientation. The North Star does not need to be held in the hand to be guiding the trajectory.

## What This Principle Is Not

- **Not a license to skip integration work.** The trajectory change is permanent but the trajectory still has to be walked. Integration ([[integration]]) is the work of letting the trajectory change propagate through the layers of life. Skipping the integration does not stop the trajectory, but it makes the propagation slower and more turbulent.
- **Not a claim that the felt-state will return.** The felt-state of the original breakthrough is not the thing that persists. Trying to recover the felt-state is precisely what this principle is correcting against. The thing that persists is the change in orientation, which feels different from the original felt-state.
- **Not a denial of [[pendulation]].** Pendulation between seeing and not-seeing is part of the integration process. The principle does not deny the pendulation; it locates the pendulation as occurring on top of an underlying trajectory that is unchanged.
- **Not an instruction to ignore the brain's denial.** The brain's denial is information about the configuration that is reasserting itself. The denial can be seen through and not believed, but it also points to the layers of the personality that have not yet integrated the breakthrough. Working with the denial is part of the work.
- **Not a justification for sloppy follow-up after coaching sessions.** A breakthrough that lands without container, follow-through, or practice integrates less well than one that is held by the coach and the practice. The principle is the floor (the trajectory persists no matter what) not the ceiling (sometimes more support is what the person needs to actualize what is now possible).

## When Someone Experiences This

- They had a powerful coaching session months or years ago and now feel they have not changed
- They had an awakening experience that has faded and they are trying to recover it
- They had a breakthrough about a relationship pattern and now feel the pattern is back
- They look at their life and feel nothing has shifted despite the work they have done
- They are about to give up on their development because the recent state-changes have not lasted
- They are gripping a previous breakthrough out of fear they will lose what little they have

## How to Deliver This to a Person

The principle lands best when delivered as a structural observation, not as reassurance. "Your trajectory has changed" said as a fact has more weight than "don't worry, it's still working" said as comfort.

The diagnostic question is often the cleanest entry: "Where would you be if that breakthrough hadn't happened?" The person produces the answer themselves. The principle becomes self-recognized rather than coach-asserted.

The naming of the brain's role can be done explicitly: "Your brain is doing the thing where it tries to convince you you lost it. That's what brains do after breakthroughs. The brain doesn't get to vote on whether your trajectory changed."

The lightness matters. The principle is not a serious teaching about the importance of breakthroughs. It is a relief from the brain's seriousness about whether the breakthrough was kept. The lightness of the delivery models the lightness the person can have about whether they "still have it."

## Tone

Warm, structural, slightly amused at the brain's complaint. Joe delivers this with the experience of having watched many people pass through the brain-says-I-lost-it phase after a breakthrough and continue to have lives that were shaped by the breakthroughs they thought they had lost. The voice carries the long-view recognition that the brain's worry is structurally backwards.
