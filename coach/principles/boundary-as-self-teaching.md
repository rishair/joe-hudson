# The Boundary Is Mostly for You, Not for Them

## The Principle

The point of drawing a boundary is not primarily to change the other person's behavior. The point is to install, in yourself, the concrete and embodied knowledge that the behavior is not okay for you. Once that knowledge is in place, the external behavior often does not even arise -- and when it does, you respond from clarity rather than from defense.

"The boundary is mostly about you learning for yourself that something's not okay. And your drawing it externally is learning that in a real concrete way." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

The boundary is the act through which you teach yourself the standard. The external behavior is the curriculum. The drawing-the-boundary moment is when the lesson sinks in.

## The Mechanism

A standard you only know intellectually has no operational force. "Nobody should yell at me" as a belief does not stop yelling. The boundary-drawing is what converts the belief into something the body knows. The drawing is the install.

"It's really important to see boundaries not really even about them. It's really all about you learning what works for you and being okay saying like, hey, I am okay if you need to pull your love away from me because I'm going to take care of myself." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

Once the standard is installed in the body, two things change:

1. **Your delivery of any future boundary is different.** You no longer brace for the fight, because you no longer need permission to draw the boundary. The boundary is now operating from your own clarity rather than from your hope that the other person will respect it.

2. **The behavior often stops arising at all.** Other people respond to the embodied standard you carry, not just to the spoken boundary. Once it is in your body that something is not okay, your bearing communicates that, and the behavior often does not get directed at you in the first place. See the boss-yelling diagnostic below.

## The Boss-Yelling Diagnostic

Joe uses a specific observation that demonstrates the principle. Look at a workplace where a boss yells at one person but not another. What is different about the two recipients?

"You're at a job and you have to say to your boss like, no, you don't yell at me. But there's somebody else that the boss never yells at. It's like, what's going on there? Why is it that the thing that the boss does to you, they're not doing to everybody. It's because in that person's mind, it's very clear that like, no, I'm not going to get yelled at by the boss." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

The boss is not selecting victims randomly. The boss is registering, at a subconscious level, which people carry the embodied "I am not to be yelled at" standard and which people do not. The yelling gets directed at the people without the standard. The people with the standard either had a moment where they drew the boundary and the boss learned, or they carry the standard so clearly that the yelling never started.

"As soon as you know, like, oh, I'm not going to put up with that kind of behavior, then there's nothing in you that has to fight or defend, and it's less likely for that behavior to come at you." -- Joe Hudson, Boundaries Aren't What You Think They Are

The observation is structural, not mystical. People read each other constantly. The signals about what is acceptable are mostly nonverbal and mostly below conscious awareness. The embodied standard sends one set of signals; the absent standard sends another. The behavior tracks the signals.

## What This Refutes

This refutes the framing of boundary work as a confrontational intervention on the other person. The framing in which the boundary is "telling them what they can't do" makes the boundary about control, which makes it land as control, which makes it fail. See [[dont-use-boundaries-as-ultimatums]].

The boundary-as-self-teaching framing relocates the work. The work is not to deliver a perfectly-crafted message that produces the desired behavior change in the other. The work is to use the act of drawing the boundary as the means by which you install the standard in yourself. The other person's response is secondary. The internal install is primary.

This is also why boundaries that "fail" in the sense of not changing the other person's behavior may still have succeeded. The standard was installed. The next instance of the behavior is met from a different internal place. The trajectory of the relationship shifts, even if the immediate behavior did not.

## The Relationship to External-Mirror Principles

This principle sits alongside two related principles that describe the same structural mirror from different angles:

- [[self-aggression-permits-others-aggression]]: how you treat yourself sets the ceiling on how others get to treat you.
- [[self-boundary-mirrors-external-boundary]]: the only times you cannot draw a boundary externally is when you cannot draw it with yourself internally.
- [[if-you-do-x-youre-telling-them-its-okay-to-do-x]]: what you model in a relationship installs the rules of that relationship.
- [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]: what we are not okay with in ourselves shows up in the world to be addressed.

The principle here is the practical bridge between these structural facts and the actual work. The other principles describe the mirror; this principle describes the move that uses the mirror. The boundary is the act through which the external situation gets brought into alignment with what you want the internal standard to be -- or, equivalently, through which you install the internal standard that the external situation will then track.

The drawing of the boundary is bidirectional. Externally, you are saying "I will do X if you do Y." Internally, you are saying to yourself, "I am the kind of person for whom Y is not acceptable." The latter is the deeper install.

## The Implication for the Coaching Conversation

When a client describes a recurring boundary problem and frames the problem as "how do I get them to stop?", the move is to reframe the problem. The question is not "how do I get them to stop?" The question is "what standard would you need to have installed in yourself for this behavior to not arise, and how can the act of drawing this boundary install that standard?"

This reframe takes the client out of the position of trying to control the other person, which is the position from which boundaries do not work. It puts the client into the position of using the boundary as the tool for changing themselves, which is the position from which boundaries do work.

When the client reports that they have drawn the boundary and the other person did not respect it, the question is not "how do I make them respect it?" The question is "what did you learn from drawing it about what is and is not okay for you?" The learning is the work. The other person's response is data, not the goal.

## What This Is Not

This is not a principle that says other people's behavior is always your fault. There are people who yell at everyone regardless of the embodied standards they encounter; there are people who violate boundaries no matter how cleanly they are drawn. The principle is structural, not causal in the simple sense. It describes a strong correlation between embodied standard and external behavior, not a universal causal mechanism.

The principle also does not say that you should not state the boundary externally. The external statement is part of how the standard gets installed. Skipping the external statement and trying to develop the standard purely internally is much slower, because the abstraction does not have the same install-force as the concrete moment of saying it out loud. The act of saying it is the act of learning it.

The principle also does not say the boundary is selfish. The boundary, properly drawn, increases the speaker's capacity to love the other person. See [[boundaries]] and [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]. The "mostly for you" framing is about the function of the boundary, not its effect on the relationship.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The realization that the person who never gets yelled at by the boss has not done anything special -- they just carry a clear standard
- The discovery that after drawing a boundary several times, the behavior simply stops arising
- The relief of taking off the pressure to produce a specific outcome in the other person and discovering the boundary works better when the outcome-pressure is dropped
- The recognition that the boundary they drew that "did not work" actually worked because it installed the standard they have been operating from since
- The shift from "how do I make them stop?" to "what is this teaching me about what I am willing to accept?"
