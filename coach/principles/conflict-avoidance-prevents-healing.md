# Conflict Avoidance Prevents Healing (Yours and Theirs)

## The Principle

Conflict is the medium through which shame heals. Both the shame you carry and the shame other people carry require conflict to be encountered, felt, and metabolized. Avoiding conflict is not kindness. It denies the people around you the chance to grow, and it denies yourself the chance to heal.

"How else are you going to heal your shame?" -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The question is a trap and a gift. Once you understand [[shame-as-disconnection]] and [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]], conflict avoidance reveals itself as the strategy that maintains the very shame it claims to manage.

## The Two Costs

**Cost to you:** Your shame cannot heal without being encountered. The shame needs to be felt, and the felt sense of shame requires the kind of disconnection that comes from conflict. If you never offend, you never trigger your shame, you never feel it, you never integrate it. The shame stays as a permanent background condition organizing your behavior. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

"How else am I going to heal? It's good. I know it's always a good spot when I like lose all thoughts." -- Guest, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

**Cost to them:** Other people heal their shame the same way you do. By being offended, by being disagreed with, by having to encounter someone who does not fit their preferences. If you smooth every interaction, you remove their opportunity to feel what was theirs to feel.

"Correct. If you look at Tibetan teaching, the first stage of the teaching is we're friends and you build this deep connection. And then the second stage of the teaching is they do everything they possibly can to offend the student. Because if I offend you, then I am pointing to an ego that still is running your life." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

See [[tibetan-teacher-offending-students]] for the full example.

## In Leadership

The principle has a direct organizational form. Conflict-avoidant leaders create stagnant organizations. The team's growth depends on the leader's willingness to have hard conversations.

"The conflict-avoidant leader does not have a team that is transforming, evolving, overcoming. They just sit on stagnant problems." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

"Whereas leaders who aren't conflict avoidant, say Steve Jobs would be a great example of that, innovation all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

Joe is careful to qualify: this is not a license to yell. "Not that I'm suggesting you have to yell at people, please, I'm not. But my point is that that conflict avoidance pretty much can guarantee stagnation in an organization and in people and in marriages."

## The Trust Cost in Leadership

The leadership form has a specific cost: the team does not trust the conflict-avoidant leader. The team can feel that the leader is sidestepping the real conversations. They learn to read the avoidance. The trust erodes in ways the leader does not see.

"Any conflict-avoidant manager, if you are listening to this and you are a conflict-avoidant manager, your team does not trust you. I guarantee it. I guarantee your team doesn't trust you. You think you're being nice, but what you're doing is you are destroying the trust between you and your team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The mechanism: trust is built through conflict survived together. See [[trust-requires-conflict]]. The leader who avoids the conflict denies the team the experience that would have built the trust. The team's experience of the relationship is bounded by what they have actually been through with the leader. If they have never been through anything difficult together, the relationship has no proven depth.

This is the team-level statement of the larger principle. The personal forms of conflict avoidance (in marriages, in friendships, with oneself) all prevent healing. The leadership form additionally destroys trust at the team level, with downstream effects on output, retention, and culture.

## The Conflict-Coming-To-You Diagnostic

A common self-deception of the conflict-avoidant manager: they feel they are not avoiding conflict because conflict is constantly coming to them. The constancy is the diagnosis, not the disconfirmation. Conflicts keep coming because they are not being resolved. See [[conflict-coming-to-you-means-youre-avoiding]] for the full diagnostic.

## Trading Short-Term Discomfort for Long-Term Discomfort

The conflict-avoidant leader makes a trade they do not see they are making. The short-term discomfort of a hard conversation gets converted into the long-term discomfort of a stagnant team, ongoing political mess, and gradual erosion of trust.

"It's oftentimes you see the leaders trade the short-term discomfort for the long-term discomfort. And so if you're okay with the short-term discomfort, you have a lot less long-term discomfort. That's a critical piece as well." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The arithmetic favors the short-term discomfort by a wide margin. A single difficult conversation produces minutes or hours of discomfort. The unresolved version produces years. The leader who is willing to be uncomfortable for an hour saves the team months of degraded function. The team feels it both ways.

## In Trust

Trust is built through conflict survived together, not through conflict prevented. See [[trust-requires-conflict]].

"Trust is built by having conflict and overcoming it together." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The least offensive person is also the least trustworthy. Without conflict, you do not know what they actually think, where their boundaries are, or whether they will hold their ground.

## The Choice, Made Explicit

Joe lays the choice out as a list. The clarity of the comparison does the persuasive work.

"You can be yourself and you can heal your shame and you can give the opportunity for other people to grow and you can have a non-stagnant life, or you can try to not offend people and you cannot heal your shame and you can prevent the opportunities for other people to grow and you can have a stagnant organization." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

"The choice is quite obvious. However, we don't make choices logically, we make choices emotionally." See [[decisions-are-emotional]]. The point of laying out the choice is not to win the argument; it is to give the cognitive frame that supports the emotional work the body still has to do.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You realize the people you have been "protecting" from your truth are not getting what they need from you.
- You notice that the relationships in your life are flat — pleasant but not growing.
- You see that the team you lead is repeating the same problems because nothing is being named.
- You see that your own development has plateaued.
- You catch yourself thinking "I'm being kind by not saying it" and realize the kindness is for you, not them.

## The Daughter Example

When the principle lands somatically, the implications travel into other relationships. Mid-session the guest connects the pattern to his 3.5-year-old daughter: she has learned that the way to get connection with him is to solve problems for him. The conflict avoidance with himself is being transmitted as a relational pattern to his child.

"That's how she's, that's how she's found to find connection with you. She has started to realize at 3 and a half that the way to get connection with dad is to solve problems, to help him solve problems." -- Joe Hudson, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The principle scales: the parent's conflict avoidance becomes the child's emotional management training.

## Tone

Direct and clarifying, with examples drawn from leadership, parenting, and spiritual traditions. Joe makes the case methodically. The persuasion is the laying-out of the structure, not emotional pressure.
