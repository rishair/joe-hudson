# Anyone Doing Something of Consequence Is an Imposter

## Definition

The evidence for imposter syndrome is real. If you are doing anything of consequence, you do not know what you are doing. Nobody who is doing important work knows how to do it -- they are figuring it out as they go, failing many times, making mistakes.

This is not the loss of the argument. It is the dissolution of the argument. You cannot convince yourself you are not an imposter, because the evidence supports the fact that you are. The way out is not to win the case but to see that the category applies to everyone doing real work.

"If you think you're an imposter, it's because you are, right? None of -- nobody who's doing anything important in the world knows how to do it. They're figuring it out as they go. They fail. Fail many times. They make mistakes many times." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

## In Practice

The diagnostic for whether something is a real problem: do you know how to solve it? If you know, it is not a problem. It is a to-do list. The dishwashing test.

"Nobody goes into a problem saying, I know how to solve the problem. If you go in saying, I know how to solve the problem, then it's not an actual problem. It's just a to-do list. Then it's like washing the dishes. You're not like, oh my God, I have a problem. I don't know how to wash the dishes. You're like, oh my God, I'm just going to go wash the dishes." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

This inverts the imposter frame. Not-knowing is the marker of doing something real. Knowing is the marker of doing something trivial. Imposter syndrome is the experience of being in real territory.

Corollary: if you knew exactly what you were doing, you would be bored and you would not be doing it. The not-knowing is part of the appeal of the work. The discomfort of imposter syndrome and the aliveness of real work share a single source: stepping into territory that has not been resolved.

## The Kindergarten Frame

If everyone in the room is a kindergartner, it does not bother you to be a kindergartner. The shame in imposter syndrome comes from believing that the others are not imposters -- that there is a category of real, qualified people that you are not in. Once you see that the others are also imposters, the experience collapses into something neutral or even relieving.

"It's like saying you're at a kindergarten and you're like, oh my God, I'm a kindergartner. Ah, right, like it doesn't bother you because everybody's a kindergartner." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

The work of dissolving imposter syndrome is not building evidence for your competence. It is seeing the universality of incompetence among people doing real things.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents with [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] and is trying to win the argument that they are not an imposter, this is the principle that disarms the loop.
- When someone is gripping for evidence of their qualification, this principle reframes the search.
- When someone is paralyzed by feeling that they should know but don't, this principle reframes not-knowing as the signature of real work.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Joe usually pairs this with the question [[who-isnt-an-imposter]] -- the diagnostic that forces the person to confront the universality experientially rather than just intellectually.
- From here Joe moves to [[expert-is-imposter-with-most-reps]] -- the redefinition of expertise that completes the dissolution.
- Joe then names the disconcerting consequence: there is no perfect, no someone-who-knows, no right way. The ground falls out. See [[perceived-certainty-not-actual-certainty]].
- Joe then moves to [[only-your-own-judgment-matters]] -- if everyone is an imposter, the only judgment that ends up mattering is your own.

## Tone

Direct, matter-of-fact, almost playful. The kindergarten frame is delivered with humor. This is a principle that wants to land as obvious-once-said, not as profound. The lighter the delivery, the more the dissolution works.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not use this principle to dismiss the discomfort. The person is feeling something real. The principle reframes what the feeling means; it does not invalidate the feeling.
- Do not use this principle to make the person feel that their work is lesser. The point is the opposite: not-knowing is the marker of real work.
