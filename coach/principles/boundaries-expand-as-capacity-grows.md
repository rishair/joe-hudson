# Boundaries Refine as Your Capacity Grows

## The Principle

The boundary you need at the beginning is rarely the boundary you need later. The first boundary is the one that convinces you that you deserve to be treated well. As that conviction settles, the boundary becomes finer-grained. What was once a wall becomes a door with conditions. The conditions get more specific as the underlying self-worth deepens.

"Boundaries move. And each one of them allowed me to love him better." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

This is not a sign that the early boundary was wrong. The early boundary did its job by establishing the deserving-ness underneath. Without it, the later refinement would not be possible.

## The Mechanism

A new boundary, especially after a long history of not having one, has to do double duty:

1. It has to stop the behavior that is harming you.
2. It has to convince you that you deserve the stopping.

The second job is usually the harder one. The boundary is not just for the other person. It is for the part of yourself that does not yet believe it is okay for you to refuse what is happening.

"It was really important for me to have that really big strict boundary at the beginning of just, I'm not gonna be around you, because I needed to convince myself that it was okay for me not to be around that person." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

This is why minimal, "reasonable" first boundaries often fail. The reasonableness is the leftover doubt about deserving. The strict initial boundary is the structure that holds you while the doubt resolves.

## The Sequence

[[joes-dad-drinking-boundary]] is the canonical example. The boundary evolved across years:

1. **Stage 1: total separation.** "I'm not going to be around you." Joe needed to convince himself that he did not have to be around his father at all. The starkness was the proof of deserving.
2. **Stage 2: conditional separation.** "I'm not going to be around you when you're drinking." Once the deserving was internalized, the boundary could discriminate. The relationship could exist within new constraints.
3. **Stage 3: behavior-specific.** "I'm not going to be around you when you're being a prick because of the drinking." The drinking itself stopped being the issue. The actual behavior was.

Each stage was the right boundary for that stage of capacity. Stage 3 would have been impossible at Stage 1. Stage 1 would have been an overcorrection at Stage 3.

"With my dad, it was, I'm not gonna be around you. And then once I realized that, oh, I don't have to be around his drinking. But I miss my dad. I like to be around my dad. Okay, I'm gonna be around my dad, just not while he is drinking. Oh, I don't really mind that he is drinking as long as he's not being a prick. Okay. You know, but I had to learn that I could not be around his drinking. And that's why the boundary was such like a huge tool." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

## A Second Example: Trust as a Coaching Boundary

Joe describes the same arc in his coaching practice. Early on, he held himself responsible for clients who did not trust the process, and the dysfunction this produced led him to draw a boundary. The boundary then refined to the point that it gets drawn so early it rarely needs to be invoked.

"Earlier on when I was teaching and coaching, I felt like I held a little bit too much responsibility for other people. And so when people started to not trust the process, I would feel responsible to make sure that they were taken care of. I had to draw the boundary of like, oh, if you don't trust the process, then I'm not in the process with you anymore. I had to draw that boundary with several clients to get to the point where now that boundary gets drawn so early that it never seems to get to that point anymore. Where it's just so early. It's like, 'Oh, I see that there's not trust here, and I don't wanna work like that.'" -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

The mature boundary is so quick that it is almost invisible. The early version was loud and disruptive because it had to be.

## What This Implies for the Coaching Conversation

When someone is drawing a boundary for the first time around a long-standing pattern, the right move is to support the strict version, even when it looks excessive. The strictness is doing work that is not visible from outside.

The temptation is to coach toward nuance immediately: "Could you state it more compassionately? Could you leave a door open?" This is often a mistake. The starkness is what convinces the person they are allowed to have the boundary at all. Once that conviction is in place, the nuance arrives on its own.

The reverse-engineering does not work. You cannot start at Stage 3. Anyone who tries usually ends up at Stage 0, where the boundary never gets drawn because the "right" version is too sophisticated for someone who does not yet believe they deserve to draw any version.

## Connection to Self-Worth

The boundary is the external structure that establishes the internal worth. A person who has internalized their worth can draw delicate boundaries because the worth is not in question. A person who has not yet internalized it needs the strict boundary because the strict boundary is what teaches the worth.

This is also why first boundaries are so scary. They are tests of self-worth, conducted in front of someone whose response will be read as a verdict.

"That moment is really scary to actually draw it the first time because you're still not sure if you deserve that love. You don't know if the world's gonna provide for you because your whole life it hasn't. There is this great moment of fear when you're drawing especially important big boundaries, is like this big feeling of vulnerability because you're testing to see if the world is gonna work the way that your projections say it's gonna work." -- Joe Hudson, What's So Scary About Boundaries?

If the world responds well, the projection collapses and the model of reality has to be rebuilt. See [[boundaries]] for the "scary on both sides" dynamic.

## What This Is Not

This is not a license to soften every boundary over time. The principle is about the boundary becoming more precise, not weaker. Stage 3 in the dad story is not weaker than Stage 1. It is more discriminating. Joe could leave his father's presence in Stage 3 just as easily as in Stage 1; the difference is that he stayed when the conditions were tolerable.

This is also not a developmental hierarchy where Stage 3 is "better" than Stage 1. The right stage is the stage that matches present capacity. Trying to skip stages produces the same fragility that the earlier boundary was meant to dissolve.

This is also not a guarantee. Some boundaries stay strict forever. The embezzlement example: "I don't want to do business with anybody who stole from me." That boundary does not refine; it just is. The principle applies where the underlying relationship has the capacity to survive refinement.
