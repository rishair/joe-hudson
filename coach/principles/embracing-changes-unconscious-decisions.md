# Embracing Emotions Changes the Unconscious Decisions That Run the Day

## The Principle

The decisions a person consciously deliberates about ("should I take this job? should I leave this relationship?") are a small fraction of the decisions actually being made in a given day. The much larger volume is the unconscious choices that happen automatically: what to eat, when to scroll, what to say next, what to ignore, what to react to, when to leave the room.

Those automatic choices are controlled by emotion even more directly than the conscious ones. They are the unprocessed emotional state expressed as behavior, without an intervening deliberation step.

The leverage point: trying to change those automatic choices through willpower or rules fails almost universally. They change reliably only as a byproduct of feeling the emotions underneath. Once the underlying emotion is welcomed and felt, the automatic choices reorganize on their own.

"If you look at all those little choices that happen automatically that we don't think about, those are controlled even more by emotions than anything else. And so as you go through these big decisions and find these big emotions you don't want to feel, as you go through the judgment and find these emotions you don't want to feel and you start feeling them and loving them and accepting them, all those automatic decisions, all those 'oh, I'm going to just scroll Facebook for a bit,' or 'oh, I'm going to go take a walk,' or 'I'm going to eat here' -- all those little decisions change, which is super cool." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

## Why Willpower Fails on the Small Choices

The standard approach to changing automatic behavior is to apply willpower against it. "Every time I put on my shoes I will do it differently." "Every morning I will drink tea instead of coffee." These efforts produce short bursts of change followed by reversion. The pattern is so reliable it has a name: decision fatigue.

The structural problem with willpower-against-habit is that the willpower is operating one level above the level at which the habit is generated. The habit is the emotional state expressing itself through the path of least resistance. The willpower is trying to insert a deliberation step into a process that does not have one. The deliberation costs energy. The emotional state does not change. Eventually the energy runs out, and the path of least resistance reasserts.

"It's really hard to say change a habit and say, 'Okay, I'm going to remember every time I put on my shoes, I'm going to do it differently,' or every time I wake up, I'm going to drink tea instead of coffee. Those things can be really challenging. But what I notice is that when a certain emotional stuff gets loved and unhinged and it's not a hook anymore, some habits just change automatically. A lot of them change automatically." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

Brett's framing of the same mechanism: willpower-based behavior change requires constantly overriding the emotional system, which produces decision fatigue. Feeling the emotions changes the emotional background within which all decisions occur, which means the small choices reorganize themselves without further effort.

## The Pot Habit as the Worked Example

Joe's own pot habit in his twenties is the most-cited example of this principle in action. He had been beating himself up for smoking. The beating-up did nothing to change the habit; it intensified the use, because the shame about smoking was avoiding the same underlying emotions the pot was avoiding. Once he ran toward the underlying sadness and anger, the pot lost its appeal. No willpower. No plan. The choice "should I smoke" stopped arising because the emotional state that had been generating it had changed.

"A lot of the habits that we have are used to help us not feel something. And so if we -- bad, what we call bad habits. And so the more that you allow those emotional experiences, you learn to love them and you embrace them, then the habits are unnecessary." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

See [[joes-pot-habit]] for the full story and [[full-understanding-of-a-behavior-dissolves-it]] for the related mechanism (feeling the behavior in the act rather than feeling the underlying emotion).

## The Strategic Implication: Solve Through Feeling, Not Through Decision Architecture

The principle has a counterintuitive implication for anyone trying to change their life. The conscious decisions a person is wrestling with often matter less than they appear to. The question "should I leave this job or not" may be objectively much less important than the hundred unconscious choices each day about where to put attention, energy, and presence within whatever job is occurring.

"We get hung up on these three decisions, and we'll call those decisions ABC, but they might not even be decisions we should be making. We should be thinking completely about X, Y, and Z. X, Y, and Z is what's going to actually change our world. A, B, and C, doesn't matter what we decide, the chances of it changing our world is 2%, but X, Y, and Z decisions, those decisions might change our world 100%." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

The X, Y, and Z decisions -- the small, constant, automatic ones -- are where most of life's trajectory is determined. They cannot be controlled consciously. They change only when the emotional substrate changes. Which is to say: feeling the avoided emotions is what changes the part of life that conscious effort cannot reach.

This is why marriages, companies, and creative projects succeed or fail based on what looks like "where you're putting your time and energy" -- those are the X, Y, and Z decisions surfacing as observable patterns. The leader who is wrestling with the big strategic decisions while the small automatic ones are running on unprocessed emotion is solving the wrong problem.

## The Connection to Decision Fatigue

The cognitive science literature on decision fatigue describes the phenomenon at the willpower level: after a certain number of conscious decisions, decision quality degrades. The standard recommendations are to reduce the number of decisions through habits, defaults, and routines.

This principle reframes the same phenomenon. The fatigue is not from making decisions; it is from the constant overriding of the emotional system. A person operating from a clear emotional state can make hundreds of decisions a day without fatigue because the decisions are not requiring overrides. A person operating from suppressed emotion runs out of energy quickly because each decision is a small fight against the underlying state.

"You can do it for a while and then eventually you run out of dopamine or you run out of whatever willpower they take that because you're constantly overriding your emotional system. Whereas if you just go in and you feel the emotions, then you get to refactor that emotional background that creates this context for within which all of your decisions occur." -- Brett Kistler, Embracing Emotions: The Key to Great Decisions

Brett's word "refactor" is precise. The work is not at the level of individual decisions. It is at the level of the substrate from which decisions emerge. Refactoring the substrate changes what all the decisions look like, without having to author each one.

## What This Implies for Self-Assessment

When someone asks "how do I stop doing X" where X is a small automatic behavior, the question itself often misses the leverage point. The behavior is the visible end of an unfelt emotional process. The work is not "find a way to stop X" but "find what feeling is being avoided through X, and feel it."

The diagnostic question: "If I never did X again, what would I have to feel?" The answer is the work.

This is the same structure as [[shame-drives-behavior]] applied to behavior in general. Shame is one form of avoided feeling that holds behavior in place. Many other emotions can also hold behavior in place. The principle is the same: feel the held emotion, and the behavior loses its purpose.

## The Diagnostic for Whether This Is Operating

A person who reports "I keep deciding to X and then not doing it" or "I have a hundred different things I'm trying to change about my routine" is usually working at the level of conscious decisions while the underlying automatic decisions remain on emotional autopilot. The number of decisions being attempted is itself the diagnostic. The fix is not to be more disciplined about the decisions. The fix is to find what emotion is generating the autopilot and feel it. The conscious decisions then become much smaller in number and easier to execute, because the autopilot has begun to align with what is wanted rather than against it.

## Why This Is Better Framed as a Byproduct Than as a Goal

The principle has a paradoxical edge. If a person tries to feel their emotions in order to change their habits, they are back in the management trap (see [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]]). The motivation taints the practice. The emotions do not move because the system reads the strategy.

The habits-change-automatically benefit is real, but it cannot be the goal of the feeling work. It is a byproduct that arrives when the feeling work is done for its own sake. The instruction is to welcome the emotions because the emotions are yours, not because welcoming them will produce convenient behavior change. The behavior change shows up anyway, but only if the welcoming was not contingent on it.

## Tone

Joe presents this as a delighted side effect of the main work, not as a sales pitch for emotional inquiry. The tone is something like "and here's another cool thing that happens." The lightness matters because the moment the principle gets weaponized into "feel your emotions so you can stop doing X" it stops working.
