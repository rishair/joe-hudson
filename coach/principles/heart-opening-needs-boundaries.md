# Heart Opening Needs Boundaries (Or You Leave Yourself Out of the Opening)

## The Principle

A heart-opening that does not also include strong boundaries leaves the self out of the opening. The person ends up loving everyone and everything except themselves. They can feel the care behind every behavior, the wisdom in every situation, the love available in every moment -- and the absence of boundaries means they extend that love to relationships and situations that are not safe for them. The heart-opening becomes a form of self-abandonment.

The corrective is not to close the heart. It is to discover that boundaries are part of the architecture that makes deep heart-opening sustainable. Boundaries are not opposed to open-hearted love. They are what allows the love to include the person who is loving.

"Those heart openings, your boundaries are there to allow the heart opening, not have the heart opening so you don't need any boundaries." -- Tara Howley, Marriage, Love, and Death

## Why This Failure Mode Is Common

The first sustained taste of open-heart living often produces a felt sense of unity with everything. The person can see, in this state, that everyone is doing their best, that every behavior comes from love (however twisted), that every conflict is a misunderstanding to be met with care. The conclusion -- understandable from inside the state -- is that boundaries are no longer needed. Boundaries belonged to the old defended self. The new open-hearted self can simply love its way through anything.

This conclusion is wrong, but the wrongness is not visible from inside the state. The person learns the wrongness through living it. Boundaries do not get tested by the abstraction "should I have boundaries." They get tested by specific situations where the heart-opening keeps them in contact with what is harming them.

Tara's report of her own version of this:

"One of my biggest learnings was after experiencing sort of a heart opening, was I was just in love with everything. And so that I could see the, I could see what everything was coming from or the care behind it. And I could feel the love for it and from it, even if it was kind of twisted. And I didn't have boundaries. So I had this huge heart opening without access to my own boundaries, which was a huge hole for me. With Joe and everybody else not having boundaries. You know, those heart openings, your boundaries are there to allow the heart opening, not have the heart opening so you don't need any boundaries. So that was like, I would say that was a good year or two of my life of having to find the, the boundaries so that I was included in the heart opening." -- Tara Howley, Marriage, Love, and Death

The phrase "so that I was included in the heart opening" carries the principle. The heart opening was real. It just did not include her. The work was not to close it. The work was to find the boundaries that brought her inside it.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A person who has done significant inner work and reports that they no longer get triggered, no longer have conflict, can love anyone -- and is exhausted, depleted, has stopped saying what they want, has stopped attending to their own needs. The heart-opening is producing a slow erosion of the self.

A person who returns from a transformative retreat and finds that they cannot say no to anyone. The love they feel for everyone makes every request feel reasonable. They take on more than they can hold and resent it underneath the loving response.

A person who, after a heart opening, "couldn't" leave a relationship that they would clearly need to leave from a more boundaried state. The love they feel for the partner reads as evidence that the relationship should work, even when the relationship is consistently violating their needs.

A spiritual teacher or facilitator who, after their own awakening, cannot draw boundaries with students. The love feels like it should be unbounded. The unboundedness produces dysfunction in the teacher's life and dependency in the students.

## What "Boundaries That Include You" Look Like

A boundary that includes you is one that names what you will do, drawn from the same love that opened the heart, not from the defended pre-opening self.

"I love you and I can't be around you while you're doing this. I'll come back when you stop."
"I love you and I won't take on this project. I have capacity for X but not Y."
"I love you and the way I will receive this conversation is on these terms."

The love is real. The boundary is also real. They are not in conflict. The boundary is what allows the love to remain love rather than degrade into resentment or self-erasure.

## The Two-Person Version: Boundaries in Sustained Partnership

The principle applies acutely in partnership. A person who has had a heart opening and is in love with their partner can lose the capacity to draw boundaries with them. The love floods the relationship and overwrites the need for the person to remain a self inside the relationship.

This is one of the failure modes of "we're so in love" early-relationship dynamics. The love is real. The self-collapse it enables is destructive. The boundaries that re-include the person in the relationship feel, at first, like a betrayal of the love. Over time they reveal themselves as what makes the love sustainable.

Tara names this as part of her own marriage learning:

"That was definitely hard in our marriage 'cause certain things had to change. And I had, and Joe had to watch me really struggle to find and hold, not say them, but hold them. That was the part. I could name a boundary, but holding it was a very different action." -- Tara Howley, Marriage, Love, and Death

The struggle of holding the boundary is itself part of the work. Naming the boundary is the easy half. Holding it -- staying with the boundary even when the open heart wants to dissolve it again -- is the harder half. This holding is often what produces the second-level heart opening, the one that includes the person doing the opening.

## Connection to the Projection-Work Failure Mode

Tara names a parallel failure mode in projection work: discovering that you are projecting and concluding that therefore you have no boundaries, because the boundary is "just your projection."

"In the projection work, I'd be like, oh, I'm projecting. And then I assumed it was my responsibility to take care of everything. Oh, if I'm projecting that this person is crossing my boundaries, then I'm crossing my own boundaries. And that's about me. And I kind of could go into spin on the projection work as opposed to like, oh, and I still get my boundaries... Like it is about me and I still get to name what I want. I still get to say I don't want that and I can't be around you if this." -- Tara Howley, Marriage, Love, and Death

Same structural shape. A valuable inner-work insight (projection, heart-opening, awakening) gets misapplied as a reason to abandon boundaries. The correction in each case is the same: the insight does not replace your boundaries. The insight refines the form your boundaries take. You still get your boundaries. You also see the projection. You also feel the open heart. The "and" is what was missing.

## When This Principle Comes Up

- A person reports a transformative experience and has subsequently lost the ability to say no.
- A person reports being in love with everyone and is exhausted.
- A person is staying in a relationship that is harming them because they "see the love behind" the harmful behavior.
- A person has been through significant spiritual or therapeutic work and reports that they no longer have conflict -- and is bleeding capacity.
- A person dismisses boundaries as "the old defended self" and is being walked over.
- A teacher or facilitator cannot maintain boundaries with their students or clients.
- A person who has done projection work has concluded they have no right to boundaries.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- After a heart-opening practice, retreat, or breakthrough.
- After the discovery of projections has become routine.
- When a person has been doing inner work for years and reports increasing depletion alongside increasing equanimity.
- When the report is "I love everyone" paired with "I'm exhausted."

## Tone

Plain, slightly rueful. The principle is most often stated by someone who learned it the hard way. The teaching carries the weight of having spent a year or two without the boundaries before finding them. The tone is not "you should have boundaries." The tone is "the heart opening will eventually require you to find them, and the finding is its own work."
