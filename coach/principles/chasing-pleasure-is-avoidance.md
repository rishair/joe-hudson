# Chasing Pleasure Is Itself the Avoidance of Pleasure

## The Principle

Pleasure that has to be chased is not the pleasure the practice is pointing at. If pleasure is something the system has to reach for, the system is in chase-mode, which is the same nervous-system configuration as anxiety. The reaching enacts the assumption that pleasure is not currently here, which is itself the resistance that keeps it from arriving. The pleasure that produces the felt sense of safety is the pleasure that does not require chasing.

"If there's any kind of hunger in getting the pleasure, then that usually is a clear sign that it's become either avoidant or you're pushing the pleasure away." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The principle is structurally identical to [[opposite-of-trying]] applied to pleasure specifically. Trying-for-pleasure is the configuration that prevents pleasure. Chasing-for-pleasure is the more energetic version of the same configuration. The chase is the avoidance dressed up as the pursuit.

## Why the Chase Disables the Felt Safety

The mechanism is precise. Pleasure produces felt safety because pleasure is the body's signal that everything is okay right now. The signal can land only when the body is not in get-mode. Get-mode requires something not yet here. The body cannot simultaneously be in "this is the way it is" (which is what makes the pleasure safe) and "I need to get to where pleasure is" (which is what the chase requires).

"It's not safe if you have to go find it, right? Safety is like, oh, this is the way it is. That's what creates the safety, not like, oh, if I once I do this, then I'll get — you know, that is the anxiety." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The pleasure may still register as a bodily sensation when the chase succeeds. What does not register is the deeper signal: the sense that the system is okay. That deeper signal requires the chase to be absent. As long as the chase is present, the pleasure is a transient body-state with the anxiety of pursuit underneath it. The transient body-state is what most people are calling "pleasure" — and it is why most people's relationship with pleasure does not produce the structural changes the practice is supposed to produce.

## The Two Configurations Side By Side

The same person can experience the same sensation in either configuration. The configuration determines the meaning.

**Chase configuration:** "Once I do this, then I'll get the pleasure." The pleasure is at the end of the action. The action is in service of arrival. The body is in produce-the-conditions mode. Even when the conditions arrive and the sensation lands, the body's underlying setting is "make the conditions arrive again." The whole posture is forward-leaning.

**Notice configuration:** "Oh, the pleasure is here, in what is already happening." The pleasure is in the current sensation. No action is required to produce it. The body is in noticing mode. The underlying setting is "this is how it is." The posture is unleaned.

The first is what most pleasure-seeking is. The second is what the practice is pointing at. They use the same word for entirely different operations.

## The Heroin Analogy

Joe makes the structural point with an extreme case. If pleasure were what you got from doing X, Y, and Z, you would do X, Y, and Z all the time — which is the heroin configuration. You cannot do X, Y, and Z all the time. The conditions cannot be sustained. So a pleasure-practice that is structured around chasing is structurally guaranteed to fail.

"If you're really chasing pleasure, it can't be something that you get when you do X, Y, and Z, because you can't do X, Y, and Z all the time. If you're really chasing pleasure, it has to be able to be extracted from any given moment." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The reasoning is from the requirements of sustainability. A pleasure that requires conditions cannot be sustained. A pleasure that does not require conditions can be. Therefore the only pleasure-practice that survives the constraint of being sustainable is the one that operates on what is here. The chase, no matter how successful in the short term, runs out of fuel as soon as the conditions stop being available.

## The Tell: Hunger in the Pleasure-Getting

The diagnostic Joe offers for whether a person has slipped from notice-configuration into chase-configuration: is there hunger in the pleasure-getting?

Hunger looks like:
- Reaching for the pleasure as if its absence were urgent
- Defining oneself by how much pleasure one is feeling
- A felt sense of needing the next hit of it
- The pleasure-getting taking on the quality of acquisition rather than reception
- Anxiety in the body even as the pleasure-sensation is occurring
- The orientation of "more, more, more" rather than "here, here, here"

When any of these are present, the practice has slipped. The pleasure may still be felt, but the deeper signal it would otherwise produce — the felt safety — is not arriving, because the system underneath is still in the get-mode that disables it.

## The Push-Away Variant

A second failure mode worth distinguishing: the system can also push the pleasure away. This looks different from the chase but operates through the same underlying mechanism — the body is in some configuration other than the one that would let the pleasure land. The chase reaches outward; the push-away resists inward. Both prevent the felt sense that pleasure produces.

"Pushing the pleasure away kind of pushes the pleasure away. Like you might get the bodily sensation of pleasure a little bit, but you're not getting that felt sense of safety that comes with pleasure because it has to be chased." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The push-away is the conservative half of the failure. The chase is the active half. The corrective for both is the same: drop the maneuver, notice what is actually here.

## The Practice Implication

The principle gives a specific test for whether a pleasure-practice is going in the right direction. If the practice produces more chasing, more hunger, more reaching, it is operating in the failure mode. If the practice produces more noticing, more this-is-the-way-it-is, more felt safety, it is operating in the working mode.

The first sign of the working mode is often a small, undramatic ease. The chase mode produces high-intensity pleasure events with anxiety underneath. The notice mode produces low-intensity pleasure that arrives more often with safety underneath. The latter is what the practice is reaching for, even though the former is what the chase-trained system expects pleasure to look like.

This is also why the practice is so often discovered through small, mundane objects — the breath, the dishes, the flower. The mundane objects do not feed the chase. They are too small to be worth chasing. So the practice can land without the chase apparatus interfering. Once the practice has landed in the small objects, it can be extended to larger ones without the chase reactivating. See [[joe-thich-nhat-hanh-dishes]] for the origin story and [[notice-pleasure-and-sensation-as-grounding]] for the formal practice.

## Tone

Direct. Joe is not warning against pleasure-chasing as morally wrong. He is naming it as structurally self-defeating. The chase does not produce what the chaser thinks it produces. The principle is offered as information about how the system works, not as a prohibition.
