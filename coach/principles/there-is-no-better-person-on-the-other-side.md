# There Is No Better Person on the Other Side (The "You" Being Measured Fades)

## The Principle

The work does not produce a better version of the person. It does not produce an upgraded model of the self. The far side of the path is not the original person plus some improvements bolted on.

Two things happen instead. First, the better-or-worse frame itself dissolves. There is no longer a yardstick along which someone is higher or lower. Second, the very self that was supposed to be the subject of the improvement begins to fade. The measurer thins out alongside the measurement.

"You're not going to be a better person on the other side. Actually, on the other side, the idea of better or worse goes away, and the idea of like a you that's there to be measured starts to fade away as well." -- Joe Hudson, How To Measure Your Progress In Personal Development & Transformation

## The Reasoning

Most personal development is built on a hidden frame: there is a present self that is deficient, and there is a future self that will be improved, and the work moves the deficient self toward the improved self. The entire structure presupposes a fixed, continuous "you" that gets carried forward and made better over time.

The actual structure is different. The work, when it goes far enough, dissolves the structure itself.

- The fluidity of [[emotional-fluidity]] dissolves the predictability of reaction that identity is built on.
- The expansion of [[expanding-sense-of-self]] dissolves the boundary between self and not-self.
- The expansion of [[sense-of-time-expansion]] dissolves the past-future axis that the continuous self lives along.
- The settling of [[pleasure-capacity]] dissolves the threat-vigilance that holds the protective self in place.

When the four things being measured have all moved far enough, the entity that was doing the measuring has thinned out to the point where the comparison-frame "better than I was" stops making sense. There is no fixed self to compare to a previous fixed self. The frame itself is gone.

This is why "better" stops applying. Better requires a fixed you and a yardstick. The transformation dissolves the fixed you.

## Why This Is a Guardrail, Not a Goal

Read as a goal, this principle becomes another improvement project ("I will dissolve my self faster than other people dissolve theirs"). The improvement frame is endlessly creative; it can recruit even self-dissolution into its own project. See [[tools-to-change-not-love]].

Read as a guardrail, this principle does its actual work: it removes the basis for self-attack around progress. If there is no "better person" being produced, then the measurement cannot be used to flagellate the current person for not being far enough along. There is nothing far enough along to be.

"It's really important not to beat yourself up through the measurement process. The measurement process is more of a map. There is no rush to get anywhere." -- Joe Hudson, How To Measure Your Progress In Personal Development & Transformation

The "no rush" line is structurally tied to the "no better person" line. Together they pull the foundation out from under the entire weaponize-the-measurement dynamic. See [[dont-use-measurement-to-beat-yourself-up]].

## Distinguishing From "Don't Try"

This principle is not "don't do the work" or "the work is pointless." The work is real and the markers move. People become more emotionally fluid, their sense of self does expand, their pleasure capacity does increase, their time horizon does shift.

What is not real is the picture of these changes accruing to a fixed "you" who is becoming the improved version. The changes happen. The "you" they were supposed to accrue to thins out as they happen. By the time the changes are substantial, the entity that was tracking them has loosened enough that the "I got better" narrative no longer fits the felt experience.

The accurate description from the inside is closer to: "things happened, and the self that was watching them happen is less defined than it used to be, and the question 'is this better' has stopped feeling like a real question."

This is structurally identical to the Buddhist observation that the practitioner who finally arrives is no longer the practitioner who started. The continuity of the person is more porous than the improvement narrative assumes.

## The Parallel With Wants

The same dynamic shows up at the level of wants. People assume their goals will hold steady through the journey. They never do.

"I've seen a lot of things not change as people go through this journey, and a lot of things change, but I've never seen the goals of a person not change through the journey. That always changes." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement (quoted in [[improvement-vs-authenticity]])

If goals always change, then the person trying to become the version of themselves who has achieved their current goals is trying to become someone the future them will not want to be. The goal-haver dissolves. The wants refine into different wants. By the time the original target would have been reached, the person aimed at the target no longer exists in the same configuration. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]].

The principle here is the same one read from the wants axis instead of the identity axis. The you that wanted X is not the you that arrives at X. The same dissolution that produces "no better person" produces "no preserved goal." Both are the natural consequence of letting the structure of the fixed self loosen.

## What This Means for the Coach

A coach holding this principle will not pitch transformation as becoming a better version of yourself. The pitch is dishonest on two counts: it preserves the improvement frame that the work dissolves, and it sets up a comparison structure ("better than now") that becomes the basis for self-attack later.

The honest framing is closer to: the work changes the person in ways the person cannot predict from where they currently are; the changes are not improvements on the current configuration so much as the dissolving of the configuration itself; the "you" reading this will be less recognizable on the other side, not because you have become someone better but because the very fixity of being someone has softened.

This framing tends to make the work less attractive to people who are looking for an improvement project, and more attractive to people who are actually ready to let the project go. The first group will continue to be attracted to coaching that promises the improved self; that coaching exists in abundance elsewhere.

## What This Means for the Person Doing the Work

The practical implication: stop trying to measure yourself against a future improved self. The future improved self is a fiction. The actual transformation is moving sideways into a different relationship with being a self, not vertically up a ladder of selves.

The measurement frame in [[measuring-transformation-across-three-brains]] remains useful for orientation. It is not useful as a vertical-progress meter. Held lightly, the three markers show where the system has been moving. Held tightly, they become the basis for the same self-rejection that the work is supposed to address. The light hold is the right hold.

The relief, once this lands, is substantial. The hidden weight of having to become someone is what most personal development carries. Putting that weight down — the recognition that there is no someone to become — is itself one of the markers of the work having gone somewhere.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person who has done years of work and is finally finding it strange that the question "have I made progress?" no longer makes the same sense it used to.
- A practitioner who notices that the future self they were trying to become no longer feels like the actual destination of the work.
- Someone whose measurement habit has produced a lot of self-attack and is ready for a different relationship to the markers.
- A person who has heard "love yourself as you are" and is finally able to feel the difference between that and "improve yourself so you can love yourself."

## Tone

Closing-statement tone. Joe delivers this at the end of a teaching, after laying out the framework, as the recontextualization that makes the framework safe to use. The tone is steady, slightly slower, with no salesmanship. The point is not to make the teaching sound profound but to remove the basis for misusing it.
