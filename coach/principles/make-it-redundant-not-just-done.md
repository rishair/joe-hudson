# Make Things Redundant, Not Done

## The Principle

The default question about a to-do list is "how do I get this done?" That question has a ceiling. Whatever speed you can do the tasks at, the list will refill at a rate that matches your speed. The faster you go, the faster the list grows.

The higher-leverage question is "which three things on this list, if done, would make everything else easier or redundant?" That question changes the relationship to the list itself. Some tasks are not meant to be done. They are meant to be made unnecessary. The work is to find those.

"My job wasn't to get things done. My job was to not do things and get them done. Or make them irrelevant." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The principle holds in personal work, team work, and company strategy. The highest-impact work is almost always work that removes the need for other work, not work that completes other work.

## The Origin Move

Joe's account of how he discovered this:

"There was this same timeframe where I would look at my to-do list, and it would go from 'how do I get this done?' to 'what are the three things that I can do on this list that'll make everything else easier, redundant?' And that started becoming a practice where my job wasn't to get things done. My job was to not do things and get them done." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The shift is mechanical. Same list. Different question. The new question pre-filters the list for leverage rather than urgency. The downstream effect is that the work being done is the work that disappears the most other work.

## The VC Lesson: Great Ideas Over Hard Work

The same principle generalizes to leadership work, which Joe articulates as a teaching he repeated to CEOs in his VC years.

"I used to say this to CEOs all the time when I was a venture capitalist. Your job isn't to work hard. Your job is to have great ideas. If you have one great idea, it could save this company two years, three years, four years. The person who started the local tire shop and the person who started Google probably worked about the same amount of hours." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The comparison is sharp. Two founders, similar hours, vastly different outcomes. The difference is not in the labor. It is in what the labor was pointed at. The founder whose ideas removed years of work from the company arrived somewhere the other one could not get to with any amount of additional labor.

The implication for any CEO, founder, or leader: optimizing for harder work optimizes for the wrong variable. The variable that actually moves the outcome is idea-quality. The leader's primary job is to think well, not to grind. The grinding is the comfort behavior. The thinking is the leverage.

## What This Looks Like at the Personal Level

For an individual's to-do list:

- Before doing any task, scan the list for tasks that would eliminate other tasks. If "build automation X" would remove five recurring tasks, do that before any of the five.
- For each recurring task, ask whether the recurrence itself is necessary. Often it is not. Often it is a habit that someone could break.
- For each "I should do this" item, ask what it is protecting against. Often the answer reveals that the task is not actually necessary, just defensive.
- For each external request, ask whether saying "no" or "not this way" would make the request itself disappear. Often it would.

The cumulative effect: a to-do list that shrinks faster than it grows, because the work being done is the work that reduces future work.

## What This Looks Like at the Team Level

For a team or company:

- The product feature that obsoletes ten support tickets is higher-leverage than ten new features.
- The process change that removes a meeting is higher-leverage than optimizing the meeting.
- The architecture decision that makes a class of bugs impossible is higher-leverage than fixing the next bug.
- The hire who can make decisions independently removes the bottleneck the team has been routing through.
- The decision to stop doing a product line frees more capacity than any amount of additional engineering on it.

In each case, the visible work is "doing something." The actual work is removing the need for other work. The visible work and the actual work look the same in the moment. They diverge dramatically over months and years.

## Why This Is Hard to See

Three reasons the redundancy frame is hard to find:

**1. The dopamine of completion.** Crossing items off a list produces a felt sense of progress. See [[productivity-as-dopamine-fix]]. The dopamine comes from completion, not from leverage. Doing five medium tasks produces five hits. Doing one high-leverage task produces one hit, no matter how much downstream work it eliminated. The system optimizes for hits, not for leverage.

**2. Urgency presses harder than importance.** Urgent tasks announce themselves. Important tasks (the ones that would make other tasks redundant) usually do not. The leader who responds to whatever is loudest will spend their time on urgent-but-low-leverage work. The work that removes future work tends to be quiet today.

**3. Redundancy work looks like avoidance.** The person who spends a day designing a system instead of grinding through tickets looks like they are avoiding the tickets. To an outside observer (or to their own inner critic), the day looks unproductive. The downstream measurement -- a hundred fewer tickets next month -- is invisible at the moment the work is happening.

## The Uncomfortable-Task Corollary

The redundancy move pairs with another principle Joe surfaces in the same conversation: the most productive thing to do is often the most uncomfortable thing.

"There was a recognition that one of the most productive things I could do is do the most uncomfortable thing. That there was a moment of seeing that the thing I was most likely not going to do was the thing I didn't know how to do, and it was usually the most important thing to do if I was creating a business." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The reason: the redundancy-creating work is often the work the person does not know how to do. If they knew how to do it, they would have done it already. The not-knowing-how is what kept the redundancy from happening. The willingness to do the thing you do not know how to do is what opens the leverage. See [[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]] for the general principle this is a productivity-specific application of.

The pattern: the items on the to-do list you have been avoiding are often the items that would, if done, eliminate the rest of the list. The avoidance is correlated with the leverage. Pull on the avoided one and the list collapses.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They are working at maximum speed and the to-do list is not shrinking.
- They cross five things off the list and three new ones appear.
- They notice that every week is the same shape regardless of how much they did.
- They suspect that their effort is going to maintenance, not to progress.
- They feel the dread of opening their task manager because nothing they could do there would actually change anything.
- They notice an avoided item on the list has been there for months and the items they keep doing have not changed.

## In Practice

The practice is one substitution.

The default question: "How do I get this done?"
The replacement question: "Which of these would, if done, make the others easier or redundant?"

Ask the replacement question before starting any session of work. Apply it to the list. Identify the two or three highest-leverage items. Do those. Let the others wait.

For most people, this practice immediately exposes that the items they have been doing are not on the high-leverage list. The work has been busy work. The actual leverage work has been waiting. The practice surfaces it.

For teams, the same practice becomes a team-level question: "If we did these three things this quarter, what work would not need to happen?" Done well, the answer reshapes the roadmap. Done poorly, the answer is just a justification for whatever the team was going to do anyway.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't use the redundancy frame to procrastinate on tasks that just need doing.** Some work is just work. The frame is meant to elevate leverage work, not to provide cover for avoiding ordinary work. If everything on the list could be "made redundant," nothing will get done.

- **Don't optimize for cleverness over execution.** The redundancy move rewards finding high-leverage tasks. It does not reward inventing high-leverage tasks that do not actually exist. The discipline is to honestly assess whether a task would actually reduce future work, not to perform the appearance of strategic thinking.

- **Don't confuse "great ideas" with "ideas I'm excited about."** Great ideas are ones that, if executed, would change the trajectory. Many exciting ideas would not change anything. The test is downstream impact, not present-moment enthusiasm.
