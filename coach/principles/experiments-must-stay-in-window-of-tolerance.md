# Experiments Must Stay Inside the Window of Tolerance

## The Principle

When an experiment moves into territory that triggers significant fear, the person stops running the experiment and starts running the old protective patterns. The experimental mindset (playful, iterative, willing to be wrong) cannot coexist with the fear-management mindset (urgent, controlling, scanning for threat). The same nominal experiment, attempted inside fear, is no longer an experiment.

The implication: design experiments to stay inside the window of tolerance. If the experiment needs to touch a high-stakes domain, run it on a lower-stakes corner of that domain. Do not try to run the experiment in the middle of the threat.

"You lose the mindset. So on the things that are really important, you do what you know, that you know will work, or you find experts who know what will work and you do it. But in the things that you don't know or the places where you're curious, you figure out that little experiment you can run on the side to learn about things." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## Why Fear Destroys the Experiment

The experimental mindset requires:
- Willingness to be wrong
- Curiosity about what will happen
- Playfulness with structure
- Detachment from outcome

Fear produces the opposite of each of these:
- Need to be right
- Certainty about what will happen
- Rigidity with structure
- Attachment to outcome

The two states cannot occupy the same person at the same time. When fear arrives, the experimental mindset leaves. The person may still be performing the actions of the experiment, but they are now performing them in fear-management mode rather than in iteration mode. The data produced is unreliable. The learning does not happen.

"In the things that are really important... not run experiments where there's a lot of fear because they usually don't go well." -- Joe Hudson

## The Brett Articulation

Brett names the structural form during the conversation: "You stop running the experimental mindset. So the same experiment then becomes not the experimental."

Joe confirms: "You lose the mindset."

This is the precise mechanism. The experiment is defined by the mindset, not by the activity. Walking up to a person you find attractive and asking a question can be an experiment (curious, iterative, willing to be wrong) or a high-stakes performance (scanning for rejection, attached to outcome, rigid in execution). The action is the same. The mindset is opposite. Only one of the two is an experiment.

## The Operational Move

Three parts:

**Part 1: For the high-stakes domain, do what you know.** Use the proven approach. Do not iterate here. The cost of getting it wrong is too high to sustain the experimental mindset, and without that mindset, the iteration produces no learning.

**Part 2: For the unknown adjacent to the high-stakes domain, run small experiments.** The unknown is where the learning is. The small-and-adjacent design is what keeps the experiment inside the window of tolerance. See [[mit-media-lab-principle]].

**Part 3: Move the learning from the experiments into the high-stakes domain when the learning is stable.** The experiments are how the unknown converts to known. Once it is known, deploy it in the high-stakes domain using the "do what you know" approach.

## What Joe Names About His Own Business

Joe tells the same principle from his own practice: he runs experiments that cost the business significant money, but the experiments are designed to keep the experimenting itself enjoyable and to keep him in connection with the pull. The cost is real. The fear is managed. The experimentation is preserved.

"I'll run experiments in our business that cost us a shit ton of money, and I'd far rather do that than try to get it right. I'd rather feel in connection with like the pull and the migratory path and each other and where our wonder is than I would in trying to get something perfect. And it works really, really well in our business and it, and it has worked really, really well in businesses like early Google or early Microsoft." -- Joe Hudson

The contrast Joe is drawing: a business that tries to get it right at every turn cannot sustain the experimental mindset because the cost of being wrong is felt as catastrophe. A business that has internalized the iterative mindset can run expensive experiments without the fear contaminating them, because the expense is treated as the cost of learning, not as the failure to be perfect.

This is also how Microsoft and Google operated in their early periods. The companies ran many experiments that failed. Billions of dollars of failed experiments. The willingness to lose the money was what kept the experimentation alive. The companies that try to win every bet do not generate the variation that the winning bets emerge from.

## The Urgency Trap

Joe notes that the felt urgency in high-stakes situations is almost always larger than the actual urgency. The fear inflates the size of the decision in real time. The person feels like every choice is permanent and consequential. The fear is real. The permanence usually is not.

"That sense of urgency is far greater than it actually is." -- Joe Hudson

When urgency is high, the experimenter has to either run the experiment somewhere else (per the main principle) or first work to bring the felt urgency back into proportion. The experiment in inflated urgency is not running.

## The Window Itself

The window of tolerance is the range of activation in which the person can stay present, curious, and adaptive. Outside the window (too activated above, too shut down below), the person reverts to default patterns. The same experiment, attempted inside the window, produces learning. Attempted outside it, produces re-traumatization or shutdown.

Experiments are designed at the edge of the window, not outside it. The edge produces growth (the window expands). Beyond the edge produces collapse (the window contracts). The skilled experimenter feels the edge and stays there.

The chicken-and-eggy version: developing experimental capacity in low-stakes domains is how the window expands enough to eventually run experiments in higher-stakes domains. The capacity has to be built. You cannot jump straight to experimenting in the highest-stakes part of your life. You have to develop the trust in the process by running smaller experiments first, then medium ones, then the bigger ones.

"I've seen myself now run 100 experiments and I have a deeper sense of knowing if I'm... following my migratory path or where I'm jumping out of the discomfort and how big my window of tolerance can become and where my edge is, where my playfulness becomes self-protection and the experiment falls apart. And then with that kind of meta-awareness, but also an emotional kind of grounding in it, then you can expand. And trust experimentation, trust that process in more and more high-stakes areas of your life." -- Brett Kistler, describing the developmental arc Joe is teaching

Joe confirms: the trust is the result of the practice. It is not pre-given. The 100 experiments produce the trust that lets the 101st experiment happen in a higher-stakes domain than would have been possible without the prior 100.

## The Performance/Play Echo

This principle is the operational form of [[performance-vs-play]] for experimentation. Play is where iteration happens. Performance is where what was learned in play gets deployed. The mistake is treating every domain as performance, where every move has to be right. The corrective is finding the play zone inside the performance domain, running the experiments there, and bringing the learning back to performance.

## Tone

Joe delivers this practically, almost as a piece of operational advice. The principle is not romantic about experimentation. It does not say "experiment everywhere always." It says experimentation has a specific operating range and the skilled experimenter respects that range. The energy is mature -- the energy of someone who has run experiments badly enough times to know the cost of running them in the wrong place.
