# Caretaking Prevents the Bottom That Healing Requires

## The Principle

Caretaking does not prevent the worst outcome the caretaker fears. It usually produces it. The bottom that recovery requires is exactly what the caretaking is constructed to prevent. By preventing the bottom, the caretaking prevents the recovery.

"Most of the time, the codependence is just making it so that the person doesn't hit their bottom. And so you're actually hurting them. You're not giving them, you're again trying to avoid somebody from feeling pain so that you don't have to feel the loss of your loved one." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

## The Evidence

Joe cites a specific pattern as evidence: the alcoholics who have the hardest time breaking the addiction are independently wealthy alcoholics with no one depending on them.

"There's evidence of this where the alcoholics who have the hardest time to break alcoholism are the people who have independent wealth and they have nobody particularly depending on them. So they have no bottom to hit to get out of it." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The structure: recovery typically requires the consequences of the behavior to become unbearable. The wealthy alcoholic never faces unbearable consequences because money insulates them. The caretaken alcoholic never faces unbearable consequences because the caretaker absorbs them.

In both cases, the conditions for recovery are absent. The wealth and the caretaking function identically: as insulation from the natural feedback the behavior would otherwise produce.

The whole Al-Anon tradition exists to teach this. "There are whole rooms of Al-Anon built to teach those living with addicts how to take care of themselves and not take care of the addict."

## The Generalization Beyond Addiction

The principle generalizes beyond addiction to any pattern that produces bad outcomes for the person stuck in it.

The angry husband who is consistently managed by the wife learns that anger produces compliance. The caretaking trains the behavior. Without the caretaking, the anger would face friction. The friction would eventually produce change.

"If I get angry, you will stop being yourself and you will try to manage my experience, which gives me all the power in the relationship." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The underperforming employee whose mistakes are covered by colleagues never gets the feedback that produces growth. The caretaking colleagues prevent the bottom that the underperformer needs.

The adult child who never has to be financially responsible because parents always step in never develops financial responsibility. The caretaking parents prevent the bottom that would teach the skill.

In every case, the caretaker is intervening before the natural consequence arrives. The natural consequence is the teacher. Removing the teacher prevents the learning.

## The Reframe Question

When someone insists they have to caretake because the alternative is unbearable consequence (death, harm, collapse), Joe asks a structural question:

"What makes you think that they're going to not die of a drug overdose or not go homeless if you're taking care of them? Like, how does that work in your brain? Do you think that every single person who has a drug overdose is because their brother/sister/mother/father stopped caring for them? Do you think that every one of them that's homeless is because they stopped caring for them?" -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The question forces examination of the implicit assumption: that caretaking is what is preventing the worst outcome. The evidence does not support the assumption. Most overdose deaths occur in families with caretakers. Most homeless people have family members who at some point tried to help. The caretaking does not prevent the outcome.

What the caretaking does is prevent the bottom that might have prevented the outcome.

## The Grief Layer Underneath

The hardest case Joe addresses is the one where the feared outcome is death. The caretaker's terror is not abstract. They believe stopping the caretaking will kill the person they love.

The principle does not deny that this could happen. It might. The instruction Joe gives is to feel the grief in advance.

"If you're being honest with yourself, feel the loss. Feel the loss before you even make your decision. I'm not saying stop or start, just feel the loss. Like, oh yeah, if I stop this, they're gonna die. Feel your person's death, mourn it, go all the way through it so that you're making decisions that are based on what's best for them, not because you are trying to avoid the grief." -- Joe Hudson, Care over Caretaking

The function: the caretaking is largely grief avoidance. Once the grief is felt, the caretaking loses its motive force. The decision can then be made from clarity about what is actually best for the other, rather than from the unfelt grief.

See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] for the broader practice. The pre-feel collapses the felt unbearability of the consequence and clears the path for actual choice.

## The Trap of Continued Caretaking

The caretaker's logic: "If I stop, they will collapse. If they collapse, I will not be able to bear it. Therefore I must continue."

The structure of the logic: present pain (continued caretaking) is preferred to feared future pain (their collapse). The future pain is treated as unbearable because it has not been felt. Everything is organized around preventing the unfelt feeling from arriving.

The cost: the present pain is real and continuous. The dynamic that produces the feared collapse is reinforced. The future is being purchased with the present.

The principle says: the feared future is more likely to arrive because of the caretaking, not less. The trade is bad on its own terms.

## What This Does Not Mean

The principle does not prescribe abandonment. It does not say "stop caring." It says "stop caretaking." The distinction matters.

A clean version of care for an addict family member: continue to love them, continue to be in relationship with them, stop financing the addiction, stop covering the consequences. Be present for them. Do not be the buffer between them and their life.

See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for what this looks like in practice. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] for how to draw the boundaries that distinguish care from caretaking.

The principle also does not prescribe a particular timeline. Some people, given the bottom they need, recover. Some do not. The principle is not a guarantee. It is a structural observation that caretaking blocks recovery, not that stopping caretaking guarantees recovery.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

When a person says "but if I don't take care of them, they will [die / collapse / fail]."

When a person describes years of escalating caretaking with no improvement in the other's condition.

When a person uses the other's neediness as justification for ongoing self-sacrifice.

When the caretaking is being framed as morally required.

The principle delivered with care can produce a substantial reframe. The person has been operating under the assumption that the caretaking is helping. Naming that it is likely hurting changes the entire calculus.
