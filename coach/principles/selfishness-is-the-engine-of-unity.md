# Selfishness Is the Engine of Unity

## Definition

Selfishness creates interdependence, not isolation. The selfish gene makes the human. The selfish human makes the human race. Selfish cells, bacteria, and fungus make the ecosystem. Without tension, no evolution. Without resistance, no transformation. Without selfishness, no unity.

"Selfishness is the engine of unity, right? Like it's the selfish gene that makes the human and it's the selfish human that makes the human race." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Love the Feeling, Love the Resistance

## In Practice

The key to good selfishness is understanding the underlying want. Surface-level selfishness ("I want to get drunk and do whatever I want") does not satisfy. Following the want deeper, and deeper again, the deepest want turns out to be compassionate toward the people around you.

"Eventually you find the deepest want, even close to the deepest want, ends up to be a deeply compassionate thing for the people around you as well." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Love the Feeling, Love the Resistance

The false dichotomy is: I must choose between being good to me and being good to you. In Joe's experience, true self-compassion and compassion for others are the same action.

The problem is not selfishness. The problem is undeveloped selfishness. When a parent calls a child "selfish," they almost always mean: "I wanted you to do something different." Joe has never called his own children selfish. The accusation of selfishness is itself a form of [[resistance]] to the other person's autonomy.

"I don't even know if the word selfish -- I don't know if I even believe in it particularly. I feel like that's just a word that some adult created to control their children." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

## The Working Definition of Selfishness

A specific, useful definition: selfishness is what your parents told you you were when they wanted you to do something else.

"It's what your parents told you you were when they wanted you to do something else. I think that's really what selfishness is. Every time I've watched a parent call somebody selfish and then they become adults and then they think they're selfish, it's always because the parent wants them to do something else." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The definition is not a joke, although it sounds like one. It identifies the actual social function the word performs. The word does not describe a moral property. It describes a parent's frustration projected onto a child's want. The accusation transfers the parent's want-conflict onto the child as a character defect.

A two-year-old does not contemplate world peace. A two-year-old wants the cookie. That is the job of a two-year-old. Calling the two-year-old selfish is, on Joe's framing, itself a selfish act (you are demanding the child be different to suit your preference).

"To even call your kids selfish is a selfish act, you know, if you're defining it the same way, because a kid's whole job is to learn how to assert their will and to get their wants and needs met." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The adult who carries the internalized label is carrying their parent's frustrated want, not a description of their own character. See [[am-i-being-selfish]] for the coaching path with someone presenting this concern.

## Altruism Is Also Selfish, And That Is Fine

A common objection to this framing: "but isn't true selflessness the goal? Don't we want people to act for others, not for themselves?"

Joe collapses the distinction. Altruism feels good. There is a physical pleasure in helping others. There is an emotional satisfaction in being generous. Acts framed as selfless are also acts that the actor benefits from internally. The internal reward is not a contamination of the altruism; it is part of what makes altruism work.

"Altruism feels good. There's a great feeling, physical, in helping other people. There's a great emotional, mental feeling of being selfless. So I don't really see a difference. I see it more as an evolution. And the more that we allow ourselves to follow our wants, the more we become selfless in the standard way of looking at it, but it's just really refining how to be really good at being selfish." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

The implication: the goal is not to escape selfishness. The goal is to become very, very good at selfishness, which means following the wants deep enough that the deepest wants are intrinsically generous because the actor's wellbeing is recognized as inseparable from others' wellbeing. See [[what-is-truly-compassionate]].

The reframe also dissolves a category of fake moral high ground. The person who claims to do everything for others without any reward is either confused about their own internal experience or performing the lack of reward. Either way, the supposed selflessness is unstable. Owned selfishness ("I do this because it feels good to me and is good for you") is more stable than performed selflessness.

## Moralistic Selflessness Does Not Work

Doing the generous thing because you are trying not to be selfish does not produce the benefits of actual generosity. The action is the same; the internal motivation is the difference. Moralistic selflessness comes from fear of being judged selfish. Real generosity comes from the felt pleasure of caring.

"If you're doing it out of a moralistic sense, if you're doing it because you're trying not to be selfish, then it's worthless and you don't actually get the benefit of it. And you don't learn to do it because of the pure love of doing it. You're doing it out of just a form of self-preservation. So it's just a horrible way to teach it." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

This is why moralistic teaching of generosity (you should share, you should put others first) tends to produce adults who resent the people they serve. The shoulds installed the behavior without installing the love that would have made the behavior nourishing. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] for the broader principle.

The functional teaching of generosity is to support someone's wants, let them follow them, let them notice when a want did not deliver, let them refine. The natural endpoint of that process is generosity that feels good. The unnatural endpoint of moralistic teaching is performed generosity that produces burnout.

## Selfishness as a Developmental Gauge

A more precise framing: the level of selfishness someone is operating from is a usable diagnostic for where they are in their personal development. The level itself is the data.

"The levels of selfishness seem to be a good way to gauge where you are in your own personal development." -- Joe Hudson, The Business Behind our Courses

The levels (rough sketch):

- **The most contracted level:** "I want this now and I don't want you to have it." This is the selfishness of scarcity. The person experiences others as competitors for finite resources. The body is contracted. The level is correlated with significant misery.
- **The expanded but still contracted level:** Service of others as the means to one's own benefit. Less contracted than the first, still partial. There is recognition that helping others is rewarding, but the underlying calculus is still about the self.
- **The further-expanded level:** Genuine service that emerges from the recognition that self and other are not as separate as the contracted levels assume. The "selfishness" of caring for others as caring for oneself.
- **The further levels:** Joe suggests that even being-of-service-to-people is itself a form of misery compared to deeper levels he does not specify.

The key observation: each level is more spacious and less miserable than the one below it. The person operating from the most contracted level is suffering most. The progression is not from selfishness to selflessness. It is from contracted selfishness to expanded selfishness, with the selfishness remaining at every level but expanding to include more of what is.

This reframes the corrective: not "be less selfish" (which adds a should and stalls development) but "follow the want deeper until the deeper level becomes available" (which is the actual mechanism).

The principle has direct application as an anti-pattern guardrail. See [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the related warning about dressing up undeveloped selfishness in spiritual language. The corrective is not to deny the selfishness but to develop it.

## When Someone Experiences This

When torn between self-interest and generosity. When guilt about wanting things creates paralysis. When "selfishness" has been internalized as a character flaw rather than an evolutionary force.

The coaching path: follow the want deeper. What do you really want underneath the surface want? The deeper want almost always includes the wellbeing of others. The resistance to selfishness (see [[resistance]]) is itself a form of fight that keeps the person stuck at the surface level of wanting.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone believes they must sacrifice themselves for others
- When guilt about self-interest creates stuck patterns
- When [[wants-vs-partiality]] is in play: the person is hiding their wants because they think wanting is selfish
- When [[am-i-being-selfish]] is the presenting concern
- When a leader is hiding wants to avoid being seen as selfish, producing [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]]

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward deeper exploration of underlying wants
- Toward [[impartiality]]: once the deep want is known, partiality loosens
- Toward the recognition that self-care and other-care are not opposing forces
- Toward the [[need-behind-the-want]] practice for tracing surface wants to underlying needs
- Toward [[what-is-truly-compassionate]]: the related principle that self-care and other-care converge at depth

## The Developmental Mechanism

The principle connects to [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]]. The deepest want does not become available unless the wants leading to it are met or fully felt. The reason undeveloped selfishness looks unattractive is that the person is still on the surface wants and has not yet traveled to the deeper layers. Telling them to skip directly to the generous wants installs another should and freezes the process.

The corrective is not to suppress the surface wants. It is to actually meet them so the deeper wants can surface. This is also why [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]] is part of the same architecture. Owning the surface want lets it transform into its more accurate version, which is usually generous.
