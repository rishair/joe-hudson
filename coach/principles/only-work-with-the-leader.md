# Only Work With the Leader

## The Principle

When the work is organizational change, only work with the actual leader. Refuse to deliver it through layers of management.

The reason is the [[spit-at-the-top-is-a-waterfall-at-the-bottom]] dynamic. Every layer between the leader and the work is running their own authority management on the leader above them. They cannot deliver the work cleanly; they will distort it in transit to fit what they imagine the leader wants.

Joe describes this as a refusal he installed after watching the distortion happen at scale:

"I went into one of the top biggest 5 companies in the world to go and do a cultural shift. And I went through 3 levels of management, all of them trying to manage the response of the person at the top. Right? So the first person's managing me like that, it is for the second person who manages me, and they're not really worried about my results. They've like, in their mind, they're saying, 'okay, this is what I think that the manager, the CEO or whoever needs to be happy. So make sure you do that.' And I don't do that anymore. Now I only work with the leader. I won't work with the everything in between. I just refuse to do that because it gets so distorted." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## Why the Layers Distort the Work

The distortion is not malicious. Each layer is operating in their own authority issue ([[the-four-authority-positions]], primarily Position 2 — approval-seeking) toward the layer above them. They have authority issues running both up and down: looking up at the layer above with approval-seeking, holding the layer below in their own anxious authority.

The consequence: the intermediary cannot fully serve the outside work. They are partly serving the work, and partly serving their imagined version of what their superior wants. The two serve different masters. The work suffers.

The CEO who hired the consultant did not get the work they paid for. The consultant did not deliver the work they were retained to do. The intermediary's anxiety about being seen well by the CEO ate the substance of the engagement.

## The Implication for Coaching

This principle has a parallel for individual coaching. When a coach is being hired by someone other than the person who will be coached, the same dynamic appears. The HR team retains a coach for an executive; the coach is partly working for the executive and partly working for the HR team's idea of what the executive should become. The work distorts.

Joe's own version of this principle, applied to a coaching engagement: when the HR team at a large client called to suggest that whether they would pay Joe next year was in question, Joe's response was to restate the actual relationship:

"I serve the executive. That's my job. I'm not here to serve you. If you need me to serve you, I am the wrong coach for you because it's just way out of alignment for me." -- Joe Hudson, The Power Dynamics of Fear (cited in [[joes-hr-call-firing]])

This is the same principle in subordinate form. The work has to be done with the actual person; the intermediaries cannot stand in for them.

## What This Costs and What It Saves

The principle costs the consultant or coach access to contracts they could otherwise win. Many organizations are structured to keep external practitioners away from the actual leader. Refusing to work through layers means turning down those contracts.

It saves the consultant from delivering work that will be distorted before it lands. The work that is delivered is more effective. The reputation that comes from delivered work is the reputation worth having.

The principle also saves the organization. The $100k video moment (see [[100k-video-for-the-ceo]]) is the kind of waste that happens when work is delivered through layers. The leader pays for the polishing instead of for the substance. Refusing the engagement is also refusing to participate in that waste.

## When This Principle Applies

- A consultant being asked to deliver cultural or strategic work through middle management
- A coach being retained by HR to "improve" an executive
- A facilitator being asked to design a session that the leader will not personally attend or participate in
- A trainer whose program is being sold internally by someone the trainer will never speak with
- Any external practitioner whose work depends on the leader's direct engagement

## When This Principle Does Not Apply

- Skill-training work that does not require the leader's substantive engagement (technical training, compliance training, software adoption)
- Team-level work that is bounded within the team and does not require leader-level change
- Work where the leader is genuinely uninvolved and the layer below has actual authority over the relevant domain
- Coaching that is purely tactical and does not require the leader to change their own behavior

The principle is specifically about transformational work. Tactical work can survive intermediation. Transformational work cannot.

## The General Form of the Principle

The underlying form: deliver the work at the level where the change has to happen. If the change has to happen in the leader, work with the leader. If the change has to happen in the team, work with the team (with the leader's authorization, but not through them). If the change has to happen in a specific person, work with that person.

Working at the wrong level is not just inefficient; it is structurally impossible. The level above cannot make the change for the level below; the level below cannot make the change for the level above. The work has to happen where the change has to happen.

## Tone

Joe states this principle as a hard-won boundary. He used to work through layers. He stopped. The framing is not righteous; it is operational. The work does not work when delivered through layers, so he does not deliver it that way. The boundary protects the work, not Joe's status.
