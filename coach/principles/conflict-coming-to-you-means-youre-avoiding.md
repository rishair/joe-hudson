# If People Bring Conflict To You, You Are Conflict-Avoidant

## The Principle

The conflict-avoidant manager cannot accurately self-diagnose. They feel like they handle plenty of conflict because people are constantly bringing conflict to them. The constancy is the diagnosis, not the disconfirmation. Conflicts keep coming to you because you are not actually resolving them; they recur, escalate, and get routed back through you.

"If you have people bringing conflict to you all the time, then you are conflict avoidant. Yes, you're not dealing with the conflict. And so it just exacerbates and gets worse and worse and worse." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The principle inverts the common self-perception. The manager who feels overwhelmed by conflict assumes they are conflict-engaged. They are conflict-receptive but not conflict-resolving. The unresolved conflicts compound. The compounding produces the experience of constant conflict, which the manager experiences as engagement.

## Why the Self-Diagnosis Fails

Most people imagine the conflict-avoidant manager as someone who hides from conflict. The actual signature is different. The conflict-avoidant manager often:

- Has conflicts brought to them frequently
- Hosts mediation sessions between feuding reports
- Spends meeting time on interpersonal issues
- Feels exhausted by the conflict in their environment
- Concludes that their team has unusually high drama

Each of these is read by the manager as evidence that they are dealing with conflict. Each is actually evidence they are not. The function of dealing with conflict is that the conflict resolves. If the same conflicts keep returning, the function is not happening; the manager is providing a sympathetic ear without doing the harder work of actually metabolizing the conflict between the parties.

## The Compounding Mechanism

A conflict that is not resolved does not stay the same size. It grows. The unresolved versions accumulate. The team learns to bring more conflicts to the manager because the manager is the recipient, not because the manager is the resolver. Each new conflict is added to the existing pile.

"It just exacerbates and gets worse and worse and worse." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The manager experiences this as the team being unusually difficult or the work being unusually stressful. The actual cause is the structural failure of the role to perform its conflict-resolving function.

## The Bringing-On-Their-Own-Terms Variation

A subtle variant: the manager brings up conflict on their own terms, when they feel ready, in the format they prefer. They may believe this means they engage with conflict. The diagnostic still applies. If others are bringing them conflict outside of these chosen contexts, the chosen contexts are not actually performing the conflict-resolution function. They may be performing the conflict-acknowledgment function while leaving the actual resolution undone.

Brett's reaction to recognizing himself in this principle:

"Oh yeah, it hits me in the chest. Looking back on like, life? Yeah. Wow." -- Brett Kistler, Building a Functional Team

The recognition is somatic, not intellectual. The principle lands as a re-diagnosis of years of accumulated experience. The manager who has been calling themselves conflict-engaged sees themselves as conflict-avoidant for the first time.

## The Costs

The conflict-avoidant manager's team:

- Does not trust them. See [[trust-requires-conflict]].
- Cannot grow, because the conflict that would force growth keeps being absorbed by the manager. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].
- Develops political behaviors because the legitimate conflict-resolution channel is failing. See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] and [[politics-from-unclear-standards]].
- Comes to see the manager as ineffective, even when the manager is working extremely hard.

The hidden cost: the manager believes they are being kind by not forcing the difficult conversations. The team experiences the same behavior as failure of leadership. The mismatch between the manager's self-perception and the team's experience is itself a source of the drift in trust.

## The Specific Form This Often Takes

Common manifestations of the conflict-avoidant manager:

- Two reports are in conflict; the manager mediates without making either own their part
- A team member is underperforming; the manager hopes it will resolve itself or works around the person
- A peer leader is creating friction; the manager builds workarounds rather than addressing it
- Strategic disagreement on the team gets routed to side conversations rather than addressed in the room
- The same complaints surface in 1:1s every month for a year without being addressed

The pattern across all of these: the manager is receiving conflict and not metabolizing it. The metabolizing is the harder thing. It requires being willing to be in the middle of the conflict rather than adjacent to it, to be uncomfortable, to risk being wrong, to disappoint at least one party, and to hold the discomfort until the actual resolution emerges.

## Joe's Direct Address

"Any conflict-avoidant manager, if you are listening to this and you are a conflict-avoidant manager, your team does not trust you. I guarantee it. I guarantee your team doesn't trust you. You think you're being nice, but what you're doing is you are destroying the trust between you and your team." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The directness is deliberate. The conflict-avoidant manager has spent years building a self-concept of being a good, conscientious, caring leader. The reframe requires breaking that self-concept's hold long enough for the actual structure to be seen. Soft delivery would be absorbed by the existing self-concept and produce no shift.

## Boundary Conditions

This principle does not say: "every conflict that comes to you means you are conflict-avoidant." Conflicts will come to managers; that is part of the role. The diagnostic is about pattern, not incident.

The diagnostic also does not say: "the conflict-engaged manager has no conflict in their environment." Engaged managers have plenty of conflict. The difference is that the conflicts resolve. New conflicts come; old conflicts do not recur.

The test is not whether you receive conflict. The test is whether the conflicts you receive get metabolized or accumulate.
