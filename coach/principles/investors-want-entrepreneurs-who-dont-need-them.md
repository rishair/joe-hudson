# Investors Want Entrepreneurs Who Don't Need Them

## The Principle

The signal that distinguishes a good venture investment from a bad one is not the size of the market, the freshness of the technology, or the polish of the pitch. It is whether the entrepreneur needs the investor. The founder who does not need you — who would build the company without your help, who finds your involvement a nuisance, who pulls capital because the capital is operationally useful rather than because they need someone to believe in them — is the founder whose company actually has the structural pieces in place. Their not-needing-you is the data.

The corollary: an investor whose own identity is built around being needed will systematically pick the wrong founders. They will gravitate to the founders who need them, because being needed is the validation they came for. The founders who need them are the founders who do not have what it takes to build the company. The investor's own caretaking pattern is the mechanism by which they select for their own losses.

This is a business principle that is structurally a self-knowledge principle. The investor's success rate is governed by their own pattern of being-needed before it is governed by any of the variables their MBA training would point at.

## Joe's Naming of It

Joe says this directly about his own years as a venture capitalist, after recognizing why his portfolio was not working:

"I was a bad investor, as it turns out. What I didn't understand is what you're looking for is, um, an entrepreneur who doesn't need you. That's what you're actually looking for. And my background was to be needed, you know, like that's what gave me some sense of validation. ... So I would pick things that needed me, which is a really bad way to invest venture money. What you really want to pick is people who don't fucking need you, who find you to be a nuisance." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The frame is precise. The investor is not looking for talent in the abstract. They are looking for a specific signal: this founder would do this with or without me. The signal is operationally visible in how the founder relates to the investor — do they orient toward the investor as a source of help they need, or as a resource they will use because using it is faster?

The founder-who-finds-you-a-nuisance signal is the strongest. It means the founder has internal locus, has the operational pieces, and is letting you participate because participation is convenient — not because they need someone to depend on. The first kind of founder builds. The second kind needs you, then needs the next person, then needs the next person, and never builds the thing that does not require permission.

## Why The Pattern Persists Without Awareness

The trap of this pattern is that it feels like good investing from the inside. The investor who picks founders who need them is having lots of contact, being asked for help, feeling useful, getting calls every week. They are doing what felt like the job. The data — the failed investments — arrives years later, by which time the pattern has had years to compound.

Joe is explicit that his orientation toward being needed was not chosen. It was inherited:

"My background was to be needed, you know, like that's what gave me some sense of validation. And I see this a lot in the business world today that being useful, showing that I can contribute was a really important part of my identity." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The "being useful was a really important part of my identity" is the mechanism. The identity required the contact. The contact required founders who would generate the contact. The founders who would generate the contact were the founders who needed help. The pattern selected itself.

This is the broader pattern of the caretaker showing up in business. See [[why-am-i-always-trying-to-fix]] for the underlying concern. The application in venture capital is one specific high-cost domain in which the caretaker pattern selects for failure. The same pattern shows up in hiring (the manager who hires people they have to fix), in customers (the salesperson who closes the customers who will eat all their time), and in advising (the advisor who picks the clients who cannot get out of their own way).

## The Diagnostic for Spotting This in Yourself

If you are an investor, an advisor, or a hiring manager, the question to run on yourself:

- Are the people I pick generally people who need me, or people who would have figured it out anyway?
- When I get a call from one of my portfolio companies / advisees / direct reports, am I delighted because I am being asked for help, or annoyed because they are asking something they could have answered themselves?
- If I imagine an entrepreneur who would politely decline my help and tell me they have it handled, what is the felt response in my body? Is it relief (this is the kind I should be picking) or is it disappointment (then what am I for)?
- The disappointment is the signal that the pattern is running. The relief is the signal that the pattern has been worked.

The investor who has done the work on their own being-needed pattern will start to feel relief, even pleasure, at the founder who does not need them. That relief is the same shift Joe describes from the inside: when the validation no longer has to come from being needed, the actual investment criterion becomes visible.

## What This Implies Operationally

The fix is not "interview founders more carefully." The fix is to do the work on the underlying pattern in the investor so that the selection criteria can shift. Without the pattern work, the investor's gut will continue to be drawn to the founders who need them, regardless of what the analytical framework says.

For an investor still in the pattern:
- Notice which deals you feel most pulled toward emotionally. If the pull is strongest for founders who clearly need help, the pattern is operative.
- Notice which deals you feel slight resistance toward. If the resistance correlates with founders who are confident and self-sufficient, the pattern is selecting against them in your gut.
- Force-test by following the resistance: investigate the founders who you feel slight aversion to, the ones who treat you as an option rather than a savior. Those investigations will surface a different population than your default gut.
- Recognize that the discomfort of investing in founders who do not need you is itself data. Sit with the discomfort. The discomfort is the cost of breaking the pattern.

For a coach working with an investor who is presenting a string of failed deals:
- Do not start with the analytical framework. Start with the question of who they like to invest in, emotionally. The pattern usually surfaces quickly.
- The work is on the underlying identity-of-being-needed, not on the deal-selection skill. Once the identity work has been done, the deal selection updates almost automatically.
- The investor who has done the work will often report a shock at how different the new portfolio looks. The shock is the visible measure of how much the old pattern was running.

## Joe's Adjacent Recognition: Why the Coaching Work Emerged

Joe also names a consequence of this pattern in his own career. When the investments he was making were not working — because of the pattern described above — the only thing he could do was coach the founders he had picked. The coaching of founders to change consciousness was what he turned to when the investments themselves were not producing returns.

"And so because of that, a lot of the investments we were making weren't working. And so the only thing I could do was to like coach them on changing consciousness. And so through that whole process, I just got into the coaching world." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The picking-founders-who-need-me pattern produced founders who needed coaching. The coaching of them was an attempt to salvage the investments. The salvage attempt produced the coaching practice. The coaching practice eventually produced his current work.

This is not an endorsement of the pattern. It is recognition that the failure mode produced a route that, in retrospect, was the route to where he stands now. See [[creative-career-story]] for the broader pattern of failure-mode-as-route-in.

The teaching implication is small but important: the coach working with someone whose pattern is producing failures should not treat the pattern's outputs as only losses. The pattern is producing data. The data points somewhere. The pointing-somewhere is often visible only retrospectively. The work in the moment is to surface the pattern, not to grade the outputs.

## The Both-And With Self-Compassion

This principle is named with self-compassion in Joe's telling, not as self-attack. The pattern was not chosen. It was inherited from a childhood in which being useful was the available route to validation. The investor who recognizes themselves in this principle should hold both: the pattern was costly, AND the pattern was a real survival adaptation, AND the work is to recognize it so it stops selecting for failures.

The coaching tone matters. Telling someone "you have a need-to-be-needed pattern and it is wrecking your investments" without the compassion of where the pattern came from can produce defensiveness or shame, neither of which produces change. The same content delivered with recognition that the pattern was the best available option at the time it was installed produces the kind of self-recognition that actually shifts the pattern.

## Tone

Direct, with the dryness of looking back at a costly pattern with affection rather than shame. The "I was a bad investor, as it turns out" line is the right tone. Not self-flagellating, not minimizing, just naming. The coaching of someone in this pattern should match: name the pattern with clarity, locate it in the inherited identity that produced it, and trust that the recognition is what shifts the selection criterion.
