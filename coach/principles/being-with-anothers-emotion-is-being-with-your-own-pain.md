# Being With Another's Emotion Is Actually Being With Your Own Pain

## The Principle

When you find it hard to be with another person's emotion, the thing you cannot be with is not their emotion. It is your own emotion that their emotion is evoking. The other person's pain is the stimulus. Your own evoked pain is what your nervous system is actually trying to avoid. The work of "learning to be with their emotion" is therefore the work of learning to be with what their emotion produces in you.

If their emotion produced nothing in you, being with them would be easy. The difficulty is your own response. The difficulty is yours.

"I would say that that is a beautiful way to work it. And the actual thing you're being with is your own pain. Absolutely. Yeah, so it's, it's their pain is evoking something in you. So maybe their pain is evoking your helplessness, or maybe their pain is evoking your pain. But you're being in that, you're not being -- being with them is evoking something in you. And if it isn't, then it would be easy to be in there, be with them in their pain, right? So it's learning to be with yourself in that." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered!

## The Implication

This principle reframes "I can't be with my partner's anger" or "I freeze when my child is sad" or "I shut down when my client cries." The framing flips from "their emotion is too much" to "my evoked emotion is what I am not able to be with."

This is not a semantic trick. It changes what the work is. The previous framing puts the obstacle outside the self: "I need to develop the capacity to handle their emotion." The new framing puts the obstacle inside: "I need to develop the capacity to be with what their emotion calls up in me."

The first framing is bottomless and rotates. Each new person produces a new emotion to learn to handle. The second framing locates the actual material and lets the work happen there. Once you can be with your own helplessness, anyone's pain (which evokes helplessness in you) becomes available to be with.

## What Their Emotion Usually Evokes

The list is short and recurrent:

**Helplessness.** Their pain evokes the helplessness you have not been able to feel. You cannot be with their crying because their crying activates the felt sense of being unable to fix it, which activates the helplessness you have been avoiding all your life. Most caretaking is a defense against this evoked helplessness. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

**Your own version of the same pain.** Their grief evokes your ungrieved grief. Their anger evokes your unmet anger. Their fear evokes your unfaced fear. The body recognizes the emotion and produces its own version. If your version is unprocessed, the produced version overwhelms you, and you must shut down their expression to stop your own from rising.

**The original parental scene.** Their pain evokes the scene with the parent who was in pain and whose pain you could not be with as a child. The body is back in that moment. The current moment is being filtered through it. See [[trigger]].

**The threat of being responsible for it.** Their pain evokes the felt belief that their pain is your fault, your job to fix, or your moral obligation to absorb. This belief is usually inherited from caretaking parents or trauma configurations.

Identifying which one is evoked is the start of the work. The evoked emotion is the actual material.

## The Practice This Creates

The practice is no longer "be present with my partner's anger." The practice is now: "when my partner's anger arises, notice what arises in me, and learn to be with that."

The structure:

1. The partner's emotion arises.
2. Your own emotion arises in response. (This happens whether you notice or not.)
3. You notice the arising. The noticing is the move.
4. You bring attention to your own arising rather than to managing theirs.
5. You feel your own emotion enough that you can stay present.
6. From the now-stable position, you can actually be with theirs.

The standard inversion: people try to manage step 1 (the partner's emotion) without ever working on step 2 (their own response). The work cannot succeed at step 1 because the obstacle is at step 2. Until step 2 is worked, every new emotional event from the partner re-triggers the unworked material.

See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] for the actual mechanism of working step 2.

## Why This Is Liberating

The principle is good news in disguise. The work of being with one specific partner's specific emotional pattern is large and never-ending. The work of being with your own evoked emotion is finite. Once your helplessness can be felt without overwhelm, helplessness in any form becomes available. Once your grief can be felt, grief from anyone becomes available. The internal work generalizes; the external work does not.

The partner who has done their own emotional work can be present with almost any partner's almost any emotion. They are not performing presence. They are not running technique. They are simply not destabilized by what the other person is producing.

## How This Connects to Holding Space

This principle is the inside view of [[stay-centered-while-holding-space]]. Staying centered is the external description: you remain yourself while they have their experience. This principle gives the internal mechanism: you remain centered by being with your own evoked emotion rather than letting it pull you off center.

A coach who has not done this work either dissolves into the client's emotion (because their own evoked emotion is unbearable and merging is the only way to numb it) or walls off from it (because the evoked emotion is unbearable and distance is the only way to manage it). Neither is presence. Both are responses to the unworked material in the coach.

## The "Their Pain Evokes My Pain" Map

A useful self-coaching map:

- When my partner is angry, what arises in me? (Fear of conflict? My own anger? Helplessness?)
- When my child cries, what arises in me? (Shame at not preventing it? My own ungrieved grief?)
- When my client gets stuck, what arises in me? (My fear of inadequacy? Annoyance at them for slowing the work?)
- When my friend is depressed, what arises in me? (My own depression? Resentment at the burden? Helplessness?)

Each "what arises in me" is the actual material. Each is a doorway to the unworked emotional content the situation has been pointing to all along.

## What This Principle Refutes

This principle refutes the framing that presence with another's emotion is a generalized skill that can be acquired by practicing with many different others. It is not a skill in that sense. It is the result of having faced and integrated your own emotional responses.

It also refutes the framing that the difficulty of being with another is information about them. "She's too intense." "He's too emotional." "They're too needy." These framings locate the problem in the other person. The principle relocates the problem: the intensity is yours. The other person is just the occasion.

This is the same mechanism as [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] applied to emotion specifically. The trigger is showing you what you carry. The unbearableness of the other's emotion is showing you what you have not yet been able to be with in yourself.

- The discovery that "I can't handle their X" is precisely a map of their own X
- The shift from trying to change the partner's emotional expression to working with their own response
- The freedom of presence once the evoked material has been integrated

## The Childhood-Origin Diagnostic

A particularly clean self-diagnostic for which emotions are the unworked material: the emotions you have a hard time sitting with in your partner or your kids are usually the exact emotions you were not allowed to sit with as a kid. The match is often one-to-one. The parent who could not let you cry produced an adult who cannot sit with their partner's crying. The parent who could not let you be angry produced an adult who cannot be with their kid's anger.

"What I've noticed is every emotion that I have a hard time sitting with, with Tara or with my kids, is an emotion that I wasn't allowed to sit with as a kid that I don't like sitting with." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Fixing Your Partner

The diagnostic value: the specific emotion that destabilizes you in the people closest to you names, with precision, an emotion from your own childhood that did not get welcomed. The current intolerance maps to the original prohibition. The work is to give yourself, now, what was not available then — permission to feel that specific emotion fully — and the intolerance with others dissolves as a side-effect.

This refines the broader principle. The article's earlier framing ("their pain evokes your pain") is correct but general. The childhood-origin diagnostic makes it specific: the pain their pain evokes is usually the pain you were specifically forbidden from feeling. The forbidden emotion lives in the body as an un-completed emotional task. Other people's expression of that emotion is one of the only consistent stimuli that activates the un-completed task. The activation feels like "I can't handle this from them" but is really "I am being asked to feel something I was trained to suppress."

A worked example Joe uses: the airplane. When a kid starts crying on a plane, look around at the adults who are visibly aggravated. The aggravated adults were the ones who were not allowed to cry as kids. The kid's crying is activating, in them, the suppressed crying of their own childhood. The aggravation is the felt cost of holding that suppression in the presence of evidence that it could have been otherwise.

"You go into an airplane and a kid starts to cry and you stand up and you look at the airplane and everybody who's frickin' frustrated, they were the ones that weren't allowed to cry as kids. It's just like, as simple as that." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Fixing Your Partner

The airplane example is generalizable. The annoyed-at-the-public-tantrum adult, the parent who shuts down at their teenager's anger, the partner who freezes at sadness — all are diagnostic moments. The reaction is naming the emotion that was forbidden in the reactor's own childhood. See [[esme-just-having-my-emotions]] for the airplane example in its broader context, and [[every-trigger-with-your-kid-is-projection]] for the parallel diagnostic in the parent-child relationship.

The instruction for an AI coach: when a person reports difficulty being with a specific emotion in their partner or child, the most direct line of inquiry is "what happened with that emotion in your own family?" The answer almost always reveals the suppression. The work then becomes the work on the suppression, not the work on the relationship.

## The Self-Protective Function of Managing the Other's Emotion

The principle deepens once the self-protective function of the management becomes visible. Trying to make the partner feel a different emotion is not just a failure of presence. It is an active strategy for not having to feel the emotion the partner is producing in you.

"What I'm actually doing is I am trying to make them feel a certain way so I don't have to feel a certain way. It's even more manipulative than I thought it was. I thought it was at the beginning, like, I'm trying to help this person. And then it was, oh, I am trying to make them feel good for me. So I don't have to feel a certain way so I can continue to be neurotic around my emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Stop Fixing Your Partner

The frame "manipulation" is heavy and exact. The behavior that looked like caring is, structurally, the management of the partner's state for the avoidance of one's own. The recognition does not require self-attack — see [[loving-all-parts]] — but it does require honesty. The cleaner the recognition of the function, the more cleanly the behavior can change.

The mechanism: as long as the partner's emotion is the one being managed, the emotion in the self that is evoked by the partner's emotion never has to be felt. The whole project of caretaking is, at the level of mechanism, a sophisticated avoidance of the caretaker's own evoked feeling. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for the systematic distinction this principle rests on, and [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] for the structural principle that follows.
