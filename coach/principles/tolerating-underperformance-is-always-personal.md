# Tolerating Underperformance Is Always Personal

## The Principle

The common phrase "it's not personal, it's just business" gets the structure exactly backwards. The clean business move — releasing someone who isn't delivering, having the hard accountability conversation, ending the arrangement — is the impersonal one. The thing that makes a leader tolerate underperformance is always personal. Something in the leader is preventing the clean business move from being made.

The inversion: business is impersonal by default; toleration is personal by default. The cleaner the business decision is, the less personal it is. The more the underperformance is being tolerated, the more personal something is.

"There's that saying, it's not business, or it's not personal, it's just business. Which is pretty hilarious because the only thing that could make it that, you would put up with something that is not working in the organization, the only reason you would allow paying somebody $200K who's not holding up their role is because something personal is going on." -- Joe Hudson, How to Delegate Without Micromanaging

## The Structural Logic

When the ROI on someone isn't working — the work isn't getting done, the role isn't being filled, the cost isn't justified — the impersonal business decision is to address it. Hold the conversation. Reset the expectations. Make the change. The business case is clean.

If the leader is not making the clean decision despite the clean case, something is in the way. That something is personal by definition. The cleanness of the case has already been established; the only remaining variable is what's preventing the leader from acting on it.

"It's just, it's a very matter-of-fact situation, right? Because again, the better option isn't just better for you, it's better for the entire team." -- Joe Hudson, How to Delegate Without Micromanaging (the matter-of-fact framing the impersonal version actually takes)

The matter-of-fact framing is what's available when the personal stuff is not running. The leader who is not running personal stuff can name the situation plainly, address it, and move forward. The cost to the team of not addressing it is also visible from this position. The decision is made.

## What "Personal" Means Here

The personal stuff blocking the clean business decision usually takes one of several forms:

**Thinking you need to save them.** The leader feels responsible for the underperforming employee's life — their financial situation, their career, their feelings. The responsibility-for-them is the caretaking move (see [[feeling-responsible-for-them-disempowers-them]]) showing up in the business decision. The leader can't release the person because they've taken on the person as their charge.

**Not thinking you deserve the A-player.** The leader is tolerating the underperformer because some part of the leader believes they don't deserve someone who would actually deliver. The underperformer matches the leader's self-image. Releasing them would require the leader to also release the self-image.

**Thinking it's your fault.** The leader has collapsed the team member's failure into their own failure as a leader. "If they're not delivering, I must have hired wrong or managed wrong, and the right response is for me to fix myself or work harder, not to release them." See [[blame-is-the-abandonment-of-leadership]] for the related dynamic. The over-responsibility for the failure is itself the block.

**Conflict avoidance.** The leader can't have the hard conversation, so they tolerate the underperformance instead. The toleration is cheaper in the short term and more expensive in the long term. The conversation that wasn't had today becomes the resentment, drama, or quiet decline of months later.

**Identity protection.** Releasing the person would require the leader to be the kind of leader who releases people. The leader has built an identity around being warm, generous, loyal, or caring, and the release would feel like a violation of that identity. The toleration is protecting the identity.

Joe surfaces three of these directly in the source transcript:

"Whether it is that you think you need to save them or you don't think you deserve the A-player or whatever it is, or you think it's your fault as the leader." -- Joe Hudson, How to Delegate Without Micromanaging

Each of these is a flavor of [[just-business-vs-impersonal|the not-impersonal-yet]] state. The leader has personal material that the situation is hitting. The personal material is what makes the toleration possible.

## The Diagnostic Move

The principle gives a leader a fast diagnostic for their own toleration. Wherever the leader is tolerating something that the clean business case says should be addressed, the question is: what is personal here?

The question is not "is there personal stuff." There always is when there's toleration. The question is "what specifically." Naming the specific personal material is the first move toward freeing the decision.

A second-order diagnostic: a leader who claims their tolerance is "just professional" or "because the situation is complicated" or "because firing people is expensive" is usually running personal material that has been dressed in business language. The professional framing protects the personal material from being seen. The diagnostic question reaches under the framing: if the personal weren't there, would the case look the same? Usually the answer is no.

## The Counterpart: Responsibility vs. Fault

The principle has a subtle distinction the leader needs in order to apply it. There is a real responsibility the leader has for the company's health that is not the same as taking on every failure as their personal fault.

"You know, the nuance from, it's my responsibility to make sure that the company is healthy versus it's my fault, everyone's failures are my own fault." -- Joe Hudson, How to Delegate Without Micromanaging

The leader is responsible for the company's health. That responsibility includes making the impersonal business decisions when they need to be made. It does not include taking on every team member's failure as evidence of the leader's own failure. The two get confused. The confusion produces toleration: the leader who thinks the team member's failure is their fault won't release the team member because releasing them would feel like punishing them for the leader's own failure.

The clean stance: I am responsible for the company's health. The team member is responsible for their performance. If their performance isn't working, my responsibility for the company's health requires me to address it, and their responsibility for their performance requires them to either rise to the standard or move on. Neither responsibility is the other's. The decision is made cleanly because the responsibilities are sorted.

## The Tone of the Clean Decision

A specific quality of the impersonal decision: it is not punitive. The leader is not making the change because the team member is bad. The leader is making the change because the arrangement isn't producing what either side wanted. The framing is structural, not moral.

"It isn't have to be like, 'Grr, you're in trouble.' Right, it's just, 'Hey, the ROI on what we're doing here isn't working and there's a better option. If I'm gonna take the better option, I'm gonna take the better option.'" -- Joe Hudson, How to Delegate Without Micromanaging

The plainness of the framing is the tell that the personal isn't running. The leader who has to dress up the decision in moral language ("they really aren't a good person," "they don't have what it takes") is still running personal material that is leaking out as evaluation of the other. The leader who can name it as a structural mismatch ("this arrangement isn't producing what we need") is operating from the impersonal place. See [[just-business-vs-impersonal]] for the dedicated treatment of the tone difference and how clients can use the language as a diagnostic.

## The Cost of Not Addressing It

The toleration is not cost-free. The principle is sometimes received as if the leader is being asked to be harder on people. The actual frame is that the toleration is doing damage that the leader is not accounting for.

The cost to the team: working with someone who isn't holding up their end is exhausting. The other team members are absorbing the work, working around the gap, or holding the relational tension of not being able to talk about what everyone sees. The leader's toleration is being paid for by the team.

The cost to the underperforming person: staying in a role they cannot fill is corrosive to them. They know on some level the work isn't getting done. The mismatch chips away at their self-respect. Releasing them is more humane than tolerating them — it frees them to find work they can actually do well, which is what most people actually want.

The cost to the leader: the toleration produces resentment in the leader that will eventually leak as harshness, withdrawal, or a sudden release that lands as betrayal because no warning was given. The chronic toleration is also costing the leader's bandwidth, attention, and trust in their own judgment.

The clean impersonal decision is the move that respects all three.

## The Counterpart Principle: What You Tolerate IS Your Leadership

The principle interlocks with [[leadership-is-what-you-tolerate]]. What the leader puts up with is the quality of their leadership. The principle here adds: what the leader puts up with is also what is personal for them. The pattern of toleration in a leader's team is a map of the personal material the leader is carrying.

This means the toleration audit is also a self-knowledge audit. List what you are tolerating in the team. For each item, ask what personal material is keeping you from addressing it. The answers reveal the leader to themselves. The toleration list and the personal-material list are the same list, viewed from two angles.

## What This Is Not

**Not "be cruel about firing people."** The principle is not a license to harden up and make decisions without care. The clean impersonal decision can be delivered with full presence, real care, and openness to whatever the other person is feeling. See [[three-ways-to-fire-someone]] for the dedicated treatment of how to do the impersonal version with humanity.

**Not "every tolerance is personal in a bad way."** Some toleration is appropriate. A new hire needs time to ramp up. A skilled employee going through a hard personal time may warrant temporary accommodation. The principle is about chronic toleration of underperformance that the clean business case says should be addressed. The discernment between appropriate and inappropriate toleration is its own work.

**Not "make all decisions cold."** The impersonal in this principle does not mean cold or unfeeling. It means free from personal material that is blocking the decision. The decision can be made with full warmth toward the person, full sadness about the change, full care for the relationship — and still be the clean impersonal decision. See [[just-business-vs-impersonal]] for the distinction.

## When This Applies Beyond Hiring/Firing

The principle generalizes. Anywhere a leader is tolerating something that the clean situational analysis says should be addressed, the question is what personal material is blocking the action. This applies to:

- The team member who keeps missing deadlines
- The partnership that isn't producing the value it was supposed to
- The investment that isn't paying off but you keep funding
- The vendor relationship that has degraded but you keep renewing
- The product line that isn't profitable but you keep supporting
- The strategic direction that the data says isn't working but you keep pursuing

In each case, the impersonal decision is the structural one. The toleration is the place where personal material is running. The work is to see the personal material clearly enough to free the decision.

## The Compound Application: Tolerating Yourself

The principle also applies inside the leader. The leader who is tolerating their own underperformance — in some domain, on some dimension — is doing so for personal reasons. The clean self-leadership move would be to address it. The toleration is being held in place by something the leader doesn't want to feel, recognize, or change.

This is the same structure scaled down. The leader who can apply the principle outward (to the team) but not inward (to themselves) is missing half the leverage. The personal material that produces the toleration of the underperforming team member is often the same personal material that produces the toleration of the leader's own gaps. Seeing one usually reveals the other.

## When to Reach for This

- A leader is tolerating an obvious performance issue and explaining it in business language
- A leader is feeling stuck on a hire/fire decision that the data clearly indicates
- A leader is exhausted from working around someone they should release
- A leader is asking how to be "kinder" in a way that is actually delaying the decision
- A leader is in a coaching conversation about a team member and the conversation keeps returning to the leader's own discomfort
- A leader has just used the phrase "it's not personal, it's just business" — the diagnostic fires immediately

## Tone

Direct and structural. The principle does not moralize. It surfaces a structural inversion in how most people use the phrase "it's not personal, it's just business" — the inversion is meant to liberate the leader from the framing that has been holding the toleration in place. The tone is the tone of naming a structural fact, not the tone of criticizing the leader for tolerating. The leader is usually relieved to see the framing flip.
