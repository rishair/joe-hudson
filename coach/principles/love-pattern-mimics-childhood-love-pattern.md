# Adult Love Mimics the Childhood Love Pattern

## The Principle

A person responds to love in adulthood the way their body was trained to respond to love in childhood. If love came with abuse, the arrival of love now produces the abuse-response. If love came with abandonment, the arrival of love now produces the bracing-for-abandonment response. If love came with engulfment, the arrival of love now produces the suffocation response. The mechanism is direct: the body learns what love is by what comes with love the first time, and the body's response to love is the trained response from the original pairing.

The implication for adult relationships is that good love does not get a clean reception. Good love arrives, and the body produces the response that the original love-configuration produced. The person who was beaten when loved may, when loved well, produce anger. The person who was abandoned when loved may, when loved well, produce withdrawal. The body is doing what it learned to do. The body does not know yet that the love is different.

"Anybody who's had that experience of getting hit like that is going to have a combative relationship with love. They're going to have some sort of combative situation going on. And so any exposure that the therapist and/or coach would give in that moment there — you should assume that there's going to be some way in which that person is going to use that if they feel exposed, if they feel loved, particularly because if love came with abuse, when they start to love, they're going to go towards abuse because it's what's wired in them." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of People

## The Specific Form: Love-Equals-Abuse

The principle's most legible form is the love-equals-abuse imprint. A child who was beaten by a caregiver — or screamed at, criticized, exploited, used — by the same person who said "I love you" learns that love and abuse are the same package. The neural wiring connects the two. In adulthood, when love arrives without the abuse, something is missing from the body's expected configuration. The body, trying to complete the pattern, produces the abuse half itself.

The abuse-half can show up in three directions:

1. **Toward the self.** The person who is loved well becomes self-abusive. They self-criticize, self-punish, sabotage their own life. The abuse that came with childhood love has been internalized as self-attack, and adult love triggers the self-attack mechanism. This is the form Will demonstrates in the Good Will Hunting scene when he breaks up with Skyla and becomes visibly angry at himself.

2. **Toward the lover.** The person who is loved well becomes combative with the lover. They pick fights, find faults, find ways to make the relationship hard. The lover is loving, the person is being loved, the body is producing the conflict that completes the original pairing. The person may not know why they keep fighting with someone who is being good to them.

3. **Toward the coach or therapist.** The person being met with open-hearted attention starts trying to provoke a reaction. They test the limits, withhold, criticize the work, deflect. The hostility is the abuse-response activated by the contact. This is what the Good Will Hunting therapist is preparing for — and what eventually arrives when Will hits him.

In all three forms, the abuse-response is not a moral failure. It is the wiring doing what wiring does. The body is producing the pattern it was taught is the shape of love.

## Why the Body Cannot Just "Trust the New Love"

A common assumption is that with enough good love, the body should eventually update and accept that love is different now. The principle says this is not automatic. The wiring is not an opinion that can be updated by counter-evidence. The wiring is a trained response that fires before the conscious mind has time to evaluate the situation.

The good love arrives. The body produces the abuse-response. The conscious mind, after the fact, may say "wait, this love is different." But the response has already happened. The body has already pushed away, attacked, withdrawn, or self-attacked. The new love has been treated as the old love — by reflex, before reflection.

For the new love to land differently, the body has to be given a different experience often enough that the wiring updates. The mechanism is not a single moment of insight; it is a sustained pattern of contact in which the new love is not met with the abuse-response, and the body slowly learns that the response is no longer required.

The sustained contact requires the lover (or coach, or partner) to not react to the abuse-response with the abuse the wiring expects. When Will hits the therapist, the therapist holds the open heart. If the therapist had hit back, withdrawn, or shamed Will, the wiring would have been confirmed. The therapist's non-reactivity is the new information that the body needs to begin updating. See [[keep-the-heart-open-and-repeat-the-truth]] for the structural move that this principle requires.

## The Generalization Beyond Abuse

The principle is not specific to abuse. It applies to whatever the original love-configuration was. Several common patterns:

- **Love came with conditionality.** The child was loved when performing. In adulthood, the person cannot receive love that is not earned. They keep performing for the love. They cannot rest into unconditional regard. The performance is the response love produces.

- **Love came with engulfment.** The child was loved by a parent who needed something from them. In adulthood, the person experiences love as a demand for self-loss. They withdraw from love to preserve themselves. The withdrawal is the response love produces.

- **Love came with abandonment.** The child was loved and then the love disappeared (death, divorce, depression, addiction). In adulthood, the person braces for the disappearance the moment love arrives. They may engineer the leaving themselves to control the timing. See [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]] for the engineered-leaving form.

- **Love came with criticism.** The child was loved by a parent who also constantly evaluated. In adulthood, the person feels evaluated in any love-context. They may produce evaluations of themselves to preempt the evaluator. The pre-criticism is the response love produces.

- **Love came with absence.** The child was technically loved but not contacted. In adulthood, the person cannot recognize love as love. They are looking for the absence they recognize. They may report not feeling loved while being deeply loved, because the love does not match the texture the body recognizes.

In each case, the body is doing what it learned. The childhood pattern is the template. Adult love arrives and is fit to the template. The person experiences the template, not the love.

## The Implication For Coaches and Partners

The principle has direct operational consequences.

**Expect the abuse-response.** A coach working with a love-equals-abuse client should expect that as the contact deepens, the client will try to provoke an abuse-response. This is not a sign that the work is failing. It is a sign that the work is reaching the wired layer. The coach's job is to not produce the abuse. The non-production is the new information.

**Do not retreat from the contact when the response shows up.** The instinct, when the client becomes combative, is to back off. Back-off is sometimes appropriate (when the trust hasn't been built, when the nervous system is overwhelmed). But the back-off can also be the move that confirms the wiring: "love arrived, abuse was incoming, now love is leaving." The retreat completes the original pattern. The contact maintained through the response is the variable that the original pattern did not include.

**Name the pattern, gently.** The client often does not know they are running the pattern. Naming it does not interrupt it (the wiring is faster than the naming), but it gives the client a frame for what they are about to feel themselves do. "You may notice that as I get warmer, you want to push me away. That's the wiring. It doesn't mean we have to stop."

**Hold the non-reactive stance for longer than feels natural.** The wiring updates slowly. The new information has to arrive many times. The coach who can hold the non-reactive stance for many sessions is the coach who can let the wiring update.

For partners (rather than coaches), the principle is harder. A partner is not professionally trained to absorb the abuse-response without reacting. A partner has their own wiring. A partner has their own limits. The principle does not say partners should accept ongoing abuse from their loved one as a fix-it move. It says that some of what is showing up in adult love is the trained response, and that the work to update the wiring takes specific conditions — often a coach or therapist who can hold the contact without being damaged by the response.

## The Inversion: Choosing Partners Who Confirm the Wiring

The principle also explains a common selection pattern. The person who learned love = abuse may, instead of receiving good love and producing the abuse-response, simply choose partners who deliver the abuse directly. The body recognizes those partners as familiar; the wiring is being honored. The person calls this "chemistry" or "spark," but the chemistry is recognition. The body knows this love.

Partners who would deliver good love do not produce the recognition. The body categorizes them as boring, lacking spark, missing something. The person walks away from them in search of the recognized love. The search is for the wiring's confirmation.

The work to undo the selection pattern is the same as the work to receive good love: the body has to update. Until the body updates, the selection mechanism keeps producing the partners the wiring recognizes. See [[i-keep-chasing-emotionally-unavailable-people]] for one common form of this dynamic.

## What This Principle Replaces

The principle replaces three common but inaccurate frames:

1. **"They don't really want love."** The person wants love. They do not have a body that can yet receive it. The wanting and the receiving are different operations.

2. **"They need to learn to trust."** The framing of trust as something the conscious mind decides to do misses the wiring. The conscious mind can decide to trust. The body keeps producing the trained response. Trust is what happens when the body has been given enough new information to update, not what the conscious mind agrees to.

3. **"They are pushing love away."** The push-away is real, but it is not deliberate. The push-away is the trained response to love. The person is not refusing love; they are producing what their body was taught to produce when love arrives.

The principle redirects all three: from interpretation of intent to description of wiring, from cognitive update to embodied update, from blame for the response to understanding of its origin.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not say all difficult responses to love are childhood-imprint responses. Some difficult responses are appropriate present-tense reactions to actual current conditions (the partner is not actually safe, the love being offered actually has strings attached, the contact actually is intrusive). The principle is about the case where the love is reasonably good and the response is reliably negative. When current conditions are also bad, the work is more complicated and involves discriminating present-tense reality from imprint-driven reaction.

The principle also does not say all imprints are negative. A person whose childhood love was abundant, safe, and steady will have a body that recognizes good love and receives it. The principle is generally about the body's pattern-matching, not specifically about damage. Good imprints produce good reception. The principle is most operationally useful for cases where the imprint is one the person wants to outgrow.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with structural clarity. The frame is not blaming or pathologizing. The person is not failing at love; the wiring is doing what wiring does. The matter-of-fact tone is part of what makes the principle land — the client can hear the description without it being weaponized against them.

The principle is also delivered with implicit warning. Joe notes that the therapist in the scene "really thinks about whether he's going to share this information because at some point it may be used against him." The principle is operationally consequential for the coach: this is what you are walking into, do not be surprised when it arrives, hold your seat when it does.
