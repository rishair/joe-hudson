# Hierarchy Collapses Support

## The Principle

A [[support-system]] functions because accountability runs sideways between peers. When the group becomes about one person — by their seniority, their charisma, the others' deference — the support function disappears. What remains is a hierarchy or an accountability process, but the felt support is gone.

"When it becomes that it's about one person, then the support system goes away completely. It becomes more of a hierarchy or it's more of an accountability process, but the support feeling of it really goes away." -- Joe Hudson, How To Build A Great Support System

## The Sports Team Analogy

The clearest case: a team where everyone is accountable to the team performs better than a team where everyone is accountable to the coach. Two surface descriptions; entirely different functions underneath.

"Imagine a sports team that everybody felt accountable for their performance to the team, or everybody felt accountable to the performance to the coach. You know, the one that feels accountable to the team is gonna do a lot better." -- Joe Hudson

The reason is structural. In the coach-accountability version, every member's attention is oriented upward, toward whether the coach is satisfied. Lateral attention — what teammates need, what teammates are noticing, what teammates are asking for — is suppressed because it is not what the system rewards. The lateral channel where support flows is closed.

In the team-accountability version, every member's attention is partially oriented sideways. The members notice each other, support each other, hold each other to the shared aim. The lateral channel is open. Support actually happens.

## The Business Default

The default in business is the coach-accountability mode. Members of a team think of themselves as accountable to the boss, not to each other. The team meeting feels like a series of reports upward rather than a peer container.

"In business, for instance, oftentimes what is supposed to be our support system is just other employees and we all feel accountable to the coach, right? We just feel accountable to the boss. Oftentimes." -- Joe Hudson

This is why most workplaces do not function as support systems even when they have all the surface features (shared work, regular meetings, shared time). The accountability direction is wrong. Fixing the surface features (more team building, more all-hands, more lunches) does not fix the structural problem. Fixing the accountability direction does — and that fix is usually painful because it requires the leader to give up something the system has been organized around.

## The Drift Toward Hierarchy

Even support systems that start peer-structured tend to drift toward hierarchy if not actively defended. The drift happens through several routes:

- **The most senior member is asked too often.** Their answer becomes the default. Other members stop offering their own answers. The group becomes a one-teacher container.
- **The most charismatic member is followed too easily.** Discussion converges on their framing before other framings have been heard.
- **The founder of the group is treated as its leader.** The founding role calcifies into a permanent authority position.
- **The most successful member is deferred to.** Their success in one domain (which may not be the domain of the group) gets imported as overall authority.

Each of these is normal. None is fatal if named and corrected. The remedy is structural: rotate leadership, make the agreements about how decisions get made explicit, name the drift when it happens, refuse to install a permanent leader. See [[become-unnecessary]] for the version of this principle applied to the teacher in a community of practice.

## Why People Pull Toward Hierarchy Anyway

The peer structure is less familiar and more uncomfortable than the hierarchy. People resist it.

"Power struggles, w n sù, and a whole bunch of other things, which is why the agreements are so important. So if you don't have a series of principles or agreements of this is how we interact in the support group, what happens is that somebody will feel uncomfortable because they're, who the fuck's in charge? And then somebody will try to be in charge and that won't work." -- Joe Hudson

The discomfort is real. Without explicit agreements, the discomfort produces a power vacuum, which produces an attempt by someone to fill it, which produces resistance, which produces a power struggle. The agreements close the gap. With agreements in place, the question "who is in charge?" stops being live, because the answer is "the agreements are." People can relax into the lateral support without the structural uncertainty.

## Leadership That Doesn't Collapse Support

Leadership is not absent from a healthy support system; it rotates and serves.

"There might be some leadership, don't get me wrong, or the leadership is best if it changes hands. Oh, that— oh, I'm taking this on, they lead for a while. I'm taking this on and I lead for a while. But it's that hierarchy being gone, very effective." -- Joe Hudson

Rotating leadership is project-shaped. A member takes the lead for a specific phase or initiative; another takes the next. The leadership is a function, not a status. Members move into and out of the lead role without changing position in the group.

The contrast with a standing leader: a standing leader is treated as having authority outside of any specific function. Their opinion carries weight regardless of which question is on the table. The rotation prevents this accumulation by making leadership domain-bound and time-bound.

## What This Implies for the Person Who Notices the Drift

If you are inside a group that is drifting toward hierarchy:

- Name it. The drift survives by being unnamed. Calling it out — "I notice we keep deferring to X" — is often enough to shift it.
- Propose rotation. If there is a coordinating role, propose that it rotate.
- Solicit the quietest voices. The drift is partly produced by some members not speaking. Asking them directly opens the lateral channel.
- Check the agreements. If there are no agreements, that is the first work. If there are agreements but they have eroded, that is the work.
- Be willing to leave. A group that cannot self-correct from the drift is no longer the support system you signed up for. Leaving with honor preserves both you and the possibility that the group will eventually correct.

If you are the person being deferred to:

- Refuse the role explicitly. Compliment other members' contributions. Ask questions instead of giving answers. See [[compliment-people-to-become-the-authority]] for the related move that, paradoxically, undoes the standing-leader dynamic by lifting others.
- Step back. Skip a meeting. Stop being available as the default voice.
- Name what you see. "I notice the group keeps looking to me. Let's talk about that." The naming is itself the structural correction.

## Tone

Structural, not moralizing. Joe is not saying hierarchy is bad. He is naming what hierarchy does to a support function: it collapses it. The principle is descriptive. The implication is design.
