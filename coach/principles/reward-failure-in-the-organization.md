# Reward Failure in the Organization (Reward the Bold Attempt, Not Just the Outcome)

## The Principle

If you want innovation, you have to reward the boldest failures. Not metaphorically. Materially. The person who tried the bold thing and had it not work gets recognized, celebrated, and protected. The leader who does not reward the bold failure is selecting, by attrition, for employees who never attempt the bold thing.

Innovation has a cost. The cost is the attempts that did not work. Refusing to pay that cost does not produce innovation. It produces the absence of attempts.

"Reward failure. I've seen this in companies who had the biggest failure, like the most bold failure, who's the one who tried something that. And actually reward that. If you truly understand this, that is not a scary thing to do. That is a no-brainer. Like, you want innovation from your company, and you know that innovation is going to cost you money. This is the way it costs you money, is people trying stuff." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

## Why Most Companies Get This Wrong

Most companies say they want innovation. Most companies have performance review structures that punish missed targets, missed quotas, and missed budgets. The two signals are incompatible. The performance review signal is louder because it is what determines pay, promotion, and retention. The innovation signal is what gets mentioned at all-hands.

Employees update on the louder signal. They optimize for not missing targets. The bold attempt -- which by definition has a substantial chance of missing the target -- is selected against. The employees who would have made the bold attempts either learn not to, or leave.

The remedy is structural: actual material rewards for the boldest failures. Not "we don't punish failure" -- that is a non-signal. Actual celebration, actual recognition, actual upside for the person who tried something big and had it not land.

## What Rewarding the Bold Failure Looks Like

Three concrete forms in the transcript:

**The boldest-failure award.** Some companies Joe has seen literally celebrate the boldest failure of the period. The person who attempted the largest thing that did not work gets named, acknowledged, sometimes given an actual award. The signal is unambiguous: bold failures are how this company makes money long-term.

**No emotional backlash to mistakes.** The leader who walks in after a $50,000 mistake and says "no problem, how are we going to handle it?" with zero emotional charge. See [[50k-mistake-no-backlash]]. The absence of backlash is the reward. The reward signals: this kind of attempt is normal here, not catastrophic.

**Framing attempts as the cost of innovation.** The leader explicitly frames failed attempts as the way innovation gets paid for. "You want innovation from your company, and you know that innovation is going to cost you money. This is the way it costs you money, is people trying stuff." The framing converts the failure from a budget overrun to a line-item investment in innovation.

## The Distinction: Bold Failure vs. Same Mistake Three Times

The principle is not "reward all failure." Joe is explicit about the limit.

"Maybe like a mistake being made 2 or 3 times in a row for no, like, not, that's a whole different thing. Nobody wants to make mistakes. So if they're making a mistake, it means they're most likely trying something. And that's an important part of how we grow as a business." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

The first time a mistake happens, it is data. The second time, still data. The third time, the system has not learned. At that point the failure is no longer a sign of attempted iteration. It is a sign that the learning loop is broken. The right response shifts from celebration to inquiry: what is preventing the learning?

The principle is structural about which failures get rewarded:
- Bold attempts that did not work: rewarded.
- Mistakes that produced learning: rewarded or at minimum un-punished.
- Same mistake repeating: investigated, not celebrated.

The repeated-mistake case is not about character or capability. It is about a broken learning loop. Often the loop is broken because the environment did not give the person time to integrate the prior lesson, or because the relevant feedback never reached them, or because they did not actually try anything different the second time. Each of these is a structural problem to solve, not a person to punish.

## The Frustration Calibration

The leader is not emotionless. Joe is direct: there is frustration, but it is calibrated differently than most leaders calibrate it.

"There'll be anger if I don't see progress being made... If I get frustrated, if I don't see the pace. But if I see someone trying something and it doesn't work, or somebody making a mistake, I'm totally good with it. I'm not good with the fact that that pace isn't being kept. That's what will cause me frustration." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

The frustration tracks pace, not outcome. The leader is not upset when an attempt fails. The leader is upset when the iteration count drops -- when people stop trying things, when the learning loop slows. The frustration signal is calibrated to the actual cost: not the cost of any individual failure, but the cost of not iterating.

This is a hard calibration to install in a leader who has been trained to react to specific failures. The retraining is not "stop feeling frustrated." It is "shift the trigger of the frustration from outcome to pace." The same emotion. Different signal.

## The Front-Door Framing

The principle pairs structurally with framing the iteration value at the front door of the organization. See [[nike-fail-bigger-board]]. The reward structure alone is not enough; the framing has to be visible from day one. New employees need to see the framing before they encounter any other signal about what this company values.

The Nike pushpin sign is the canonical front-door framing. The reward structure (boldest-failure award, no backlash to mistakes, framing as innovation cost) is what operates underneath the framing day-to-day. Both are required. The framing without the rewards becomes wall decoration. The rewards without the framing become confusing -- employees do not know why this leader keeps celebrating things that other leaders punish.

## The Connection to Process Metrics

Rewarding the bold failure is one half of the structural intervention. The other half is tracking process metrics rather than outcome metrics. See [[track-process-metrics-not-outcomes]]. If the system tracks how many phone calls a salesperson makes, the salesperson can be rewarded for the bold attempt that did not produce a sale. If the system tracks only revenue, the bold attempt that did not produce revenue cannot be rewarded -- the data does not show it.

The two principles together: track process metrics so the bold attempts become visible, then reward the bold attempts that did not produce outcome.

## What the Principle Is Not

The principle is not "outcomes do not matter." Outcomes matter. The principle is that the path to consistent outcomes runs through bold attempts, most of which will not work. Punishing the attempts kills the outcomes. Rewarding the attempts produces the outcomes. The outcome focus and the failure-reward structure are not in tension; the failure-reward structure is the long-form path to outcomes.

The principle is also not a recommendation to ignore quality. The bold failure that gets rewarded is the bold attempt at the right thing, executed with care, that did not work. The careless failure is not the bold failure. The two need to be distinguished.

The principle does not apply to safety-critical domains where the cost of failure is human life or unrecoverable harm. In those domains the iteration arena needs to be separate from the live domain. See [[performance-vs-play]].

## Tone

Joe describes this principle with a flat, declarative confidence. The voice is "this is just how it works." There is no salesmanship. The principle is offered as a structural insight: if you want X (innovation), here is what produces X (rewarding bold failure). The conviction comes from having watched companies that do this and companies that do not.
