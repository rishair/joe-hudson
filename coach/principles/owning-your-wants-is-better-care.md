# Owning Your Wants Is Better Care, Not Worse

## The Principle

The common confusion: owning your wants is selfish; making sure the other person is okay is caring. The principle inverts the assumption. Owning your wants is the better care. Hiding them is the failure to care. When you do not state what you want, you have not given the other person the instructions they need to succeed with you.

"A lot of people somehow confuse the fact that, oh, that I -- if I own my wants, I'm not taking care of you. And the reality is, if you own your wants, you are taking care of the person better. And the reason is, I am telling you how to succeed in our relationship. So if I express my wants to you, then you know if it's a match, if you want to join, if you want to give them to me, if you don't want to give them to me. You know how to win. You know how to make me happy. If I don't express those things to you, none of that is there. So you're really not taking care of me very well." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The reframe is exact. The stated want is the recipe for how to make the person happy. Withholding the recipe is not generosity. It is the deprivation of information the other person needs.

## Why the Reframe Lands

People who hide their wants almost always tell themselves they are being considerate. The story is: "I do not want to burden them. I do not want to be demanding. I want them to feel free." Each of these framings positions the hiding as care.

The principle exposes the structural failure. The other person cannot read minds. Without the stated want, they have no way to:

- Tell whether a relationship with this person is something they want
- Calibrate their behavior to actually make this person happy
- Know whether they are succeeding or failing
- Choose freely whether to join, give, or refuse

The withholding produces a relationship in which the other person is operating in the dark. They cannot tell whether what they are doing is welcome or unwelcome. They cannot tell whether they are wanted or merely tolerated. They cannot tell whether the love is landing. This is not generosity. It is the operational form of leaving them alone in the relationship.

## The Agreeableness Trap

The principle is especially important for people (across cultures, more often women on average) who have been socialized into agreeableness. The agreeableness training conflates "find common ground" with "do not own your wants." The first is care. The second is not.

"Being agreeable means that, hey, I want to find the common ground. And that's something that women have a greater tendency to do than men on average. But I can make sure that you're doing okay and care for you and still own my wants. Part of caring is to tell somebody what you want." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The reframe: agreeableness does not require want-hiding. The person who is genuinely agreeable can also own their wants. The conflation of the two is a cultural training, not a structural feature of caring. Untangling the conflation is part of the work for people who learned that caring meant erasing themselves.

## The Gift-Form of the Want

A useful way to land the principle: the stated want is a gift to the recipient. The recipient now has information they can use. They can give freely. They can refuse cleanly. They can be in a real relationship with a real person who has real wants, rather than a phantom relationship with a person whose wants are operating invisibly underneath.

"You know how to win. You know how to make me happy." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The "you know how to win" framing is the principle made operational. The wanted thing is no longer guess-work. The recipient is empowered to actually succeed at the relationship. Refusing to give them this information is refusing to let them succeed.

This is the connection to [[leaders-wants-are-the-organizations-wants]]: at the organizational level, the leader's hidden wants deprive the team of the same information. The team cannot succeed at the leader's wants because they do not know what they are. The same structural failure operates in couples, in friendships, in families.

## What This Resolves

The principle resolves a specific kind of guilt that blocks want-stating. The person believes that stating their want would be selfish, and stating the want would burden the other person. They suppress the want to be a "better" partner or friend. The relationship degrades because the suppression has hollowed it out, but the person attributes the degradation to other causes.

The principle reframes the question. The stating of the want is not selfishness. It is the act that makes care actually possible. The suppression is not generosity. It is the act that has been making care impossible.

Once this lands, the person can state wants without the guilt. The reframe is not "fight the guilt"; it is "see that the guilt was based on a misreading of what care is." The guilt usually fades once the misreading is visible.

## The Reciprocal Form

The principle also implies a reciprocal teaching for the want-receiver. When your partner, friend, or colleague states a want, the move is not to feel demanded-of. It is to receive the gift of information. See [[i-love-that-you-want-that]] for the operational move on the receiving side.

The two principles together describe a relationship that runs on stated wants rather than guessed wants. Both partners can give and receive freely because both know what is actually wanted. The relationship has more love available because it has less guessing-work consuming the available energy.

## Why Hidden Wants Are a Specific Kind of Distrust

A subtle implication. When you hide your wants from someone you love, you are implicitly communicating: "I do not trust you to handle my wants. I do not trust you to be able to choose freely. I do not trust you to refuse cleanly. I do not trust you to deliver what I want without resenting me."

The hiding looks like care; structurally it is the assertion of distrust. The partner who feels this distrust (often without naming it) experiences the relationship as a place where they are not trusted. They cannot say so because the want-hider has framed the hiding as care. The distrust operates underneath the surface and corrodes the relationship over time.

The reframe: stating the want is also an expression of trust. "I trust you to hear this. I trust you to choose. I trust you to say no if you need to. I trust you to give freely if you want to." The trust is part of what the partner is receiving when the want is stated. The withholding deprives them of the trust as well as the information.

## The Doormat Counter-Evidence

A common objection: "I have been the agreeable one, the one who never demanded anything, the one who hid my wants to make everyone happy. And my partner is happy because of it." The principle predicts the opposite. Look at the partner.

"Think about the person who is the biggest doormat in a relationship and then look at their partner and say, are they happy? And the answer is absolutely not. They're probably a curmudgeon. They're probably angry most of the time because they don't feel met. They don't feel like they've got someone they can trust. I can't trust you to even tell me what you want." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The partner of the doormat is rarely happy. They are usually angry, curmudgeonly, distrustful, or distant. The reason is that they cannot feel met by someone who has not let themselves be seen. The doormat's hiding has produced a relationship in which the partner is, in their own way, alone. The agreeableness did not deliver what it promised.

This is the empirical evidence the principle predicts. If hiding wants were care, the cared-for partner would be happier. They are not. The hiding is doing something other than caring.

## What This Is Not

- Not a license to demand. The principle is about stating wants cleanly, not about insisting they be met. See [[wants-vs-partiality]] for the discipline that prevents the stated want from becoming a coercion.
- Not "tell everyone everything." Some wants are appropriate to share, some are not. The principle is about the wants that are operating in the relationship, not about every fleeting want.
- Not a replacement for empathy. Caring for the other person is still part of care. The principle adds that owning your own wants is also part of care, not in opposition to caring for them.
- Not a guarantee the want gets met. The stated want gives the other person the choice to give or not. They may not give. The principle is about the act of stating, not about the response.
- Not anti-agreeableness. Genuine agreeableness — finding common ground, being responsive to others — is fine. The principle is against the version of agreeableness that requires self-erasure.

## Tone

Direct. The reframe is doing significant work — it inverts what most people have been trained to believe about care. The principle should be delivered with weight commensurate with the inversion. Not heavy, not dramatic, but clearly. The "you know how to win" framing is the most useful single line because it lands the operational point.
