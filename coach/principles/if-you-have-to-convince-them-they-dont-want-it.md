# If You Have to Convince People to Buy, You Have Something They Don't Want

## The Principle

Sales is not the art of convincing. Sales is the act of listening to a person, connecting with them, finding a problem they have that you can solve, and offering the solution. If the offering matches an actual problem they actually have, no convincing is required. The decision arises in them, not in your pitch.

If convincing is required, the offering is wrong for that person. They do not actually want what is being offered. The energy spent on convincing is the visible sign that the underlying fit is missing.

"You don't have to convince anybody of anything. If you have to convince people to buy, well then you have something that they don't want, that it's not good for them. And no great business came out of what people don't want." -- Joe Hudson, How Connection Helps You Succeed In Business

## The Reframe of What Sales Is

The standard cultural picture of sales is performance: the slick pitch, the closer, the technique that overcomes objections. Under this picture, the salesperson's job is to bend the customer toward the close.

The reframe: the salesperson's job is to listen, to connect, to understand the customer's actual situation, and to find -- not invent -- the place where what they have to offer solves a problem the customer is already trying to solve. The decision is the customer's. The salesperson's contribution is the connection that makes the actual problem visible enough that the right solution becomes obvious to both parties.

"All sales is, is really helping people solve the problem, get to where they wanna go. And if you can do that, then you're connecting with them. And if you really connect with somebody, they're gonna wanna work with you." -- Joe Hudson, How Connection Helps You Succeed In Business

This applies to every form of sales: fundraising, selling a product, selling a service, selling an idea. The instrument is connection. The mechanism is finding the problem, not manufacturing the want.

## The Convincing-Is-the-Signal Diagnostic

The principle is operationally useful in real time. If you find yourself convincing, the answer is not to convince better. The convincing itself is the data. Either:

- The offering is wrong for this person. Stop convincing. Move on.
- The offering is right but you have not yet found the actual problem the person is trying to solve. Stop convincing. Ask more.
- The offering is right but you have skipped the connection. Stop convincing. Reconnect.

In none of the three cases is "convince harder" the right response. The convincing is the symptom of one of the three upstream issues.

This is the same structural diagnostic as [[management-required-to-launch-predicts-management-required-forever]]. The amount of push to close is data about the fit. If the push is high, the fit is low. The push will continue throughout the customer relationship. Acting on the signal early prevents the long downstream cost.

## Why "Great Businesses Don't Come From What People Don't Want"

The claim is stronger than "sales is easier when people want it." Joe is making a structural claim about what produces a great business at all.

A business built on what people authentically want has a flywheel. Each sale produces a satisfied customer, who tells others, who arrive already half-sold. The connection that closed the original sale propagates as word-of-mouth. The product improves because real users are giving real feedback. The market response reinforces the work.

A business built on convincing has the opposite flywheel. Each sale required push, which means each customer arrived with some doubt that was overcome rather than dissolved. The doubt remains. The customer is less satisfied. The product does not improve because the feedback is contaminated by the buyer's regret about the purchase. The market response erodes the work.

The structural difference compounds over years. The two businesses look similar at the start. They diverge increasingly. By the time the divergence is undeniable, the conviction-based business is in the slow-decline pattern. See [[disconnection-precedes-decline]].

## The Connection Inversion

The standard cultural picture treats convincing as the active move and listening as the passive one. The picture is inverted from how it actually works.

Listening is the active move. The salesperson who listens carefully is doing the actual work of sales -- the work of making the actual problem visible. The convincing salesperson is doing the easier thing of running their pitch. The work feels harder for the listener because attention is taxing in a way that scripted delivery is not.

The same inversion runs through coaching, leadership, parenting, and partnership. The form that looks active (talking, convincing, persuading) is often the lower-leverage version. The form that looks passive (listening, asking, connecting) is the higher-leverage version. See [[follow-the-demand]] for the underlying principle this expresses across business and coaching.

## What Top Performers Actually Do

The data on top-performing salespeople converges on the same point. The best sellers in any company Joe has worked with are the ones who treat the relationship first. They are not the most aggressive closers. They are the ones the customer most wants to talk to next month.

"If you look at the top performers of almost any company, and every company I've ever been in, the top sales performers always treat the relationship first." -- Joe Hudson, How Connection Helps You Succeed In Business

The relationship-first orientation is not a technique. It is what happens when the salesperson actually cares about the customer's outcome more than about closing the deal. The caring is what produces the relationship. The relationship is what produces the sustainable sales. The technique-first orientation produces the opposite: a short-term close at the cost of the relationship.

This is the same principle treated in [[vulnerability-in-the-workplace]]'s sales section. Vulnerability and care are what produce the relationship that produces the sustainable sale. The persona of the slick closer is the visible failure mode the principle warns against.

## What This Rules Out

This principle rules out:

- **The convincing pitch as the central skill of sales.** It may be a skill. It is not the central skill, and reliance on it is the signal of a deeper problem.
- **The "we just need a better salesperson" diagnosis** for a product that is not selling. The problem is more often the offering or the connection, not the closer.
- **Manufactured urgency and us-vs-them framings** as the strategic move. These are the signature techniques of low-fit selling. See [[us-vs-them-as-universal-manipulation-pattern]].
- **The product roadmap that imagines convincing the market.** If the offering requires convincing, the roadmap is starting from the wrong end. Start with what the customer actually wants. See [[follow-the-demand]] and [[serial-ceo-builds-products-through-customer-connection]] for the build-from-the-customer pattern that this principle implies at the product level.

## What This Does Not Mean

This does not mean every customer will immediately recognize they want the offering. Some education is part of sales. The line: education clarifies what the customer is already trying to do. Convincing tries to make the customer want what they were not already trying to do. The first is service. The second is the signal that the fit is wrong.

This also does not mean salespeople should never share what they have to offer. The offering has to be made visible. The principle is about what happens after it is visible: if the customer's response is "I don't see why I need that," the correct response is curiosity, not pressure. Either the connection is incomplete (more listening required) or the fit is wrong (move on). Pressure is the signal that the principle is being violated.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A founder discovers that the product they have been pushing for two years has been failing because they were convincing investors and customers who did not actually want it.
- A salesperson stops trying to close a deal they have been working on for months, listens for the first time, and discovers the customer's actual problem is something else.
- A leader realizes that the "great salesperson" they hired has been producing short-term numbers at the cost of long-term customer relationships.
- A coach watches a client describe their marketing struggle and recognizes the underlying problem is fit, not communication.

## The Salesperson-Proving-Themselves Variant

A common variant: the convincing operation is not just about the product but about the salesperson. The pitch is also a proving — that the salesperson is competent, knowledgeable, worth working with. The proving and the convincing are operating simultaneously, and the buyer is receiving both.

This is captured operationally in the [[proving-instead-of-connecting]] read and structurally in [[proving-yourself-is-agreeing-youre-not-valuable]]. The salesperson's proving installs the doubt about the salesperson's value, while the convincing installs the doubt about the product. The buyer leaves with two doubts they did not arrive with.

The fitness coach in the 2025-10-02 session had been running this dual engine: hundreds of outreaches per day, the leads came in, the leads left. The leads were not leaving because of the fitness training. They were leaving because the salesperson-proving-engine and the product-convincing-engine were transmitting two layers of doubt that the prospect's body responded to before the prospect could analyze the offer.

The diagnostic for the variant: when the principle "if you have to convince, they don't want it" is presented to the salesperson, watch for whether the salesperson hears it as a comment on their product or as a comment on themselves. If they hear it about the product, the convincing-engine is the main issue. If they hear it about themselves (often subtly defensive: "but I have to show value"), the proving-engine is also operating and needs to be addressed alongside.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and structural. Joe is not moralizing about pushy salespeople. The claim is operational: convincing is a signal, the signal is reliable, ignore it at your cost. The framing makes the principle testable. Watch your own sales process for a month. Notice where you find yourself convincing. See what happens when you stop and follow the signal instead.
