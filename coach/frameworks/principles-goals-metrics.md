# Principles, Goals, Metrics (How They Stack to Produce Aligned Action)

## The Framework

Three layers, in this order, doing different jobs:

**Principles** -- how you drive. The internal commitments about how you operate in any area. Independent of any specific situation. The personal constitution. "I don't work with assholes." "I put connection first." Principles are the orientation, the way of being that any specific work will be done from.

**Goals** -- the North Star. Where you are oriented in driving. A specific direction the work is pointed toward, derived from a real want. "I want to build the best company in this category." "I want to reach 10,000 people." Goals give the work direction.

**Metrics** -- the dashboard. The instruments that tell you how you're driving and whether you're getting there. Sales calls made, pipeline moves executed, weight on the scale, money in the account. Metrics tell you what's happening; they do not tell you what to aim at.

The three layers are not interchangeable. Each does a job the others cannot do. The most common dysfunction in goal-setting is collapsing them: treating principles as goals, or treating metrics as goals, or treating goals as principles.

## The Stack in One Sentence

"Principles are the how am I going to be in any area of my life. Goals are where might I be oriented in driving towards following those principles. ... The principle is how do you wanna drive. The goal is the north star, the metrics of the dashboard. It lets you know how you're doing, if you're getting there, what you need to do." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The stack produces alignment when each layer is doing its own job. The principles handle the "how." The goals handle the "where." The metrics handle the "are we getting there." None of them handle each other's job.

## Why Principles Sit Underneath the Goals

Principles set the parameters within which all goals are pursued. A goal that violates the principle is not pursued, regardless of how attractive the goal is. "I don't work with assholes" is the principle. The goal of "earn a billion dollars" cannot be pursued by working with assholes if the principle is held. The principle constrains the goal-set.

This is also what makes alignment possible across an organization. If everyone is operating from shared principles, the goals each person sets within their domain will be implicitly aligned. The principles ensure that the goals fit together even when nobody is coordinating each individual goal.

"If there's alignment and the principles are what ensure alignment in the goals, and then they help you see how you want to live as if you've achieved those goals because you're literally, oh, this is my North Star. This is living as if I've gotten to the place that I want to go. This is how I know that they're in alignment. Those principles really tell us." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The principles also tell you, in any moment, whether you are operating in alignment with the eventual goal. Living as if the goal has been achieved is structurally the same as living from the principles. The principle is the version of the goal that operates today. See [[acting-as-if-already-true]].

## Why Goals Are the North Star Rather Than the Destination

A North Star is not where you are going. A North Star is the fixed point that orients you while you go wherever you go. The goal does the same job. The goal orients the work without specifying the exact destination.

This frame relieves a common tension. People often confuse "the goal" with "the outcome I must arrive at." When the outcome doesn't arrive on the predicted schedule, they treat the goal as failed. The framing is wrong. The goal was the orientation. The actual destination is whatever ends up being arrived at, which is almost always different from the goal and almost always better.

"I've never gotten everything that I've wanted, but I've also never been able to look back and say what I got wasn't a shit ton better than what I wanted." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The goal as North Star did its job. The destination was downstream of the orientation, not predetermined by it. See [[intention-vs-goal]] for the related distinction and [[goal-beyond-the-goal]] for what happens when the orientation points past the immediate target.

## Why Metrics Belong on the Dashboard, Not the Steering Wheel

A dashboard tells you what's happening with the engine. It does not tell you where to drive. The metrics in business -- revenue, conversion rate, hours, pipeline -- are dashboard instruments. They report on whether the work is functioning. They do not tell you what the work is for.

The most common failure mode is confusing the dashboard for the destination. The goal becomes the metric: "I want $12 million in revenue." But $12 million is the dashboard reading, not the destination. The destination is what the $12 million serves -- the impact, the security, the building, the contribution. The $12 million is the strategy to get there.

"Oftentimes people confuse the metrics with the goals. And I think that happens on a personal level and on a business level all the time, where people think the goal is I want to be loved. The metric is how many people are my friends on Instagram. And the metric becomes a goal, and then you're not loved." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The mechanism by which metric-as-goal fails: the metric was designed to measure progress toward a real outcome. When the metric becomes the goal, the work optimizes for the metric. The metric goes up. The underlying outcome does not. The Instagram friend count rises; the love does not. The revenue hits the number; the impact stays flat. See [[dont-confuse-the-metric-with-the-goal]] for the full anti-pattern treatment.

## The Same Failure Inside a Relationship

The metric-as-goal collapse also happens at the dyadic scale.

"Or is my wife happy with me right now? ... Every moment. ... Yeah, that's the metric. The metrics don't tell me if I've hit the goal. The metrics just tell me how am I driving, how am I getting there?" -- Brett Kistler / Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

"Is my wife happy with me right now" is a metric. It reports on whether something is going well in the moment. If it becomes the goal, the husband begins managing his wife's mood instead of being a husband. The mood improves intermittently; the marriage hollows out. The goal -- a deeply intimate, mutually grateful marriage -- requires actions that sometimes make the spouse temporarily unhappy. Metric-as-goal optimizes against the actual goal.

The right relationship: the metric reports. The husband uses the metric ("hmm, she's not happy right now") to look at his driving. He does not adjust his goals to optimize the metric. He adjusts his driving to honor the principles in service of the goal.

## How Alignment Works Across the Stack

When the three layers are operating cleanly:

- The principles are stable and rarely change. They are the operating system.
- The goals are reviewed yearly or so. They drift as the wants of the system evolve.
- The metrics are checked weekly or daily. They are the leading indicators.

When the principles are aligned across an organization, the people in different roles set goals within their domain that fit the larger picture without coordination. When the goals are aligned with the principles, the metrics that get tracked are the ones that actually predict the goals being met (rather than vanity metrics or surrogate metrics).

The board-meeting test for alignment, in business: if the board is looking at the same data the CEO uses to run the company, the company is aligned. If the board needs custom decks translating one set of numbers into another, the company is misaligned. See [[board-prep-as-misalignment-signal]] for the structural diagnostic.

## How Alignment Works Internally

The same framework operates inside a single person. The personal version:

- Principles are how the person operates regardless of domain. ("I follow my body's signals." "I do not lie to myself." "I put connection first.")
- Goals are what the person is oriented toward in specific domains. (Health, work, relationship, creative life.)
- Metrics are how the person knows day-to-day whether they are operating well. (Sleep, energy, the quality of conversations, what the body is signaling.)

The internal misalignment Joe specifically names: the voice in the head ("the board") issues goals that the rest of the system does not endorse. The metrics get measured, the goals get attempted, but the principles are absent or unconscious. The person grinds without alignment. See [[the-inner-board]] for the full treatment of the internal misalignment.

The internal version of the alignment work: surface the principles, derive the goals from what the system actually wants, design the metrics to measure the right things, and check the dashboard regularly to adjust the driving without changing the destination.

## The Sequence of Discovery

In practice, the layers are not always discovered in order. Many people start by knowing their metrics ("I want $10M in the bank"), then have to back into the goal it serves ("what is the $10M for?"), and finally into the principles that the goal is in service of ("how do I want to operate as I move toward it"). The discovery direction is bottom-up.

The discovery is structurally the same as [[need-behind-the-want]]: ask what the metric is for, then ask what the goal is for, then ask what the principle is. Each layer reveals the layer underneath. At the bottom is usually a basic principle the person already lives by but has not articulated.

Once articulated, the stack inverts. Now the principles set the goals, the goals set the metrics, the metrics report on the daily work. The stack runs top-down once the bottom-up discovery has surfaced what was already there.

## What This Implies for Building Companies

Three operational implications for organizations:

1. **Spend time on principles before goals.** Principles take longer to surface and they constrain everything downstream. A company with clear principles can re-derive goals as conditions change. A company with goals but no principles has to start over each time strategy shifts.

2. **Set goals from the want, not from the metric.** The goal is not the number; the goal is what the number serves. Track the number, but make sure everyone knows what it's for.

3. **Design metrics that measure the actual goal, not the proxy.** Friend count on Instagram is a proxy for love. Revenue is a proxy for impact. The proxies are useful as instruments but dangerous as goals. The work is to keep the proxy in the dashboard role.

For the related leadership treatment, see [[track-process-metrics-not-outcomes]] (the metric design problem at the team level) and [[principles-free-you]] (the principle layer as the source of empowered decision-making).

## What This Implies for Personal Work

Three operational implications for individuals:

1. **Articulate the principles.** They are running you anyway. Surfaced, they can be examined and revised. Unsurfaced, they are inherited from childhood and run the show without consent. See [[unconscious-principles-run-your-life]].

2. **Set goals from the system, not from the head.** The voice in the head will issue goals all day. The question is whether the system endorses them. See [[the-inner-board]] and [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]].

3. **Use metrics as feedback, not as identity.** When the metric becomes the self-worth measure ([[i-need-a-scorecard]]), the whole stack collapses. The metric is information about driving. It is not information about whether you are okay.
