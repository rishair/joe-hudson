# The Three Categories of Downregulation (Breath, Sensation, Attention)

## The Framework

There is an effectively infinite number of ways to downregulate the nervous system in the moment. They sort into three operational categories:

1. **Breath.** Practices that change the rhythm, depth, or quality of the breath. The breath has a direct line to the parasympathetic nervous system through the vagus nerve and through the regulation of carbon dioxide; changing the breath changes the system within seconds.

2. **Sensation.** Practices that put attention on sense-data — usually on the outside of the body. The sensory apparatus is rich; once attention goes to it, the system has something present and real to engage with, and the thought-generated activation begins to quiet.

3. **Attention.** Practices that redirect attention away from the foreground content (the stressor, the thought, the worry) toward a different field — silence between words, ambient sound, peripheral vision, the space around the body. These do not require modifying breath or generating sensation; they only require pointing attention somewhere else.

"There is literally an infinite number of ways to do it... and they come in about 3 categories. One is breath... another one that's not breath-related is sensation-related... I call it coming to your senses." -- Joe Hudson, How To End 60,000 Negative Thoughts a Day

## In Practice

### Breath Category

The breath category contains many specific practices. A small sample:

- **5.5-second or 6-6 breath.** Breathe in for 6 seconds, breathe out for 6 seconds (or 5.5 and 5.5). The lengthened breath is what downshifts; the count just provides a structure.
- **Box breathing.** Inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Used widely in tactical and military contexts because it works under high activation.
- **Humming on the exhale.** See [[humming-exhale-for-vagal-tone]]. Adds nitric oxide release and direct vagal vibration to the lengthened-exhale mechanism.
- **Attention on the breath at the nostrils.** Just noticing how the breath feels going in and out of the nose. No modification, only attention.

All of these route the regulation through the breath's relationship with the vagus nerve and the CO2 system. They differ in mechanism details (humming adds nitric oxide; box breathing adds breath-holding to maintain CO2; nostril-attention adds nothing, just observation). The common feature is breath as the medium.

### Sensation Category

See [[coming-to-your-senses]] for the canonical practice. The category contains:

- Putting a hand on a surface and feeling the texture
- Wiggling toes and feeling them against the shoe or floor
- Feeling air on skin
- Rubbing thumbs together slowly
- Feeling the weight of the body in the chair
- Touching the face

The general form: direct attention to sense-data on the outside of the body. Created sensation (rubbing thumbs) works as well as found sensation (air on skin). What matters is that the attention has somewhere external and present to land.

### Attention Category

The attention category often gets missed because it requires no action — no breath modification, no created sensation. Just a shift in what attention is doing.

- **Listen to the silence around the words.** When someone is speaking, instead of listening to the words, listen to the higher frequencies and lower frequencies that are silent right now. Pay attention to those.
- **Notice the space around the body.** Attention on the room behind, above, around — without moving.
- **Soften the gaze and let the visual field be ambient rather than focal.** Peripheral vision activates the parasympathetic nervous system through a different pathway than breath or touch.
- **Find sounds at the edges of hearing rather than what is in the foreground.**

The pattern: attention has been narrowly focused on the stress-content. Redirecting attention to a wider, more ambient, or sense-adjacent field produces the downshift.

## Why the Categorization Matters

People who learn one downregulation practice often think they know "the" downregulation move. The categorization reveals there are three families, each with its own access point. This matters because:

- **Different stressors yield to different categories.** A person whose stress is showing up as racing breath responds best to breath. A person whose stress is showing up as cognitive looping may respond better to sensation or attention.
- **Different bodies respond differently to each category.** Some nervous systems calm fastest through breath; others through touch; others through attention shifts.
- **In moments when one category is unavailable, another may be.** In a meeting where humming would be conspicuous, rubbing thumbs together or shifting attention to the silence between sentences works.
- **Variety prevents the practice from becoming rote.** A single practice done constantly can lose effectiveness — both because the body adapts and because the practice becomes part of the management identity. The three categories give the system room to rotate.

The point of naming three categories is operational: the person has the whole toolkit, not just the one practice they happened to learn.

## The Categories Share One Mechanism Underneath

The categories look different but they share a structural mechanism: each of them moves attention away from the thought-content that has been producing the activation, and onto something else (breath, sensation, or another attention-field). The downshift is not produced by the breath, sensation, or attention itself — it is produced by attention no longer being on the thought-content.

This is why the practices work even when the content of the stress is severe. The system is not being asked to think differently about the stress. The system is being given something else to attend to. The change in attention is what allows the threat-response to begin quieting.

It is also why "more" of any one practice does not necessarily produce more downshift. Once attention is off the thought-content, the system has access to its natural regulatory capacity. Continuing the practice past that point does not deepen the downshift; it just maintains the attentional redirect.

## What This Framework Is Not

This framework names the toolkit for in-the-moment downshift. It is not:

- A solution to the underlying causes of chronic stress. See [[downregulation-handles-the-symptom-not-the-source]].
- A replacement for emotional inquiry. See [[breath-to-self-regulate-instead-of-feel]] for the related anti-pattern of using downregulation to skip the feeling.
- A constant-management protocol. See [[over-regulating-nervous-system-creates-stress]] for what happens when these tools become the always-on default.
- The whole of nervous-system work. The downregulation tools are one piece. The activation-release-rest cycle ([[activation-release-rest-cycle]]) is the larger frame these tools serve.

These tools are scalpels, not surgery. They are exquisitely useful for the moments they are designed for. They are not the whole work.

## The Three Categories in the Coaching Move

The framework is operationally useful in coaching in several ways:

- **Offering a person something they can do right now.** When a coachee presents as overwhelmed and there is no time for a longer process, naming the three categories and offering one specific practice from each gives them immediate options.
- **Diagnostic of what category a person is most defended against.** A person who runs all the breath practices but never the sensation practices may be avoiding the body. A person who runs sensation but never attention may be in too much body-focus.
- **Variety prescription for someone who has been doing one practice and losing effectiveness.** Add a practice from a different category.
- **Coverage of the in-the-moment toolkit before deeper work.** Make sure the person has access to all three categories so that whatever situation arises, they have something available.

## Tone

Operational. The categorization is delivered as a useful map, not as a profound teaching. The teaching itself is humble — "there is literally an infinite number of ways to do it" — and the three categories are positioned as practical groupings of the infinite set, not as the truth of how regulation works.
