# The Three Games: Outer, Inner, Team

## The Framework

When AI takes over the work that used to be done by intelligence and learnable skill, the differentiator between people and between teams becomes three capacities, each one a distinct "game" with its own training, its own failure modes, and its own metrics.

1. **The Outer Game**: How you take care of yourself externally -- body, sleep, food, exercise, endurance over long timelines.
2. **The Inner Game**: How you talk to yourself in the middle of the work -- whether you can play the game without getting in your head about it.
3. **The Team Game**: How well the people you work with actually work together -- whether the hard conversations get had or get avoided.

"There's 3 components that are going to help a person be successful or a team be successful. The first component is how well they can play what I would call the outer game... The inner game is how you talk to yourself... But then the third part is the game of the team. How well does that team work together?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

A person or a team that is strong at all three outperforms a person or team that is strong at only one or two. Most environments train one of the three at the expense of the others. The framework names the gap so it can be closed.

## The Outer Game

The outer game is whether you can keep going.

Can you eat well? Can you exercise? Can you feel good in your body? Can you sustain the long-term effort that the work requires -- writing the book, building the company, raising the kids? Do you have the endurance for the marathon, not just the sprint?

The outer game is the most visible of the three. People know whether they are sleeping or not. The failure mode of the outer game is treating it as separate from the work -- as a chore that competes with the work for time. The reality is that the outer game is what makes the work sustainable. The leader who works 996 (9am-9pm, six days a week) and treats sleep and exercise as luxuries is degrading the work, not amplifying it.

The outer game is also the most easily delegated to discipline. It can become an obligation, an "I should." The version of the outer game that actually works is the one that is connected to felt experience -- you exercise because the body asks for it, you sleep because the body asks for it, you eat the food that lets the body do what you need it to do. The discipline-only version of the outer game eventually breaks.

## The Inner Game

The inner game is whether you can do the thing you already know how to do, without getting in your own way.

The reference is Timothy Gallwey's *The Inner Game of Tennis*: most people who consistently mess up their forehand or backhand do not have a technique problem. Their body knows how to do it. The problem is in their head -- the self-critical voice, the second-guessing, the rehearsal of the previous failure, the worry about the next one. The inner game is the capacity to play the game without the head running interference.

"There's this great book called The Inner Game of Tennis. It talks about how basically most people who keep on messing up, like, their forehand swing or their backhand swing, it isn't because they don't know how to do it. Their body does know how to do it. It's because they get in their head about it. So how much can you not get in your head when you're playing the game and just play the game? That's the inner game." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The inner game is the most under-trained of the three in standard education and standard work environments. Most schools and most jobs train the technical skill (the equivalent of the forehand) without ever training the capacity to access it under pressure. The result is highly skilled people who choke at the moments that count.

The inner game is what most of the AOA methodology is training. The work on emotions, on the inner critic, on the patterns that hijack decision-making, on the capacity to be present -- all of it is inner-game work. The link to the outer environment is direct: the leader who has done the inner work makes cleaner decisions under pressure, has access to their full capacity in the high-stakes moment, recovers faster from setbacks, can be in the work without bracing against it. See [[emotional-cycling-as-superpower-in-exponential-change]] for the specific velocity advantage in exponential environments.

## The Team Game

The team game is whether the people you work with can have the hard conversations.

Every major company decline can be traced to a conversation that didn't happen. The CTO and the CEO had a conflict that didn't get resolved. The hard decision didn't get made. The pivot was needed and didn't happen because someone wouldn't say the thing. The cost compounds: three weeks, three months, three years.

"We've all been in companies where the hard conversation wasn't had. It cost 3 years, 3 months, 3 weeks, hundreds of thousands of dollars because the hard decision wasn't made, because the conflict between the CEO and the COO wasn't resolved, because they wouldn't have the hard conversation that they needed to pivot. Every single massive downturn in a company or even long-term decline of a company is based on teams not working together well." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The inverse is also true: when the team game is strong, the team has a massive competitive advantage. The decisions happen at the speed they need to happen. The pivots get executed. The friction that would otherwise stall the work gets metabolized. The team that can address the difficult thing over and over outperforms the team that can't, by orders of magnitude over any meaningful timeline.

The team game requires the inner game in each individual. A person who cannot tolerate their own discomfort cannot stay in the room for the hard conversation. The team game is the inner game expressed at the group level. See [[run-toward-friction]] for the orientation that makes the team game possible, and [[approach-the-pink-elephant]] for the specific move of naming what everyone is avoiding.

## How The Three Games Interact

The three games are not independent. Each one supports the others, and weakness in one degrades the others.

- The leader with a strong outer game and weak inner game has the energy to keep going but cannot make clean decisions under pressure.
- The leader with a strong inner game and weak outer game has access to their full capacity in moments but burns out before the work completes.
- The leader with strong inner and outer games but a weak team has the personal capacity but cannot translate it into organizational outcomes -- the team's avoidance limits what the leader can do alone.
- The leader with a strong team game but weak inner game becomes the bottleneck the team is avoiding -- their lack of self-awareness produces the situations the team has to manage around.

The all-three case is the high-leverage version. The leader who has trained all three has the personal capacity, the access to it under pressure, and the team that can move on it. This is the configuration the era is asking for, and it is the configuration the small high-stakes teams of the AI era are being selected for.

## Why The Three Games Become the Differentiator Now

For most of recent history, the differentiator was intelligence, hard work, or domain knowledge. These could be hired or purchased. The leader of a ten-thousand-person company did not need to be personally strong on all three games -- they could buy more headcount to compensate for the weakness.

The small-team era removes the compensation. A team of two or three hundred running what used to be a ten-thousand-person company cannot afford weakness in the team game; there is no buffer. The pace of the environment cannot afford weakness in the inner game; there is no time to recover from a leader's emotional collapse. The intensity of the work cannot afford weakness in the outer game; there is no slack to absorb burnout.

The framework is operational. It names what to train and where the leverage is. The training in each game has been described elsewhere in the compendium; this article names the structure those trainings sit inside.

## What the Framework Does Not Say

It does not say there is a single right way to play each game. The outer game looks different for different people. The inner game looks different at different stages of development. The team game looks different for different team sizes and cultures. The framework names the three categories of work; the specific practices within each one belong to the rest of the methodology.

It does not say weakness in one game is fatal. It is fixable. The framework is a diagnostic tool, not a verdict.

It does not say the three games are the only things that matter. Strategy still matters. Product still matters. Capital still matters. The framework is about the differentiator among people and teams who have access to roughly comparable strategy, product, and capital -- which describes most high-stakes environments in the AI era.

## How To Use The Framework Diagnostically

For an individual or a team, walk through each game:

- **Outer game**: How sustainable is the pace? What signs of breakdown are showing up (sleep, body, illness, depletion)? What is the felt cost of the current schedule?
- **Inner game**: What is the quality of the self-talk during high-stakes moments? Where does the head get in the way of the body's capacity? What is the recovery time after a setback?
- **Team game**: What conversations are getting deferred? What conflicts are being managed around? What decisions are not getting made because someone won't say the thing? What is the cost of the avoidance, measured in time and money?

The weakest game is the highest-leverage place to invest. The leader who is strong on inner and outer but weak on team will get more from one improvement in team work than from five more improvements in the games they are already strong in.
