# Head, Heart, Gut

## Definition

Three distinct intelligences a person has access to. Each speaks differently. Each listens differently. Each is good at different things. Most Americans default to the head. Some default to the gut. Some default to the heart. Each is incomplete on its own. The skill is to access all three and to recognize they are pointers at one larger system, not separate organs.

The framework is a pedagogical convenience. The actual intelligence is the whole system. "I don't think our intellect just operates in our brain, and I don't think our love just operates in our heart, and I don't think that our emotions just operate in the gut. It is like they are just good pointers." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

## The Three Voices

### Head
The intellect strategizing. Decides whether something is good or bad, right or wrong, useful or useless. Wants to debate, sharpen, see clearly. Binary in nature. Excellent at puzzle-solving once postulates are set. See [[feel-over-figure]].

Heady speech is facts and data. A head introduction is what you do for work, where you live, your credentials. Heady listening is binary — agreeing or disagreeing, judging right or wrong.

### Heart
Less binary. Cares more about who you are than what you do. A heart introduction surfaces who you are as a person. Heart listening drops the yes/no — "it does not mean that you believe everything that I am saying, but it does not mean you do not either. The idea of judging something to be right or wrong is far less important to the heart." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

### Gut
The body's intelligence. Reports on the present condition: nervous, scared, hungry, alive, restless. A gut introduction surfaces what you are actually feeling right now. Gut listening picks up what is happening between people that words do not carry — the social nervous system reading.

## The Exercise

A simple practice that demonstrates the three intelligences: introduce yourself three times.

1. "What does my head want to say?" — listen to the brain. Speak from there.
2. "What does my heart want to say?" — listen to the heart. Speak from there.
3. "What does my body want to say?" — listen to the whole body. Speak from there.

The content shifts each time. The exercise reveals that the three are not the same and that the person has been habitually speaking from one.

"The first head introduction was just a lot of facts and data about who we are. And then speaking from the heart, simply from that instruction, speak from the heart, it resulted in a lot more about who we are as people as an introduction. And then speaking from the gut, it really just resulted in a lot more about how we actually feel right now." -- Brett Kistler describing the group exercise

## You Can Listen From Each Too

Speaking is only half. Listening from head, heart, or gut produces different conversations.

- Listening from the head: tracking the argument, deciding if it is right.
- Listening from the heart: receiving without immediate judgment, open to the person.
- Listening from the gut: picking up the social and emotional substrate underneath the words.

The same talk lands differently depending on which intelligence is receiving.

## Each Has Its Failure Mode

All three intelligences can lie. None is the irrevocable truth. See [[body-lies-as-much-as-mind]].

- Head failure: cannot see its own emotional postulates. Believes it is reasoning when it is rationalizing.
- Gut failure: believes its stories without questioning them. Walks into a room, feels anger, and is certain it was wronged.
- Heart failure: confuses care with truth. Sentimentality.

"You see people who are like very much operating from the gut and they have a bias against those operating from their intellect. And you see this in relationships all the time, somebody who's more emotionally led and somebody who's more intellectually led, or at least they think they are." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

## The Asymmetry of the Head-Dominated System

Most Americans default to the head. Cultures that have dismissed emotional intelligence rely heavily on the intellect. The reasoning is usually defensive: emotions caused pain, so emotions need to be managed. The intellect promises control.

A person who is head-dominant has emotions running them but cannot see it. "They have emotions but they don't think they do. They're driving them but they can't recognize it." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The reverse is true for gut-dominant people: they have stories driving them but cannot see the stories. "Maybe they walk into a room and they start to feel anger and so they just believe that the anger means that they've been wronged." (Brett) "That's exactly right. And then they have a story about how they were wronged, but they don't question it." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The work is not to switch dominance. It is to cultivate the underused intelligences until all three are accessible.

## The Path of Learning Has a Trap

A natural step on the path: someone who has been head-dominant discovers the body, becomes overwhelmed by its wisdom, and now treats the body as the absolute truth and the intellect as the enemy.

"I see people who are really intellectual and all of a sudden they understand like, holy crap, my body has all this wisdom. And they start using the body wisdom and then the body becomes the irrevocable truth and direct connection to God and intelligence and wisdom, which is not true either." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The same trap exists for someone who newly discovers the heart. The framework is a corrective to a current imbalance, not a replacement of one master with another.

## Applied to a Single Phenomenon: Anxiety

The framework's utility shows up clearly when applied to a single experience and the three intelligences are asked to report on it separately. Anxiety is a useful test case because each intelligence has a different and equally true definition of what it is:

- The **head** sees anxiety as constant preparation for attack -- the mind scanning for what could go wrong and modulating behavior to prevent it.
- The **heart** sees anxiety as unmet needs -- the same signal mammals get when their needs are not being addressed.
- The **gut** sees anxiety as constriction of life force -- energy that is being clamped down on at the nervous-system level.

Each definition is true. None is complete. A treatment plan that only addresses one will miss what the other two are reporting. See [[anxiety-three-definitions]] for the full development of this application.

The same exercise can be run with any emotion or experience. The three intelligences give three different reports, all valid, that together produce a richer picture than any single one.

## Coaching Use: Speak the Language of the Dominant Intelligence

A coach can meet a person in their dominant intelligence and use that as the entry. "If I'm coaching a CEO who was an athlete, I'll use the body metaphor all the time because they get it. And if I'm talking to a CEO who's had like a really healthy Christian background, I'll speak to the heart a lot because they really get it." -- Joe Hudson, How Your Body Speaks

The cultivated intelligence is the on-ramp. From there, the other intelligences can be developed.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Sitting in a meeting making lists of pros and cons and still cannot decide. The gut and heart have not been consulted.
- Walking into a room and feeling certain about someone's intent without being able to articulate why.
- Saying the right words in a conversation and sensing they did not land.
- Receiving advice from a friend whose argument is perfect but whose presence feels off.
- Being told to "go with your gut" and not knowing what that means.
