# SAVE: The Four Components of Leadership People Want to Follow

## The Framework

SAVE is a four-component model of what a leader provides that makes people want to stay with the organization. The components are operational descriptions of culture, not abstract values. Each is a specific thing the leader either does or does not do that the team registers and responds to.

- **S — Self-development.** People feel that they are better this year than they were last year. Their growth is happening through the work.
- **A — Autonomy.** People feel they are actually in control of their own domain. When they take initiative, it gets followed. When they take leadership, it gets honored.
- **V — Vision.** The leader is acting as if the reality they are working toward is already real, AND they share the context for how decisions get made.
- **E — Esteem.** The way the leader treats people increases their esteem of themselves and of the organization.

"If you do those things, you're a great leader. If you do those things, people want to keep on being with you in the organization." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The framework is offered as a working model rather than a definitive taxonomy. There are other models. What makes this one useful is the specificity of each component — each one names a thing the leader can either be doing or not doing.

## S — Self-Development

The team feels they are growing. The growth is not adjacent to the work — it is happening through the work itself. The leader is creating conditions where being on the team makes the person more capable, more themselves, more developed than they were before.

"S stands for self-development, meaning that if that leader, if in your leadership, in that culture, people feel like they're better this year than they are last year, that they are constantly improving as people, they're going to stay." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The diagnostic: ask team members "are you better this year than last year, because of being here." If the answer is yes, the S is in place. If the answer is no, the S is missing — and over time the people who would grow elsewhere will leave for elsewhere.

The component matters because most high-functioning people are not optimizing solely for compensation. They are optimizing for their own trajectory. The team that supports the trajectory keeps the people. The team that does not supports their departure to a team that will.

## A — Autonomy

People feel they actually have control over their domain. The autonomy is real, not nominal. When a team member takes initiative, the initiative gets followed. When they exhibit leadership in their area, the leadership gets honored. The autonomy is the operating reality, not the stated value.

"The second is autonomy, where people feel like they actually are in control of their own world, that when they do have initiative, it gets followed. When they do take leadership, it's followed. That level of autonomy, you need to create alignment, but you need that level of autonomy for people." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The autonomy is paired with alignment. The alignment is what prevents the autonomy from becoming chaos. The autonomy is what prevents the alignment from becoming control. The two must come together — autonomy without alignment is the team running in different directions; alignment without autonomy is everyone waiting for permission. See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for the organizational design that holds both.

The operational test: when someone on your team has initiative, do you follow them, or do you make them prove the initiative is worth following? Following the initiative IS the autonomy. The proving-it-first frame negates the autonomy regardless of how it is stated.

The Mun-live-podcast example from the same episode demonstrates this concretely. Mun had conviction about doing live podcasts. Joe did not want to do them. He followed her conviction anyway — that act of following her initiative was the autonomy operating, and was itself a leadership move for the broader team to register.

## V — Vision

Vision has two layers, both of which the leader has to provide.

**Layer one: where we are going.** The leader has a sense of the world they are working toward and they can name it. This is the entry-level vision component.

**Layer two: the context for how decisions are made.** The leader does not just declare the destination — they share the texture of their thinking about how to get there. The team is invited into the reasoning, not just the conclusion.

"V is vision. Vision means, oh, here's where we're going to be. This is the world I see that is possible for us. That's like the very beginning of vision. The second part of vision is the context. If you let me share all the context of what makes me do this, because this is where we're headed. So oftentimes most of the miscommunication that happens in an organization, what pisses somebody off with the leaders, they don't actually know what the leader's thinking, how they came to the conclusion. They didn't feel like they were a part of that process. That's where a lot of the rub happens." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The diagnostic for the second layer: when a leader announces a decision, does the team understand the reasoning, or do they just receive the conclusion? The conclusion-only version produces the rub. The reasoning-included version produces the alignment.

**Layer three: acting as if the vision is already true.** The deepest form of vision is not what gets said but what gets embodied. The leader acts as if the reality they are working toward is already present.

"At its purest sense, vision is the leader acting as if the reality that they're looking to live in is already existent. So an example of this, back to Martin Luther King. His vision was equality, that people would be treated equally. He acted as if he were already being treated equally. He acted as if his voice was just as important. So acting as if the thing that you're working towards is already in existence is like a very deep form of vision." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

See [[acting-as-if-already-true]] for the broader principle. The vision in SAVE is the leadership-context application of that principle: the leader acts as if the world they want is real, and the team begins to act from that same stance.

## E — Esteem

Esteem is appreciation. It is the way the leader treats people such that the people end up with more regard for themselves and for the organization. The work increases their sense of their own worth and the worth of being part of the team.

"E is esteem, means appreciation. It means you're treating somebody with respect and appreciate. All the ways that you're working with them increases their esteem of themselves and of the organization." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The component is not "give compliments." It is the broader stance from which the leader treats team members as people whose work matters and whose presence is valued. The compliments are downstream. The diagnostic test: do people leave their interactions with you feeling more or less of themselves? More — the E is present. Less — the E is missing, regardless of stated values.

This connects to [[view]] — the vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, and wonder applied in relationship creates the conditions for esteem to actually be felt. The leader who is using VIEW with the team is, structurally, providing the E whether or not they name it.

## Why The Four Together Make People Stay

Each component independently supports retention. Together, they describe an environment where a person's best version is welcomed, their domain is theirs, the destination is shared and embodied, and they feel valued by being there. The combination is rare. The rarity is why people stay when they find it.

Note the structural implication: the four components are also what produces decentralized leadership. The S develops people into leaders. The A gives them the room to lead. The V gives them the shared direction to lead toward. The E supports the confidence required to take the lead. The framework is not just a retention model — it is a description of how a leader builds an organization where leadership emerges everywhere.

"You'll notice that part of that is that you're following everybody else's leadership because of that autonomy. It's part of how that all works." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

See [[leadership-is-for-everyone]] and [[leadership-emerges-from-confident-direction]] for the principles SAVE supports operationally.

## The Diagnostic Checklist

A leader can use SAVE as a four-question check on their team's culture:

1. **S:** Are my people better this year than last year because of being here?
2. **A:** When my people take initiative, does it actually get followed?
3. **V:** Do my people understand my reasoning, not just my conclusions? Am I acting as if the world we want is already real?
4. **E:** Do my people leave interactions with me feeling more of themselves, or less?

Any "no" answer points to a leadership move that is missing. The move is not to install the missing component as a program. The move is to recognize what is currently happening in its place (often: the leader is the bottleneck, the autonomy is performative, the vision is a deck rather than embodied, the appreciation is sporadic or transactional) and to shift the underlying pattern.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- A leader asking why their team is leaving for less money elsewhere
- A leader who has good intentions about culture but cannot describe what culture actually means operationally
- A founder hiring senior people who underperform because the autonomy is nominal
- A team that is technically aligned but disengaged
- A leader who wonders why their "compliments" do not seem to produce loyalty
- A leader trying to design culture from values statements rather than from operational patterns

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader struggling with retention and reaching for compensation as the lever
- A leader whose team is complying but not committing
- A leader who has a vision deck but cannot get the team to embody it
- A leader who values autonomy in principle but reasserts control in practice
- A leader who has been performing appreciation rather than living the stance from which it flows

## Tone

Joe delivers SAVE as a working model, not a doctrine. The acronym is offered with light hands. Each component is grounded in something operational the leader can actually do. The texture is "here is one model I think about often" rather than "this is the truth about leadership."
