# Three Moves to Save a Relationship

## The Framework

When a relationship is in serious trouble -- when disdain has set in, when one partner has stopped engaging, when the question is whether anything can be done -- there are three moves available. The third move applies when the first two have shown that the other party is not coming back.

The framework is offered in answer to the question "what do you have to say to someone who wants to try to bring a relationship back from the brink." It is a coaching framework, not a marriage-counseling protocol. The moves are for the person doing the asking; they require nothing from the other party.

## The Three Moves

**Move 1: Speak your truth with an open heart.**

Say what is true for you. Not as accusation. Not as ultimatum. With kindness, with the heart open, even when it produces a fight. The first move is to stop hiding the held-back material that has been slowly killing the relationship from inside.

"The first one is speak your truth with an open heart. You want to save a marriage, speak your truth with an open heart, speak your truth with kindness, say what's true for you." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This is the operational form of [[hold-back-from-relationship-is-what-needs-healing]]. The truth-speaking is the surfacing of the material that the partner cannot meet because they have not seen it. The act of surfacing is what gives the relationship anything new to work with.

The qualifier is essential: open heart, with kindness. The truth-speaking that has been weaponized as accusation is not this move. The truth-speaking that has been swallowed for years is not this move. The middle path is bringing the truth out with the heart still open, even when the truth is hard.

**Move 2: Listen to them with VIEW.**

Move into [[view]] -- vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder -- and let everything they have to say be heard. Not managed. Not corrected. Not defended against. Heard, with the recognition that what they are saying is true for them and worth understanding.

"The second thing I'd say is listen to them with view. Let, let all of it be heard. Let connect, move to connection and, and, and see them fully without, without, without taking it personally." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The move includes seeing them as they are without needing them to change. No management. No fix-it. No subtle steering toward what you want them to think or do.

"To see them as they are without needing them to change without any kind of management of them. So again, it's view, it's, it's having them feel seen and not taking it personally." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This is the inverse of the chase. The chase is "I will get you back by working harder." Listening with VIEW is "I will see you fully as you are right now, including the parts that say you are done." The seeing itself is the offer. Whether the partner moves toward it is theirs.

Joe gives a worked example of what this can sound like:

"So yeah, I see that you disdain me right now and you wouldn't put the words in your mouth, but just, oh, I see you right now. I see you're really frustrated with me right now. I see that you want to give up on the marriage and I get why that would be the case. Let's talk about it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The naming includes the hard things. "I see that you want to give up on the marriage" is offered without panic, without manipulation, without softening. The cleanness of the naming is what makes safety for the partner to actually say what they are holding.

**Move 3: If they have given up, grieve.**

The third move is what becomes available when the first two have surfaced the structural fact that the partner is done. The work is no longer about the relationship; it is about completing the grief for the relationship that is ending. See [[cant-chase-someone-into-loving-you]] for the principle that operates here.

"If the person you're with doesn't want to work on the relationship, then grieve. Then you're not -- nothing's going to happen. You can't chase somebody into loving you. It doesn't work that way." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The grief is functional. It releases the energy that was going into the chase. It allows the next phase of life -- whether that is a new relationship, a different relationship with the same person at a different distance, or the long work of integrating what this relationship was -- to actually be available.

See [[mourn-the-breakup-fully]] for the practice of the grief, and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] for the longer-term reason for letting the grief complete.

## Why the Order Matters

The order is important.

**Move 1 first** because without surfacing your held-back truth, the relationship cannot move. Whatever has been hidden has been preventing the partner from meeting you. The truth-speaking is what gives the relationship something new to work with. Skipping to listening before speaking your truth keeps the asymmetric structure in place -- you continue to manage what you reveal while expecting the partner to be transparent.

**Move 2 second** because once your truth is out, the partner needs space to respond. The listening with VIEW is what allows the partner's response to actually be heard rather than processed through your agenda. If the partner is going to come back to the work, the listening is what they will come back to. If they are not, the listening is also what reveals that clearly.

**Move 3 only when needed** because grieving prematurely is itself an avoidance of the work. The grief move is for when the first two moves have revealed that the partner is structurally done, not when you are scared they might be.

The first two moves often dissolve the question. The relationship that looked dead from disdain often re-animates when the held truth is finally spoken and the partner finally feels seen. Move 3 is the case where this does not happen.

## The Diagnostic the Framework Provides

The framework also functions as a diagnostic. After running moves 1 and 2 in a clean way, the question of whether the relationship is over becomes legible.

- If the partner softens, engages, brings their own truth: the relationship is not over. The work continues.
- If the partner stays in disdain but is still in contact: the question is still open. More iteration may be needed.
- If the partner has fully disengaged and the first two moves produce no response: move 3 applies. The relationship is over and the work is grief.

The framework removes the guessing. Most people in a dying relationship spend years trying to decide whether to stay or leave. The framework offers a clean test: do the speaking, do the listening, and observe what arrives. The decision becomes downstream of the observation.

## What This Framework Is Not

This is not a guarantee. The framework gives the relationship its best chance to come back; it does not promise it will. Some relationships are genuinely over and no amount of well-done speaking and listening will change that. The point of the framework is not the guarantee; it is the clean test.

This is not a manipulation strategy. If the first two moves are done with the agenda of saving the relationship, they cease to be the moves. The truth-speaking has to be actually open-hearted; the listening has to be actually without management; the grief, when it comes, has to be actually surrendered to. Performing these moves as tactics does not work, for the same reasons all VIEW-as-technique does not work. See [[view-as-technique]].

This is not a substitute for the longer work of the relationship. The framework is for the crisis moment. The day-to-day work of a healthy relationship is described in [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]], [[hold-back-from-relationship-is-what-needs-healing]], and the broader teaching about [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]]. The framework is what to do when the day-to-day work has not happened and the relationship has reached the brink.

## When to Use This Framework

- A client is in a marriage that is failing and asking what to do
- A relationship has reached the disdain stage (see [[apathy-vs-disdain-as-end-of-marriage]])
- One partner has stopped engaging and the other is still trying
- A person is paralyzed between "stay and try" and "give up and leave"
- The walking-on-eggshells stage has lasted years and the question is whether to break the pattern

## Tone

Joe delivers the three moves quickly and cleanly. The framework is offered as a piece of practical information rather than as a deep teaching. The plainness matches the situation: a relationship in crisis does not need elaborate philosophy; it needs the three moves named simply enough that they can be acted on.

The third move ("then grieve") is delivered without softening. The plainness is the kindness. The chaser does not need to be talked into the possibility of grief; they need to hear that grief is the work when the other has decided.
