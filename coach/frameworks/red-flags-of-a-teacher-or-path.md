# Red Flags of a Teacher, Path, or Practice

## The Framework

A diagnostic for evaluating how functional a teacher, spiritual path, self-development practice, or transformative community is. Not a binary good/bad sort. A set of markers that, when present, signal the path is more likely to do harm than good. When absent, signal a path that holds itself in a way that supports the student rather than capturing them.

The frame is not about finding the perfect path. There is no perfect path. Every path comes from human consciousness, and there is no perfect human consciousness. The diagnostic is for how much wonkiness is operating in the path and whether the wonkiness is in service of the student's growth or in service of the teacher's needs.

"There is no such thing as a perfect path. And if you believe it, then it's like a religion, it's like a faith. Because all path comes from human consciousness, and there is no such thing as a perfect human consciousness." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## Red Flag 1: Identification With the Teaching

The teacher is identified with the teaching. They think the teaching is right. They defend it when challenged. They cannot say "you're full of shit, you're right, this is just what I made up" when someone calls out a contradiction.

Certainty in *delivery* is necessary and fine — people do not want to listen to a teacher who hedges everything. The flag is not the certainty of speech. The flag is whether the teacher is identified with what they are saying.

See [[identification-with-the-teaching]] for the full distinction.

"For me, it's identification. The way I would describe it is identification. If the person is identified with their teaching, they think their teaching is the right way." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## Red Flag 2: Delegation of Authority

The structure of the relationship transfers authority from the student to the teacher. The teacher assumes authority over the student, or the student gives their authority to the teacher. Strong hierarchy. Gatekeepers to levels of power. Inner circles closer to the teacher.

A healthy teacher occasionally and explicitly points back: the truth is in you, not in me. A teacher who never says this — who positions themselves as the source of the truth rather than as a pointer to the truth the student already has access to — is showing the structural problem of the path.

"If the people are delegating their authority to a teacher or the teacher is assuming authority over students, and that usually can look like very strong hierarchy or gatekeepers to some levels of power that move up — that's usually a good sign of it. But if the teacher isn't directly occasionally saying, 'hey, the truth is in you, it's not in me,' that's a really important sign that there's something else going on." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

See [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] and [[community-over-teacher]] for the corrective framings.

## Red Flag 3: The We-Have-to-Save-the-World Frame

The teaching positions itself as the solution to a crisis only it can address. The world is in trouble, only we can fix it, and there is urgency. The world needs to be saved from billionaires, or from the close-minded, or from climate change, or from the radical left, or from the radical right.

The flag is the structure, not the content of the crisis. There may be real problems in the world. But the teaching's framing as the necessary intervention, the only-we-can-do-it positioning, the urgency, the anxiety behind the mission — these together are markers of a path that is operating from fear and recruiting students into fear-based action.

"There's like that kind of urgency and anxiety behind the spiritual tradition also shows some level of dysfunction." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## Red Flag 4: Us-vs-Them Structure

The teaching defines an in-group and an out-group. There are people who get it and people who do not. Membership in the in-group is contingent on subscribing to the teaching. The out-group is variously framed as asleep, unconscious, unhealed, regressive, or dangerous.

This is the same structure that operates in political cults, marketing campaigns, and abusive relationships. The us-vs-them creates a feeling of safety inside ("we are the ones who see") and a corresponding distance from anyone outside the frame.

See [[us-vs-them-as-universal-manipulation-pattern]] for the broader pattern that this red flag is a special case of.

"We all want to be on the inside of something. It feels safe there. And it's especially easy to feel on the inside of something if there's an outside and others." -- Brett Kistler (Joe affirms), Who Owns Your Authority?

## Red Flag 5: Secrecy and Non-Transparency Without a Clean Reason

The teaching contains material that students are told not to share with outsiders. Not because the material is dangerous when applied without context (a legitimate reason for some practices). Because the secrecy itself is the structure that keeps the group inside the group.

A specific example: a male teacher who only teaches women and tells them not to discuss the work with their husbands or boyfriends. The framing is "framed control" — using a frame (the privacy of the work) to control the student's relationships outside the group.

The distinction matters. Some material legitimately needs to be held with care. A week-long retreat that is meaningful in part because participants do not know what comes next, where naming the structure spoils the experience for future participants, is a legitimate reason to ask for discretion. A practice that can harm people when applied by someone untrained, where the teacher asks others to seek permission before propagating, is a legitimate reason to gatekeep.

The flag is secrecy that has no functional reason beyond keeping the group together, or that isolates the student from their other relationships.

## Red Flag 6: Hierarchy of Levels Where Higher-Level People Are Better

The path has discrete levels. You progress through them. People at higher levels are positioned as better than people at lower levels. The hierarchy is treated as a hierarchy of being, not just a hierarchy of skill or experience.

"You hit this level and then you get this door opened and then you hit this level and then you get this door opened and then people on level 7 are better than people on level 5. That's a pretty big sign that there's some dysfunction going on." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

Skill differentiation is fine and often necessary. Being-differentiation is the flag.

## Red Flag 7: Feigned Reluctance to Be the Authority

The teacher frames their position as not-their-choice. "I just have to do this." "The world needs me." "I would not be doing this if it weren't required of me."

The complexity here: this can be the teacher's actual experience of their calling. Joe describes feeling choiceless about his own work in a way that is not pretense. The distinction is between owning the experience as yours ("I feel called to this, I have to do this, this is my experience") and disowning the position by attributing it to external necessity ("the world needs me to do this, I have no choice").

"If they can say, 'I think this is important, this is important to me, I don't want to do that, but I don't feel like I have a choice' is very different than 'I don't have a choice.' There's an ownership of their experience." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The reluctance itself is also a flag when it is performed rather than felt. The performed reluctance maps to the "I am special and burdened" identity. The felt choicelessness with ownership is just honest report of an internal state.

## Red Flag 8: Certainty Supply

The teacher's role becomes supplying certainty to students. The students come for confidence, and the teaching delivers confidence by reinforcing the rightness of the path. The path becomes a new framework the student buys into rather than a tool for seeing through frameworks.

"Freedom doesn't come from buying one set of thoughts. Freedom comes from not buying any thoughts. And that's very scary because most people are looking for some sort of ground and certainty. And so it's much easier to sell certainty than it is to sell lack of certainty." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The functional teaching does not sell certainty. The Hindu tradition Joe references: in the path, the last things to go are the teacher and then the teaching. The student sees through the scripture and through the teacher. A path that positions either as the destination has missed the work.

## Red Flag 9: A Clear End State (Enlightenment, Awakening, Completion)

The promise of the path is a clear arrival point. Once you get there, you are good. The suffering ends. The work is done. Everything is sorted.

This is the [[no-finish-line]] problem applied to spiritual paths specifically. A path that organizes the student's experience around an unattained future arrival keeps the student in the future rather than in the present.

"What is the promise? If the promise of the path is some clear end state that everybody seems to be constantly seeking, and there's something magical about it — that's another thing I watch for. You can do all kinds of work on yourself and move a lot of trauma and still continuously organize your momentary experience around something that hasn't arrived yet in the future and around the notion that the present is not actually good enough." -- Brett Kistler (Joe affirms), Who Owns Your Authority?

## Red Flag 10: A Teacher Who Is Not Constantly Learning

The teacher has stopped growing. They speak from a position of finished knowledge. They no longer report being changed by their students, by their own ongoing inquiry, or by what life is bringing them.

"A teacher that always sees that they're learning is another really good sign." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

The inverse is the flag.

## Red Flag 11: Identification by Tenure or Closeness to the Teacher

Members of the community start to define their value by how long they have been in the work, how many courses they have done, how close they are to the teacher. The hierarchy of belonging is based on tenure or proximity to power.

This is a structural emergence — the teacher may not have caused it, may not even see it. But it is a feature of an unhealthy community, regardless of the teacher's awareness.

"People start defining how good they are by how long they've been with the teaching or the teacher or how close they are to the teacher. That is super dysfunctional, whether the teacher is aware of it or not." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## What the Healthy Path Looks Like

The inversion of the red flags:

- The teacher holds the teaching loosely. When challenged, they can say: yeah, this is what I made up, this is what worked for me, it might not be right.
- The teacher consistently points the student back to their own authority. The truth is in you.
- The teaching does not frame itself as the necessary intervention for a world crisis.
- There is no in-group/out-group structure. Membership does not require believing.
- Secrecy, where it exists, has functional reasons that the student can examine.
- Differentiation is by skill, not by being.
- The teacher owns their position as theirs, not as forced on them by the world.
- The teaching produces less certainty over time, not more.
- There is no promised arrival.
- The teacher is visibly still learning.
- Tenure and proximity to the teacher do not confer rank.

## How to Use the Framework

These red flags are signals, not condemnations. Many paths have some of them. The question is degree, and the question is trajectory: is the path getting healthier over time or less healthy?

"I have seen paths and teachers go from pretty healthy to pretty wonky. And I've actually seen once one go from a little wonky to more and more healthy, which was a fascinating journey to see." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

A path can have a red flag and still be worth engaging if the student can be conscious about the flag and not be captured by it. Joe describes consciously entering paths he knew had wonky elements to extract the useful tools while not buying the rest. The capacity to be in a path while seeing the flags is itself a measure of the student's developed authority.

The danger arrives when the student does not see the flag and lets the path operate on them without consciousness. Then the flag operates whether or not the student notices.

## The Other Half: How the Student Contributes

The framework above is for evaluating the path. The full picture requires also evaluating the student-teacher relationship as a relationship — see [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] for how the same path can become healthier or unhealthier depending on how the students show up.

The reflexive criticism stance — looking for red flags everywhere — is its own problem. A student who arrives at any path with skepticism designed to confirm dysfunction will find it. A student who arrives with full reception and then discernment will get more from the path while still being able to leave it when needed.

"Once someone's looking for red flags, you'll find them everywhere. And especially if you're stepping into a process that is really powerful, it will bring you into resistance. And one of the first ways many people's resistance will show up is in criticizing, finding all the ways it's wrong, putting yourself above it, separating yourself from it, separating yourself from the group." -- Brett Kistler (Joe affirms), Who Owns Your Authority?

The pairing matters: discernment without absorption misses the gift. Absorption without discernment loses the self. The work is both at once.

## When This Framework Applies

- A coachee is considering joining a spiritual community, retreat, or transformative program and wants to evaluate it
- A coachee is in a community and is starting to notice things that feel off
- A coachee has left a community and wants to make sense of what was happening
- A coachee is themselves a teacher and wants to assess their own work
- A coachee is in a relationship, company, or political community that is operating with cult-like dynamics, and the framework helps them see the structural similarity

The framework is not only for spiritual paths. The same red flags apply to political movements, companies, and relationships. See [[us-vs-them-as-universal-manipulation-pattern]] for the broader application.

## Tone

Joe holds the framework with care. He is not anti-teacher or anti-path. He has been a teacher himself and knows the difficulty of the position. The framework comes from someone who has seen many paths from inside and knows which signals are early warnings of harm. The tone is precise rather than alarmed. The signals are signals, not verdicts.
