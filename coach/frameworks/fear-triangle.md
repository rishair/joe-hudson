# The Fear Triangle

## Definition

Three roles people take when they are scared and trying to seek power over a situation: victim, savior, bully. Often called the drama triangle. Joe calls it the fear triangle because each role corresponds to a fear response: fight, flight, freeze.

- **Bully = fight.** When scared, attack.
- **Victim = freeze.** When scared, collapse.
- **Savior = flight.** When scared, flee yourself by going into someone else and fixing them.

"The reason I don't call it the drama triangle very often, and I'm more prone to call it the fear triangle, is because the victim, bully, and savior correspond with fight, flight, or freeze, which are the states of fear." -- Joe Hudson, Empower over Power

## The Roles

### Bully

The most obvious form of power-over. Society identifies it easily. The bully attacks to control.

The signature feeling: **alone**. "If I am feeling all alone in it, that's the bully."

### Victim

Control through fragility. "I'm so fragile that you can't tell me your truth." If there is someone in your life you cannot tell your truth to because you fear hurting them, someone is controlling through victimhood.

The example: a group of mothers laughing about how guilt was an effective way to control their kids. Guilt is the victim's power tool.

The signature feeling: **stuck**. "If I feel stuck, that's the victim."

### Savior

The hardest to see, because it looks like helping. The savior runs away from their own experience by going into the other person to fix them. "If I can make it so you don't get drunk, I'll feel safe. If I can make it that you are happy, then I'll feel safe."

Examples: wealthy families that maintain control over children by keeping money available to save them. The Al-Anon member who keeps the alcoholic alive. Philanthropy done from guilt rather than exchange.

The signature feeling: **obligation**. "If I feel obligation, that's the savior."

See [[caring-vs-caretaking]] for the related distinction.

## All Three at Once

In any given fear moment, you can feel all three. Slow it down and they all appear.

The example: wife comes home in a horrible mood. The mind goes through:
- "I can't do anything." (helplessness, leading to victim)
- "Oh God, I'm the only one who has to fix this." (alone, bully)
- "Oh my God, I gotta do something for her so she feels better." (obligation, savior)

Most people have a dominant tendency. Some default to fight (bully). Some default to freeze (victim). Some default to flight (savior). But all three live in everyone.

## How Blame and Shame Build the Triangle

Underneath the fear is helplessness. Underneath the helplessness is the story of blame and shame. The triangle is built on the unwillingness to feel the helplessness that the blame or shame story creates.

"It's that helplessness is the feeling of that blame and shame felt all the way through that we don't want to feel. And that's the amazing thing about feeling helplessness. Feeling helpless doesn't make you more helpless. Feeling helpless makes you more capable." -- Joe Hudson, Empower over Power

See [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]].

## The Triangle in Teams

The fear triangle is the political infrastructure of disempowered teams. Where there is heavy politics, there is heavy disempowerment.

"The less empowered the team feels, the more drama. And that's a great, like, as soon as you walk into a team, if it's super political, it's just like, wow, everybody feels disempowered. You just know it." -- Joe Hudson, Empower over Power

Empowered teams have minimal politics. The drama drops when people feel they can actually affect change inside well-defined roles. See [[structure-and-empowerment]].

## How to Recognize Which Role You Are In

The body knows before the mind. The feelings function as the diagnostic:
- Alone, fighting against everything = bully
- Stuck, nothing to be done = victim
- Obligated, must take care of this = savior

A person can transition rapidly between roles. Savior often flips to bully when the saving fails ("after all I did for you..."). Victim often flips to savior when the pressure of helplessness becomes intolerable.

## Exit From the Triangle

The exit is not finding the right role. The exit is moving through the helplessness that the triangle is built to avoid. See [[helplessness-is-the-gateway]] and [[empowered-vs-power]].

Empowered action means making the choice from truth and accepting the consequences. The triangle is what happens when truth feels too costly.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone describes a situation in terms of "they did this to me" (victim)
- When someone is exhausted from taking care of everyone (savior)
- When someone is angry and isolated, fighting everything (bully)
- When a team is described as "political" or "drama-filled"
- When [[caring-vs-caretaking]] has been identified and the person is in caretaking mode

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward identifying the helplessness underneath, and inviting the person to feel it
- Toward [[empowered-vs-power]]: the only exit is empowerment, which requires accepting the feared consequences
- Toward [[shame-drives-behavior]]: the shame underneath the role is what keeps it spinning
