# Stages of Emotional Development

## The Framework

Emotional development unfolds in a recognizable sequence. Different people start at different points, but the sequence runs the same. Each stage is a real epiphany. Each stage also becomes the next constraint that the next stage dissolves. See [[epiphany-becomes-rut]].

## The Stages

### Stage 1: Identification

The earliest stage. Recognizing that an emotion is happening at all. People raised in environments where emotion was repressed or punished often cannot feel what they feel. The body has learned to cut off sensation, in the same way someone who was physically abused may not be able to tell if they are holding a quarter or a key in their hand.

The work at this stage is to recognize that emotional experience is constantly happening. There is no moment of consciousness without an emotional reality. The way you feel right now is slightly different than the way you felt a moment ago.

"You're constantly in an emotional state as long as you're conscious." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

### Stage 2: Expression

Once emotions can be identified, the next stage is allowing them to be expressed. Through the body, through words, through movement. Crucially: not at anybody. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]]. Expression here means letting the emotion have its full motion in the system, not delivering it to another person to change them.

### Stage 3: Inquiry

The third stage opens curiosity about the emotion itself. What does it feel like in the body? Where is it located? What color is it? How dense is it? This is the somatic inquiry.

A second layer of inquiry: how does the emotion change when you relate to it differently? When you are angry at your emotions, what happens? When you are in love with them? When you try to get rid of them? When you tickle them? Each relationship produces a different experience of the same emotion.

### Stage 4: Love

The fourth stage is being deeply in love with your emotions. At this point, emotions become fluid. They flow without resistance. Decisions become crisp. Action becomes decisive. The emotional life becomes a source of pleasure rather than a problem to manage.

"There comes this place where you're just deeply in love with your emotions, and then emotions become very fluid, and it becomes just like this beautiful flow of life." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## The Trap of Stage 1

The first stage -- identification -- is a real epiphany. It is also where many people get stuck. They learn to name what they are feeling and substitute the naming for the feeling.

Joe describes this happening to himself a decade into his work. He told someone, "I'm feeling angry right now." The person responded: "No, you're not. You're naming an emotion so you don't have to feel it."

"The thing that was once freeing to be able to see it and name it had become the new constraint. And then I realized, oh, the feeling of it is a completely different thing." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

This is the clearest illustration of [[epiphany-becomes-rut]] in the emotional domain. Stage 1 frees you from emotional unconsciousness. It then becomes a way of avoiding stage 2 if you let it.

## How Feeling Differs from Naming

Feeling is letting the body move. It is like dancing without self-consciousness. Emotions know how to move the body when they are not interrupted. The intellect cannot direct the movement. The instruction is to allow rather than to perform.

People who have not cried much often think their first real cries are not "real" because they do not resemble the rare cries from before. There are at least twenty different ways of crying: joy, sadness, grief, yawning-like. None of them are "wrong." None of them need to look like any particular form.

"Your emotions know how to move your body. Your emotions know what to do. Don't judge them. Don't tell them how they're supposed to look." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## Tone

Patient. The stages take time. The framework is not a checklist. It is a map of a trajectory that proceeds at its own pace.
