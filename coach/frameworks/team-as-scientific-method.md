# Team Design as Scientific Method (Thesis, Principles, Metrics, Iterate)

## The Framework

Teams are not designed once. They are run as experiments. The leader proposes a thesis (a definition of what the team should look like), defines principles (how the team will operate), defines metrics (what they will measure), and then iterates: the thesis refines, the principles refine, the metrics refine, all in response to observed reality.

The framework has four moving parts:

1. **Thesis:** What this team is for, what success looks like
2. **Principles:** How the team will operate, the behavioral norms
3. **Metrics:** What gets measured to know if it is working
4. **Iteration:** Continuous refinement of all three in response to observation

"It is an experimental process. So you have your thesis and your thesis is your goals. And then you run the thesis and you see how performance happens through enjoyment, through sustainability of your company. And then you have this is how we operate, a set of principles." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## Why Teams Cannot Be Designed Once

The specifics of what makes a team functional change with the team's context, the people on it, the moment in the company's life, and the broader environment. Designing a team once produces a structure fit for the moment it was designed and increasingly unfit for every moment after.

"I do think that there's a constant wrestling with the measurement system. What Apple measures today, I hope, is crazily different than Apple measured in 1984." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

This is the team-design parallel to [[epiphany-becomes-rut]]: every team design that is treated as final becomes a constraint. The same design that liberated last quarter constrains this quarter.

## The Common Failure Modes

Most leaders do not run the scientific method. They do one of two things instead:

**Default unconscious design.** The team operates the way the leader operates, because the leader has not made the design choices visible. The leader's behavioral patterns become the team culture by accretion.

"This is the way the CEO is and therefore this is how the CEO runs. CEOs who will measure frickin' everything, but they don't measure culture. Or they measure culture, but they're not aware that basically the culture they want isn't the most effective culture. It's just the culture of their behavior patterns. They're just reflecting their behavior patterns into the company. I can't tell you how many people I've spoken to are just like, the company is run by trying to make the CEO happy. Which is wholly ineffective." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

See [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]] for the explicit anti-pattern.

**Borrowed design.** The leader adopts the culture from a book or another company. The design is imported wholesale without the experimental process that would calibrate it to the actual team.

"There's an assumption that this is the way that the culture should be based on some reading. So it's either the culture should be this way because of who I am as a CEO or because of some reading, instead of, oh, well, if we tweak this part of culture, what happens to performance? It's not iterative, it's not an interactive thing." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The iterative quality is what is missing in both. The leader either inherits a design from themselves or imports one from elsewhere, then defends it against the feedback that should be refining it.

## Metrics Are the Hardest Part

What you measure determines what you produce. Choosing the right metrics is the substantive work of the scientific method applied to teams.

The Walgreens example Joe cites: when Walgreens was outpacing the rest of the drugstore market, their distinguishing metric was not sales per store but sales per customer. The metric reflected what they were actually trying to optimize: customer experience.

"They talked about how their main metric wasn't sales per store, their main metric was sales per customer because they were measuring the customer experience. Did the customer buy nine dollars of things on average or eleven dollars of things on average? And so their whole metric was based on customer experience rather than just revenue." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

See [[walgreens-sales-per-customer]] for the example as standalone.

The lesson: revenue is downstream of customer experience. Measuring revenue tells you what already happened. Measuring customer experience tells you what is about to happen. Picking the upstream metric changes what the organization optimizes for.

## Metrics Are Domain-Specific

The framework asserts that there is no universal "right" set of metrics. The metrics for a hedge fund differ from the metrics for a tomato processing house. The metrics for a Silicon Valley family differ from the metrics for a sub-Saharan family.

Joe's example for his own family:

"For me, the way I measure the functionality of my family, the bottom line of my family is connection. Do we feel connected with one another? Do I come home and I feel connected with my kids? Do my kids come home and feel connected to me? Do we feel connected with ourselves? That's the bottom line for me." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

See [[connection-as-family-metric]] for this specifically.

The framework does not specify the metric. The framework specifies the process by which the right metric emerges.

## The Goal vs. The Rollout of the Goal

The metric is not the only variable. How the metric was arrived at also matters. A goal rolled out by leadership lands differently from a goal the team co-created.

"It's not just the goal that's on the team, it's how the goal was rolled out to the team. Did the team create the goal or did you create the goal and give it to your team?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

This is an additional layer of the experimental process. You are not just refining the metrics; you are refining how the metrics are produced. The team that produces its own metrics will own them differently than the team that receives them.

## The Underlying Assumption: Everyone Wants to Do Well

The framework rests on a foundational assumption. People want to do well. The job of the experimental design is to find the structure that allows them to.

"Everybody wants to do a good job. There's nobody, I'm sure there's somebody, but there's very few people who are like, you know what, I just wanna do a shitty job. Everybody wants to be successful. The question is, what is the environment doing to take away their desire to be successful? What's the environment doing that is allowing them to be victims to the machine rather than the creators of the machine?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The framework is not "find ways to motivate unmotivated people." The framework is "design conditions under which the existing motivation can express itself." The leader's question shifts from "how do I get my people to perform?" to "what is in the way of my people's existing desire to perform?"

This is the team-design parallel to [[inherent-goodness]]. People want to do well, the way the oak tree wants to grow. The job of the design is to remove the obstacles.

## The Harness-the-Wisdom Reframe

A specific application of the underlying assumption: when leaders worry about how the team will receive a change, they have the question backwards.

"Oftentimes people are worried about how the company is going to take this new thing, this new change that comes from management. That's like the worry that you see with a lot of CEOs. I'm gonna make this move. I wonder how the company is going to take it, instead of, oh, the company has all this wisdom. How do I harness it? How do I harness the wisdom to sharpen my own ideas of the company?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The reframe: the team is not an audience that has to be managed. The team is a source of intelligence that should be informing the leader's thinking. When the leader's question is "how will they receive it?" the leader has positioned themselves as the source and the team as recipients. When the question is "what wisdom can I draw from?" the positions are reversed.

This is the team-internal version of [[follow-the-demand]]: do not impose the agenda; find out what is actually being asked for.

## The Iteration Never Ends

The framework explicitly accepts that the work is never done.

"That's a great question. I don't think it's ever not an infinite loop, meaning evolution changes. You can get some things that stay pretty for a long period of time. I don't think you're going to run a company and not be measuring your revenue. But what Apple measures today, I hope, is crazily different than Apple measured in 1984." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The framework is not "design the right team and then operate it." The framework is "operate the team as a continuously refined design." The maintenance is the work.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A leader realizes their company culture is just their personality reflected
- A leader discovers their metrics are measuring the wrong thing and have been for years
- A team is asking why nothing changes despite reorganizations
- A founder is wondering whether to stick with a chosen culture or change it
- A leader is comparing their company to others and confused why theirs underperforms despite the right ingredients
