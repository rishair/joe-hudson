# The Seven Limiting Beliefs

## The Framework

Approximately seven main limiting beliefs structure how people interfere with their own natural goodness. All seven rest on one foundational belief: that you are not inherently good. Removing the foundation collapses the others. This is the architecture that the [[art-of-accomplishment]] undoes.

"There's probably about 7 main limiting beliefs, but they're all resting on one limiting belief, and the one limiting belief is that you're not inherently good." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## The Foundation

**You are not inherently good.** The belief that you must put effort to make yourself good enough, of value, better. That goodness is not your natural place. That you are not there yet, and may never be. See [[inherent-goodness]].

The oak tree test: when is an oak tree good enough? When it is an acorn? When it is two years old? When it is 150 years old? When it is collapsing into dirt? The question reveals the absurdity. The oak tree is always inherently good. The same is true of the human, but the human is the only being that disbelieves it about itself.

The signal that this belief is active: defense. If someone tells you the sky is purple at noon, you do not need to defend the fact that it is blue. You defend only what you already half-believe might be true. The defensive impulse is the giveaway.

"Every time you defend something in yourself, there's some way that you're believing that you're not inherently good, that you have to defend something about yourself." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## The Specific Limiting Beliefs

Joe names several explicitly in this episode. Each is a derivative of the foundational belief.

### 1. Perfection over connection

Trying to be perfect (in the product, the presentation, the date, the meditation) instead of being in connection (with the customer, the audience, the person, yourself). The perfectionist chooses an idealized standard over an alive relationship. See [[connection-over-perfection]] and [[perfectionism]].

The first date that goes wrong: you tried to be perfect, so they were dating an idealized version of yourself. Eventually that goes sideways. The version of meditation that becomes torture: you tried to have the perfect meditation experience instead of being in connection with yourself. Meditation as connection vs. management as torture.

### 2. Improvement over authenticity

Trying to be a better version instead of finding out what you actually are. The improvement path is slower than the authenticity path because it presumes you know what the better version should look like, and the model is borrowed from outside yourself. See [[improvement-vs-authenticity]].

"I want to be enlightened" is slower than "I want to know what I am." "I want to work successfully 60 hours a week" is less effective than "I want to understand my natural rhythm."

### 3. Shoulds before wants

Trying to motivate yourself with shoulds instead of with wants. Children from 0 to 7 have no shoulds. They develop more in those years than at any other time in life. Shoulds slow everything down. Wants pull. Shoulds drag. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[impulse-to-should]].

### 4. Defense before love

Defending yourself before receiving the care underneath what is being offered. The boss giving you difficult feedback took a social risk because they cared enough about your success to risk the relationship. The default move is to defend. The undefended move is to receive the care. See [[defense-vs-love]].

"My boss just took a social risk on me, potentially risked our relationship because he cared or she cared enough to help me be successful. We don't think, oh wow, thanks, thanks for taking the risk of telling me that." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

### 5-7. Others not enumerated in this episode

Joe states there are about seven main limiting beliefs but does not enumerate all of them in this transcript. The full enumeration may emerge across other episodes. Until then, the architecture is: one foundation (not inherently good) supporting approximately seven specific patterns.

## How the Architecture Works

Each limiting belief justifies itself in terms of the others. Perfectionism justifies improvement-seeking ("I must improve to be perfect"). Improvement-seeking justifies shoulds ("I should be working on myself"). Shoulds justify defense ("I am doing what I should, so your feedback is wrong"). The whole structure self-reinforces.

The undoing happens at the foundation. When inherent goodness is felt as real, the entire upper structure becomes unnecessary. You do not need to be perfect because you are already good. You do not need to improve because you are not lacking. You do not need shoulds because your wants are trustworthy. You do not need to defend because there is nothing to defend.

This is why [[shame-drives-behavior]]: the foundational shame ("I am not inherently good") generates the entire structure of limiting behaviors. Remove the shame and the structure relaxes.

## What Someone Experiences

If you find yourself constantly producing reasons why you are right, you are likely in defense (which rests on not-inherently-good).

If you find yourself producing endless self-improvement projects, you are likely in improvement (which rests on not-inherently-good).

If you find yourself dragging through obligations, you are likely in shoulds (which rests on not-inherently-good).

If you find yourself unable to ship anything because it is never quite ready, you are likely in perfectionism (which rests on not-inherently-good).

The work is not to address each pattern individually. The work is to address the foundation.
