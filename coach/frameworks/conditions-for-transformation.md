# Conditions for Transformation

## Definition

Before you pack your bag for the transformative journey, these conditions need to be in place. Borrowed from an acupuncturist's concept of "conditions for healing": what must be present for transformation to be possible at all.

Each condition is both a prerequisite and something the journey develops. Start with whatever level you have and make the weakest condition your first focus.

## The Conditions

### 1. Safety

Fear reduces the capacity to learn. Two people in a yelling match will never produce insight. This is neurological, not psychological.

The critical distinction: safety vs. defensiveness. These are different signals in the body. Safety often feels electrical, in the shoulders and belly, connected to fight/flight/freeze. Defensiveness is a hardening, a rigidity, muscles tightening. See [[safety-vs-defensiveness]].

"You've never seen two people in a yelling match and the other person say, 'Oh yeah, you know, you're right. You've got a point there.' It doesn't happen because it's neurologically impossible." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

### 2. Trust

Trust that transformation is possible. Trust in a teacher if you have one. Trust in yourself, which matters more than trusting the teacher.

The placebo effect is a feature, not a bug. Believing change is possible makes change possible. Every person who goes through a course and does not change has one thing in common: they never believed they could.

"If you're that, just don't do it. It's a waste of your time and it hurts the other people that you're on the journey with." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

The litmus test for a teacher: tell them "I don't trust teachers and I want to, and how can we work together?" If they react defensively, wrong teacher. If they point you to taking ownership of the mistrust, teacher worth trusting.

### 3. Seeing Beyond the Intellect

The body has intelligence. Emotions have intelligence. Mirror neurons do not register in the intellect. Kids with sensory processing disorder (insufficient proprioceptive experience) develop different brain function. Movement changes thought.

"If you are trying to do all of your change through the intellect, you're going to be screwed. It's going to be slow. You're going to be able to describe everything that's wrong with you, but very little is going to have changed." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

See [[emotions-live-in-muscles]], [[staying-in-your-body]].

### 4. Vulnerability

If you cannot explore the depths of your pain and constriction and express it to other people, nothing can be approached. Attention must be able to go to the discomfort. See [[vulnerability]].

This does not mean believing you are broken. "I'm broken, fix me" is its own trap. But the opposite, "Yeah, it's not that bad, I'm good," prevents transformation equally.

### 5. Full Responsibility

No blaming a teacher, a spouse, parents, or yourself. "Every time you blame anything for where you are, including blaming yourself, you are slowing the process down tremendously." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking on the Journey

Blame and shame are different from accountability. Loving accountability (see [[five-principles-of-the-path]]) is honest reckoning without brutality. Blame assigns fault. Responsibility says: "I am exactly where I need to be."

## The Catch-22

These conditions are both prerequisites and effects. A person may seek transformation precisely because they do not trust themselves or cannot be vulnerable. The resolution: make the weakest condition your first focus. Trust issues? Make trust the first thing to work on. It probably relates to parental attachment patterns, but begin with trust.

"Don't make your mommy issues the first thing. Make trust the first thing. And it probably relates to your mom issues or your dad issues, but make trust the first thing and really focus there." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

Working any condition creates a feedback loop. Safety enables vulnerability. Vulnerability enables trust. Trust enables deeper safety. The system accelerates. As the loop runs, speed becomes less important than enjoyment.

"There is a point in that path where everything that's uncomfortable, you trust. You like that uncomfortable thing approaches and you're like, oh, I can trust this thing because it's going to teach me something. I just have to be vulnerable in it and take full responsibility." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

## How to Approach a Teacher When You Cannot Yet Trust Them

The specific move for a person who wants to engage with a teacher but knows their own trust capacity is low: name it directly to the teacher. "I don't trust teachers and I want to, and how can we work together so that this isn't a burden for you and this isn't a burden for me?"

The act of saying this is itself the four conditions in operation simultaneously. It is responsibility (owning the trust issue rather than placing it on the teacher). It is vulnerability (admitting the limitation). It is trust (trusting yourself enough to speak it, and trusting the teacher enough to bring it). It is also a small experiment in safety: the teacher's response will reveal whether further engagement is safe.

The teacher's response is the test. A teacher worth working with can receive the mistrust, especially when the person is owning it. A teacher who reacts defensively ("if you don't trust me, motherfucker") or who deflects into spiritual framings ("this is all about you letting go into my words") has failed the test. A teacher who can sit with the mistrust, and who points the person back to their own ownership, has passed.

"Especially if you're taking ownership. And if you're not taking ownership and they point to the ownership, then also a teacher worth trusting." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey
