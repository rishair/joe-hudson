# The 4 A's of Interpersonal Behavior Change (Announce, Apologize, Ask, Act)

## The Framework

A four-step protocol for converting a recognition ("oh, I've been doing this thing and it's not how I want to be") into actual changed behavior. The four steps:

1. **Announce** the behavior to as many people as possible.
2. **Apologize** uprightly, without shame, to those affected.
3. **Ask** for specific help and reflection from the people you announced to.
4. **Act** immediately on five different contrary actions.

The form is simple. The work underneath each step is significant: each A is also a feeling-through of the emotions the old pattern was avoiding. The framework's effectiveness comes from the combination of the procedural surface (easy to remember, easy to do) and the emotional substrate (the avoided feelings get felt as you execute).

"It's a simple method. Just requires you to feel a whole bunch of stuff on the way." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## When This Applies

The framework is for *interpersonal* patterns where you have just had a clear recognition. Not for bad habits in isolation (smoking, scrolling, not exercising). For those, see [[apologize-as-daily-practice-for-bad-habits]] and [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] — the underlying dynamic is different and the 4 A's do not fit cleanly.

The 4 A's work when:
- The pattern is relational (it operates with specific other people: team, family, partner, kids).
- A real recognition has just happened. Not "I think I should change this." Actual "oh, I see it now, that's not how I want to be."
- The recognition is fresh enough to ride. The framework is most effective applied immediately after the moment of seeing.

"This is perfect for once somebody has seen through a pattern, because one thing we notice is that a lot of times people will see a pattern, maybe they've seen it for years, and their life hasn't changed. They just see the thing." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The diagnostic is the gap between recognition and change. The 4 A's close that gap by structuring action that drags the recognition into the relational field.

## The Recognition Prerequisite

The framework fails if applied to a *should*. A should is "I think I'm supposed to change this." A recognition is "oh wow, that's not how I want to be." The difference determines whether the 4 A's produce real change or messy performance.

"It works really well when you have a recognition, when you have the realization, when you get off a coaching call with me and you say, oh, whoa, I've been doing that thing. I don't need to do that thing anymore. That's the best time to implement this." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

When run from a should, the framework becomes a boot camp: white-knuckled action, pushed-down feelings, identity performance. The result is messiness toward yourself and toward everyone you involve. See [[dont-run-the-four-as-from-a-should]].

The recognition test before deploying: can you locate the moment you saw it? Can you feel the *of course* of not wanting to do this anymore? If the answer is "I just think I should," wait. The recognition has not arrived yet.

## Step 1: Announce (Widely)

Announce the behavior change to as many people in your life as possible. The wider the better. The mechanism is twofold: identity is partly held by other people's expectations of you, and announcing widely shifts the social field that has been holding the old pattern in place.

"The most effective way is to announce as widely as possible to as many people in your life as possible... Part of my identity is held in place by who I think I am, but part of my identity is held in place by who you think I am. And so if I'm used to having an interaction with you where you're like, oh, you're Joe, you're gonna take care of me instead of hold me accountable, then that's how you're going to treat me. But if I've announced to you, I'm not doing that anymore, the next time it happens, you're going to be like, okay, well, what's going to go down here?" -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The feelings the wide announcement forces you to feel: fear of reputational loss, fear of failing publicly, fear of not following through. These are usually the exact feelings the old pattern was protecting you from feeling.

"There's something really cool about that too, where a lot of those feelings are actually feelings you've already been having. Like the fear of reputational loss is probably one of the things that's been driving the pattern." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The mechanism: by feeling the fear up front (in the announcement) instead of avoiding it (via the pattern), you stop needing the pattern to avoid the fear. The pattern was the symptom of the avoided fear. The announcement is the direct contact with what was being avoided.

The execution detail that distinguishes real announcement from cover: ask for explicit acknowledgment. "I asked every single person in the company to acknowledge that I'd written it. Just send me a happy face or a thumbs up. I very much wanted to make sure everybody saw it." This step prevents you from telling yourself you announced when really you sent a message you suspect was skimmed.

See [[announce-widely-the-behavior-change]] for the move-level treatment.

## Step 2: Apologize (Uprightly)

Apologize to those affected by the old pattern. Critical: the apology must be upright. A shame-based apology reinstalls the pattern. An upright apology releases the shame that was holding the pattern in place. See [[upright-apology]] for the full treatment.

"You have to apologize. You don't have to, but it's far better to apologize without any shame. So it's just, hey, I was doing this. This is not how I want to be anymore. If you're apologizing it with shame, then you're not feeling everything that comes along with having that apology. There's — it's actually quite a really good feeling to apologize in an upright way. You're just saying, oh, this isn't how I want to be. It's very empowering." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The diagnostic: does the apology produce relief, or does it produce smallness? Relief is the upright form. Smallness is the shame form. The shame form loops you back into the very identity ("I'm a screw-up") that has been driving the pattern.

"There's a feeling of relief in it. But if you're in shame, then it's like, oh, I'm bad. There's something wrong with me. And you're still in the identity of that you're screw-up... And that's just looping you right back into the old behavior." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The apology includes acknowledging the impact on the other, not just the speaker's own behavior: "It's not just, I'm sorry, but I acknowledge that this was shitty for you. And that's not how I want to be. And I don't want you to be in that shitty position." The acknowledgment of impact is what allows the speaker to actually have empathy for what their pattern has been doing to others — which is itself a feeling event, not just a verbal recognition.

## Step 3: Ask (For Specific Help)

Ask the people you announced to for specific help reminding you and supporting the change. The ask transforms you from a person trying to change a habit alone into a person doing the change inside a relational field that supports the new behavior.

Three things the ask does:

**1. Creates reflection / accountability with love.**
"It's like you're basically asking for accountability in one way or another. So which is not accountability the way most people think about accountability, but accountability, like, with love, right? So you're asking for people to remind you, to see you, to show you, to help you." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The reflection accelerates learning. You learn faster when someone outside you can see you doing the old pattern and name it back. The asking is also useful when you get defensive about being reminded: that defense is data about how much shame you still have around the pattern, and you can work it directly.

**2. Reveals you're not alone.**
"The other thing is it reminds you that you're not alone in it. And oftentimes these patterns hold in place because we think we're alone in it. And so if you can see, oh, there's other people who wanna support you... that immediately allows me to see that my reality might be different than everybody else's. And people do wanna help me and they see the same thing. And so I'm not alone in it, which is really, really useful for changing behavior." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The presumption of judgment (which has been holding the shame in place) breaks when people actually respond with support. The pattern was partly held by the imagined social cost of dropping it. The ask reveals the imagined cost was higher than the real cost.

**3. Invites the other person into a different action.**
"In inviting people to interact with you in a certain way, you're inviting them to change their behavior by taking a different action. If the action might have been hold back, resent you, and then blow up at you later, the invited action is tell me, throw a tennis ball at me. By inviting them into a different action, it actually changes their side of the dynamic with you." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The relational dynamic is bidirectional. By giving others a clean, specific action to take when they notice the pattern, you stop them from having to discharge the dynamic in less functional ways (holding back, resenting, blowing up later). Your changing changes them.

**The specificity requirement.** Vague asks ("just call me out") don't work. Specific asks tied to the antidote do. Joe's example: the "I see how much you care" phrase the team developed for him when he rushed, because being told that immediately resolved the felt-engine of the rushing (he'd been told as a kid he didn't care if he was late).

"Just that work, just the work of knowing it and then getting it is unbelievably useful. The more specific you can be, like, in these moments, at these times, this is the kind of thing to say that supports me. It makes you really understand the pattern and what it is that neutralizes that pattern." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The example of the tennis ball: instead of being awkward when asked to flag a pattern, the tennis-ball game changes the energetic register entirely. No shame, no scolding, just an agreed-upon signal that lets the work happen in play. See [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]] for the broader principle.

## Step 4: Act (Five Counter-Actions, Immediately)

Take five specific contrary actions as soon as possible after the announcement. Five matters. Immediate matters. Both matter.

**Why five.**
"One of them might go wrong. So there is no perfect way of acting that's gonna make everybody happy with you all the time. So typically what people will do is they'll take like one contrary action, they'll be very scared about it. They'll take that action and they'll do it in the exact place where they are the least likely to get the positive result." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

Five gives you variety in difficulty and surface area for the new behavior to find its shape. Start with easier instances. The hardest one (the mom, the most loaded relationship) is the wrong place to begin. Five also forces the realization that the pattern is more pervasive than the mind wants to admit — if you can only find one or two, you haven't actually surveyed the pattern.

"A lot of times what your brain will do is, I can only find 1 or 2. And it's like, there's no way. If you're dealing with a pattern, you're going to see it everywhere. And so part of finding the five is you have to actually dig through the gunk to actually see the pattern more clearly." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**Why immediately.**
Acting fast is doing reps. The recognition is the fuel; the reps consolidate the change. Spread across weeks, the recognition fades and the reps lose their effect. Done in a day (or close to it), the body files the new behavior as "this is what we do now."

"Taking contrary behavior on 5 of them quickly is like doing reps. It's like, oh, I know how to do this now. I, I, I can do it." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

**Why "clean."**
Clean means the contrary action is taken open-heartedly, not as a defense or attack. Not "fishing for an attack," not "armoring up to repel attack," not "making excuses or hedging," not "being really strong and hardcore about it." Just stating or doing the thing the pattern had been preventing.

"Oh, I'm open-heartedly saying the thing that is the contrary action. And getting those reps in, super critical. And to do that, you gotta feel all the crap of, oh, I might get hurt, I might get attacked, I might lose somebody that I love. You have to feel all that experience to be able to get to the, to do it cleanly." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

Cleanness emerges through feeling. The first rep is rarely clean. The second is cleaner. The fifth is usually pretty clean. The reps teach you cleanness by surfacing each layer of armor the pattern had been wearing.

See [[take-five-counter-actions-immediately]] for the move-level treatment.

## The Emotional Substrate

The procedural surface is the easy part. Underneath each A is feeling-through work. The framework's effectiveness depends entirely on whether the feelings are being felt.

"You can just do the stuff rote and it works pretty well, works a lot better if you do the stuff and feel everything that comes up while you're doing the stuff. That's when it really works super well to change the behavior." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The mapping of feelings to steps:
- **Announce** forces you to feel fear of reputational loss, exposure, public failure.
- **Apologize** forces you to feel the impact your pattern has had on others.
- **Ask** forces you to feel the guilt of needing help and the relief of not being alone.
- **Act** forces you to feel the fears the pattern was protecting you from (rejection, attack, loss).

These are usually the feelings the pattern existed to avoid. By structuring contact with them, the framework dismantles the avoidance the pattern was built on.

"Picturing a milk box that says the 4 A's, you're like, oh, this is easy. And then someone flips it over in the back and they see the ingredients. Oh, decades of unfelt shame and inadequacy and fear of rejection and all the love that I was pushing away." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The closing reframe of the ingredient-list metaphor: seeing the substrate clearly is itself part of why the framework works. "Just look at the entire ingredient list and be like, oh, this is what's been poisoning me for years. Put that down now." -- Joe Hudson, You See The Pattern. Your Life Hasn't Changed. Now What?

The instruction is not "feel each ingredient one at a time forever." It is "see what has been in this all along, and stop ingesting it." The 4 A's are the structured form for putting the contents down. Each A surfaces a piece of the ingredient list and makes it possible to release it through action.

The grim version of this is: those feelings have to be felt at some point if the change is going to happen. The 4 A's just compress and channel the feeling work into a structured form that produces visible behavior change as a byproduct.

"This methodology makes all of that easier than doing it otherwise. You're gonna have to do it otherwise, right? If you're changing your behavior, feeling all that pain, feeling all that shame, feeling the grief, going through the fear that people are gonna reject you or whatever it is, is all gonna have to happen. It is harder to just wake up and go, okay, today I'm gonna journal on this and feel this thing." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## Why The Order Front-Loads The Work

The order (announce → apologize → ask → act) is not arbitrary. Each step prepares the ground for the next.

By the time you reach the act step, you have:
- Already surfaced the fears (announce)
- Already softened the shame (apologize)
- Already restructured the social field around the new behavior (ask)
- Already mapped where the pattern lives (the five instances)

The acting then becomes simple. Most of the heavy lifting has been done in the preparation. You aren't fighting yourself to act in a vacuum. You're acting into a field that has been deliberately reshaped to receive the new behavior.

"So the more you front-load those emotions by announcing widely and specifically and asking, and apologizing, that whole part makes the acting easier, makes the actions you're going to take more expected and more well received. And makes it more likely that those actions... having felt that, apologizing and getting underneath and feeling what had been behind shame all this time, holding those habits in place. And then asking and changing the social field around you through that dynamic. That's a lot of work before you get to the action. The action then is just pretty simple." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

This is also why the framework is "efficient use of willpower and dopamine." You don't have to keep mustering will every time the situation arises. The will is spent once, in the announcement and apology and ask. The acts are then mostly enabled by the field you have already created.

"Once you have done the thing where you announce, you have announced. Once you have apologized, you have apologized. Once you've asked for how you want to be interacted with, you've done that. And then once you have identified 5 ways to act, you do them one time each. And chances are the behavior has shifted deeply." -- Joe Hudson, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

## What This Doesn't Work For

The 4 A's are an interpersonal-domain tool. They don't fit bad-habit work (smoking, eating, exercising, scrolling).

"This doesn't work with bad habits... Smoking, overeating, not exercising. Because oftentimes bad habits are changing, is stopping a behavior. It's not starting a new behavior. Let's even say, I want to exercise every day. How do I take 5 actions today exercising every day? You're all of a sudden, it's not immediately getting done. It's you have to do it daily, which requires some discipline." -- Joe, How to Change Your Interpersonal Patterns

The structural mismatch: bad habits often require daily repeated stopping, not a discrete shift in how you show up with specific people. The "apologize to" target is also unclear — apologizing to yourself works (see [[apologize-as-daily-practice-for-bad-habits]]) but the relational mechanism of the 4 A's (your changing changes them) doesn't operate the same way.

For bad habits, the corresponding work lives in [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] and the daily upright apology practice. For interpersonal patterns, the 4 A's are the structured form.

## How This Can Go Wrong

The main failure mode: running the framework from a should rather than a recognition. See [[dont-run-the-four-as-from-a-should]].

The secondary failure mode: executing the steps rote, with the feelings actively avoided. The procedural form works pretty well, but the feeling-while-acting is what makes it work super well. Skipping the feeling reduces the framework to behavioral compliance, which tends to revert.

The third failure mode: not actually going wide enough on the announcement. Telling one person and calling it done leaves the social field unchanged, which leaves the identity grip of the pattern intact.

The fourth failure mode: shame-based apology. A shame apology reinstalls the very loop the framework was supposed to break. The upright apology is non-negotiable here. See [[upright-apology]] and the warning in the apologize step above.

The fifth failure mode: only one or two acts instead of five. The lonely contrary action is fragile. Five gives you the spread to learn the cleanness and the reps to consolidate.

## Tone

Joe presents the 4 A's matter-of-factly, with a quiet enthusiasm for how simple-but-not-easy it is. The framework's simplicity is the appeal: easy to remember, easy to deploy when the recognition lands. The not-easy part is acknowledged honestly: "It's simple, but it's not easy."

The pitch is not "do this and your life will change." The pitch is "when you have the recognition, this is the cleanest structure for the work the recognition is asking for."

## When To Reach For This Framework

- When a client has just had a recognition about an interpersonal pattern.
- When someone reports "I've seen this for years and nothing changes."
- When the work is at the level of how someone shows up with their team, family, partner, or community.
- When the standard advice to "feel the unfelt emotions" has surfaced the recognition but the person doesn't know how to operationalize it.
- When the consciousness-side work has stalled and behavior change is needed to update the consciousness. See [[change-behavior-to-change-consciousness]] for the underlying principle.
