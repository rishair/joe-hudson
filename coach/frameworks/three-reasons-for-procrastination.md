# Three Reasons for Procrastination (Timing, Method, Emotional Avoidance)

## The Framework

Once the orientation has shifted from bullying-through to listening (see [[procrastination-is-wisdom]]), the next question is: *what specifically is the not-doing telling you?* There are three answers Joe sees again and again. Each is a distinct kind of misalignment between the intellectual self that says "do it" and the rest of the system that won't.

1. **Wrong timing.** Your intuition perceives a pattern your logical brain cannot articulate. The action is right, but the moment is wrong.
2. **Wrong method.** The way you are thinking about doing it (perfectionism, dread, overplanning) makes the doing intolerable. The body refuses on quality grounds.
3. **Avoided emotion.** Doing the thing requires feeling something — failure, exposure, grief, embarrassment — that you have been protecting against. The body refuses to walk into the unfelt feeling.

> "Typically, there's 3 reasons why you're not in alignment and why you're not doing the thing that you think you should be doing. The first one is that it's not the right time. The second reason is because the way that you're doing it or the way you're thinking about doing it isn't gonna be a good way. And the last one is because you have an emotional experience that you're trying to avoid." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

The framework is a triage. When the procrastination is investigated, one of these three (sometimes more than one) is operating. The intervention for each is different.

## Reason One: Wrong Timing

The most invisible of the three because the logical brain has no language for it. The right side of the brain perceives something — a pattern, a quality of the situation, a felt sense — that has not yet been translated into reasons. The left side, demanding reasons, cannot find any and concludes the not-doing is unjustified. The intuition is right anyway.

> "Couple years later, this person had a big fallout. It was a big failure, it was a public failure. And I immediately reached out because I knew at that point there was gonna be enough humility where that person knew that they could be wrong, that they didn't see everything, and therefore the work that I was gonna do with them was gonna be more meaningful to me and more effective for them." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

The canonical case: see [[joes-famous-ceo-not-the-right-time-example]]. Joe was offered a high-profile coaching engagement, his logical brain said yes, his intuition said no. He waited. Two years later, a public failure produced the humility that made the work meaningful. The intuition had been reading the readiness condition that the logical brain could not see.

The intervention: stop trying to rationalize the not-doing into yes or no. Drop into the felt sense. Ask "is this the right time?" and notice what the body says. If the answer is wait, wait without needing to defend the wait. Patience with intuition is itself a discipline.

> "If you look for a rational explanation, then you're gonna actually forego some of the intuition that's actually happening in the right side of your brain." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] for the somatic test that distinguishes intuitive wait from conditioned avoidance. Both feel like "not now"; only one carries the heart-open signature of intuition.

## Reason Two: Wrong Method

The procrastination is not about whether to do it. It's about how. The way the person has been imagining doing it — perfectly, comprehensively, definitively — makes the doing emotionally intolerable. The body knows the cost of engaging the loaded version and protects against it through refusal.

The canonical case in the source episode: Joe coached someone whose procrastination was driven entirely by perfectionism. Every attempt to do the work was an attempt to do it perfectly. The work was therefore unfun, the body refused, the procrastination set in. Joe's intervention: redirect from "how do I do this right" to "how do I enjoy doing this" (see [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]]). The moment the perfectionism dropped, the work became available.

> "All the procrastination was that he was trying to do everything perfectly... as soon as they saw, oh, instead of trying to get it perfect, my job is to enjoy the process, boom, they started working on it right away." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

The diagnostic signature: the person cannot get the thing started but is functional in other domains. They are loading this specific task with importance, perfectionism, or some other quality-demand that makes the doing intolerable. See [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] for the deeper principle on importance-as-engine-of-procrastination.

The intervention: surface the standard the person has been holding themselves to, drop it, and reframe the work as something to enjoy and iterate. Iteration on the unloaded version beats refusal on the loaded version on every dimension that matters. See [[the-perfectionism-that-stops-you-from-starting]] for the read.

## Reason Three: Avoided Emotion

The procrastination is the body's protection against a feeling the person has been refusing to feel. Doing the thing requires walking into the unfelt emotion. The body, sensing the cost, declines.

The canonical case in the source episode: a technologist with major space-industry credentials was offered a high-profile job with a big title and salary. She declined and somewhat regretted it. In coaching, the avoidance became visible: she did not want the responsibility of a potentially big public failure. The unfelt feeling of "I'm not good enough" / "I might fail" was the lock. The avoidance was protecting her from having to feel that.

> "What we started to realize was there was just an emotion there that she was trying to avoid, that feeling of failure, that feeling of I'm not good enough. And so what we started to do is we saw that that feeling of failure was preventing her from doing a lot of things, speaking her mind in meetings or advocating for the thing that she knew was the right thing to do." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

The intervention: pre-feel the avoided emotion. The technologist visualized failure scenarios repeatedly over three to four weeks, feeling everything that would arise. She also visualized success and felt that. Both ends of the spectrum got pre-felt. The fear of failure dissolved. The capacity for the big role became available. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] for the practice and [[fear-is-about-feeling]] for the principle underneath it (fear is always about what you'll have to feel, not about the event itself).

The diagnostic signature: the person can articulate a logical reason for not doing the thing, but the logic doesn't quite hold up. There is an emotional charge around the task that exceeds the rational case against it. When asked what would be the worst thing about doing it, the answer points at a feeling rather than at an external consequence.

## Why the Action That Follows Is Better

A structural feature of the framework: the action that emerges after the diagnosis is consistently more effective than the action that would have come from bullying through. This is not coincidence; it is the consequence of the misalignment having been resolved rather than overridden.

> "If you actually solve those problems, how you're going to do the thing is going to be far more effective than if you just force yourself to do it." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

The CEO Joe waited two years for did more meaningful work because the timing was right. The perfectionist who shifted from perfection-mode to enjoyment-mode produced a better product because iteration was now available. The technologist who pre-felt failure was more capable in big roles because the fear was no longer driving the room. The diagnosis-then-act sequence consistently outperforms the bully-through sequence.

## How to Use the Framework

The framework is a triage, not a checklist. When chronic procrastination shows up, ask in order:

1. **Is the timing right?** Drop into the body. Notice if there is a felt-sense "wait" that you cannot explain. If yes, the wisdom is wait — and use the intuition. (See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] to distinguish from avoidance.)
2. **Is the method right?** Notice the standard you are imagining holding yourself to. Is the work loaded with perfectionism, importance, or dread? If yes, the wisdom is *change the method.* See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]].
3. **Is there an emotion under this?** Notice what would have to be felt if you actually did the thing. Failure? Exposure? Loss? If yes, the wisdom is *feel the thing* and proceed from there. See [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]].

Often more than one is operating. The technologist's case had emotional avoidance as the primary lock, but also a method problem (she had loaded the role with stakes that made it harder than it needed to be). The perfectionism case had method as primary, but also an emotion (the fear of being seen as imperfect) underneath. The triage surfaces the primary lock; secondary locks are addressed once the primary one releases.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They have been telling themselves to do something and haven't done it for an extended period
- The bullying voice is the dominant talker in their relationship with the task
- They have tried discipline, accountability, and time-blocking and the task is still undone
- Asked "what would happen if you just sat down and started" they report dread, freeze, or distraction
- They are functional in other domains but not on this specific task (suggests reason two or three)
- They are broadly stuck across many domains (suggests deeper avoidance pattern, see [[how-do-i-stop-watching-porn-and-procrastinating]])
