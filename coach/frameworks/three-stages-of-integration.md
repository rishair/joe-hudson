# Three Stages of Integration

## Definition

Every integration moves through three stages, whether it takes minutes or years. The pattern is visible in infant development and repeats at every level of growth.

## The Three Stages

**Stage 1: Surprise (What the hell is that?)**
The new capacity or perception arrives. It is not understood. It may be disorienting or even frightening. The baby's hand hits its face; the baby does not know the hand is part of it.

"There's the original epiphany that happens, and it leaves us in this kind of unknown, like, what the hell's going on?" -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

**Stage 2: Recognition (I see it, but I can't control it)**
The new thing is identified as real and as belonging to the person. Understanding begins but mastery has not arrived. The baby sees the hand is its own but cannot direct it well.

**Stage 3: Mastery (It's just normal)**
The new capacity is integrated into ordinary functioning. It no longer requires conscious effort. The baby uses its hand without thinking about it. A 3-year-old walks without excitement or awareness of walking.

## The Piaget Frame

The three-stage pattern maps onto what Piaget called primary, secondary, and tertiary circular reactions in infant development. Joe references the terminology to ground the framework: this is not Joe's invention. It is a documented pattern in how human beings acquire new capacities, observable from the first weeks of life.

"Piaget calls this like, I think, primary, secondary, and tertiary circular reaction, I think is the terminology for it. But that doesn't happen just with the use of our hands. It happens like when people like fully access for the first time, let's say, their emotional intelligence or their somatic intelligence or their awareness." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

This matters because adults often expect adult learning to bypass the unclear-to-clear progression. A person has an awakening, cannot articulate it, tries to reconstruct everything they previously believed, and treats the disorientation as a problem. The Piaget mapping says: this is the same sequence the body went through with its own hand at three months old. The disorientation is not a glitch. It is stage one. There is nothing to fix; there is something to allow.

The application to awakening specifically: "Oftentimes they can't even talk. They're like, 'What's going on?' And they like try to reconstruct their life because they're like, 'Well, everything I believed is gone. What the hell's going on?' And that's the way it works. It's like they've walked into this new world that they can't control and they can't even identify the parts of. But slowly you just hang out in the world and everything takes care of itself just like it does with an infant." -- Joe Hudson, Embarking On The Journey

## In Practice

The critical mistake is cutting stages short. Allowing not-knowing to persist as long as it naturally persists produces the deepest integration. Forcing understanding (trying to put words on the experience, striving to figure it out) limits what the transformation can become.

"If you strive to put words on it, strive to understand it, then you're limiting it, you're containing it in a way that doesn't allow it to fully transform you." -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

It is not about trying to stay in the not-knowing. It is about not trying to force the knowing. The knowing arrives on its own. "It's not figured out, it's just like, oh, I get it. And then all of a sudden you can articulate it."

The analogy to infant development is concrete: babies need to crawl for a certain period to develop left-right brain cohesion. Skipping stages has structural consequences.

When mastery arrives, the new capacity becomes invisible. The person may forget it is there because the euphoria of discovery is gone. They confuse the skill with the feeling of the epiphany. Walking at age 3 is not exciting. But the capacity is real.

## When Someone Experiences This

At Stage 1: "I don't know what's happening to me." Disorientation, confusion, feeling unmoored. The coaching response is normalization, not explanation.

At Stage 2: "I see it but I can't seem to hold onto it." Pendulation between the old pattern and the new way. The coaching response is patience and encouragement.

At Stage 3: "I don't even think about it anymore." The person may not recognize the change because it has become ordinary. The coaching response is pointing it out.
