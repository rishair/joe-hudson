# Notice Every Place You Shame Yourself and Be Gentle in That Moment

## The Practice

A daily in-the-moment practice. The instruction has two parts: notice every place you shame yourself, and at that moment, see how gentle you can be with yourself.

The practice is positioned by Joe as the starting move on shame — not the complete work, but the first step. The complete work involves emotional inquiry, feeling the underlying emotions, and the deeper releases described in [[emotional-inquiry]] and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. This practice is the threshold practice that makes the deeper work available.

"The critical piece is to just at least first start noticing every place you shame yourself and see how gentle you can be at that moment." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Change

## How to Do It

**Through the day, notice every place you shame yourself.**

The targets are everyday and small. Most self-shaming is not dramatic. It is brief, almost subliminal, layered into ordinary thoughts. Common forms:

- A should ("I should have gotten more done today")
- A criticism ("that was a stupid thing to say")
- A comparison ("everyone else has this figured out")
- A judgment of a feeling ("I shouldn't be so anxious about this")
- An I'm-no-good thought ("there's something wrong with me")
- A body or appearance criticism ("look at this body")
- A capability dismissal ("I'm bad at this")
- A self-narration about the past ("why did I do that")

The noticing is the whole instruction. Not stopping it. Not analyzing it. Not arguing with it. Just noticing.

**At the moment of noticing, see how gentle you can be.**

The instruction is precise: see how gentle you can be. Not "be perfectly gentle." Not "stop the shame." See how much gentleness is available in this moment. Whatever is available is the right amount.

Gentleness can be a hand on the chest. A softening of the breath. A quiet acknowledgment ("there it is again"). An offer of permission ("it's okay that this is hard"). A small kindness aimed at the same self that was just being shamed.

The form does not matter as much as the orientation. The orientation is from self-attack to self-softening. The size of the softening is whatever fits in the moment.

## Why It Works

Self-shame is the loop's fuel. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] and [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]. The loop runs in the moment-by-moment thoughts of the day. Trying to "stop shaming yourself" as a global project does not work because the global project itself becomes another should, which is another moment of self-shame.

The practice operates at the right scale. It does not try to dismantle the architecture of shame in one move. It interrupts the loop at the moment the loop is happening. Each interruption is small. The interruptions accumulate.

The gentleness piece is structural, not motivational. See [[gentleness-not-trying-to-be-patient]]. Gentleness is what stops adding fuel to the loop. Once the loop loses fuel, the behavior the shame was holding in place loses its lock. The practice is doing structural work even though it looks like soft work.

The "see how gentle you can be" framing matters. It is permission-based, not demand-based. The instruction does not require any specific amount of gentleness. The opening "see how" makes it an experiment rather than a performance. This is critical because if the instruction were "be gentle" it would land as another should, and the practice would become its own engine of self-shame.

## What This Practice Produces

Over time, three things tend to happen:

**The shame becomes visible.** The first stretch of the practice is mostly about discovering how often it is happening. Most people are surprised by the frequency. The shame was not absent before; it was just not being noticed.

**The shame becomes less automatic.** Once it is being noticed, it stops being subliminal. It becomes a thought the person sees rather than a state the person inhabits. The shift from "I am bad" to "I am noticing the thought 'I am bad'" is the beginning of separation from the shame.

**The grip loosens.** As gentleness gets introduced at the moment of shaming, the loop has less fuel. The underlying emotions that the shame was locking start to surface. At this point the deeper work — [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]], [[emotional-inquiry]] — becomes possible.

The practice is not aimed at producing visible behavior change. The behavior change is downstream. The aim is to start interrupting the loop. The downstream effects show up on their own, in their own time.

## When to Reach For This Practice

- A client in [[ive-been-trying-to-change-this-for-years]] who needs a daily handle to work with between sessions.
- A client who has just completed [[list-things-trying-to-change-for-4-years]] and is asking what to do next.
- A client who has just intellectually understood [[shame-drives-behavior]] and needs a way to test it in their own life.
- A client whose self-attack loop is loud enough to dominate the day. The practice gives them a way to relate to the loop other than being inside it.
- A client who has tried the bigger shame-work practices and found them too overwhelming. The in-the-moment scale is more accessible.
- A new client who needs a starting move that does not require a session container.

The practice is also useful as a first prescription. It is small enough to start without preparation, does not require any specific belief, and produces its own data.

## What Makes This Practice Distinct

The shame-work literature has many practices. What is specific to this one:

**The unit is the moment, not the session.** The practice happens whenever shame happens, not at a dedicated time. This is closer to the actual structure of the problem — the shame is not happening once a week in a session, it is happening many times a day in ordinary life.

**The instruction has no completion criterion.** There is no "done." There is just the next moment of noticing. This removes the should-architecture that would otherwise reform around the practice.

**The gentleness is calibrated to availability.** "See how gentle you can be" rather than "be gentle" leaves the magnitude open. Some moments will admit more gentleness than others. The practice respects this rather than demanding uniform performance.

**It is positioned as the first step, not the whole work.** Joe is explicit: this is what to start with. The fuller practices (emotional inquiry, working with the underlying emotions) follow. The first-step framing prevents the practice from being mistaken for the whole answer and protects against the disappointment of "I'm doing this and the behavior isn't changing yet."

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a thought-stopping technique. The instruction is to notice, not to stop.
- It is not a positive-thinking replacement. Replacing "I'm bad" with "I'm great" is just running the should in a different direction.
- It is not a self-soothing performance. The gentleness is real or it is nothing. Performed gentleness is another form of the should.
- It is not a substitute for the deeper shame work. The practice opens the door; the work behind the door is the rest of the methodology — emotional inquiry, the underlying emotion, the welcoming of the shame itself.

## Tone

Light, undramatic, almost throwaway in its delivery. The practice is offered as the obvious place to start, not as an elaborate technique. The casualness of the framing is part of what makes it accessible. A person can begin in the next minute, with the next thought.

The "at least" in Joe's phrasing — "the critical piece is to just at least first start noticing" — is doing work. It positions the practice as the minimum starting point, not the maximum requirement. The person is not being asked to do everything. They are being asked to start with this much, and that this much will move things.
