# Ask From Slowness, Not From the Intellect

## The Practice

When facing a decision that the intellect is pushing to resolve, deliberately consult a different part of yourself: the part that is unhurried. The part that knows stability and calmness. Ask that part what it wants to do. Notice that the answer is often not what the intellect would have produced and not on the timeline the intellect would have demanded.

The practice has two moves. First, notice that the intellect is in push-mode and trying to force a resolution. Second, redirect the question to the slower, quieter part. Let that part answer in its own time.

## The Example That Generated the Practice

Joe describes the decision about whether to do a particular event the next year. Facilities have to be rented far in advance. The question demands a decision. The intellect is pushing. Joe notices the push and tries a different inquiry:

"What if I ask from my slowness? What if I just go to the part of me that knows that stability and calmness and ask that, 'Oh, what do I want to do?' And so I did." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The answer that came was not an instruction. It was: "more will be revealed."

"What did I expect to ask slowness? Like, to ask slowness, 'Well, what's the answer?' It's like, well, this is the answer and go do that right now. It was no. Slowness was like, 'Yeah, you know, just relax. It'll come to you. More will be revealed. You're fine.'" -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The answer was the recognition that the urgency was itself the problem. The decision was not yet ready to be made. The slowness was not refusing the question. It was reframing the question. The push to decide was the actual thing to drop.

## Why It Works

The intellect, when pushed by overwhelm, operates inside the urgency that produced the overwhelm. It asks: "what is the answer to this question?" inside a frame of "this question must be answered now." The frame itself is often the problem. Inside a more relaxed frame, the same question may not require the same urgent answer.

A different part of the self -- call it the slow part, the centered part, the part connected to deeper knowing -- is not operating inside the urgency. It can see the larger picture, including the picture of whether the question really needs to be answered today, whether more information is coming, whether the apparent forcing of the situation is actual or imagined.

The practice gives that part of you a voice. The voice may not arrive immediately. The slowness may need time to respond, because slowness is what it is.

## What the Slowness Often Says

The slowness rarely says: "Here is the answer. Do this."

The slowness often says:
- "Wait. You don't need to decide right now."
- "More will be revealed."
- "Relax. You're fine."
- "The push to decide is not coming from clarity. It is coming from fear."
- "Stay focused but stop forcing."

This is not vague spirituality. It is the systematic reframing that comes from a part of you that is not running on the urgency the rest of you is running on. The reframing is often what was needed, not the decision the urgency demanded.

## When This Practice Is Especially Useful

- When you notice you are pushing to decide
- When the decision has a real deadline but the deadline is not actually right now
- When multiple options are circling and the intellect cannot settle
- When the overwhelm is partly about the decision and you cannot tell if the urgency is real
- When you have been trying to figure something out for a long time and the figuring is producing more anxiety

## The Anti-Pattern Reversed

This practice is the deliberate counter to [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]]. If the felt sense is "I must decide this now," the felt sense itself is fear. The practice of asking from slowness is one way to test whether the urgency is real or whether the urgency is the fear talking.

The practice is also a specific case of [[feel-over-figure]]. The slowness is more felt than figured. The intellect is figuring. The slowness is sensing. Different organ. Different timescale. Different kind of answer.

## What Joe Found in Retrospect

"If I look back in my world, the more I have forced a situation to make a decision, usually the worse the decision." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The forced decisions tend to be the bad ones. The decisions that emerged from a less forced state tend to be the good ones. This is a pattern visible across many situations once you start looking for it.

## How to Do It

1. Notice that the intellect is pushing. The signature: rapid mental motion, the sense that an answer must be produced now, the recycling of the same options without resolution.
2. Pause. Take some breaths. Let the body settle a little. See [[breath-to-exit-overwhelm]].
3. Turn the question to a different part of yourself. The phrasing can be: "What does the slowness in me want to do?" or "What does the calm part of me know?" or just "What if I ask from the part of me that isn't in a hurry?"
4. Wait. The answer may not arrive immediately. The slowness operates on slowness's timescale.
5. Receive whatever comes. The answer is often not an instruction. It may be a reframing of the question. It may be permission to not decide. It may be a small, quiet preference that the intellect had been overriding.
6. Notice what the answer does to the system. Does the body relax? Does the spinning stop? Does the question feel resolved or differently held?

## What Someone Experiences

The answer from slowness, when it comes, often produces a recognition that the urgency was self-generated. The decision that felt like life or death starts to feel like a normal decision that can be made when ready. The push that was driving the overwhelm releases a notch. Sometimes the decision becomes obvious in the absence of the push. Sometimes the decision is still not ready, and the slowness has named that.

The experience is closer to remembering than to figuring. The slow part of you already knew. The intellect was just running too fast to hear it.

## Tone

Slow, slightly amused at the absurdity of expecting slowness to produce a fast answer. Joe finds the moment funny in retrospect: he asked slowness what to do, and slowness said "more will be revealed," which was somehow exactly the right response.
