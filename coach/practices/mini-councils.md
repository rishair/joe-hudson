# Mini-Councils

## The Practice

Take the structure of the AOA Council program (a multi-day intensive on building high-trust relationships and learning to embrace conflict within a group) and adapt it into a smaller, on-demand format that can be deployed inside a company when specific friction emerges between functions, teams, or individuals.

The mini-council is not a workshop. It is a structured conversation, designed using the same principles as the full Council program, scaled to the specific conflict at hand. Two to six people in a room with the protocol; the goal is to surface the projection, name the trigger, add the missing data points, and emerge with deeper trust and a workable next step.

"We've also taken those concepts and now have started what we call like mini councils. So if there is like smaller conflict between, between functions or overwhelm, we set up those mini council groups where we like talk about these things and bring them up." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

The structure is borrowed. The original Council program is a weekend-long intensive run by AOA where leadership teams learn to have a different kind of conversation. The mini-council takes the conversation pattern that the original program teaches and uses it as the unit of work in smaller contexts.

## What the Mini-Council Borrows From the Original Council

The structural elements that get carried over:

**Projection-first.** The conversation starts with each participant naming what they are projecting onto the other. The triggers, the annoyances, the assumptions they are making. The projection-first move shifts the conversation from "what you are doing wrong" to "what I am bringing to this." See [[projections]] for the underlying concept.

**Adding data points.** Each participant brings information the others have not had. The full picture of the friction emerges only when everyone has spoken. The structured turn-taking ensures that the loudest voice does not dominate.

**Embracing conflict rather than avoiding it.** The protocol pre-commits the participants to running toward the friction rather than smoothing over it. See [[conflict-as-evolutionary-tension]] and [[embrace-intensity]] for the underlying orientation.

**Self-understanding as the goal, not winning.** The conversation is structured so that the win-condition is mutual understanding, not one side prevailing. The participants are working on themselves through the conflict, not just resolving the conflict.

These four elements together make the mini-council structurally different from a normal meeting about a problem. A normal meeting moves to "what do we do about X?" The mini-council holds space for "what is each of us actually bringing to X, and what does that change about how we move forward?"

## When to Use a Mini-Council

The practice fits specific kinds of friction:

**Cross-functional tension.** Sales blaming product. Product blaming engineering. Engineering blaming sales. The mini-council surfaces the projections each function is running on the others. The functions emerge with a different understanding of each other, not just a different decision about the disputed item.

**Repeated small conflicts that point at a larger pattern.** When two teams keep clashing over different things, the mini-council can surface the underlying dynamic. The specific conflicts are symptoms; the mini-council goes to the root.

**Overwhelm in a sub-team.** When a team is in overwhelm and the leader is not sure why, the mini-council provides a structured way to surface what each member is actually carrying. The implicit load becomes visible. See [[reducing-overwhelm-in-organizations]].

**Pre-decision alignment.** Before a difficult decision that affects multiple functions, a mini-council can surface the concerns each function would otherwise raise after the fact. The decision then incorporates the data the council surfaced. The team commits to the decision with less downstream re-litigation.

**Post-crisis processing.** After a project failure, a missed quarter, or a customer escalation, the mini-council allows the affected parties to surface their actual experience of the event. The post-mortem becomes a relational repair as well as an analytical one.

## What This Practice Replaces

The default mode for company conflict is one of three patterns, each of which the mini-council improves on:

**Suppression.** The conflict is not addressed; the participants work around each other. The friction compounds invisibly. See [[toxic-niceness]] and [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]].

**CEO arbitration.** The conflict is escalated to the CEO, who decides. The decision usually leaves at least one party feeling unheard, and the underlying friction reappears in the next conflict.

**Bureaucratic resolution.** The conflict is processed through HR or a formal grievance procedure. The procedure protects against legal exposure but produces no relational change.

The mini-council replaces all three with a structured conversation between the actual parties, mediated by the protocol rather than by an authority. The parties do the work themselves. The conflict gets resolved through understanding rather than through arbitration or avoidance.

## What This Practice Requires

The practice does not work without preconditions:

**Shared vocabulary.** The participants need a common framework for what they are doing. In Johannes's company, the AOA work over years had installed the vocabulary (projection, embrace intensity, open the drawer). Without that vocabulary, the mini-council would need a facilitator to provide the framing in real time, which is slower and harder.

**Some baseline trust.** Mini-councils between people who have no relationship history do not work. The practice scales a Council-trained team's relational capacity into smaller engagements. It does not bootstrap relationships from zero. For zero-baseline teams, the full Council program (or equivalent intensive) is the prerequisite. See [[trust-requires-conflict]] for the trust-building mechanism that has to happen first.

**Time and stillness.** A mini-council needs uninterrupted time and a place where the participants can be present. A quick 15-minute conversation between meetings does not produce the same depth. The minimum is usually an hour; longer for more complex conflicts.

**Leadership endorsement.** The practice has to be visible enough that participants do not feel they are doing something subversive. When the CEO has done their own Council work and the leadership team uses the practice, the rest of the organization adopts it more readily.

## Why This Scales the Original Council

The full Council program (the weekend intensive) is high-investment. Companies can do it once or twice a year. The frictions that emerge between offsites cannot all wait for the next intensive. The mini-council variant is the operational solution: the same conversation pattern, applied as the frictions arise, without requiring everyone to clear a weekend.

The trade-off: the mini-council does not produce the depth of relational transformation that the full Council program does. It produces a smaller version of the same kind of work, applied to a specific friction. The depth comes from the cumulative effect of many mini-councils over time, plus the periodic full-Council intensives that reset the baseline.

The Johannes example shows the layering: the leadership team did the full Council program as a weekend intensive. They then deployed mini-councils for the smaller frictions that emerged. The combination produced a culture where hard conversations were available at multiple scales.

## What Was Surprising About the First Council Application

The first time Johannes proposed bringing the Council program into his company, the framing was novel. Joe had not conceived of running Council as a team exercise -- the program had previously been run with mixed groups of leaders who did not work together. Johannes asked whether they could do it as an intact team.

"Johannes was, you know, did the math and he was like, this, if I get my team to do the council, that'll be cheaper than you doing offsites with us. Can we do this as a team? And I hadn't even conceived of it as a team thing. And I was a little nervous, but Johannes reminded me of my own principles. And, and one of them is everything is an iteration. And so I said, great." -- Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles

The experiment produced an outcome neither had predicted: the intact-team Council was more powerful than the mixed-group Council in specific ways, because the participants had history with each other. The patterns they were working on were not abstract; they were the patterns that were active in their daily work.

"You did the same work that these other councils were... except for you all then work together. So you had this massive history and you got to see through so much of your own patterns in a way that the folks -- yes, it took a lot longer for the folks who, who are, you know, just like getting to know each other in leadership or our councils or friends. But it's like working with together, it's like a -- it's bringing your backpack." -- Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles

The "backpack" framing: the intact team brings their existing relational patterns into the Council. The Council becomes a context for working through those patterns directly rather than abstractly. The downside is that the patterns are sticky; the upside is that the work transfers immediately into the daily work because the daily work is what was being worked on.

The mini-council inherits this property at smaller scale. The team that does mini-councils on their actual conflicts is working on the patterns that show up in their real work. The work transfers automatically because it never left.

## Boundary Conditions

The mini-council is not a replacement for the founder's own work. The leader who runs mini-councils for their team without doing their own work installs the practice as a team-level intervention while their own patterns continue to operate. See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]]. The practice scales the leader's own relational capacity; it does not substitute for it.

The practice also requires real consent. A mini-council that participants attend because they were told to attend is structurally different from one they enter willingly. The protocol assumes participants want the conversation. If they do not, the practice produces performance, not real engagement.

The practice is also not a one-size-fits-all replacement for direct intervention. Some conflicts require a direct decision, fast, from someone with authority. The mini-council is not appropriate when the situation needs a unilateral move. The leader's judgment about which conflicts get a mini-council and which get a direct intervention is part of what the leader has to develop.

## When Joe Reaches for This Practice

- A leader is asking how to address recurring cross-functional friction without escalating it to themselves every time
- A team has done the Council program and is asking how to apply it between intensives
- A CEO is in conflict-arbitration burnout and is looking for a structural move
- An organization has the AOA vocabulary installed but does not have a regular practice that uses it
- A team is in overwhelm and the source is not clear -- the mini-council is the discovery mechanism

## Tone

Pragmatic, not ceremonial. Joe describes the mini-council as a practical adaptation, not a sacred ritual. The structure is what does the work. The participants do not need to be reverent about the form; they need to follow it. The work is in the conversation, and the conversation works when the structure holds.
