# Reflection After an Hour

## The Practice

Once an hour, stop. Take a moment. Ask: what did I actually do in the last hour? What project did I actually move forward?

There is one rule. "Responded to people" is not an answer. Responding is not a thing you did. The question is what moved.

If the answer is "nothing actually moved," that is the data the practice exists to produce. The data is not bad. The data is useful. The data is what makes the next hour different.

## Joe's Words

"Take a moment to reflect every once in a while. Okay, I just worked for an hour. What did I actually do? And you don't get to say responded to people. Responded to people isn't a thing you do. Like, what did I actually move forward? What, what project did I actually move forward?" -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The "you don't get to say responded to people" is the operative constraint. Without it, the practice collapses. With it, the practice becomes a reliable instrument for detecting spin.

## Why the Constraint Matters

The cultural default is to count responding as doing. Email replies are counted as productivity. Slack messages handled are counted as productivity. Meetings attended are counted as productivity. The constraint of the practice disallows these as valid answers, because they are not the work; they are the channels of the work.

Once the channels are disallowed, the question forces a real answer. Either something moved, or it didn't. The honesty is the value.

The most common discovery: large stretches of "productive" time produce no actual forward motion. The hour was spent on the channels, not the work. The practice surfaces this in time to do something about it, rather than after a week or a month of having spent the hours on channels.

## How to Run It

1. **Set a timer.** Hourly is the rhythm Joe names; it is short enough to catch the drift before it has eaten the day.
2. **At the chime, stop for 30-60 seconds.** Not longer. The practice is light. The weight is in the question, not in the time spent.
3. **Ask the question.** "What did I actually move forward in that hour? What project did I actually advance?"
4. **Apply the rule.** "Responded to people" is not an answer.
5. **If something moved, name it specifically.** Not "worked on the deck." "Finished the section on competitive positioning." Specificity is part of the discipline.
6. **If nothing moved, name that.** "Nothing actually moved." The naming is the practice working. Now you know.
7. **Use the answer to set the next hour.** If nothing moved, what would actually move something in the next hour? Pivot.

## Why the Hourly Cadence

A day is too long. A week is way too long. By the end of a day spent on channels, the person has already paid the cost; the reflection is post-mortem. By the end of a week, the cost is compounded.

An hour is short enough that a missed hour does not collapse the day. The practice catches the drift early, when course-correction is still cheap.

It is also short enough that the practice itself doesn't become another spin-producing meta-task. The 30-60 seconds it takes is negligible compared to the cost of an hour of unexamined work.

## What People Discover Running This

- **Most "productive" hours are channel-hours, not work-hours.** The discovery is uncomfortable and useful.
- **The dopamine of completion is independent of actual advancement.** A person can have a full hour of completion-dopamine and zero actual movement. The practice teaches the body the difference.
- **The leveraged tasks rarely happen by accident.** When the leveraged task is the focus of the hour, the answer to "what moved?" is satisfying. When the leveraged task is not the focus, the answer is hollow.
- **The body's tone in the post-hour reflection is also data.** Hours that produced real movement leave the body feeling settled. Hours that produced channels leave the body feeling buzzy or hollow. The body knows; the practice gives the body permission to report.

## Pairing With Other Practices

- **With [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]]:** the three-things practice sets the week. The hourly reflection checks the alignment hour-by-hour. The three-things practice without the hourly reflection drifts. The hourly reflection without the three-things practice is reactive.
- **With the body-presence practice:** the hourly reflection is a natural moment to check breath, body, posture. The reflection on what was produced and the reflection on how the body was during the production are the same check at different layers.
- **With [[what-is-the-productivity-for]]:** the hourly reflection is the operational moment when the forest question lands. If the hours are full and the forest is missing, the practice will keep producing hollow answers. The hollow answers point to the missing forest.

## The Adjacent Practice (Week-Level Retrospective)

Joe pairs the hourly reflection with a slower version:

"There's something really sweet about retrospectively looking at how did I spend my week. Is that how I want to spend my week? That's also really useful. It's not quite in the moment, but it's, it's in the very near term." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The week-level retrospective catches what the hourly reflection cannot: the structural patterns. A week of hours that each looked fine in isolation can produce a week-level retrospective that surfaces a structural problem with how the time is being spent. Running both rhythms is the practice in full.

## The Macro Counterpart (30,000-Foot View)

A third altitude exists above the week-level retrospective. The hourly check asks "did this hour produce something?" The weekly retrospective asks "did this week look the way I wanted?" The 30,000-foot view asks "is this getting me where I want to go at all?" See [[thirty-thousand-foot-view-direction-check]].

The three altitudes catch different failure modes. The hourly reflection alone misses direction-drift. The 30,000-foot view alone misses hourly spin. A person with full coverage of all three altitudes is unlikely to spend long stretches doing work that is either non-productive at the hour level or pointed in the wrong direction at the multi-month level. Most people run zero of the three. Even one is a major upgrade. All three is structural.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a productivity-tracking app. The practice depends on internal honesty, not external measurement.
- It is not a self-criticism practice. The hollow answers are data, not evidence of failure. Treating them as failure-evidence would re-introduce the fear-based productivity engine the practice exists to interrupt. See [[productivity-as-fear-based-engine]].
- It is not a time-management practice. Time management asks "how do I fit more in?" This practice asks "did anything actually move?" These are different questions at different layers.

## When to Use It

- During any period of intense work where spin is a risk.
- When the person is in [[i-keep-getting-stuff-done-but-nothing-changes]].
- When the channels (email, Slack, meetings) are dominating the day.
- When the person reports an emotional hangover at the end of "productive" days.
- As an ongoing rhythm for anyone whose work involves multiple channels of incoming demand.
