# Say "I'm Excited" Ten Times

## The Practice

When the imposter feeling -- or any fear that has the flavor of stepping into a bigger room -- arrives, say out loud "I'm excited" ten times.

"I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited."

Ten repetitions. Out loud. In the moment.

"Just say, I'm excited 10 times. I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, just like that. 10 times. I know it sounds absolutely crazy, but try it. It works so well." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

## Purpose

The imposter feeling is fear. The fear is excitement without the breath. See [[fear-vs-excitement]]. The same physiological energy presents as fear when the breath is constricted and as excitement when it is allowed.

Verbally repeating "I'm excited" does two things at once. First, it relabels what the body is doing: the activated state gets routed through "excitement" rather than through "fear." Second, the act of speaking requires breath. The constricted breath that was holding the energy as fear loosens. The body's state shifts toward what it would have been with breath all along.

The repetition matters. One declaration does not land. Ten declarations move the system. The practice trades one explanation for another and lets the body catch up.

## When To Use It

- When imposter syndrome shows up before doing something. Speaking in public, stepping into a bigger room, traveling somewhere new, taking on a project bigger than the last one.
- When the fear is the kind that has the flavor of expanding rather than the flavor of survival. Use this hack for the bigger-room fear, not for danger-fear. (For survival-fear, see [[fear-vs-excitement]] for the somatic distinction.)
- When the person is in their head trying to convince themselves out of imposter syndrome and losing. The verbal practice bypasses the argument.

## What It Produces

The activated state stays. The constriction loosens. The same energy that was being read as fear gets read as excitement. The bigger room becomes accessible without the gripping.

"What you're really doing is just allowing that fear to be unrestricted and turn into the excitement that's actually there. And that's a great way to integrate it because it's not just a thought, it's an emotional integration." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

The distinction from purely mental reframing: this is somatic, not intellectual. The body is doing something during the repetitions. The breath returns. That is the integration.

## Variations and Notes

- A research-supported version adds jumping up and down while saying "I'm excited." The added movement amplifies the body shift. See the brief reference in [[fear-vs-excitement]].
- Saying it silently does not work as well. The breath-on-the-words is part of the mechanism.
- The "feels absolutely crazy" objection is part of the practice. Do it anyway. The cringe is the system noticing that a habit is being interrupted.

## The Stage-Fright Use (Pre-Performance Discharge)

Tara teaches this practice as the first move for stage fright proper -- not just for the lighter imposter-syndrome flavor of fear-of-a-bigger-room, but for the full body-flooded version of pre-performance anxiety. The structure is the same as the imposter use; the application is broader:

"It's like, I'm scared, I'm scared, I'm scared. Breathing. And then reframe it as excitement. I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

She pairs the verbal repetition with body movement -- pumping the hands over the head, flapping the wrists like bird wings, panting the breath -- to amplify the discharge. The body movement is doing what the breath alone does at smaller scales: giving the held activation an outlet.

The COVID-era variant: executives who had no problem presenting in a room would suddenly freeze on Zoom calls. Tara's intervention was two minutes of hand-movement and deep breathing under the table before the Zoom call started. The same mechanism. The same body-shift. The freeze did not happen.

What differs from the imposter-syndrome use:

- The activation is often more severe -- panic attacks, throwing up, complete body shutdown rather than just the felt edge of imposter syndrome.
- The body component is more important. Verbal repetition alone may not move the larger activation. Add the movement.
- The practice may need to happen for longer than ten reps -- more like two minutes of sustained repetition with movement until the body has actually discharged.
- The setting matters. The COVID-era under-the-table version is the small-scale teaching: do this where you can, in whatever form is available, before stepping into the moment.

What stays the same: the same energy that the body is reading as fear is what would be read as excitement with breath. The verbal repetition does the relabeling; the breath required for the repetition does the unkinking; the movement helps the activation move when it is too much for breath alone.

## Use After a Receiving Breakthrough (The Post-Deconstruction Discharge)

A second use of the practice arrives mid-session, not as preparation for an upcoming fear-flavored event but as discharge for tension that is exiting the body live.

When a structural belief like "I'm not enough" has just been deconstructed (see [[prove-to-me-youre-not-enough]] and [[run-the-compliment-deconstruction-protocol]]) or a compliment has just begun to actually land, the body often produces a shakiness or trembling as the long-held tension releases. The shakiness is the body discharging the energy it has been holding to maintain the deflection. Left alone, the shakiness can be metabolized as discomfort or even as fear-of-the-shift. Routed through the I'm-excited practice, the same shakiness gets metabolized as excitement and the body completes the discharge fully.

"That shakiness, just do me a favor and just say I'm excited 10 times." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Boss Never Sees You

The instruction is delivered immediately after the client reports the somatic shift. In the source session, the client said: "I can feel a lot of tension getting out of my body. Yeah, I can feel that little shakiness." Joe responded with the practice instruction before the client could try to figure out what to do with the shakiness.

The mechanism is the same as the imposter-syndrome use: the verbal labeling routes activated energy through "excitement" rather than through "fear" or "anxiety" or "discomfort." The breath required to say the words ten times opens the channel for the discharge to complete. The body finishes what it started.

The two uses share structure. In the imposter-syndrome use, the practice prevents activated energy from being read as fear before an event. In the post-receiving use, the practice helps activated energy that is already moving through the body get read as excitement during the release. Either way, the practice routes activation toward "I am excited" and lets the breath open the channel.

## When To Use the Post-Receiving Version Specifically

- Right after the body has reported tension exiting ("I can feel tension getting out of my body")
- Right after a long-held belief has been deconstructed and the body has confirmed the deconstruction somatically
- Right after a compliment has begun to actually land for the first time
- When the client reports shakiness, trembling, tingling, or other discharge sensations and the next move is to ride them through rather than examine them
- As the closing somatic move of a session that has done structural work, to let the body integrate before the cognitive register resumes

The instruction in this variant is shorter and more directive than the imposter-syndrome use. The client is already in the somatic field. The instruction does not need to explain the practice; it just deploys it. "Say I'm excited 10 times" — delivered immediately, with the expectation that the client will simply do it. Explanations of why come after, if at all.

## The Stuckness Use (The In-the-Moment Move for Fear-of-Consequences)

A third application: when stuckness is the felt state and the cognitive layer has surfaced that the stuckness is fear-of-consequences in disguise (see [[fear-of-consequences-creates-stuck]]), the practice converts the held-as-fear excitement into available motion. The instruction is specific: say "I'm excited" with the actual thing you have been refusing to do.

"The next time you're noticing that you feel stuck and you're scared of consequences, just say to yourself, 'I'm excited,' 10, 20 times, particularly about the thing, like, 'I'm excited to ask for' -- I'm excited to ask for a promotion. I'm excited to ask. I'm excited to start my new business because there's some truth in it. If there's something in you that wants it, there is some part of you that is deeply excited about it. And if you hold that excitement back, if you're not feeling it, it's going to come out as fear. It's going to come out as anxiety and it's going to come out as you feeling stuck." -- Joe Hudson, The More Stuck You Feel, The Closer You Are To Freedom

The variant here is the explicit attachment of the practice to the rejected action. Not just "I'm excited" but "I'm excited to ask for the promotion." Not just "I'm excited" but "I'm excited to start the business." The naming of the specific thing routes the activation toward the specific action, which is what was being avoided. The practice does not generate excitement out of nothing; it surfaces the excitement that was already there underneath the fear-of-consequence label.

The reasoning Joe gives for why the practice works in this case: if there is something in you that wants the action, there is excitement underneath. The wanting is what produces the activation. The fear-of-consequence frame holds the activation as fear; the practice routes it back through excitement. Without the practice, the held excitement comes out as fear, anxiety, and the felt sense of being stuck. With the practice, the excitement is allowed and the held fear-as-stuckness dissolves.

The dosage Joe names here is slightly broader: "10, 20 times" rather than the strict ten of the imposter-syndrome use. The stuckness presentation often has more accumulated held energy than the in-the-moment imposter-syndrome flavor, and the longer repetition lets the body catch up to the reframe.

What stays the same: the same verbal labeling, the same breath-on-the-words mechanism, the same somatic shift from constriction to availability. The application has moved from "imposter syndrome before a bigger room" to "stuckness around a specific feared action" -- the underlying mechanism is the same body of held activation being routed through a different label.

## The Good-Life Use (The Shoe-Drop Anxiety Conversion)

A fourth application: when someone has the good life they wanted and is presenting anxiety about losing it -- the "I'm with my family and it's like I'm missing it" configuration. The anxiety is the body running the kinked form of excitement about the good life. The practice converts the anxiety to the excitement directly, in the room.

Joe deploys the practice in a coaching session where the coachee is presenting chronic anxiety about her good life (loving husband, three healthy kids, the home she worked for). She names the felt experience: "I think the anxiety is like, I'm with my family, it's like I'm missing it."

"Cool. All right. So just think about that. And then just -- it's an odd little hack. It works 80% of the time. Just say, I'm excited, 10 times, but feel the excitement." -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

The coachee says "I'm excited" ten times out loud in the session. Joe's follow-up question -- "And now how do you feel about your family?" -- produces the answer "I'm excited." The somatic shift has arrived in the room. The same situation that produced anxiety thirty seconds earlier now produces excitement.

The specific instruction worth replicating: "but feel the excitement." The practice is not just verbal. The instruction is to let the body actually have the excitement during the repetitions, not just say the words. Joe's "80% of the time" framing names the realistic batting average and removes the pressure for the practice to always work.

What stays the same as the imposter-syndrome use: the verbal labeling routes activation toward "I am excited," the breath required for the repetition opens the constriction, the body completes the shift the constriction was preventing.

What is distinct: the target is not an upcoming event but the present moment that has been being missed. The anxiety the practice converts is the chronic shoe-drop anxiety about the good life rather than the situational fear before a bigger room. The structure is the same; the application is broader. The principle in [[fear-vs-excitement]] -- anxiety is excitement that has been constricted -- applies in both cases.

The downstream Joe teaches alongside this use: the reason the excitement has been constricted in the first place is often the childhood "calm down" training (see [[childhood-imprinting]]). The body was trained to constrict on excitement; the constricted excitement reads as anxiety; the anxiety appears especially around the things that would produce the most excitement (the family, the dream life, the goal coming true). Naming this lets the coachee see the anxiety pattern as data about what they have been excited about all along, not as a verdict on their relationship to the good life.

## Boundary Conditions

This is the lighter-weight version of the fear-to-excitement work. The deeper version is the somatic distinction in [[fear-vs-excitement]]: feel which one is actually present and let it move through. The deeper version is more accurate; the verbal practice is faster.

Do not use this practice to suppress a fear that is carrying real information about misalignment. If the fear is the body telling you that something is genuinely wrong (the equipment is broken, the conditions have changed, the room is wrong for you), the relabeling can paper over the signal. See [[grounded-excitement-vs-ungrounded-excitement]] for the diagnostic on whether the excitement after the practice is real or compensatory.

## The Pure-Somatic Companion: Fill the Room

The verbal practice has a pure-somatic companion that works on the same underlying state without any verbal component: [[fill-the-room]]. The instruction is to let your felt presence expand to occupy the entire room you are in.

"I gave them this like very, very simple little exercise to do. I said, hey, next time you get up on stage, I want you to fill the whole room. I want you to feel like you are the entire room. That's it. Just put your feeling into the entire room." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The mechanism is different from the verbal version. The verbal version uses breath plus relabeling to open the constriction. The fill-the-room version expands the felt-self directly to match the size of the activation. Both resolve the same underlying mismatch -- the body producing activation for a bigger room than the person is letting themselves occupy -- and both convert the held excitement into accessible excitement.

When to use which:

- For a person who is mostly in their head, the verbal version is the better entry point. The breath-on-the-words does the work the somatic awareness would otherwise have to do.
- For a person who is somatically aware, the fill-the-room version alone often suffices. The friend in the source story is described as "somatically very aware" -- the friend can feel where their felt-self is and expand it on instruction.
- For maximum effect, run both in sequence: say "I'm excited" ten times to open the breath and route the label; then fill the room with the now-acknowledged excitement.
- When the setting does not permit speaking out loud (mid-stage, mid-presentation, mid-meeting), the fill-the-room version is the only option and works on its own.

See [[fill-the-room]] for the full treatment of the somatic move. The two practices are not in tension. They are different access points to the same fear-to-excitement conversion.
