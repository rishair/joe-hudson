# Say "I'm Excited" Ten Times

## The Practice

When the imposter feeling -- or any fear that has the flavor of stepping into a bigger room -- arrives, say out loud "I'm excited" ten times.

"I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited."

Ten repetitions. Out loud. In the moment.

"Just say, I'm excited 10 times. I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, I'm excited, just like that. 10 times. I know it sounds absolutely crazy, but try it. It works so well." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

## Purpose

The imposter feeling is fear. The fear is excitement without the breath. See [[fear-vs-excitement]]. The same physiological energy presents as fear when the breath is constricted and as excitement when it is allowed.

Verbally repeating "I'm excited" does two things at once. First, it relabels what the body is doing: the activated state gets routed through "excitement" rather than through "fear." Second, the act of speaking requires breath. The constricted breath that was holding the energy as fear loosens. The body's state shifts toward what it would have been with breath all along.

The repetition matters. One declaration does not land. Ten declarations move the system. The practice trades one explanation for another and lets the body catch up.

## When To Use It

- When imposter syndrome shows up before doing something. Speaking in public, stepping into a bigger room, traveling somewhere new, taking on a project bigger than the last one.
- When the fear is the kind that has the flavor of expanding rather than the flavor of survival. Use this hack for the bigger-room fear, not for danger-fear. (For survival-fear, see [[fear-vs-excitement]] for the somatic distinction.)
- When the person is in their head trying to convince themselves out of imposter syndrome and losing. The verbal practice bypasses the argument.

## What It Produces

The activated state stays. The constriction loosens. The same energy that was being read as fear gets read as excitement. The bigger room becomes accessible without the gripping.

"What you're really doing is just allowing that fear to be unrestricted and turn into the excitement that's actually there. And that's a great way to integrate it because it's not just a thought, it's an emotional integration." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

The distinction from purely mental reframing: this is somatic, not intellectual. The body is doing something during the repetitions. The breath returns. That is the integration.

## Variations and Notes

- A research-supported version adds jumping up and down while saying "I'm excited." The added movement amplifies the body shift. See the brief reference in [[fear-vs-excitement]].
- Saying it silently does not work as well. The breath-on-the-words is part of the mechanism.
- The "feels absolutely crazy" objection is part of the practice. Do it anyway. The cringe is the system noticing that a habit is being interrupted.

## Boundary Conditions

This is the lighter-weight version of the fear-to-excitement work. The deeper version is the somatic distinction in [[fear-vs-excitement]]: feel which one is actually present and let it move through. The deeper version is more accurate; the verbal practice is faster.

Do not use this practice to suppress a fear that is carrying real information about misalignment. If the fear is the body telling you that something is genuinely wrong (the equipment is broken, the conditions have changed, the room is wrong for you), the relabeling can paper over the signal. See [[grounded-excitement-vs-ungrounded-excitement]] for the diagnostic on whether the excitement after the practice is real or compensatory.
