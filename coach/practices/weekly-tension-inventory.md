# Weekly Tension Inventory

## The Practice

On a weekly basis, ask: where is the tension here that I am not addressing, and what would it look like to address it this week? Small, regular contact with the tension in your relationships and your work keeps the tension processable. The tension you do not name does not stay the same size. It compounds. The weekly cadence prevents the compounding.

"It doesn't need to be that diligent, but if you on a weekly basis say, okay, what's -- where's the tension here that I'm not addressing and I'm going to address it, then it's usually easier to, to move through most of it." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The practice is preventive. It addresses friction while the friction is still small enough that the conversation about it is also small. The alternative is the eventual rupture: the conflict that finally surfaces years later and blows the relationship apart precisely because it has been gathering for so long.

## How To Do It

1. **Pick a day.** Sunday afternoon, Monday morning, Friday end-of-week -- whichever cadence fits. The specific day matters less than the regularity.

2. **Run through the domains.** For each significant relationship or system in your life (partner, kids, key colleagues, team, business partner, parents, close friends), ask: is there a tension here I am not addressing?

3. **Notice the felt signature, not just the thought.** The tension you are avoiding usually shows up as a small contraction or aversion when you bring the person to mind. The aversion is the marker. Look for it.

4. **Pick one or two to address this week.** Not all of them. The point is small frequent contact, not the heroic cleanup. One tension addressed cleanly produces more learning than ten addressed in a discharge.

5. **Address it.** This means a direct conversation, said kindly. See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] for the form.

6. **Notice what you learned.** What was actually there? What was the other person's experience? What was the relief afterward?

## What "Address" Means

Addressing the tension is not unloading. It is naming what is present, asking what is going on, hearing what the other person has to say, and seeing what wants to move. The conversation is short. The tone is curious. The goal is mutual seeing, not winning.

The form Joe gives elsewhere: "Hey, what's happening? What made us have a conflict here? How do we both get what we want?" The orientation is exploratory. The conflict is not the enemy. It is the place where information is showing up about how you and the other person are mismatched, and the conversation is the metabolic process that uses the information.

See [[conflict-as-evolutionary-tension]] for the larger frame this practice operationalizes.

## Why Weekly

The cadence is not arbitrary. The weekly rhythm fits the rate at which tension accumulates in most relationships. Daily is overkill for most contexts. Monthly is too slow -- a month of unaddressed tension is already a meaningful accumulation. Weekly is the cadence at which the tension stays small enough to be moved through in a single conversation.

Joe is explicit that the rhythm is heuristic, not mandatory. The principle is small-and-frequent. The specific cadence adjusts to the relationship and the person. Some relationships call for daily check-ins. Some are fine with monthly. The default is weekly because that is the cadence that works for most people in most relationships.

## What This Practice Prevents

The classic rupture pattern that this practice addresses:

- A small irritation arises in week 1
- The person decides it is not worth bringing up
- The irritation accumulates with related small irritations in weeks 2-12
- The accumulation forms a stance: I am tired of this, this has been going on for months
- The eventual conversation is not about week 1's small irritation. It is about three months of accumulated material, all delivered at once.
- The other person reacts to the size of the delivery, not to the content of any individual item. The conversation becomes a fight.
- The fight produces distance rather than resolution because the material is too much to metabolize in one sitting.

The weekly practice intervenes at week 1. The small irritation gets named while it is small. The conversation is small. The metabolism happens in real time. The accumulation never forms.

## What This Practice Is Not

This is not constant monitoring of the relationship for problems. The practice is one moment of checking, weekly. The rest of the week is lived without the audit running in the background. Constant monitoring is its own problem and produces a different kind of relationship damage (the chronically-evaluated partner).

This is also not picking fights. The check is for tensions that are already present. If nothing is up, nothing is up. The practice does not require manufacturing material to address.

## When To Use Variations

- **For very high-conflict relationships:** the cadence may need to be more frequent, with the practice oriented toward keeping the friction processable rather than letting it batch.
- **For very low-conflict relationships:** the practice may surface that there is no current tension. This is fine. The check is preventive, not productive.
- **For team contexts:** the weekly check can be built into a structured meeting -- "what is the friction we are not naming this week?" -- and run with the team rather than alone.
- **For internal use:** the same practice can be run on yourself. "Where is the tension I am not addressing with myself?" See [[avoided-conflict-goes-internal]] for why this matters.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The first week, surprise at how much was there that the person had not been seeing
- A felt lightness after a small thing gets named that had been weighted down
- The realization, in later weeks, that fewer items are surfacing because the small ones are no longer accumulating
- A change in the texture of the relationship -- more aliveness, less low-grade pressure
- The discovery that the partner has had their own things they have been not addressing, and the practice opens that side too

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader is recognizing they have been letting friction accumulate on the team
- A long-term partner is starting the work of becoming less conflict-avoidant and needs a sustainable cadence
- The eventual-rupture pattern has been seen and the person wants to prevent the next one

## Tone

Joe gives this practice almost as an aside. "It doesn't need to be that diligent." The casualness of the delivery is part of the teaching: the practice does not need to be onerous. It is a question asked once a week. The lightness is the point. A heavy practice produces resistance; a light practice produces consistency.
