# Show Up in Small Ways Every Day

## The Practice

Each day, find at least one small place where you have not been showing up as yourself. Have the conversation you have been avoiding. Say the thing you have been not saying. Ask for what you have been not asking for.

The practice does not require dramatic disclosures or stakes-loaded conversations. The point is the daily, low-stakes accumulation of small acts of being seen. The body learns through repetition that being seen is survivable, and the shame architecture loosens with each successful exposure.

The instruction:

> "What are the ways that you're not being yourself? What are the ways that you're not showing up, asking for what you want, saying what's wrong, having the hard conversations? Because if you aren't doing that, you're not actually with anybody. You're all — you're by yourself in this reality. And so literally, I think it was like 3 days later, because, you know, Esme is amazing. She, she called me up and she was like, I've had 3 hard conversations in 2 days. I feel so much better. I've like had these, like, I just looked everywhere that I wasn't saying the thing or being the person that I wanted to be, and I just did it and I feel so much better. And it's — and it happens just like that. It's amazing how quickly loneliness can be cured. If you actually show up in a way that you're proud of saying the things that are important to you." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen and the Strange Way Out

The practice is fast-acting. Esme's report — three hard conversations in two days, dramatically better — is typical. The loneliness and the fear-of-being-seen are sustained by the chronic non-showing-up; reversing the non-showing-up reverses the felt cost quickly, often within days.

## The Components

**Daily inventory.** Look at where, in your current life, you are not being yourself. Where are you saying the polite thing instead of the true thing? Where are you avoiding the conversation? Where are you withholding the request? Where are you performing instead of being? The inventory is the first half of the practice; the action is the second.

**Choose the smallest one.** The practice is not about heroic disclosures. It is about small, manageable steps that the body can complete without being overwhelmed. The kid you have been not having the hard talk with. The colleague you have been managing instead of being direct with. The partner you have been hiding the minor want from. Pick one.

**Have the conversation.** Say the thing. Ask for the thing. Be direct in the small place. The point is the act itself, not the outcome of the conversation. The other person's response is data, not the metric.

**Notice the somatic shift.** After the action, attend to what changes in the body. Most people report a felt sense of relief, often disproportionate to the stakes of the conversation. The body is registering "I showed up and I survived." That registration is the work landing.

**Repeat.** Tomorrow, find another place. The practice is the accumulation, not the single instance. Each small exposure adds to the body's evidence base.

## What This Practice Produces

**The loneliness lifts.** This is the most reliable and most rapid effect. Loneliness is sustained by the gap between the actual self and the curated self that is being presented. Each act of showing up closes the gap by a small amount. After a few such acts, the gap is small enough that the loneliness — which is shame-based disconnection, see [[loneliness-as-shame-based-disconnection]] — does not have material to run on.

**The Eye of Sauron quiets.** The internal self-attack runs on chronic self-betrayal. Each act of showing up reduces the betrayal. The internal eye has less material to attack with. See [[the-eye-of-sauron]].

**The body learns the dissolution route.** The shame's architecture predicts catastrophic outcomes if the person shows up. The practice produces direct counter-evidence — the catastrophic outcome does not happen, the person survives, the relationship often deepens. The body files the new pattern. Over time, the prediction shifts.

**The capacity for the bigger moves grows.** A person who has had three hard conversations in two days has a body that can have the fourth. The practice is the daily strength-training that makes the larger moments possible. By the time a high-stakes moment arrives, the body has been showing up enough times that the showing-up is no longer foreign.

**The relationships reorganize.** Showing up changes what the other people in your life are interacting with. People who were interacting with the curated self begin interacting with the actual self. Some relationships deepen. Some fall away. Both are useful data.

## What Joe Notices About This Practice

The speed of the effect is what Joe names. Loneliness is described elsewhere as a chronic, multi-year condition. The practice can shift it in days. "It happens just like that." The speed is itself diagnostic — the loneliness was not real in the sense of being a fixed condition. It was sustained by the daily ongoing non-showing-up. Reverse the non-showing-up and the loneliness has nothing to stand on.

The other notable feature: the practice does not require fixing the inner shame first. The action precedes the cognitive shift. The body's recognition of "I can do this and survive" is what shifts the shame, not the other way around. Trying to dissolve the shame before having the conversation is the [[the-pattern-seer-using-pattern-knowledge-to-stay-stuck]] move. The practice bypasses the seer by going directly to the doing.

## Anti-Patterns Around This Practice

**Don't make it about leverage or outcomes.** The practice is for the body's learning, not for the other person's reaction. If the conversation is being instrumentalized to produce a specific outcome from the other, the practice is not happening — what is happening is more of the same management.

**Don't go big when small will do.** A person who has been chronically hiding does not need to immediately confront their boss about the unresolved conflict from three years ago. The body cannot complete that move and would learn "I cannot do this" from the failure. Pick the small one. Build from there.

**Don't beat yourself up for what you have been hiding.** See [[dont-list-whats-wrong-with-you]]. The inventory is for finding the next move, not for cataloging defects. The shame that the practice is dissolving will reinstall itself if the inventory becomes a self-attack.

**Don't make the practice into a should.** The practice works because the body recognizes the showing-up as an act of self-love. If the practice becomes another item on the list of things you should be doing better, see [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]] — the same anti-pattern applies. The practice has to come from the felt desire to be seen, not from a new performance of conscientiousness.

**Don't expect every conversation to go well.** Some will not. The point is that you showed up. The other person's response is downstream of their own work, not a verdict on the practice. See [[every-no-contains-a-better-answer]].

## When to Use This Practice

The practice is the take-home for:

- Anyone presenting with chronic loneliness who is also chronically not showing up. See [[loneliness-as-shame-based-disconnection]] and [[i-feel-lonely-i-cant-find-real-community]].
- Anyone presenting with fear of being seen who has done enough recognition work that they need the action component. See [[i-get-anxious-in-relationships]] and [[the-desire-to-be-seen]].
- Anyone who has been doing inner work for a long time without behavioral change. The practice is the bridge from the cognitive recognition to the embodied shift.
- Anyone in the post-recognition phase of fear-of-being-seen work who is asking "what do I actually do?" The practice answers the question with daily small actions rather than dramatic single moves.

The practice is not the first move for someone in acute crisis or severe trauma. The titration matters. For trauma-loaded cases, see [[loneliness-titrates-like-exercise-pain]] and [[trust-building-before-exposure]].

## The Container Question

The practice works best when the small-stakes conversations are happening with people who have at least some capacity to receive. A conversation in a deeply abusive environment is not the practice; it is a survival situation that requires different work. The practice presupposes a baseline of safety in the conversations being chosen. If no such conversations are available, the prior work is to build or find a container where they are. See [[community]].

When the container is available and the small daily showings-up start happening, the practice is self-reinforcing. Each successful showing-up makes the next one easier. The container's responsiveness becomes evidence that disconfirms the shame's prediction.

## Tone

The practice is delivered as encouragement, not as instruction. The body has been doing the non-showing-up for a long time; the new pattern is being installed gently, not imposed. The tone is closer to "let's see what wants to happen if we try this small thing" than to "you need to start having hard conversations."

The speed of the effect — when it comes — is named as part of the encouragement. The person does not need to commit to a long campaign. They need to try the next small thing and notice what happens. The body's report does the rest.
