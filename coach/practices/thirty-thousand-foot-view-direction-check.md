# The 30,000-Foot View Direction Check

## The Practice

Every once in a while -- not every hour, not every day, but periodically across weeks and months -- stop the work. Pull back from the trail in front of you. Look at the map. Ask: am I in the place I'm supposed to be? Is this getting me to where I want to go? Then go back to the trail.

The practice is the macro-cadence counterpart to [[reflection-after-an-hour]]. The hourly reflection catches whether the last hour produced anything real. The 30,000-foot view catches whether the last several months are pointing the right direction. Both are required. Each catches what the other misses.

> "Every once in a while you got to look at your phone or your map and go, oh, am I in the place that I'm supposed to be? Yes or no? Is this getting me to where I want to go? And then once you've done that, then you can go back to looking at the trail in front of you. But every once in a while you got to stop, pull up." -- Joe Hudson, Escape Time Anxiety

## The Cost of Not Doing It

Without this practice, the default is to spend months -- sometimes years -- doing the same shape of work without examining whether the shape itself is right.

> "We will spend 5, 6, 7 months in a row answering the emails at 2 o'clock in the afternoon without thinking, what are we doing? Is this getting us to the place that we really want to be?" -- Joe Hudson, Escape Time Anxiety

The example is small but the principle scales. A team can spend years shipping product the way it has always shipped product. A founder can spend years running the company the way they started it. A person can spend a decade structuring their life around the answer they gave to "what should I do?" at 25. None of these structures are wrong. They are also not necessarily right anymore. The 30,000-foot view is the practice of finding out.

The drift is invisible from inside the trail. The trail looks like the trail. Looking up reveals whether the trail still leads where you want to go. Most of the time it does. Some of the time it does not. The "some of the time" is the entire reason the practice exists. Without the pull-back, the wrong-direction periods continue indefinitely. With the pull-back, the wrong-direction periods get corrected within weeks of being noticed.

## Why It Is Different From Hourly Reflection

Two distinct practices live at two distinct altitudes. Confusing them is common.

**[[reflection-after-an-hour]]** asks: "what did I actually move forward in the last hour?" The data is whether throughput is producing output. The risk it catches is the dopamine-loop, the spin, the all-channels-no-work day.

**The 30,000-foot view** asks: "is this getting me to where I want to go?" The data is whether the direction is right at all. The risk it catches is having spent six months executing flawlessly in the wrong direction.

The hourly practice can be running perfectly while the macro practice is missing. A person can be moving the ball forward every single hour, with the ball pointed at a goal they no longer want. The hourly check will report success. The 30,000-foot view will report that success on this trajectory is failure.

The macro practice can be running while the hourly practice is missing. A person can know they are on the right trail and spend each hour spinning. The 30,000-foot view will report confidence. The hourly check will report that nothing is moving.

Run both. Each catches what the other cannot see.

## How to Run It

1. **Set a recurring cadence.** Quarterly is reasonable. Some seasons of life or work warrant monthly. Other seasons warrant only twice a year. The point is that the cadence exists -- not the specific interval.

2. **Stop the work.** This practice cannot happen while in the work. Block time. The block does not need to be long. An hour is often enough.

3. **Pull up to the map.** Imagine looking at your life from 30,000 feet. Where are you actually going? What is the trajectory? Where will you be in another year on this path? In five?

4. **Ask the core question.** "Is this getting me to where I want to go?" The question is yes-or-no but the answer requires sitting with it. The fast answer is usually a defense. The slower answer is the real one.

5. **If yes:** go back to the trail. The practice has done its job. The confidence to keep going is now earned, not assumed.

6. **If no:** do not panic. Ask the next question. What needs to change? What would the new direction look like? What is the smallest course correction that gets the trajectory back on path? The pull-back is not for catastrophic redesign. It is for course correction.

7. **Make the course correction.** Then return to the trail. The practice is not for sitting with the map indefinitely. The trail is where the work happens. The map is for making sure the trail goes somewhere.

## Joe's Worked Example: The Way We Shoot Videos

The example Joe gives is small enough to be operational and concrete enough to make the practice visible.

> "A great example of this is the way that we shoot our videos. And it's really cool because you can actually look back at our videos and see the change that's happened because of this. So some of our time we're shooting videos and it feels good and we're like, boom, checking off the box. We got another video done, we got another video done. But some of the time we're pulling back and we're saying, hey, is this how we want to shoot a video? Is this us? Is this something that we enjoy? And so maybe we started the way that everybody else shoots videos, and then slowly we try another format, and then we try another format, and we're constantly iterating to say, oh, is this the way that we want to be in the world?" -- Joe Hudson, Escape Time Anxiety

The mechanics:
- Without the pull-back: keep shooting videos the way everyone else shoots them. Each video gets checked off. The library grows. The library is also generic.
- With the pull-back: ask whether the format reflects what the team actually wants to make. Try another format. Try another. Iterate the format itself, not just the content within the format.

The difference shows up over years. The library shaped by the periodic pull-back looks different from the library shaped by always-trail-never-map. The first library has voice. The second library has volume.

The pull-back is the input to [[iterative-mindset]] at the strategic layer. Without the pull-back, iteration only happens within the current format. With the pull-back, iteration includes iterating the format itself.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not productivity tracking. The 30,000-foot view does not measure output; it measures direction.
- It is not strategic planning in the corporate sense. The practice does not require a deck, an off-site, or a quarterly objectives document. It requires stopping the work and looking at the map.
- It is not for chronic re-evaluation. The practice is periodic, not constant. People who run it every week destroy the ability to commit to anything; the trail never gets walked.
- It is not for catastrophizing. Most pulls-back will report "the direction is still right." This is good news, not a sign the practice is unnecessary.
- It is not a substitute for the hourly reflection. They are different practices serving different purposes. See [[reflection-after-an-hour]].

## What People Discover Running This

- Most of the time the direction is still right. The practice does not produce constant pivots. It produces occasional, well-considered course corrections.
- The pivots that do come from this practice are usually obvious in retrospect. The drift had been visible for months. The practice was what made looking up possible.
- The teams and individuals who run this practice end up with bodies of work that have shape. The teams that do not run it end up with bodies of work that are large and generic.
- The relief of pulling up is itself useful data. If the relief is intense, the trail had been more wrong than was being admitted. If the relief is mild, the trail was mostly right.
- The discomfort of pulling up is also data. If the discomfort is intense, the person was avoiding looking because of what they suspected they would see. The avoidance is the signal that the practice is overdue.

## Pairing with Other Practices

- **With [[reflection-after-an-hour]]:** the two practices form the full reflection rhythm. The hourly check keeps the trail honest. The 30,000-foot view keeps the direction honest. Run both.
- **With [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]]:** the leverage question scales up at the 30,000-foot view. "Which three things would make most of next quarter easier or irrelevant?" The leverage move runs at multiple altitudes; this practice supplies the altitude where the largest leverage lives.
- **With [[what-is-the-productivity-for]]:** the forest-vs-trees question is what the 30,000-foot view is actually asking. "Is this getting me to where I want to go?" presupposes a "where I want to go." If the answer is unclear, the 30,000-foot view collapses into the forest question.
- **With [[time-can-be-invested-not-just-spent]]:** the macro-reflection often surfaces the highest-leverage time investment. The kinds of seeds visible from 30,000 feet are different from the seeds visible from the trail.
- **With [[fourteen-day-time-experiment]]:** one of the three techniques in the 14-day experiment is this practice. Running it daily for two weeks builds the muscle for running it periodically thereafter.

## When to Use It

- Quarterly, as a default cadence for most people and teams.
- When the work has been the same shape for many months without examination.
- When something is off but cannot be named -- the off-ness often points to a direction problem that the trail-level lens cannot see.
- When a major milestone has been reached and the next chapter is forming.
- When a goal that seemed important has been hit and the felt-experience is flat.
- When a leader notices they are tired in a way that hourly rest is not fixing -- often the macro direction is wrong, and the body knows before the mind does.
- When the team is busy and the metrics are not moving in the way that suggests the wrong direction, not the wrong execution.

## Tone

Calm, slightly observational, slow. The practice cannot be done in a rush. Joe delivers this with the energy of someone who has watched many people (himself included) lose years on a wrong trail because no one ever stopped to look at the map. The directive "every once in a while you got to stop, pull up" is gentle but firm. The pulling-up is non-negotiable; the cadence is flexible.
