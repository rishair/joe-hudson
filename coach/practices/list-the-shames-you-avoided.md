# List the Shames That Stopped You (And the Actions You Didn't Take)

## The Practice

Sit down with a notebook. Write down every shame you can remember feeling over a chosen period (a week, a month, a year). For each one, write the action you would have taken if the shame had not been there, and what the result might have been.

The practice surfaces the cost of shame by making the foregone actions visible. The list is not for self-criticism. It is to see what was paid for what.

Brett describes doing the practice the night before recording this episode:

"I wrote a list of all the different kinds of shame that I felt over the past year, shames that I avoided and I didn't take any action based on. And I looked at all the things that would've occurred in my life if I had actually just taken the actions that I had the shame around. And some examples were, you know, shame of missed opportunity, shame of feeling transactional, shame of being salesy, shame of not being able to take care of people, shame of drawing a messy boundary, and all these different things that it was just a little bit easier just not to do that. And I look back and I'm like, man, I would've had a really different year." -- Brett Kistler, The Anatomy of Shame

## How to Do It

1. Pick a time window. A week if you are starting. A year if you want the full picture.

2. Write a flowing list. Do not categorize at first. Just write "shame of X" or "ashamed about Y" as fast as you can. Aim for at least 20 items. The first ten will be obvious. The next ten will be the more interesting ones, the subtler shames that were hiding.

3. For each shame, name the action you did not take. "I was ashamed of being too sales-y, so I did not follow up with that person." "I was ashamed of drawing a messy boundary, so I let the meeting run over." "I was ashamed of wanting attention, so I did not raise my hand."

4. Read the whole list. Notice the pattern. Many people find a thread: the same root shame producing dozens of foregone actions, or a category of life (work, intimacy, money) where almost everything got blocked.

5. Do not fix it. The purpose of the practice is awareness, not improvement. Awareness alone changes the relationship to the shame.

## What This Practice Produces

Two things almost always surface:

**The cost is much larger than the person realized.** A year of small avoidances adds up to a meaningfully different life. The list often produces a moment of "I would have had a completely different year." This is the practice working.

**Most of the shames lose their grip when written down.** Many items on the list, seen on paper, are immediately recognizable as silly or arbitrary. The shame had been operating below conscious inspection. Bringing it to the page strips it of mystery.

A third often follows: **the same shame shows up across many different surface situations**. The person was not avoiding twenty things. They were avoiding one thing in twenty disguises.

## The Subtlety Layers

The first pass finds the obvious shames. The deeper work finds the shames that are so embedded they did not feel like shame -- they felt like personality.

"It can be subtle. There's just -- when you have a couple layers of shame over a possible action you might take, it just doesn't occur to you that that action is even possible or that this version of you might even exist. There's just -- no, I'm just not that way. I've always been this way and not that way. I'm, you know, I'm not a salesy kind of person, somebody might say." -- Brett Kistler, The Anatomy of Shame

The diagnostic for these deeper shames: any time you say "I'm just not that way" or "I'm not the kind of person who," check whether a shame is doing the work. Often the "just not that way" identity is the fence built around a forbidden version of the self.

## Connection to Joe's 26-Year-Old List

Joe describes elsewhere a similar list he made at 26: things in his life that had not changed in ten years. Every one of them had shame around it. See [[joes-26-year-old-list]]. This practice is the time-reversed version: instead of "what hasn't changed and why?" it asks "what did I not even attempt and why?"

Both practices produce the same insight from different angles: shame is the lock, and listing the locks is the first step in seeing them.

## What To Do After

The list is the diagnostic. The actual work is to investigate the individual shames, ideally one at a time, using whatever practices are available:

- [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: invite the shame back, let it move
- The body-first practice: where in the body does this specific shame live?
- The inherent-goodness reframe: see [[if-i-were-inherently-good]]
- Sharing the shame with a person or group who can receive it without trying to fix it -- see the AA/group-work principle in [[container-of-love]]

The list itself is a moment of [[shame-as-disconnection]] becoming [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] becoming legible. Once it is legible, it can move.

## Tone

Personal. Curious. Not self-flagellating. The practice is most effective when done with a sense of "I wonder what's been running me?" rather than "what have I been doing wrong?" The first stance produces information. The second stance produces more shame.
