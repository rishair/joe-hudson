# The Purpose-Checkin Alarm Practice

## The Practice

Set an alarm 2 or 3 times a day for a week. Each time it goes off, ask: "What is my purpose asking me to do right now?" or "What would living my purpose look like in this moment? What would I need to say or do or listen to or understand to be living my purpose as fully as possible in this moment?"

That is the entire practice. Two or three asks per day, for seven days. The accumulation produces the result.

"The best way to make this really practical in your life is to just run that experiment I've talked about multiple times a day. So set an alarm 2 or 3 times a day for a week. Ask yourself in this moment, what would living my purpose look like? What would I need to say or do or listen to or understand to be living my purpose as fully as possible in this moment? And if you do that 3 times a day for a week, you'll start noticing your purpose appears. It like comes out of the background and it starts showing up and you realize, oh, it's here with me always. And it's leading me if I just allow myself to follow it." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

## How to Run It

1. **Set the alarms.** Pick two or three times during the day when it would not be disruptive to pause for thirty seconds. Common choices: mid-morning, after lunch, late afternoon. Use phone alarms, calendar reminders, or any prompting device.

2. **When the alarm goes off, ask the question.** Don't analyze the situation first. Don't justify why this moment is or is not a good time. Just ask: what is my purpose asking me to do right now?

3. **Wait for the answer.** The answer arrives "through" the person, not from constructed reasoning. The wait might be a few seconds. Sometimes the answer is immediate. Sometimes it requires a quiet moment.

4. **Follow it.** If the answer is "listen more deeply to the person across from me," do that. If it is "stop pretending I'm not tired," do that. If it is "say the thing I've been not saying in this meeting," do that. The answer is meant to be acted on, not contemplated.

5. **Do this for seven days.** The seven days are not arbitrary. The accumulation is what produces the emergence Joe describes — "your purpose appears. It like comes out of the background." A single asking does not produce this. The repetition does.

## What the Practice Produces

The intended result is not a list of insights about purpose. It is a shift in the person's experience of purpose:

- **Purpose comes out of the background.** Before the practice, purpose is theoretical, future, abstract — something the person is supposed to find. After a week of asking, purpose is foreground, present, available — something the person is already in conversation with.
- **The "it's here with me always" recognition.** The repeated asking produces accumulating evidence that purpose has been here, asking, the whole time. The person was just not asking back.
- **The "if I allow myself to follow it" capacity.** The doing of what arrived builds the capacity to do what arrives next. The trust accumulates through the small followings, not through any single dramatic one.

These are not promises about specific content. The practice does not deliver a specific answer ("your purpose is to do X"). It delivers a specific relationship — purpose as ongoing conversation rather than as fixed answer.

## Why Alarms

The alarms are operational scaffolding for the principle that purpose is asking now, not waiting to be found later. Without the alarms, the person tends to ask the question only in moments that already feel purposeful — at retreats, on hikes, during big decisions. The alarms drag the practice into ordinary moments — meetings, errands, dinners. The ordinariness is the point. Purpose is asking in ordinary moments. The practice surfaces what is already happening, by inserting the asking where it would otherwise not be inserted.

The alarms also disrupt the mental-model search. The head, given time, will construct another model. The alarm interrupts. The question arrives before the head has had time to construct an answer. The answer that comes is therefore more likely to come from where Joe describes — "through you," not from the head's planning.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a mindfulness practice. The point is not to be present in the moment for its own sake; the point is to ask the question and receive what comes.
- It is not a planning practice. The answers are about now, not about later. "What is my purpose asking me to do this week" is the wrong question for this practice.
- It is not a journaling practice. The point is to act, not to record. Writing down the answers can be useful for tracking, but the practice is the asking and the following, not the documentation.
- It is not a meditation. There is no required posture, no closed eyes, no quieting of mind. The asking can happen in the middle of activity.

## Failure Modes

- **The head answers.** The person asks the question and the head produces a constructed answer — "my purpose is to be a good father, so I should call my dad." Constructed answers are not wrong; they are not the answers this practice is meant to produce. The body or the felt-sense produces answers that arrive more like "say this" or "stop doing this." The differentiator is whether the answer feels constructed or received.
- **The person dismisses the answers as too small.** "My purpose can't be to listen more carefully in this meeting; that's not impressive enough." The smallness is the point. The accumulation of small followings is the practice.
- **The person does the practice for a day and quits.** The seven days matter. The emergence happens through accumulation. A single day produces a single experience; the seven days produce the shift in relationship.
- **The alarm goes off and the person silences it.** The practice does not run. The week ends with no data. The exit is to set the alarms in a way that makes them harder to silence — calendar invites with notifications, alarms with annoying ringtones, public commitments to a partner who will ask.

## Tone

Practical, light, operational. The practice is offered as something the person can actually do, not as a heavy spiritual undertaking. Joe's framing — "just run that experiment" — keeps the stakes low. The experiment can fail. The week can be tried again. The point is to give it a real shot, see what happens, and let the experience produce the conviction (or the disconvinction) the lecturing cannot.
