# List the People You Subtly Blame

## The Practice

A two-step written exercise. Both steps matter.

**Step 1.** Write down all the people you subtly blame, and what you blame them for. Not the people you openly blame — those are easier to see. The subtle blames. The ones you might not even think of as blames. The mild resentments. The small grievances. The "she's always like that" or "if he would just..." or "I wouldn't have to deal with this if not for..."

**Step 2.** For each person on the list, write down how you are being aggressive toward them. The aggression can be passive (the more common form), direct, or both. The point is to make the connection explicit: wherever there is blame, there is aggression. The blame is the cover; the aggression is what is being covered.

"Write down a list of all the people that you subtly blame and for what. So just like, go, okay, here's a list of all the people I subtly blame and for what. And then when you've made that list, find out how you're being aggressive to them. Like, write, here's the ways that I'm being aggressive. And so it can either be passive or direct or both, or it could be many. But just like, notice that wherever there's that blame, there's some form of aggression that's happening, and write it down." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## What It Produces

The practice produces three things:

1. **An inventory of where your passive aggression is operating.** Most of it is invisible to you in real time. Writing it down makes it visible.
2. **A pattern recognition.** Once you have ten or fifteen entries, the patterns appear. The same forms repeat across different people. Your characteristic passive-aggressive moves become visible to you.
3. **A felt sense of the blame-aggression coupling.** This is the part Joe names as the heart of the practice. Once you have done the writing, you will notice it in real time afterward: the moment blame arises, the aggression is also arising.

"It'll really help you see it more clearly when it's happening. It'll also help you get in touch with like 'Oh, I'm blaming somebody. Like they're hurting me and they need to stop' like that. You'll get to, and you'll start feeling that right away, which is really the root of that is to really be able to be in contact when you're blaming somebody else." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## Why It Works

Blame is the cognitive form of passive aggression. The story "they are hurting me, they need to stop" is itself a form of aggression — an attempt to control another's behavior by holding them responsible for your experience. The blame produces the felt-sense that you are stuck (because they are the cause) and that they should change (because they are the agent).

Behind every blame is some form of action being taken (or withheld) toward the blamed person. The action is the aggression. By making the inventory, you connect the cognitive form to the behavioral form. You see what the blame is producing.

Joe places this practice in the context of his teaching that the victim role and passive aggression are linked. The victim feels stuck and blames. The blame is the legitimation of the passive-aggressive behavior. Both are part of the same configuration.

## The Companion Practice: Upright Apologies for Past Passive Aggression

Joe adds a second experiment at the end of the same episode, often delivered as an addendum to this one: make upright apologies for the passive-aggressive actions you can identify.

"You can do is, and very powerful, is to do an upright, not a regular, but an upright apology for all the places that you've been passive-aggressive. So I know that if you probably make 20 apologies for passive aggression, you're — the amount of passive aggression that you're going to be doing in the world will probably decrease by at least 30 or 40%." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The list practice produces the material. The upright apologies make the material actionable. See [[upright-apology]] for the form.

The warning: the apologies must be upright, not passive-aggressive. The temptation to apologize passive-aggressively is high here.

"Upright apology. It's like, 'I'm sorry that you made me get angry at you the other day. I'm sorry that you made me guilt trip you. There was just no other way to do it. Sorry I put up with your bullshit.' Oh my gosh. It just goes to prove like you can take any, any recognition and weaponize it." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

A bad apology for the passive aggression is still passive aggression. The form has to be clean.

## How to Sit Down To It

The practice works best in writing, not in the head. Get an actual list going. The act of writing makes the passive aggression specific. "I am sometimes irritated at my mother" is too general to do anything with. "I blame my mother for criticizing the way I parent, and I am being passive-aggressive by talking about her with my partner and by being three minutes late every time she visits" — that is specific enough to work with.

The kind of detail to capture for each entry:
- Who (the person you blame)
- What (specifically what you blame them for)
- How (specifically what forms of aggression you are doing in response)

The list does not have to be complete. Even ten entries is enough to produce the recognition. Add to it as more become visible.

## When to Use This Practice

- After absorbing the teaching on passive aggression, as the assigned experiment
- When a person reports they cannot see their own passive aggression but suspects they have it
- When a person is identifying it in others and the work needs to turn back inward
- As a regular check (monthly, quarterly) — the list will be different at different points in life
- Before a difficult conversation with a specific person — making your blame and aggression toward them explicit can clear the conversation

## What Tends to Go Wrong

A few common patterns:

- **The list becomes the thing.** The person writes the list with care, feels good about themselves for doing the work, and never acts on it. The companion upright-apology practice is the antidote.
- **The list becomes ammunition.** The person uses the list to confront the people on it. "Look, I admit I blame you for these things, now you should..." This is the very dynamic the practice is trying to dissolve. The list is for you. It does not have to be shared with anyone.
- **The list becomes self-flagellation.** The person reads the list and feels shame. The practice was supposed to produce recognition, not self-attack. If shame is arising, the practice has been hijacked by the inner critic. See [[passive-aggression-toward-self]].
- **The blame becomes wholesale.** "I am wrong to blame anyone, everyone is fine, it is all my fault." This swing to total self-blame is its own form of aggression. The practice is about specific recognition, not about a verdict.

## Tone

Joe delivers this practice with a kind of dry warmth. The teaching is direct but not heavy. The recognition that "wherever there's that blame, there's some form of aggression" is named as a piece of useful information, not as a moral indictment.
