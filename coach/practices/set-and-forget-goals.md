# Set and Forget Goals (The Annual Heart-Want Ritual)

## The Practice

Once a year, in a gathering that is not high-stakes, each person speaks aloud four or five goals for the next year. The goals are recorded -- in Joe's family's version, into a phone. Then the recording is not reviewed until the next year's gathering.

A year later, listen back to the previous year's recording. Notice that most of the goals (typically three or four out of five) actually happened, even though they were not tracked, reviewed, or pursued as targets.

The practice is performed lightly, without high seriousness. It is not a planning session. It is a heart-want ritual. The goals are spoken from what the heart wants for itself and for the people the person cares about. Then the goals are released.

"We have this tradition in our family. Every Thanksgiving we sit down and we all talk into the phone and say, what are our goals for the next year? It is not like a hardcore process. We just know that we're going to do this on Friday night after Thanksgiving, and we usually don't do it till Sunday. But, and then we just talk out our goals, like, here are 4 or 5. Each, each of us does it, and then we don't look at it again until the next Thanksgiving. And then we listen, and almost always there's like 5 goals and 3 or 4 out of the 5 goals were hit." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

## Why It Works

The mechanism is the same one that operates in [[acting-as-if-already-true]] and [[intention-vs-goal]]. Once a goal has been articulated from a real want -- spoken aloud into the world, owned, witnessed -- the person begins to live as if the goal is already orienting them. The orientation does the work. The tracking does not.

The tracking would actually interfere. Daily or weekly review of a personal goal turns the goal into a metric. The metric becomes the focus. The work shifts from living the intention to managing the dashboard. The intention -- which is what produces the result -- gets crowded out by the measurement.

Set-and-forget keeps the intention as the operating force. The speaking is the commitment. The forgetting is the trust. The year passes. The orientation operates. Most of the goals arrive.

## The Held-Gently Quality

The practice depends on a specific quality of holding. The goals are spoken "very gently." They are not heavy. They are not contracts. They are not deadlines.

"This is the thing that we're expressing our heart's want into the world and we're owning that thing. And whatever comes out of it comes out of it. ... it's held so gently." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

This is the operational form of holding goals as intentions rather than as mandates. The goals can be very specific ("I want a house that I love to live in") or relational ("I want a deeper relationship with this person") or qualitative ("I want my business to feel like this in my system"). The form varies. The holding stays light.

If the holding becomes heavy -- if the recording becomes a tracker that gets reviewed monthly, if the missing of any goal produces self-attack, if the practice becomes about whether you hit the targets -- the practice has stopped being set-and-forget. It has become the kind of goal-setting that [[goals-make-me-miserable]] describes.

## What the Goals Look Like

In Joe's family the goals span the practical, the relational, and the qualitative:

- Practical: "I want a house that I love to live in."
- Relational: "I want this specific deeper relationship with this person."
- Qualitative: "I want my business to feel like this in my system."

What they have in common is that they come from the heart. They are not externally imposed targets. They are not strategic objectives. They are not "what would be impressive to accomplish." They are what the person actually wants when the head's shoulds drop away and the heart gets to speak. See [[the-inner-board]] and [[goals-must-come-from-actual-want]] for the source-of-the-want concept.

## Why This Differs from Business Goal-Setting

The practice is described as a personal practice. Joe is explicit that business goals require tracking for alignment:

"In business you have to track it for alignment, but personally, it's not as necessary." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The business version has additional constraints: multiple people need to coordinate, capital allocation depends on knowing where the work is, the metrics produce the dashboards that the executive team and board use to operate. Set-and-forget would not produce the alignment a company requires.

The personal version is different. There is one person operating, and the operating happens automatically once the intention is held. The tracking is not needed for coordination because there is nothing to coordinate. The tracking would actually interfere by turning the intention into a metric.

## What Set-and-Forget Reveals

When practiced over years, the practice reveals several patterns the conventional goal-setting approach hides:

**Most goals from a real want happen.** The 3-or-4-out-of-5 hit rate is striking because the goals were not pursued in any conscious sense. They were spoken and released. The hit rate suggests that the orientation, once installed, does most of the work. Conscious pursuit is sometimes counterproductive.

**The misses are usually misalignments.** The 1-or-2 goals that don't happen often turn out to be the ones that were "wanting-to-want" rather than wanting. See [[wanting-vs-wanting-to-want]]. The retrospective is informative: the misses tell you which goals weren't actually yours.

**The results often exceed the goals.** Joe's broader observation -- "I've never gotten everything that I've wanted, but I've also never been able to look back and say what I got wasn't a shit ton better than what I wanted" -- applies to this practice. The set-and-forget orientation produces destinations that the original goal could not have specified.

**The relationship to goals shifts.** Over years of the practice, the person stops experiencing goals as oppression or as deadlines and starts experiencing them as orientations the heart releases into the year. The practice itself trains the gentle holding.

## How to Adapt the Practice

The Thanksgiving form is one instance. The structural elements can be carried into other forms:

- Choose a time once a year that is recurring and unhurried.
- Gather with people you trust enough to speak heart-wants in front of.
- Speak the goals aloud rather than only writing them. The speaking carries energy the writing doesn't.
- Have a recording (audio or written) but do not look at it during the year.
- Return to it at the same time next year.
- Notice both what arrived and what didn't.

The practice does not require Thanksgiving, does not require family, does not require five goals. It requires the once-a-year cadence, the speaking aloud, the recording, and the discipline of not looking until next year.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not goal-tracking. Tracking violates the structure.
- It is not visualization. Visualization is a different practice. This one is the speaking of the heart-want and then letting go.
- It is not affirmation. The goals are not statements of what you want to believe; they are statements of what the heart actually wants.
- It is not a planning exercise. No action items are derived from the speaking. The orientation does the deriving.
- It is not for business goals. Business requires tracking for alignment.

## What Gets in the Way

The most common failure mode is converting set-and-forget into set-and-track. The conversion happens almost automatically because the trained habit of goal-setting includes tracking. The discipline is to set without tracking, which feels wrong at first.

The second failure mode is treating the practice with high seriousness. The Thanksgiving practice works partly because it is not a hardcore process. If it becomes a hardcore process, it stops working. The lightness is part of the structure.

The third failure mode is bringing the inner-board voice into the goal-setting. If the head is generating the goals from shoulds, the practice produces shoulds-spoken-aloud. The goal-elicitation question to ask before the speaking: "What does your heart want?" See [[the-inner-board]] for the structural source of the failure mode.
