# Don't Improve for Two Weeks

## The Practice

Spend two weeks doing your best to not improve. Don't try to learn anything. Don't try to grow. Don't try to have realizations or recognitions. Just be.

The instruction sounds simple. People discover that it is hard. The improvement habit runs in the background of almost every decision. Catching it and releasing it for two weeks is itself revelatory.

"Try to not improve for a week. Or take 2 weeks and do your best to not improve. Don't learn anything, don't grow, don't have any realizations, don't have any recognitions. Like, try that for 2 weeks." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The Predicted Outcome

People who try this report:
- Many recognitions.
- Many realizations.
- More learning than they expected.
- Performance improvements in their work and relationships when they return to them.

The expected outcome is stagnation. The actual outcome is growth. The growth was always available. The improvement project was suppressing it.

"I told someone to do that once and they were like, 'oh my God, so many recognitions, so much realization,' because they stopped trying." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The Holiday Version

Most adults have experienced the milder version: a two-week vacation. The return to work is often marked by smoother performance, better decisions, clearer thinking. The vacation worked not because rest was needed (though it was) but because the improvement project paused.

"We feel this all the time when we're on holiday. You have 2 weeks off and then you come back and you just perform better. It's smoother. The whole thing works better. You make better calls because you didn't improve for 2 weeks." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## When Joe Reaches for It

Joe offers this practice when:
- A person is sure they will stagnate without their improvement project and needs empirical evidence.
- A person is doing too much self-work and needs to interrupt the cycle.
- The teaching of [[nature-improves-itself]] needs to land at the level of personal experience, not just argument.
- A person is afraid of what would happen if they let go of their improvement frame. See [[im-afraid-of-stagnating]] and [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]].

The practice is an empirical move. It bypasses the intellectual defense by inviting the person to test the principle themselves.

## What Counts as "Not Improving"

- Do not start new habits during the experiment.
- Do not try to be better at things.
- Do not measure yourself.
- Do not plan optimizations.
- Do not consume content aimed at making you better.
- Do not work on self-development practices, including the ones you usually find helpful.

What is fine:
- Doing your work.
- Following enjoyment.
- Responding to what is in front of you.
- Resting when tired.
- Being curious without aiming the curiosity at improvement.
- The recognitions and growth that arise on their own. You cannot help having them. You just stop reaching for them.

## What Makes It Hard

The improvement habit is often invisible. Catching it requires noticing many small moments throughout the day:
- "I should start exercising again this week." (Improvement project.)
- "I want to be less reactive." (Improvement project.)
- "I should read that book." (Improvement project.)
- "I keep forgetting to do my meditation." (Improvement project.)

Each catch is the practice. The catching itself begins to release the grip.

## What People Discover

- How much of their internal dialogue is improvement-flavored.
- How much energy was going into the improvement project.
- How much enjoyment was being deferred.
- How natural learning is when not forced.
- That their fear of stagnation was unfounded. The growth happens anyway.

## Tone

Joe offers this casually, almost as a dare. The casualness is the right tone. The practice is light, not heavy. The point is not to add another rigorous discipline. The point is to do less rigorous discipline.

## Boundary Conditions

- Two weeks is a starting point. Some people benefit from a longer experiment. Others find shifts in a few days.
- The practice is not the same as nihilism or apathy. The instruction is "don't try to improve," not "don't engage with life."
- If actual changes are needed (a job is harming, a relationship is unsafe), address those. The practice is about releasing the chronic improvement project, not about ignoring acute necessities.
