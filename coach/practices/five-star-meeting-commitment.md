# The Five-Star Meeting Commitment

## The Practice

A leader commits that within a month, they will have two consecutive weeks in which every single meeting they attend rates as a 5-star meeting on a 1-to-5 scale. A 5-star meeting is one that leaves the leader inspired, invigorated, and energized. The leader must do whatever it takes to reach this bar: telling the chairman of the board they are not coming to a meeting, walking out mid-meeting, saying "this meeting sucks, how do we fix it?" The bar is non-negotiable; the methods are not.

"Your goal is within a month have 2 weeks in a row where every meeting that you attend, you consider it a 5-star meeting. Like, you rank your meetings 1 to 5 stars, and every one is a 5-star meeting. And that means you feel inspired, invigorated, and energized by the meeting. And you have to do whatever it takes to do that, whatever you need to do, like whether it's telling the chairman of the board you're not coming to a meeting, or whether it means walking out of meetings or whether it means saying, this meeting sucks, how do we fix it? Whatever you need to do to get to that place." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## Why It Works

The commitment is structurally incompatible with the major leadership dysfunctions. You cannot have all 5-star meetings if you are political, blaming, indecisive, conflict-avoidant, or allowing defense. The 5-star bar forces the leader to confront each of these in turn, because each one produces meetings that fall below 5 stars.

"You can't have all happy meetings and avoid. It's just you can't have all happy meetings and blame. You can't have all happy meetings or productive and invigorating meetings if you are political. It just doesn't allow for it." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The leader who commits to the bar finds themselves having to address the dynamics that prevent 5-star meetings. The commitment, not the technique, does the work.

## Why Two Weeks, Not One

The two-week requirement is load-bearing. A single week can be gamed by avoiding the meetings that would expose what the leader is avoiding. Over two weeks, the avoided thing surfaces, and the leader has to handle it inside the commitment.

"That's why I do it for 2 weeks, because eventually the thing they're avoiding is going to be put onto their lap, and they're going to have to deal with it. That's exactly why." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

The hidden lever: the commitment to 5-star meetings forces the leader's relationship with the dynamics they have been routing around. Once they discover they can produce a 5-star meeting even out of the avoided conversation, the avoidance loses its grip.

"The other thing that happens is when they realize how to — the techniques of creating a 5-star meeting for themselves, they're less likely to avoid. Because they realize it's not the issue that they're avoiding, it's the conflict that arises around the issue that they're avoiding." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## What Happens to the Organization

A CEO or leader who actually commits to this changes their organization in a month. Not because the practice itself is profound, but because the leader stops absorbing dysfunction as the price of staying in the room.

"And if a CEO or a leader commits to that, man, within a month that organization is completely different. It's like a beautiful little hack to totally transform that thing because it's basically saying, I'm not gonna put up with dysfunction." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Dysfunctional Team

## How to Begin

1. Decide the start date. The two-week window is fixed in advance.
2. Set the rating ritual: at the end of every meeting, rate it 1-5 silently.
3. Commit to one of three responses for any meeting under 5: name what would make it 5-star and ask for the change; walk out; refuse to attend the next instance.
4. Track which meetings are habitually below 5. The pattern tells the leader where the dysfunction lives.
5. At the end of the two weeks, examine what had to change for every meeting to clear the bar. That is the leadership work.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A leader describes feeling exhausted by their calendar without being able to name why
- A leader says their team is dysfunctional but cannot point to the cause
- A leader has read [[functional-team]] material but has not converted understanding into action
- A leader is conflict-avoidant in a way they have not yet named (see [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]])
- A leader senses that meetings are draining the team without knowing how to intervene

- Toward [[invigorated-vs-depleted-as-functionality-measure]]: the underlying diagnostic the practice is built on
- Toward addressing whichever dysfunction the bar surfaces -- indecision, blame, conflict avoidance, defensive culture
- Toward the leader naming the dynamic in front of the team. See [[public-truth-bomb]]
- Toward team accountability for the dynamic, not just leader accountability. See [[team-feels-oppressed-not-accountable]]

## Anti-Patterns

- **Gaming the bar by selecting only meetings the leader enjoys.** Defeats the purpose; the avoided meetings are precisely the ones whose dysfunction the practice surfaces.
- **Rating leniently to avoid having to act.** The bar must be honest. A 4 is not a 5.
- **Using the bar to be tyrannical with the team.** The 5-star bar is the leader's commitment, not a club to beat the team with. "This meeting sucks, how do we fix it?" is collaborative. "This meeting sucks, you all need to do better" is blame in a new costume.

## Tone

Joe describes the practice with humor and the confidence of repeated success. "It's like a beautiful little hack." The framing is playful, not heavy. The practice is delivered as an experiment the leader gets to try, not a discipline the leader must endure.
