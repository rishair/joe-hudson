# How to Release Anger Out Loud

## The Practice

When a person has never released anger out loud before, or has been blocked at the threshold of doing it, the technique itself needs to be taught. The body does not automatically know how. Joe gives the technique in a single compressed instruction:

"The trick with anger is you speak it, you speak it in an angry tone, and you don't stop. So it's like, fucking, what the fuck? I dedicated my entire fucking life to you and you show up one day with a fucking letter? Are you fucking kidding me? Fuck you, I deserve better than that. We deserve better than that. That's what anger sounds like, but it doesn't stop. And if you can't think of something to say, just fuck you, fuck you, fuck you. It works too." -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Get Over My Ex

The three rules: speak it (out loud, with the voice), in an angry tone (not in a flat reporting voice), and do not stop. The third rule is the one most people break, because anger that surfaces produces immediate breakpoints — pauses to think of what to say next, pauses to check if this is okay, pauses to soften what just came out. Each pause lets the channel re-clamp. The continuity is what keeps the channel open long enough for the held charge to actually move.

The fourth, often unstated rule is what to do when the words run out: just "fuck you, fuck you, fuck you" works as a bridge. The body does not need new content. It needs continuous voicing in an angry tone. The repeating phrase keeps the channel open until the next real piece of anger arrives.

## Why the Three Rules Matter

**Out loud, not silent.** The voice is the body's anger instrument. Silent anger — even angry thought — does not discharge the charge held in the throat, jaw, chest, and diaphragm. The voice doing angry work moves the held musculature. Without the voice, the anger remains in the body even if the mind has registered it.

**Angry tone, not narrative tone.** People who are unpracticed at expressing anger often try to do anger work in the tone they use to tell a story. They say "I am angry that she left" the way they would say "I am sad that the dog died." The body does not register the angry-tone speech as anger. The same words spoken in a flat tone do not discharge. The anger has to be in the voice — pitch raised, volume up, edges sharp, breath driving the sound from below. The body needs to hear itself be angry for the discharge to happen.

**Don't stop.** The pause is the resistance pattern. Each break in the stream is a moment for the inner inhibitor to take back over. People who are new to anger release will release for ten seconds, pause to check, release for ten more seconds, pause to soften. The two-second pauses functionally cap the release. The technique is to keep going, even if what is coming out is repetitive, even if it is just "fuck you, fuck you, fuck you," even if it feels uncomfortable to keep voicing without checking. Five minutes of continuous voicing produces what fifteen minutes of fragmented release does not.

## When Joe Uses This Instruction

The instruction is for the person who is at the threshold of releasing anger and does not know how. They have surfaced the anger. They have agreed to express it. They are ready. They are about to start. They need the form.

This is different from [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]], which is for the person who cannot find the felt anger and needs to perform it first to summon it. This instruction is for the person who has the felt anger and needs the technique to discharge it.

In the divorce session, Joe gives the instruction in the moment of transition. The client has just said "kind of, yeah" to whether he wants to express the anger. Joe gets group consent first ("can we just unmute everybody?" so the client has support), then delivers the three-rule instruction with the specific example using the actual content of the client's situation ("you show up one day with a fucking letter"). The example is built from the client's own story so the client can immediately step into it.

The client's first attempt comes out exactly to form: "Goddamn it, why would you do things this way? If there was — if there was that much of a problem, why wouldn't you just come and tell me about it?" The voice is angry. The speech is continuous. The technique works on the first try.

## What It Produces

The client, given the technique, releases sustained anger for roughly two minutes — covering the marriage, the secrecy, the relationship coach who never spoke to him, the betrayal, the loss of ten years. The release is structured, articulate, and continuous. The shame that had been visibly present at the start of the call had been blocking exactly this material. Once the channel was opened with the technique, the held content moved through quickly.

After the release, Joe directs the client back to the love practice (looking at people on the call). The shame returns somewhat — confirming that this was not the entire anger that had been turned inward, just one round. The closing instruction is the prescription for ongoing work: "Get like punching bag, get a lot of that anger out." The technique learned in the room becomes the home practice. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the home-practice form.

## The Specific Wording Matters

The example Joe provides — "fucking, what the fuck? I dedicated my entire fucking life to you and you show up one day with a fucking letter?" — is built from the client's situation. The wording is full-throated, profane, and immediate. Several features are deliberate:

- **Profanity is not optional.** "Fucking" appears multiple times in the example. The expletive does work that polite anger does not — it carries the body's intensity into the voice. People who do anger release without permission to swear often produce a thinner, more cognitive version that does not discharge as effectively. The permission to swear is part of the permission to be in the anger.

- **The phrasing is incomplete sentences.** Real anger does not produce well-formed paragraphs. "Are you fucking kidding me?" is one anger speaking another. The choppy, rhetorical, incomplete form is what anger actually sounds like in the body. Teaching the practice as if it required complete sentences invites the inhibitor back.

- **The example uses the client's specific content.** Joe does not give a generic "say what you are angry about." He uses the actual material — the letter, the ten years, the betrayal. Specificity makes the practice startable. Generic instructions ("just yell what comes up") often produce nothing because the body cannot find the entry point. Specific content seeded from the client's own story gives the body a place to begin.

## The Bridge Phrase

"And if you can't think of something to say, just fuck you, fuck you, fuck you. It works too."

This is the rule that prevents the first failure mode. People who are releasing anger for the first time often run out of content quickly. They get through two sentences of real material, then there is a pause, then the inhibitor takes over: "Well, I guess that is it. I do not want to be too much. Maybe that was enough."

The bridge phrase prevents this. When the content runs out, the body keeps voicing on auto-pilot. The continuous "fuck you" is not a placeholder; it is a piece of anger work in its own right. The body is still discharging through the voice. Often the next layer of real material arrives during the bridge — the mind was empty for thirty seconds, then a new specific anger emerges out of the continuous voicing. The bridge holds the channel open for the next piece to come up.

This is the same principle as the don't-stop rule, made operational. The way you don't-stop when there is nothing to say is to say "fuck you" until there is.

## The Group Consent Setup

Before the technique can be deployed in a live group, the group has to consent to receive the anger. In the divorce session, Joe asks: "By a show of hands, who — actually, can we just unmute everybody? And everybody who would love to hear this anger come and who understands that it's really just like a love that's moving the pipes out? Can you just say yes so you—"

The framing of the consent matters: not "is it okay if he yells" but "who would love to hear this anger come and who understands it is really just like a love that's moving the pipes out?" The group is being invited to receive, not to tolerate. The framing rests on the [[anger-is-love]] principle — the anger is not aggression toward anyone in the room; it is the love-form of the energy that has been blocked.

The client's own confirmation that the group is supporting him ("Just so that Eric knows that he has support?") is the green light. The technique is delivered after the consent. This is the same structural prerequisite as [[get-angry-in-the-car]] — the anger needs a container that can receive it. In private, the car or the woods is the container. In group, the group becomes the container if it has consented to be.

## What Goes Wrong Without the Technique

People who are new to anger release without the explicit technique typically produce one of these:

- **Narrative anger.** They tell the story of what they are angry about, in the tone of telling a story. The body registers this as reporting, not anger work. Nothing discharges.

- **Single-burst anger.** They produce one strong angry sentence, then stop. The pause closes the channel. The held charge is barely touched.

- **Apologetic anger.** They release one sentence and then apologize for it, or qualify it, or explain it. Each apology is a re-clamp.

- **Performed anger.** They produce something that sounds like what they think anger should sound like — louder volume, more emphatic words — but without the felt charge in the voice. The body is not actually moving the anger; the mind is producing a representation of anger. See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] for the read that catches this.

The three-rule technique addresses all four failure modes. Out loud (not silent thought), angry tone (not narrative), don't stop (not single-burst, not apologetic). The bridge phrase addresses what to do when the content runs out, which is the moment most often used to legitimize stopping.

## The Relationship to the Container

The technique works because there is permission. In the divorce session, the permission comes from the group's explicit consent and from Joe's modeling. The client steps into the released voice because the room is holding the release as welcome.

The same technique in a hostile or uncertain room would not produce the same result. The body needs to know it will not be punished for the discharge. This is why the home version uses private containers (car, beach, woods) — the body needs the felt safety that nothing will land on anyone and nothing will come back as judgment. The technique itself is the same; the container changes.

## Tone

Joe delivers the technique with practical immediacy — neither solemnly nor casually. The instruction has the texture of a craftsman teaching a craft: here is how it works, here are the rules, here is what to do when the obvious problem arises. The casualness around the swearing ("just fuck you, fuck you, fuck you. It works too") signals that the practice is not precious, not requiring special conditions. It is something the body can do, simply, with the right form.

The lack of solemnity matters. If the technique is delivered as if it were a sacred ritual, the client will perform the sacredness and miss the discharge. If it is delivered as practical mechanics — speak it, this tone, don't stop, here's the bridge — the client can step into it without having to manage the meta-frame.
