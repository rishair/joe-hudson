# Feel the Pain of Not Being Loved (When You Hide Yourself)

## The Practice

Sit down and feel through, in your body, the actual cost of your conflict avoidance. Not the conceptual cost. The felt cost. Let it hurt enough that the avoidance stops looking like the cheaper option.

The instruction in Joe's words:

"Feel the pain of not being loved. You're not showing up, so no one can love you. Feel the pain of that. Oftentimes it is that pain, when you become so acutely aware of it, you can't avoid conflict anymore. If you really sit down and say, oh, I am not -- there's no -- I have set it up so that my wife, husband can never love me because they don't know who I am, what I feel, what's really going on for me, the problems that I have. So they can't freaking love me. So I've set up this world where I can't be loved and they can't love me. And, and feel your pain in that and feel their pain. Like, oh, imagine that they want to love you and they can't because you're not there. Let that, let that hurt. And if that hurts long enough, you're gonna stop it. You're gonna be like, okay, here it is, this is me." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

## How to Do It

1. **Sit down somewhere private.** This is not a mental exercise to do while walking around. The body has to be in it.

2. **State the structural fact aloud or in writing.** "I have set it up so that [partner / family member / friend / colleague] cannot love me, because they do not know who I am, what I feel, what's really going on for me, or the problems that I have. They cannot love me. I have arranged this."

3. **Let the sentence land in the body.** It will produce something. Often grief. Sometimes shame. Sometimes a sharp pain in the chest. Stay with whatever arrives. Do not redirect to fixing it.

4. **Add the second sentence.** "They want to love me and they cannot, because I am not there." Notice what changes in the body when the pain is felt from the other person's side as well as your own.

5. **Stay with the pain until it has moved through.** Do not move to action while you are still in the constriction of the discovery. The action emerges on its own once the pain has been fully felt.

## What This Is For

The practice is not about producing motivation to "do better." It is about feeling the actual cost of the avoidance fully enough that the body changes its calculation about what is the easier option.

Conflict avoidance persists because, in the moment, the avoidance feels like the cheaper choice. The cost is invisible because it is distributed across time and across small moments. The practice concentrates the cost. When the cost is felt all at once, the math changes.

"And if that hurts long enough, you're gonna stop it. You're gonna be like, okay, here it is, this is me." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The shift is not willpower-based. It is the natural response of a system that has now actually felt what it has been doing.

## Companion Practice: Visualize the Walking-On-Eggshells Cost

A related move in the same teaching is to visualize what happens if you stop managing the partner's mood, and feel through everything you have to feel there. This pairs with the love-pain practice.

"What happens if you don't? And visualize and feel everything you have to feel there. So just like anything, you're avoiding conflict so that you don't have to feel certain emotions. So go feel the emotions and then you won't avoid the conflict." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The shape: you have been preventing your partner from being upset because their being upset would produce a feeling in you that you do not want to have. The way out is to feel through the feeling without the situation. Once the feeling has been felt, the strategy that was protecting you from it loses its function.

The two practices work together. The love-pain practice surfaces the cost of the continuing pattern. The eggshells-feel-through practice releases the emotion that was driving the pattern in the first place. Together, they break the structure that was keeping the conflict avoidance in place.

## When to Use It

- After [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]] has been named conceptually and the person has not yet shifted
- When a person is intellectually clear that conflict avoidance is costing them but cannot find the motivation to change
- When the standard reframes ("just be honest with your partner") are not landing
- When the person is asking what to do and the answer requires a felt-sense recognition, not a procedure
- As part of the developmental sequence from [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]]: anger work first, abandonment work second, this self-pain-felt-fully work as part of the third stage

## What It Tends to Produce

- Grief about how long the pattern has been running
- Recognition of specific moments where the love was offered and not received because the person was not present
- A felt sense of the partner's parallel loneliness
- A decrease in resistance to the next honest conversation (the pain of avoidance is now larger than the pain of conflict)
- Sometimes a spontaneous decision to have the conversation that has been avoided

## What to Avoid

- Do not turn the practice into self-flagellation. The pain is to be felt, not amplified into shame.
- Do not jump to action while still in the initial constriction. The clarity emerges after the pain has moved.
- Do not use the practice as ammunition against yourself or against the partner. The honesty about the structure is about freedom, not blame.
- Do not skip the second half (feeling the partner's pain as well). The mutual recognition is what makes the felt sense complete.

## The Broader Mechanism

This practice is one specific application of the principle that you go around the avoided emotion by feeling it. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. The conflict avoidance is itself an avoidance of feeling. The most direct route is to feel what was being avoided.

The practice connects to [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]] in technique but applies it to a different kind of outcome: not the dramatic feared catastrophe, but the slow, structural, lived-now consequence of the strategy that was supposed to prevent the catastrophe.
