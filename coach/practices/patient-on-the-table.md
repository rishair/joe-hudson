# Patient on the Table (Depersonalize the Problem)

## The Practice

When something has gone wrong on a team or in your own life and the temptation is to assign fault, reframe the problem the way an emergency-room doctor reframes a body in front of them: what are the symptoms, what is the next step in treatment? Treat the situation as a patient on the table rather than as a referendum on who is to blame.

"Let's just look at this like a patient on the table. What are the symptoms? And then what's the next step in treatment? And so, you know, solving problems like that and not worrying about like, well, whose fault is something, I think is just a really good way to keep the shame from cropping up in people. And then they can stay much more in the problem-solving mode." -- Jaime Waydo, Taking Care of Yourself First

## Why It Works

The practice runs on the mechanism of [[shame-drives-behavior]]: shame holds problems in place. The moment the question becomes "whose fault," the person who feels implicated starts managing their own shame instead of working on the actual problem. The energy that should be solving the problem goes into self-protection, blame-deflection, and damage control.

When the problem is framed as a patient -- symptoms, diagnosis, treatment -- there is no one to blame. The body is just doing what it is doing. The task is to figure out what is happening and what to do next. No moral weight attaches to the symptoms.

This depersonalization is structurally similar to [[just-business-vs-impersonal]] and [[money-is-impersonal]]: removing the personal stake unlocks clearer action. The personal stake was never doing useful work. It was creating a second problem (the shame management) on top of the first.

## When to Use It

- After a project failure, before the team meeting where the failure is reviewed.
- When the team starts looking for someone to blame.
- When you notice your own voice starting on "you should have known..." -- run the patient frame on yourself.
- When emotions are running so high that the actual problem is invisible behind the fault dynamics.
- When onboarding a new member onto a team that has cultural shame -- modeling the frame teaches the team a different way.

## How to Apply It

The verbal moves are simple:

- "Let's look at this like a patient on the table. What are we seeing?"
- "What are the symptoms?"
- "Given those symptoms, what's the next step?"
- "Not what should have happened, but what is happening and what do we do."

The internal moves are more important than the verbal ones. Notice when the "whose fault" question is forming and consciously substitute the "what's the next step" question. Notice when shame starts rising in yourself and substitute the symptom-and-treatment frame.

For yourself, the same practice applies. When the voice in your head starts on "you should have done X," step into the ER doc frame: what are the symptoms here, what is the next thing to do? The voice loses its purchase when the question is reframed.

## What It Replaces

The default mode in many teams when something goes wrong:

- Search for the person responsible.
- Apply social pressure or formal consequence to that person.
- The named person manages their shame instead of the problem.
- Other team members watch and learn to hide future problems.
- The actual problem either gets a poorly-implemented fix or recurs.

The patient-on-the-table frame interrupts the cycle at the first step. Nobody is searched for. The problem is named neutrally. The next step is identified. The team moves.

## The Edge Case

The frame does not absolve [[fault-vs-responsibility]]: responsibility for taking the next action still attaches to someone. The point is not that nobody owns anything. The point is that ownership of the next step is different from blame for the past step. Owning the next step is empowering. Carrying blame for the past step is shame-installing.

Brett's reframe sharpens this: "What if it's not your fault? It's your responsibility. ... Like my own self-responsibility, not like an outwardly imposed responsibility that I should do something, but just, I'm responsible for the outcomes in my life." See [[fault-vs-responsibility]] for the full distinction.

## When This Practice Is Particularly Useful

- Leadership teams reviewing failed product launches.
- Couples reviewing a fight without spiraling into recrimination.
- Parents talking to children about a broken thing.
- Personal reviews of mistakes the voice in your head wants to make into shame.
- Therapy or coaching sessions when the client is trying to assign blame to themselves or others as a substitute for working the problem.
