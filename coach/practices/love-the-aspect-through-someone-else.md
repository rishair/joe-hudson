# Love the Disowned Aspect by Loving It in Someone Else First

## The Practice

A specific hack for learning to love a part of yourself that you cannot directly love. The mechanism reverses the usual flow. Instead of starting from yourself, start from someone else you already love who has the same trait. Notice that you love them. Notice that this trait is one of the things present in them. Then ask: how can I love them and not love myself for the same thing?

The practice routes self-love through already-existing love, where the resistance is much lower.

"Here's a trick to learning how to love that aspect of yourself. You just think of somebody else who has that aspect that you love, and you go, 'Oh wait, how can I love them and that aspect of them and not love myself?' And actually give yourself that feeling of love back to yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

## How To Do It

1. **Name the aspect.** Be specific. Not "I'm bad" but "I'm a perfectionist" or "I'm sometimes dishonest" or "I think I know more than I do."

2. **Find someone you already love who has that aspect.** This is the load-bearing step. Anyone — a parent, partner, friend, sibling, child. The person must be someone you genuinely love, and they must genuinely have the trait. You do not need to ask them. You just need to know it about them.

3. **Notice the love.** Bring the person to mind. Let yourself feel the love you already have for them. The love is not for the trait. It is for them, including the trait.

4. **Notice the inconsistency.** You love this person. They have this trait. How is it that this trait is lovable in them and not in you? The inconsistency is not a logical puzzle. It is felt.

5. **Channel the love back.** Actually feel the love and direct it toward yourself with the same trait. Joe is explicit: "give yourself that feeling of love back to yourself." It is the same feeling, redirected.

## Why It Works

Direct self-love often fails because the person is trying to love what they have organized their identity around hating. The trait is bundled with shame, with the story of who they are at their worst, with the parental voice that told them this trait was unacceptable. Asking the person to love it directly is asking them to override the entire structure.

Loving it in another person is much easier because the entire structure is not in the way. The shame is about you, not them. The parental voice is about you, not them. The love is already flowing toward the other person and just needs to recognize that the trait is one of the things being loved.

Once the love is flowing toward the trait — even in someone else — the trait stops being "the thing that cannot be loved." It becomes "the thing that is lovable, including in me." The redirection is the integration.

## What Makes the Trait Universal

Joe gives several examples to help the practice along. The pattern: every trait the person rejects in themselves is a trait that has been present in many people they love.

"Whatever it is that you're judging yourself for, say it's dishonesty. We've all been dishonest. Let's say that one of the things you judge yourself for is being a perfectionist. We all have some aspect that's a perfectionist in us. Let's say something that you're judging yourself for is, um, being a know-it-all. There's some part of us that all thinks we know something that we don't know. That's just the reality of things." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The implicit move: universalize. Whatever the trait, the universe of humans who have it is large, and many of them are people you love. The trait is part of being human, not your particular defect.

This is structurally the same move Joe makes in the cunningness reframe in [[loving-all-parts]]: "Can you imagine a 5-year-old girl who is cunning? I don't know any." The trait is normalized by being placed inside the universal experience.

## When To Assign This

- A person has identified a projection (via [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] or [[five-triggers-projection-experiment]]) and asks "how do I love that part of myself?"
- A person says "I cannot love this part of me" with conviction
- A person is collapsing into self-criticism about the trait they just recognized
- A person is at the second step of the upright-apology sequence and the shame is preventing the upright body
- A person presents [[loving-all-parts]] as conceptually clear but not landing in any specific part

## What This Is Not

Not "find someone who is bad at this and judge them less." The practice requires actual existing love for the person, not tolerance. The love is the ingredient.

Not "find an example who handles it better than you do." The person must have the same trait — not a moderated, polished, or socially acceptable version. The point is to see that the actual trait is lovable in the actual person, which means it is lovable in you.

Not a one-time exercise. The trait will continue to surface. The practice is to keep redirecting the love, again and again, until the loving of the trait in oneself becomes spontaneous.

## What This Pairs With

This is one of two specific hacks Joe offers for when "awareness alone" does not produce self-acceptance. The other is [[self-forgiveness-as-spoken-move]] — literally giving yourself forgiveness for the trait. Either can work alone; together they form a tight pair for converting a recognized projection into actual self-loving.

"If that awareness doesn't immediately create acceptance in your system, then there's two more steps you can take. The first one is to learn to love that aspect of yourself, and the second is to forgive yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The practice here is the "learn to love that aspect" step. The forgiveness move comes after if love alone has not landed.

## Why Awareness Alone Sometimes Isn't Enough

"Again, sometimes the awareness is just enough to do it, and you don't have to do anything else." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

This is the orienting condition. For many recognitions, simply seeing the projection produces immediate relief — the trigger drops and the trait integrates without further work. The practice in this article is specifically for when that does not happen. The recognition has been made, but the loving has not arrived. The hack is the bridge.

If the practice is being assigned when awareness alone would have sufficed, it can become extra work. The diagnostic: did the recognition produce relief on its own? If yes, no further work needed. If no, the practice has a purpose.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Surprise at how easy it is to love this trait in the chosen person
- Resistance to redirecting the love back to themselves (the diagnostic that the redirection is the work)
- A felt sense of the love arriving at the part of self that has been exiled
- The trait becoming smaller — not by going away but by being held inside love
- Spontaneous compassion for other people they had previously judged for the same trait
- A reduction in the trigger that originally surfaced the projection

## Anti-Patterns

- **Choosing a person you do not actually love.** Picking someone you tolerate or admire from a distance does not generate the feeling. The feeling is the ingredient.
- **Choosing a trait that does not actually match.** "I'm bad at exercise" and "my friend who is bad at exercise" — but the trait you are working on is not exercise, it is self-discipline. The mismatch makes the bridge wobble.
- **Loving the person despite the trait.** This routes around the trait instead of through it. The practice requires loving the trait as one of the things present in the person you love. See [[dont-add-anyway-to-love]] for the related anti-pattern.
- **Performing the practice.** Saying "I love this in them" without actually feeling it. The body has to be in it. If the feeling is not there, the redirection has nothing to redirect.
- **Using this as a way to avoid the deeper work.** The practice is a bridge to self-love, not a substitute for the [[upright-apology]] or [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] underneath the part.

## Tone

Joe gives this as "a great little hack." The tone is light and practical, not heavy. The practice is offered as a workaround that often succeeds where head-on self-love fails. Keep the lightness. The person trying this should not feel they are doing serious spiritual work. They are doing a small redirection that has surprisingly large effects.
