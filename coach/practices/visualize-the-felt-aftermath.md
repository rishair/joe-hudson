# Visualize the Felt Aftermath (Not the Performance)

## The Practice

When facing a high-stakes event with performance anxiety, do not rehearse the performance. Do not run scenarios of what you will say, what could go wrong, how to recover. Instead, close your eyes and visualize the *aftermath* in felt-sense detail. Not what you accomplished. What it feels like when it is over.

The instructions:

1. When anxiety arises in the lead-up to the event, close your eyes.
2. Picture the moment the event ends. Put the computer down. Take the headset off. Pack up the materials.
3. Walk through the post-event sequence in sensory detail. What is the look on a teammate's face when you high-five? What does the producer say as you take the headset off? Who is texting you? What are they saying?
4. As you go to bed that night and put your head on the pillow, what is the predominant feeling? Is it pride? Is it relief? Is it gratitude? Play it all the way out.
5. Stay with the felt quality of that aftermath until the body settles.

The shift is from rehearsing performance to inhabiting the post-event emotional state in advance.

## Where the Practice Comes From

Aaron Taylor, a former NFL offensive guard and CBS Sports broadcaster, describes receiving this instruction from his wife (a two-time Olympic beach volleyball player) the night before his first NFL broadcast. He had spent days hyper-preparing: studying film, calling everyone he knew, researching team history back to 1911. The preparation was producing escalating anxiety.

His wife: "I'm a little worried that you're so excited about this that you're going to squeeze it so tightly that you're going to fumble this opportunity. I would offer you this. Every time that you're nervous or want to do something or thinking about how you're going to do X, Y, or Z instead of thinking about what it is you want to do, think about how it is you want to feel after the event is over."

"That was the best game I've ever called on the biggest stage in my biggest moment. I'm in the College Dang Football Hall of Fame. I've been on TV for 14 years, but that was the only time I never had performance anxiety and wasn't worried about what I was going to do, because I shifted my focus from what I feared to what I wanted to feel." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The transcript captures Joe noticing what was structurally elegant about the instruction: "She literally was just like, 'You might fumble.' It's like the whole thing was like you could feel it in your -- like when you were describing your wife's words to me, I'm like, 'Oh, I can feel that in my body.' She is -- she was talking to your body."

The instruction works at the somatic level because the visualization is of a feeling, not a result.

## Why This Is Distinct From Other Visualizations

This is not the same as:

**[[visualizing-feared-outcomes]]**: that practice has you feel the fear in advance so it does not control the moment. This practice has you feel the post-event aliveness in advance so the body has somewhere to orient that is not the fear.

**[[goal-beyond-the-goal]]**: that principle has you aim past the target so the target becomes easier to hit. This practice does not change what you aim at. It changes whether your nervous system is in the fear-of-failing state or the felt-aftermath state during the performance.

**Sports-psychology visualization of performance**: that has you rehearse the actions of the event in detail. This practice deliberately skips the performance and goes straight to its emotional aftermath.

The distinction matters because rehearsing the performance often increases the gripping. The body adds the performance to the list of things to control. Visualizing the felt aftermath does the opposite: it puts the body in the post-stakes state, where the performance is no longer load-bearing.

## What the Practice Produces

The reliable effects:

- The fight-or-flight loop interrupts. The body, which was preparing for threat, is given a different orientation: the safety after the event.
- The dopamine and oxytocin associated with the felt aftermath actually release. Aaron's framing: "to my brain, it didn't know the difference."
- The "squeezing" of the opportunity releases. The grip relaxes because the body has already received what it was gripping for.
- Presence in the actual moment increases. Without the anxiety running, attention is available for the work itself.

"And to me, presence is the portal to a better outcome and reality. And that's what focusing on my feelings afterward allowed me to do in the moment because there was no downside as far as I was aware." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

## When Joe Reaches For This

This practice fits when:

- A coachee is facing a high-stakes performance moment (pitch, broadcast, public speaking, big meeting, surgery, custody hearing).
- The preparation is producing escalating anxiety rather than confidence.
- The coachee is "squeezing it too tightly" -- gripping the opportunity in a way that will produce the fumble they fear.
- The coachee is over-rehearsing the actions of the event and the body is bracing harder with each rehearsal.
- The fear is about specific feared outcomes ("what if I forget what to say") rather than diffuse anxiety.

It does not fit when the anxiety is about something the person is genuinely under-prepared for. In that case the answer is preparation. This practice is for when the preparation is sufficient and the bracing is the actual obstacle.

## The Mechanism: Fear of the Felt Outcome

This practice operationalizes [[fear-is-about-feeling]]. Performance anxiety is not really about the performance. It is about the feelings the person will have to feel if the performance goes badly: the shame, the embarrassment, the loss of standing. Those feelings are what the body is bracing against.

The felt-aftermath visualization gives the body the opposite emotional outcome to inhabit. The bracing was against the feared felt aftermath. Inhabiting the desired felt aftermath releases the bracing.

This is consistent with [[fear-vs-excitement]]: fear is excitement without the breath. The visualization restores the breath by giving the body something forward to move toward rather than something behind to defend against.

## The Variation: Cross-Domain

Aaron applies the practice to broadcasting. The structure transfers to any high-stakes moment:

- Before a difficult conversation: visualize the felt aftermath of having said what was true. Not what you said. How it feels at the end of the day when you have spoken truth.
- Before a pitch: visualize the felt aftermath of having pitched from your full self. Not whether you got the funding. The felt quality of having shown up fully.
- Before a hard parent-child conversation: visualize the felt aftermath of having held both love and the difficult truth. Not how the child responded.
- Before death (in palliative settings): visualize the felt aftermath of having lived fully. Not what was accomplished. The felt quality of having been here.

The pattern: visualize the *emotional state* you will be in after the event, in maximum sensory detail. The outcome is downstream of the state, not the other way around.

## The Anti-Pattern Inside the Practice

The practice fails when the visualization becomes a should: "I should feel proud after this." That is the brace re-installing itself in spiritual clothing. The visualization is not of the feeling you think you should have. It is of the feeling you actually want to have. The honest naming of which feelings you want is part of the practice. See [[wants-over-shoulds]].

The practice also fails when used as a guarantee: "If I visualize feeling proud, then I will perform well, so I had better do this visualization." That makes the visualization a performance, which produces the same bracing the practice is meant to release. The visualization is offered, not demanded. The aftermath is felt, not enforced.
