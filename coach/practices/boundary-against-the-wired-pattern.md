# Boundary Against the Wired Pattern

## The Practice

A specific boundary move that separates love from the companion it got wired with in childhood. The structure is always the same:

**"I love you. I want your love. But I am not willing to [the wired companion] for your love."**

The variants substitute the specific companion the person's childhood paired with love:
- "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to be criticized for your love."
- "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to be dominated for your love."
- "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to chase you for your love."
- "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to fight for your love."
- "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to be guilted for your love."
- "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to suffer this abuse for your love."

"How do I draw the boundary and say, no, I want your love, but I want it without the jealousy. Like, I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to chase you for the love. I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to be dominated for your love." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The boundary is the operational form of the recognition that what got wired with love in childhood is not actually part of love. It is the spoken assertion that love can come without the companion.

## The Three Components

The boundary statement has three structural components that all have to be present:

**1. The love.** "I love you." Stated first. The boundary is delivered from inside the love, not from outside it. This is what makes it [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against|a boundary with rather than a boundary against]]. Without the love-statement first, the boundary reads as rejection or punishment.

**2. The want.** "I want your love." The want is named explicitly. The boundary is not "I don't need this anymore" or "I'm done with you." The want is still in the picture. The relationship is still being reached for. The boundary is what conditions the reaching, not what ends it.

**3. The limit.** "But I'm not willing to [companion] for your love." The specific named refusal. The companion is named. The refusal is what the person will not do anymore. The structural form is "I am not willing to" — language about the speaker's choice, not about what the other has to do.

The three components together make the boundary workable. Any one of them missing breaks the structure. Without the love, it reads as rejection. Without the want, it reads as exit. Without the specific limit, it is too vague to actually operate.

## Why This Boundary Is Required

The wired pattern (see [[what-was-wired-with-love-needs-untying]]) installed an implicit agreement: the way to get love is to accept the companion that came with it. The body operates under this agreement automatically. The agreement is what keeps the wired pattern running across decades.

The boundary breaks the agreement. The spoken statement is the revocation of the deal the body has been operating under. "I will accept love without the companion" is the new term. The boundary does not change the wiring directly; what it changes is whether the person continues to act on the wiring. The wiring continues to fire; the boundary determines what the person does when it fires.

This is why the boundary is one of the operational moves inside the larger work of untying what was wired with love. The untying work surfaces what got wired. The boundary is the practical move that stops re-installing it through ongoing compliance.

## Why This Boundary Is Scary

The body, having lived under the wired agreement for decades, treats the boundary as a threat to receiving love at all. The agreement was "I get love by accepting the companion." The boundary says "I will get love without accepting the companion." The body's prediction: I will not get love anymore. The only love I knew came with the companion. Refusing the companion means refusing the only love I know.

"What I was doing is I was making a stand saying, hey, I won't accept love in this variety anymore. I will only have love that comes without criticism. But the reason it was scary in me is because I was thinking I was saying I might not ever get love again. Because the only love I knew was with criticism." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The scariness is structural. The boundary requires the person to act on a love they have not yet experienced — the love without the companion. Until they have experienced it, the boundary is faith in something the body has no memory of. The faith is what makes the boundary one of the scariest moves a person does in the love-work, and what makes it a precise marker that the wiring is being addressed at the level it actually lives at.

## What the Boundary Sometimes Produces

The other party often does not respond immediately. The boundary may be met with silence. Months. Joe's worked example (see [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]]): 18 months of silence after he called his parents and named the criticism. The silence is part of the cost. The body's prediction that "I will not get love anymore" appears, for the duration of the silence, to be confirmed. Holding the boundary through that period is the work.

When the other party eventually comes back — if they do — the relationship often resumes on different terms. The companion behavior is reduced or stopped. The relationship that resumes is the one that was possible without the companion all along.

"My parents didn't call me for almost a year and a half. They were just, they were really upset. And then eventually they called me up and they're like, okay, we wanna spend time with you. I was like, great, I wanna spend time with you too. And they were much, much, much, much better at not criticizing me." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

"In that process, I learned, oh, I can have love without criticism. And now, once I did that, I don't need to push love away as much anymore. Why do I need to push it away? Because it's actually safe."

## The Self-Version Comes First

A specific sequencing rule: the boundary against the wired pattern in external relationships is much easier to hold if the same boundary has been drawn internally first. If the wiring is criticism, stop criticizing yourself. If the wiring is domination, stop dominating yourself. If the wiring is the fight, stop fighting yourself.

"As soon as the internal work has happened, where we can say, oh, I don't need to dominate myself for love, I don't need to chase myself for love, I don't need to fight myself to get love. And I can do that externally. I can say that boundary out loud." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The internal version is what gives the external version traction. A person who is still criticizing themselves all day cannot easily refuse criticism from a partner — the body still expects criticism as love and will keep accepting it. The internal boundary changes the body's expectation. The external boundary then operates on the new expectation.

This is why the self-talk work and the boundary work are the same work in two directions. See [[negative-self-talk-reveals-love-imprint]] for the diagnostic that makes the self-version visible. See [[what-was-done-to-you-youre-now-doing-to-yourself]] for the broader principle the self-version operates from.

## Where the Boundary Lands

The boundary can be drawn with any of the relationships where the wired companion is operating:

- **With parents.** The canonical worked example is [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]] — Joe calls his parents in his twenties, names the constant criticism, draws the line. 18 months of silence. Criticism stops in the family.
- **With a romantic partner.** "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to fight for your love anymore" delivered to a partner. The partner adjusts, or the relationship cannot continue in the form it had.
- **With a friend.** Same structure. "I love you, I want this friendship, but I am not willing to be the one who always reaches out / be teased about this / be the audience for the same complaint."
- **With a sibling, an in-law, a colleague.** The structure does not change. The relationship does.

The boundary is the same in every case. The work of finding the right language for the specific relationship is the work of locating which companion is operating in that particular contact.

## When This Practice Applies

- When the wired pattern has been identified (see [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]] and [[what-was-wired-with-love-needs-untying]]) and the question is what to actually do
- When a client has been blaming the partner or the parent for the companion behavior and the work is to shift from blame to boundary (see [[convincing-ourselves-were-not-pushing-love-away]])
- When a relationship has been organizing around the wired companion for years and the client is recognizing they cannot continue the same way
- When the internal version of the boundary (stopping the self-criticism, stopping the self-domination) has been worked enough that the external version becomes possible
- When [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]] has been delivered and the client is asking what the actual move is

## What This Practice Is Not

It is not an ultimatum. The boundary names what the speaker will do, not what the other has to do. "I will not be in this configuration" is not "you have to change or I am leaving." It is the structural form that allows the speaker to remain in love while not in the configuration.

It is not a single moment. The boundary often has to be redrawn. Each time the companion behavior appears, the boundary may need to be held again — sometimes in the form of physical departure (leaving the room, the visit, the call), sometimes in the form of the spoken statement, sometimes in the form of silent non-engagement with the companion.

It is not a cure. The boundary does not undo the wiring. The wiring continues to fire. The boundary is what the person does when the wiring fires. Over time, holding the boundary while the wiring fires produces the updating — the body learns that love can come without the companion. The boundary is the condition under which that learning becomes possible.

It is not vengeance. The 18 months of silence in Joe's example were not Joe punishing his parents. The silence was the structural consequence of Joe's choice to not be in the criticism-configuration. The parents could choose how they wanted to be in relationship to that choice. They chose silence for a long time. They eventually chose to come back on different terms.

## Tone

The practice is delivered straightforwardly but with the acknowledgment that this is one of the scariest things a person does. The structural simplicity (three components, named explicitly) belies the depth of the work. Joe names the scariness explicitly — "it was one of the scariest things I'd ever done" — which is part of what makes it possible for the client to take the step. The scariness is normal. The work is real. The structure holds.
