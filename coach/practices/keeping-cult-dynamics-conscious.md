# Keep Cult Dynamics Conscious by Joking About Them

## The Practice

When a group develops its own culture, language, and ways of relating that differ from the mainstream, the cult-dynamic possibility is structurally present. The practice is to name the dynamic openly and playfully -- to make jokes about being a cult -- so that the awareness of the possibility stays in the room. The naming keeps the dynamic visible. The visibility makes it harder for the dynamic to take over.

The opposite move -- defensive denial ("we are not a cult") -- is itself a marker. A group that cannot tolerate the cult joke is a group where the dynamic is already operating below conscious access.

## The Move

In conversation, when the cult possibility is present (a tight-knit transformative community, a group with shared practices and language, a household that does things differently than the surrounding culture), introduce the cult frame as a joke. Let it be funny. Notice how the group responds. The capacity to laugh together at the possibility is the signal that the awareness is alive.

If the joke produces defensiveness, that is data. If the joke produces uncomfortable silence, that is data. If the joke produces shared laughter and a continuation of the conversation, the awareness is functional.

The aim is not paranoia. The aim is conscious participation. The joke is the mechanism by which the awareness gets refreshed.

## In Joe's Words

Joe arrives at this recognition mid-conversation when Brett offers the reframe:

Brett: "Even if we're playing with the idea that this is a cult or not a cult, or letting ourselves play with the idea of letting the concept of cult show up in our jokes so that we are at least self-referential and self-aware of the tendency that we might have to become insular."

"Oh, I've never thought about it that way. So what you're basically saying, like, on some level, the jokes are, potentially the jokes are, a way to, it keeps it in consciousness. Like it creates an awareness of it so that it doesn't get out of hand potentially. And that actually makes me really appreciate it in a different way. I hadn't thought about it that way." -- Joe Hudson, Group Cohesion vs. Cult Dynamics

## Why It Works

The cult dynamic operates by becoming invisible. People stop seeing the structure they are inside because the structure becomes the water they swim in. The joke breaks the invisibility. The joke says: we see this. We are inside something that has features that another group would call a cult. Holding that recognition together makes it harder for the structure to become unconscious.

The defensive denial does the opposite. "We are not a cult" treats the cult possibility as something to push away. The pushing-away preserves the structure by removing it from examination. The structure can then operate without anyone noticing.

The joke is the consciousness-preserving move. The denial is the consciousness-evading move.

## The Diagnostic Test

Brett offers the test directly: "If we were just like defensive, like, 'No, we're not a cult,' like then that would be like sort of a, I don't know, maybe a red flag."

The capacity to engage the question lightly is the sign that the awareness is intact. The inability to engage it lightly -- the defensiveness, the rigidity, the seriousness about the question -- is the sign that the dynamic may be operating.

This test works for the individual person as much as for the group. A person who cannot laugh at their own attachment to a teaching, a practice, or a community is more likely to be in unconscious surrender to it. A person who can laugh at it is more likely to be in conscious relationship to it.

## The Concrete Move Joe Designs

Joe describes a structural move that does the same work: if the group ever had a physical center, he would put pictures of "the guru" on the wall, but change the picture monthly to a different person from the community. Some of the gurus would be cartoons or mythical animals.

"I had this idea though, if we ever did have a center, if there was ever a place where everybody was coming together to do this work consistently, that we would have a picture of a guru on the wall in like 5 or 6 places, but every month it would change. So we would just pick one of the people and like put them in the guru picture." -- Joe Hudson, Group Cohesion vs. Cult Dynamics

The move works on the same principle as the joke. It puts the guru dynamic on display in a way that makes the dynamic visible while preventing any single person from occupying the slot.

## The Test in Real Time

In the conversation, Joe asks Brett to test it on him directly: "Say, give me a cult joke or something like that about this work being a cult. And I want to feel what happens in my system." Brett delivers one. Joe notices: "Oh, there's more humor in it for sure. I can laugh at it. I couldn't laugh so easily at it recently. So that's a good sign."

The capacity to laugh is data about the internal relationship. The increase in capacity to laugh is data about movement. The practice can be done with oneself: try saying or hearing the cult joke, notice what happens internally, take that as information about where the attachment is.

## When to Use This

- In any group that is developing its own culture and language
- When the cult question is in the air, explicit or implicit
- When a person new to the group asks if it is a cult
- When the group is becoming insular and the boundary between us and them is hardening
- When a leader in the group is starting to occupy a special slot
- With oneself, when noticing that a practice or teaching has become unquestionable

## What Not to Do

Do not turn the joke into a way of dismissing the legitimate cult concern. "Ha ha, we joke about being a cult, so we are obviously not one" is itself the defense it was supposed to prevent. The joke has to remain a real engagement with the possibility, not a verbal inoculation against it.

Do not use the joke to test loyalty. "If you can't joke about us being a cult, you are not one of us" is the cult dynamic operating through the very mechanism that was supposed to prevent it.

## Tone

The joke is the tone. Light, playful, knowing. It is not delivered with edge or with judgment. It is delivered with the warmth of insiders who can see what they are inside and choose to engage it openly.
