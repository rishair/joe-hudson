# The Menu of Responses to the Self-Critical Voice

## The Practice

The inner critical voice cannot be silenced by force, but the way you respond to it determines whether it continues to run or begins to soften. There is a menu of available responses, each doing different work. The practice is to experiment with the menu — to try different responses and notice what each one produces. The voice changes through the relationship with it, not through suppression of it.

"Right now it tells you you're never going to get out of it, and you believe it. So if you believe everything it says, it continues. Why would it not? Think about it like it's a politician. And if the population believes in it, it just keeps going. And at some point, the population stops believing in it, it stops going... So how do you want to react to that?" -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The phrasing matters: "how do you want to react" rather than "you should respond by." The work is experimentation. The menu is not a hierarchy of correct answers. It is a set of options the person can play with to discover what each one does.

"There's a thousand of them. Experiment, play." -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

## The Four Anchor Responses

Joe names four specific responses across this session. They are the operational anchors of a menu that is in principle much larger.

### Response 1: "Ouch" (Register the Hit)

The bare noticing. When the voice says something attacking, respond with "ouch" — out loud, audibly. The ouch registers that the attack landed, makes the attack visible to the body, and refuses to let the attack pass as background.

This is the first move in the menu because it does the prerequisite work: it makes the voice audible to the person as a thing that is attacking, not as the way things are. Without this surfacing, no other response can be deployed. The voice is too embedded to work with.

See [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]] for the full treatment of this response, including the extended phrasing "Ouch, that hurts. Please don't treat me like that" and the structural reasons each component of the extended phrasing does its specific work.

### Response 2: "I See That You're Scared and I'm Right Here With You" (Hold the Frightened Part)

A response that addresses the voice as a frightened being rather than as an attacker. The voice attacking is the voice's defensive activation; underneath the attack is the fear that is producing the attack. This response speaks to the fear, names it, and offers presence.

"Also good ones are things like, I see that you're scared and I'm right here with you." -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The structural mechanism: the voice does not need to keep escalating its attack if the underlying fear is being met. The attack is the voice's strategy for getting the part of self the voice is protecting to listen. When the part is listened to directly, the voice no longer has to attack to be heard. The attack can soften because the protective function is being performed by the relationship rather than by the attack.

This response is most useful when the voice is loud, repetitive, or saying things that obviously do not match reality (catastrophizing, predicting disaster, telling the person they are about to fail). Those signatures are the voice in defensive activation. Speaking to the fear under the defense is what allows the defense to relax.

### Response 3: "I Wonder If That's True. How True Is That?" (Inquire Rather Than Believe)

A response that opens the voice's claim to inquiry. The voice asserts; the inquiry asks whether the assertion holds. Joe deploys this with a specific shape: the voice has just said something attacking, and the response is to wonder out loud whether the attack is accurate.

"Also, I wonder if that's true. That's a good one. Huh, how true is that?" -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The mechanism: the voice has been operating with the assumed authority of truth. The inquiry strips that authority and converts the voice's claim into a hypothesis. The hypothesis can then be examined. Most of the voice's claims do not survive examination — "I'm never going to get out of this" is empirically false in most cases, and asking how true it is opens the body to notice the falseness.

This response is most useful when the voice makes specific, examinable claims (predictions, judgments, attributions). It is less useful when the voice is making vague generalized attacks ("you're terrible") where the examination has nothing concrete to land on.

The inquiry must be genuine. "I wonder if that's true" said as a rhetorical setup for refuting the voice is the voice fighting itself, which does not work. The inquiry has to be open: maybe it is true, maybe it is not, let me look. The body's answer to the actual examination is what does the work.

### Response 4: "Give It the Love and Compassion That You Want" (Hold the Critical Part as a Suffering Being)

A response that turns the voice into the recipient of the love it has been denying the person. The voice has been criticizing the person. This response stops engaging the criticism and offers the voice — as a part of self that has been operating without love — the love it has been refusing.

"Understand it. Like, give it the love and compassion that you want. I'll show you. Close your eyes, let it do its thing, and see what it's like just to give it love and compassion." -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The instruction is operational: close the eyes, let the voice do its thing, and direct love and compassion at it. Not at yourself in general. At the part that is producing the criticism. The compassion is for that specific part, in the moment it is producing the criticism.

The mechanism: the voice that has been criticizing is itself a part of self that has been suffering. It is doing the criticism for a reason — usually because it was installed by a parent who criticized the same way, and the part has been running the original script. When that part receives the love it has been denied, the criticism it produces softens because the part no longer has to demand attention through hostility.

The client in the session reported what the receiving felt like: "I feel a sympathy. I feel an understanding... feel a kind of like an understanding. Yeah, like when you see someone suffering and you're just like, Oh, okay. It's okay, I'll be with you."

The "I'll be with you" is the operational form. The voice is being treated as a being. The body knows how to be with a being that is suffering. That capacity, redirected toward the inner critic, is the response. See [[loving-all-parts]] for the broader principle this response operationalizes.

## What the Menu Has in Common

All four responses share a structural feature: none of them fights the voice. None of them tries to silence the voice. None of them argues with the voice. Each one offers a different relationship to the voice — registering it, holding it as frightened, inquiring into it, loving it.

See [[dont-fight-the-voice]] for the structural principle that explains why fighting does not work. The voice cannot be used to silence the voice. The menu is the alternative to fighting — different relationships, not different attacks.

## Why "A Thousand of Them"

Joe's "there's a thousand of them, experiment, play" is doing operational work. The menu of responses is open, not closed. The four responses above are anchors. Many others are possible:

- "Thank you for trying to protect me, and I'm okay right now."
- "I hear you, and I'm not going to do what you're asking."
- "What are you actually afraid of?"
- "I love you anyway."
- "Tell me more about that, voice." (Genuine curiosity about the voice's content.)
- Naming the voice as a specific person from the past: "That's mom talking" / "That's the coach from 8th grade."
- Singing the voice's content to a silly melody.
- Drawing what the voice looks like.

Each of these does different work for different people in different moments. The instruction to experiment is not flippant; it is recognition that the relationship with the voice is personal and develops through play. What works for one person may not work for another. What works in one moment may not work in another.

The experimentation posture is also the cure for the meta-version of the trap. A person who is told "you must respond to the voice this specific way" has been given a should about how to deal with shoulds. The voice will immediately produce content about how the person is failing to respond correctly. The experimentation posture refuses that loop by treating each response as a try, not as a correct answer.

## When Joe Uses This Menu

- After the [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]] practice has surfaced how often the voice is attacking
- When the person has been silenced by, paralyzed by, or constantly arguing with the voice
- When the person has been doing positive self-talk that is not working (see [[passive-aggression-toward-self]] and [[positivity-as-doubt]] for why)
- When the person has tried to ignore the voice and found that the voice gets louder
- When the order-of-operations correction has been made (see [[do-self-love-before-pursuing-the-thing]]) and the person needs the operational forms for the self-love work
- When the person asks "how do I want to react to that?" — the menu is what makes alternatives available

## The Question That Opens the Menu

The framing question Joe uses to introduce the menu:

"So how do you want to react to that?" -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The question is doing three things:

1. **It positions the response as a choice.** The voice has been treated as something that demands a fixed response (belief). The question makes visible that there are options.
2. **It locates the choice in the person.** Not "you should respond by..." but "how do you want to react." The agency is in the person, not in the coach.
3. **It treats the voice as something to react to.** The voice is not the person. It is something the person is in relationship with. The relationship is the work.

Once the question is asked, the menu becomes the practical answer to it. The four responses are the demonstration that options exist.

## What the Daily Form Looks Like

Joe gives an operational suggestion in this session for how to install the menu into daily life:

> Client: "Is that helpful if I just start [saying ouch every day]?"
> Joe: "Yes."

The simple form: pick one response from the menu (often the ouch, because it is the most immediate) and use it consistently for a period. Once the response is installed, add another from the menu. Build the menu in the body over time.

A more rigorous form (from [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]]): 20 minutes a day, contained, with the response of choice. The contained window allows full attention without converting the practice into a constant project that becomes its own should.

The longer-term form: experiment fluidly across the menu, noticing which responses work in which contexts, building a personal relationship with the voice that is responsive to what the voice is doing in the moment.

## The Frame Underneath the Menu

The frame that makes the menu coherent: the voice changes through how it is met. The voice has been met for years with belief (the person believed what the voice said) and that belief is what allowed the voice to keep running. Each response in the menu is a different way of meeting the voice that is not belief. The voice cannot keep running on responses that are not belief.

"Right now it tells you you're never going to get out of it, and you believe it. So if you believe everything it says, it continues. Why would it not?" -- Joe Hudson, You Don't Have A Money Problem

The politician metaphor: the voice is a politician. As long as the population (the rest of the psyche) votes for the politician (believes the voice), the politician stays in office. The menu is the operational form of withdrawing the vote. Each response is a way of not voting for the voice's content.

## Tone

Playful, experimental. The "there's a thousand of them, experiment, play" is the tone marker. The menu is not a clinical protocol. It is an invitation to discover what works. The playfulness is part of how the voice loses its grip — a voice that has been treated with deadly seriousness for years is met with a slight smile and an experimental posture, and the seriousness it required to be effective begins to dissolve.

The introduction of each response in the menu also carries lightness. "Also good ones are things like..." — Joe is listing options the way a friend lists possibilities, not the way a teacher lists correct answers. The lightness keeps the menu live rather than dogmatic.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not pick one response and treat it as the right answer. The menu is the answer. Different responses for different moments.
- Do not use the menu as a new should ("I should respond with compassion every time"). The shoulding turns the practice into the voice's territory.
- Do not engage the voice's content while trying to use the menu. The menu is for the relationship with the voice, not for arguing with the voice. If you find yourself arguing, return to one of the menu responses.
- Do not skip the experimentation. The menu items are not interchangeable. Each one does different work. Knowing which one works in which moment requires trying them.
- Do not turn this into a meditation discipline. The menu is for in-the-moment responding when the voice fires. The 20-minute contained practice (from [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]]) is the structured form, but the menu is meant to be available in any moment the voice is heard, not only in formal practice.
