# Hold the Memory of When You Felt It Most Completely

## The Practice

When you want to access an emotion that arrived earlier and is now gone, do not try to generate the emotion. Hold the memory in your body and in your mind of the time you felt that emotion most completely. Then see what happens.

"The best way to do that typically is to hold the memory in your body and in your mind of the time that you felt that most completely. And then see where it goes from there." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

## How to Do It

1. **Identify the emotion** you want to make contact with. Name it: anger, grief, fear, longing, shame.
2. **Locate the time you felt it most completely.** A specific memory. Not a general period. A particular moment when the emotion was most fully alive in your body.
3. **Hold the memory in your body and in your mind.** Both layers. The body remembers what posture, what tension, what breath you had then. The mind remembers what was happening, what you were seeing, what you were thinking.
4. **Wait.** Do not push. Do not try to recreate the intensity. Notice what arises.
5. **Let whatever comes be what it is.** It will not be the full intensity of the original. It does not need to be.

The practice is not "remember the situation and try to feel as bad as you did then." That is generation, not access. The practice is "let the body recall its own previous orientation to the emotion, and let whatever shows up from that recall be the material to work with."

## Why This Works

Emotions are stored in the body more than in the cognitive memory of an event. The body that held a particular grief, fear, or anger has a somatic record of the holding. Recalling the situation alone is cognitive. Recalling the body-state alongside the situation invites the body to re-enter the emotional terrain.

The body is not a passive container for emotions. It is the substrate. When the body is invited into the previous configuration, the emotional substrate can become available again. The mind is the entry point but the body is what hosts the actual material.

## Why You Get a Lesser Version (And Why That's the Point)

The practice produces a milder version of the original emotion, not the full triggered intensity. This is a feature, not a limitation.

"You will not get the full complete triggered experience. And that's good because then you get to first work with and be exposed to a lesser version of that experience. And that works just fine." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The full intensity of an in-the-moment trigger is often more than the person can be impartial with. They are flooded. They cannot bring [[wonder]] to it. They cannot apply [[emotional-inquiry]]. They get swept by the emotion rather than meeting it.

The lesser version is workable. There is enough of the emotion present that contact is happening. There is not so much that the contact gets lost in the flood. The practice is in the meeting, not in the intensity. The lesser version is often what produces the actual integration.

## The Distinction From Manufacturing

This practice can look similar to [[manufacturing-the-feeling]] on the outside but operates differently inside.

Manufacturing: the mind constructs a feeling-shaped object to present, generating tension to simulate the emotion. The result is a simulacrum, not the actual emotional substrate.

Holding the memory: the body recalls a previous configuration and invites whatever wants to arise. The result is genuine emotional material at a milder intensity.

The difference is in the direction of effort. Manufacturing pushes feeling into existence. Holding the memory creates the conditions and waits to receive what arrives.

The diagnostic: if the practice feels effortful in the gripping sense, it has slid into manufacturing. If it feels receptive in the inviting sense, it is holding the memory. The body knows the difference. The somatic quality of the practice matters more than its outward form.

## When to Use This

- A powerful emotion arose at an inopportune moment and now is gone
- A specific emotion is calling to be worked with but has not arrived spontaneously
- The person is between trigger events and wants to do work on a known pattern
- An emotion has been repeatedly partially-felt and the person wants to deepen the contact
- The person is in [[emotional-inquiry]] practice and needs an entry point

## When Not to Use This

- A spontaneous emotion is already arriving -- meet that one, don't import a memory of a different one
- The person is using the practice to avoid an emotion that wants attention now
- The person is in the middle of a flooding experience and adding more emotional material would compound the flood
- The person is in deep dissociation -- the memory recall may not produce contact and may produce frustration

The practice has its time. The principle from [[unwelcomed-emotions-return-until-felt]] still operates: if the emotion will come back on its own, you may not need this practice. Use it when you want to deliberately engage with material that you have reason to believe is present.

## What to Do With What Arises

The material that surfaces is not different in kind from the material that arises spontaneously. The same practices apply:

- Meet the emotion with VIEW (see [[emotional-inquiry]])
- Stay in the body (see [[staying-in-your-body]])
- Do not try to make it move (see [[trying-prevents-the-feeling]] -- letting the emotion be is the work)
- Let it inform whatever it informs (decisions, conversations, the next step)

The practice is the access. The meeting is the rest of the work.

## Tone

Light, practical. The practice is offered as one option among many for working with the inner life. It is not central to the methodology. It is a useful technique for a specific situation. The instruction is delivered without ceremony.
