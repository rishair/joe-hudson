# Listen to the Gut Impulse for Direction You Don't Yet Know You Want

## The Practice

The body produces pre-verbal signals about what the person wants before the head has the language to articulate the want. The signal arrives as a sound, a movement, an energy, a pulling — something the body wants to do that does not yet have words. The practice is to notice the signal, stay with it, and let it articulate itself.

The classic example: Tara was working one-on-one with clients and felt a sound moving through her system — "arrr." Not a word. An energy. Her hands wanted to move out. Her body wanted to be bigger. She did not yet know what the signal meant. She stayed with it.

"I used to just work one-on-one with clients and I started working more with groups and I could feel this like, 'Arrr,' wanting to move through my system. It was like, 'Arrr.' And I didn't know I wanted to work with groups yet, but as I listened to this, arrr. And I remember one day just being like, arrr. It was like my hands wanted to move out and I wanted to be bigger. My body wanted to be bigger. And I just started arrr, playing with it. And like, what is that? And I was like, ah, I wanted to hold bigger groups. It actually was through the body and that somatic awareness, that felt sense that it really hit me that there was something bigger, a bigger thing I wanted to hold than working one-on-one." -- Tara Hawley

The signal preceded the conscious knowledge by an unknown duration. The conscious knowledge — "I want to work with groups" — arrived only after the body had been allowed to express the pre-verbal energy.

## What the Gut Knows Before the Head Knows

The body integrates more information than the head can hold in conscious attention. The body has been tracking the work, the people, the energy, the small wins and small drains across thousands of interactions. The integration is happening below the threshold of conscious thought. Eventually the integration produces a signal: this current configuration is too small. Or this current configuration is wrong. Or this direction is right.

The signal arrives in the body first because the body is where the integration was happening. The head receives the signal only when the body has been listened to enough for the signal to be allowed up.

For the person who lives in the head and ignores the body, these signals are produced and then unheard. The person stays in the too-small configuration for years longer than necessary because the wisdom that would have moved them was generated in a body that they were not consulting.

For the person with body access, the signal is heard in the early stages — when it is still a pre-verbal "arrr" — and can be followed before it has to escalate into a crisis to be noticed.

This is the same teaching as [[wants-as-compass]] applied at the somatic level. The wants the head can articulate are the surface of a deeper signal the body has already received. The body is ahead of the head on direction.

## The Practice in Detail

When the body produces a sound, a movement, an energy, or a pulling that is not yet a fully formed thought, the practice is to:

1. **Notice the signal.** Do not dismiss it as random. Body movements, vocalizations, and energy surges that arrive uninvited are often the early form of a want.

2. **Stay with it.** Do not require it to articulate immediately. The signal is in its larval form. It needs time to develop.

3. **Play with it.** Make the sound out loud. Let the body do the movement. Let the energy move through. The exteriorization helps the signal complete its expression.

4. **Ask what it is.** Once the signal has been expressed somatically, the head can be invited to receive it. What does this energy want? What is this movement pointing at? What would satisfy this pull?

5. **Trust the answer.** The answer that arrives this way is often more accurate than the answer the head would have produced through analysis alone. The body has been integrating data the head did not see.

The whole practice takes anywhere from minutes (for small directional signals) to weeks or months (for major life-direction signals like Tara's wanting to work with groups). The duration is not the issue. The willingness to stay with the signal until it articulates itself is the issue.

## What This Is Not

- It is not the same as the gut's emotional reaction in the moment. A wave of fear in the gut is information about the current moment. A persistent pull or energy is information about direction. Both come from the body; they have different functions.
- It is not a guarantee that any body signal is wise. The body, like the mind, can produce signals from conditioning rather than from clean wanting. See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] for the test that distinguishes them: the heart-opens-or-closes diagnostic and the felt-quality of the signal.
- It is not a substitute for considered thought. The body signal points the direction. The head still has to figure out how to follow it responsibly — what changes are required, what conversations are needed, what the timing should be.
- It is not the same as [[emotional-inquiry]]. Emotional inquiry meets an arriving emotion. This practice receives a directional signal. The body access required is similar; the use of what arises is different.

## Why This Practice Matters

The major direction-changes in a life often arrive through the body first. The wanting to leave a job, end a relationship, start a project, move cities, work with different kinds of people — these are usually integrated below conscious thought before they are available to consideration. The head's "I think I should consider whether I want X" is usually trailing a body signal that has been running for months.

The person who can read these signals can make life-direction shifts earlier, with less crisis. The person who cannot read them stays too long in configurations that the body has already outgrown, until the misalignment escalates into illness, depression, or a forced exit.

For the person doing meaningful work — vocation, calling, contribution — this practice is one of the most important instruments in the toolkit. The work the body wants to be doing is usually larger or different from what the head has currently arranged. The body's pull toward the larger configuration is the signal that the next move is available.

## The "Bigger" Signal in Particular

Tara's specific signal — "my body wanted to be bigger" — is worth naming as a category. The body sometimes signals that the current container is too small for what the person is becoming. The signal can show up as:

- Wanting to take more space (literally; the body wants to be larger).
- Wanting to be seen by more people.
- Wanting to hold more, lead more, teach more, contribute more.
- A restlessness in the chest or solar plexus that does not resolve through the usual adjustments.
- Energy that exceeds what the current role can absorb.

This is not the same as ambition or status-seeking. The "bigger" signal is the body reporting that the container it is in is too small for what wants to come through it. Following the signal usually means stepping into more visibility, more responsibility, or a different kind of work. Not following it produces stagnation that gets read as depression.

## What This Looks Like as Coaching

- When a client describes a vague restlessness or a "something is off" without being able to name it, ask what the body is doing. Is there a movement that wants to happen? A sound? A pulling toward something?
- When a client is at a decision point and the head is producing competing analyses, drop the question into the body: what does the body want? Stay with the body's answer even if the head cannot justify it yet.
- When a client reports they have been doing the same thing for a long time and something feels wrong, look for the pre-verbal signal that has been getting suppressed. The "I keep wanting to..." that they never finish.
- When a client is contemplating a major change but cannot commit, the body often already knows. Help them access the body's vote.

The coach's job is to make space for the pre-verbal signal to be heard. The signal is usually there. The barrier is that the head has been treating it as noise.

## Tone

Tara delivers this with the lightness of someone who lived the example. The "arrr" is delivered without self-importance — it sounds funny when said out loud, which is part of why most people would dismiss it as nothing. The teaching is that the funny-sounding signal was, in fact, her next major chapter. The body is allowed to be silly. The signals do not arrive in dignified form.
