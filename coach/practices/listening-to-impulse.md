# Listening to Impulse

## Definition

The alternative to management. Instead of planning what to say, do, or produce, wait for the impulse and follow it. The impulse is a somatic experience: a felt sense of what wants to happen next, available in the body when the mind is not overriding it with plans.

"Right now, if you listen for the impulse as to what to say next, that's a very particular somatic experience." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy over Manage

## The Practice

In conversation: instead of planning the next sentence, listen. Wait for the impulse to speak. Determination and intention can coexist with this. You can want resolution and still wait for the impulse. But wanting your sentence to *do something* to the other person is management.

In creative work: instead of specifying the exact output, give direction (three adjectives, an intention, a clear objective) and wait until the result feels right. When directing actors, demanding a specific performance produces stiff results. Recognizing "that's it" when it happens produces excellence.

In business: instead of pushing people toward a result, present the case and ask "what speaks against it?" Follow the flow of information. The objections tell you what needs addressing. The resistance tells you where the better solution lives.

In life: say yes to what shows up. If it is enjoyable, keep saying yes. If it is not, say no. See [[creative-career-story]].

## What Blocks It

Planning the future from fear rather than from openness. When the mind is having conversations to control the future (spin), the impulse is inaudible. The belief that you must be *prepared* rather than *responsive* keeps the person in the future rather than the present.

"We're literally having conversations in our head to control the future. Have you noticed that the conversations that you've had about how you want the future to go, they have never worked out specifically as you planned?" -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy over Manage

When someone adds the extra step of predicting how their words will be received, they build all their projections into the conversation. The impulse is lost under layers of calculation.

The diagnostic for when impulse-following has tipped into managing the conversation:

"When I want your next sentence to be something, or I want my sentence to do something to you, to get to a particular place, then I'm in management." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

Determination and intention can coexist with impulse-following. Wanting resolution in the conversation is fine. Wanting the other person's next sentence to be a specific thing is not. The first is intention. The second is management. The body knows the difference.

## What It Produces

- Better conversations (the other person feels heard, connection deepens)
- Better creative output (the result is alive rather than controlled)
- Better business decisions (objections surface before they become failures)
- More enjoyment (the process feels like flow rather than effort)

## Impulse and Want Are the Same Thing

What this practice calls "impulse" and what [[the-want]] calls "want as impulse" are the same somatic phenomenon. The impulse moves a person to act before any plan or should can form. When it meets friction, it registers as wanting (an emotion). When it does not meet friction, it just becomes action.

This is also why [[impulse-to-should]] is the failure mode of this practice. The impulse arises, and instead of being followed, it is intercepted and converted into a should. The should kills the impulse. The practice of listening to impulse is partly the practice of catching the should before it intercepts.
