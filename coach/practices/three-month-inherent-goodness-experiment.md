# Three-Month Inherent Goodness Experiment

## The Practice

For three months, consistently tell yourself that you are inherently good. When you find yourself in the "I'm bad" frame, redirect to "I'm inherently good." Watch what happens.

The experiment rests on a simple observation: we tend to become what we tell ourselves we are. A child told repeatedly that they are naughty starts acting naughty. A person who repeatedly tells themselves they are bad keeps producing evidence of badness. The experiment tests whether the reverse also holds.

"For the next, say, 3 months consistently say that you're inherently good. Instead of saying I'm bad or thinking to yourself you're bad, think, oh, I'm inherently good, and see what happens. Because a lot of the times we are the things that we tell ourselves that we are." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## How to Run It

The instructions are minimal.

1. When you notice "I'm bad" thinking arising (the inner critic, the shame, the felt-sense of broken-ness), notice it.
2. Redirect to "I'm inherently good." Not as a should. As a noticing-correction.
3. Do this for three months.
4. Watch what happens to your behavior, your feelings, your patterns.

The three-month duration matters. Shorter periods do not give the nervous system enough time to absorb the new framing. The change is gradual and cumulative.

## Why It Works

Two mechanisms.

**Self-fulfilling prophecy.** Behavior tends to follow self-concept. A person who believes they are inherently good behaves like a person who is inherently good. A person who believes they are bad behaves like a person who is bad. The belief shapes the behavior, which shapes the evidence, which confirms the belief.

"If you can't tell a kid he's naughty all the time, they're going to act naughty. That's how it works. If you tell yourself you're good all the time, then there's a good chance — go test it, don't take my word for it — that all of a sudden you start acting in a way that's good, or at least seeing your actions as good." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

**Reframing the existing evidence.** Many "I'm bad" judgments come from interpreting neutral or even positive actions as evidence of badness. Telling yourself you are good reorients the interpretive frame. The same actions get re-read.

"Sometimes our idea of good is just some more morality or something like that. That's not actually the feeling of goodness." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

This is the second move: not just the affirmation but the felt question of what goodness even means. The "good" you are affirming is not moralistic compliance. It is closer to the felt sense of being aligned with what you are.

## The Experimental Frame

The practice is framed explicitly as an experiment. Joe is careful not to assert it as truth ("you are inherently good and you should believe this"). He invites testing: tell yourself this for three months, and see what your own data shows.

"Go test it, don't take my word for it." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

This matters for two reasons. First, the practice cannot work as a should. The moment "I should believe I'm good" enters, the inherent-goodness frame collapses back into the structure of obligation that produced "I'm bad" in the first place. Second, the experimental framing invites the person to be the authority on their own results, which is itself part of the goodness orientation.

## What to Watch For

Over the three months, watch for:

- **Behavior changes.** Are you doing things you would not have done before? Are you treating yourself differently?
- **Interpretation shifts.** Are you reading the same actions differently? Things that you would have called "bad" now appearing as something else?
- **Body changes.** Is there less constriction around the shame areas? Does the body soften?
- **Relationship changes.** How are others responding to the version of you that believes you are good?
- **What resists the practice.** When does the inherent-goodness frame feel wrong, unsafe, or impossible? Those moments point at the specific shame-pockets that need attention.

The last one is often the most useful. The experiment is not about installing a positive belief through sheer repetition. It is about discovering where the inherent-goodness frame cannot land, because those are the places where the foundational shame is still operating.

## Connection to the Three Keepers

This practice is the intervention for the "I'm bad" keeper from [[three-keepers-of-stuck-beliefs]]. When limiting-belief work is not landing because the underlying assumption is "I'm broken," addressing that assumption directly through the experiment can unlock the rest of the work.

It also pairs with the related coaching question [[if-i-were-inherently-good]] (the inherent-goodness reframe): if you were inherently good, how would you interpret this want, this thought, this behavior? The practice is the daily form; the question is the moment-to-moment form.

## Common Failure Modes

**Doing it as a should.** "I should believe I'm good" produces the opposite. The practice has to be invitational, curious, lighthearted. The instruction is: notice "I'm bad," redirect to "I'm inherently good," and continue. Not: "I must successfully believe I'm good."

**Treating it as a one-time fix.** The three-month duration is not arbitrary. Years of "I'm bad" do not undo in a week. The practice is daily, accumulative.

**Confusing goodness with moral perfection.** The inherent goodness being affirmed is not "I am a moral exemplar." It is closer to "I am a fundamentally okay being, like an oak tree is a fundamentally okay tree." The oak does not have to earn it. Neither do you. See [[inherent-goodness]].

**Skipping when the badness feels real.** The practice is most useful precisely when the badness feeling is strongest. The redirect is the move.

## What the Experiment Often Reveals

People who run this experiment seriously often discover that "I'm bad" was holding a lot more in place than they realized. Behaviors they thought were independent (the procrastination, the eating habit, the way they speak to themselves at the end of the day) all shift when the underlying frame shifts. The experiment surfaces the architecture of limiting beliefs that rested on "I'm bad."

The experiment also reveals that the badness feeling was largely a habit, not a description. Once practiced enough times in the redirect, the inherent-goodness frame becomes accessible as a stance. The shame still arises sometimes; the relationship to it changes.

## Tone

Joe presents this lightly and as an experiment, not a prescription. The lightness is functional. The practice cannot work if it has the weight of a moral assignment.

"Test the belief, see if you're bad, or if it's just that you've been telling yourself you're bad so long that you are acting like it." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs
