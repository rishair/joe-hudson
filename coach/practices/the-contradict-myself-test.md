# The Contradict-Myself Test

## The Practice

When you suspect that the fight you are in (or the fight that is starting) is displaced discharge — not about the content but about the other person needing somewhere to put their charge — test the read by reversing your position. Agree with what they said. Or contradict yourself. Or change your stance to the opposite of what you had just said. Then watch what happens.

If they contradict your reversed position the same way they contradicted the original — if they object regardless of what you say — the fight is not about content. The discharge is the function. The reach is for friction, not for resolution. See [[everything-i-say-they-contradict]] for the read this test confirms.

"I even tested it. I was like, 'Oh, I'm gonna contradict myself and I'm gonna agree with her.' And I did, I agreed with her. And then she was like, 'No, why would you ever do that?'" -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The test is small, fast, and diagnostic. It does not require explanation. It does not require the other person to know what is happening. It is a quiet inner experiment that produces a clear result.

## When to Use the Practice

The test is useful in two situations.

**Situation 1: You suspect the read.** You are in a conversation where every statement seems to be landing as wrong, regardless of what you say. The pattern feels content-independent. You want to confirm that the dynamic is displaced discharge before responding to it. The test is the confirmation.

**Situation 2: The partner has named a complaint that does not seem to match the size of the reaction.** You wonder if the named complaint is the actual material or if it is the surface story the discharge is attaching to. The test can disambiguate. Agree with the named complaint and see if the upset settles or finds new content.

The test is not useful when the fight is clearly about content and the named complaint is proportional to the reaction. In those cases the fight is the [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] kind, not the displaced-discharge kind, and a different set of moves applies.

## How to Run the Test

The mechanics are simple.

1. **Take the position they would expect you to defend.** Say it cleanly. Do not signal that you are testing anything.
2. **Wait for their response.** Notice whether they engage with the reversed position substantively, or whether they find the new contradiction to object to.
3. **If they engage substantively:** the fight is about content. The reversed position has opened a real conversation. Continue from there with the real material.
4. **If they object to the reversed position the same way they objected to the original:** the read is confirmed. The fight is displaced discharge. Move to the next set of options.

The "next set of options" depends on the relationship and the moment. Possibilities include the move [[go-to-separate-rooms-and-yell]], the internal question [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]], or simply choosing not to escalate while the dynamic plays out and noting it for a later conversation.

## What the Test Tells You

A positive result (they contradicted the reversed position too) gives you four pieces of information:

1. The fight is not about the named content.
2. The other person is carrying held charge from somewhere else.
3. Whatever you do or say in this conversation will be heard through that charge, not on its merits.
4. The most effective move is not to keep arguing the content. It is to address the underlying state.

A negative result (they engaged substantively with the reversed position) gives you one piece of information:

1. The fight is about content. The named complaint is real. The conversation should continue on the actual material.

Either result is useful. The test is diagnostic in both directions.

## Why the Test Works

The test exploits the structural difference between content-driven fights and discharge-driven fights. Content fights require coherent content; if you offer different content, the fight reorganizes around the new content. Discharge fights require friction; if you offer agreement, the discharge looks for a new target.

The signature of a discharge fight is that it cannot accept agreement, because agreement does not deliver the friction the discharge is reaching for. The agreement is itself something to object to. Joe's example: he agreed with his wife, and her response was "No, why would you ever do that?" The agreement became the new target. The reach for friction did not pause when the friction-source was removed; it kept reaching.

This is a falsifiable test. If the fight could survive substantive agreement, then the agreement-and-continued-engagement would happen. When the discharge dynamic is running, that does not happen. The contradiction follows wherever you go.

## What Not to Do With the Result

**Do not use the test as a weapon.** "I see what you're doing — I agreed with you and you contradicted me, so this isn't even about anything." Said in the moment, this converts the diagnostic into an attack and creates the very fight it was supposed to help diagnose. The test is for the tester, not for the diagnosed.

**Do not deploy the test on every fight.** Some fights are about content. Running this test on a content fight reads as deflection and damages the conversation. The test is for the specific signature where you have a reasonable hypothesis that the fight is displaced — not as a universal screen.

**Do not perform the test cynically.** If you agree with them in a way that is obviously fake — eye-roll, tone of mockery, the I-don't-mean-this-but-let's-see-what-happens energy — the test does not produce a clean result, because the cynicism itself becomes the new target. The agreement has to be offered honestly enough to be testable.

**Do not use a positive result as evidence that you are blameless.** A positive result means the fight is displaced. It does not mean you have no work to do, no contribution to the dynamic, no patterns of your own that are also operating. The result tells you about the structure of this fight; it does not absolve you from your part in the relationship.

## What Comes After a Positive Result

Once the test confirms the read, the available moves are:

- **Internal.** Recognize you are in the discharge-target role and decide how to be in it. You can hold the container with awareness, knowing the contradicting is not about you. You can step out of the role and announce that you are taking some time. You can ask the underlying question [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]] for yourself, because being the discharge target is itself loading something into your own body that you will need to process.

- **Relational.** Name the dynamic if the relationship can hold it. "I think you might be looking for a fight tonight. Did something happen today?" This depends entirely on whether the relationship has a foundation for this kind of naming. Many do not.

- **Move-level.** Propose the separate-rooms move. "Hey, let's both go yell in different rooms and then come back." See [[go-to-separate-rooms-and-yell]]. This is the cleanest move when the relationship has been introduced to the framework.

## What Comes After a Negative Result

The fight is about content. Stay in the conversation with the actual material. The reversed position has opened a real exchange. The work is in the content of the disagreement, not in any diagnostic move.

## Tone

The test is run quietly. There is no announcement. The agreement or contradiction is offered as if it were the position you were about to take anyway. The mind doing the testing is curious, not adversarial. The energy is something like "let me check what is actually happening here," not "let me catch you in something."

After a positive result, the tone toward the other person remains neutral. They are not being bad by needing the discharge; their body is doing what bodies do. The test result is information for you, not ammunition.
