# Notice You Are Already Connected, Then Be Grateful

## The Practice

When you have lost the felt sense of connection, the practice has two steps and only two.

1. **Notice.** Ask whether connection is here right now. The noticing is the move. As soon as you wonder whether you can come back to presence, presence has already arrived.
2. **Be grateful.** Receive the connection that was already there. Gratitude deepens it without effort.

That is the whole practice. No retrieval. No reconstruction. No reach for the lost state. Just notice, then thank.

"Then the best thing is gratitude, is just to be thankful for it. That's the easiest thing, is to be -- even right now, if you feel into this moment, you can feel your connection. It's always there and you can be grateful for it. Easy peasy lemon squeezy." -- Joe Hudson, Connection: A State Beyond States

## Why This Works

The practice rests on a structural feature of connection: it is always available. There has never been a moment in your life when you were not connected -- to your breath, to the air, to the cascade of influences that produced your current thought. The disconnection story is a story. The connection is the underlying reality.

If connection is always available, the work is not to retrieve it but to recognize it. Recognition is fast. The mind that says "I am disconnected" is the mind that is already conscious enough to wonder. Wondering is a form of presence. Presence is connection.

"On some level, it's not until you think about that, I want to be back in connection -- not until connection presents itself that you remember that you're even able to. So if you're completely non-present, there's no way. But as soon as you think, oh, I could be present again, then presence has already occurred. Then connection has already occurred. As soon as you think, 'Oh, I wanna be in connection.'" -- Joe Hudson

## The "Am I Conscious?" Analogy

Brett offers a parallel from a study. University students were assigned to ask themselves "am I conscious?" at intervals throughout the day. The study found that the practice itself increased the felt sense of being conscious, of being aware, of being less on autopilot.

The structure is the same. The noticing produces the thing being noticed about. The question "am I in connection?" produces a returning to connection. The question does not search for an absent state. It surfaces a present one.

This is why the practice does not require force. The wondering is already the work.

## Even the Desire for Connection Is Connection

The deepest move in the practice: when you notice you want connection but feel disconnected, the wanting itself is connection. The wanting is not a sign that connection is missing. It is a thread back to the experience that the surface mind has forgotten.

"Even the desire for connection is a form of connection." -- Joe Hudson

This dissolves the trap of trying to achieve connection. The achieving moves the person further away. The noticing of the wanting is the move back.

## Why Trying Pushes It Away

There are many things a person can do that point toward connection: meditate, get in the body, exercise, eat well, pray, do community service, do metta. All of them can help. None of them are required.

"On some level, you also need to see that it's always there and always available. 'Cause the effort takes you away from it." -- Joe Hudson

The instruction does not collapse into "do nothing." The practices remain useful. The instruction is that the strain to reach connection -- the felt sense of pulling toward a state that is somewhere else -- itself produces disconnection. The body cannot strain toward what it is already in.

See [[no-finish-line]] for the parallel structure with happiness and the arrival fantasy. The mechanism is the same: future-locating an experience that exists only in the present produces the experience of missing it.

## How It Differs from Forced Positivity

The practice is not "tell yourself you are connected when you do not feel it." That move is denial wearing the costume of gratitude. The practice is more honest: actually feel into this moment, find the contact that is here (even if it is contact with disconnection), and let the gratitude meet that.

If the felt sense is "I am not in connection," meeting that with honesty is itself connection. The noticing-of-disconnection, met without trying to fix it, is the connection. The gratitude can be for that exact noticing.

See [[connection]] on being in connection with the state of being out of connection.

## The Sequence After a Drop

The practice is most useful after a drop. Someone leaves you, you get triggered, you fall into rage, you do something self-destructive, you spiral. From the bottom of the spiral, the question is how to come back.

"How do you drop a hot frying pan?" -- Joe Hudson's framing. You do not need a method. You drop it. The connection is the frying pan: you do not need to figure out how to come back. You come back by noticing you can.

The instruction for the bottom: do not try to retrieve the connection. Notice that it is here. Receive it with gratitude. That is enough.

## When Joe Uses This

Joe uses this practice in two contexts.

1. **When someone asks how to get back to connection after losing it.** The temptation is to give a method. The actual answer is that the asking is already the return.
2. **When someone is treating connection as a goal to achieve.** The practice short-circuits the achievement frame by surfacing that the goal is already met.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The surprise of noticing connection is here when they were sure it was gone.
- A felt drop in the urgency that previously surrounded "getting back to connection."
- Gratitude arising spontaneously rather than as a performed feeling.
- The recognition that they have been working hard at something that did not require work.
- A daily ease in returning to the connected state because the trip is short.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone reports having lost connection and is asking what to do to get back.
- When someone is sustaining effort to maintain a state and the effort is producing the opposite.
- When [[connection]] has been introduced and the person is asking for a daily practice.
- When someone is in the spiral after a rupture and is bottoming out.
- When someone has been doing many practices and is exhausted from the doing.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

## Tone

The instruction is delivered lightly. "Easy peasy lemon squeezy." The lightness is not flippant -- it is structural. If the practice is heavy, the heaviness is itself a sign that the person is still trying to reach connection rather than noticing it.
