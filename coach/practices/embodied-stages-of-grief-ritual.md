# Embodied Stages of Grief Ritual

## Definition

A practice for moving stuck grief: take the list of emotional states associated with grief, and embody each one in sequence -- a minute or so per state -- using your body to find the posture that holds that emotion. The practice extends the six-positions emotional-fluidity exercise (Joe's foundational practice where the practitioner embodies six emotional states by finding the body posture that holds each) into the specific domain of grief.

The version Joe and Brett's partner Alexa developed during early COVID lockdown: sadness, fear, anger, shock, denial, acceptance, gratitude. About a minute per state. All three people doing the practice together had significant emotional movement.

## How It Works

The practice rests on the same mechanism as the six positions: your body knows the shape of each emotion. If you put your body into the shape, the emotional state tends to arrive. The intellect cannot manufacture an emotion, but the body can. The shape comes first, the feeling follows.

For grief specifically, this matters because grief contains a menu of emotions that often get blocked (see [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]]). The ritual systematically invites each one. Whichever one was the bottleneck gets touched. The whole grief cycle moves more freely once the stuck emotion has been allowed.

"Brett led us through something that he learned from you, the Six Positions. And then I just had this idea to take it on into the seven stages of grief, embodying each one of them. For about a minute each, per stage of grief, we embodied one of these, and all three of us had these really big emotional movements." -- The interviewee (Alexa), The Beauty of Grief

Joe's response: "That's awesome. That exercise is one of many exercises that are about helping us with emotional fluidity."

## Instructions

1. Set aside 10-15 minutes in a private space.
2. Have a list of grief-associated emotional states. Use a standard list (Kübler-Ross extensions: denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) or build your own from what is actually present in this grief. Joe and Alexa's version: sadness, fear, anger, shock, denial, acceptance, gratitude.
3. For each emotion, find a body posture that holds that emotion. Let the body decide. Knees curled, head down, arms thrown wide, fists clenched -- whatever comes.
4. Stay in that posture for about a minute. Let the emotion arrive. Do not perform it; let the body move you into it.
5. Move to the next emotion. Find the next posture. Stay for another minute.
6. Move through all the emotions. Do not skip the ones that feel uncomfortable.
7. After completing the cycle, sit with whatever is present.

## Why the Order Does Not Matter Much

The order of the emotions in this practice is less important than people often think. The stages of grief were never meant to be linear (see [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]] for the longer treatment). Grief moves in a non-linear pattern that cycles through the same emotions multiple times. The practice is not training a particular sequence; it is opening a particular range.

What matters: all the emotions get touched. Whichever one you have been skipping is the one most worth lingering on.

## Why the Body, Not the Mind

A common mistake when working with grief is to try to think your way through the emotions. "I should be feeling anger right now -- am I feeling angry? What would I be angry about?" This is the mind trying to do the body's job. It does not work.

The body knows the shape. If you give the body permission to take the shape, the emotion follows. The intellect cannot manufacture an emotion at will, but the body can prime the emotion by getting into its physical configuration. See [[exercises-need-body-voice-emotion]] for the broader principle of why effective exercises engage the body and not just the mind.

This is the same mechanism as [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] -- using the body's shape to prime an emotion that the mind cannot summon directly.

## The Wider Pattern: Emotional Fluidity Practices

This practice is an instance of a larger family. The general design: pick an emotional domain (grief, anger, fear, joy), list the states within it, and cycle through embodying each one for a short time. The cycling stretches your emotional range the way physical stretching stretches your muscular range.

"That exercise is one of many exercises that are about helping us with emotional fluidity, which is, if you're thinking about the work of the heart instead of the head or the gut, there's kind of two main components of it. One is to learn how to be undefended in your love, unconditional in your love. And the other part is emotional fluidity, allowing all the emotions and letting them move through you." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

Joe's response to hearing about the grief version of the practice was immediately to think about how to extend the design pattern to other emotional arcs:

"I'm immediately thinking about how you could use that same principle. Okay, what are the emotional arcs that people take under different circumstances and how do you put them in some sort of order so that allows for the full movement." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The design is generalizable. The grief version is one application of a broader practice format.

## When to Use This

When grief has been moving on one emotion (often sadness) and other emotions feel locked.

When a specific grief has been going on for a while and feels stuck.

When a person knows they are grieving something but cannot get any of the emotions to actually move.

When working alone and there is no one to guide the grief externally.

In a group, where the collective embodiment can support each person more deeply than working alone.

## When Not to Use This

When acute grief is already moving fluidly. The practice is for stuck grief, not for fresh grief that is doing its job.

When the person has not yet developed enough body trust to let their body take postures spontaneously. In that case, start with [[let-your-body-shake-it-out]] or other simpler somatic practices first.

When the practice is being approached as a way to "get the grief over with." See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]]. The practice is for cycling through what is present, not for finishing the grief.

## Tone

The practice is approached with the same orientation as any emotional fluidity work: warm, curious, willing to be moved. It is not a performance. The body postures should feel ridiculous if they feel anything at all; that is fine. The ridiculousness is part of why the practice works -- it bypasses the part of the mind that wants emotions to look dignified.
