# Find What You Absolutely Believe

## The Practice

When you have located an area of tension or stuckness, the next step is figuring out which specific belief is producing it. The trick: it is usually the one you absolutely believe to be true. The unexamined thing. The "obvious" thing. The thing you would not bother questioning because it is just how things are.

"It's usually the one that they absolutely believe. So if you want a trick to that, like in a situation that's creating tension, go to the things that you absolutely believe to be true. Those are probably your culprits." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## Why Certainty Is the Tell

The mind questions things it is unsure about. The mind does not question things it is sure about, by definition. The unquestioned beliefs therefore accumulate authority over a lifetime. They become the rails the rest of thought runs on.

These are also the beliefs that do the most damage, because nothing in the system pushes back on them. A belief that the person is occasionally suspicious of is partially defanged by the suspicion. A belief that the person treats as obvious has full operating power.

"They're the water that you swim in. So look for the context that you're not questioning." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

The water-you-swim-in framing parallels [[cult-of-one]]: you cannot see the water you are swimming in. The unexamined assumptions are not even visible as assumptions; they are visible as reality. The first move of the practice is to make the invisible assumption visible as an assumption.

## How to Do It

1. **Identify the area of tension.** A specific situation, relationship, decision, or pattern where something is stuck or constricted. See [[limiting-belief-detection-signals]].

2. **List what you take to be obviously true about it.** Not what you think you should believe. What you actually believe with no internal doubt. The things you would never bring up in a coaching session because there is nothing to bring up -- they are just true.

3. **Pick the most absolute one.** The one with the least felt wiggle room. The one you almost did not write down because of course it is true.

4. **Treat that one as the culprit.** Even if it does not look like a limiting belief, the absoluteness is suspicious. Start the inquiry there. See [[inquiry-on-limiting-beliefs]].

## What Often Surfaces

Common things people "absolutely believe" that turn out to be limiting beliefs:

- "I have to provide for my family" (definition of providing unexamined)
- "I'm not good at relationships" (sample size, definition of good)
- "Money is hard to make" (depends entirely on context)
- "I have to keep my word" (about what, to whom, at what cost)
- "I should grow" (according to whose timeline)
- "I'm responsible for their feelings" (which is impossible)
- "I have to be productive" (productive at what, by what measure)

None of these are necessarily false. Each is sufficiently uncertain that holding it as absolute is its own problem. The absoluteness is what makes it limiting, not the content.

## Why This Works When General Inquiry Does Not

A person can sit down with the question "what are my limiting beliefs?" and produce nothing useful. The known beliefs have already been worked. The accessible-but-uncomfortable beliefs have already been chewed on.

What is unworked is what cannot be seen. The absoluteness diagnostic finds the unseen, not by asking the person to imagine what they might be missing, but by asking them what they are not even bothering to question. The unquestioned thing is the unseen thing.

## Pairing With Felt Sense

The practice is most precise when combined with the felt sense of where the tension lives. Notice the constriction in the body. Stay with the situation that produces it. Now ask: what am I taking to be obviously true here?

The constriction tells you where the belief is. The absoluteness diagnostic tells you which belief is doing the work. The pair locates both the address and the specific occupant.

## What Comes After

Once the absolutely-believed thing is identified, the next phase is the actual dismantling. The mind has to stop believing it. The emotions have to stop believing it. The body has to stop believing it. See [[mind-unsees-first-body-last]] and [[inquiry-on-limiting-beliefs]].

The identification phase is not the end of the work. It is the prerequisite. Most failed limiting-belief work fails because the wrong belief was being worked. The absoluteness diagnostic is meant to fix that.

## Tone

The practice is quietly investigative. Not "what am I doing wrong?" but "what am I not even noticing that I am holding?" The discovery is often surprising. The thing that gets caught is rarely the thing the person came in expecting to find.
