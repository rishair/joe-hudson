# Talk to Your Parts and Ask for Trust

## The Practice

Speak directly, out loud or internally, to the part of you that is producing a response you don't like -- the fear, the anger, the body that won't relax, the mind that won't stop. Three moves in sequence:

1. **Name what you don't like.** Not at the part. About the situation. "I want to name that I'm unhappy with what's going on here."
2. **Reassert love regardless.** "But I love you."
3. **Ask for trust, with an exit if trust is not available.** "Can you trust me? Can you trust me to take care of this? This is what I'm going to do. Will you trust me to take care of it? If you can't trust me, that's good. We'll work it out. I still love you. But can you give me a chance?"

The shape is a real conversation, not a positive affirmation. The part has its own intelligence and its own concerns. The practice treats it that way.

## Émile DeWeaver's Description

"I have conversations with my body, my heart, my mind, my fears all the time. And it's like, look, man, I want to name that, like, you know, I'm unhappy with what's going on here, but I love you. And like, can you trust me? Can you trust me to take care of this? This is what I'm gonna do. Will you trust me to take care of it? If you can't trust me, that's good. We'll, we'll work it out. I still love you, right? But can you give me a chance? You'd be surprised, though. Like, you know, people might think like, man, that sounds pretty crazy, but you'll be surprised, right? Giving yourself the time of day, giving your feelings, giving your body the time of day to acknowledge and say that you matter, how much space that creates for you to move through them." -- Émile DeWeaver, Life After Murder

The practice came out of years of post-prison somatic therapy. It is the natural extension of [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] into actual dialogue.

## What Makes It Work

Three components have to be present together. Missing any one of them collapses the practice into something less effective:

1. **The acknowledgment of what's actually happening.** Not pretending the part's strategy is fine. "I'm unhappy with what's going on here." The honesty is part of the love.

2. **The unconditional reassertion of love.** Not contingent on the part's cooperation. The love is not what you give the part if it does what you want. The love is the ground the conversation happens on.

3. **The real ask for trust, with the part's right to say no.** "If you can't trust me, that's good. We'll work it out." The exit is not a manipulation. It is the truth of the relationship: the part does not have to comply, and the love does not depend on its compliance.

The practice fails when any of these is missing. Acknowledgment without love produces shame. Love without honest acknowledgment produces enmeshment. The ask for trust without the exit produces coercion dressed in soft language.

## Why It Works

Parts that have been doing protective work for years respond to being seen, named, and respected. They have been operating in default mode -- firing whenever a pattern-match registers, regardless of whether the current situation actually calls for the response. The conversation gives the part new information: there is an adult here now who is paying attention, who knows the part is doing something, who wants to update the arrangement.

See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] for the principle this practice expresses. See [[voice-as-care]] for the related concept about the inner critic. See [[loving-all-parts]] for the broader orientation.

The result Émile names: "How much space that creates for you to move through them." The parts that were standing in the way of the emotion start moving with it instead. The body that wouldn't relax relaxes because it has been heard. The fear that wouldn't quiet quiets because it has been treated as a partner rather than as an obstacle.

## The Range of Parts You Can Talk To

Émile names four: body, heart, mind, fears. The practice generalizes to any part that has its own activity:

- The body that is rigid, fatigued, or in pain
- The heart that is closed, defended, or hurting
- The mind that is racing, rehearsing, or judging
- A specific fear that won't quiet
- The anger that is sitting in the chest
- The voice in the head that won't stop critiquing
- The part that wants to caretake
- The part that wants to run

Each part can be addressed as a "you." The grammar matters. Not "I am scared." But "you are scared, and I am here with you, and I love you, and can you give me a chance?"

## A Specific Form for Fear

Because fear is one of Émile's named parts, the practice includes a fear-specific application:

"Oh, I feel nervous about that. Okay, what's going on with that? What's beneath that? What's happening for you? What is it that's crying out to be healed in this moment? What is it that's crying out to be taken care of in this moment?" -- Émile DeWeaver, Life After Murder

The questions are part of the conversation. The fear is asked what it is doing and what it needs. The asking presumes the fear has answers, which it usually does. See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]] for the related concept.

## What People Think Is Crazy About It

The practice sounds odd to people who have not done it. Talking to your body. Asking your fear to trust you. Saying "I love you" to the part of yourself that is making your life harder. It can read as self-help theater.

Émile names this directly: "People might think like, man, that sounds pretty crazy, but you'll be surprised, right?"

The defense against the practice usually evaporates the moment the person tries it. The surprise is not that the practice works. The surprise is how much it works. The part responds to being treated as a part rather than as a malfunction.

## How to Run It

Find a moment when a part is active and unwanted -- when the body is buzzing, the fear is loud, the inner critic is going, the chest is tight. The practice does not require a special setting.

1. Notice the part. Where is it in the body? What is it doing? Name it specifically.

2. Address it directly. Speak to it as a "you," in your head or out loud. Some people find out-loud works better, especially at the start.

3. Say what's happening that you don't like. "I want to name that this fear is making it hard for me to do this thing."

4. Say "I love you" anyway. Not as performance. As the actual reset of the relationship.

5. Ask for trust. "Can you trust me to handle this? This is what I'm going to do." Be specific about what you're going to do.

6. Offer the exit. "If you can't trust me, we'll work it out. I still love you." Mean it.

7. Wait. The part may respond -- with a softening, with a new piece of information, with a different feeling. Or it may not. Both are fine. The conversation has happened either way.

The practice can be repeated daily, in moments of need, before high-stakes events, or as part of a regular practice. The relationship with the part deepens with repetition.

## What This Practice Is Not

This is not a way to manipulate parts into stopping their behavior. The love and the ask have to be real. If the part senses the underlying intent is to get it to stop, the practice collapses into [[loving-to-transform]]. The exit ("if you can't trust me, that's good") is the protection against this collapse. If you cannot say the exit and mean it, you are not doing the practice; you are doing manipulation dressed as the practice.

This is also not a substitute for actually addressing what the part is signaling. If the body is rigid because something in your life needs to change, the conversation will likely surface that. The conversation is not a way to convince the body the situation is fine when it isn't.

## When to Use It

- When a part is firing in default mode and you can tell the current situation does not actually call for it
- When you are about to do something the part is scared of, and you want to bring the part with you rather than override it
- When repeated attempts to "make the part stop" have not worked
- When self-attack about the part is the loudest layer
- When a part has been doing protective work for years and the protection is now in the way

## Tone

Sincere and conversational. The practice cannot be done as an exercise. It has to be done as an actual relationship. The part can tell the difference. The tone is the tone of speaking to someone you love who has been doing hard work on your behalf and whose default strategy needs to update.
