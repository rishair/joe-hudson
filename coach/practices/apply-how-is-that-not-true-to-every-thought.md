# Apply "How Is That Not True?" to Every Thought

## The Practice

When a thought arises, ask the question: "How is that not true?" Apply the question to whatever the mind produces — observations, judgments, predictions, beliefs about self, beliefs about others, even small narrative captions on present experience.

The question is not asked to dismiss the thought. It is asked to find the place where the thought breaks down on inspection. Every thought has a small untruth in it. The practice is to find the untruth and let the thought lose its grip when the untruth is seen.

"Intellectually, when you're here, intellectually, the best job for the mind is to deconstruct itself. Every thought. How is that not true? How is that not true?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The practice is small and infinitely repeatable. Each thought is one application. The practice can run continuously in the background of the day or be deployed when a specific thought is starting to grip.

## Why This Practice

The practice gives the mind a job that supports presence rather than competing with it. The standard alternative — telling the mind to stop thinking — is rarely workable. The mind responds to instructions it can carry out. Deconstructing thoughts is something it can carry out.

The practice is also a daily-life version of [[mind-deconstructs-itself]]. The principle says the mind's best job is to deconstruct itself; this practice is what that looks like in operation.

For the head-dominant person, the practice is particularly valuable. The strong intellect, redirected to the deconstructive work, keeps doing what it does well — examining, analyzing, finding edges — but the outputs no longer accumulate. The mind that was felt as the obstacle becomes the tool.

## The Instructions

1. Notice a thought arising. Any thought. "I should call my mom." "That meeting was bad." "I'm tired." "He's so annoying." "I need more discipline."
2. Ask: "How is that not true?"
3. Let the answer come. The answer can be a fact ("she actually called me yesterday"), a perspective ("from her side, the meeting was fine"), an alternative reading ("I'm not tired, I'm under-stimulated"), a missing context ("I only think he's annoying because I'm hungry"), or a simple recognition that the thought was overgeneralized.
4. Let the thought lose its grip. Do not replace it with the answer. The answer is not a new thought to carry. The answer is what dissolves the original thought.
5. Return to whatever was happening before the thought arose.
6. Repeat when the next thought arises.

The practice does not require a notebook, a sit, or a specific time. It runs in the gaps between activities, during activities, and especially during activities that the mind has been trying to comment on.

## What the Practice Produces

**Quieter mind over time.** Not because the mind has been silenced but because most of what was making the noise has been dissolved on inspection. The thoughts that survive are more grounded. The thoughts that don't survive simply don't.

**Less reactivity.** A thought that has been deconstructed has less power to drive behavior. The reaction-pattern that follows the thought ("he's annoying" → withdrawal) is interrupted at the thought-stage, before the reaction launches.

**More presence.** The deconstructive work returns attention to the present moment after each application. The thought was a departure; the question and the answer together are the return.

**A different relationship to one's own mind.** The mind is no longer felt as a problem to be solved or an enemy to be subdued. It is felt as a tool that has a clear job. The internal antagonism softens. The person and their mind become collaborators rather than adversaries.

## Common Failure Modes

**Using the practice to dismiss real feelings.** The deconstructive question works on thoughts, not on feelings. If a feeling is present and the thought is the feeling's caption, deconstructing the caption does not address the feeling. The feeling still has to be felt. The practice complements feeling-work; it does not replace it.

**Turning the practice into a self-attack.** If the answer to "how is that not true?" is delivered with judgment ("you stupid, of course that's not true"), the practice has been converted into another instance of inner criticism. The question is asked in good faith; the answer is held with curiosity, not contempt.

**Becoming attached to deconstruction as identity.** "I'm a person who deconstructs my thoughts" is itself a thought to deconstruct. The practice does not produce a new identity-position. It dissolves identity-positions as they arise.

**Skipping the deconstruction on the comfortable thoughts.** The practice applies to all thoughts, not just the painful ones. Comfortable thoughts often have the most unexamined untruth in them. "I'm doing fine" can deserve the question as much as "I'm failing." The practice is universal in scope.

**Stopping at the first answer.** Sometimes a thought has several layers of untruth. The first answer reveals one; staying with the question reveals others. The practice does not have to be quick. It can be patient.

## Recursion

The practice can be applied to its own outputs. "The thought 'I should call my mom' is not true because she called me yesterday" is itself a thought. "How is that not true?" The recursion is not a flaw. It is a feature. Each layer dissolved reveals more clearly what is actually here.

The recursion has a natural endpoint. At some point the thought-chain dissolves into present-moment perception — a sensation, a sound, the feeling of breathing. The practice has done its work when the mind has nothing left to deconstruct because nothing is being added. The person is just here.

## When to Deploy the Practice Most Acutely

- When a thought is starting to grip and produce reactivity.
- When a familiar painful thought-loop is restarting.
- When the mind is producing content faster than it can be examined and presence is being lost.
- When [[everything-i-touch-fails-drives-fixing]] has surfaced and the fix-thoughts are starting to drive action.
- When the meta-thought arises ("I should be more present") and another instance of the should is loading.
- When the after-the-session thoughts arise about whether the session worked and what to do next.
- When the take-home practice is needed and the somatic recognition has happened but the mind is restarting.

## The Practice in the Coaching Session Itself

When deployed in the coaching session, the practice is what the coach gives the client as a take-home after the somatic work has landed. The session opens the recognition; the practice is what keeps the recognition available between sessions.

In the source session, the practice was the closing instruction. The conceptual work had been done. The being-with had been demonstrated. The principle ("the mind's best job is to deconstruct itself") had been delivered. The practice ("every thought, how is that not true, how is that not true") was the operating form the client could carry into the rest of her life.

The pairing matters. The principle without the practice would have been an interesting idea the mind would carry as content. The practice without the principle would have been a technique without an anchor. Together they gave the client a complete take-home: here is what the mind is for, and here is what to do with it.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not affirmation work. The practice does not produce positive thoughts to replace negative ones. The replacement-thoughts would be more thoughts to deconstruct.
- It is not skepticism as a stance. The practice does not claim that thoughts are always wrong. It examines each thought for the specific untruth it carries. Sometimes the answer is "actually that one holds up."
- It is not a cognitive-behavioral technique for managing distorted thinking. The practice is broader; it applies to all thinking, not just distorted thinking. The aim is presence, not corrected cognition.
- It is not a way to avoid feeling. If the thought is the cover for an unfelt emotion, the deconstruction reveals the emotion. The next move is to feel it. The practice does not bypass feeling; it surfaces it.

## Tone for the Coach Teaching the Practice

Casual, plain, almost off-handed. The practice is presented as a small tool, not a major teaching. "Every thought. How is that not true? How is that not true." The casualness is part of what makes the practice usable. A gravely-delivered practice becomes another thing to perform; a casually-delivered practice becomes something the person can actually do without ceremony.
