# Trace the 'I Want to Coach Like That' Impulse to Its Underneath

## The Practice

When the impulse arises -- watching a coaching session, hearing about the work, encountering a facilitator -- and the felt sense is "I want to be able to do that," the practice is to trace the impulse before acting on it. Do not immediately move toward becoming a coach. Stay with the wanting and ask what is underneath it. What specifically do you want? When you strip away "be a coach" as the surface answer, what is the want pointing at?

The impulse is information. It tells you something about what you want. The mistake is to take the surface form of the impulse ("I want to be a coach") as if it were the want itself. The actual want is usually underneath.

## When To Use It

- Watching a coaching session and feeling pulled to learn how to do it
- Encountering this work for the first time and immediately wanting to train as a facilitator
- Reading about the lineage and starting to fantasize about being part of it as a teacher
- Noticing yourself imagining being in the chair where the coach is sitting
- Any time the impulse "I want to do that" arises in response to seeing someone work
- Recognizing in yourself one of the three dangerous primary motivations (savior, surrogate intimacy, wisdom-holder) and wanting to investigate further

## How To Do It

1. **Notice the impulse without judging it.** The impulse is data. It is not a problem and it is not a mandate. It is something to look at.

2. **Stay with the felt wanting.** Do not immediately convert the want into an action plan. Sit with the wanting. What does it feel like in your body? What is its quality?

3. **Ask: what specifically am I wanting?** "I want to be a coach" is not specific. What would having that give you? What does coaching represent? Strip away the surface form and ask what the surface is pointing at.

4. **Keep asking underneath.** Each answer can be the entry to the next question. "I want that level of self-awareness" -- okay, what does self-awareness give you? "I want to be able to feel what's happening in a room" -- okay, what does that capacity give you? Each layer reveals more of the actual want.

5. **Stop when you arrive at a felt yes.** The actual want has a specific quality when you find it -- it feels like the truth. It is not strategic. It is not "this would be good for me." It is more like "yes, that is what I want."

6. **Take the actual want as the orientation, not the surface form.** If the actual want is "I want to live with that level of self-awareness in my own life," then the orientation is the self-awareness, not the coaching. Self-awareness can be pursued directly without coaching being involved. The coaching may emerge later as a downstream expression of having lived the self-awareness for a long time. Or it may not. Either way, the underneath want is what to orient around.

## What This Tends To Produce

The most common outcome of this practice is the discovery that the want is not actually for the coaching role -- it is for something the coaching role visibly demonstrates. The capacity for self-awareness, the emotional fluidity, the ability to read a room, the freedom in conversation, the depth of connection with others. These can all be developed without becoming a coach. The pursuit of them directly is the actual path.

A second common outcome: the surface "I want to coach" reveals one of the three dangerous primary motivations -- wanting to save, wanting intimacy without risk, wanting to be the one who knows. (See [[coaching-as-savior-role]], [[coaching-as-surrogate-intimacy]], [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]].) The surfacing of the motivation is itself the work. Once seen, the motivation can be addressed at its root, which usually involves work that has nothing to do with becoming a coach.

A third, less common outcome: the actual want, after tracing, really is to coach. The wanting is sustained, specific, deeply felt, and pointing at the role itself -- not at what the role would give the person. This is the kind of call Joe describes as "they can't, they just have to do that thing." When the want is this clear and this rooted, the next step is the long apprenticeship of [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]] and [[training-coaches-and-facilitators]] -- the want is real, but the work to embody it is years long.

## What the Coach (or AI Coach) Does With This

If a person is presenting "I want to be a coach" or "I want to do what you do" or "I want to be a facilitator," do not immediately help them plan how to become one. The first move is the impulse check. Ask what is underneath. Help them trace the want to its actual form. Then orient them to the actual form, not the surface form.

If the actual want is something the coaching role represents, name what they actually want and point them at how to pursue it directly. If the actual want is one of the dangerous primary motivations, gently surface it and invite the work that addresses the underneath. If the actual want, after tracing, is for the role itself, name the long apprenticeship that the role requires.

The conversational structure:

- "I noticed you said you want to do that. I'm curious -- what specifically about it?"
- "And if you had that, what would that give you?"
- "What does [the deeper answer] mean to you?"
- "When you imagine yourself having that, what does your body do?"
- "What was happening for you in your own life that made you start wanting this?"

The pace is slow. The questions move underneath. The aim is the actual want, not the surface want.

## Example: Brett's Version of the Practice in the Transcript

The transcript itself contains an inline example. Brett mentions that he sometimes gets the impulse watching Joe's rapid-fire coaching: "I want to be able to do that." Joe agrees -- "fair." Brett then traces it himself:

"I want that level of self-awareness. I want the level of emotional fluidity. I want to be able to feel what's going on in a room. Yeah, I want that." -- Brett Kistler, Coaching as a Practice

Joe affirms the tracing: "Yes, that's fair. If there's some recognition underneath -- yeah, I'm not suggesting by any chance any way to sublimate a want, but just be in it for the self-awareness and be self-aware in that want." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The example shows the practice in operation. The surface impulse ("I want to be able to do that") is met by the tracing into the underneath wants (the self-awareness, the emotional fluidity, the room-reading). The underneath wants are nameable, specific, and pursueable directly. The orientation shifts from "become a coach" to "develop the underlying capacities."

The detail in Joe's affirmation matters: do not sublimate the want. The point is not to suppress the wanting-to-coach. The point is to be self-aware about what the wanting is actually about, so the wanting can be honored at the level of its underneath rather than acted on at the level of its surface.

## The Connection to Broader Want Practices

This is a specific application of the broader want-tracing practice. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] for the version of want-tracing when a should is in the way. See [[need-behind-the-want]] for the version that traces from want to underlying need. See [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]] for the related dynamic in which owning the want often dissolves it because the wanting was structurally about something else.

The practice here is the version specifically calibrated to the impulse to become a coach, because that impulse has its own predictable surface and its own predictable underneaths. The structure of the practice is general; the specific application is for would-be coaches and facilitators.

## Boundary Condition

The practice is not a way to talk people out of becoming coaches. Some people, after the tracing, find that the want really is for the role itself. That is a valid outcome. The practice is just to ensure that the want being acted on is the actual want, not a surface impulse that would be better addressed by pursuing the underneath directly. If the actual want is the role, the practice has served its purpose by confirming it. If the actual want is something else, the practice has served its purpose by surfacing it.

## Tone

Gentle, curious. The practice is not a gatekeeping exercise; it is a self-inquiry. The pace is slow. The questions are warm. The aim is the person's own clarity about what they actually want, not a verdict on whether they should become a coach.
