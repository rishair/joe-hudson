# Noticing the Matrix Pattern (The 3-Step Diagnostic for an Active Avoidance Loop)

## The Practice

A three-part diagnostic for catching yourself inside an active pattern of unfelt-emotion avoidance. The diagnostic does not require sitting down to journal or going into a session. It can be run in the middle of an ordinary moment when the body is doing one of the avoidance behaviors that keep the pattern in place.

The three parts:

1. **Notice the emotion you are avoiding.** The most subtle of the three. The avoided emotion is usually one layer underneath the surface emotion the person is aware of.
2. **Notice how you avoid having that feeling.** The specific behavior, posture, or strategy that the system reaches for when the emotion starts to arrive.
3. **Notice how that avoidance actually brings the feared emotion to you.** The closing of the loop: the avoidance behavior is the production line for the very feeling it was meant to prevent.

The practice runs all three parts in sequence in the same moment. Each part opens the next. Running only the first part produces awareness without the structural insight. Running all three makes the loop visible from inside the loop, which is what allows it to dissolve.

"There's an emotion underneath any of these patterns. We can learn how to see when you're in the pattern itself. The way you do it is in three parts." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Out Of The Matrix

## Step 1: Notice the Emotion You Are Avoiding

The instruction is to look at whatever surface behavior is happening and ask: what emotion is being avoided right now? The answer is usually one layer down from what the person is consciously feeling.

Joe's examples of the surface-to-underneath move:

"You go and jump on your phone, what's the emotion you're avoiding? Oh, you're judging somebody, what's the emotion you're avoiding? Oh, you're feeling guilt, but there's an emotion underneath it. What's the emotion underneath it? What's the emotion you're avoiding?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Out Of The Matrix

The grammar matters. The question is "what is being avoided," not "what is being felt." The conscious emotion is often the cover, not the buried one. Phone-grabbing covers something. Judgment covers something. Guilt covers something. The diagnostic asks for the something, not for the cover.

The specificity matters as well. "I'm avoiding pain" or "I'm avoiding discomfort" is too generic. The specific emotion -- helplessness, shame, abandonment, the felt sense of being unwanted -- is what makes the rest of the diagnostic operable. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the depth-of-specificity principle.

The avoidance signals that point at where the diagnostic should be run:

- Reaching for the phone for no clear reason
- Judging another person and not being able to drop the judgment
- A surface emotion (guilt, frustration, irritation) that won't release
- Starting a behavior the person has identified as a problem behavior
- A felt constriction in the body in a specific situation
- Three signals from [[three-signals-of-unfelt-emotion]]: looping thought, decision paralysis, persistent judgment

## Step 2: Notice How You Avoid the Emotion

Once the avoided emotion is named, the second question is: what specifically am I doing to not feel it? This is the diagnostic that catches the avoidance behavior in the act of running.

Joe's worked example of his own emotional-abandonment pattern names two specific strategies:

"Say when I was emotionally abandoned as a kid, the way that I would avoid that feeling is I get really hard. I'd be like, hmm, I defend myself. Hmm, that would be one of the ways that I would not get abandoned. And the other way is that I would make people dependent on me. So that I would create an environment where they needed me enough where I was not going to be emotionally abandoned." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Out Of The Matrix

Two distinct avoidance strategies for the same underlying emotion. Each one a specific behavior the body could reach for. The diagnostic asks the person to identify their own, not Joe's. Common categories of avoidance strategies:

- **Hardening / defending.** Going stoic, getting short, raising the chin (see [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]]), tightening the jaw, withdrawing emotionally
- **Caretaking / making people dependent.** Becoming the helper, the indispensable one, the one whose absence would hurt the other
- **Performing.** Becoming bigger, brighter, funnier, more impressive in the moment
- **Numbing.** Phone, food, alcohol, scrolling, work
- **Storying.** Going into analysis, building a narrative about who is at fault, deconstructing what just happened
- **Controlling.** Trying to manage other people's emotions, behaviors, or perceptions
- **Distancing.** Leaving (physically, emotionally, attentionally) the moment the emotion starts to land

The diagnostic is precise about which one the person reaches for in this configuration. Not all of them. The specific one (or two, or three) that is doing the work right now.

## Step 3: Notice How the Avoidance Brings the Emotion to You

The closing move. Once the avoided emotion is named and the avoidance strategy is named, the third question is: how is this strategy producing the very feeling it was supposed to prevent?

This is the move that makes the loop visible from inside. Without it, the diagnostic stays at the level of self-observation. With it, the diagnostic becomes structural insight: the thing I am doing to escape the feeling is what is generating the feeling.

Joe's worked example continues:

"Every time I went, 'mm,' because I wasn't gonna be emotionally abandoned by you, it pushed you away. So of course I'm gonna get more emotionally abandoned. Or every time I tried to make you dependent, then there was this moment where I felt all alone, like I couldn't depend on anybody because I was always the helper. And then I'm like, 'oh my god, I've been abandoned again because I have to do this all on my own again.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Out Of The Matrix

The structure is identical for both strategies: the avoidance behavior is the production line for the avoided experience. The hardening pushes people away, which produces abandonment. The caretaking produces the lonely position of the helper, which produces abandonment. Same avoided feeling, two different avoidance strategies, same closing of the loop.

This is the [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] mechanism running in the small scale of a single moment rather than across a life. The diagnostic compresses the principle into a real-time observation.

## How the Three Parts Land Together

Each part of the diagnostic does specific work. Run together, they produce a recognition that running any one alone does not.

- **Step 1 alone:** "I am avoiding X." Useful for naming what is happening. Not yet structural.
- **Steps 1 + 2:** "I am avoiding X by doing Y." Useful for catching the behavior. Still external -- the person can observe the avoidance without yet seeing the loop.
- **All three:** "I am avoiding X by doing Y, which is producing X." Structural. The loop is visible from inside. The behavior cannot continue being innocent once this is seen, because it has been recognized as the production line for the feared outcome.

The third recognition is what produces the natural dissolution of the strategy. The person does not need to decide to stop the avoidance. The avoidance loses its rationale once it has been seen as the cause of the feared thing rather than the protection against it.

## What This Practice Is Not

The diagnostic is not the dissolution. It is the recognition that opens the door to the dissolution.

The actual dissolution happens through feeling the avoided emotion (see [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]). Without that, the diagnostic produces awareness of the loop without releasing it. The person can spend years saying "I see I'm doing this again" without ever actually feeling what is underneath. The diagnostic is the first half of the work. The welcoming is the second half.

The diagnostic is also not a tool for self-attack. The recognition is meant to produce structural clarity, not shame. If the diagnostic becomes a way of beating yourself up ("of course I did it again, I'm broken"), see [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the corrective: apply the diagnostic to the beating-up itself. The shame is an avoidance of an underlying emotion too. Feel that one.

## How to Use the Practice

Joe's recommendation for a sustained run of the practice:

"Every day at the end of the day or at the beginning of the day, you notice an emotion that you've been avoiding and you bring it into the emotional inquiry. You just do that every day for 2 weeks. Every day for 2 weeks. And notice how much your life changes." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Out Of The Matrix

The two-week commitment is the practice container Joe offers for the public-facing teaching. Daily noticing for fourteen days. The cumulative effect of repeated recognition is what shifts the underlying configuration. One run produces a flash of insight. Fourteen runs accumulate the body-level evidence that the loop is structural, not contingent.

For the in-the-moment version: when the avoidance signal appears (the phone-grab, the judgment, the surface emotion that will not move), run the three parts on the spot. Thirty seconds. Notice what is being avoided. Notice what the body is doing to avoid it. Notice how the doing is producing the thing.

For the after-the-fact version: when a familiar pattern has just played out (the same fight, the same withdrawal, the same kind of partner), run the three parts on the moment. Name the avoided emotion. Name the avoidance behavior that ran. Name how that behavior produced the configuration you keep ending up in.

## The Companion Practice: Emotional Inquiry

The diagnostic pairs with [[emotional-inquiry]]. The diagnostic opens the door; the inquiry walks through it. Once the avoided emotion has been named and the loop has been seen, the next move is to actually welcome the emotion -- not to make it go away, but to give it the love it never got.

See [[the-matrix]] for the broader teaching frame this diagnostic sits inside, and [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] for the welcoming work that completes what the diagnostic begins.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- Does not promise a specific outcome from the diagnostic alone
- Does not treat the diagnostic as the solution -- it is the recognition that opens the door
- Does not use it as a tool to evaluate other people's patterns
- Does not let it become another "should I'm doing right"

## Tone

Practical and matter-of-fact. The instruction is offered as something the person can do without prior training, in a single moment, in the middle of their ordinary life. No mysticism. No setup. The three questions, asked in sequence, applied to whatever is happening right now.

The two-week framing is offered as an experiment rather than a regimen. "Notice how much your life changes" is the invitation. The promise is implicit: the person who runs this for two weeks will see something they cannot see now. Not specific outcomes. Just a different relationship to the patterns that have been running them.
