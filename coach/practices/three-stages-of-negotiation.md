# Three Stages of Negotiation

## The Practice

When negotiating a position or a job, run the conversation in three sequential stages:

1. **How do you want to work together?**
2. **What resources do you need to do this job really well?**
3. **What is the salary?**

Most people invert this order. They start with salary because salary feels like the most concrete and consequential variable. But salary is not what motivates the work, and salary-first negotiations consistently produce matches that are wrong on the dimensions that actually determine whether the work goes well.

"When I talk to people about negotiating for a position or for a job, I say there's 3 stages of the negotiation. The first is, how do you want to work together? That's the first stage. Because that actually motivates everybody if there's a match, or it pushes people away if it's not a match, which is perfect. The second is, what are the resources you need to actually do your job really, really well? And then the third is, what's the salary? And I notice that when it goes in that position, it's just clarity the whole way, or you get to a no quicker, rather than if what most people do is work on a salary, which is really not what motivates us." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

## Why the Order Matters

**Stage 1: How do you want to work together?** Reveals the fit on the dimensions that actually determine day-to-day experience. Do you both value transparency? Do you both want emotional honesty? Do you both want long uninterrupted blocks of work? The fit on these dimensions is what produces the energy in the work. The mismatch on these dimensions is what produces the burnout. See [[burnout-cycle]] and [[energy-management-over-time-management]].

A mismatch found in Stage 1 is a no, fast. Both people save the time of negotiating salary for a job that was never going to work.

A match found in Stage 1 means both people are motivated to find their way to a yes on the subsequent stages.

**Stage 2: What resources do you need to do the job well?** Now that fit is established, what does each party need from the other to succeed? Tools, support, decision-making authority, autonomy, team, training. Most jobs fail because resource needs were not articulated before terms were set, not because salary was wrong.

**Stage 3: Salary.** With fit and resources established, salary is just a number that reflects the work. It is real, but it is the smallest of the three variables in determining outcomes. People will take less money for a job that is the right fit with the right resources than for a job that is the wrong fit with insufficient resources. The studies on this are consistent.

"There's great studies on this, but most people would take a little less money if they were 3 times as happy in their job, you know?" -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

## Why People Invert the Order

Salary feels concrete. The other two stages feel soft. The cultural training is to think hard-nosed people start with the money, and people who start with "fit" are being precious.

The inversion is wrong. Starting with salary commits both parties to a number before knowing whether the work itself would be sustainable. If the work turns out to be a bad fit, the salary becomes the price of obligation: you took the money, you can't leave. The number that felt concrete becomes the chain.

## What This Practice Produces

- Faster nos. Mismatches surface in Stage 1, not after three months of work.
- Better yeses. The matches that survive Stage 1 are made on the dimensions that actually predict whether the work will go well.
- Reduced burnout for both parties. See [[burnout-cycle]]: a job that is a fit on how-we-work-together does not generate the chronic adrenaline that mismatched work generates.
- Salary numbers that reflect work-fit rather than negotiating leverage.

## When to Use This

- Joining a new company.
- Hiring someone for a key role.
- Restructuring an existing working relationship.
- Negotiating a partnership or collaboration.
- Any context where the work is going to be sustained over time and the daily experience matters as much as the outcome.

## What the Stages Sound Like

**Stage 1 questions:**
- How do you want to communicate? Sync or async? Frequency?
- How do you handle conflict? When something is off, how do we name it?
- What does decision-making look like between us? Who decides what?
- How do you give and receive feedback?
- What kind of pace do you want? Sprints? Steady?
- What do you want from this relationship beyond the work itself?

**Stage 2 questions:**
- What support, tools, or team do you need to do this well?
- What decisions do you need authority over?
- What information do you need access to?
- What does your week look like at full capacity?
- What kills your productivity in a job like this?

**Stage 3 questions:**
- Now what's the number?

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't run the stages too rigidly.** A real conversation will flow between them. The point is the order of weight, not the order of words.
- **Don't use Stage 1 as a screening test.** It is a real conversation about real preferences, not a checklist for filtering. If you are running it to "test" the other person, they will feel it and the conversation will not produce useful information.
- **Don't skip a stage because it feels awkward.** The awkwardness is signal. Stage 1 in particular is often skipped because both parties are afraid of finding a mismatch. Finding the mismatch is the point.
