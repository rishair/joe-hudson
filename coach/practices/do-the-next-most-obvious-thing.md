# Do the Next Most Obvious Thing

## The Practice

When a choice has hardened into the felt experience of "I have to make a decision," do not try to make the decision. Treat the decision-feeling as a signal that fear is in the system. Ask: what is the fear here? What is the next most obvious thing I can do to address that fear? Do that. Then check again: am I still in decision-feeling, or has it dissolved into the next step?

Iterate. The decision never gets made the way a decision-maker expects to make it. The decision evaporates as the fear gets addressed step by step. The person looks up and finds they have already acted.

"Anytime I thought I had to make a decision, what's the next most obvious step? What's the fear that's not being addressed here, and what's the next most obvious step that'll lead me to --" -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## When to Use It

The practice applies the moment the felt experience of "I have a decision to make" arrives. That experience is itself the diagnostic. See [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]]. The body and the system already know how to act; thousands of choices a day happen without any felt deciding. What feels like a decision is fear with a particular shape.

Specific situations:
- A high-stakes investment, hire, or business choice that has been hanging
- A relationship choice (marry, leave, ask, confront)
- A purchase decision that keeps getting revisited
- Any choice that has produced pro-con lists, polling friends, or sleepless nights

## How It Works in Practice

The practice is iterative, not linear. Each loop:

1. Notice the decision-feel. "I have to decide whether to invest in this person."
2. Ask: what is the actual fear underneath? "I don't know if this person is great."
3. Identify the next most obvious step that addresses the fear, not the surface decision. "I'll do some reference checks."
4. Do the step.
5. Check: am I still in decision-feel, or is the action obvious now? If still in decision-feel, return to step 2 with the next fear.

Joe's own example from philanthropic investing:

"I used to think when I saw that, I used to think, 'Oh, that's a decision that I have to make and I have to make it well.' And through my time working in philanthropy, what I realized was that's a false, that's not true. It's never true. What's true is you just do the next most obvious thing. Oh, if I'm thinking, do I put the money in or do I not put the money in? It means there's some fear. What's the fear that I have to address? Okay, I don't know if this person's going to be great. Okay, I'll do some reference checks. Okay, now do I still -- is it just am I sending them money now or am I still trying to make a decision? Oh, I'm still trying to make a decision. Okay, well, maybe it's not -- what's happening? Oh, maybe they're not serving. Okay, the next most obvious step is to call some of the people they're serving and make sure that their product is as good or their service is as good as I thought it was." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## The Disappearance of the Decision

When the practice is followed cleanly, the decision is never experienced as having been made.

"It was just constantly looking. Anytime I thought I had to make a decision, what's the next most obvious step?... And then all of a sudden I'm like, yeah, we should invest or no, I'm outta here. And it wasn't -- a decision never felt like it got made, right? It was never like, oh wow, I have made the decision. I'm doing this or I'm not doing this. I just looked up and was like, oh, I guess I didn't invest there, or I guess I did." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

This is the same texture as flow. The choice has returned to being a choice rather than a decision. The action becomes the action it always would have been if the fear had not loaded the experience.

The everyday version: a couple arguing about where to eat. The decision-energy escalates. The practice: drop the decision frame. Just drive. See what feels right. Often the choice makes itself en route.

"You're driving and you're like, where are we going to eat? And that's a decision. And it's like all of a sudden you're in the conversation... And then if you just say, you know what, I'm not going to -- I'm just going to find out where I'm going to end up. And then all of a sudden you're at this place." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## What Counts as "Obvious"

The most common misreading: treating "the next obvious thing" as permission to either jump ("fuck it, I'll just go") or escape ("I'll go to the fridge and grab a beer"). Both are forms of speed-bumping the emotional experience the practice is meant to keep contact with.

The next obvious thing means staying focused on the actual problem and addressing the actual fear -- not jumping past the not-knowing, not avoiding through distraction.

"It means that you're staying focused on the problem and you're not jumping in, you're not speed bumping an emotional experience. So what that means is that it's not like, oh, the next most obvious step is to ignore or to avoid... I'm just so tired of being in this not knowing that I'm gonna fucking just do something. That's one level of avoidance. And the other level of avoidance is I'm gonna go grab a beer." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

A second misreading: looping. Re-checking the same charts, polling the same friends, re-running the same scenarios. Looping is not doing. It is fear extending its territory.

"If you're looping, then you're not doing the next most obvious thing to do, right? So looping is like checking the charts, I would say, isn't doing. What's the thing that you have to do? What's the next most obvious thing to do?" -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

The third misreading: assuming the next step must always be external action. Sometimes the next obvious step is to take care of yourself: rest, do yoga, eat. The practice does not require continuous problem-solving. It requires that when attention returns to the problem, it returns through the next genuine step.

## The Body of the Practice

The practice trains a specific stance: the willingness to be in the not-knowing without forcing resolution. The deciding-feel wants resolution. The practice declines resolution and addresses fear instead. Fear handled, action emerges.

This is structurally identical to [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]]. The blocker is the fear. The symptom is the decision-feel. Working on the symptom (more pro-con lists) does not move anything. Working on the blocker (the fear underneath) dissolves the symptom.

## What It Tends to Produce

- Decisions that feel like they made themselves
- Faster action under uncertainty, because the analysis paralysis loop is interrupted at the level it actually operates on
- Less attachment to outcome, because the action emerged from working with reality rather than predicting it
- A different relationship to high-stakes choices: they stop feeling like life-or-death events

The practice also tends to produce a portfolio of better outcomes over time, not because each individual decision was correct but because the fear-driven errors that come from forcing decisions through deliberation drop out. See [[decisions-are-emotional]] and the related [[principles-free-you]] for the longer-arc version of this.

## What It Is Not

This is not a substitute for thinking. Genuine inquiry -- exploring options, gathering information, sense-making -- continues. The practice applies specifically to the moment when thinking has hardened into deciding-feel. It separates exploration from forced resolution.

This is not "just trust your gut." The body lies just as much as the mind. See [[body-lies-as-much-as-mind]]. The practice does not ask the gut to override the head. It asks the system to keep moving on the actual fear rather than stalling on the decision frame.

- The felt experience of "I have to make a decision" -- see [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]]
- Analysis paralysis, looping pro-con lists, polling friends repeatedly
- A high-stakes choice (investment, hire, marriage, leaving a job)

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[fear-is-about-feeling]]: the fear under each step is something that needs to be felt, not just addressed strategically
- Toward [[principles-free-you]]: principled living is the longer-arc version of this practice -- principles handle the fear in advance so the deciding-feel never arises
- Toward [[good-decision-is-how-you-make-it-not-the-outcome]]: the practice reorients success toward how the action emerged rather than whether the outcome was what was wanted
- Toward [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]] when the fear under the decision-feel turns out to be an unmet need that needs naming

## Tone

Direct, slightly amused. Joe describes the practice with the relaxation of someone who has stopped treating decisions as events. The texture is "you're going to laugh when you realize how much easier this is."
