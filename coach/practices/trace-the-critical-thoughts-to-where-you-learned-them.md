# Trace Each Critical Thought to Where You Learned It

## The Practice

The practice has three steps:

1. **Speak every thought aloud and record it.** Run the [[narrate-every-thought-out-loud]] practice for 5 minutes, with recording. This produces the raw material.

2. **Convert the recording to written form.** Use AI transcription, or write it out by hand. Each thought becomes a line on a page.

3. **Next to each critical thought, write where you learned it.** Particularly for the critical ones — where did you learn that you weren't pretty enough? Where did you learn that you needed to work harder? Where did you learn that you messed up at the last conversation? Write down who gave you this critical voice in the head.

"And then next to each one of the things you say, you're going to write down where you learned it from. It's particularly the critical ones. Where did you learn that you weren't pretty enough? Where did you learn that you needed to work harder? Where did you learn that you messed up at the last conversation? Like, where did you learn those things? And you're just going to write down who gave you this critical voice in the head." -- Joe Hudson, How To End 60,000 Negative Thoughts a Day

## What It Demonstrates

The practice produces a specific recognition: the critical voice in the head is almost never the person's own original assessment. It is a collection of inherited tapes, each one from a specific source — a parent, a teacher, a sibling, a coach, a culture, an early peer group.

The recognition is harder to argue with when it is on paper. Listed as a column of attributions, the pattern becomes visible: "Mom said this. Dad said this. 5th grade teacher said this. Mom said this again. Dad said this in a different form." The voice that has felt like "me" reveals itself as a chorus of borrowed voices.

This is the operational form of the principle [[what-was-done-to-you-youre-now-doing-to-yourself]]. The principle states that the external voices have become the internal ones. The practice makes the statement concrete and specific — not just "you internalized voices" but "this specific sentence came from this specific person at this specific time."

## Why the Source-Tracing Matters

Inner-critic work can stay stuck in argument with the voice. The voice says something; the person tries to disagree with the content; the voice continues. The disagreement does not work because the content is not the issue — the content is the inherited tape, playing for reasons that have nothing to do with the current accuracy of the assessment.

Once the source is named, the relationship to the voice changes. The thought "I'm not pretty enough" stops being a current assessment of one's appearance and becomes "this is the thing my mother said to me in 1987 that I have been repeating to myself ever since." The same words; entirely different relationship.

From the new relationship, several moves become available that were not available before:

- The voice can be heard as borrowed rather than as truth.
- The original speaker can be engaged with — what was going on for that person? What were they actually saying? Were they talking to themselves? (See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]].)
- The decision to keep or release the introjected belief becomes a choice rather than an automatic assumption.
- The recognition that "I have been doing to myself what was done to me" produces a specific kind of compassion for the part of self that has been receiving the inherited attack.

## What Often Surfaces

When people do this practice, common patterns:

- **Most critical thoughts trace to two or three primary sources.** Usually one parent (or both) accounts for the majority. A teacher or a coach may account for a specific domain. The map is more compact than expected.
- **Some thoughts cannot be traced.** The person genuinely does not know where the thought came from. These are worth noting too — they may have come from the culture rather than from a specific person, or they may have come from somewhere too early to remember.
- **Some thoughts feel like the original speaker's voice.** The tone of the introjected thought matches the tone of the original speaker. This is part of why the [[narrate-every-thought-out-loud]] practice insists on the same-tone speaking — the tone is what reveals the source.
- **Some thoughts are mosaics.** A specific thought combines language from one source with the cadence of another. The mapping then shows the layering of introjects over time.

Often the practice produces an unexpected grief. The person realizes that what they have been telling themselves for years was never theirs, and that the person who originally said it was operating from their own unfelt material. The grief is for what was done and for the years spent doing it to oneself.

## Why This Practice Belongs After Narration

The practice presupposes the [[narrate-every-thought-out-loud]] recording. Without the recording, the tracing has to work from memory — which means the most loaded thoughts get edited or smoothed in retrospect. The recording captures the thoughts in their actual operating form, which is what allows the source-tracing to be accurate.

The sequence:

1. [[narrate-every-thought-out-loud]] — 5 minutes, recorded, in the original tone.
2. Transcribe the recording.
3. This practice — trace each critical thought to where it was learned.

The two practices are operationally linked. The narration produces the data; the tracing locates the data's origin. Together they constitute the full introject-mapping work for a single session.

## What to Do With the Map

Once the map exists, several next moves:

- **Direct engagement with the inherited voices.** The work of [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] becomes available — the recognition that the parent who said these things was talking to themselves.
- **The empathy that arrives from the tracing.** Often the work of recognizing the inherited voice produces an unexpected understanding of the original speaker. The parent who said "you're not enough" was usually carrying the same voice themselves. The mother's criticism was her unfelt self-criticism. The compassion that arrives from this recognition is structural, not forced.
- **The decision about which introjects to release.** The map makes the introjects discrete and specific. Each one can be examined: is this true? Is this useful? Do I keep this or do I let it go? The decision is mine to make now, even if it was made for me originally.
- **The relocation of authority.** The introjected voice has been operating with the authority of self — "this is what I think about me." The tracing relocates the authority: "this is what someone else said about me that I have been repeating." The relocation is the prerequisite for the voice no longer having automatic credibility.

## When Joe Uses This

The practice is given as homework after teaching contexts on the inner critic. It is also useful in coaching when:

- A person has done the foundational work on noticing the voice and is ready for the next layer
- A person is stuck in argument with the voice and needs the source-relocation that breaks the stuck pattern
- A person is doing parental work and needs to map what was inherited from each parent
- The work has surfaced [[what-was-done-to-you-youre-now-doing-to-yourself]] as a principle and the practice operationalizes the principle
- A person is about to do confrontation or repair work with a parent and needs to know what specifically was internalized from that parent
- A person has done one round of this practice and is ready to go deeper — repeating the practice produces more specific tracings and more subtle introjects

The practice can also be done in a more compressed form mid-session: pick three of the most loaded critical thoughts the person can identify in the moment, and trace each one to where it was learned. The compressed version produces the recognition without requiring the full 5-minute-recording protocol.

## Tone

The practice should be done with curiosity, not with prosecution. The point is not to indict the original speakers. The point is to map the inheritance so the relationship to the voice can change.

A common failure mode: the person uses the practice to assemble evidence against a parent. The voice of grievance takes over from the voice of investigation. When this happens, the introjects continue to operate because the work is now about the parent rather than about the present self. The practice is most effective when held as inquiry — what is here, where did it come from, what do I want to do with it now.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not skip the recording-and-writing step. Memory smooths the data. The recording-and-writing produces the data in its actual form.
- Do not stop at "some childhood thing." The specificity of the source is part of the work. Push for the actual person, the actual moment, the actual phrasing if possible. "Mom" is more useful than "childhood." "Mom on the phone when I was 7" is more useful than "Mom." The specificity makes the introject discrete; the discreteness makes it inspectable.
- Do not turn the practice into a parent-blame session. The point is to relocate the authority of the voice, not to litigate against the original speakers.
- Do not stop after one round. The first round produces the most obvious introjects. Subsequent rounds produce subtler ones — the ones that have been operating under the radar because they were not loud enough to catch the first time.
- Do not try to release the introjects through cognitive understanding alone. The mapping is the necessary precondition for release work, not the release work itself. The release happens through engagement with the source (often via [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] and related moves), not just through having identified the source.
