# Get Angry in the Car (or at the Beach, or in the Woods)

## The Practice

Find a private place. Get angry. Out loud. With the body. About anything that wants to be angry about. Do not aim it at anyone. Let it move through.

The car on the commute is the most accessible version because most adults are alone in a car for predictable stretches of time. The beach works. The woods work. Anywhere the body can yell without scaring anyone or being judged.

"Get in your car, do it on your commute. Every night, just get pissed at everything that happened that you want to get pissed at." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

## Why Most People Need This

In modern life, almost no one is regularly alone in a place where they can yell. The body has nowhere safe to discharge anger. The anger accumulates and either leaks at the people closest (see [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]]) or settles into depression and low-grade disempowerment.

"So many of us are never really alone in the woods or alone on the beach, or so rarely. In fact, the time that we might be alone might be when we're driving. Which might explain how so much road rage tends to come out when people feel alone, but they're actually interacting with others between metal and glass." -- Brett Kistler, The Wisdom of Anger

The car is the available private container. The practice uses what already exists.

## What It Produces

Joe describes the change in an executive client who started this practice. She entered the work feeling like a victim of a bureaucracy she could not control. He gave her the homework: get angry every night on your commute.

"Literally within like 2 or 3 weeks, her empowerment changed significantly. Her level of empowerment changed significantly. She didn't feel as trapped anymore. And I would say like a kind of a low-level depression started changing for her into much more empowerment." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

See [[joes-angry-executive-commute]] for the full example.

The general pattern: 2-3 weeks of consistent nightly release produces noticeable change in baseline empowerment. Depression lifts. The feeling of being trapped reduces. The capacity to make moves the person wants to make returns.

## What If the Commute Is Not Enough?

If the anger is large and the commute does not contain it, keep going. Do not bring the unfinished anger into the house.

"Keep driving. Keep driving. Walk in the door, go, oh shit, I'm so angry. Hey guys, look, I'm really angry right now. I don't want to take it out on any of you. I'm going to go drive and yell for a couple 20 minutes and I'll be back." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The willingness to keep driving rather than walk in carrying it is itself a piece of the practice. The signal to family or partners ("I'm going to be back in 20 minutes") is critical -- see [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for why the timeline distinguishes this from punishment-by-absence.

## Variations

The principle is private discharge of anger through the body, not at anyone. Specific variations Joe mentions:
- **The car on the commute.** Yelling, banging on the steering wheel, swearing freely.
- **Yelling at the ocean.** One of Joe's executive clients went to the beach in San Francisco. The ocean does not hear or judge.
- **Writing something angry and burning it.** The body moves through the writing; the burning marks the completion.
- **Going to the beach or the woods and yelling.**
- **Quieter ranting where someone might be nearby.** "You can just be like, goddamn motherfucker, I fucking hate when that happens. Why the fuck is that always happening? Like it can literally just be like a rant that anybody could not hear from a different room. It's not gonna release the musculature, the trauma musculature the same way, but it still is a great process of moving the anger." (Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II)
- **Yelling in a pillow.** When a container has thin walls and full voice is not possible.

The form is whatever the body wants. The constraint is: not at a person, and not as a story you keep telling yourself. The voice + body version is the most complete; the quieter versions are useful when fuller expression is not available.

## On-Ramps for the Practice

Several specific tricks help start the practice or keep it moving when it stalls.

**Tell the body it's safe.** Speak directly to the body before beginning. Name what is not happening here. See [[tell-the-body-its-safe]].

**Negotiate with the voice in the head.** When the voice is producing reasons not to ("they had reasons," "be compassionate"), do not argue. Offer a contract: "I've listened to you for a decade, now I'm going to listen to anger for 20 minutes." See [[convert-thought-to-time-bounded-listening]].

**Fake it.** If the body cannot find the felt state, perform anger first. The body learns the shape; the real thing arrives in a few minutes. See [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]].

**Use angry music.** Joe mentions Tool specifically: "I like Tool in particular because it's so angry and aggressive, but it's also speaking to this kind of deep spiritual truth." Any aggressive music gives the body a context that justifies the motion. The body matches the music's energy.

**Breathe like the Hulk.** "If you can breathe in a way that builds up your anger, great. That's a great way for those people who've repressed anger in a way that it's self-abusive or the passive aggression. To be able to breathe in such a way that allows you to get angry. Breathe like the Hulk." (Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II)

**Listen to the anger like you would a friend.** "I'm just gonna listen to anger for 20 minutes." The framing of listening (rather than performing) gives some bodies more permission than the framing of release.

## When Release Goes Wrong During Practice

Three failure modes can interrupt the practice. Each has a specific move. See [[three-things-that-go-wrong-when-releasing-anger]] for the full taxonomy.

- **Collapse:** the body folds halfway through. Use [[i-exist-affirmation-against-collapse]].
- **Dysregulation:** the person gets captured by the story, leaves their body, says things they did not mean. Use [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]].
- **Recreating shame:** the release damages something and produces shame. Use awareness without making it a should.

The practice is not failing when these happen. They are expected in the early stages. Recognizing the failure mode is part of the practice itself.

## What to Look for at the End

Real release produces [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]]: a small, felt sense of "I know what to do" structured around the self. If after consistent release the same anger keeps recycling without producing clarity, the anger is probably covering a deeper emotion. See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] and the read [[recycling-anger-without-clarity]].

## Why the Brain Resists Finding the Outlet

Even when the practice is obvious in retrospect, the mind in real time often does not search for it. The default frame collapses to two options: get angry at the person, or repress the anger. The third option (release somewhere else) does not appear.

"What limits our thinking to that small instead of, I'm gonna write something really angry and then I'm gonna burn it up, or I'm gonna go to the beach and yell, or all sorts of ways that you can express anger. What makes it that we just don't do any of that, don't even think to do any of those things?" -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

Part of the function of this practice is to install the third option as a reachable thought. Once a person has done it a few times, the option is no longer invisible.

## What Stops People

"There's always a way to do it. And there's always a reason not to. Because our brain will find any way that it can to not be angry. If we were told as a kid that anger was bad, or if we were punished for our anger, or if we got to experience someone's abuse through anger, we'll just shut it down in us, in ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

The reasons-not-to are themselves diagnostic. The person who cannot find time to yell in the car for ten minutes a night is the person who most needs the practice.

## The Constraint: Not At Anyone

This is the line that distinguishes the practice from emotional abuse. The anger is moved through the body privately. The conversation with the person about whom the anger arose, if there is one, happens after the release, from clarity.

"That's just resisted anger. We're talking about loving your anger. We're talking about letting it move, letting it get out. We're talking about learning to have anger unresisted instead of being lost in your anger." -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger

See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] and [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]].

## Relation to Daily Emotional Yoga

This is the anger-specific case of the broader [[daily-emotional-yoga]] practice. The same principle: dedicated time, body movement, private container. The car version is a low-friction entry point that uses time that is already scheduled.

## Tone

Joe presents this almost casually. Get in your car. Yell. That is it. The tone signals that anger work does not require a retreat or a ritual; it requires showing up to a few minutes a day in a place where the body can move.
