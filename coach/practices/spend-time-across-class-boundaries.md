# Spend Time Across Class Boundaries Each Week

## The Practice

Each week, spend deliberate time with people whose financial reality is meaningfully different from your own. Not in passing, not as a transaction, but as actual contact. The week should include time with people who are homeless or very poor, time with people who are very wealthy, and time with people in the middle. The point is not service. It is exposure.

"To this day, I will spend a part of every week going and interacting with people who I would not normally interact with. And sometimes those people are homeless, and sometimes those people are very poor, and sometimes those people are very wealthy, just so that I can constantly feel how different perspectives live and to be a part of that." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Save You

## Why It Matters

Money tends to insulate the people inside any particular wealth bracket from the lived experience of the others. The wealthy become out of touch with the daily reality of people without money. The poor become out of touch with the issues of the wealthy. Each group constructs a mental model of the other that gets calcified by the absence of contact.

The mental models are usually wrong, and they are always preloaded with [[comparative-mind]] judgment in both directions. The wealthy frame the poor as either deserving sympathy or deserving blame. The poor frame the wealthy as either deserving sympathy or deserving blame. Both framings stop being framings once contact is direct. The other person stops being an idea and becomes a person.

This is the practice version of [[money-is-impersonal]]: the impersonal stance toward money is hard to hold from inside a single class. Direct contact across class lines is what keeps the stance alive in the body, not just in the head.

## Why Joe Does It Specifically

Joe describes the practice as a way to "constantly feel how different perspectives live." The verb is feel, not understand. The practice is not information gathering. It is sensory contact with the other reality. The body needs to know what poverty looks like in a face, what wealth looks like in a body, what worry looks like across a kitchen table, what comfort looks like in a living room.

The practice also functions as a defense against the silent drift that happens when a person's social network is composed exclusively of people in the same wealth bracket. The drift is unconscious. The practice is the conscious counter-move.

## What It Looks Like in Practice

The form is open. What matters is regular, direct, non-transactional contact. Not "I'm here to help you" (which produces the savior dynamic; see [[money-must-flow]] on the harm of charity without exchange). Not "I'm here to learn about you" (which produces the anthropologist dynamic). Just contact, the way one would have contact with a friend across the table.

Joe does not prescribe a method. The practice is structural: make sure every week includes this contact. The specific form follows the specific life.

## What Tends to Shift

- Judgments about other people's money soften. The diagnostic from [[money-is-impersonal]] — the daily judgments that arrive without effort when money is in the room — gets smaller.
- The internal scarcity loop loosens. The realization that someone with a tenth of one's resources is not in tenth of one's distress, and someone with ten times one's resources is not in ten times the contentment, makes the [[same-scarcity-story-regardless-of-wealth]] visible firsthand.
- The body starts to recognize a wider band of "people" as people, not as categories.
- The political stances about wealth get more textured. The system-level statements stop being the only available frame.

## Variations

- For someone in a city: regular contact with people experiencing homelessness in the local area, paired with attendance at events or contexts where significant wealth is also present.
- For someone in a rural area: contact with both the visibly struggling and the visibly comfortable in the local community. The contrast may be subtler but still trackable.
- For someone whose work already places them in one class bracket: deliberate, weekly contact with the other end of the spectrum, outside of work.

## Boundary Conditions

The practice is corrupted when it becomes a form of self-congratulation ("I am the kind of person who spends time with the poor") or a form of voyeurism ("I want to see how the other half lives"). Both produce the opposite of impersonal money. The corrective is to notice the savior dynamic if it shows up and treat its presence as data: this is not yet the practice that was intended.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a coachee describes a hardened position about wealth or poverty
- When a coachee is making leadership or money decisions from inside a class bubble
- When a coachee reports daily judgments about other people's money and wants to soften them
- When parents are trying to raise children with a sane relationship to money
- As a long-arc practice that sits underneath [[money-is-impersonal]]
