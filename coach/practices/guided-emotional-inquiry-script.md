# The Guided Emotional Inquiry Script (The Three-Stage Question Sequence)

## The Practice

The scripted, meditation-form of [[emotional-inquiry]]. The practitioner picks a difficult emotion, calls it up in the body, and is led through three rounds of questions in a specific order. Between each round, the practitioner releases the feeling, takes a breath, and re-invites it. The questions are not answered aloud. The practitioner's job is to put attention where the question points and notice what is there.

The script exists because the in-the-moment practice is hard to describe in the abstract. The guided form gives the practitioner a felt sense of what the practice is, so the in-the-moment version becomes possible later. See [[emotional-inquiry]] for the underlying practice and its principles.

## The Setup Instructions Before Starting

Two things the practitioner is told before any question is asked.

**The job is to feel, not to answer.** "There's no response needed. So if I say, where's the center of it, your job is just to find the center of it. It's not to say the center of it is right below my abdomen." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique. The intellect's job is to direct attention. The body's job is to notice. Verbal response would re-engage the analytic mind and pull the practitioner out of the felt sense.

**Pick a difficult emotion.** "You can pick the emotion that is difficult for you. Maybe there's a fear, or maybe there is an anger, or maybe there's pain or an early childhood trauma. So just the emotion that you're not particularly wanting to feel." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique. The criterion is avoidance, not intensity. The practice is for the emotion the practitioner has been pushing away.

**Call it up however you can.** Memory, imagination, or the present-moment feeling if it is already there. "Maybe you need to remember a time when you felt it. Maybe you can just bring it up in that moment. Maybe you have to think of a scenario that would trigger it, you know, somebody yelling at you or some death or whatever it is." The call-up method is whatever works.

## The Cycle Structure

The script has a specific rhythm: invite the feeling, inquire, release, invite again, inquire again, release, invite again, inquire again, close. Three rounds of inquiry separated by deep breaths. The release-and-reinvite is part of the practice -- it is not just structural padding.

"Every time I say, okay, come back to the emotion, we're going to go back to it, and then we'll inquire around it, explore it, and then we'll come back to it, then we'll explore it, then we'll come back to it, then we'll explore it, then we come back to it." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique

The come-back-to-the-feeling cycle does two things:
- **It practices invitation.** Each re-invite is itself a piece of welcoming. The practitioner is rehearsing the move that the practice is teaching: feelings come and go, and the relationship to them is "let it in" rather than "keep it out" or "hold onto it forever."
- **It allows release between intensities.** The exploration can be intense. The deep breath between rounds resets the nervous system so the next round can be entered fresh rather than escalating.

## Opening (Before Round 1)

Get comfortable. Close the eyes. Relax for a minute. Then a pre-noticing pass:

- Notice if anything is being resisted.
- Notice if there's anything that wants to protect.
- Notice if anything wants something to be different.

These three notices surface the protective patterns before they hijack the inquiry. They are not asking the practitioner to fix the resistance. They are asking the practitioner to see it. Often, the seeing alone softens it.

Then call up the feeling.

## Round 1: Locate and Map (The Sensory Geometry Stage)

The first round establishes the felt-presence of the emotion as a thing with location and dimension. Questions are spatial and kinetic.

- How does this feeling sit in your body?
- Where's the outside edge of the feeling?
- Where's the center of the feeling?
- How thick is the feeling?
- How wide is it?
- How does the feeling move in your body? Does it pulsate? Does it vibrate? How does it move?
- Can you find the exact center of the feeling?
- Can you move to the very middle of the feeling?
- Can you take one step even closer to the center of the feeling?

The progression: outside edge -> center -> dimensions -> motion -> precise center -> moving toward center. By the end of the round, the practitioner has located the feeling in the body, mapped its boundaries, and moved their attention inside it. The "step closer to the center" is the move from observing-the-feeling to being-inside-the-feeling. This is the inside-out vs. top-down distinction in operational form -- see [[emotional-inquiry]] for that distinction.

Then: relax, let the feeling go, take a deep breath. Then invite the feeling back.

## Round 2: Properties and Temporal Manipulation (The Texture and Speed Stage)

The second round deepens the felt-encounter through additional sensory modalities and introduces time-manipulation of the feeling. Questions are about properties (color, density, vibration) and about what happens when the practitioner alters the felt experience.

**Spatial relationship to the body:**
- How close is the feeling to your back? How close is it to the front of your body?

**Vibration and edges:**
- Do the edges of this feeling vibrate differently than the center?
- Can you find the exact point where the edge of the feeling ends and you begin?
- What is it like between the very edge and the very middle of the feeling?

**Color:**
- What color is the feeling?
- Does the color change?
- Is the center a different color than the edges, which is a different color than the middle, or is it all the same color?

**Density and space:**
- How solid is the feeling?
- How dense is it?
- Are the edges a different density than the middle? And is that a different density than the center?
- If you explore the most dense part of it, is there still space there? Or is there no space?

**Temporal manipulation:**
- What happens to the feeling if you really slow it down to really take a look at it?
- And what happens to the feeling if you speed it up?

The temporal manipulation introduces the felt-discovery that the practitioner can alter the experience by changing the speed of attention. The feeling is not a fixed object; it changes shape based on how it is engaged. This is the operational ground for [[resisted-vs-unresisted-everything-shows-up-different]] -- the practitioner is given direct evidence that the feeling shows up differently depending on the relationship to it.

Then: let it go, take a deep breath, shake it off if needed. Then re-embody for the final round.

## Round 3: Playful Interaction and Polarity (The Kid-With-A-Frog Stage)

The third round is the most distinctive. The frame is explicit: treat the feeling the way a little kid would treat a frog they just found.

"And this time, just treat the feeling like you're a little kid with a frog. You're like, you found a frog, you picked it up, and you're looking at it, you're smelling it, you're tasting it. Even little kids will even lick that stuff, you know. Let's really embrace the feeling, like you, with that just genuine sense of curiosity." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique

The questions in this round are polarities. Each pair gives the practitioner direct felt-evidence of how the feeling shows up differently depending on the receiving relationship. The polarity is the teaching.

**Sensory poking (gentle/forceful):**
- What is this feeling like if you poke at it? If you poke at it in its sensitive spots?
- What is this feeling like if you take a deep breath into it?
- What is this feeling like if you hold your breath?
- What is this feeling like if you tickle it, if you play with it gently?

**Welcome vs. Reject (Polarity 1):**
- What is this feeling like if you say, "I don't want to feel you. Stop it. Get out of here."?
- What is this feeling like if you give it a big old hug? Embrace it?

**Pursuit:**
- What is this feeling like if you chase it, chase it around the room?

(See [[chase-it-around-the-room]] for the full development of this move as it appears in live coaching with avoidant feelings.)

**Listen vs. Dismiss (Polarity 2):**
- What is this feeling like if you listen to it really, really, really, really carefully, like it was the most intelligent person you ever met, or the deep spiritual connection, and all you wanted was just to hear what it had to say?
- And how is this feeling if you were just not willing to listen at all? You're just like, "You're an idiot. Stop talking."

**Open vs. Defend (Polarity 3):**
- And how is this feeling if you just let it penetrate you completely? If you just open yourself up completely and allow it in with no resistance?
- And how is this feeling if you don't let it in, you defend yourself, you block yourself from this feeling?

**The closing move - loving attention:**
- And how does this feeling act if you love it and you open your arms to it the way you would a child who's been acting bad, but just totally has been doing it to get mom's attention, and you just give the child the attention, you just give this emotion that loving attention?

The polarities deserve attention as a teaching device. The practitioner is not being asked to choose one stance. They are being asked to feel each one. The contrast is the teacher. The "I don't want to feel you, stop it" stance produces a felt sense of one thing; the "big old hug" stance produces a felt sense of something completely different -- with the same feeling. The practitioner cannot be told this; the practitioner has to feel it. The script is engineered to deliver the felt comparison.

This is the same mechanism as the resist-vs-welcome experiment that Joe describes in the conceptual form of the practice. The practitioner closes their eyes, resists the emotion as hard as possible, then welcomes it as fully as possible, and feels the difference. The third round of the script is that experiment elaborated across multiple welcome/resist pairs. See [[resisted-vs-unresisted-everything-shows-up-different]] for the principle the experiment teaches.

## The Closing Frame: The Acting-Out Child Wants Attention

The final question in round 3 is the one that consolidates the practice as a loving practice rather than as a sensory-mapping exercise. The image is precise: a child who has been acting bad to get mom's attention, and the parent simply giving the attention rather than addressing the behavior.

The frame is structurally identical to [[the-monster-wants-hugs-teaching]] but operates at a more granular level. The monster frame is for the major figure (the demon, the inner critic). The acting-out-child frame is for the present-moment emotion. The feeling that has been bothering the practitioner is doing what an attention-starved kid does: escalating until it is seen. The cure is the same as for the kid: give the attention. Not after the behavior changes. Not as a reward. Just give it.

This frame is what the entire script has been building toward. Rounds 1 and 2 establish that the feeling is something the practitioner can be present to. Round 3 establishes that the feeling responds to how it is being met. The closing question establishes what kind of meeting changes the feeling: loving attention given without prerequisite.

## Closing Instructions After the Inquiry

Joe gives the practitioner a few instructions for life-after-the-script:

**The questions can be improvised.** Once the basic structure is felt, the practitioner can ask their own questions of the feeling. "The inquiry can work with any question you have around the felt sense of this emotion, just as long as the inquiry is truly curious, as long as the inquiry has no agenda." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique. The script is training wheels; the actual practice is improvisational inquiry held inside genuine curiosity.

**Two failure modes to watch for:**
- **The inquiry stops being curious.** If the practitioner starts pursuing answers rather than exploring, the inquiry has become investigation. See [[wonder-vs-curiosity]].
- **The inquiry develops an agenda.** If the practitioner starts wanting the feeling to do something -- shift, soften, leave -- the inquiry has become management. See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].

**Repeat for triggering emotions.** "Oftentimes with a very triggering emotion, it's good to go through this exercise many times." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique. One pass is rarely enough for material that has been avoided for years. Multiple passes over time let the emotion reveal itself in layers.

## The Marker That the Practice Is Working

The closing image Joe leaves the practitioner with:

"Pretty soon what you'll notice is that you'll be out in the world and this feeling will come up, and you'll be excited that the feelings come up because you get to inquire more, you get to learn more about it. And you get to play with it some more." -- Joe Hudson, The #1 Emotional Processing Technique

The diagnostic for whether the script has done its long-term work: the feeling shows up in life, and the practitioner is glad. Not tolerant. Not coping. Glad. The arrival of the feeling is the arrival of more material to play with. This is the same internal test as [[can-you-look-forward-to-feeling-it]], delivered as the natural endpoint of the script-based practice.

## When to Use This Script (vs. the Conceptual Practice)

The script form is for:
- **Initial learning.** The practitioner who has not yet felt what emotional inquiry is. The script delivers the felt sense.
- **Hard emotions.** When the in-the-moment practice keeps slipping into management, the structured script provides enough form to hold the practitioner in inquiry until the inquiry takes hold.
- **Dedicated practice time.** When the practitioner has set aside time to work with a known difficult emotion. The script is the form of that dedicated time.
- **Teaching.** When walking another person through the practice for the first time. The script is more reliable than asking the person to "just be with the feeling."

The script is not for:
- **In-the-moment use during a triggered state.** The conceptual practice (see [[emotional-inquiry]]) is faster and more flexible.
- **Substitute for the relationship with the feeling.** Doing the script perfectly without genuine welcoming is the failure mode the script is designed to avoid. See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].

The relationship to [[daily-emotional-yoga]]: that practice is the scheduled dedicated time without a specific script. This script is one way to fill that time, especially for practitioners who do better with structure.

## Why the Sequence Is in This Order

The order is not arbitrary. The sequence tracks how somatic experiencing and occupational therapy approach a sensation: locate it first, then learn its properties, then play with the relationship to it. The sensory modalities (location, dimension, color, density, motion, edges, depth) are deliberately moved through to engage multiple processing systems in the brain.

The progression from round 1 to round 3 also tracks a deepening intimacy:
- **Round 1:** The practitioner observes the feeling from inside their body, mapping where it is.
- **Round 2:** The practitioner explores the feeling's properties and discovers it changes when engaged.
- **Round 3:** The practitioner enters into active relationship with the feeling -- speaks to it, listens to it, plays with it, loves it.

By the end of the script, the practitioner has gone from "there is a feeling here" to "I am in active loving relationship with this feeling." The arc is the practice.

See the corresponding section in [[emotional-inquiry]] for the underlying theory of why the three-stage architecture works (Joe's reference to somatic experiencing and the deliberate engagement of multiple sensory modalities).

## Tone

Joe delivers the script in a slow, even, spacious voice. The questions are asked without urgency. Long pauses are left between questions so the practitioner can find what is being pointed at. The tone is not therapeutic-grave; it is the tone of someone curious about a thing they find genuinely interesting, inviting the practitioner to share the curiosity. The third-round polarities are delivered with a light playfulness, especially the "you're an idiot, stop talking" line, which is meant to be felt as the absurdity it is.
