# Anxiety as an Unarticulated Need or Boundary

## The Practice

When anxiety arises, treat it as evidence that a need has not been articulated or a boundary has not been set. The anxiety is the system's pressure-gauge for a request that has not yet been made.

The practice has three steps:

1. **Feel the anxiety.** Do not try to think your way to its cause. Let it be in the body first. See [[fear-vs-anxiety]] for why the felt step has to come first.

2. **Ask: what need have I not articulated? What boundary have I not set?** The question is asked of the body, not of the mind. The answer often surfaces as a specific thing: I have not told them I need more time. I have not said no to this meeting. I have not asked for what I need to do this work.

3. **Name the need or set the boundary.** The anxiety usually quiets once the request has been made -- not once it has been granted. The relief comes from the articulation, not from the outcome.

Ant Taylor, applying this practice in his daily life after a year of work with Joe:

"If I'm feeling anxiety, many experiments on from this first one, that it typically means I haven't articulated something I need, either like a boundary hasn't been set, or I'm in that state of like managing people where I feel like, oh, I've got to go do something to bring something out of this person or protect them from something. And therefore I need to not talk about the things that I or the organization needs so that I can make sure they're okay." -- Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

## The Specific Form: Caretaking Produces Anxiety

A common shape: the anxiety arises when the person is in a caretaking pattern -- managing someone else's emotional state, holding back what they actually need to say in order to keep the other person comfortable. The unarticulated need is the need that got swallowed in service of the caretaking.

This connects the practice to [[caring-vs-caretaking]]. The caretaker chronically sacrifices their own needs for the comfort of others. The anxiety is what the body does with the swallowed need. It does not disappear. It becomes pressure.

The decoding: when anxiety shows up, ask "whose comfort am I protecting at the cost of my own ask?" Often the answer is immediate.

## Why This Practice Works

Anxiety is information that has not been heard. See [[fear-as-signal-of-care]]. The need being signaled is something the person cares about and has not protected. When the need is named -- to oneself first, then to the relevant other -- the signal has been received. The pressure-gauge falls.

The reverse is also true: when the need stays unnamed, the anxiety persists or escalates. The system is trying to deliver a message. It keeps trying until the message lands.

This is why anxiety-management techniques (breathing exercises, meditation, distraction) often fail to produce lasting relief. The techniques regulate the symptom without addressing the message. The message keeps arriving.

## The Articulation as the Move

The key insight is that the relief is in the articulation, not the resolution. The boss may say no to the request. The partner may not be able to give what was asked for. The colleague may not change. The anxiety still quiets, because what the anxiety wanted was the asking. The unasked need was the pressure.

This is empowering and uncomfortable in equal measure. Empowering because the person can act on the practice without depending on others to cooperate. Uncomfortable because the practice requires the person to ask things they have been not-asking, often for years.

The asking is itself the [[vulnerability]] move. The fear of asking -- of being told no, of disappointing, of being seen as needy -- is the same fear that has been holding the need unnamed. The articulation crosses that fear.

## Surface Need vs. Deeper Need: The Anxiety Hits the Wrong Layer

The first impulse from anxiety almost always points at the most surface form of the need, not the deeper one. Action taken on the first impulse addresses something, but rarely the thing that would actually quiet the anxiety. The practice has to be patient enough to let the deeper need surface.

The boss example. A common shape: a person is anxious that their boss will fire them. The first impulse is to perform harder, to be more careful, to never make a mistake. The surface need: keep the boss happy. The action: rigidity that actually makes the person worse at their job.

The deeper need is almost never addressed directly. The deeper need is for clarity about expectations, agreement about what success looks like, and freedom from the continuous fear of arbitrary judgment. The conversation that would meet the deeper need is one most people never have:

"Hey, to their boss, I noticed that I'm operating out of anxiety that I'm going to get fired at some point, and I want to not operate out of that anxiety because I'm gonna be more efficient and I'm gonna do better and I'm gonna enjoy myself more. I'm gonna burn out less. I'm gonna have more energy for you. So I'm wondering, can we get really clear expectations of what you want from me so that I know exactly what's needed to keep my job and that we can make an agreement that if I do this, then we get there. It's not happening. That just, that simple conversation. And that's an example of getting your needs met." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The conversation does several things the surface action cannot. It names the anxiety, which reduces the shame about having it. It converts the fear from abstract to concrete. It produces an explicit contract that the body can verify against. It opens the relationship from one-way performance to two-way agreement.

The point generalizes. Anxiety's first impulse is to address the surface fear. The deeper need is almost always relational, requires direct articulation, and is the thing that would actually quiet the anxiety. The practice is to slow down enough that the deeper need has time to surface. See also [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for why action on the surface impulse often produces the feared outcome.

## In Leadership

For leaders, this practice is high-leverage. Leaders chronically swallow needs to keep the team comfortable, the investors comfortable, the customers comfortable. The accumulated swallowed needs become a baseline anxiety that the leader treats as the cost of the job.

The reframe: that anxiety is not the cost of the job. It is a backlog of unmade requests. Each request that surfaces and gets made reduces the load. The leader who works this practice becomes less anxious not by becoming more skilled at managing anxiety but by becoming someone who asks for what they need as soon as the anxiety signals.

See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] for the broader pattern. The internal practice (decode the anxiety, name the need) is what makes the external practice (lead with the fear named, ask for what is needed) sustainable.

## The Daily Application

Ant describes the practice as a hack he uses in real time:

"When I feel the anxiety, I feel the anxiety, I name it. My hack is, is to name it, to my team, and then to find the need." -- Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

The sequence in action:
- Notice the anxiety.
- Name it ("I'm feeling anxious about this") -- often to the team, not just internally.
- Look for the need it is signaling.
- Make the ask or set the boundary.
- Notice that the anxiety has shifted.

The practice is fast once it is familiar. Early in the practice it can take minutes to find the need. With repetition the decoding becomes near-instantaneous.

## The Cross-Check: Is It Old Stuff?

Sometimes the anxiety is not signaling a current need. It is an old emotion surfacing in present circumstances. The practice has a cross-check for this:

If the anxiety seems disproportionate to the situation, or the named need does not produce relief once articulated, the next move is to ask what age the anxiety feels. Often the answer is young. The practice then pivots to feeling the old feeling rather than decoding the present need.

Ant describes a moment after firing someone when his anxiety was disproportionate. Joe's instruction on a phone call:

"Sit down for a second, close your eyes. What comes up?" -- Joe Hudson, recounted by Ant Taylor, Embracing Emotions

The anxiety was a child's fear masquerading as a CEO's stress. The decoding move was not "what's the need" but "who's feeling this." See [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]].

## Relation to Other Anxiety Frames

This practice sits inside a broader set:

The practices are not alternatives. They are the same move at different levels of specificity. This one is the most immediately actionable in the moment.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The practice is not solemn. It is a diagnostic, like checking a fuel gauge. The anxiety is data. The data points to a need. The need can be named. The practice has a rhythm to it that becomes second nature with use.
