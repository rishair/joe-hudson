# How and What Questions

## Definition

The primary technique layered on top of [[view]]. The practice is simple: ask questions that begin with "how" or "what" instead of closed or leading questions. This is the entirety of the VIEW technique. Everything else is the state of mind.

"That's the entirety of the technique is to ask how and what questions." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW

## Why How and What

How and what questions are open-ended. "Do you like ice cream?" produces yes or no. "How do you feel about ice cream?" might produce a childhood story, a favorite flavor, a dairy allergy, a memory of a pet cow. Open questions yield exponentially more data, which reflects genuine [[wonder]].

They also force reframing. "What is it that's making you such a dick?" is not a question people normally phrase. The unusual structure does something to the listener's neurology. It opens something.

## Why Not "Why"

"Why" questions are excluded for two reasons:

1. **Judgment carrier.** Most "why" questions carry implicit judgment. "Why wasn't this done on time?" is not curiosity. It is a demand for an excuse from a place of judgment.
2. **Unanswerable.** "Why is the sun?" is a question scientists cannot answer. "What makes the sun?" and "How did the sun get there?" are answerable. "Why" pushes toward abstraction; "how" and "what" produce concrete information.

See [[why-questions]] for the full anti-pattern.

## In Practice

The practice transforms conversations in a specific way: a person starts to form a statement or a closed question, pauses, finds what they are actually curious about, and asks it as a how/what question. The conversation goes in a different direction, usually more meaningful and productive.

In rooms of executives, after a first ten-minute VIEW conversation, Joe asks: "Have you ever had such a productive ten-minute meeting?" The realization is consistent: without an agenda, more gets solved than in an hour-long meeting with a direct report.

Open questions also serve the other person. They get to be heard. They speak to their own wants and needs rather than checking boxes. Often, being fully heard is all someone needs to release their resistance.

"Tell me a time that you were at your peak productivity and you felt unheard." -- Joe Hudson, Introduction to VIEW. Nobody can.

## Tone

How and what questions can carry any tone. "What's making you such a dick?" from VIEW (vulnerable, impartial, empathetic, wondering) lands as warm and open. The same words from judgment land as attack. The state of mind behind the question matters more than the words.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When practicing [[view]] and wanting a concrete entry point.
- When conversation defaults to closed questions or statements.
- When someone is in [[wonder]] and wants to express it.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Produces richer information than closed questions.
- Creates the experience of being heard, which dissolves resistance.
- Opens space for the other person's creativity and self-knowledge.
