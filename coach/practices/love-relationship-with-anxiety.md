# The Love Relationship With Anxiety Experiment

## The Practice

A time-bounded experiment: for a period of weeks or months, commit to being in a "love relationship" with your anxiety. As much as possible through the day, when anxiety arises, feel it in the body without trying to change it, without trying to do anything about it. Just be in contact with it, with appreciation rather than aversion. Notice how the anxiety moves through the system.

The practice does not require setting time aside. It runs in the background of normal life. The commitment is to the orientation, not to a specific exercise.

"It was an experiment that I wanted to do, which was to, as much as possible through the day, feel whatever anxiety I might be having. So to get in touch with it in my physical person. And just like, okay, just be in touch with it without trying to change it, without trying to do anything, just to like increase my awareness of how anxiety moves through my system." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

## The Two Commitments

The practice has two simultaneous commitments:

1. **Stay in contact with the anxiety when it arises.** Notice it. Feel it in the body. Do not try to make it leave. Do not try to figure out what to do about it.

2. **Hold it with appreciation, not aversion.** Treat the anxiety as something to be welcomed rather than tolerated. Look forward to its next appearance. Cultivate something like affection for it.

The first commitment alone is "being with." The combination of both is loving. See [[love-the-anxiety-not-just-with-it]] for why this distinction matters.

## What Joe Noticed

Joe ran this experiment for an extended period. His report:

"During it, I recall just an appreciation for the anxiety, the love for the anxiety, and that the more that I paid attention to it, the more that it transformed inside of me, the more it... just showed that it was life force. That's the best way that I can describe it." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The transformation he describes is not the anxiety going away. It is the anxiety revealing what it was underneath: life force, energy. The constriction loosens enough that what remains is visible as vitality rather than threat. See [[anxiety-as-constricted-life-force]].

## The Child-Seeking-Attention Mechanism

Joe describes the underlying mechanism with an analogy:

"It's like almost like a child acts one way if it's wanting your attention and acts another way when it gets your attention." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The anxiety has been clamoring for attention. The standard response is to try to make the clamoring stop, which the body reads as more rejection. The loving response is to give the attention the anxiety has been asking for. The clamoring quiets not because the anxiety has been managed but because it has been heard.

## The Experiment Does Not Last Forever

A nuance Joe surfaces: experiments come and go. The love-relationship phase does not need to be permanent. It can be run for a season and then released. The capacity it builds remains accessible later.

"You know, for whatever reason, experiments come for a while and then they go for a while and then sometimes they come back." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

This matters because some people approach the practice as a permanent identity ("I am now someone who loves anxiety"). The lighter framing is that this is an experiment to run for a while, see what it teaches, and then let evolve.

## The Brain's Hijack Attempt

The brain will try to convert the experiment into a strategy:

"If you start doing that process, if you're like, 'oh, where is the anxiety right now?' You're gonna notice that the anxiety loosens and like the life force is less constricted. And then the brain is gonna say, 'oh, if I'm with this, if I be with this, then I can get rid of it.' Which isn't actually addressing the underlying need, and then it stops working." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The hijack is the most common failure mode of the practice. The moment the loving becomes a method for making the anxiety go away, the loving stops working. The practice requires noticing when the brain has done this and returning to actual loving rather than loving-as-strategy.

The simple test: would you welcome the next round of anxiety? If yes, the practice is operating. If "well, no, I'd rather not have any more," the brain has taken over.

## The Companion Question

The practice pairs well with the question [[how-is-my-anxiety-right-now]]. The question functions as the regular check-in that keeps the practice running. Without it, the practice can fade into background intention. With it, the loving stays active across the day.

The cadence is informal: ask the question whenever it occurs to you, especially when you notice the anxiety is present. Feel what is there. Stay in contact. Continue with whatever you were doing.

## Why This Works

Three mechanisms make the practice effective:

1. **Direct contact replaces avoidance.** The standard relationship with anxiety is avoidance. Even noticing it is often a form of trying to address it. Sustained, non-instrumental contact breaks that pattern.

2. **The loving posture releases the secondary clamp.** The anxiety has a secondary clamp on top of it -- the resistance to it, the shame about it, the fear of it. The loving posture releases that secondary layer, which is often what was making the anxiety unbearable.

3. **The unmet need can be heard.** With the secondary clamp released and direct contact established, the signal the anxiety has been sending can finally land. See [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]]. The need can be named, and the relief that comes from naming completes the cycle.

## What to Expect

People who run the experiment often report:

- The anxiety does not disappear, but its quality changes
- The shame about being anxious dissolves first, before the anxiety itself shifts
- New information becomes available about what the anxiety has been signaling
- Energy that was tied up in the anxiety becomes available for other things
- A surprising affection develops for what had been the enemy

People also commonly report:

- The brain hijacking the practice repeatedly
- Periods where the practice feels effortless and periods where it disappears
- The practice working better in some life domains than others
- Renewed self-judgment when they notice they have stopped doing the practice

## In Coaching

A useful prescription for a client who has been struggling with anxiety for a long time and is ready to stop fighting it. The practice can be assigned for a defined period (a month, a season) with the explicit framing that this is an experiment, not a new identity.

Check-ins focus on what is being noticed, not on whether the anxiety has reduced. Reduction is not the metric. Quality of contact is the metric.

## Tone

Curious, experimental, light. The practice is offered as an interesting investigation, not as a treatment plan. The lightness is part of what allows it to work.
