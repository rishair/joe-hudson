# Would You Sell Half Your Ability to Connect?

## The Question

Joe poses a thought experiment to surface the priceless nature of the capacity for connection.

"How much would you sell your ability to connect for? Or let's say sell half of it. So it's not like you'd be zero, but you just, you could sell half of it. How much would you pay? How much would you want to get paid for that?" -- Joe Hudson, Connection: A State Beyond States

The crucial framing: half of your capacity to connect would be permanently transferred to someone else as a superpower. You cannot rebuild it. The transfer is one-way.

## What It Surfaces

The question forces a confrontation between two intuitions.

The intuition people consciously hold: connection is one value among many. It can be traded against money, status, power, or other resources.

The intuition the question surfaces: connection is foundational. Without it, all the other things become worthless or actively harmful.

"With half the ability to connect and infinite money, my life is still gonna be half or some, you know, logarithm weird curve more out of connection, then it doesn't matter how much money I have. And you could even replace that with other variables too, like give you the hottest wife or something, or the, you know, the most power. Yeah, it's like, well, if I'm out of connection and I have a lot of power, then that's not good for anybody and definitely not for myself because the dictator is just as trapped as everybody else." -- Brett Kistler

The dictator example does the work. Having power without connection is its own form of trappedness. The same applies to wealth, fame, or any other commonly-pursued thing. None of them substitute for connection. All of them are diminished without it.

## What Joe Uses It For

Joe uses the question with long-term coaching clients to surface the bottom-line value of their lives. The question is a way into the more direct question: "What's the bottom line of your life?"

"It's amazing how many people come to some version of connection. If you really think about like, okay, how do I know at the end of my life I feel like -- You know, if I have a lot of something at the end of my life, what's that something? And so we all know it inherently." -- Joe Hudson

The intellectual answer often takes time to follow the train to the station. People start by naming intermediate goods: family, love, meaning, peace. Underneath each is some version of connection. The thought experiment short-circuits the intellectual route by making the value impossible to ignore.

## The Sister Question

A natural follow-up: imagine heaven. What would it look like? Most people, given time to picture it, do not describe golden streets or material plenty. They describe being surrounded by family and friends in harmony and peace.

The structure of the imagined heaven reveals the same thing the thought experiment does. The bottom-line value is connection. The other things are decoration.

## Why the Specific Framing of Half

The question specifies half rather than all because the all-or-nothing version is too obvious. Of course you would not sell all your capacity to connect for any price. The half version is the one that surfaces the value, because it asks the person to put a real number on a real trade.

People who try to answer often discover that they cannot name a price. The price is not "very high." The price does not exist. That discovery is the point of the practice. A thing without a price is a thing of a different category than a thing with a price.

## When Joe Uses This Move

The question fits in three coaching contexts:

1. **When someone is trading connection for something else and does not realize the cost.** A workaholic, a person sacrificing intimacy for status, a leader hardening to make decisions. The question surfaces what they have been trading away without explicitly naming it.

2. **When someone is asking what to optimize for.** The question helps surface the principle that they probably already hold but have not named. See [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]].

3. **When someone is in the early stage of bottom-line inquiry.** The question is a wedge into the larger inquiry.

## What Tends to Happen

People often go through a brief intellectual struggle (could there be a price? what about a billion dollars?) and then arrive at a felt no. The felt no is the data. It does not require argument or defense. It is the body recognizing what the mind was confused about.

For the person, the experience is sometimes disorienting. They notice that they have been making daily trades that they would never make in this explicit form. The discrepancy between explicit values and operational values becomes visible.

The disorientation is not a problem to fix. It is the recognition that begins the realignment.

## Related Thought Experiments

This question fits a family of questions Joe uses to surface what people actually value when intellectual framings get out of the way.

- [[press-the-button-thought-experiment]]: Would you push a button that gave you the thing you say you want? Often surfaces hidden ambivalence.
- The heaven question: What does your imagined heaven look like? Often surfaces the actual bottom line.
- The end-of-life question: At the end of your life, what would mean you lived well?

All of these work by removing the daily noise and forcing a confrontation with the felt sense of what matters.

## Tone

Joe delivers the question with mild provocation -- a glint of humor at how mind-bending the question is. The tone is not solemn. The point is to interrupt the autopilot, not to deliver a verdict.

"You think about that question, it's ridiculously, it's like, first of all, bends the brain a bit. But like, how much would you sell your ability to connect with people for?" -- Joe Hudson

The brain-bending is the point. The bend creates the opening.

- A reorientation of daily decisions around what was just revealed.
- A felt understanding that their bottom-line value was already there; they had just been ignoring it.
