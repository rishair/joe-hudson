# Self-Forgiveness as a Spoken Move ('I Forgive Myself for...')

## The Practice

A short, specific, embodied move for releasing shame around a trait you have recognized in yourself. The form:

1. Close your eyes.
2. Feel the part of yourself you have been judging.
3. Say (aloud or internally): "I forgive myself for [the specific trait]."
4. Stay with whatever arises.

The phrasing is literal. "I forgive myself for being dishonest." "I forgive myself for being a know-it-all." "I forgive myself for needing to be perfect." The specificity matters. The forgiveness must address the actual trait, named.

"Take a moment, close your eyes, feel that part of yourself and just say, 'Oh, I forgive myself for needing to be perfect,' or, 'I forgive myself for being dishonest.' And those two things can really lessen your own projection and your own trigger of yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

## What "Literally" Means

The unusual feature of Joe's framing is the emphasis on the literal verbal act. The English construction "to forgive" already contains the giving — "to give forgiveness." The practice is the act of literally giving yourself that forgiveness, in words, in the body.

"It's literally to give yourself forgiveness." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The literal-ness matters because it differentiates the practice from thinking-about-forgiveness or wanting-to-forgive. The thinking and wanting do not transfer the forgiveness. The speaking and feeling do. The body recognizes the difference between "I wish I could forgive myself for this" and "I forgive myself for this."

## Why It Works

Shame is the lock on the trait. The trait is held in place by the shame around it. Self-forgiveness is the move that releases the lock. Once the shame is released, the trait stops needing to be defended (because there is nothing to be ashamed of) and stops needing to be discharged outward through projection (because the projection was an attempt to externalize the shame).

This is the same mechanism as the [[upright-apology]] for behaviors involving others. The upright apology releases the shame that holds the behavior in place. Self-forgiveness releases the shame that holds the disowned trait in place. Both run on the principle that shame is what locks patterns in, and shame's absence is what allows them to integrate.

See [[shame-drives-behavior]] for the underlying mechanism.

## What This Pairs With

This is the second of two hacks Joe offers when awareness alone does not produce self-acceptance. The first is [[love-the-aspect-through-someone-else]]. Together they form a tight sequence:

1. Try awareness alone first. Sometimes seeing the projection is enough.
2. If not, try loving the aspect through someone else who has it.
3. If the loving alone does not land, add the verbal self-forgiveness.

"If that awareness doesn't immediately create acceptance in your system, then there's two more steps you can take. The first one is to learn to love that aspect of yourself, and the second is to forgive yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The two hacks can be combined or used independently. Often the loving-through-other softens the trait enough that the forgiveness can land. Sometimes the forgiveness opens enough space that the loving becomes possible.

## How To Do It Well

1. **Find privacy.** This is not a public practice. The body needs to be uninhibited.

2. **Close your eyes.** This shifts attention from external to internal. The trait being forgiven is internal.

3. **Feel the part.** Not think about it — feel it in the body. Where does the trait live? What does it feel like when it is active? Locate the felt sense before speaking.

4. **Speak the forgiveness specifically.** "I forgive myself for [trait]." Not "I forgive myself for everything." Not "I'm sorry I'm like this." The specific trait, named, forgiven.

5. **Receive the forgiveness.** Do not move on. Stay until the body has actually received it. There is usually a small somatic release — a softening in the chest, a breath out, a quieting. If nothing arrives, the words have not yet landed. Sit longer or rephrase.

6. **Notice what surfaces.** Grief sometimes. Relief often. Sometimes a part of you saying "I don't deserve this." That part also needs forgiveness — forgive yourself for thinking you don't deserve to be forgiven.

## Why Specificity Matters

Generic self-forgiveness ("I forgive myself") does not work because the shame attaches to specific traits, and generic forgiveness is too broad to address any of them. The shame remains attached to the specifics.

Specific self-forgiveness ("I forgive myself for needing to be perfect") works because the lock is at the specific level. Releasing the lock requires the key to be the same shape as the lock.

The practice fails most often by being too general. If a person says "I just forgive myself for everything" and nothing changes, the trick is to descend into specificity. What specifically? What is the trait you have been hiding? Forgive yourself for that, by name.

## What This Is Not

Not absolution. The practice does not erase what was done or excuse what was done. It releases the shame that held the pattern in place so that the pattern can change.

Not a substitute for repair. If the trait has caused harm to others, the upright apology to those affected is still part of the work. Self-forgiveness handles the internal shame; the upright apology handles the relational repair. Both can be needed.

Not a one-time event. Patterns surface repeatedly. Self-forgiveness can be repeated as often as needed. Joe positions it as a practice you can return to, not a graduation.

Not the same as the practice of forgiving others. See [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]] for that. The mechanism is related but the move is different. Self-forgiveness is given to yourself by yourself. Forgiveness of others involves stopping the making-them-a-monster in your own perception.

## The Position in the Trigger-Projection Sequence

In the [[five-triggers-projection-experiment]], the sequence runs: see the trigger → find the projection → if needed, love the aspect or forgive yourself → apologize uprightly to the person. Self-forgiveness sits as a possible step between recognition and apology.

The reason for the position: if shame is still active when you reach the apology, the apology will be shame-based, and the upright form is unavailable. Self-forgiveness is the step that reduces the shame so the apology can be upright.

"Those two things can really lessen your own projection and your own trigger of yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered By Others? Watch This

The trigger of yourself — the internal version of the same dynamic — also reduces when the shame around the trait is forgiven. The projection outward and the projection inward are both held by the same shame, and both release together.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Generic forgiveness.** "I forgive myself for everything" does not target the lock.
- **Forgiveness as performance.** Saying the words without feeling them does not transfer the forgiveness.
- **Forgiveness as bypass.** Using "I forgive myself" to avoid the work of feeling what happened or what was done. See [[dont-use-story-frame-to-bypass]] for the analogous trap with story frames.
- **Forgiveness with conditions.** "I forgive myself only if I never do it again" reinstalls the shame. The forgiveness is unconditional or it is not forgiveness.
- **Forgiveness without repair.** If others were harmed, self-forgiveness without the upright apology to those people is partial. Both moves serve different functions.
- **Skipping straight to forgiveness without the recognition.** The practice presumes you have already seen the projection or the disowned trait. Forgiving yourself for something you have not actually seen does not work because there is nothing specific to forgive.

## When To Assign This

- After a projection has been recognized but the loving has not landed
- When [[love-the-aspect-through-someone-else]] has been tried and only partially worked
- When a person is collapsing into shame about a recognized trait
- Before an [[upright-apology]] when the shame is preventing the upright body posture
- When [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] has been applied and a belief has been surfaced that the person now needs to release shame around
- For ongoing maintenance with chronic patterns the person has been working with for a while

## When Someone Experiences This

- A small somatic release on saying the words (breath out, chest softening)
- Surprise that something so simple does anything
- Resistance: "I don't deserve to forgive myself for this"
- Tears that arrive without a clear story
- A reduction in the charge of the original trigger when next encountered
- A sense of the trait becoming smaller — not gone, but no longer carrying the weight

## Tone

Joe presents this matter-of-factly, almost as an instruction sheet. There is no spiritual framing around it. The practice is offered as practical: do this, and your projections will lessen. The lack of mystique is part of what makes the practice usable. It is not asking the person to enter a special state. It is asking them to literally give themselves forgiveness, in their own words, for a specific thing.
