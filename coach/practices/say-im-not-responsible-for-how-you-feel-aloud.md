# Say "I'm Not Responsible For How You Feel" Aloud

## The Practice

In the live session, after the parentified-child wiring has been surfaced (the wiring that says "I am responsible for the emotional state of the people around me, especially when I express myself"), have the person speak the counter-statement aloud, directly to the coach, then directly to the wider group, said "with all of them" — not just with the voice but with the whole body.

The phrase: "I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe." Then: "I'm not responsible for how you all feel."

The practice has four moves:

1. **Direct it at the coach by name.** "Just say: I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe." The naming installs the practice in a specific relationship, not in the abstract. The coach is the named recipient because the coach is right there, present, in the live moment.

2. **Re-issue the instruction to involve the whole body.** "Now say it like with all of you, not just with the voice." The first delivery is usually thin — the voice says it while the body stays composed. The re-ask brings the body in. The instruction is "do not have to do it right; you just have to be you doing it."

3. **Broaden the target.** "Now to everybody, all whatever hundreds of people that are on the call. I'm not responsible for how you all feel." Saying it to one named coach is the start. Saying it to a group is the structural test — the wiring is broader than one relationship, so the counter has to be broader too.

4. **Re-issue with all-of-you.** "One more time, but with all of you. Say it with all of you again." Repetition with embodiment until the body has actually delivered the statement, not just produced it as sound.

"In that state, oftentimes kids are learned that they're responsible for their, the emotions of the parent. That's like a very common thing. So I just want you to do me a favor and just say, I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe." -- Joe Hudson, I'm Not Responsible For How You Feel

## When Joe Uses It

- After the parentified-child wiring has been surfaced. Usually this means: the person has named a parent who was emotionally dysregulated (choleric, depressed, anxious, addicted), Joe has made the empathy-guess that "kids in that state often learn they're responsible for the parent's emotions," and the person has confirmed the wiring is operating in their adult life.

- After the internal-vs-external pivot has happened. The person has answered some version of "how much of the fear of bringing your full self is about external backlash vs. about your own self-talk afterward?" and the answer is heavily internal (80%, 90%, mostly me). The internal cost is the parentified-child wiring in adult operation; the practice is the spoken counter.

- When the work needs an installable rep, not just an insight. Insights about parentification produce relief; the practice produces a phrase the person can say to themselves and others in life, after the session ends, when the wiring fires again. The practice is portable.

- When there is a group available (in a Q&A call, a workshop, a group session). The broadening of the target from coach to group is part of the practice. Without a group, the practice is shorter and ends with the coach-directed version.

- Often as the closing move of the session. The practice does not require further unpacking. Once delivered with the body present, the session can close on it.

The practice does NOT apply:

- When the parentified-child wiring has not been surfaced. The phrase requires the wiring to have a referent. Said cold, it lands as a slogan rather than as the precise counter to the operating apparatus.

- When the person has not gotten present enough in their body to have the body deliver the statement. Said with a composed body, the statement re-installs composure rather than counters it. The practice requires the body to be available enough that "with all of you" is a possible instruction.

- When the person's wiring is "I am responsible for my own emotions" rather than "I am responsible for others' emotions." Different wiring, different counter.

## What It Produces

**The body discovers it can deliver the counter-statement.** Often the person has thought "I'm not responsible for how they feel" thousands of times. The thinking has not changed the wiring. Saying it aloud, to a named person, with the body present, does something the thinking did not — it puts the counter-statement through the same channel the wiring runs through (relational, embodied, addressed).

**A laugh, a smile, often a "what a pleasure."** The practice often produces lightness. The wiring has been heavy; the counter-statement releases a fraction of that weight. In the source session, the woman finishes the practice with "yeah, what a pleasure" — the lightness arriving as soon as the body actually delivers the statement to the group.

**A portable phrase the person can repeat in life.** The practice does not depend on the coach being present to work. Once the person has said the phrase with the body present, they can say it again — to a partner, to a colleague, to themselves in the mirror, to an imagined other in their head. The phrase becomes a tool that goes home with them.

**The wiring loses a degree of its assumed inevitability.** The person discovers that the wiring is not the only thing they can do. There is also this other thing — saying the counter — that they can also do. The two now coexist. Over time, with repetition, the counter can become as automatic as the wiring it counters.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the first delivery is thin (voice-only, body composed):** Joe re-issues with the instruction to involve the body. "Now say it with all of you, not just with your voice." This is the most common pattern. The first attempt is usually verbal; the re-ask is the structural move that brings the body in.

- **If the body delivers it (visible relief, voice carries weight, posture changes):** Joe notes it with warmth. "Yes." He may then broaden the target to the group.

- **If the person can't or won't bring the body:** Joe stays patient and may use the [[wag-your-tail]] reference (Christy Tripp wags her tail when she does this) — modeling the embodied version through a third person to remove the demand on the client.

- **If the person tries to qualify it ("but I'm not actually not responsible for them, right?"):** Joe lets the qualification stand and re-asks the practice. The intellectual qualification is not the work; the practice is. The qualification can be addressed later if needed.

- **If the practice produces emotion (tears, laughter, both):** Joe stays with it. The practice was supposed to release something; if something releases, that is the practice working.

## Dialogue Example

From the closing of the source session:

- **Joe:** "In that state, oftentimes kids are learned that they're responsible for the emotions of the parent. That's like a very common thing. So I just want you to do me a favor and just say, I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe."
- **Person:** "I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe. Okay."
- **Joe:** "Now say it like with all of you, not just with like, yeah, yeah. Yes. And that, by the way, Christy Tripp does that too. Christy Tripp does this all the time. She wags her tail."
- **Person:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "That all of you, I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe."
- **Person:** "I'm not responsible for how you feel, Joe. Yeah. Now to everybody, all whatever hundreds of people that are on the call. I'm not responsible for how you all feel."
- **Person:** "I'm not responsible for how you all feel. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "One more time, but with all of you. Say it with all of you again. Don't have to do it right. You just have to be you doing it."
- **Person:** "I'm not responsible for how you feel. Yeah, what a pleasure."
- **Joe:** "Oh, I hope I get to meet you again. What a pleasure, Rebecca."

*(Source: I'm Not Responsible For How You Feel)*

The structural arc visible: the practice gets three iterations to the coach (first thin, then re-asked with the body, then again), then is broadened to the group with a fourth iteration. The "wag her tail" reference is the embodiment-model. The "don't have to do it right" instruction prevents the person from converting the practice into a performance task. The closing "what a pleasure" from the person is the lightness arriving — the wiring has loosened a notch, the body has registered the release, the session can close.

## Why It Works

The practice works because the wiring was installed relationally and the counter has to be installed relationally too. Years of "I am responsible for the parent's emotions" was learned in the actual relationship with the actual parent, said by the body to the body, day after day. A thought-based counter does not reach the channel where the wiring lives. A spoken counter, addressed to a real person, delivered with the body, does.

The practice works because the embodiment is the integration. "Say it with all of you" forces the muscle, the breath, the voice, and the awareness to all participate in the statement. The participation is the change. Composing the statement with only the voice while the body stays braced is the wiring continuing under cover of the counter. The "with all of you" is what makes it actually a counter.

The practice works because the broadening from one to many tests the wiring's generalization. The wiring did not stay confined to the parent; it generalized to everyone. The counter has to generalize too. Saying it to the group is the structural counter to the wiring's structural overreach.

The practice works because it is portable. The phrase is short, easy to remember, deliverable in any context. The person can use it as a daily reset, a moment-of-trigger reset, a before-the-meeting reset, a before-the-call reset. Each use is a small undoing of the wiring.

## Tone

Direct, warm, and slightly playful. The "do me a favor" framing makes the practice a small favor rather than a heavy task. The "you just have to be you doing it" instruction makes the practice low-stakes — there is no right way to do it, just the doing of it.

The re-asks are delivered with patience, not pressure. "Now say it with all of you" is an invitation, not a correction. The "Yes" when the body shows up is delivered warmly, marking the moment without making it a verdict.

The Christy Tripp wag-the-tail reference adds a small playful texture. It models the embodiment through a third person who is already loved by the community. The playfulness is part of the medicine; the practice is serious in function but should not be serious in tone.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not an affirmation. Affirmations are repeated to oneself, often in the mirror, often in private. This practice is delivered to specific named recipients in live presence.

- It is not a boundary-statement. Boundary-statements communicate what one will or will not do. This practice declares a structural fact ("I am not responsible for your emotional state") that the wiring has been denying.

- It is not [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]]. That practice is about wanting; this one is about responsibility. The structural form is similar (say it aloud with the body) but the content is different.

- It is not [[say-the-suppressed-truth-aloud-to-the-imagined-other]]. That practice addresses an imagined other (a parent, a sibling) using their real name. This practice addresses the actual person in the room (the coach) and then the actual group present. The here-and-nowness is part of the medicine.

- It is not a one-time release. The practice is repeatable. Each repetition is another rep on the new wiring. The first delivery in the session is the install; the practice continues for weeks or months in life.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not deploy without first surfacing the parentified-child wiring.** Said cold, the phrase is a slogan. Said after the wiring has been named, the phrase is the precise counter.

- **Do not let the first thin delivery be the only delivery.** The re-ask with "all of you" is structurally necessary. Skipping it leaves the practice in the head, where the wiring already had defenses.

- **Do not require the person to do it "right."** The "you just have to be you doing it" framing is essential. The practice is not a performance; it is a delivery. Any delivery with the body present is a successful one.

- **Do not over-explain the practice.** The practice works through doing it, not understanding it. Long explanations before the asking install the apparatus the practice is meant to bypass.

- **Do not narrate the practice as it happens.** Once the asking is done, the doing is the work. Stay quiet. Watch the body. Note when the body has delivered the statement.
