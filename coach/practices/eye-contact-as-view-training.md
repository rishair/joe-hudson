# Eye Contact as VIEW Training (Bumpers on a Bowling Alley)

## The Practice

Eye contact during a VIEW conversation is a training tool, not a requirement. It raises oxytocin in both parties, which makes the [[view]] state of mind easier to access. Think of it as bumpers on a bowling alley: it keeps the ball in the lane more easily, but the goal is the strike, not the bumpers.

"What the eye contact is all about is raising the oxytocin in your system so that it's easier for you to find the place of view. It is just like a training tool. It's like bumpers on a bowling alley. It keeps you in the lanes more easily." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The practice is to use whatever produces the felt-sense of connection that VIEW operates within. Eye contact is one option among several. The substrate is somatic connection. The eye contact is one route in.

## The Alternatives

Joe lists several substitutes that work as well or better depending on context and person:

- **Holding hands.** Skin contact produces the same oxytocin response as sustained eye contact, often with less intensity.
- **Having a conversation while hugging.** The full-body contact creates a stronger somatic anchor than eye contact alone.
- **Touching each other.** A hand on a shoulder, knees touching, any sustained physical contact.
- **Gratitude before the conversation.** A round of explicit gratitude raises oxytocin without requiring physical proximity. Useful for groups and meetings. See [[gratitude-rounds-before-meetings]].
- **A few minutes of presence together** before talking, with no agenda.

"You could use holding hands. You could use having a conversation while hugging. Or just touching each other. You could have gratitude for each other before you start the conversation. And all of those things will help you be in VIEW more naturally until you start to have a somatic memory of view, of being vulnerable, impartial, in empathy, and in wonder." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

## The Underlying Principle

The work is to build a somatic memory of the VIEW state. Eye contact, touch, gratitude, hugs -- all of these are scaffolds for installing that memory. Over time, the body learns to enter the state on its own. The scaffolds become less necessary.

"So it's just a way to do it. It's also, for most people who get used to it, it becomes incredibly enjoyable as well." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The scaffolds are not pretenses. They are functional. The oxytocin response is real. The state they produce is real. They are not a substitute for the state; they are a route into it.

## What This Means for Neurodivergence

Joe explicitly acknowledges the limits of his knowledge here. Some people on the autism spectrum or with ADHD have different experiences of eye contact -- it may not produce the same oxytocin response, or it may produce overwhelming intensity that interferes with VIEW rather than supports it.

"There's definitely some folks that neurologically are atypical, and I don't know, I don't understand completely or have full knowledge of how some neurological atypicalness will affect eye contact and the experience of that for somebody in the body." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The instruction for anyone in this position:

"If eye contact is not possible for you, then find something that allows you to have that deep sense of connection that is not intellectual, that is somatic and/or physical, emotional, nervous system pleasure -- something that allows you to have that sense. Since the important part is that this is not just an intellectual thing." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The non-negotiable is the somatic/emotional/nervous system access to connection. The eye contact is one of many possible routes to that access. The principle holds even when eye contact does not work.

## When Avoiding Eye Contact Is Avoiding the Felt Sense

A subtle case: sometimes a person avoids eye contact not because of neurological atypicality but because the felt sense in their body is too overwhelming when eye contact is sustained. The avoidance of the eyes is the avoidance of the body.

"What I notice is some folks are avoiding the eye contact -- what they're actually doing is avoiding the felt sense in their body because it's too overwhelming. And so instead, just gently go into the body a little bit. Just gently go into the body a little bit and give yourself time and space. It's all good. Just whatever it does, whatever you need to do, go at the speed that allows it to be pleasurable for you." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

The intervention here is not to push more eye contact. It is to gently grow the capacity to be in the body. The eye contact will become available as the body becomes more familiar to the person. Pushing the eye contact before that capacity has grown is overwhelming and produces the opposite of connection.

The pace matters. "Go at the speed that allows it to be pleasurable for you" is the calibration. If the practice is producing distress, it has moved past the working edge. Back off, build capacity, return when the body is ready.

## The Underlying Universal

Joe makes a claim about the underlying need, separate from the form it takes:

"All of us -- I haven't really met anybody who doesn't want to connect. There are folks who connection is intense, and so they don't want to do it as much. But I haven't really met anybody who doesn't want to feel seen, who doesn't want to feel understood, who doesn't want to feel heard. That person I haven't met. And so that tells me that there's an inherent space in all of us that accesses VIEW, wants to access VIEW." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #2

This matters for the AI coach: the form of connection varies. The need for connection is universal. If eye contact is not available, the form changes; the need remains. The coach's job is to find the form that works for this person, not to insist on the canonical form.

Brett's addition: "Everybody wants to be seen and heard, and for some that might be felt and heard, that might be seen and felt." The vocabulary of being seen extends to being felt, being heard, being touched -- whatever modality the person's nervous system can receive.

## How to Use This Practice

1. **For most people in most contexts**, eye contact during a VIEW conversation is a useful default. Hold the gaze. Allow the oxytocin response. Let the somatic state of connection develop.
2. **For groups or settings where eye contact is impractical**, use gratitude. A round of gratitude before a meeting produces a similar effect.
3. **For people who find eye contact overwhelming**, substitute touch or co-presence. Use less rather than more. Build capacity gradually.
4. **For people with neurological differences**, find whatever produces somatic/emotional connection for that specific nervous system. The principle holds; the form varies.
5. **In all cases**, the actual practice is VIEW itself. The eye contact (or substitute) is a scaffold for the VIEW state, not the practice.

## When This Is the Wrong Move

- When eye contact is producing intensity that is preventing rather than supporting connection
- When the social context makes sustained eye contact uncomfortable or weird in ways that overshadow the benefit
- When the person experiences sustained eye contact as predatory or invasive (which can happen for trauma reasons unrelated to neurodivergence)
- When the relationship has not yet developed the trust to sustain eye contact -- jump too quickly and the practice can feel violating

In these cases, the scaffold is doing more harm than good. Switch to a different scaffold. The goal is the VIEW state. The route is whatever the body can use.
