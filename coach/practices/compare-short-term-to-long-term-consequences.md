# Compare the Short-Term Consequence to the Long-Term Consequences

## The Practice

When you know there is something you need to say or do and you are not doing it, the mind is usually focused on the short-term consequence. The mom will be upset. The boss will be disappointed. The partner will be hurt. The mind treats the short-term consequence as the load-bearing reason to avoid the action.

The practice is to list the long-term consequences of not taking the action alongside the short-term consequence of taking it. Make the comparison concrete. Name the specific costs of the avoidance over time.

Once the comparison is on the table, the right move becomes obvious.

"So yeah, if I'm going to be myself, I'm going to say something to my mom and she's going to be upset. That's a short-term consequence. Here's the long-term consequence. I want to say something to my mom and I'm not going to say it, and therefore I am going to constantly have a mom who thinks I should be different than how I am. I want to say something to my mom, but I'm not going to say it. And so now every time I'm around my mom, I'm resentful of her because she wants me to be different than who I am. I want to say something to my mom and I'm not going to -- I can't say it. And therefore, for the rest of our relationship, I know I'm not being honest with her. And so she doesn't even know who I am. She can't even love me because there's no me there to love. There's just this thing that I'm pretending to be in front of her." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

"So if you think about the short-term consequence of her being upset and the long-term consequences of all those things, it becomes really apparent what the right move to make is." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

## How To Run It

1. **Name the action you are not taking.** What is the specific thing you know you need to say or do? Be concrete. "Tell my mom how I actually feel about X." Not "be more honest."

2. **Name the short-term consequence.** What does the mind say will happen in the moment? "She will be upset." "He will be disappointed." "They will be angry." One or two specific reactions.

3. **List the long-term consequences of not taking the action.** Be specific. The list usually includes:
   - The other person continues to relate to a version of you that is not you.
   - You build resentment toward the other person for not knowing the real you (which they cannot, because you have not shown them).
   - You carry the dishonesty as a continuous load.
   - The relationship cannot deepen because the real you is not in it.
   - The other person cannot actually love you because the version of you they are loving is a performance.
   - The unspoken truth becomes harder to speak the longer it stays unspoken.

4. **Hold the two side by side.** The mom-will-be-upset moment versus the years of relating-from-behind-a-mask. The clarity of the right move arrives in the comparison itself. You do not have to argue yourself into it.

5. **Take the action, knowing the short-term cost is real.** The point of the practice is not to convince yourself the short-term consequence will not happen. It will. The mom will be upset. The point is that the short-term consequence is the smaller cost, and the larger cost is the avoidance that has been operating up to now.

## Why The Comparison Works

The avoidance is being held in place by a mismatch in salience. The short-term consequence is vivid because it is near, specific, and imaginable in the body (the mom's face, the boss's tone). The long-term consequences are abstract because they are distant, diffuse, and not imagined in the body (the years of resentment, the slow death of the relationship).

The mismatch tilts the scale toward avoidance. The practice corrects the mismatch by making the long-term consequences as specific and imaginable as the short-term one. Once the long-term costs are concrete -- not "I'll be inauthentic for years" but "my mom will never know who I am and I will be resentful at every family dinner for the rest of her life" -- the body can weigh them against the short-term cost in real time.

The right move is usually obvious once the comparison is in the body. The body knows which cost is larger when it can actually feel both.

## What This Practice Treats

This practice addresses the first source of anxiety in [[two-sources-of-anxiety]] -- the action you know to take but are not taking. Specifically, it addresses the version of the avoided action that is being held in place by fear of the short-term interpersonal cost.

The practice is particularly useful when:

- The avoided action is a conversation, a boundary, a request, or an honest expression.
- The person can name what they need to say or do, but the named short-term cost has been blocking them.
- The person is reporting anxiety, resentment, or distance in the relationship where the unspoken thing lives.
- The person has been not-doing the thing for months or years and treats this as just how it is.

See [[three-blocks-to-speaking-truth]] for the larger taxonomy of what blocks honest expression. The consequence-comparison move addresses specifically the "fear of consequences" block. The other two blocks (doubt and justifying conditioning) require different moves.

## The Relief That Follows

The action will produce the short-term consequence. The mom will be upset. The boss will be disappointed. That part is real.

What is also real: the anxiety relief that follows the action is usually immediate. The body recognizes that the truth is now in the room. The held position has been released. Even if the short-term reaction is bad, the long-term weight has been lifted.

"And you'll make the move and maybe it'll be upsetting, but I guarantee even afterwards, the anxiety will be relieved a little bit because you have been authentic. You've been who you want to be." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The relief is the system's confirmation that the right move was made. The mind expected the relief to come after the other person responded well. It comes from having spoken at all.

## Distinction From "Short-Term vs. Long-Term Partiality"

[[short-term-vs-long-term-partiality]] is a distinction about kinds of partiality -- fear-based grasping at immediate outcomes versus principles-based orientation toward a vision. That distinction operates at the level of how a leader sets direction.

This practice operates at the level of a single avoided action right now. It is the moment-by-moment cousin of the broader distinction. The principle underneath both is the same: short-term thinking and long-term thinking produce different right answers, and the long-term answer is usually the one that produces the life you want.

## Boundary Conditions

The practice assumes the action you are avoiding is actually a healthy, aligned action. The mom example works because being honest with the mom is the aligned move; the short-term consequence (the mom's upset) is the only price.

The practice does not work if the action you are avoiding is itself harmful -- if the "thing you should say" is actually a destructive impulse, an unprocessed reactive blurt, or a manipulation. In those cases, the practice can rationalize harm by listing speculative long-term costs of the restraint.

Check first: is the action you are avoiding the kind that, if you took it, would put you more in alignment with yourself? If yes, the practice fits. If you are not sure, do the alignment work first -- see [[am-i-in-alignment-with-me-right-now]] -- and then run the comparison.

## Tone

Direct, instructional. The practice is not a meditation. It is an analysis you do in the moment, with the body present enough that the long-term consequences can be felt as well as named. The tone says: you already know the right move; here is the move that lets you see it.
