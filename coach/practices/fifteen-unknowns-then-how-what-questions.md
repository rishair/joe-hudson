# 15 Unknowns Then How/What Questions (The Stress-Reset Wonder Practice)

## Definition

A structured pen-and-paper practice for converting a specific stressor into a field of [[wonder]]. The neurology refuses to hold fear/stress and wonder at the same time. The practice deliberately produces wonder about the thing that is producing the stress, by listing what you do not know about it and then turning each unknown into an open question.

The reset is not metaphorical. It is in your brain right now. As soon as the wonder arrives, the stress drops.

## The Instructions

1. Pick the single thing producing the most stress for you right now. Work, relationship, money, health — whatever the loudest stressor is. Write it at the top of the page.

2. Write 15 things you do not know about this stressor. Not opinions. Not strategies. Things you genuinely do not know.

3. For each of the 15, write one question that begins with "how" or "what."

4. Sit with the list. Notice what happened to the stress while you were writing.

5. Notice the action items that arrived that you did not have before — people you can now ask, threads you can now follow, experiments you can now run.

## Worked Example

Stressor: fighting with my wife.

15 things I don't know:
- What the experience is like for her in this fight.
- What the neurological causation of fights is in general.
- What I am actually scared is going to happen.
- What I am fighting for, exactly.
- What part of my ego is showing up in fights.
- (... continue to 15)

Each becomes a how/what:
- What is the experience of this fight like for her?
- How do fights work neurologically?
- What am I scared will happen?
- What am I actually fighting for?
- How does my ego show up in fights?

## Why It Works

The mechanism is [[wonder]]. Wonder and stress cannot coexist in the same nervous system. Stress runs the fear-control-resistance loop. Wonder is an open posture — there is nothing to defend in a question. The body cannot hold both states.

The act of writing the unknowns is also the act of pulling the brain out of the problem-solving frame that stress runs in. Under stress the brain narrows to "how do I solve this" with whatever cached solutions are available. Wonder reopens the aperture. The questions that get written from wonder produce better answers than the questions stress was asking.

Most stress-mode problem solving runs "I have these 6 things to do that everybody tells me to do." Wonder-mode problem solving runs "I wonder what exercise I'd want to do so much I'd get up off TikTok for it" or "I wonder how I could have a marketing campaign that makes me feel more connected to the people we're selling to." The first set is generic and effortful. The second set is alive and specific to your actual life.

## The Number 15 Is Not Arbitrary

15 is enough that you have to keep going past the obvious unknowns. The first 5 are the things you already know you don't know. By 10, you are reaching. By 15, you are finding unknowns you didn't know were unknowns. That last reach is where the aperture actually opens.

If you stop at 5, the practice produces a thinner shift. The depth of the reset tracks the depth of the wonder.

## The How/What Constraint Is Not Arbitrary Either

How and what questions are open questions. They cannot be answered with yes or no. They produce expansive responses. See [[how-what-questions]].

"Why" questions tend to slide into accusation or self-criticism. "How" and "what" stay clean. The question "why am I fighting with her so much" is loaded with "what's wrong with me/her." The question "what is the experience of this fight like for her?" opens a door.

## What It Produces

Three immediate outputs:

1. **Felt drop in stress.** Often noticeable mid-practice. By the time the 15 questions are written, the somatic charge around the stressor has dropped substantially.

2. **Action items that did not exist before.** The unknowns reveal people to ask, experiments to run, conversations to have. Most of these would not have been visible from inside the stress.

3. **The conversion of the stressor from object to field.** The stressor was a thing pressing on you. Now it is a landscape with edges, contours, and unmapped territory. The relationship to it changes.

## When to Use It

- When stress is concentrated on a specific situation rather than diffuse.
- When the standard tools (breath, walk, sleep) have not produced a shift.
- When the person is stuck in problem-solving mode and the solutions keep recycling.
- After a fight, before deciding what to do next.
- Before a hard meeting, instead of rehearsing arguments.
- As a daily practice during a high-stress period — pick the loudest current stressor each morning.

## How It Fits With the Larger Stress Work

This practice operates primarily on cause 1 of [[four-causes-of-stress]] — thought-generated stress. The thought "this is a problem I need to solve" is replaced with the posture "this is a field I am wondering into." The shift at the thought layer produces cascade effects in the other layers: the body softens, the identity loosens, the feeling becomes more available.

It is also a tactical instance of [[stress-as-rejection-of-stress]] — the practice is one way of stopping the rejection. Writing the unknowns is implicit acceptance that the stressor is here and that you are now in relationship to it.

For the conversational version (without paper, in dialogue with another person), see [[find-what-you-dont-know]].

## Tone

Practical, instructional. The practice is given as a recipe. The teaching is that the recipe works. The wonder is what wakes up when the recipe is followed, not what has to be summoned by force.

## Anti-Patterns

- Doing it as a should. The practice has to land in actual wonder, not as an item to check off. If the writing is mechanical, the wonder does not arrive and the stress does not drop.
- Skipping the writing and doing it in your head. The writing slows the brain down enough for the unknowns to surface. In the head, the brain races back to what it already knows.
- Treating the questions as problems to solve immediately. The practice is to be in the questions, not to answer them on the spot. See [[wonder-vs-curiosity]].
- Cherrypicking only intellectual unknowns. Some of the most powerful unknowns are about the other person's experience, your own body's response, what you are actually scared of. Include those.
