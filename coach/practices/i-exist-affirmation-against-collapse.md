# 'I Exist' (and Other Sentences Against Collapse)

## The Practice

When anger starts to move and then collapses (see Failure 1 in [[three-things-that-go-wrong-when-releasing-anger]]), find a sentence that pushes back against the collapse. Say it. Repeat it. Let it generate the friction that lets the anger continue moving.

The sentences vary by person. The function is constant: a verbal pushback against the felt oppression of the collapse.

"Just repeat one sentence like, no, no, I won't collapse, I won't collapse, or you can't stop me. Just whatever that sentence is that triggers the anger response. It's to feel the oppression of the collapse instead of the oppression of the anger, to feel the oppression of the collapse and respond to it and be like, 'No, I'm not going there. No, I will be empowered. You will not stop me. You will not stop me. You will not stop me from being angry.' That is a really good thing. It often has huge releases for people if they can just find that phrase in response to that very early childhood, 'No, you can't be angry.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

## Why It Works

The collapse is not random. It is the body re-enacting an old injunction, usually a childhood one: "no, you cannot be angry." See [[childhood-imprinting]] and [[three-childhood-origins-of-anger-repression]].

When the body collapses, the felt experience is that some force is pressing the anger back down. The force feels external because it is internalized but old. The sentence directs energy back at that force.

This is the inversion of the usual move. The usual practice with the voice in the head is to not fight it (see [[dont-fight-the-voice]]). But in collapse, the practice is the opposite: locate the specific old injunction that is pressing the anger down, and push back against it directly with a sentence.

The difference: the general voice in the head benefits from being welcomed. The specific childhood injunction that says "you cannot exist" or "you cannot be angry" benefits from being defied. The defiance is the act of refusing the injunction, which is exactly what was not possible as a child.

## Common Sentences

The right sentence varies by person and by the specific childhood injunction. Common ones:

- **"I exist."** Defies the injunction that the person should not take up space.
- **"I will not collapse."** Defies the felt force pressing the body down.
- **"You will not stop me."** Defies the injunction not to express.
- **"I am allowed to be angry."** Defies the injunction that anger is forbidden.
- **"I will be empowered."** Defies the collapse into helplessness.

The sentence has to land for the person. A borrowed sentence that does not match the specific old injunction will not work. The right sentence is the one that, when said, the body responds to.

"I exist" is one Brett's partner used. Joe describes it as a great one.

"My partner was like, 'I exist.' [...] It's just an affirmation, which is letting it move and getting out of the collapse." -- Brett Kistler, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

## How to Find Your Sentence

The sentence usually arrives when the person locates the felt voice of the original injunction. The internal voice that is collapsing the body is saying something. It is rarely literal at the surface. Underneath, it is some version of:
- "Be quiet."
- "Do not take up space."
- "Anger is bad."
- "Good people do not feel this."
- "You will be punished."

The sentence is whatever responds directly to that injunction. The form: "no, X" where X is the inversion of the injunction.

Sometimes the sentence finds itself. The body, given permission, produces the sentence that breaks the collapse. Other times the person has to experiment.

## How to Use It

1. Be in the process of releasing anger.
2. Notice the collapse beginning. The body curls. The breath shortens. The energy drops.
3. Speak the sentence out loud. Volume helps. The body needs to hear it.
4. Repeat it. Several times. With increasing force if needed.
5. Let the resulting anger move.

The repetition matters. The collapse has the weight of years. One utterance rarely reverses it. The sustained refusal does.

## Where the Sentence Can Be Used

The sentence is for the moment of collapse during release work. It is also useful in other contexts:

- With a partner, when the collapse is happening in the relational space.
- Out loud in a private setting (car, woods, ocean) as part of the regular anger practice.
- Internally in a moment where the collapse is happening publicly and cannot be voiced.
- As a chant during physical movement that helps the body keep moving.

"It seems like you could do that internally with your voice. You can do that with a partner. You can do that have that come out at society or around society in your safe place to express anger." -- Brett Kistler, The Wisdom of Anger Part II

## What This Is Not

This is not affirmations as feel-good repetition. The "I exist" affirmation is not a generic positive thought. It is a specific countermeasure to a specific felt force.

The difference: feel-good affirmations try to install a new thought. This practice unblocks an existing flow. The energy that wants to move is already there. The sentence is just clearing the gate.

If a person says "I exist" but is not in the felt experience of collapse, nothing happens. The sentence only does its work when it is responding to the felt force pressing the anger down.

## What This Produces

When the sentence works, the anger continues moving. Often there is a noticeable shift: the body opens, the voice gets louder, the held charge starts flowing. Joe describes "huge releases" when people find their phrase.

Often, finding the right sentence is itself emotional. The person has been carrying the injunction so long that finding the words to refuse it can produce immediate tears. The grief about all the times the injunction succeeded surfaces alongside the anger.

## In Coaching

When a person is collapsing during release work:
- Name what you are seeing: "I notice your body just curled. Something just pressed the anger down."
- Ask what the felt voice is saying: "What does the part of you that just collapsed believe about you being angry?"
- Help locate the inversion: "What is the sentence that responds to that?"
- Coach the practice: have them say it out loud, repeat it, let the resulting energy move.

## Tone

The practice is fierce when it lands. The sentence has to be said like the person means it. Whispered "I exist" rarely works. Full-voiced "I exist" often does.
