# The Seven-Day Be-Yourself Iteration

## The Practice

Every day for one week, do two things:

**Each day, look back at one regret.** Pick one specific thing you regretted (a conversation, a moment, a choice). Ask yourself: how could I have been in that situation in a way that I would not regret, regardless of the outcome? Not "what should I have said to get the result I wanted?" Specifically: what would have made me proud of how I behaved, even if the outcome were the same?

**Then go into the next day's situations and apply what you learned.** When you find yourself in a moment where you are worried about something going right or wrong, just be yourself. Be somebody you are proud of in that moment.

Iterate. Each day, you regret-review and you apply. Each day, you become a little more in alignment. Seven days of iteration. Seven days of experimentation. Then look at how much regret you are feeling at the end of the week.

"Every day for the next week, you think about one thing that you regret. And every day you think to yourself, how is it that I could have been in that situation that would have made me not regret it? No matter the outcome, no matter if I got a yes or no or rejection or acceptance, I just was 100% something that I'm proud of. And so there could be no regret. And then learn from that. And the next day you're in a situation where you're worried about something going right or wrong and just be yourself, be somebody that you're proud of in that moment. And then every day you're learning, every day you're iterating so that you become more and more in alignment and just do that for a week. So 7 days of iteration, 7 days of experimentation. Of being more and more truly yourself and notice how much regret you're feeling at the end of that week." -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

## What the Practice Is For

The practice is offered specifically as a proof-of-concept experiment. Joe sets it up that way: "Don't trust my word for it. I want you to actually do an experiment to prove it to yourself."

The teaching that frames it -- [[regret-dissolved-by-pride-in-how-you-behaved]] -- can be heard intellectually and then forgotten. The mind acknowledges the logic and continues running the contortion pattern. The practice operationalizes the teaching at a timescale (one week) short enough to commit to and long enough to produce observable change.

By the end of the week, the person has data. Either the regret-volume has dropped (in which case the teaching is confirmed for them by their own experience) or it has not (in which case they have something specific to bring back, usually about what made the experiment hard).

The practice is structured to leverage [[regret-as-fractal-conversation-day-year-life]]. A week is one scale of the fractal. The week-scale operating system is the same operating system as the life-scale operating system. A week of being-yourself iterations is a sample of what a life of being-yourself iterations would feel like. The sample is enough to make the larger pattern visible.

## The Daily Structure

The practice has a specific shape per day:

1. **Look back.** Choose one regret from the recent past. Not a list. Just one.
2. **Reframe the question.** Instead of "what should I have done to get the outcome?", ask "what would have made me proud of how I behaved, regardless of outcome?"
3. **Specify the answer.** The answer should be concrete. Not "I should have been more authentic." Specifically: "I should have said the thing I was holding back" or "I should have asked the question I was scared to ask" or "I should have admitted I didn't want to."
4. **Carry it into the day.** As situations arise where you notice the same kind of pull (the pull to contort, the worry about outcome), use the answer from the morning as the operating instruction.
5. **End-of-day review.** What did you notice? Where did you contort and regret it? Where did you stay yourself and not regret it? This becomes the next day's data.

The structure is iterative not because it produces a final answer but because each day teaches the next. The practice is learning-through-doing, not insight-then-execution.

## The Fears That Will Arise

Joe names the fears that show up when the practice begins:

"You're going to have all sorts of fears. Oh, I can't be myself here because I might have somebody upset at me, or I can't be myself because I'm going to feel too vulnerable, or I can't be myself because -- and you're going to have this long litany of things. But those are the things that you're going to regret. Those are the things that you're going to say, oh, I wasn't myself because I was scared of some result. But what you're actually scared of is the result in being yourself." -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

The fears are part of the practice, not obstacles to it. Each fear that surfaces is a specific instance of where the contortion-pattern has been operating. The fear says "I can't be myself here because X" -- and "X" is the specific cost the person has been paying to avoid the consequence of being themselves.

The practice does not require overcoming the fears. It requires noticing them, naming the consequence they are protecting against, and then choosing whether to contort or to be yourself in light of that specific consequence. Sometimes the fear is right and the consequence is one not to take on. Often the fear is overestimating the consequence. The week is for discovering which is which.

The reframe Joe offers: what you are actually scared of is not the result of being yourself externally; it is the result internally -- the feeling of having been visible, having taken up the space, having let the outcome land on the real you instead of the contorted you. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]] for the deeper mechanism.

## Why a Week (Not a Day, Not a Month)

A day is too short to see the iteration loop close. The first day might be the day the person tries it once and either succeeds or fails. The pattern that repeats across days does not show up.

A month is too long for the commitment to take. The person says "I'll try this for a month" and then misses day three and concludes they failed.

A week is the right unit. Long enough to see the pattern across multiple iterations. Short enough to commit to without flinching. The seventh day produces enough data to make the larger teaching real to the person, while still being a contained experiment they can complete.

See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]] for the broader principle about why experiments need to be done clean to produce data. The week structure helps the experiment stay clean.

## What This Practice Is Not

It is not a self-improvement routine to add to an existing self-improvement stack. The practice is specifically a one-week experiment designed to produce evidence. If at the end of the week the person says "this works; I want to keep doing it," they can choose to. The structure is not "do this forever"; the structure is "do this for a week to find out."

It is not a daily-journaling practice. The look-back is brief: one regret, one reframe. The point is not the introspection; the point is the operating-instruction for the next day.

It is not "act fearlessly." The practice does not require the person to take every consequence on. It requires them to notice the contortion-pattern, choose consciously about whether to do it again, and observe what happens. Sometimes the choice will be to contort; the practice does not forbid it. The practice surfaces the choice so it can be conscious instead of automatic.

It is not the same as the practice in [[dont-take-my-word-for-it]] -- though it shares the proof-by-experiment frame. This practice has a specific weekly cadence and a specific content (the regret-pride teaching); the other is a general principle about how to relate to teachings.

## What Tends to Happen

In the source teaching, Joe does not report on outcomes (this is a teaching episode, not a coaching session reporting results). But the structure of the teaching makes specific predictions:

- The first day or two, the person tries the practice and notices how strong the contortion-pull is. Many of the small interactions get done in the old pattern.
- By midweek, the person has begun to notice the contortion in real time, even when they cannot interrupt it.
- By the end of the week, there have been at least a few moments where the person chose being-themselves over the contortion and noticed both the felt-result (often invigorating, sometimes uncomfortable) and the actual external result (often better than predicted; see [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]]).
- The regret-volume by the end of the week is noticeably lower than at the start. Not zero. Lower.

The practice tends to produce one or two specific moments of "oh, that worked" or "oh, the consequence I was scared of did not happen" that anchor the teaching in the person's lived experience. From there, the practice may continue spontaneously without the explicit weekly structure.

## Tone

The practice is offered with an experimenter's invitation, not as a prescription. The frame "don't trust my word for it; do the experiment to prove it to yourself" sets the tone. The week is a manageable commitment that the teacher is asking the person to actually do, not just think about.

When the practice is given to a client, the tone is concrete and short. The instructions are simple enough to communicate in two minutes. The point is not for the client to remember every detail; the point is for them to remember "one regret, reframe it, carry it into tomorrow" and start.
