# Listen to Everyone as the Wisest Person You Know (For a Day)

## The Practice

A two-day experiment to demonstrate how much the quality of listening determines what gets shared, how honest people are, and what kind of world you live in.

**Day 1.** Spend the entire day listening to everyone you encounter with skepticism. Treat what each person is saying as something to be evaluated, doubted, contested. Notice what people share with you. Notice the texture of every conversation. Notice what you experience.

**Day 2.** Spend the entire day listening to everyone as if they are the wisest sage you have ever encountered. Treat what each person says as if it contains a teaching you need to receive. Notice what people share with you under this listening. Notice the texture of every conversation. Notice what you experience.

The point is not which mode is "right." The point is to feel directly how the listening shapes the world that arrives back to you.

## The Quote

"Spend one day going around listening to everybody with skepticism and then take the next day and listen to everybody like they are the most wise sage speaking to you and see how your world changes in a day." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

## Purpose

The practice demonstrates a structural fact: how you listen creates different stories that you hear. People give different levels of honesty, intimacy, and transparency to a skeptical listener than to a receptive one. The same person, talking to you in both modes, will tell you different things.

This makes the listening side of conversation a more powerful lever than the speaking side. Most people prepare for a meeting by thinking about what they will say. Almost no one prepares by asking how they will listen. The practice surfaces what is being missed.

"The way that we listen, basically how well we receive, how well we allow the other person to be, will create different stories that we hear. We'll get different levels of honesty, intimacy, transparency. Our whole world will be different by the way we listen." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

## The Underlying Mechanism

Listening is the receiving form of [[opposite-of-trying]]. It is not passive. It is the active orientation of letting the other person be exactly as they are without preloading the response. The skeptical mode is the trying mode — the listener is producing an evaluation rather than receiving the speaker. The sage mode is the receiving mode — the listener is letting the speaker arrive.

Brett's image: a child runs to the front door covered in mud, excited to show their mother the mud pie they made. The mother stands with arms on her hips. The immediate deflation in the child shows what the mother's listening just did to the child's offer. The same applies to adults, just less visibly.

"The quality of the listener does directly back-channel and impact the way that we share." -- Brett Kistler, The Opposite of Trying

## What People Notice

The most common reports after running the experiment:

- The conversations on the sage day are surprisingly different in content. People volunteer things they did not volunteer on the skeptical day, sometimes even when the person is someone the listener sees often.
- The energy load is different. Skeptical listening is heavier on the listener; the constant evaluation is metabolically expensive. Sage listening is lighter; receiving costs less than evaluating.
- The listener's own state shifts. Sage listening tends to settle the body. Skeptical listening tends to harden it.
- The world itself looks different. People appear more interesting under sage listening because they are sharing more interesting things. People appear less interesting under skeptical listening because they are sharing less.

The recognition that often lands: the listener has been listening with skepticism by default in many of their relationships, and the relationships have been shaped by that default. The practice reveals what the relationships could have been under a different listening.

## When to Use This

- When someone is trying to improve a relationship by changing what they say, and the actual lever is how they listen.
- When a coachee is asking how to be more connected with people and has not considered the listening dimension.
- When introducing the receiving side of [[opposite-of-trying]] and wanting a concrete personal experiment.
- When a leader is trying to fix team dynamics by changing what they communicate, and the receiving capacity is the missing piece.
- When a person reports that conversations feel flat or transactional and is looking for the cause.
- Before introducing [[view]] or [[empathy]] as concepts, to ground them in the felt difference.

## What It Tends to Produce

A recognition that the speaking side of conversation has been over-weighted in the person's attention. A first felt sense of listening as an active state with consequences. A new question becomes available: not just "what will I say in this meeting?" but "how will I listen in this meeting?"

For some people, the practice surfaces the discovery that they have been chronically skeptical-listening in important relationships — with a partner, a child, an employee, a parent. The recognition is often uncomfortable. The repair is straightforward: change the listening mode and stay with what arrives.

## A Variant for a Specific Meeting

The same practice compressed into one meeting. Before walking into a conversation that matters, decide explicitly: "In this meeting, I am going to listen to this person as if they are the wisest person I know." Do not try to perform agreement. Just receive what they say with that stance. Notice what gets said that would not have been said under your default listening.

## What This Is Not

The practice is not a manipulation tactic for getting people to disclose more. The receiving mode is real or it is not; people can tell the difference. Performing sage-listening while internally still skeptical produces only a slightly worse version of skeptical listening.

The practice is also not a permanent commitment to never being skeptical. Skepticism is sometimes appropriate. The point is to develop the receiving capacity so that it becomes available, not to replace one default with another.

## Tone

The practice is offered in the same tone as [[hands-together-exercise]] — as a small experiment with a surprising result. Joe does not make grand claims about what it will produce. He invites the listener to run it for two days and see what changes. The understatement is part of the design; the result speaks for itself once the experiment is done.
