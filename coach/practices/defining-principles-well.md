# Defining Principles Well

## The Practice

A principle is only useful if it can be used. The act of writing one is its own discipline. Four constraints make a principle functional rather than decorative:

1. **About five principles, no more.** Past five, you cannot remember them or wrestle with them well.
2. **Defined by what it IS and what it IS NOT.** A principle taken to its extreme often becomes its opposite.
3. **Felt in the body, not just understood by the mind.** Principles drive fast decisions; the body decides faster than the intellect.
4. **Memorable.** Use marketing techniques to make them stick.

Each constraint addresses a specific failure mode of poorly-defined principles.

## 1. Keep It to About Five

"It's really important to keep to about 5 principles. Not having too many is you don't remember them and you can't wrestle with them as well." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

Memory has a limit. A list of fifteen principles is a list, not a set of operating commitments. The discipline of paring down forces you to find the principles that contain the others.

Joe's own current five: connection, embrace intensity, everything is an iteration, wonder, loving accountability. Each is broad enough to cover many specific situations, narrow enough to produce specific moves.

The trimming is part of the wrestling. Two principles often consolidate into one. A principle that sounded right when first articulated turns out to be a special case of another. Five-ish is the discipline; the exact number matters less than the willingness to consolidate.

## 2. Define by What It IS and What It IS NOT

This is the single most important step. Any principle, taken to an extreme, becomes destructive or self-defeating. The principle must include its own boundary.

Joe's example: inclusion as a principle.

"Inclusion taken all the way to some end is very destructive and it isn't what's meant by inclusion anymore. So are we including murderers? Are we including people who are toxic for the environment? Are we including -- what are we inclusive of exactly?" -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

A useful definition of inclusion as a principle:
- **What it IS:** Being open to different points of view from different backgrounds. Seek to understand before you seek to prove wrong.
- **What it IS NOT:** Giving voice to people who are not contributing. Including things that are outside the other principles.

Without the "what it is not," the principle drifts. People who weaponize the principle do so by pushing it to the boundary the principle did not name.

Joe's own example with "everything is an iteration":
- **What it IS:** Constant learning, no shame about getting it wrong, treat the result as data for the next iteration.
- **What it IS NOT:** "Just iterate, don't care about quality." That is a misapplication. The principle does not justify shipping garbage. The right framing: "We're going to do our best work, but we're going to see that the best work is going to have problems and that to get it done and test it is more important than to try to get it perfect."

The principle must contain the limit. Without the limit, the principle either gets ignored (because its extreme is obviously wrong) or gets weaponized (because its extreme can be used to justify what people wanted to do anyway).

## 3. Felt Sense in the Body

A principle that lives only in the intellect is too slow for real-time decisions. The body decides faster. Principles must be installed somatically.

"What does it feel like to be in everything is an iteration? What does it feel like to be embracing intensity? So there's a somatic response for me to really feeling into what it is to live in that principle. What's the state that it brings me to and what's the state it doesn't bring me to? So I don't have just an intellectual one. I have a felt sense of it because the felt sense is what helps me make decisions quicker than just an intellectual one." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The transparency example illustrates the felt-sense work:

"If transparency is my principle, there's a very clear feeling that I have when I'm hiding something. There's a very clear feeling that I'm like... and I'm sneaky. There's a very sneaky feeling. And so for me, transparency very much is like, oh, this -- and transparency also, if I'm living it at its very edge, then there's a little bit of fear. Like, to allow, you know, to open the kimono fully, so to speak, to video that thing, there's going to be a little bit of fear to it. So, I know like, 'Oh, that's the edge of my transparency.' Whereas being transparent in a way that hurts somebody wouldn't feel good in my system and it wouldn't be an edge of like, 'Oh my gosh.' It wouldn't be like walking into a giant room and being on a stage. It would be like hurting somebody and that would be a very different somatic experience." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

Notice what the felt sense distinguishes:
- The body feels the violation of the principle (sneaky)
- The body feels the edge of the principle (fear, the giant-room sense)
- The body feels the misapplication of the principle (hurting someone -- different somatic signature)

This is the diagnostic value of the somatic anchor. The body knows which one is happening before the mind can articulate it.

## 4. Make Them Memorable

The principle has to compete with everything else for attention. If it cannot be summoned in a moment of decision, it does not exist for that moment.

"You're trying to affect behavior. And so, you need to use some marketing techniques. Marketers are really good at affecting behavior, right? So, for me, making them memorable, everything is an iteration, loving accountability, these are things that are very wonder. These are things that are very memorable to me. And so, I'm more apt to use them and to make them memorable." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

Techniques that produce memorability:
- **Repetition.** "Connection, connection, connection." The triple naming both emphasizes and tracks the three places (start, middle, end of any interaction) where connection applies.
- **Punctuation as feel.** "Wonder!" with the exclamation point carries the energy of the state. The punctuation is part of the principle.
- **Short phrases.** "Loving accountability." Two words that combine to point at a specific stance.
- **Emojis or symbols in organizations.** Some companies create emoji shortcuts for their principles so people can reference them quickly in chat.

The marketing frame is not vanity. It is the recognition that a principle that is not memorable is a principle that is not used.

## The Daily Test

Once a principle is defined, the only way to refine it is by use. The practice is to run an experiment with each principle every day, sometimes multiple times per day, and observe what works.

"Every day you do an experiment. Every day, maybe 2 or 3 times a day you do an experiment. How did that work? What worked for me? Does it work overall?" -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The experimentation produces three things:
1. Refinement of the principle's edges (what it is, what it is not)
2. Refinement of the somatic anchor (you know more specifically what the felt sense is)
3. Evidence about whether the principle is actually serving the life it was meant to serve

Without daily experimentation, the principle stays abstract. The definition is only the starting point. The use is what makes it usable.

## Why This Is Hard

The temptation is to write the principles once, post them, and feel finished. The four constraints all demand more work:

- **Five-max** requires hard consolidation. Most people draft fifteen.
- **What-it-is-AND-what-it-isn't** requires anticipating the edges and the misapplications.
- **Felt sense** requires somatic attention, which most people skip.
- **Memorable** requires editing, which most people resent.

A principle written without these constraints will not survive contact with real decisions. It will be remembered as a slogan and ignored when the slogan does not match the moment.
