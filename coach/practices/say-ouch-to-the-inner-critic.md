# Say 'Ouch' Out Loud to the Inner Critic (Make the Attack Audible to Yourself)

## The Practice

A simple, immediate experiment that surfaces how often the inner voice is attacking. The instructions:

1. Close your eyes.
2. Listen to whatever the voice in your head has to say.
3. Anything it says that is negative — "you'll never get out of it," "you can't do it," "you have to do this and this and this," anything that "a really crappy boss would say to you" — respond by saying "ouch" out loud.
4. Every time the voice says something mean or not nice or abusive or super micromanaging, say "ouch."

The experiment runs for a short period — a minute, five minutes, however long the person can hold attention. The point is not endurance. The point is discovery.

"In this experiment, we're just going to learn a little something about the negative self-talk in our head. So it's a really simple experiment. You're going to close your eyes and you're going to listen to whatever the voice in your head has to say. Just simple. And anything that it says negative, if it says, 'You'll never get out of it. You can't do it. You have to do this and this and this,' anything that a really crappy boss would say to you, you're going to just respond by saying, 'Ouch,' out loud. So every time it says something mean or not very nice or abusive or just super micromanaging, you just keep saying ouch. By this time, you'll notice that the voice in the head is saying negative stuff to you often, and that's stressful. It's just stressful to live with something that's talking to you like that all the time." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Causes of Stress

## What It Demonstrates

Most people do not realize how constant the inner critical voice is. They have lived with it for so long that it is background. The voice runs at the level of "the way things are" rather than at the level of a discrete experience that can be observed.

The "ouch" makes the voice audible to the person. Each "ouch" is a marker that the voice just delivered an attack. The number of "ouches" in a short period is data — and the data is almost always shocking.

The teaching: "you'll notice that the voice in the head is saying negative stuff to you often, and that's stressful. It's just stressful to live with something that's talking to you like that all the time."

The discovery is structural. The person realizes that a significant portion of their stress is coming from this internal source, not from external circumstances. The boss did not yell at them. The voice did. The customer was not rude. The voice was. The activity that "I should be enjoying but I'm not" — the voice is what is making it un-enjoyable.

## The Variant That Speaks the Voice's Content First, Then "Ouch"

A specific variant of the practice has the listener speak the voice's actual statement aloud first, then say "ouch" after it. The instruction:

1. Sit down, close your eyes.
2. Listen to the voice in your head.
3. **Speak out loud whatever it says.**
4. Then say "ouch" after each critical thing.

The added step — speaking the voice's content aloud before the "ouch" — does the work [[speaking-the-voice-out-loud-engages-the-whole-brain]] describes. The internal-only thought activates the prefrontal cortex; speaking it aloud activates additional brain regions including auditory processing. The same content lands as a different kind of event because it is now being heard, not just thought. The audibility produces the recognition that internal noticing cannot produce on its own.

"You can sit down, close your eyes, listen to the voice in your head, speak whatever it says out loud, and then just say 'Ouch,' after everything that it says that's at all critical. And that does two things. So first is, neurologically speaking, if we have a thought and it's just inside and we don't speak it, it only activates our prefrontal cortex. But if we speak it, it activates other parts of our brain. So when we speak the internal critic, we all of a sudden can hear it in a different light. Like, holy crap, that's abusive as shit. Like, you'll just hear it, you'll be like, what is going on? Like, I would never talk to a friend like this ever." -- Joe Hudson, 1 Word That Changes Your Inner Critic

The recognition the variant produces is the friend-test recognition: "I would never talk to a friend like this ever." The asymmetry between what the listener tolerates from themselves and what they would tolerate from anyone else becomes structurally visible. This is also the doorway into [[extend-the-no-asshole-rule-inward]] — the rule the person already has for external speech can now be applied to the internal speech that has been operating outside the rule.

The bare-ouch version of the practice (without speaking the voice's content first) is the lighter on-ramp. The speak-then-ouch version is the more powerful version because it does the audibility work on both ends: the attack is voiced, then the noting is voiced.

## Why This Belongs in the Stress Framework

The experiment is the thought-level intervention in [[four-causes-of-stress]]. The first cause of stress is thought, and one of the four sub-forms of thought-stress is the critical voice. The "ouch" experiment is the live, immediate way to make the critical-voice cause visible.

It is also a precondition for the deeper work on the voice. You cannot work on something you cannot see. The first job is to see it. The "ouch" is the seeing-tool.

Once the voice is visible, the work can proceed to:

- [[experimenting-with-the-voice]]: a broader set of practices for shifting the relationship to the voice
- [[inner-critic-prevents-rest-and-integration]]: the principle that softening the voice accelerates everything else
- [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]]: the deeper recognition that the voice is often a parent's voice that was internalized

## How It Differs From the Other "Ouch" Practice

There is a related move at [[say-ouch]] — saying "ouch" in response to passive aggression or criticism from another person. The two share a name and an underlying logic (the "ouch" names the hit without retaliating) but they are different practices.

[[say-ouch]] is interpersonal: saying "ouch" out loud to another person to name what just happened in the relationship.

This practice is intrapersonal: saying "ouch" out loud to make audible to yourself how the inner voice has been attacking. The "ouch" is for you, not for the voice. The voice does not care that you said "ouch." You care, because you are noticing.

Both are useful. They are not the same.

## What Tends to Happen

When people do this experiment, common observations:

- The frequency of "ouches" is much higher than expected
- The content of the attacks is sometimes more brutal than expected (people are often saying things to themselves that they would never accept from a boss)
- A subtle shift after a few minutes — the voice can become less constant once it is being noticed
- A felt sense of compassion arising for the part of self that has been receiving these attacks
- Sometimes anger at the voice, sometimes grief, sometimes humor

Each of these is data. The point is not to feel any particular way. The point is to make visible what has been invisible.

## Variations

The experiment can be extended:

- Do it for an hour with a notebook, writing down each attack
- Do it during a specific task you usually find stressful, to see what the voice is doing during that task
- Pair it with a question: after each "ouch," ask "whose voice is that?" to surface the original source
- Do it as a couple practice — each person says "ouch" out loud and the other hears it

The "whose voice is that" variation connects to [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]]: the inner voice is almost always an internalized voice from a primary caregiver. Naming the voice — "that's mom's voice," "that's dad's voice," "that's my coach's voice" — is the next layer of the work after the noticing layer this experiment opens.

## The Extended Phrasing: "Ouch, That Hurts. Please Don't Treat Me Like That."

A variant Joe also uses, with the same logic but a second clause appended that does additional work:

"Ouch, that hurts. Please don't treat me like that." -- Joe Hudson, End Toxic Relationship Patterns

The form Joe prescribes around this variant: 20 minutes a day, listening to the voice, every time it says something — "ouch, that hurts. Please don't treat me like that." The instruction is matter-of-fact about how immediate the demonstration is. He notes that closing your eyes for 30 seconds and listening will produce the voice without effort, "going to say something that you would never say to another person... something that's just mean."

What the extended clause adds beyond the bare "ouch":

- **The "that hurts" specifies what the ouch is for.** The bare "ouch" notes the hit. The "that hurts" extends the noting to the felt cost of the hit. This activates the [[awareness-of-pain-stops-the-behavior]] mechanism: the body registers the cost, not just the event. The voice continuing to deliver attacks while the body is feeling each attack as painful is the condition under which the voice's habitual content begins to lose its automaticity.
- **The "please don't treat me like that" addresses the voice as a counterparty.** This is the same operation as the "ask it to speak to you with respect" entry in [[experimenting-with-the-voice]]. The voice has been operating as an unquestioned narrator. The request reframes it as someone in a relationship with you who can be asked for different behavior. The reframe is what allows the relationship to be flexible.
- **The "please" carries no fight.** The earlier version in [[dont-fight-the-voice]] warns that the voice cannot be used to silence the voice; the request form with "please" deliberately does not match the voice's energy. The body asks plainly, the way a body would ask anyone for less aggression. The asymmetry — the voice attacking, the request meeting it gently — is part of what produces the change.

The extended phrasing is therefore not just longer; it is doing more work. The bare "ouch" surfaces the cost. The extended version surfaces the cost, registers the feeling, and addresses the voice. All three operations together are what make the practice fast-acting.

Joe's claim about the speed: "as soon as you hear it, it's going to say something that you would never say to another person... and you're going to go, 'Ouch, that hurts. Please don't talk to me like that.' And it's as simple as that. So you do that, and immediately you'll — it'll start becoming quieter." -- Joe Hudson, End Toxic Relationship Patterns

The 20-minutes-a-day frame is also the operational form Joe gives. The instruction is not "do this every time the voice fires throughout the day." That instruction would convert the practice into a constant project that would itself become a should. The instruction is to dedicate 20 minutes — a contained window — during which the practice can happen with full attention. The contained window does the work; the rest of the day will reflect the work without requiring the explicit practice everywhere.

## The Practice as the Way Into Rest

A specific framing of the practice that arrives in the rest-teaching context: the practice is also the most direct way for a chronically restless person to actually get rest. For most people, the obstacle to rest is not external circumstance. It is the inner voice that fires the moment the body tries to settle. The voice says "you haven't done enough," "you should be doing something," "you're falling behind." The body re-activates. No rest happens.

The "ouch, please don't treat me like that" practice, applied in the moment the body is trying to rest, addresses the obstacle directly. The voice fires; the response meets it; the voice quiets; the body settles. The practice does not have to be done at a special meditation time. It can be done while sitting on the couch, lying in bed, or in any moment the person is trying to rest and the voice is preventing it.

"Just close your eyes, hang out, and listen to the voice. And every time it says something that you wouldn't say to somebody else, say, 'Ouch, ouch, please don't treat me like that.' What's going to happen is that voice is going to get quieter and quieter. It's going to go into the background and you're going to get some rest. Maybe you'll fall asleep, maybe you'll just enter spaciousness, something will happen, but it'll become less and less." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

This rest-context framing makes explicit what was implicit in the earlier teachings: the practice is not just about reducing the voice's impact in general. It is the operational instrument for accessing the specific kind of rest the voice has been blocking. The natural follow-on is either falling asleep, entering spaciousness, or the inspiration to act that signals the rest actually worked. See [[rest-that-satisfies-produces-inspiration]] and [[inner-critic-prevents-rest-and-integration]].

The practice in this framing also pairs with [[feel-the-part-of-you-already-at-rest]] as the natural sequence: quiet the voice with "ouch," then notice the part of yourself that has been at rest all along. The voice was the only thing preventing the noticing.

## When Joe Uses This

The experiment is used in teaching contexts to demonstrate the cost of the critical voice. It is also useful in coaching when:

- The person reports being stressed without a clear external cause
- The person says "I don't know why I am so hard on myself" — the experiment makes the harshness visible
- The person is in the early stages of inner-critic work and needs to see the pattern
- The person has been told the voice is critical but does not yet believe it
- The person has just been confronted with [[every-question-becomes-whats-wrong-with-me|the deficiency lens]] and needs the operational tool that interrupts each appearance of the lens

For coaching, the experiment can be done in-session for a minute and then discussed. The number of "ouches" in a single minute is often enough to make the point.

A common in-session use is the ouch installed mid-session as the immediate practice the person takes home. The client in the You Don't Have A Money Problem session asked "Is that helpful if I just start [saying ouch every day]?" Joe: "Yes. Yeah, really helpful." The installation moment is short — the client adopts the practice in the seconds after the lens has been named. The practice then becomes the daily continuation of the session's work between meetings.

In-session, the ouch also serves as the self-catch tool when a positive reframe arrives with a hidden qualifier. The client in the same session produced a positive statement — "I am going to have the freedom that I want" — and then caught the qualifier "Eventually" themselves with "Ouch." The practice had been installed minutes earlier and was already operating as the self-noticing capacity. See [[catch-the-tiny-self-doubt-inside-the-answer]] for the move that pairs with this self-catch.

## Anti-Patterns

Do not turn the experiment into a self-attack. "I had so many ouches, I am terrible at this" is the critical voice doing its work on the experiment itself. Notice that, say "ouch" to it, and keep going.

Do not try to stop the voice during the experiment. The goal is to notice, not to suppress. Trying to stop the voice gives the voice more material ("you can't even stop your inner critic, what's wrong with you"). Just notice.

Do not skip saying "ouch" out loud. The audibility matters. Saying it silently keeps the attack inside the same internal space that produced it. Saying it out loud breaks the loop by making the attack visible in physical space.

## What Comes Next

After the experiment, the natural extensions:

- A broader experimental relationship with the voice (see [[experimenting-with-the-voice]])
- The fuller menu of responses to the voice — including "I see that you're scared and I'm right here with you," "I wonder if that's true," and "give it the love and compassion you want" — each doing different work for different moments (see [[menu-of-responses-to-the-self-critical-voice]])
- Inquiry into where the voice came from (see [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]])
- Practices that soften the voice over time (see [[three-month-inherent-goodness-experiment]])
- Recognition that softening the voice is upstream of nervous-system rest (see [[inner-critic-prevents-rest-and-integration]] and [[pleasure-tells-system-its-safe]])

The "ouch" experiment is the opening move — the diagnostic that makes the voice audible as a thing that is attacking, not as the way things are. Once that surfacing is done, the menu of other responses becomes available to deploy. The work that follows is what changes the voice over time.

## Tone

Playful, immediate, slightly humorous. The "ouch" is itself a small bit of comic relief — the voice says something mean, you go "ouch," it is almost funny. The humor is part of how the practice works. The voice loses some of its weight when it is met with this small, almost theatrical noticing.
