# Say 'Ouch' Out Loud to the Inner Critic (Make the Attack Audible to Yourself)

## The Practice

A simple, immediate experiment that surfaces how often the inner voice is attacking. The instructions:

1. Close your eyes.
2. Listen to whatever the voice in your head has to say.
3. Anything it says that is negative — "you'll never get out of it," "you can't do it," "you have to do this and this and this," anything that "a really crappy boss would say to you" — respond by saying "ouch" out loud.
4. Every time the voice says something mean or not nice or abusive or super micromanaging, say "ouch."

The experiment runs for a short period — a minute, five minutes, however long the person can hold attention. The point is not endurance. The point is discovery.

"In this experiment, we're just going to learn a little something about the negative self-talk in our head. So it's a really simple experiment. You're going to close your eyes and you're going to listen to whatever the voice in your head has to say. Just simple. And anything that it says negative, if it says, 'You'll never get out of it. You can't do it. You have to do this and this and this,' anything that a really crappy boss would say to you, you're going to just respond by saying, 'Ouch,' out loud. So every time it says something mean or not very nice or abusive or just super micromanaging, you just keep saying ouch. By this time, you'll notice that the voice in the head is saying negative stuff to you often, and that's stressful. It's just stressful to live with something that's talking to you like that all the time." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Causes of Stress

## What It Demonstrates

Most people do not realize how constant the inner critical voice is. They have lived with it for so long that it is background. The voice runs at the level of "the way things are" rather than at the level of a discrete experience that can be observed.

The "ouch" makes the voice audible to the person. Each "ouch" is a marker that the voice just delivered an attack. The number of "ouches" in a short period is data — and the data is almost always shocking.

The teaching: "you'll notice that the voice in the head is saying negative stuff to you often, and that's stressful. It's just stressful to live with something that's talking to you like that all the time."

The discovery is structural. The person realizes that a significant portion of their stress is coming from this internal source, not from external circumstances. The boss did not yell at them. The voice did. The customer was not rude. The voice was. The activity that "I should be enjoying but I'm not" — the voice is what is making it un-enjoyable.

## Why This Belongs in the Stress Framework

The experiment is the thought-level intervention in [[four-causes-of-stress]]. The first cause of stress is thought, and one of the four sub-forms of thought-stress is the critical voice. The "ouch" experiment is the live, immediate way to make the critical-voice cause visible.

It is also a precondition for the deeper work on the voice. You cannot work on something you cannot see. The first job is to see it. The "ouch" is the seeing-tool.

Once the voice is visible, the work can proceed to:

- [[experimenting-with-the-voice]]: a broader set of practices for shifting the relationship to the voice
- [[inner-critic-prevents-rest-and-integration]]: the principle that softening the voice accelerates everything else
- [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]]: the deeper recognition that the voice is often a parent's voice that was internalized

## How It Differs From the Other "Ouch" Practice

There is a related move at [[say-ouch]] — saying "ouch" in response to passive aggression or criticism from another person. The two share a name and an underlying logic (the "ouch" names the hit without retaliating) but they are different practices.

[[say-ouch]] is interpersonal: saying "ouch" out loud to another person to name what just happened in the relationship.

This practice is intrapersonal: saying "ouch" out loud to make audible to yourself how the inner voice has been attacking. The "ouch" is for you, not for the voice. The voice does not care that you said "ouch." You care, because you are noticing.

Both are useful. They are not the same.

## What Tends to Happen

When people do this experiment, common observations:

- The frequency of "ouches" is much higher than expected
- The content of the attacks is sometimes more brutal than expected (people are often saying things to themselves that they would never accept from a boss)
- A subtle shift after a few minutes — the voice can become less constant once it is being noticed
- A felt sense of compassion arising for the part of self that has been receiving these attacks
- Sometimes anger at the voice, sometimes grief, sometimes humor

Each of these is data. The point is not to feel any particular way. The point is to make visible what has been invisible.

## Variations

The experiment can be extended:

- Do it for an hour with a notebook, writing down each attack
- Do it during a specific task you usually find stressful, to see what the voice is doing during that task
- Pair it with a question: after each "ouch," ask "whose voice is that?" to surface the original source
- Do it as a couple practice — each person says "ouch" out loud and the other hears it

The "whose voice is that" variation connects to [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]]: the inner voice is almost always an internalized voice from a primary caregiver. Naming the voice — "that's mom's voice," "that's dad's voice," "that's my coach's voice" — is the next layer of the work after the noticing layer this experiment opens.

## When Joe Uses This

The experiment is used in teaching contexts to demonstrate the cost of the critical voice. It is also useful in coaching when:

- The person reports being stressed without a clear external cause
- The person says "I don't know why I am so hard on myself" — the experiment makes the harshness visible
- The person is in the early stages of inner-critic work and needs to see the pattern
- The person has been told the voice is critical but does not yet believe it

For coaching, the experiment can be done in-session for a minute and then discussed. The number of "ouches" in a single minute is often enough to make the point.

## Anti-Patterns

Do not turn the experiment into a self-attack. "I had so many ouches, I am terrible at this" is the critical voice doing its work on the experiment itself. Notice that, say "ouch" to it, and keep going.

Do not try to stop the voice during the experiment. The goal is to notice, not to suppress. Trying to stop the voice gives the voice more material ("you can't even stop your inner critic, what's wrong with you"). Just notice.

Do not skip saying "ouch" out loud. The audibility matters. Saying it silently keeps the attack inside the same internal space that produced it. Saying it out loud breaks the loop by making the attack visible in physical space.

## What Comes Next

After the experiment, the natural extensions:

- A broader experimental relationship with the voice (see [[experimenting-with-the-voice]])
- Inquiry into where the voice came from (see [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]])
- Practices that soften the voice over time (see [[three-month-inherent-goodness-experiment]])
- Recognition that softening the voice is upstream of nervous-system rest (see [[inner-critic-prevents-rest-and-integration]] and [[pleasure-tells-system-its-safe]])

The "ouch" experiment is the opening move. The work that follows is what changes the voice over time.

## Tone

Playful, immediate, slightly humorous. The "ouch" is itself a small bit of comic relief — the voice says something mean, you go "ouch," it is almost funny. The humor is part of how the practice works. The voice loses some of its weight when it is met with this small, almost theatrical noticing.
