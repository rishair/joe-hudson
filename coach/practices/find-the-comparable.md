# Find the Comparable (The Person Who Lives the Opposite of Your Belief)

## The Practice

When you suspect a limiting belief, find a person who holds the opposite belief and lives the consequence well. The existence of that person is evidence that your belief is at least partly limiting — that there is a different way to hold the same situation, and the different way works.

This is among the simplest and fastest first moves on any limiting belief. It works at multiple layers: it tests whether a belief is limiting, it loosens the belief by introducing alternative data, and it points toward the consequence that the belief was protecting against.

"The quickest hack to seeing if you have one of those things is you just look for a comparable in the space. You just say, oh wait, is there entrepreneurs that feel safe? Oh, is there entrepreneurs that are actually more secure financially than me in a job? Oh yes, there are. Therefore, there must be something that I'm not seeing. Or is there people who can enjoy failure? Is there people who failed several times and then succeeded, i.e., Abe Lincoln or Sam Walton? And that can allow you to see how your limiting belief is untrue or can be untrue from a certain point of view." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## The Move

Pick the belief. State it cleanly: "It's not safe to be an entrepreneur." "Change is hard." "I'm bad." Then ask: who lives the opposite? Who is an entrepreneur who feels safe? Who finds change easy and enjoyable? Who lives as if they are inherently good and operates well from there?

The comparable can be:

- A specific person you know.
- A historical figure.
- A public figure.
- Someone you have read about.
- Multiple people who together demonstrate the pattern (Abe Lincoln, Sam Walton, anyone who failed many times and built something).

The bar is low. You do not need to find a perfect example. You need to find evidence that the opposite of your belief is also a viable way to live.

## Why It Works

Three reasons.

**It loosens certainty.** The belief presented itself as describing reality. The comparable shows it describes one possible way of holding reality. The categorical frame collapses into a contingent one.

**It creates spaciousness.** This is the underlying mechanism. The mind that was clamped on a single view opens to multiple views. See [[spaciousness-is-the-mechanism-of-belief-dissolution]].

**It tests the cause of blindness.** When you offer the comparable to the person, their response tells you which of the [[three-reasons-we-dont-see-limiting-beliefs]] is operating. If they say "oh, I never thought of that," the cause was ignorance and the belief loosens immediately. If they say "yes but that doesn't apply to me because...," the cause is logical fallacy now made visible. If they get tense and defensive, the cause is consequence avoidance and the next layer of work is emotional.

## What the Comparable Does Not Do

The comparable does not prove your belief is false. The original belief can remain true for your situation. What the comparable shows is that other ways of holding the same situation exist and work. The relationship to your belief shifts from absolute to optional.

The comparable also does not, on its own, dissolve a belief held by consequence avoidance. The comparable opens the door; the emotional work walks through it. A person who has fully accepted that someone else can be a successful entrepreneur may still hold tightly to their own employee identity because the consequence of changing (facing fear of failure) is unbearable. The comparable made the alternative visible; only the emotional work makes the alternative livable.

## Worked Examples

**Belief:** "I can't lose weight."
**Comparable:** People who have lost weight, including people similar to you.
**What it produces:** The categorical claim collapses. The next question becomes: what is it about how I am doing this that differs from how they did it? The belief shifts from "it's impossible" to "it might be the method."

**Belief:** "I'll be unsafe if I leave this job."
**Comparable:** People who have left similar jobs and built lives that are more secure than yours.
**What it produces:** The safety frame is challenged. The deeper question: what does safety actually mean for me, and is this job providing it or am I just calling it safety?

**Belief:** "I'm too overweight to be attractive and safe."
**Comparable:** Joe's observation: overweight people who got molested can stay overweight as a protective strategy. When they see through the molestation and recognize they can be attractive and safe, weight often releases. The comparable here is themselves — the version of themselves that exists in the future after the dissolution work. The existence-proof is of their own potential.

**Belief:** "It's not possible to enjoy being healthy; it has to be a chore."
**Comparable:** People who genuinely enjoy exercising, eating well, sleeping. They exist. The "has to be a chore" frame is one way of holding it, not the only way.

**Belief:** "I am bad."
**Comparable:** "Are all overweight people bad? Are all people with this trait bad? Of all the people who share the specific thing I think makes me bad, are they all bad?" The comparable here is everyone else with the trait — and the answer is no, they are not all bad, which dissolves the inference.

## When the Comparable Doesn't Exist

For some beliefs, no comparable exists. The belief is so universal that no human has done the opposite. In those cases, the work is different — usually toward seeing that the universality is itself the point (everybody has this; it's not personal) or toward finding the small variation (no one has done the exact opposite, but some have held it more lightly).

The absence of a comparable is itself diagnostic. If you really cannot find anyone who holds the opposite of a belief, the belief might be pointing at a structural feature of human experience rather than at a limiting belief specific to you. The work then is acceptance rather than dissolution.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not insist the comparable proves you should be like them. The point is not "be more like Sam Walton." The point is "Sam Walton's existence shows your belief is not absolute."
- He does not deny the difficulty of your specific situation. The comparable acknowledges that what you face is real and also that others have faced it and lived another way.
- He does not pretend the comparable will dissolve the belief alone. It is a first move. The deeper work follows.

## Tone

Light, almost casual. Joe calls this a "hack" deliberately. The simplicity is the point. The belief is making itself feel monolithic; the practice gently shows that it is not. The lightness is part of how the practice works — heavy, serious engagement with the belief reinforces it; casual "yeah, but is there an entrepreneur who feels safe?" loosens it.
