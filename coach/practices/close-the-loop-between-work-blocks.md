# Close the Loop Between Work Blocks

## The Practice

Between any two units of work -- meetings, tasks, calls, blocks of focused work -- the body needs a moment to close the cycle of the work that just ended before the next one begins. Without this, the prior work remains as an open loop in the system, the body has not returned to itself, and the next work happens on top of unprocessed residue from the previous one.

The practice has two distinct components, usually run in sequence:

**Release.** A brief reflection on what just happened. Not an analytical debrief. A simple recognition: that just happened, here is what occurred, here is how it landed. The release can include a sense of satisfaction or appreciation, but its core function is letting the experience be acknowledged rather than displaced by the next thing.

**Relax.** A brief drop into nothing. Not reflection. Not productivity. Just a moment of doing nothing. The body unwinds, the breath returns, the system returns to baseline. This is the shavasana of work -- the dedicated emptiness after the dedicated effort.

"To me, completion is a word for it. Release and relax is what I notice is, you know, if I'm going to do a high-intensity workout, 20 seconds on, 10 seconds off, 20 seconds on, 10 seconds off, whatever it is. If I'm going to do yoga, I'm going to have shavasana at the end of it. Not even appreciation. Appreciation is great. Sense of satisfaction that it's done. Those are great. That's, I think, more of the release side of it. There's also the relaxation side of it." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

## Why Just Appreciation Is Not Enough

Many self-help approaches recommend "celebrating wins" or "appreciating what you accomplished." This is the release side of the practice. It is good. It is also not sufficient. Release alone gives the experience acknowledgment but does not actually let the body unwind.

Relaxation -- the deliberate moment of nothing -- is what lets the nervous system actually drop. Without the relaxation phase, the body remains in a low-grade activation state from the prior work, and the next work happens on top of that activation. Over a day of back-to-back meetings or rapid task-switching, this accumulates into a chronically activated state that does not return to baseline even at the end of the day.

Both phases matter. Release gives the experience its moment. Relaxation lets the body return.

## The Workout Analogy

A high-intensity interval workout structures itself as "20 seconds on, 10 seconds off, 20 seconds on, 10 seconds off." The 10 seconds is not optional. It is what makes the next 20 seconds possible. A 20-on-0-off workout would be much less effective because the muscles would not get the micro-recovery they need to perform the next interval.

The same logic applies to cognitive and emotional work. A meeting followed immediately by another meeting is 20 seconds on, 0 seconds off. The next meeting does not happen at full capacity. The capacity has been degraded by the absence of the recovery interval. Across a day, the cumulative degradation is severe.

The same logic applies to yoga ending with shavasana. The hour of practice is not complete until the body has had ten minutes of stillness to integrate. Skipping shavasana to get on with the day is a common practice and a common error. The integration happens in the stillness. The integration is what makes the practice produce sustained change.

## The Back-to-Back Meetings Drain

Joe identifies this as one of his three known energy drains:

"One is back-to-back meetings. If I don't have a way to close a cycle, if I don't have a little bit of rest, recalibrate what just happened, get back into myself after a meeting, and I go meeting to meeting to meeting to meeting, I feel like I have a whole bunch of open loops that aren't closed in my life, and it totally sucks the energy out of me." -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The phenomenology is precise. Open loops in the system. Loops that did not get closed because there was no moment to close them. The accumulation of open loops is what produces the end-of-day depletion. The depletion is not caused by the amount of work. It is caused by the absence of the loop-closing intervals.

This is one of the more common forms of self-inflicted depletion in corporate work cultures. The calendar fills with meetings. Each meeting is back-to-back with the next. The day looks productive on the calendar. The body ends the day in a state of chronic activation that takes hours or days to fully discharge.

## What the Practice Looks Like

Between meetings or tasks, the minimum viable version:

1. **Stop.** Do not immediately open the next thing.

2. **Release (30-60 seconds).** Briefly recognize what just happened. What occurred? What landed? What's the felt-sense of it? A breath of appreciation if appreciation is there. Not a debrief. Just acknowledgment.

3. **Relax (30-90 seconds).** Drop into nothing. Look out the window. Close your eyes. Lean back. Take a few deep breaths. Do not plan the next meeting. Do not think about anything. Just be in the body for a moment.

4. **Then move to the next thing.**

The total duration is one to three minutes. The investment is small. The downstream effect on the next meeting is significant. The cumulative effect across a day is the difference between depleting and sustainable work.

For longer work blocks (a 45-90 minute deep work session), the same practice scales up. 15 minutes between blocks is a common rhythm. The programmer Joe describes who found his peak at 45 minutes on, 15 minutes off, used those 15 minutes specifically for body-based release and relaxation. See [[fifteen-minutes-in-the-body-between-work-blocks]].

## Common Failures

**Spending the inter-block time on email.** The most common failure mode. The break that was supposed to close the loop instead opens twenty new loops. The body does not relax. The accumulation continues. The next work block starts more depleted than the last one ended.

**Spending the inter-block time on planning.** Mental rehearsal of the next meeting is not release-and-relax. It keeps the system in activation. The benefit of the pause is lost.

**Skipping the relaxation phase.** Many people will do the release (the brief acknowledgment) and skip directly to the next thing. The release alone does not produce the body's return to baseline. Both phases are needed.

**Performing relaxation rather than relaxing.** Sitting with closed eyes while mentally running through the to-do list is performative relaxation. The body knows the difference. The practice requires actual relaxation, not its appearance.

**Treating the practice as optional when busy.** The busier the day, the more important the practice. The instinct is the opposite -- to skip the pauses to fit more in. The result is reduced capacity for everything that follows. The pause is a leverage move, not a luxury.

## What Builds in Over Time

After practicing this for some period, several shifts tend to occur:

- The body becomes more attuned to when a loop has been opened and not closed. The signal is direct rather than retrospective.
- The capacity to drop into relaxation gets faster. What initially required several minutes can later happen in twenty seconds.
- End-of-day depletion decreases noticeably for the same amount of work.
- The quality of attention in the next work block measurably improves. People notice -- "you seemed really present in that meeting."
- The instinct to avoid open loops starts to influence calendar choices. Back-to-back meetings stop getting scheduled. Buffer time becomes structural rather than incidental.

The practice trains a capacity, not just a behavior. The capacity is the body's facility for returning to itself between activities. Once developed, the capacity operates more automatically.

## When This Is the Move

The practice is the right intervention when:

- A person is reporting depletion from a calendar that looks reasonable on paper
- Back-to-back meetings are the default rhythm
- The person notices they cannot remember the prior meeting by the time the next one ends
- End-of-day feeling is consistently off in a way that does not match the work volume
- The person is in [[burnout-cycle]] running phase and needs an immediate intervention they can implement at the calendar level
- A leader is trying to reduce burnout on their team and needs a structural recommendation

## What This Is Not

- This is not a meditation practice. It is operational. One to three minutes, not twenty.
- This is not "celebrate every win." Celebration is one form release can take. It is not the whole practice. The relaxation phase is what most "celebrate" frameworks miss.
- This is not for breaks longer than five minutes or so. For longer breaks, the practice is different -- see [[fifteen-minutes-in-the-body-between-work-blocks]].
- This is not a productivity hack in the sense of "do more in less time." It is a capacity-preservation practice. More gets done because the system doing it is functioning rather than degraded.

## The Trap to Watch For

A specific trap Brett names in the same conversation: the chasing-the-loop trap.

"Sometimes I can get into a cycle where I'm feeling productive and I'm going through, say, like a checklist and I'm getting things done. And there's a way that when I complete a task, I immediately skip over the appreciation, the enjoyment, the celebration, even just for a moment, of taking that task, and then go on to the next one. So there can actually be -- this is where Chasing the Dragon comes in, the hedonic treadmill, where we might be chasing a sense of completion, but we never actually let ourselves savor any completion that we get along the way. And so we're looking for a loop to close that we never allow to close." -- Brett Kistler, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

The diagnosis: the very system that drives productivity often refuses the close-the-loop practice that would make the productivity sustainable. The person chasing completion never lets a completion register, which keeps them chasing. The intervention is to actually let the loop close, repeatedly, until the chasing pattern weakens.

This is one of the structural reasons people in [[productivity-as-dopamine-fix]] resist this practice. Closing the loop interrupts the dopamine cycle. The next hit has to wait. The person experiences the wait as withdrawal and reaches for the next thing to avoid it. The practice itself is the interruption of the addiction.
