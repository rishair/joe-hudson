# Celebrate Failures Publicly (Everyone Names a Failure; Everyone Applauds)

## The Practice

At a team or company gathering -- offsite, all-hands, retreat -- structure a moment in which every person names at least one failure from the period being reviewed. After each naming, the room applauds.

The practice is performed sincerely. Not as gallows humor. Not as performative humility. The failures are real ones: missed goals, projects that did not land, attempts that did not work. The applause is genuine. The point is not to spin failures into wins. The point is to make the act of having tried, having missed, and having named the miss something the team values.

The structural elements:

- Every person participates. The boundary is universal; this is not a place where some people share and others observe.
- Each person names a real failure. Not a humble-brag. Not a "my biggest weakness is I work too hard." An actual thing that was tried and did not work.
- The room applauds after each naming. The applause is the public marking that what just happened is to be valued, not hidden.
- The leadership goes first or models the same vulnerability everyone else is being asked for. The leader who refuses to name their own failure breaks the practice.

## Why It Works

The practice operates on the same mechanism as [[iterative-mindset]] and [[failure-is-an-idea]] but at the team-system level. The failure is reframed as iteration. The naming makes the iteration visible. The applause installs the cultural reinforcement that converts the iteration into the operating mode of the team.

Without the practice, the team's relationship to failure is shaped by what is celebrated -- which by default is only successes. The implicit message: failures are to be hidden. Hidden failures cannot be learned from. People stop taking the risks that produce failures, which means they also stop taking the risks that produce the largest successes.

With the practice, the relationship inverts. Failures are explicitly valued. The implicit message: trying is the thing. Outcomes are downstream of attempts. The team that takes risks together and processes failures together becomes a team that can attempt larger things.

The cultural mechanism is one application of [[reward-failure-in-the-organization]]. The rewards in this case are non-monetary -- applause, attention, public recognition -- but they install the same operating signal: bold attempts are valued, including the ones that don't work.

## What Joe Observed

In Joe's company offsite, the practice produced a moment Joe found himself surprised by:

"As a matter of fact, every single person in our company announced at least one failure that they did, and everybody applauded them. And it was like this wonderful thing. ... It was like the high point of the -- I was surprised about how much of a high point that was when we were all like celebrating our failures. I got more pleasure out of celebrating our failures than I did in celebrating all the successes we had. It was kind of cool." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The "I was surprised" matters. The practice produced more felt-meaning than the success-celebration, which was also part of the offsite. The team's bond, the relief, the shared aliveness all landed at the failure-celebration moment more than at any success moment.

The relational explanation: the success-celebration honored what was done. The failure-celebration honored who was doing it. The successes were artifacts. The failures were people. Celebrating people lands deeper than celebrating artifacts.

## What This Practice Is Not

- It is not a gimmick. The practice fails if it is treated as an exercise. The failures have to be real, the applause has to be sincere, the leadership has to model.
- It is not punishment-dressed-as-celebration. The applause is not ironic. The team is not exposing each other to public shame in the guise of "celebrating." If shame is operating, the practice has miscarried.
- It is not a substitute for accountability. Failures still get examined, still get learned from, still get adjusted for. The celebration sits alongside the operational debrief, not in place of it.
- It is not a one-time event. The practice has to recur. A single celebration of failure followed by a year of business-as-usual reverts the culture to the previous mode. The recurrence is what installs the operating signal.

## What Makes This Hard

The practice requires conditions that are not always present:

**Psychological safety.** People only name real failures if they trust the room will not use the information against them. If the team has not yet built that trust, the practice will produce sanitized failures or no failures at all. The trust is the prerequisite.

**Leadership willingness.** The leader has to model. If the leader cannot name a real failure, no one else will either. The leader naming their failure first sets the floor for what counts as a real failure in this room.

**A culture that has not yet baked in success-only celebration.** Many teams have years of success-only rituals. The first failure-celebration in such a team can feel deeply strange. The strangeness is real and the practice may take several rounds to feel natural.

**Goals that were real enough to fail at.** A team that does not set meaningful goals has nothing meaningful to fail at. The practice assumes the team has been attempting things real enough that misses register as misses. If the goals were want-less and nobody cared, the failures will not have the weight that makes them worth celebrating.

## What the Practice Reveals

When done sincerely, the failure-celebration surfaces several things that are usually invisible:

- The shared experience of attempting hard things together. The bond from this experience is structural; it cannot be produced by celebrating successes alone.
- The texture of the year as it was actually lived. The success-only narrative is always incomplete; the full picture, including the failures, is more accurate and more useful for the team's planning.
- The relational courage of the people in the room. Each person who names a real failure is doing a vulnerable act in public. The accumulated vulnerability across the room builds the kind of team that can do harder work the next year.
- The leadership's stance toward iteration. A leader who applauds failures with the same energy they applaud successes is signaling that the iteration mindset is real, not just talk.

## Connection to the Goals Work

The practice is one of the things that makes the [[goals-must-come-from-actual-want]] approach to goal-setting sustainable. When a team sets real wanted goals, some of them are going to miss. The failure-celebration practice is what makes the missing okay. Without the practice, the team will quietly revert to setting easier goals (the ones they can hit) and the goal-setting becomes a performance of competence rather than a real reach.

The failure-celebration is also one of the cleanest demonstrations of the principle that goals are not identity. See [[dont-let-goals-define-you]]. A team that can celebrate missing a goal is a team that has decoupled the self from the outcome. The decoupling is the operational form of holding the goal lightly.

## How to Install This Practice

For a leader considering it:

1. Start with yourself. Name a failure publicly first -- not in this exact ritual but in any setting -- and observe the team's response. The team's reaction to your vulnerability is the data on whether the room can hold this practice.

2. Introduce it at a moment that already has team-celebration energy. The offsite, the all-hands, the year-end retro. Not at a high-stakes moment where failure-mention could shift power dynamics.

3. Make the structure clear. "We're each going to name one failure from this quarter. After each naming, we'll applaud. The applause is for the trying and the willingness to name it."

4. Model the kind of failure that's being asked for. Your failure as the leader is the template. If yours is real and substantive, others will follow. If yours is performative, theirs will be too.

5. Notice what happens in the room. The energy of the room is the diagnostic. If the room is alive, present, and warmed by the practice, it is working. If the room is awkward or anxious, the conditions are not yet right and the practice may need to be paused while psychological safety is built.

6. Repeat. The practice becomes operating mode through repetition.
