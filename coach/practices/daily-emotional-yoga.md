# Daily Emotional Yoga

## The Practice

Set aside time -- twenty to thirty minutes, several times a week -- to feel whatever emotions are not being felt. Teach the body how to feel them. Teach it how to accept them. Teach it how to unlock the musculature that holds them.

"I would take 20 to 30 minutes to do literally emotional yoga where I would feel everything that I was not feeling and teach my body how to feel it and how to accept it and how to unlock. Because as the emotions start to be felt, then the musculature unlocks, and then you can feel it deeper." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

This is not a one-time exercise. It is a sustained practice over months or years, particularly in the early stages of emotional work when the body has chronic patterns of suppression to release.

## How to Do It

The practice is unstructured by design. The body knows what it needs to feel if it is allowed to. The main task is to provide the time, the privacy, and the willingness.

1. **Find privacy.** Especially in the beginning, when the practice feels strange or shameful. Joe used to walk three miles off-trail to cry without being seen.
2. **Let the body move.** Shake. Cry. Yell into a pillow. Pace. Curl up. The body knows.
3. **Do not perform.** This is not for an audience. There is no correct way it should look. See [[stages-of-emotional-development]].
4. **Fake it if you have to.** In the early stages, especially with emotions that have been suppressed for years, the body may not know how to start. Fake crying. Fake anger. The faking can prime the real thing.
5. **Do not direct it at anyone.** The emotions move through you, not at a person. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]].

## What Faking Does

When Joe began learning to cry, he had not cried in fourteen years. He went into the woods and faked it for three months. Then one day it became real. When it became real, the relief was so great that he let it happen for four days. He cried while brushing his teeth. Years of muscular tension released in days.

The faking is not dishonest. It is a way to teach the body the shape of the motion so that the real thing can find its way out. The body is being given a track to run on. See [[joes-crying-story]].

## The Musculature Unlocks Progressively

As emotions are felt, the muscles that have been holding them release. As muscles release, deeper layers of emotion become accessible. The practice is recursive: each session opens the door to deeper material that the next session can reach.

"As the emotions start to be felt, then the musculature unlocks, and then you can feel it deeper." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

This is why daily emotional yoga compounds. The body is being progressively freed. See [[emotions-live-in-muscles]].

## What It Produces

- A baseline of fewer kinks. The emotions that arise in daily life have more room to move through. See [[kinked-emotions]].
- A daily release valve so that emotions do not accumulate and leak. See [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]].
- Increasing fluency in feeling. Eventually the practice becomes less necessary because emotions move through quickly in real time.

Joe reports that at later stages, a cry, shake, or anger release takes about five minutes during the day. The emotion moves through. It relieves stress, changes neurochemistry, clarifies decisions. This is what the daily emotional yoga produces over time.

## Why It Helps With Decision-Making

The practice is the operational form of [[feel-over-figure]]. By giving emotions a regular, dedicated channel to move, decisions made during the day come from clarity rather than from accumulated unfelt material. The hour spent feeling on a given day produces sharper, faster decisions across many other hours.

## When to Use It

Especially valuable in the early stages of emotional work, when there are years or decades of suppressed material to release. Less needed once emotions are flowing daily in real time, but still useful as a maintenance practice.

## The Two-Week Experiment Container

For a person who has not yet begun the practice, Joe offers a low-overhead time-bounded experiment that compresses the daily emotional yoga commitment into a fourteen-day container. Each day, identify one emotion that has been avoided and bring it into emotional inquiry. That is the entire prescription.

"Here's an experiment that you can try out. Every day at the end of the day or at the beginning of the day, you notice an emotion that you've been avoiding and you bring it into the emotional inquiry. You just do that every day for 2 weeks. Every day for 2 weeks. And notice how much your life changes." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Out Of The Matrix

The container has three useful properties for a beginner.

**Time-bounded.** Fourteen days is short enough to commit to without resistance and long enough to accumulate body-level evidence. The person does not have to decide whether to take on a lifelong practice; they take on two weeks.

**One emotion per day.** No accumulation pressure. The instruction is not to feel all the unfelt emotions in one sitting (see [[dont-go-after-every-emotion-at-once]]). One per day is plenty. The body integrates between sessions.

**The framing is observational.** "Notice how much your life changes" is the invitation. The person is not promised a specific outcome. They are invited to run the experiment and see what they find. The lightness of the framing is part of what allows the practice to work without becoming another should.

The two-week container pairs naturally with the [[noticing-the-matrix-pattern]] three-step diagnostic. The diagnostic surfaces what emotion is being avoided in any given day; the emotional inquiry session then welcomes the emotion the diagnostic surfaced. The combination of "notice today's avoided emotion" plus "feel it through" in a daily cadence for two weeks is the public-facing operational prescription Joe gives for breaking the Matrix pattern. See [[the-matrix]].

After two weeks, the practice can be continued, paused, or evolved. The point of the container is not the container; it is the body-level evidence the container produces. Once the evidence is there, the practice typically continues in some form because the person has discovered what it does for them, not because they have committed to keeping it up.

## Tone

Casual, practical. Joe presents this as something he just did, not as a sacred ritual. The emotional yoga is functional. It is yoga because the body is being moved and stretched into capacity, just as physical yoga does.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone is in stage 1 or 2 of [[stages-of-emotional-development]].
- When someone reports that they cannot access an emotion they used to be able to access.
- When chronic tension in the body suggests suppressed emotion.
- When the daily emotional load is showing up in leaks. See [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]].

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: this is the practice that produces the felt state.
- Toward [[stages-of-emotional-development]]: the practice moves a person through the stages.
- Toward [[joes-crying-story]]: the example Joe uses to demonstrate the practice.
- Toward fluidity in real-time emotional response, eventually replacing the need for the dedicated practice.
