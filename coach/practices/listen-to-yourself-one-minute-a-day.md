# Listen to Yourself One Minute a Day

## The Practice

Set a reminder on your phone. Once a day, when the reminder fires, stop whatever you are doing for one to two minutes. Listen to yourself with unconditional acceptance. That is the whole practice.

"You want to do little repetitions. And so you could do that with somebody else, but that will actually not be the quickest way to learn how to listen. The best way is to listen to yourself with unconditional acceptance. One minute a day. Two minutes a day, just listen. Put a reminder on your phone to just listen to yourself unconditionally with unconditional acceptance." -- Joe Hudson, How To Listen Better

The reminder is the structure. The unconditional-acceptance is the quality. The shortness is the design that makes it doable.

## When This Practice Is Prescribed

This practice is the answer to a specific question. Someone says: "I'm not really built to listen to people like this. I'm not good at it. How do I get better?"

The intuitive next move would be to assign reps of listening to others. Joe redirects. The fastest path to listening well to others is not practicing listening to others. It is practicing listening to yourself.

"Hey, like with any skill, you want to build reps, right? You want to do little repetitions. And so you could do that with somebody else, but that will actually not be the quickest way to learn how to listen. The best way is to listen to yourself with unconditional acceptance." -- Joe Hudson, How To Listen Better

The reason is structural. The person who is not in themselves cannot receive others. See [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]] and [[listening]]. Practicing listening to others while you have never listened to yourself is practicing performance, not listening. The reps build the wrong thing. The self-listening reps build the actual capacity, and the capacity then expresses naturally in conversations with others.

## Instructions

1. **Set a reminder.** A phone notification, an alarm, a recurring calendar block. The structure matters; without it the practice never happens. The reminder makes it not a choice each day.
2. **When the reminder fires, stop.** Whatever you are in the middle of, pause. One or two minutes will not break it.
3. **Listen to yourself.** Notice what your body is feeling. Notice what your inner voice is saying. Notice the quality of your breath, the tension in your shoulders, the pace of your thoughts. Do not direct anything. Just notice.
4. **Bring unconditional acceptance to whatever you find.** Whatever you are feeling — irritated, tired, scared, blank, fine — meet it without trying to change it, judge it, or improve it. The acceptance is the practice; the noticing is just the doorway.
5. **End.** Continue what you were doing. Notice if anything is different in the next conversation you walk into.

## Why Unconditional Acceptance Specifically

The reps cannot just be reps of attention. They have to be reps of *accepting* attention. Most people who attend to themselves do so with judgment: "I shouldn't be feeling this," "I should have handled that better," "I'm being weak." That is not listening. That is monitoring with a critic in the room.

The body knows the difference. Listening with judgment produces the same internal contraction whether the judging is happening to you-by-you or to-you-by-someone-else. The contraction prevents the listening from doing what listening does. The unconditional-acceptance qualifier is what makes the practice land.

The link: when you give yourself unconditional acceptance, you build the somatic template for what unconditional acceptance feels like. Then, in conversation with another person, you can offer that same quality from the same place in your body. The listening becomes available as a thing you can do because it has become a thing you have done — to yourself, in small doses, daily.

This is closely related to the live-coaching version of the same principle in [[have-them-receive-your-listening]]: the felt sense of unconditional acceptance is the install, and once installed it is portable. The daily-reps version is just the at-home, no-coach-required path to the same install.

## Why One Minute, Not Longer

The shortness is intentional. Two failure modes the brevity prevents:

**The grand-effort failure.** Long practices feel like obligations. Obligations get skipped. The reps stop happening. The capacity is never built.

**The morality failure.** Long practices are easier to attach a should to. The longer the sit, the more "I should be doing this right" creeps in. The unconditional-acceptance quality dies under the should. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]].

One minute is shorter than the resistance can mobilize against. The phone reminder fires; you do not have time to negotiate with yourself; you just do it. The next day, again. The reps accumulate.

The instruction does not preclude longer. A person who finds that one minute opens into ten is welcome to take the ten. But the entry instruction stays small to keep it doable, daily, and self-perpetuating.

## What Tends to Happen

- **Surprise at the volume of what was unattended.** Most people are not aware of how much sensory and emotional content they are tuning out moment-to-moment. The first few reps make the volume visible.
- **A small spaciousness.** A one-minute pause with unconditional acceptance produces a slight expansion. The day continues, but with a margin around it.
- **Better listening to others, unrequested.** Within weeks, people start reporting that conversations feel different. Without effort, they are catching more, asking better, receiving more. The conversion is not narrated; it happens.
- **The reps want to grow.** The body that has felt the spaciousness of one minute wants more of it. Two minutes becomes natural. Then five. Then the practice is no longer about reps; it has become the orientation.

## What This Is Not

This is not meditation. Meditation, depending on the form, often has a posture, a technique, a target, an effort. This practice has none of those. It is just listening to yourself for one minute with unconditional acceptance, in whatever situation you happen to be in when the reminder fires.

This is not journaling. There is no record, no analysis, no figuring out. The whole exchange is internal and momentary.

This is not a productivity hack. It does not promise more output. It produces a different relational capacity, which sometimes produces more output as a side effect, but the practice does not aim there.

This is not a moral practice. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]]. The reps are not about becoming a better person. They are about installing a somatic capacity. If the reps get heavy or self-evaluative, the practice has been corrupted. The fix is to make them lighter, shorter, more casual.

## The Cascade That Often Follows

Joe describes a client who started doing the related practice — listening to people in their life with unconditional acceptance. Within a week the client came to a coaching session and said something like: "Is it that simple? They just needed to be listened to?"

The realization rippled outward:

"In that came this huge epiphany for them that just was realization that, oh wait, probably my employees don't feel listened to, my direct reports don't feel listened to. And they just start opening up and listening. And all of a sudden they understood the problems of their business, they understood the problems of their marriage. People felt closer to them, they shared more deeply." -- Joe Hudson, How To Listen Better

The pattern is recurring. The self-listening reps install the capacity. The capacity then expresses outward into the relationships that had been suffering for the absence of it. Marriages improve. Businesses improve. Friendships deepen. The path that looked like "one minute a day, just to me" turns out to have been the path to everything that was downstream of the missing capacity.
