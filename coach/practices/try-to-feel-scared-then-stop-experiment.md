# Try to Feel Scared, Then Try to Stop (The Somatic Suppression Experiment)

## The Practice

A live, two-step somatic experiment that takes under thirty seconds. Anyone can do it, anywhere. It produces direct first-person evidence that emotional suppression is physical, not just mental.

**Step 1: Allow yourself to feel scared.** Right now. There is always a little fear available somewhere in the system. Notice what arises. Notice how it feels in the body.

**Step 2: Now try to stop feeling scared.** Whatever you have to do, make sure that you do not feel scared. Notice what your body does to comply.

Almost universally, what happens in Step 2 is that some muscles constrict. The jaw clamps. The shoulders pull up. The belly tightens. The breath shortens. The body has to do physical work to suppress the emotion. The proof is immediate and visceral.

## What It Demonstrates

You cannot stop an emotional experience without a physical holding. This is the principle the experiment proves. The implication: every emotion that has been suppressed for a long time is being held in the body by a chronic muscular pattern. Years of suppressing the same emotion produce chronic pain, postural distortion, and musculoskeletal issues.

"Right now if you're listening to this podcast and just like allow yourself to feel -- let's just use a simple one, like allow yourself to feel scared for a moment. So you allow yourself to feel scared. Feel what that's like in your body. And then now stop feeling scared. No matter what you do, make sure that you do not feel scared. And I guarantee what just happened is that your muscles constricted a little bit because you can't stop an emotional experience without a physical holding. And so if you've been holding the same thing physically for years and years and years, you're going to get chronic pain and stress." -- Joe Hudson, Emotional Fluidity

The experiment makes a previously-abstract principle directly experiential. The listener can verify it with their own body. They do not need to take Joe's word for it.

## Why This Experiment Works

The mechanism it surfaces is the same mechanism that produces:

- Chronic tension patterns in adults who learned in childhood that certain emotions were not allowed
- The "kinked emotion" presentation (anger comes out as passive aggression because the muscles have been holding the direct expression). See [[kinked-emotions]]
- The somatic emotional release that happens in certain yoga classes, bodywork, or coaching: when the chronic muscular pattern is invited to relax, the held emotion finally moves
- The connection between adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) and downstream disease: a body that has been holding suppressed emotion for decades accumulates the cost in tissue

The principle is [[emotions-live-in-muscles]]. The experiment is the live, fast, undeniable proof.

## Why It Lands

Most listeners have heard "emotions are stored in the body" as a New Age phrase and have not had a clear example. The experiment converts the phrase into a thirty-second somatic event the body itself performs. The conversion produces a different kind of conviction than reading or hearing the principle does. The body has shown the listener what their body has been doing.

This is also why the experiment works as the opening move of a longer teaching about emotional fluidity. Once the listener has felt their own muscles clamp in response to the instruction to stop feeling scared, the subsequent claims about decades of accumulated holding land in a way they would not have otherwise.

## Variations

The same experiment works with other emotions:

- Allow yourself to feel sad for a moment. Now try to stop. Notice what holds.
- Allow yourself to feel angry. Now try to stop. Notice what holds.
- Allow yourself to feel a small pleasure. Now try to stop. Notice what holds.

The pleasure version is particularly useful for people who do not believe they suppress positive emotions. See [[pleasure-capacity]] and [[fear-of-joy]].

## The All-Emotions Variant (Stress Demonstration)

A condensed version of the experiment that demonstrates the principle as a direct cause of stress, not just of suppression. The instruction:

"I want you to stop feeling all of your emotions right now. All right, you're still feeling something. Stop feeling all of it. All of it. Everything you can do to stop feeling your emotions. Nope, you're still feeling something. Stop. Make sure that you stop feeling. And you might notice right now your entire body is stressed out. That's what happens when you try not to feel emotions. Stresses your whole body out." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Causes of Stress

This variant skips the "allow yourself to feel scared first" step and goes directly to "stop feeling all of it." The listener is invited to attempt total emotional suppression — and immediately notice that the body goes tense everywhere. The discovery is the same as the canonical version (suppression is physical), but the framing is different: the experiment is positioned as a demonstration of the stress-cause of emotional suppression, not as a demonstration of how feelings are stored in muscles.

The all-emotions variant is the emotion-level intervention in [[four-causes-of-stress]]. It pairs with the framework's second cause of stress: unfelt emotions accumulating produce muscular holding which is itself the felt experience of stress. The experiment makes the cause visible in real time.

Use the canonical version when teaching emotional fluidity or somatic suppression. Use the all-emotions variant when teaching the four causes of stress. Both work; they emphasize different aspects of the same mechanism.

## When to Use This

- When teaching emotional fluidity to a person who has only heard the principle abstractly
- When a person reports they do not believe they are suppressing emotions
- When a person needs to feel, in their own body, that emotional work is somatic and not just psychological
- When opening a longer teaching about how emotions are held physically
- When a person resists the idea that their chronic pain might be related to chronic emotional holding

## What Happens Afterward

Once the listener has done the experiment, the natural next move is to extend the implication. If suppressing a single emotion for thirty seconds produced visible muscular constriction, what does suppressing the same emotion for thirty years produce? The body has just shown them in miniature. Now they can extrapolate.

The follow-on conversation often moves toward:

- What is the chronic holding pattern that is already in this body? Which muscles are clamped most of the time?
- Which emotion has been suppressed the longest?
- What might it feel like to allow what has been held to begin moving?

The experiment is the on-ramp. The actual unwinding happens through the slower work of [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[daily-emotional-yoga]].

## Boundary Conditions

The experiment is designed to produce a small, brief, recoverable constriction. It is not designed to be a deep emotional intervention. People in active trauma response, dissociated states, or major emotional crisis should not use this experiment as a stand-alone tool. The point is the demonstration, not the release.

For deeper emotional processing, the demonstration leads into other practices. The experiment itself is a teaching device, not a healing modality.

## Tone

Playful and immediate. Joe delivers this as an interactive moment in the podcast itself, not as a recommendation for some future practice. The "I guarantee" framing carries the confidence that the listener's body will cooperate with the demonstration. It almost always does.
