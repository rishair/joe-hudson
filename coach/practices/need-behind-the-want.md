# Need Behind the Want

## The Practice

Write down a want you have that you do not want to admit to yourself. The one you feel most guilty about. Usually it will be a sexual want, a money want, a fame want, a power want. Whatever the want is that has the most shame attached to it.

Then ask: what is the need behind that want?

Whatever surfaces, ask the question again of the new answer: what is the need behind that want?

Keep going. Each layer reveals what the previous layer was actually pointing at. Eventually you will land on a need that is essentially what every human needs to thrive: connection, safety, love, expression, dignity, contribution.

"Just write down the want that you have that you don't want to admit to yourself, right? That you're most -- you feel most guilty of, right? Some sexual thing or some money thing usually will, you know, be the right thing there, but whatever it is, some fame thing, something you have guilt about. And then just ask yourself, what's the need behind that want? And whatever you come up with, just ask yourself again, what's the need behind that want? And whatever you come up with then just ask again what the need behind that want is. You just keep on going down and you will find that every single one of your wants is based on something essential that humans need to thrive." -- Joe Hudson, What is Selfishness?

## What It Produces

The practice exposes the structure that the shame was concealing. The shameful want is a strategy. The strategy is in service of a need every human shares. The shame was attached to the strategy, not the need.

The recognition does several things:

- The shame loosens. It is hard to keep judging yourself for needing connection or safety.
- The want often shifts. See [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]]. The deeper need, once seen, sometimes makes the surface strategy obsolete. Other times the strategy stays but the relationship to it changes.
- Judgment of others' similar wants softens. If your sexual want is in service of connection, then their sexual want is probably in service of connection too. The mirror clarifies.

## Why Writing Matters

Doing this practice in the head tends to produce intellectual answers and intellectual resistance. The protective mind insists that "fame" is just shallow, "money" is just greed. Written down, the want sits there. It cannot be argued with as easily. The next question lands on the page, not in the loop.

The practice is therefore explicitly a writing exercise. Joe specifies the writing because the spoken or thought version can be defended away.

## The Trajectory the Practice Reveals

Joe describes the typical descent:

- "I want $10 million." → What's the need? "Security."
- "Security." → What's the need? "To feel okay no matter what happens."
- "To feel okay no matter what happens." → What's the need? "To trust myself / to be loved / to belong."

The endpoint is usually something close to the basics of being human. Not because the original want was wrong but because the original want was a strategy aimed at the basic need through a particular cultural channel.

This connects to [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]], which describes the same mechanism at a longer time horizon. The need-behind-the-want practice is the faster, written version of what naturally happens when wants are followed through life over years.

## Why This Defuses the Selfishness Frame

Most "selfish" wants, when traced to their need, are based on needs that everyone has. The teenager who wants attention is "selfish" only if attention is judged as a low want. Once attention is recognized as the basic human need for relational presence, the selfishness label loses its grip.

"You will find that every single one of your wants is based on something essential that humans need to thrive." -- Joe Hudson

This is why the practice is the inner version of the coaching move for [[am-i-being-selfish]]. The person worried about selfishness is shaming a strategy. Tracing the strategy to its need exposes that the need is universal, not selfish.

## How It Differs From Other Want Practices

| Practice | Focus |
|----------|-------|
| [[feeling-the-want-as-emotion]] | Sit with the want as a feeling, do not chase it |
| [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]] | Full ownership of the want lets it shift |
| [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] | Locate the want concealed by a should |
| Need behind the want (this) | Trace the want to the universal need it serves |

The four are complementary. The order is often: notice the should, locate the want underneath, sit with it as a feeling, then trace it to its need if the shame is still present.

## The "What Does Your Heart Want?" Variant

The same practice can be opened with a heart-anchored question rather than a guilt-anchored question. In Joe's company offsite, the goal-setting was conducted by asking:

"What does your heart want for yourself in this business? What does your heart want for the business? What does your heart want for the community we serve?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The questions are themselves the first cascade step. They surface what the heart wants, which is upstream of what the head would normally name as the goal. The heart's answer is closer to a need-already than what would emerge from "what's our revenue target?"

The variant is useful for two situations:

1. **Group goal-setting.** The guilt-anchored opening ("what want do you feel most ashamed of?") is too vulnerable for a team context. The heart-anchored opening produces similar depth without requiring the same exposure.

2. **People who do not feel safe naming their guilty wants.** Some clients cannot land in the guilt frame because the shame is too active. The heart frame bypasses the shame block and gets to the underlying need by a different path.

The endpoint is the same: a clearer view of what the system actually wants underneath whatever surface goal was being set. The path to the endpoint can be guilt-down or heart-up depending on what works.

## The Goal-as-Strategy Application

A specific use of the cascade for goal-setting in business or personal life. Take the surface goal ($12M revenue, lose 20 pounds, build the perfect product), then ask the need-behind cascade. The cascade typically reveals that the surface goal is a *strategy* for what is actually wanted (security, vitality, impact). Once seen this way, the strategy is recognized as one of several possible strategies, the actual want is named, and the goal-relationship transforms.

"Oh, the need behind getting $12 million in revenue. Oh, that's the want to be secure. What's the need behind feeling secure? Oh, I want to feel like I can actually make a difference in the world. ... If you see the thing as a strategy instead of the actual want ... then making that $12 million isn't about making $12 million. It is, oh, there's this way that I'm going to be able to have impact on the world." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The reframe -- "the goal is the strategy, not the want" -- is what the cascade produces when applied to a goal rather than to a guilty want. See [[goal-beyond-the-goal]] for the related principle this surfaces in.

## When to Use This Practice

- When [[am-i-being-selfish]] is the presenting concern
- When the person has identified a want but cannot allow themselves to have it
- When shame about the content of a want is preventing the want from being felt
- When judging someone else's want as selfish is creating relational distance
- When [[selfishness-is-the-engine-of-unity]] has been introduced and the person needs a way to experience the principle directly

## When This Practice Backfires

If the practice is used to "solve" the want by climbing past it to the more acceptable need, it becomes a sophisticated form of avoidance. The point is not to escape the original want by relocating to the need. The point is to recognize that the original want carries the need, so the original want is not shameful, so the original want can be felt.

If after the practice the person says "Oh good, so I do not actually want X, I want Y," check whether they are evading rather than understanding. The genuine result is: "Oh, so wanting X is one of my ways of seeking Y, and Y is something every human needs, so I can stop hating myself for wanting X."

## What to Do With the Output

Once the underlying need is named, the question becomes: what are all the ways this need could be met? The original surface want is one of them. Often there are others that are more direct, more available, or more aligned with other wants the person has.

But Joe is clear that the original want should not be skipped just because a deeper need has been named. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]]. The strategy still has to be allowed for the deeper want to fully refine.
