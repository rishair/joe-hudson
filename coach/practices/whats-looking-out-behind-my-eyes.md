# What's Looking Out Behind My Eyes?

## The Practice

Ask, mid-experience: "What's looking out behind my eyes right now?"

That is the entire practice. It can be asked at any moment — in a meeting, during a fight, on the toilet, in the grocery line. The question quiets the [[voice-in-the-head]] without trying to. It does so by shifting attention from the voice (which is identified with) to the awareness of the voice (which is what is actually looking out).

"It also kind of puts you in where your identity has moved from awareness — I mean, from the voice in your head to awareness, right? So are you the voice in your head that's constantly talking, or are you the awareness of the voice in the head talking?" -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The question is not asked in order to be answered. It is asked in order to land.

## How It Works

Most internal experience is mediated through identification with the voice. The voice talks; the person experiences themselves as the talker. The question redirects: who is the one for whom this voice is occurring? The redirect happens almost involuntarily — the mind tries to locate "what's looking out," cannot find a stable answer, and in the not-finding, something becomes spacious.

The voice tends to quiet during the redirect because the voice is what gets seen, not the seer. When the seer is being noticed, the voice fades into the background. Not because it has been silenced, but because attention has moved from "the voice and what it's saying" to "the field in which the voice is arising."

The reduction is usually temporary. The voice returns. The practice is to ask the question again. And again. Like brushing teeth.

## Why It Works In Tense Moments

Most practices for the voice require time and quiet — sitting, journaling, dialogue with the voice. This question requires neither. It works while driving, while arguing, while presenting. The mid-action accessibility is its main feature.

During a fight: the voice is loud, the body is activated, options feel limited. Ask the question. The voice does not go away, but identification with it loosens. From that loosening, more responses become available. The fight does not have to be controlled by whatever the voice is saying.

## Variants

The question has many forms, all working the same way:

- "What's looking out behind my eyes?"
- "Who is hearing this thought?"
- "What is aware of this voice right now?"
- "[[what-am-i]]?" -- the deepest form, which Joe practiced 10 times a day for 10 years.
- "Who am I?" -- a classic version with similar effect.

These are all in the family of self-inquiry questions. They are designed to be lived in, not answered.

"That's not a question to be answered, that's a question to be in. It's to be in wonder. In that question." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

See [[what-am-i]] for the depth version and [[self-realization]] for the territory these questions eventually open.

## What to Expect

The first few times, the question may produce confusion. "What kind of question is that?" That confusion is fine. It is the mind looking for a referent and not finding one. Stay with the not-finding.

After a while, the question lands more cleanly. There is a felt shift, hard to describe — a small loosening, a slight spaciousness, a moment in which the voice is heard rather than identified with. This is the practice working.

Eventually the question becomes a habit. The voice gets loud, the question arises automatically, the loosening happens. The practice becomes its own reward.

## Relationship to [[what-am-i]]

This practice is the everyday, in-the-moment version of the deeper [[what-am-i]] inquiry. The two are continuous. "What's looking out behind my eyes?" is concrete and somatic — it points at the eyes, the head, the immediate moment. "What am I?" is more abstract and sits with the metaphysical depth.

For someone new to self-inquiry, the eyes-question is more accessible. It does not require any belief about consciousness or selfhood. It just asks a question about right now.

For someone deeper in the inquiry, the eyes-question becomes a doorway to [[what-am-i]]. The two are not separate practices. They are one inquiry at different depths.

## When to Use

- Any moment the [[voice-in-the-head]] is loud.
- Before, during, or after difficult conversations.
- In meditation, as the anchor.
- As a default returned-to practice throughout the day.
- When [[wonder]] needs a doorway.

## Boundary Conditions

The practice is not for moments of acute emotional flooding. If a person is in the middle of a wave of grief, anger, or fear, the move is to be with the emotion, not to ask "what's looking out behind my eyes?" That question, mid-emotion, can land as bypass — using awareness to skip the feeling rather than to be present with it.

The practice is for the voice, not for emotion. For emotion, the practice is [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

## People Already Have Moments Of This

The question is pointing at something most people have already experienced, often without recognizing it. Looking at a vista and "spacing out" for a few seconds. Being in a high-focus activity (wingsuit flight, climbing, surgery) and noticing afterwards that thought had dropped out. Being awestruck by something and not having any words for a moment.

"We have experiences of it that people don't even know they're having. Like you in a wingsuit, I'm sure you had moments of full non-thought, right? It's just like presence... Yeah, or for some people, it's like they look at a vista and they have this moment, maybe it's 5 seconds or something where they just like, you know, and so..." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

"Awestruck is a way that it happens, and you're like, 'hey, where'd you go? I just spaced out.' You know, like we have them, but what's interesting is because we're not thinking during those times, the mind that is thinking has a hard time identifying them." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The structural point: the mind that is thinking cannot easily recognize the moments when thinking was absent. The thinker only remembers what the thinker did. The moments of non-thinking are remembered as "I spaced out" or "I lost time" rather than as glimpses of the seer the practice is pointing at.

This is useful when introducing the practice. The question is not asking the person to produce a state they have never had. It is asking them to notice a state they have had many times but have not been able to recognize because thought was not there to make a record.

## The 9-Year-Old Daughter Story

Joe asked his daughter, when she was around nine years old, "Hey, have you ever had that moment when you're looking out behind your eyes and you know that there's something greater than you looking out behind your eyes? Like, it's like, it's vastness." She said: "Oh yeah, all the time." Joe was surprised.

"I was so surprised, you know? And so I think it's something that's very natural that we lose track of, or we get into our neurotic thoughts, into our churn, and we forget that it's happening or that it's there, it's possible." -- Joe Hudson, When the Story Falls Apart

The implication: this is not a state to be attained. It is a state that small children inhabit easily and that adults forget how to recognize. The practice is partly a return to something already known, not an arrival at something new.

This is also a useful frame when working with someone who feels they cannot access the inquiry. The instruction shifts from "produce this state" to "remember when this state was natural, and notice that you have not actually lost the capacity to enter it." The remembering can be the doorway.

## The Question as Joe's Integration Prompt

Joe uses this question explicitly as a closing prompt at the end of episodes that touch the projection of I. In "Your World is a Projection," after describing the four kinds of projection, he offers it as the third integration question:

"What's looking out behind your eyes? ... I encourage you not to answer that question, just be in it." -- Joe Hudson, Your World is a Projection

The instruction to not answer is important. The question is the practice. Trying to produce a verbal answer is exactly the move the question is designed to bypass. See [[projections]] for the four-types frame in which this question is the entry to seeing through the projection of I.
