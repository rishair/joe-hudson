# Speak From Each of the Three Centers (Head, Heart, Gut)

## The Practice

In the first week of MasterClass, Joe runs an exercise where participants take turns speaking from three different locations in the body: from the head, from the heart (chest), and from the gut. The same person speaks the same kind of content from each of the three centers, and notices the difference.

"On the first week of MasterClass, we do an exercise where you speak from here, where you speak from here, and you speak from your gut. For this exact reason." -- Joe Hudson, Am I Being Authentic Or Fake?

The "this exact reason" in context: a participant was reporting a felt sense of speaking from a different place than usual, with energy moving up through the chest. Joe pointed at the exercise as the formal version of what the participant was discovering live.

## Why the Practice Exists

People do not usually notice which of the three centers their speech is coming from. Most habitual speech comes from the head — pre-edited, managed, organized through the cognitive layer. The result is speech that is fluent but disconnected from the rest of the body's intelligence.

The practice makes the location of speech visible by deliberately changing it. The person speaks from the head, then from the heart, then from the gut, and notices what is different. The differences are usually significant. Speech from the head is faster, more articulate, more abstract. Speech from the heart is slower, more about felt experience, more relational. Speech from the gut is even slower, more about knowing, more direct.

The point is not that one center is better. The point is that speech can come from any of the three, and the person can develop the capacity to choose, or to notice which one is active.

## How the Practice Works

The full form is run in MasterClass; what follows is the structural version.

1. **Choose a topic the person can speak about for a few minutes.** Something with some emotional content, but not crisis-level material.

2. **Have them speak from the head first.** Direct them to notice that the speech is coming from the head. Let them speak for a minute or two.

3. **Have them speak the same content from the heart.** Direct them to drop their attention to the chest and to let the speech come from there. Notice the difference.

4. **Have them speak the same content from the gut.** Direct them to drop their attention to the gut and to let the speech come from there. Notice the difference.

5. **Reflect on what was different.** What changed in pace, content, felt-sense, emotional quality?

## What the Practice Produces

The most common discovery is that the person had no idea their speech was coming from the head until they were asked to speak from somewhere else. The contrast makes the location visible.

The second common discovery is that speech from the heart and gut tends to be slower, more grounded, less performative. The person often reports the speech-from-the-gut as having a different kind of weight or authority. The speech-from-the-heart often has a different kind of warmth or connection.

The third common discovery is that some of what the person typically says is no longer available from the heart or gut. The head's speech includes content that the deeper centers do not endorse. When the person tries to speak that content from the heart, it comes out hollow. The exercise makes the disjunction visible.

## Why This Matters For the Work

Most of the work on authenticity involves moving the source of action and speech from the head to deeper centers. The head is where the social masks operate. The deeper centers carry the more authentic material. Developing the capacity to notice which center is active is a precondition for choosing to operate from the deeper ones.

The exercise is also a useful demonstration that the three centers exist as distinct sources. Many people have only experienced the head as the source of speech and have a hard time imagining what "speaking from the heart" or "speaking from the gut" would mean. The exercise gives the experience first, then the names. See [[experiential-before-intellectual]].

The framework the exercise rests on is the broader head-heart-gut model. See [[three-layers-of-the-work]], [[measuring-transformation-across-three-brains]], and [[energetic-system-as-fourth-layer]] for the conceptual context.

## When the Practice Applies

The exercise is run early in the work to install the basic vocabulary of the three centers. It also applies in live coaching when a person is reporting a felt-sense of speech coming from a different place than usual — often during the [[slowness-after-the-drop]] phase, when habitual head-based fluency is temporarily unavailable and the person can feel speech trying to come from a different location.

The exercise can also be run informally as a check during a session. If a person is speaking quickly and abstractly, the coach can ask: "Can you say that from your heart? From your gut?" The relocation often produces a different version of the same content, with more groundedness and less performance.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Treating the three centers as a hierarchy in which gut is best.** All three centers carry intelligence. The work is integration, not replacement.
- **Forcing the relocation when the person is in head-only state.** If the head is dominant for protective reasons, demanding relocation can be intrusive. The exercise works when the person has some access to the deeper centers.
- **Making the centers metaphorical when the person can feel them physically.** The centers are felt locations, not concepts. The exercise depends on the person actually directing attention to the physical location.
