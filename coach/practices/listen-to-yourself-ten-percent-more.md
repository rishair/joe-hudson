# Listen to Yourself 10% More

## The Practice

While speaking, place 10% more of your attention on hearing yourself. Not 100%. Not "be in your body." Just 10% more than you usually do. Continue saying whatever you were about to say. Notice what changes.

The instruction is deliberately small. Most people who have been abandoning themselves while speaking cannot find 50% or 100% of their attention to redirect inward — those numbers feel impossible or contrived. They can find 10%. The shift is enough to be felt by the speaker and audible to the listener.

"Listen to yourself just 10% more. Same thing. Just, but listen to yourself 10% more as you're saying it." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

## Instructions

1. Notice you are about to speak (or are speaking).
2. Direct a small portion of your attention — call it 10% — to your own listening of yourself. Hear yourself the way another person in the room would hear you, but from inside.
3. Continue with whatever you were going to say. The content does not need to change.
4. Notice the shift in pace, tone, presence. Notice how the words sit in the body differently. Notice the response of the listener.

The practice can be deployed in real time mid-sentence. It is not preparation; it is reorientation. The shift is immediate.

## Why It Works

A person who is not in themselves while speaking is speaking from a meta-position — a model of how their words are being received — rather than from inside their own body. The meta-position is exhausting to maintain and produces speech that the listener registers as managed or performed.

The 10% instruction does not ask the person to stop maintaining the meta-position entirely. It asks them to recover a small amount of attention into themselves. That small amount is enough to:

- Slow the pace of speech to match the speaker's own processing.
- Bring the body back online as a source of speech.
- Change the speech from "being installed in the listener" to "being offered from the speaker."
- Allow the speaker to register the listener actually listening, because the speaker is present to register anything at all.

The 10% is calibrated to be doable. A person who is told "be fully in your body" often cannot find what that means under the pressure of conversation. A person told "listen to yourself 10% more" can find that. The smallness is what makes the instruction work.

## What You Will Notice

- The speech becomes audibly more present. The listener notices.
- The person's body relaxes slightly. The shoulders drop. The breath deepens.
- The words become slower without being dragged.
- The chasing-for-understanding (see [[chasing-understanding-as-control]]) drops out automatically. Once the speaker is partly present, the impulse to push the meaning into the listener loses most of its energy.
- It becomes easier to speak about topics that previously required strain. Topics the speaker loves arrive effortlessly. Topics the speaker does not love still take effort but the effort is now visible and addressable rather than disguised as communication-strain.

A telling diagnostic Joe uses: ask the person to talk about their favorite topic in the world while listening to themselves 10% more. They will report it was easy. Then ask them about something they do not care about with the same instruction. The difficulty of the second task surfaces what is actually hard about staying in the self when there is not an intrinsic want pulling them in.

## When to Use It

- Mid-conversation, when a coachee is visibly chasing understanding or vacating themselves to manage the listener. Drop the instruction in real time and watch the shift.
- During a difficult conversation in the coachee's life — to be deployed when they notice themselves leaving.
- In any moment when the coachee is speaking from anxiety about how they are being received.
- As a continuous practice for someone whose default is to be at the meta-position. The 10% number can grow over time. Most people, once they have the experience, naturally want more than 10%.

## What This Is Not

This is not "listen to yourself instead of the other person." That misses the point. Listening to yourself 10% more while continuing to engage with the other person produces deeper presence with both. See [[listening]] for the broader teaching: the deepest listening keeps some portion of attention in the self even while attuned to the other.

This is not a technique to produce a desired response in the listener. It is not a manipulation tool. If deployed as a technique to be understood better, it stops working — because the manipulation requires the same meta-position the practice is supposed to dissolve.

This is not a rule or a morality. The instruction is offered when the person is leaving themselves. It is not a constant should. See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]].

## Demonstrating It in Session

The most powerful version of this practice is not a homework assignment. It is an in-session demonstration. The coach asks the coachee to say something — anything — and then asks them to say the same thing again with 10% more self-listening. The difference is audible in seconds. The coachee can hear it themselves. The body knows.

From the session: Savannah said "I have a tendency to fully abandon myself when I'm interacting with someone else." Joe asked her to say it again, just listening to herself 10% more. She did. The second version sounded like her. The first sounded like a performance of self-awareness. She was the first to notice the difference.

This in-session demonstration is the practice in its most useful form. It bypasses the intellectual argument and goes straight to the felt shift. The person leaves the session knowing something in their body, not just knowing about it.

## An Internal Witness If "Listening to Self" Is Too Abstract

For some people the instruction "listen to yourself" remains too vague to install. The body does not know what self-as-listener even means. A useful workaround: pick a borrowed listener — a character, a real person, or an archetype — whose listening is vivid and easy to imagine. Take up residence as that listener pointed at yourself.

Savannah's spontaneous discovery in the session: "I just got like a Frasier Crane in my head. I'm listening. That's what I got. I got to be my own Frasier Crane." The reference is the radio-psychiatrist character whose tagline is "I'm listening." Joe agrees this is exactly the right move. The person notes it is easier to imagine becoming her own Frasier Crane than to imagine "her own self listening" in the abstract.

The transferable instruction: when the abstract version of self-listening cannot be found, find a concrete listener whose listening is easy to picture, and *be* that listener attending to yourself. The borrowed shape is a scaffold. The actual practice that grows is the same — what changes is the entry point. Some people find self-listening directly. Others need the metaphor of the listener-character first; the borrowed shape eventually drops away, and what remains is the same direct self-listening.

This is a close cousin of [[have-them-receive-your-listening]] — the move where the coach asks the coachee to feel the coach's listening of them in real time. Both work by the same mechanism: the felt experience of being listened to (by a real person, an imagined character, or one's own attention) has the same somatic quality, and any of those experiences can teach the body what self-listening feels like.
