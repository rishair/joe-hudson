# Track Forgiveness Somatically

## The Practice

A three-center body-and-mind check for whether forgiveness has actually happened. Forgiveness is a state of the body, not a verbal claim. The diagnostic asks the three centers (heart, head, gut) directly and reads the answer in the felt experience.

The form:

1. Bring the person or situation to mind.
2. Check the heart. Does it feel open? Can you feel love or basic goodwill toward them?
3. Check the head. Do you still have a story about them? Is there "they are X" running?
4. Check the gut. Is it clenched? Is the boundary held or braced?
5. Read the result. If any center is not yet at rest, that is the step of [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]] that still has work.

"For me, I tend to be more somatic, so it is feeling the heart. And also, is the mind — do I still have a story about the other person? Oh, they're an asshole, or they did that wrong. Do I have a wrong/right? So it's like, how open is the mind around it? How much has the mind let go of me/them, them bad, me good, me victim, them bully, or whatever the story of separation is. And somatically, does the heart feel open? And do — can I feel love for them? Do I have boundaries in the gut? Like, how much clench is there in the gut?" -- Tara Howley, I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

## What Each Center Reveals

### Heart Check

The question: when you bring this person to mind, does your heart feel open or closed?

Open: there is space, warmth, the possibility of love or goodwill. The person can be held in mind without the chest closing down.

Closed: there is tightness, contraction, a wall. The chest braces when the person is brought to mind. Love is not available.

If the heart is closed: the emotion work (step 1 of the three-step process) is not yet complete. There are still feelings that need to be felt and moved through. See [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]].

### Head Check

The question: do you still have a story about the other person?

The story has a characteristic shape: "they are X." "They are an asshole." "They are toxic." "They are manipulative." "They are not capable of love." Any sentence that locates the other person as a fixed kind of person is the story.

The release of the story does not mean believing the person is wonderful. It means the rigid categorization has dissolved. The person can be a person — capable of what they did, capable of other things, shaped by their own history, not summarized by your judgment.

If the story is still running: the curiosity work (step 2 of the three-step process) is not yet complete. The mind has not yet opened to wonder about what produced the behavior. The story is occupying the space wonder needs to do its work.

### Gut Check

The question: is your gut clenched when you think about this?

Clenched: there is tightness, bracing, a sense of needing to defend or protect. The body is still in protection-mode about this situation.

Unclenched: the gut is at rest. There is no bracing. The boundary, if any, is held without effort.

If the gut is clenched: the boundary work (step 3 of the three-step process) is not yet complete. Either no boundary has been drawn (and the gut is holding the protection job as resentment — see [[resentment-takes-the-place-of-boundaries]]), or the boundary has been drawn but is being held as defense rather than as care (see [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]).

## The Fuck-Them Test

A specific tell that surfaces frequently:

"If my thoughts are like, fuck them, I haven't quite finished the process yet." -- Tara Howley

The "fuck them" thought is a head-check failure. The story is still running. The mind has not yet let go of "them bad." The diagnostic flags it. The response is not to suppress the thought (that drives it underground) but to take it as data: the work is not done. Where is the unfinished piece? Usually one of:

- Anger that hasn't moved (heart step)
- The story is being held because the boundary feels missing (gut step)

"What's the real boundary that the fuck them — it's like, oh, I don't want to... Often it's like, I just, I don't want to be around that. Oh, right. I get that boundary." -- Tara Howley

The fuck-them often points at an un-drawn boundary. When the boundary surfaces, the fuck-them can release. The thought was the body's way of saying "I haven't yet given myself permission to not be around this."

## What Completion Looks Like

When all three centers are at rest with the person in mind:

- The heart is open. You can feel love or basic goodwill toward them.
- The mind has no story. You see them as a human being with a history, capable of what they did, not summarized by it.
- The gut is unclenched. The boundary, if any, is held without effort. There is no bracing.

This is the somatic signature of completed forgiveness. The verbal claim is not the test; this is.

The completion does not mean the relationship is repaired. The completion does not mean you want to be in their company. The completion is the state of your own body in relation to them. The boundary may still be "I do not want to spend time with you." That boundary held cleanly is part of the completion, not a sign of incompleteness.

"Just because I have gone through forgiveness doesn't mean I have to go spend my vacation time with these people, right? Or even go out for dinner. I say, no, I don't. I'm not really interested in hanging out, but I can have total forgiveness in my system." -- Tara Howley

## How To Do It Well

1. **Take time.** The check is not a quick body-scan. Bring the person to mind and let the body respond. The response may take a minute or longer to be felt clearly.

2. **Check each center separately.** The temptation is to check globally ("am I done with this?"). The separate checks are more diagnostic because they tell you which step needs more work.

3. **Trust the body, not the story you have about yourself.** You may want to be done with it. You may believe you should be done with it. The body is the test. If the body says not yet, not yet.

4. **Don't move on if any center is not at rest.** The pattern of declaring forgiveness while the body is still holding it is the coerced-forgiveness pattern in self-administered form. See [[dont-coerce-forgiveness]].

5. **Use the result to direct the next work.** The diagnostic is not just for verifying completion; it tells you which step of [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]] needs attention.

## What This Pairs With

- **Before:** [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]] — the process whose completion this diagnostic verifies.
- **During:** Use the check periodically as you work, not just at the end. Each pass tells you what is still active.
- **After:** When the check passes, the work is done for now. The check can be returned to if new material surfaces (which it often does — old wounds layer, and a new pass through the same situation can reveal what was not yet ready).

## Why Verbal Claims Don't Work

The temptation is to declare forgiveness done by saying it. "I forgive you." "I've let it go." "I'm over it." The body knows whether the claim is true. The verbal declaration without somatic backing is the coerced-forgiveness signature — see [[dont-coerce-forgiveness]].

The somatic check bypasses this. The body either is at rest or is not. The body cannot be argued with. The diagnostic is honest in a way the verbal claim cannot be.

This is why the practice is somatic rather than cognitive. The body is the only honest reporter on whether the work has actually happened.

## When To Use It

- During or after the three-step forgiveness work
- When you think you have forgiven and want to verify
- When you find yourself still thinking about the person and want to know why
- When a memory of the situation surfaces and you want to gauge where you are with it
- When someone asks you about the person or situation and you notice your body responding
- Periodically with long-held resentments to check whether the work is ready to deepen

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The practice is offered as instruction, like checking the oil in a car. There is no spiritual elaboration. The body is asked; the body answers; the answer directs the next action.
