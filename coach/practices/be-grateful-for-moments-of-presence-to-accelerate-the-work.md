# Be Grateful for the Moments of Presence to Accelerate the Work

## The Practice

When working on undoing chronic self-editing, chronic self-judgment, or chronic approval-seeking, notice the moments where you are present and not editing — and be grateful for them. The gratitude does not need to be elaborate. The recognition of the moment plus a felt sense of thanks for it is enough.

The practice is the efficiency multiplier on the larger work. The larger work (see [[grieving-the-self-editor]]) is the slow dismantling of the editing structure. The gratitude practice accelerates the dismantling because it teaches the system that the alive-state moments are valued and welcomed.

"The trick is to be grateful every time you recognize one of those moments. As far as efficiency goes, just being grateful for those moments will make the whole thing be more efficient." -- Joe Hudson, I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me

"So take — do it for a moment, like you had that moment, and feel grateful that there was this moment where you let yourself be you without the chain of the consistent editor." -- Joe Hudson, I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me

The practice is small. Two beats: notice the moment, feel grateful. Repeat each time a moment occurs.

## When Joe Uses This

The practice is offered when:

- The person has begun noticing their self-editing in real time and is asking what to do about it
- The person is in the middle of [[grieving-the-self-editor]] and needs something to do besides feeling the grief
- The person has had a moment of presence in the session and is about to move on without registering it
- The person is asking how to accelerate the work, or how to integrate what they have just experienced
- The person has been doing the work intellectually and needs a body-level practice that does not require analysis
- The person has just experienced a moment of "letting yourself be you without the chain of the consistent editor" and the moment is in danger of being lost

The practice is also a corrective when the person treats the moments of presence as fleeting or accidental. The gratitude treats them as data, as evidence, as anchors. The treatment changes what gets installed.

## What It Produces

When the practice is run consistently:

- The moments of presence increase in frequency. The system orients toward what is being valued.
- The transition between editor-mode and alive-mode shortens. The body learns to register the alive-state faster because it is being received.
- The grieving process moves faster. The waves of grief are interspersed with explicit moments of gratitude, which gives the dismantling work a positive valence rather than a purely loss-oriented one.
- The person starts noticing alive-state moments they would have missed. The looking-for-them-to-be-grateful-for-them is itself a form of attention that produces more of them.
- The shame-loop around the editing softens. Even when the editor fires, the gratitude for the next moment of presence prevents the shame from compounding.

When the practice is not run, the alive-state moments occur but do not get installed. The system experiences them as anomalies rather than as evidence of an available state. The editor reasserts itself faster because nothing has registered the alternative.

## The Specific Move

The move has three components, in order:

1. **Notice.** When you become aware that you just had a moment of presence — a moment of laughing without then covering your mouth, a moment of crying without then composing yourself, a moment of saying the true thing without then explaining it away — notice that the moment happened. The noticing is itself the first piece of the practice. Many people miss the moments because the editor reasserts so quickly that the moment is gone before being registered.

2. **Feel grateful.** Not say "I am grateful." Feel the gratitude. This is the same somatic feeling Joe describes in [[gratitude-as-full-body-sport]] — a small tingly warm expansive feeling in the body, even if brief. The felt-gratitude is what does the integration work. The said-gratitude is performative and does not engage the body's installation mechanism.

3. **Let the moment be installed.** Stay with the gratitude for a beat. Do not rush to the next thing. The brief lingering is what tells the body: this is the kind of moment we are oriented toward. Without the lingering, the moment passes without anchoring. With it, the moment becomes part of the body's evidence-base for what is available.

The total time required is small — often 5 to 15 seconds. The frequency is what matters. Many small moments of gratitude across a day produce more change than one elaborate gratitude session.

## Why Gratitude Specifically Works on This

The practice rests on a structural feature of how the self-editor operates. The editor fires when the person's natural expression is treated as un-safe. The system has been calibrated, through years of practice, that the natural expression must be edited. The recalibration requires evidence that the natural expression is welcome — both internally and externally.

The gratitude provides the internal evidence. When the system produces a moment of un-edited presence and the person responds with felt gratitude, the body registers: this is valued, this is welcome, this is wanted. The signal is the opposite of the signal the editor has been responding to. Enough repetitions of the new signal reorient the system.

This is the same mechanism as [[gratitude-as-integration-mechanism]] — gratitude is the felt-state that allows the body to install new patterns. Without the gratitude, even the experience of presence does not stick. The mind may remember the moment, but the body does not register the moment as the new normal. With the gratitude, the body integrates.

See also [[gratitude-as-healing-medicine]] for the broader account of why gratitude is the right fuel for transformation work. The current practice is one specific application of that broader principle, targeted at the editor-dismantling context.

## How This Differs From Generic Gratitude Practice

Generic gratitude practice ("name three things you are grateful for each morning") is a different practice with different effects. The generic practice produces benefits via the regular cultivation of the gratitude state. This practice is more specific: it ties the gratitude to a particular kind of moment (the moments of un-edited presence) and runs throughout the day rather than at a scheduled time.

The specificity matters because the work being done is specific. The editor-dismantling requires that the alive-state moments be the ones receiving the gratitude. Gratitude for unrelated things ("I am grateful for my health, my family, my job") does not do the editor-dismantling work. The system does not link the gratitude to the alive-state moments because the gratitude is not pointed at those moments.

The practice can run alongside generic gratitude practice. The two do not interfere. The specific practice does the editor-dismantling work; the generic practice does the broader cultivation of the gratitude state. Both are useful for different purposes.

## How This Differs From Praising Yourself

A common misreading: the practice as praising yourself for the moment of presence. "Good job, I was present just now." The misreading installs a new editor — the praising-self — on top of the old structure. The praising-self is also approval-seeking, just internally directed. The practice has been hijacked.

The corrective: the gratitude is not for what you did; it is for the moment itself. The moment of presence happened. Be grateful that it happened. The grammar is the diagnostic. "Thank you for that moment" is the practice. "Good job for being present" is the hijacking.

The distinction is subtle but operationally important. Gratitude for the moment treats the moment as a gift; praise for the action treats the action as an accomplishment. The first orients the system toward receiving; the second orients it toward producing. Receiving is what the editor was preventing. Producing is what the editor was running on. The practice is on the receiving side.

## What This Practice Is Not

- **It is not a self-improvement track-keeping.** "I had three moments of presence today" turns the practice into a scorecard. The scorecard reactivates the approval-seeking engine that the practice is meant to dismantle. The moments are noticed and appreciated; they are not counted.
- **It is not a substitute for the grieving work.** The gratitude is the multiplier on the grieving. It does not replace it. Trying to skip to the gratitude without doing the grief produces a forced positivity that has no body in it. The grief and the gratitude run side by side.
- **It is not gratitude for the editor.** Some teachings recommend being grateful for the inner critic / editor as a way to soften it. That is a different practice (see the menu in [[menu-of-responses-to-the-self-critical-voice]]). This practice is gratitude for the moments when the editor is NOT firing. The two practices can coexist but are distinct in their target.
- **It is not performative.** The gratitude has to be felt to work. Saying the words without the felt-sense does no installation. The body knows the difference even if the mind cannot tell.

## The Specific Application in the Source Session

In the source session, the client asked Joe when he had experienced not judging himself before. Joe's response was to redirect to the moment that had just happened in the session: "About 2 minutes ago. If you're watching the film back, it's right after that moment I said you keep on shutting it down. There was like a moment there."

The client confirmed: "No, I totally felt that."

Joe then introduced the practice: "The trick is to be grateful every time you recognize one of those moments. If — as far as efficiency goes, just being grateful for those moments will make the whole thing be more efficient. So take — do it for a moment, like you had that moment, and feel grateful that there was this moment where you let yourself be you without the chain of the consistent editor."

The teaching is structurally complete: identify a specific moment, run the gratitude, let the body register it. The practice was given as the closing instruction of the session. The implication: the work continues now in the form of running this practice in daily life.

## Tone

The instruction is delivered with light conviction. Joe's "as far as efficiency goes" is the tone marker — slightly playful, slightly insistent. The practice is being offered as the smart-move shortcut, not as a serious ritual. The lightness is functional. A heavy framing would invite the practice to become another performance.

The energy when describing what is being celebrated is warm: "let yourself be you without the chain of the consistent editor." The phrasing locates the moment as the self being freed from a constraint, which is the felt-tone the gratitude is meant to engage. The warmth in the framing is itself part of the modeling.
