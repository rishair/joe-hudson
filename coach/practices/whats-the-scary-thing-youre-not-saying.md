# What's the Scary Thing You're Not Saying? (As a Team-Meeting Question)

## The Practice

A recurring opening question in team meetings: "What's the scary thing that you're not saying?" The question is asked regularly, not as a one-time intervention. Over time, it institutionalizes elephant-naming as a normal part of the team's working rhythm.

"You know, we do this in our business, you know, What's the scary thing you're not saying? In the beginning of our team meetings, one of the main questions we have is, what's the scary thing that you're not saying? Be the example. Show that that conversation can be had regularly without any defense or punishment. That is the way to do it." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The practice is the team-level extension of the individual practice [[whats-the-scary-truth]]. The individual version is a self-inquiry tool: what is the scary thing that is true for me right now? The team version externalizes the same inquiry into shared space. What is the scary thing in this room that is not being said?

## How to Run It

1. **Place it at the opening of meetings, not the close.** Asking at the end signals that the team is supposed to come up with something tidy. Asking at the open creates space for the scary thing to shape what the meeting actually addresses.

2. **Ask it routinely.** The first few times, expect nothing. People will give safe answers or pass. The practice depends on repetition. Over weeks, people learn the question is genuine and start using it.

3. **Receive what comes without defense or punishment.** This is the load-bearing piece. The question only works if what gets named gets received well. The first time someone names something hard and gets defensiveness or subtle punishment, the practice collapses. From then on, the question is performative.

4. **Model first.** The leader names their own scary thing. Often, before anyone else will. The team is watching to see whether the question is real.

5. **Let the answer shape the meeting.** If someone names something significant, the rest of the meeting's agenda may need to flex. Holding rigidly to the agenda after a scary thing has been named signals that the question was decorative.

## What "Scary Thing" Covers

The category is intentionally broad:

- A concern about a project that has not been brought up
- A worry about a person on the team
- A doubt about a strategic direction
- A discomfort with how a recent interaction went
- A personal struggle that is bleeding into work
- A judgment about another team member that has not been shared
- A piece of feedback for the leader that has not been delivered
- An observation about the team's culture that nobody wants to name

The breadth is part of the point. Narrowing the question (to project concerns, to feedback) loses the elephant-surfacing function. The scary thing can be about anything; the practice is about whether it can be said.

## Why This Works

The practice institutionalizes what naming the elephant requires: regular permission, regular reception, regular demonstration that the room can hold the hard thing.

Without the regular practice, naming the elephant requires courage every time. Each person has to overcome the hump alone, with no track record of the room being able to receive. The result: most elephants stay unspoken. They accumulate. The team's actual work gets done around them.

With the regular practice, naming the elephant becomes ordinary. The hump shrinks because it is being crossed weekly. The team builds a track record of receiving hard things. The cost of naming drops. Over time, the elephants get named while they are still small.

This is one operational form of the broader principle that [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]]. The practice creates the regular conditions under which the things that would otherwise become resentments or stagnations get surfaced and addressed.

## The Relationship to Be-the-Example

The practice cannot be implemented procedurally without the cultural underpinning. Asking the question without modeling the answer, without receiving honestly, without demonstrating that the room can hold hard things, produces a worse result than not asking at all.

Brett names the failure mode in the same conversation:

"There's the procedural way. Where I could imagine a board meeting that starts with, say the scary thing that you're not saying. But anytime somebody brings something up, it turns into a crucifixion. A shit show. Right." -- Brett Kistler

Joe's response: be the example. Show that the conversation can be had regularly without defense or punishment. There is no other way.

"Be the example. You know, we do this in our business... Be the example. Show that that conversation can be had regularly without any defense or punishment. That is the way to do it. There's really no other way to do it. No words are going to make a difference. And if you get defensive, apologize." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The "and if you get defensive, apologize" is the practical addition. The leader will sometimes get defensive. The recovery is to notice and apologize. The apology, repeated, teaches the team that defensiveness is not the death of the practice; it is something to be addressed and moved through.

## What Happens Over Time

When the practice runs long enough to become real, several things shift:

- The team's actual concerns reach the leader faster. There is no longer a multi-week lag between the scary thing forming and the leader learning about it.
- Solutions get found earlier in the failure cycle. Problems are addressed at the "this might become a problem" stage rather than the "this is now a crisis" stage.
- Trust between team members deepens, because the scary things have been said and the relationships have held them.
- People start to look forward to bringing scary things, rather than dreading it. See the leadership posture in [[the-elephant-in-the-room]] where Joe describes leaders who "look forward to someone bringing them up" once the cycle has been broken.
- The team's overall capacity for difficult conversation expands. Conversations that would have been impossible become ordinary.

"It's so rewarding. As soon as you see the cycle as a, in a, in a, as a CEO or in a family, that the freedom on the other side of the elephant in the room, you just look for them. You can't wait for them. You look forward to someone bringing them up." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

## The Distinction from a Performative Version

A team that asks "any concerns?" at the end of a meeting is not doing this practice. The "any concerns" version is a check-the-box ritual that signals concerns are not welcome:

- It comes at the end, when the meeting is already winding down
- It is phrased generically, with no acknowledgment that scary truths are harder than ordinary feedback
- It expects nothing and gets nothing
- It is asked by a leader who has not modeled the practice themselves

The "scary thing you're not saying" version is structurally different:

- It comes at the open, where the answer can shape what happens next
- It names that the relevant content is *scary* — acknowledging the cost of saying
- It is asked by a leader who has named their own scary things first
- It is asked regularly, building the trust that the question is genuine

## The Connection to Other Team Practices

The practice fits in a family of leader-level practices for institutionalizing truth-telling:

- [[whats-the-scary-truth]]: the individual self-inquiry version
- [[weekly-tension-inventory]]: a related personal practice for surfacing what has not been addressed
- [[five-star-meeting-commitment]]: the meeting-level forcing function that surfaces what the leader has been avoiding
- [[no-doctrine-leadership]]: the related doctrine for surfacing the no rather than the platitudes
- [[boeing-ford-bad-news]]: the example of a leader who rewards the bringing of hard truths

A leader using all of these is creating a complete environment in which elephant-naming becomes ordinary. The scary-thing question is the regular surfacing prompt. The no doctrine is the regular reception practice. The five-star commitment is the regular forcing function. The example of promoting truth-bringers is the periodic structural reinforcement.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A team meeting opens with a question that creates an unfamiliar silence — and then someone says something real
- A leader asks the question and nobody answers, and the leader answers it themselves
- A team that has run the practice for months names something significant that has been hovering for weeks
- A meeting that was going to be tactical becomes strategic because the scary thing reframed what was actually needed

## Anti-Patterns

- **Asking the question procedurally without modeling the answer.** Produces a performative ritual that signals the question is not real.
- **Reacting defensively the first time something hard is named.** The team learns the question is dangerous and stops answering. Recovery: apologize and ask the question again next meeting.
- **Asking the question at the end of the meeting.** Signals that the answer is supposed to be brief and not shape what just happened.
- **Treating the question as a substitute for the underlying culture work.** The question accelerates an honest culture; it cannot substitute for one.
- **Punishing the person who names the scary thing, even subtly.** The single fastest way to destroy the practice. The named person becomes the cautionary tale for everyone else.

## Tone

The leader asks this question with genuine interest. Not as a checkbox, not as a challenge, not as a test. The tone is closer to "I'm curious what's here" than to "let's surface the problems." The interest in the answer is what makes the question land. A leader who is bored by the answer or who is asking it to prove they want honesty will get nothing back.
