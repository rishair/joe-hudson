# List the People You Admire and the Mistakes They Made

## The Practice

Write down all the people you admire most. Make sure there are at least 10 or 12 of them. Under each name, write down all the mistakes you know they have made and all the ways they did not know what they were doing when they started.

Then sit with what you wrote. Allow yourself to feel it.

"Go home and write down all the people that you admire most. Make sure that there's 10 or 12 of them. And then under each name, write down all the mistakes that you've known they've made and all the ways they didn't know what they were doing when they started. And that's it. Just actually allow yourself to feel and sit in that experience." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

## Purpose

The practice operationalizes [[everyone-doing-something-real-is-an-imposter]] and [[expert-is-imposter-with-most-reps]] in the body of the person. The intellectual claim ("everyone is figuring it out") does not always land. The exercise makes the claim concrete by populating it with the specific people the person has put on pedestals.

The action of writing the list makes the gap collapse. The person discovers that the writers, founders, athletes, leaders they admire have all been in the same not-knowing they are currently in. The collapse happens not as a thought but as evidence in their own handwriting.

This is also a take-home version of the question [[who-isnt-an-imposter]]. The question, in session, points at the empty category. The practice, at home, populates the category with specific names and watches it stay empty.

## When To Use It

- After a session where imposter syndrome has been the topic. The session can dissolve the frame intellectually; the experiment integrates it.
- For someone who reports imposter syndrome that returns episodically. The list becomes a referenceable artifact they can return to when the feeling comes back.
- For someone who is paralyzed by the comparison to specific figures (a particular founder, writer, athlete). Including those specific figures in the list does the work directly.

## What It Tends to Produce

- Relief. The gap between "me" and "them" turns out to be reps, not category.
- A specific kind of confusion at first. The mind reaches for the admired figure's success story; the practice asks for their failures. The mind has not been trained to populate that side.
- An emotional component if the person allows it. Joe's instruction is to allow yourself to feel and sit in the experience -- not just to list it. The integration is in the sitting.

## Notes on Doing It Well

- Twelve people is enough. Fewer than ten leaves room for the mind to say "those are special cases." Twelve forces a pattern.
- Mistakes the person personally remembers count for the most. Borrowed examples from biographies count less. The practice works through felt knowledge.
- Do not edit out the painful mistakes. The financial failures, the relationship failures, the public embarrassments. These are the most useful entries.
- Sit with the list when you finish. Do not move on immediately. This is where the integration happens.

## Boundary Conditions

This practice does not address the somatic layer. The imposter feeling is fear in the body. The list shifts the cognitive frame; it does not move the activated state in the moment. For the in-the-moment work, see [[say-im-excited-ten-times]]. The two practices pair: the list at home, the verbal practice in the moment.

The practice can be misused if the person turns it into another performance ("I did my homework"). The instruction to *sit with what you wrote* is load-bearing. Without sitting, this becomes another item on a to-do list rather than an experiment.
