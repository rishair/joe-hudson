# Say the Thing You're Not Saying

## The Practice

Notice any thought you have to say something and then do not say. Say it. One thing a day. Learn to say it kindly. Continue until the suppressed material is no longer hidden.

The practice is deceptively simple. The mechanism is precise: every withheld thought is a place where you are managing the other person's possible reaction, which means a place where a trigger is operating -- either yours or theirs. The unspoken thought is the trigger's hiding place. Say it, and the trigger surfaces. Surface it, and it can be worked.

"Notice any time that you hesitate to say anything because your partner is gonna have an emotional reaction or your partner is too weak to handle it or because you're trying to protect your partner... any way that you're not actually -- that you have a thought to say something and you don't say it, those are great places to find the triggers. And you can do that same mechanism. Just look for all those places you're not saying anything and say them, and then see if you're not triggered." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## When Joe Assigns This

The practice is for two specific people:

**The person who reports no triggers.** "It's been three years and I've never been triggered by my relationship." Joe's response: then you need a different way to find them. The triggers are there. They are buried under things you are not saying. The hesitations are the map. See [[we-never-fight]] and [[walking-on-eggshells]].

**The person who has a strong sense of obligation.** Joe specifically names this. The person who is wired around "I should be the good partner / the good employee / the good child" usually has many things they do not say. The shoulding system filters out the unspoken material before it reaches awareness. The practice forces it back into view.

The practice is not for the person who is already saying everything they think. They have a different problem.

## How To Do It

1. **For one day, track every hesitation.** Every time you almost said something and did not, note it. The frequency itself is information.

2. **Pick one thing per day to say.** Just one. Not the hardest one. Not the easiest one. One that you would normally have skipped.

3. **Say it kindly.** Joe is explicit: this is not "yell what you have been suppressing." It is "say the thing in a way that respects both of you." Joe is also explicit about the trap: he is not telling you to hedge. He is telling you to learn to say it cleanly and kindly.

4. **Notice what happens in your body.** The discomfort you feel before saying it is the trigger. The discomfort you feel after is information about where the next layer is.

5. **Notice your partner's response.** Do not modify what you say in advance to avoid the reaction. Modify how you deliver it to be kind. The information is in their response, including responses you tried to prevent.

## The Four-Way Demonstration

Joe gives the airport-dropoff example: a wife is triggered because her husband barely stopped to drop her off, did not get out, did not hug her. There are at least four ways she could address this:

- **The discharge:** "What the fuck? What are you doing? Like, I'm your wife, get out of the fucking car." (Anger discharged at the partner. See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]].)
- **The clean and kind:** "Hey, sweetheart, I would love you to get out of the car and say goodbye to me. That'd make me feel great if you could do that leaving."
- **The guilt:** "If you loved me, you would."
- **The defensive:** "Yeah, so, yeah, I mean, if you cared, you'd get out of the car."

The practice is the second one. Joe's key teaching: "There's so many ways to ask for it. But the most important way is to ask for it and then get good at asking for it, then be kind. But whatever is required to actually ask for it directly and, and cleanly, go there."

The order matters: ask for it first. Get good at the asking. Then refine the kindness. Most people skip step one because they want to start at step three. Without step one, there is nothing to refine.

## Why It Has to Be Kind But Said

The trap on one side: people who hedge or never ask. They produce eggshells and resentment. See [[walking-on-eggshells]].

The trap on the other side: people who use "I'm being honest" or "I have to say what I feel" to discharge anger or attack. They produce defensiveness and distance.

The practice threads the needle by requiring both: say it, and learn the kindness. Joe will not let people use kindness as an excuse not to say it, and he will not let people use directness as an excuse to be cruel.

"Take it slow and maybe one thing a day, or, um, and also learn how to say the things really kindly. But I think that, that I don't want to say that because then people will hedge what they say instead of learn how to do it in a kind way." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## What It Produces

The first few uses surface small things: small preferences, small irritations, small wants. The practice gets harder as the larger withheld material starts to come up. People often discover triggers they did not know they had, or did not know their partner had.

The practice also surfaces the underlying agreements that were never spoken. The "we don't talk about your mother" rule. The "we don't bring up the money thing" rule. These were not negotiated. They accumulated. The practice makes them visible so they can be renegotiated. See [[renegotiate-commitments-as-you-change]].

## Anti-Patterns

- **Hedging the saying.** "Well, I mean, I just kind of thought maybe, you know, it might be nice if perhaps..." This is not saying it. This is performing the practice without doing it. See [[dont-half-ass-the-experiment]].
- **Saving up and dumping.** Three weeks of suppressed material delivered in one session. This is not the practice. The practice is one thing a day, surfaced cleanly.
- **Using "honesty" to attack.** "I'm just being honest" is often the discharge dressed up as the practice.
- **Modifying the partner's expected response.** The practice does not require the partner to react well. It requires you to say the thing.

## Boundary Conditions

This practice presumes a relationship where both people are in some way safe. If saying the thing produces unsafe reactions (physical threat, severe withdrawal of necessary resources), the practice is not the right entry point. The work shifts to safety first.

If the relationship is one where the person has been told "everything is fine" for so long that they cannot tell what to say, the practice can start with much smaller content: "I prefer this restaurant," "I do not like this show," "I would like to leave the party in 30 minutes." Even the smallest hesitations are diagnostic.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A jolt of fear before the saying
- The discovery that something you thought was minor was actually charged
- The partner's surprised reaction to a small thing, which surfaces a larger pattern
- The relief of having said it
- The realization that you have been hiding more than you knew
- A wave of grief about how much you have been holding back for how long

## Tone

Joe gives the practice playfully but seriously. He delivers the airport-dropoff example with humor. The underlying instruction is firm: just say it. The kindness is real, but the kindness is not allowed to be a workaround.
