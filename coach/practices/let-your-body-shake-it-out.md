# Let Your Body Shake It Out

## Definition

When a large fear has arrived -- a major business setback, a relationship rupture, a near-miss -- the body often needs to move the emotion through physically before the mind can think about it clearly. Lying down and letting the body shake is one of the simplest forms of this somatic release.

The shake is not performed. It is allowed. The body has its own knowledge of how to discharge stored fear, and the instruction is to stop interfering with that knowledge.

## The Practice

Brett describes a specific moment of using this practice when a major client reduced their contract, sending the business from cash-flow positive to cash-flow negative overnight:

"I just let myself feel that fear, like, oh no, this is the end, everything's going to collapse, ah. And I just let myself feel that. And it was just like, I laid down on my bed and just like let my body shake. Just like let my body just shake it out a little bit. And then like I came out of that and I was like, well, this is a really great opportunity..." -- Brett Kistler, Welcoming Fear

The structure of the practice:

1. Stop trying to think the problem through. The thinking will not be useful until the body has discharged.
2. Lie down or sit somewhere private and stable.
3. Allow the catastrophic story to surface. "This is the end. Everything's collapsing. I'm a failure." Do not believe the story. Do not argue with it. Let it be what the fear is saying.
4. Let the body do what it wants. Often this is shaking, trembling, rapid breath, tears, sounds. Sometimes it is stillness with intense internal activity. Do not direct it.
5. Wait until the discharge subsides on its own. Five minutes, twenty minutes, sometimes longer.
6. Notice what is available afterward.

The practice ends not when the fear is gone but when the body's process has completed its current pass. The body knows when it is done.

## Why It Works

Fear is not just a mental event. It is a body state: cortisol, adrenaline, muscle tension, breath constriction. These physiological components take time and motion to clear. Trying to think one's way past them while they are still active does not work. The thinking happens but is shaped by the unprocessed body state, so the conclusions are distorted.

Allowing the body to discharge clears the physiological substrate. After the discharge, the same situation can be thought about with a different quality of attention. Often what looked like catastrophe before the shake becomes "well, this is actually an opportunity to do X" afterward.

This is consistent with research on trauma release (Bessel van der Kolk, Peter Levine) and with cross-cultural body-based practices for fear and grief. The body is the instrument that processes the emotion; the mind comes online afterward.

## What This Is Not

This is not catharsis as performance. The shake is not done to feel better. It is allowed because it is what the body is trying to do anyway, and the alternative is to suppress it back into [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]] where it will leak out later.

This is not a substitute for action. After the shake, action becomes clearer and more available. The shake is the precondition for clean action, not a replacement for it.

This is not for situations that are still actively dangerous. Lying down to shake while the building is on fire is not the practice. The practice is for after the immediate danger or in private moments where the person has time to let the body process.

## The Reframe That Follows

What often happens after the shake is a spontaneous reframe. The thing that was "the end" becomes "the opening." The thing that was "a disaster" becomes "the thing that needed to happen." This is not the practice making the situation good. It is the practice clearing the fear-distortion so the actual range of possible interpretations becomes visible.

In Brett's case, the shake was followed by recognition that losing the big client was "a really great opportunity, an opportunity for me to get out of the golden handcuffs of having one client bringing in most of the money and to start really leveraging some of the business development that we'd been doing." Two months later the company was in a better position than before the loss.

The reframe is not always optimistic. Sometimes the shake clears the way for grief that the situation is indeed bad and that grieving is what is needed now. Either way, the response that follows the discharge is shaped by reality rather than by the fear distortion.

## When to Reach for This

- After receiving bad news that has produced a body-level shock.
- When a person describes "freaking out" or "panicking" and the body is visibly activated.
- After a near-miss in a high-stakes situation.
- When fear is preventing clear thinking and the person is grinding their way into worse decisions.
- During [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]]: the long-form version of the same principle applied to extended periods of stress.

## Relationship to Daily Practice

The shake-it-out is one acute application of the broader principle in [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[daily-emotional-yoga]]. The daily practice keeps the system clear so that large discharges happen less often. The acute practice handles moments when the suppression has built up or when an event has exceeded the daily practice's capacity to process.

A person doing daily emotional yoga still has moments that require the shake-it-out. The two practices complement each other.
