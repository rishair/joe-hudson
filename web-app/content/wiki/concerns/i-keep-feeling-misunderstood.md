# I keep feeling misunderstood / I can't make people understand me

## What Someone Says

The complaint has a distinctive shape: it pairs an *effort claim* with a *result frustration*. The person reports working hard at communicating well — using the right words, choosing the right tone, taking the right care — and getting an unwanted result anyway.

- "I want to do better with feeling misunderstood."
- "When I make all attempts and use the best communication hygiene I possibly can, and the other party seems determined to take different meaning than what I'm giving, I struggle a lot with that."
- "I'm trying to communicate with someone, it's because I want them to understand what I'm saying."
- "I tend to fall all over myself to try to correct that misunderstanding."
- "If I'm falling all over myself, I'm at least still trying. So that still gives me the illusion of control."
- "I want to feel like there's always something I can still do."
- "I'm communicating with you in a way that is clear even and with very specific words."
- "I struggle a lot with that in many areas of my life, whether that's professionally, in my relationship, in interpersonal relationships."

The frustration is genuine. The hidden hope inside the complaint is usually: *tell me the communication technique that will guarantee I am understood.* The person is asking for a tool, not for the reframe that is about to land.

## Detection Cues

- Surface complaints framed as the listener's failure: "they don't get me," "they're being obtuse," "they hear what they want to hear."
- Emphasis on the speaker's effort: words like "I tried so hard," "I was so careful," "I used the best communication," "I chose every word."
- A claim of "good faith" or "good intentions" early in the explanation: "I'm coming into this with truly good intentions."
- Anxiety language attached to not being understood: "panic," "distress," "anxiety building up like concrete blocks."
- A "pendulum" frame: oscillating between "I'll guide them to understanding" and "well, you better freaking understand."
- Asks about written communication or asynchronous communication ("how do I make sure they understand me in writing?") — the channel changes but the same control reach is operating.
- The complaint can apply across domains (work, romance, family) without the person noticing that the constant is them, not the listeners.

The most useful diagnostic: notice whether the person is monitoring the listener's reception while speaking *to you in the session*. If yes, you are watching the pattern happen in the room.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the concern is the [[wanting-others-to-listen-is-the-block]] pattern in its most painful form. The person has made it their job to produce understanding in another nervous system — a job that cannot be done, because comprehension lives in the listener, not the speaker. The harder they try, the more the listener feels controlled, and the more the listener resists. The complaint is the visible end of the loop. The control is the engine.

Underneath the control is a deeper move: [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]]. While trying to make sure the other person understands, the person has left their own body. They are not in themselves. They are at a meta-position monitoring how their words are landing. This is not just the cause of the failed communication. It is the cause of the unbearable feeling. The unheard-ness the person is reporting is more accurately *the not-being-in-themselves while speaking* than it is *the other person's failure to listen*.

And underneath that is the [[two-levels-of-hurt-in-being-misunderstood]] structure. The first hurt is not feeling understood. The second hurt is not feeling that the *goodness* of what one brings is being recognized. The person is asking the other to confirm not just the content but the giver. When that confirmation does not arrive, both layers throb.

Often underneath *that* is a childhood imprint: the person grew up around someone whose reactions were not about them — an alcoholic, a depressed parent, a mentally ill caregiver — and learned early that their own happiness was dependent on managing the other person's reaction. They became expert at communicating to get an outcome from someone whose outcome they could not actually shape. The adult complaint is the grown-up version of that childhood lesson.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people can present this concern with very different work to do underneath. Joe's read decides the path.

- If [[chasing-understanding-as-control]]: the person is performing more and more elaborate attempts to be understood, and each attempt feels (to the listener) like pressure. Joe's path: the live demonstration that the chasing pushes people away. Often via the [[say-the-same-sentence-five-ways]] move.
- If [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]]: the person stops looking at the coach, leaves their body, and starts monitoring how their words are landing. Joe's path: name it, then [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]] until the person can feel the difference.
- If [[in-the-story-im-not-being-seen]]: the person narrates the chain of misunderstanding events as proof that the listener is failing, rather than letting themselves be seen in the room right now. Joe's path: mirror the judgment back, ask how the seeing is going both directions, and stage the moment so the felt experience cannot be avoided.
- If [[ego-protection-as-need-to-be-understood]]: the person frames the need to be understood as morally neutral and is surprised when Joe names it as ego protection. Joe's path: distinguish *having ego* from *protecting ego*, then ask what they would say without the protection.
- If [[intellectual-question-with-emotional-body]]: the person asks the precise communication-strategy question while their body is showing a much deeper hurt. Joe's path: name the gap, then ask what arrives in the body once the head-question is set aside.

## Coaching Path

The arc Joe runs is not "give better communication advice." It is the inversion. Some version of this sequence repeats.

1. **Surface the cost of the control.** Ask: "what's the problem with being misunderstood?" Then ask: "what would happen if you didn't fall all over yourself?" The person discovers the anxiety. They also discover that the chasing is, in their own words, "the illusion of control."
2. **Invert the responsibility.** "What makes it your job to be understood? What makes it not my job to try to understand you?" The reframe lands; the person notices they have been carrying a job that is not theirs.
3. **Stage the dynamic live.** Joe asks the person to make him fully responsible for understanding her, then to do it with no defense, then with vulnerability. The [[say-the-same-sentence-five-ways]] move usually appears here. The person hears, in the same room, how different versions of the same words produce different responses in the listener.
4. **Name the two hurts.** Once the person is somatically open, Joe names the second hurt — that what is not being recognized is not just the content but the *goodness* the person is bringing. This often produces the deepest contact in the session.
5. **Bring the focus back to self-listening.** Ask the person to listen to themselves 10% more while saying the same thing. The shift is immediate and felt. The person discovers that being-heard-by-self changes the quality of their speech in a way that makes being-heard-by-others almost a downstream effect.
6. **Hand them the simple hack.** "Put your attention in your inner ear." See [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]]. The instruction is small enough to do anywhere and produces the felt shift on demand. If the person still cannot find self-listening as a felt experience, use [[have-them-receive-your-listening]] — direct their attention to the listening you are already giving them in the room, then name the parallel.
7. **(Optional, only if there is time and trust) Name the childhood imprint.** The deeper origin — that early life trained the person to make their happiness depend on someone else's reaction — can be named gently. The instruction stays the same: listen to yourself. The childhood pattern is the why; the practice is the how.

The arc avoids any move toward "here's a better way to phrase things" or "try active listening" or "use nonviolent communication." The teaching is consistent: a technique applied from outside the self does not work. The state of being in oneself while speaking is what works. Everything else is downstream.

## What NOT To Do

- Do not give communication tips. The person is asking for them. The asking is the symptom. Giving them deepens the pattern.
- Do not validate the framing that the other person is the problem. The other person may genuinely be a poor listener. That is not the lever the speaker has.
- Do not teach nonviolent communication as a strategy. It can be used cleanly; it can also become a more sophisticated version of the same chasing. See [[view-as-technique]].
