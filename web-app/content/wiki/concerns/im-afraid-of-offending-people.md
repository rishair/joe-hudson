# I'm afraid of offending people / How do I be myself without offending people

## What Someone Says

The concern often arrives as a workplace problem that doubles as a life problem. The person knows they are holding back. They cannot pinpoint why they cannot just speak.

- "How do I be myself at work? How do I interrupt people? How do I say what I want without it feeling like I'm attacking them?"
- "I'm confident in myself, but people get offended."
- "I have this belief that people can't handle me."
- "I want to deliver quality, but I also have this should about not offending."
- "I felt like I was doing great. Things are moving. And then I'm working with Joe. Okay, let's find a stuck place."
- "There was someone talking too much in a meeting. I was confident I had the information. I wanted to stop the meeting. And I just waited."
- "I'm afraid I'll come across as rude."

The concern often comes with a self-aware twist: the person already knows they hold back and is trying to figure out why. They have framed it as "how do I be myself" rather than "how do I avoid being myself." The framing itself is the trap.

## Detection Cues

- The person describes a specific behavior they could not do (interrupt, disagree, ask for what they wanted) and frames it as a self-expression problem.
- The word "offend" or its softer synonyms ("upset," "bother," "trigger") appears repeatedly.
- The person has theories about why other people are fragile or get offended easily.
- They reference role models (Martin Luther King, Steve Jobs, Mandela) as proof that being yourself requires offending people, but cannot follow the implication for themselves.
- Conflict avoidance is reframed as kindness, politeness, or quality.
- A logic chain: "I should be myself" + "if I'm myself I'll offend people" + "I can't offend people" = stuck.
- The person describes managing the dial: not too much, not too little, get it just right.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the offending story is shame. Specifically [[shame-as-disconnection]]: the body's report that connection is broken. The person is organizing their behavior around not feeling that shame. The strategy is: if I never offend anyone, no one will pull away, and I will not feel the disconnection.

The strategy fails on two levels. First, it is impossible. Every human offends some people some of the time. Even the most polite person violates someone's expectation. Second, the strategy itself creates shame: by not being themselves, the person disconnects from themselves, which produces the exact shame the strategy was designed to avoid. See [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]].

The real coaching question is not "how do I be myself without offending people." It is: "what's underneath my refusal to feel shame?"

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person is [[looking-for-whats-wrong]]: they have manufactured the stuckness because they believe the work requires problems. Joe will surface the question itself as a clue.
- If the person is [[rehearsing-how-to-say-it]]: they are crafting the delivery in their head, trying to find the words that would not offend. Joe will move them off the script and into the feeling.
- If the person is [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]: they are performing being okay rather than feeling the underneath. Joe will trigger the actual feeling in the room.
- If the person is in [[fight-or-roll-over-binary]]: the only options on the table are full offense or full self-erasure. Joe will name the binary and look for the third option.
- If the person uses [[linguistic-caveat]]: hedging in the language itself signals the same hedge they perform in life. Joe will question the caveat.

## Coaching Path

1. Surface the manufactured problem. If the person opens with "I'm trying to figure out what's wrong," Joe asks: "what makes it not obvious? What makes you need to figure that out?" See [[looking-for-whats-wrong]].

2. Test the premise. Joe asks for an example of someone who delivered quality without offending people. The example fails (MLK offended people, every leader offends people). The "I want to be myself but not offend" frame collapses.

3. The button question. "If I could press a button and you would never offend anybody again, would you want me to push it?" The hesitation is the data. See [[press-the-button-thought-experiment]].

4. Calibrate the threshold. "What percentage of the time do you want to not offend people?" The forced specificity makes the absolute frame examinable. See [[calibrate-the-threshold]].

5. Trigger the shame live. Joe role-plays the offended party. He gets sharp, dismissive, accusatory. The person feels the actual shame in the body rather than thinking about it abstractly. See [[trigger-the-shame-live]].

6. Reframe the shame. Joe names the felt sense as disconnection, not as evidence of being broken. The shame is a signal. See [[shame-as-disconnection]].

7. Show the cost. Conflict avoidance prevents healing in both directions: it stops the person from healing their shame, and it prevents others from growing through encountering them. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]] and [[trust-requires-conflict]].

8. Invert the question. "How else are you going to heal your shame?" The strategy that was supposed to avoid shame turns out to be the strategy that prevents healing. See [[how-else-would-you-heal-your-shame]].

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not coach the workplace tactics. He does not give scripts for how to interrupt politely. The workplace is the venue, not the issue.
- He does not validate "people get offended easily." Even if true, validating it reinforces the strategy.
- He does not let the person rehearse a less-offensive version of themselves. Rehearsal is the disease.
- He does not move quickly to childhood causes. He stays in the present pattern long enough to make it observable in the room.
- He does not let the person fix themselves. Mid-session the person tries to convert the insight into a new improvement project ("I need to stop fixing things"). Joe interrupts: "you don't need to do anything."

## What's Underneath the Surface Fears

The standard set of fears beneath this concern: fear of anger (theirs and your own), fear of abandonment, fear of consequence. The often-missed deeper layer: fear of being loved. The conflict avoidance prevents you from being seen, which prevents you from being loved. Your refusal to let yourself be loved is part of what holds this concern in place. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]].

If you have been working on the anger and abandonment layers and the offending-people concern persists, the love-fear layer is usually what is still in place. The practice in [[feel-the-pain-of-not-being-loved]] addresses it directly.

## The Internal Conflict You Are Already Having

The shape of this concern often hides the fact that the conflict it is trying to avoid is already happening, just internally. The stories ("they get offended easily," "it's not worth it," "they won't get it") are themselves the conflict in a relocated form. The choice is not between conflict and no conflict. The choice is between conflict in the room or conflict in your head. See [[avoided-conflict-goes-internal]].
