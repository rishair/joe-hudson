# What's next? I reached the big goal and now I don't know what to do

## What Someone Says

The defining feature is a freshly completed milestone and the disorientation of having no obvious next thing to chase.

- "I just hit the New York Times bestseller list and now I'm thinking, what do I want to do with my life now?"
- "I sold the company. Now what?"
- "I built up for the last three years for this big sneeze, and now the sneeze is out."
- "I have no clear next thing to do."
- "Now the possibilities have opened up a little bit."
- "I'm kind of there. So I'm like, hmm, what comes next?"
- "I was on the conveyor belt for so long and now there's no conveyor belt."
- "I literally journaled about this yesterday: what the hell do I want to do with my life now?"
- "I got most of what I thought I wanted in my early twenties. Now what?"

The person is usually not in crisis. There is a quality of intrigued bewilderment rather than collapse. The achievement was real. The disorientation is real. Both are sitting in the room.

## Detection Cues

- A recent milestone is named in the opening minute: book launch, exit, promotion, IPO, marathon, baby, marriage.
- The phrase "now what" or some variant appears unprompted.
- The person describes a long stretch (years) of clear next steps that have just ended.
- They are doing something practical (organizing a tour, recovering, traveling) while internally drifting.
- They describe the goal having been a long-time chase ("I've been chasing this for X years").
- They name the absence of structure rather than a problem with what's present.
- They use the word "should" about what they might do next, indicating the want-vs-should fork is live.
- They mention the goal having been organized around something earlier (financial freedom, recognition, escaping a conveyor belt) that they have now achieved or substantially met.

A frequent indirect signal: the person presents two topics in the opening, and one of them is "I want to talk about what to do next." This is the signal that the conveyor belt has ended and the system has not yet generated the next pull.

## What Joe Hears

The disorientation is real and useful. The system that organized itself around chasing the milestone has accomplished its job. There is now a brief gap before the next chase spins up. The gap is where freedom lives -- see [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]].

What also lives in this moment: the chance to make the next chapter run on something other than the engine that produced the last one. If the previous chase was driven by fear (of not being safe, of not being enough, of having to prove something), the post-achievement window is when the person can notice the engine and consider running on a different one.

The person almost always already knows what they want. The wants are usually about connection in some form -- to themselves, to others, to the work. The wants are obscured by the question being framed as "what should I do next?" rather than "what do I want?"

Underneath the question of "what's next" is usually a more specific concern Joe will look for: is there a money fear that drove the previous chase and is still running? See [[if-i-had-more-money]]. Is there an old identity (the striver, the achiever, the conveyor-belt-rider) that is dying and needs to be grieved? See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]]. Is there a fear of stagnation that is about to manufacture the next chase out of avoidance rather than desire?

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people can present this concern and Joe goes different directions based on what he reads.

- If the person quickly produces a list of options when asked what they want: Joe is interested in the *quality* of the wants. Are they about connection or about achievement? Are they "I get to" or "I have to"? See [[i-get-to-vs-i-have-to]]. He will gently surface the theme that runs through them.

- If the person says "I want financial freedom" or some version of safety-as-the-want: Joe traces the safety fear to its origin. If this fear existed before the person knew what money was, what would it be called? See [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]]. The question is whether the next chapter will be run by the same childhood safety fear or by something else.

- If the person's answers are intellectual with no somatic component: Joe slows down. The answers are coming from the part of the system that organized the last chase. The next chapter cannot be designed from there. He moves toward the body. See [[intellectual-process-with-no-somatic-component]].

- If the person has the body of someone who has been performing for a decade (chin slightly up, jaw a little tight, fast speech): Joe will mark the somatic pattern and gently redirect toward feeling. The post-goal moment is one of the few times the performance is interruptible. See [[chin-up-as-emotion-block]].

- If the person presents a parallel concern about not being able to feel their emotions: Joe will treat the two concerns as the same concern. The inability to feel and the inability to know what's next are the same disconnect, framed from two angles. See [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]].

- If the person reaches reflexively for the next big goal: Joe will not stop them, but he will introduce the question of whether the new chase is being designed by the same engine that produced the last one. See [[improvement-mindset-vs-maintenance-mindset]] and [[same-scarcity-story-regardless-of-wealth]].

## Coaching Path

The flow Joe tends to follow:

1. Confirm that the conveyor belt has ended and that the rules of the game have changed. "So the rules of the game have changed. And now, and just to some degree, it sounds like there is just no rules. It's what do you want?" The naming alone produces relief and curiosity.

2. Ask the question directly: "What do you want?" Not "what should you do next?" The reframe from should to want is the entire move. See [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

3. Receive the answer. If the answer includes a fear (of money, of stagnation, of losing what was built), trace it. The trace usually leads to a childhood safety pattern that the previous chase was a strategy for. See [[childhood-imprinting]] and [[how-you-related-to-parents-is-how-you-relate-to-god-and-money]].

4. Notice what the wants have in common. In a remarkable number of cases, the wants are about [[connection]] in different forms. The person often does not see this. Joe names it without asserting it: "What I notice is everything that -- all of your goals are about connection."

5. Apply the principle directly to the practical question. If connection is the want, the next move is not to design a new chase. It is to do whatever is already happening more connectedly. See [[apply-connection-to-whatever-youre-doing]] and the [[ten-percent-more-connected-experiment]].

6. Address the money fear (or other fear) that drove the previous chase. The post-achievement moment is when the fear is most clearly visible -- it no longer has the chase to hide behind. See [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]] and [[childhood-imprinting]].

7. Mark the somatic shifts as they happen. The body knows when the connection has dropped in. The body is the diagnostic for whether the new orientation is actually landing. See [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]].

## The Permission-From-the-Younger-Self Variant

A specific form of this concern: the person has achieved enough, is intellectually convinced they could stop, and finds that the part of them that did the work will not grant permission to stop. The block is not about not knowing what to do next. The block is about how to honor what was done by the younger self who paid the price.

Surface form: "I have more than enough money for a decade. I no longer need to work for money, but it feels disrespectful to walk away. How do I say thank you to that 13-year-old boy who busted his butt? I want that 13-year-old's permission to walk away."

The economic facts are settled. The unfinished business is loyalty. The younger self who suffered to acquire the resources is operating like an investor who has not been allowed to exit the position. Quitting feels like betraying them.

Detection cues specific to this variant:
- The person names a specific younger age at which the work began (often a difficult childhood that required early earning or premature self-reliance).
- The word "permission" or "respect" or "disrespectful" shows up in relation to walking away.
- The current job is described as easy and well-paying -- the difficulty is not the work, it is releasing the work.
- The pursuit has a quality of overpaying for what was once underpaid ("I make in a day what used to take me 100 days").
- The person can articulate the rational case for stopping but the body won't let them.

The coaching path for this variant has a specific shape:

1. Have the person speak as the younger self directly. Not in third person ("my 13-year-old self thinks..."), but in first person. See [[swap-roles-with-the-younger-self]] for the move that does this.

2. Ask the younger self what they actually need. The answer is usually not "more money." It is usually some form of protection -- the protection that the younger self learned to seek through earning because they were unsafe in some other way.

3. Trace the protection-need to its actual content. If the younger self needs "security" or "independence," the next question is "from what?" The answer locates the original threat. In Doug's case: "Security from assholes." See [[money-as-surrogate]] for the protection-from-assholes version of the money surrogate.

4. Check whether the protection is still functioning externally. If the person has already built strong external protection (a no-asshole rule, a safe community, financial security), the threat the younger self is protecting against has likely moved inside. See [[extend-the-no-asshole-rule-inward]] for the move that surfaces this.

5. Meet whatever internal figure the younger self is afraid of, directly. The work cannot be done at the level of money or career because the threat is not in those domains anymore. See [[chase-it-around-the-room]] for the move that meets the internal figure.

6. After the internal figure has been met, have the younger self speak again. The release usually comes through the younger self's voice, in their own words, after they no longer need protection. Doug's answer when asked what he needed around money after the demon was met: "Nothing. If the demon's not here, then money's not as important."

7. Close with what the present-self could do that would make life 10% more enjoyable, now that the protection function is no longer required. Joe's standard close: "How do you make your life 10% more enjoyable?" The answers that arrive at this point are usually about [[connection]] -- help others, family, teaching, giving back. The same pattern that shows up in the Ali Abdaal version of this concern.

The variant differs from the standard form in that the block is not about designing a new future but about releasing the past. The work is closer to grief and gratitude than to vision and planning. The younger self has done their job. The present-self is asking permission to retire them.

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not depression. The person usually still has energy, just no direction.
- It is not laziness or lack of drive. The drive engine has temporarily completed its program. The new program has not yet been written.
- It is not failure to plan. The planning gear can be engaged anytime. The question is what to plan toward, and that question cannot be answered from the gear that produced the previous plan.
- It is not a problem to solve. It is a window in which the underlying engine of the person's life is briefly visible. See [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]] for the structural treatment of the window.
