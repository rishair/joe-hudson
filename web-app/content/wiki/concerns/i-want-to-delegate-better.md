# I want to delegate better

## What Someone Says

The concern shows up in a range of phrasings, all pointing at the same underlying experience.

- "I walk around all day and there's ideas, ideas, ideas, and I really wanna be able to delegate better."
- "I'm stretching into my capacity to delegate."
- "Maybe 10% of my ideas actually make it into some kind of system and somebody actually hears about it."
- "I either tell people exactly what to do, or I leave them alone and hope it gets done."
- "I'm the bottleneck in my company."
- "I want to delegate but I don't know how to do it without micromanaging."
- "I can't tell if my team can handle more."
- "I'm afraid I'll give them too much and they'll say yes and then drop the ball."

The leader is aware that delegation is the constraint. They have more vision than they have execution capacity, and the gap is hurting the business. They have tried delegating and gotten mixed results. They know they need to get better at this, and they don't know exactly what "better" looks like.

## Detection Cues

The concern surfaces directly when the person uses the word "delegate" or describes themselves as the bottleneck. It also surfaces indirectly through:

- Descriptions of having lots of ideas that don't get executed
- Frustration with team output not matching what was wanted
- Oscillation between micromanagement and abdication, with neither working
- Working long hours while believing the team should be able to take more on
- Holding back on ambitious projects because there is no one to hand them to
- Hiring people and then continuing to do their work yourself
- Reporting that the company has plateaued at the size that fits the leader's personal execution capacity

The concern is especially common in founders whose companies have grown beyond what one person can carry. The leader's pattern that built the company to its current size is now the pattern that prevents it from getting bigger.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the surface concern there are usually several layers stacked.

**The execution-of-strategy layer.** The leader is struggling with the distillation from vision to action. They have many possible directions and have not narrowed to the first domino. Until the first domino is clear, the delegation problem cannot be solved, because the leader has not identified the specific thing to delegate. See [[focus-on-the-first-domino]].

**The delegation-form layer.** The leader is trapped in the binary of micromanagement vs no oversight. They have not learned the third option: setting criteria and letting the team solve within them. See [[set-solution-criteria-have-the-team-solve-within-them]].

**The accountability layer.** The leader does not have a structure for tracking what was committed to and surfacing what didn't get done. Without the tracking infrastructure, delegated work goes into a void. See [[ask-the-three-accountability-questions]].

**The caretaking layer.** The leader believes their job is to take care of the team, and the team's job is to execute. The actual structure is the inverse: the leader's job is to help the team take care of the team, and the team's job is to do the work. See [[leader-doesnt-take-care-of-everybody]].

**The hiring layer.** The leader has hired people they can do the job better than. They cannot delegate to people whose work they do not trust, but they cannot trust the work of people they could do better. The fix is upstream of delegation: hire people who are better at the job than you are. See [[hire-people-who-can-do-the-job-better-than-you]].

**The fear-of-mistakes layer.** The leader cannot delegate because delegation requires accepting mistakes. The leader's relationship with mistakes is the gating factor on how much they can hand off. See [[allow-mistakes-as-the-cost-of-real-delegation]].

Underneath all of these is often the leader's [[perfectionism]] or [[fear-of-failure]]: the delegation problem is the operational form of an internal pattern about control and acceptance.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two leaders presenting the same delegation concern go in entirely different directions based on what is underneath.

- If the leader has been **disempowering the team by solving for them**: the work moves toward recognizing that issuing the solution disempowers, and learning to set criteria instead. The path goes through [[set-solution-criteria-have-the-team-solve-within-them]] and the recognition that "I think I need to solve their problem for them" is the structural error.

- If the leader is **performing taking-care-of-everyone** as their identity as leader: the work moves toward [[leader-doesnt-take-care-of-everybody]] and the unwinding of the caretaking-as-leadership pattern. See also [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

- If the leader has hired people they don't actually trust: the work moves toward [[hire-people-who-can-do-the-job-better-than-you]]. The delegation problem is downstream of the hiring problem. No delegation move fixes a team the leader can't trust.

- If the leader is **afraid of mistakes**: the work moves toward making the leader's relationship with mistakes the focus. The delegation will fail until the leader can hold delegated work that has gone wrong without it triggering reactivity. See [[allow-mistakes-as-the-cost-of-real-delegation]].

- If the leader has no tracking infrastructure: the work moves toward institutionalizing tracking and accountability. The delegation cannot land without the structural follow-up. See [[direct-intervention-vs-structural-change]].

- If the leader is **trying to scale themselves rather than the team**: the work moves toward the recognition that delegation is not about cloning yourself but about leveraging others' strengths. See [[dont-try-to-replicate-yourself-across-the-team]] for the principle. "I'm trying to actually replicate 30 of me throughout a company rather than have me plus 29 other individuals with different strengths and weaknesses."

- If the leader is **tolerating an underperformer and asking how to delegate around them**: the read is that the toleration is personal — something is preventing the clean business decision. See [[tolerating-underperformance-is-always-personal]]. The path runs through naming what is personal that is preventing the clean decision.

- If the leader is **describing repeated patterns of team members saying yes and then not delivering**: the read may be [[trust-with-authority-has-to-be-rebuilt-with-the-formerly-punished]] — team members from punishing environments still operating on the old strategy. The path runs through the leader's role in maintaining the field condition that produces the trust rebuild, while also being honest about whether enough time has passed and whether the team member is actually moving through the arc.

## Coaching Path

The typical flow Joe follows from this concern.

1. **Clarify the first domino.** Before anything else, what is the one thing the leader is trying to delegate? Vague delegation produces vague results. The leader has to name the specific thing that needs to happen. See [[focus-on-the-first-domino]].

2. **Diagnose the binary.** Is the leader oscillating between micromanagement and abdication? If so, the move is to introduce [[set-solution-criteria-have-the-team-solve-within-them]] as the third option. The leader names what the solution must meet; the team finds the solution; the leader holds the criteria; the team holds the design.

3. **Surface the emotional pattern.** What is the leader feeling about delegating? Often this is fear (they'll fail), control (it has to be done my way), or caretaking (I have to take care of them). The emotional pattern is the actual constraint. Until it is named, the structural moves get sabotaged.

4. **Install the accountability structure.** Tracking is not optional. Without it, the delegated work disappears. The leader has to commit to a tracking system that surfaces what got done and what didn't. See [[ask-the-three-accountability-questions]] for the accountability-conversation form.

5. **Address the hiring.** If the team can't actually take what the leader is trying to hand them, no delegation move will solve the problem. The work moves upstream to who is on the team.

6. **Build the hard-conversations practice.** Delegation is sustained by the leader's willingness to have the conversation when something isn't working. Without that, the team learns the leader will avoid, and they stop bringing things forward. See [[block-calendar-time-for-hard-conversations]].

The arc moves from "how do I get them to do what I want" to "how do I structure the work so it gets done well without me." The second framing is the actual question. The first is the question that produces micromanagement and frustration.

## What This Concern Often Sits Inside

The delegation concern rarely stands alone. It usually sits inside one or more of:

- The leader is scaling the company and hitting the ceiling of their own capacity
- The leader is being asked to grow into a role they have not yet inhabited
- The leader has hired their first executive team and is unsure how to relate to them
- The leader is running multiple businesses or projects simultaneously and cannot personally carry all of them

The concern is a marker that the leader's role is shifting from doer to designer. The leader who absorbs this can move into the next layer of leadership. The leader who tries to scale themselves without changing their relationship to the work plateaus.
