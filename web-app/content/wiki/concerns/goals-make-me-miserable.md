# I oscillate between oppressive goals and no goals at all

## What Someone Says

The presenting form has two failure modes that the same person often oscillates between over years.

**The oppression mode:**
- "I have a shitty relationship with goals."
- "I set a ton of goals and then I feel oppressed by them."
- "I keep failing my own goals and beating myself up about it."
- "My goals are how I beat myself up."
- "I'm always behind on my own goals."

**The avoidance mode:**
- "I just avoid goals so I don't feel oppressed."
- "I get to feel free without goals, but I also kind of feel lost."
- "I don't want goals."
- "I don't need goals. I just enjoy myself in the moment."

**The oscillation:**
- "I've popped back and forth between the two for sure."
- "I oscillate -- I set them, they crush me, I throw them out, then I feel rudderless, so I set them again."

The person often arrives intellectually aware that neither mode is working. They sense that something about how they hold goals is the problem, not whether or not to have them.

## Detection Cues

**Surface signals:**
- The person describes their relationship with goals as "shitty," "complicated," "oppressive," or "shameful."
- They report swinging between phases of intense goal-setting and phases of refusing goals altogether.
- They describe themselves as someone who is "bad at" goals.
- They report a recent goal-setting attempt that produced shame or self-attack when missed.
- They report that they recently gave up on goals as a category and now feel directionless.

**Indirect signals:**
- They describe their work or life with constant references to what they "should" be doing -- the goals are operating as shoulds whether named or not. See [[i-should-be-doing-this]].
- They describe feeling rudderless or lost in a domain where they previously had a goal.
- They have a clear next step in some domains and complete blankness in others; the blank domains are usually where the goal-relationship broke down.
- They describe other people's goal-setting as oppressive when they observe it -- a projection signal.

## What Joe Hears Underneath

The goals are not the problem. Everyone has goals; the question is whether they are conscious and what they are doing. The two failure modes the person is oscillating between are both forms of the same misrelationship to goals.

The oppression mode means the goals have become shoulds. The voice in the head -- functioning as an internal board -- is issuing goals that are not actually the wants of the rest of the system. See [[the-inner-board]]. The body has different wants, the heart has different wants, the trauma has different wants. The goals from the head are not aligned with the system, so they feel like self-abuse to enact. The person tries to enact them, fails, and the failure registers as personal failure rather than as a signal that the goals were misaligned.

The avoidance mode is the next move: refuse the goals altogether to escape the self-abuse. The escape works in one direction (no more being beaten with the goals) but produces a new problem: without conscious goals, the unconscious goals run the show. The person feels lost because they have not actually become goal-less; they have just suppressed the goal-system. The orientation has moved underground, where it cannot be examined or revised.

The third option is neither mode. The goals come from the actual want of the system rather than from the inner board. They are held gently rather than as mandates. Missing them produces learning and growth rather than shame. This is what the work moves toward. See [[goals-must-come-from-actual-want]] and [[goals-reveal-your-relationship-to-yourself]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people in the same oscillation arrive at different places based on what is read.

- If the person is currently in the **oppression mode**: Joe will surface whose goals these actually are. The inner board diagnostic: are these the wants of the body, the heart, the actual person, or are they the voice in the head issuing mandates? See [[the-inner-board]] and [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]]. The coaching path moves toward owning what is actually wanted underneath the inherited shoulds.

- If the person is currently in the **avoidance mode**: Joe will name that "I don't have goals" is itself a goal -- often "enjoyment in the moment" or "freedom" -- and the issue is the lack of consciousness about it, not the lack of structure. "Bringing the goal to consciousness, there's a benefit to that." The coaching path moves toward naming the goals that are actually operating underground.

- If the person describes shame about either having or missing the goals: Joe reads the [[shame-drives-behavior]] pattern and works with the shame layer first. The relationship to goals cannot be repaired while shame is in the operating loop.

- If the person describes their goals exclusively in terms of metrics (numbers, dates, achievements): Joe reads the metric/goal conflation and will untangle them. See [[dont-confuse-the-metric-with-the-goal]] and [[principles-goals-metrics]].

- If the person reaches for an immediate yes when asked whether they want the goal ("yes, I want to lose 20 pounds"): Joe reads the un-felt yes. The brain says yes immediately but the system has not actually been consulted. See [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]].

## Coaching Path

The flow Joe tends to follow:

1. **Name the oscillation as the symptom, not the problem.** The two modes are both attempts to deal with a misrelationship to goals. The work is the misrelationship, not the choice between modes. See [[goals-reveal-your-relationship-to-yourself]].

2. **Locate the actual want underneath the goal.** Use [[need-behind-the-want]]: drill down through the surface goal to what is actually wanted. The $12M revenue becomes security becomes the capacity to make a difference. The goal is a strategy. The strategy is not the want.

3. **Check whether the want is felt or only claimed.** [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]]. The brain says yes immediately. Whether the system actually wanted it is a different question, answerable in the body.

4. **Distinguish constructive friction from destructive friction in the goal.** The question to test which is which: "Do you actually want it?" Friction in pursuit of a real want is the friction of being alive. Friction in pursuit of a should is self-abuse. See [[friction-is-required-for-life]].

5. **Reframe the role of the goal.** A goal is not primarily about being hit. A goal gives you the right questions to ask and lets you live as if the intention is already happening. See [[goals-give-you-the-questions]] and [[acting-as-if-already-true]].

6. **Introduce gentle goal-holding.** The goal is held "with a lot of love, and with awe and wonder and not through being defined by it." Missing a goal is data, not failure. See [[dont-let-goals-define-you]] and [[set-and-forget-goals]].

7. **Surface the celebration of failure.** A relationship to goals that includes celebrating failures publicly is structurally different from one that punishes them. Naming this option opens the door to a different way of holding the goals altogether. See [[celebrate-failures-publicly]].

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not laziness or lack of discipline. The oscillation is what happens when discipline is applied to misaligned goals.
- It is not a failure of goal-setting technique. The person can usually write a goal in correct form. The problem is the relationship to the goal, not its formulation.
- It is not a permanent personality trait. The person reports having been in both modes at different times. The relationship to goals can change.
- It is not about whether to have goals. "It's really about how you relate to the goals rather than if you have them or not, because everybody has them."
