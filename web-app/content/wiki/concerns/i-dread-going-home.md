# I dread going home / Visiting my family feels like an obligation

## What Someone Says

The phrasing varies but the felt sense underneath is consistent: a relationship with family that should be a source of warmth has become a source of dread.

- "I don't want to go home for the holidays."
- "I love my parents but I dread visiting them."
- "Going home feels like an obligation, not a homecoming."
- "I count the days until I can leave."
- "I always come home from visiting my parents exhausted."
- "I get smaller when I walk into their house."
- "I rehearse what to say before the call."
- "I don't tell them what's actually going on with me because I know what they'll say."
- "My mom didn't tell anyone in the family I'm pregnant because I'm not married and she's embarrassed."
- "Every time I'm home, my dad criticizes me about my job / weight / partner / life."
- "I should want to see them. What's wrong with me that I don't?"
- "Other people seem to love going home and I dread it."
- "It takes me a week to recover from a visit."

The person often presents with confusion or guilt about the dread itself. They expect to want to see their family. The not-wanting is what they cannot make sense of. They are looking for either permission not to go or a way to want to go.

## Detection Cues

How an AI coach recognizes this concern from text, even when the person does not name it directly:

- The phrase "I should" attached to visiting family ("I should call my mom," "I should visit for Christmas") with no corresponding "I want to"
- Description of family visits as something endured, recovered from, or got through
- Filtering language: what they do not tell their parents, what they hide, what they edit out
- Body-shrinking language: getting smaller, feeling like a kid again, losing themselves at the parents' house
- Aftermath language: needing days to recover, feeling depleted, feeling crappy for a week
- The specific anticipatory dread: "every time I go I tell myself I'll handle it differently and I never do"
- The cultural reference: comparison to other people's families that "feel like home"
- The hidden announcement: a major life event (pregnancy, partnership, career change) they are not telling the parents about because of the predictable reaction
- Guilt about the dread itself, often as the surface presenting layer
- The framing of the visit as something done for the parents' sake, not the visitor's

Indirect signals (when the concern is not named but is operating underneath):

- A person describing burnout that includes family interactions as part of what is depleting them
- A person who reports being "fine" with their family but who has not been in the same room with them in years
- A person who describes a parent's behavior in language that would obviously not be acceptable from anyone else but which they are tolerating because it is family
- An adult child who is reorganizing major life decisions around what will or will not produce a reaction from a parent

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the words is a specific structural reading. The dread is not random and it is not the person's failure of love. The dread is the predictable response to a relationship structured around being criticized and managed.

"It is as inherent in us as eating, as breathing. It is part of our essence. It's our nature. And nobody wants that to come with a whole bunch of judgment and criticism and management." -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

The desire to be with family is structural. What blocks that desire, when it is blocked, is the addition of criticism and management to the relationship. The person's dread is the accurate read of what the relationship is doing to them. The dread is not the problem to be argued away; it is the data about what the relationship has become.

See [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]] for the full principle.

Underneath the guilt about the dread is usually a long history of being told the dread is wrong: "they're your family," "you only have one mom," "you'll miss them when they're gone." The person has absorbed these and uses them against their own felt experience. The work is to honor the felt experience and locate what is actually producing it.

Underneath the obligation language is often the parent's projection of obligation: "after everything I did for you, the least you could do is visit." The obligation has been installed. Removing it is part of the work.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point. Different reads send Joe in different directions even when the surface complaint sounds similar.

- If the person is **still trying to be the version of themselves the parents want** during visits: the dread is partly the cost of the performance. Joe directs toward what the relationship would look like if the person stopped performing. Often the dread reveals itself to be specifically about the performance, not the parents. See [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] and [[i-keep-doing-things-i-dont-want-to-do]].

- If the person has been **receiving criticism without naming it**: the criticism has been treated as background noise. Joe names what they have been absorbing — "every time I come home, you criticize me, you tell me what I'm doing wrong" is the structural description. See [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]].

- If the person is **stuck in guilt about the dread**: the guilt is the obstacle to acting on the dread accurately. Joe addresses the guilt before addressing the family. See [[guilt-as-passive-fear]] and the work in [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]].

- If the person is **hiding major life events from the parents** because of anticipated criticism: this is the diagnostic that the relationship cannot bear the actual person. The person is already maintaining a curated version of themselves for the parents to accept. Joe surfaces this as the structural reality of the relationship as it currently stands.

- If the person is **using the parents' age or health as the reason to keep visiting**: Joe does not argue with the love that wants to be present. He does name that the visiting can take a different shape than the one currently producing the dread.

- If the person is **looking for permission not to visit at all**: Joe usually does not give that permission as the answer. The boundary-drawing move (see [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]]) is structurally different from cutting off contact. The boundary names what the person will not participate in and leaves the door open for a different version of the relationship.

- If the person is **at the point of having tried boundaries and the parents have not adjusted**: Joe acknowledges this and does not push for more boundary-drawing. The work shifts to the person's own healing of what was installed. See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] and [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]].

- If the person is **the parent of an adult child** asking why their kid does not visit: the read flips. The work is locating the criticism and management the parent has been transmitting without realizing. See [[dont-micromanage-your-kid]] and [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]].

## Coaching Path

The typical flow from this concern. Not a script. A map of likely moves with branches.

1. **Honor the dread.** The dread is not a moral failure. It is data. Joe usually does not rush to dissolve it. The first move is to make space for it to be valid.

2. **Locate the structural source.** What specifically happens during the visits that produces the dread? The person often has not articulated it. The articulation itself is part of the work. Common structural sources: criticism, management, walking on eggshells, having to perform a particular version of self, the parents' anxiety as the background field, the parents' disappointment about life choices.

3. **Name the principle.** "Nobody wants to come home and be criticized. Nobody wants to come home and be managed." See [[criticism-and-management-make-loved-ones-not-want-to-be-with-you]]. The naming releases the guilt about the dread by making clear the dread is the structural response to what the relationship is doing.

4. **Distinguish the love from the relational dynamic.** The person often loves their parents. The love does not contradict the dread. The dread is about the dynamic, not about the people. Holding both is required.

5. **Introduce the possibility of a boundary that is about the dynamic, not about the relationship.** Joe's framing: "I love you. I want to come home. I will not come home to be criticized." See [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]]. The boundary is about the criticism, not about the parents.

6. **Address the resistance to drawing the boundary.** The resistance is usually fear of the parents' reaction, fear of being wrong, fear that the boundary will end the relationship, guilt about depriving the parents. Each of these has its own work.

7. **If the boundary cannot be drawn or has been drawn and failed, work the inner side.** The criticism the parent delivered is also running in the person's own head. See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] and [[joes-call-with-dad-about-criticism]]. Addressing the inner version often changes the felt charge on the outer version.

8. **Provide the alternative move for parents who recognize themselves in this concern.** See [[name-your-concern-rather-than-manage-them]]. The parent can stop the management without becoming permissive.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

- Does not tell the person to "just love them" or "they're your only parents." This is the kind of advice that compounds the obligation and intensifies the dread.
- Does not pathologize the family system as a whole. The point is to locate the specific dynamic.
- Does not push the person toward cutting off contact as the default answer. The default answer is a boundary about the dynamic.
- Does not let the person stay in performing-the-acceptable-version as if that were sustainable.
- Does not minimize the guilt or argue with it. The guilt has its own work.
- Does not assume the parents are uniquely awful. The same dynamic runs in most families to some degree. The naming is the work.
