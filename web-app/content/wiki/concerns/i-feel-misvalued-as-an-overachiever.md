# I feel misvalued when people call me an overachiever

## What Someone Says

The concern usually arrives through a small sting. Someone calls them an overachiever, a perfectionist, a creative, a warrior, a high producer. The word is meant as a compliment. It lands wrong. The person can name that it feels off but cannot easily say why.

- "When people say like, 'oh, you're such an overachiever' or 'you're creative,' I feel like in their low-key, it's like 'thanks for that, that was awesome, bye.' It just feels very unseen."
- "All that took to deliver — they don't see it."
- "Once in a while, someone would be like, 'that must have been really fucking hard to make that happen.' That hits differently."
- "It's a feeling of being a little misvalued."
- "If you're an overachiever, you feel like you're alone and isolated and you can never really get the thing that you want."
- "I'm a perfectionist. I don't need —"
- "I don't know which came first, the overachievement or the not-being-seen."
- "I always wanted to make things better. Always."
- "I think the overachieving is survival mode."
- "I don't know how to focus on myself and not want to help others."
- "I have such a complex feeling about saying thank you."
- "Yeah, I get good at that [deflecting]."

The presenting form is often a question about how to get people to see the work, or a complaint that the appreciation they get doesn't land. The deeper structure is the [[see-me-dont-see-me push-pull|see-me / don't-see-me push-pull]] running through the specific configuration of the overachiever: producing constantly to be seen, then refusing to be seen when the seeing arrives.

## Detection Cues

- A high-functioning, productive person reports a small sting when called "overachiever," "perfectionist," "creative," "warrior," or other achievement-coded compliments
- The person can articulate that the words feel like dismissal even though they are meant as praise
- They describe wanting people to see the process or the effort, not just the output
- They report feeling alone or isolated despite producing a lot and being widely appreciated
- They mention being in "survival mode" and may identify the overachieving itself as survival-mode behavior
- Background often includes immigrant parents, struggling parents, or other early conditions where survival was emphasized over thriving
- They name themselves as the always-helper, the always-producer, the always-mentor
- The phrase "I don't necessarily want to show the steps" or some variant appears — they want recognition for the work but cannot show the work without exposing the perfectionist process
- They have a complex relationship to "thank you" — saying it feels weird, receiving it feels weirder
- They deflect compliments not just verbally but with a tightening around the mouth and a flat "mm-hmm"
- They report being "bad at" receiving compliments and have heard from friends to "just say thank you"
- When asked to focus on themselves, they immediately pivot to talking about helping others

The signature is doubled: the person is **both** producing constantly in pursuit of being seen **and** refusing the seeing when it arrives. The producing is the see-me layer. The deflection is the don't-see-me layer. Both are firing in the same body around the same trait.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is a recognizable structure that runs together as a trifecta:

**Producing to be valuable because being is not enough.** "People often become overachievers because they're trying to show that they're valuable because they're not seen as valuable in their nature." The child was not valued for being. The child learned that producing — making things better, fixing, contributing — was what made them valuable. The producing did not stop. The original wound did not heal. The producing became the substitute for the seeing that was never available.

**Overachievement as survival mode.** The producing is not joy-driven. It is fear-driven. "I'm always in survival mode. The overachieving is survival mode." The body is running the production engine on the same fuel that originally kept the child useful enough to be kept. The fuel never gets refilled by external recognition because the recognition is for the output, not for the being underneath the output. See [[overachieving-is-survival-mode]].

**Deflecting the seeing that does arrive.** When somebody finally does see — including seeing the survival mode, the effort, the process — the person blocks it. The body tightens around the mouth. The "mm-hmm" comes out flat. The person pivots to helping others. The deflection is the don't-see-me side of the push-pull, but it is also actively feeding the original story that no one sees them. Every deflected compliment is one more piece of evidence for the hungry ghost that no one is bringing the cake. See [[the-cake-metaphor-for-deflected-compliments]].

The trifecta is the concern. The work touches all three: feeling the original misvalue, slowing the production-engine that runs on survival, and learning to let the seeing land somatically when it arrives.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person says the word "overachiever" or "perfectionist" lands as dismissal: Joe takes the sting seriously and asks what they wish people would see instead. Often "the steps," "what it took," "how hard it was." This is the entry to the producing-to-be-valued structure. Joe will then offer the kind of compliment they're actually wanting — a compliment that names the effort, not the output. See [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]] and [[reframe-the-trait-they-feel-stung-by-as-evidence-of-care]].

- If the person can identify the producing as survival mode: Joe does not argue. He validates. "I see that you put a lot of effort into being in survival mode." The reflective compliment about the survival mode itself is sometimes the first compliment the system can let in because it does not require buying the smallness-protective story that they don't deserve recognition.

- If [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome|the body tightens around the mouth]] when complimented: Joe names the physical constriction in real time. "Your lips tighten and you go 'mm-hmm.' It's like you're physically constricting to not let in my compliment." See [[live-naming-the-physical-constriction-of-non-reception]]. The naming makes the deflection visible to the person doing it — often for the first time.

- If the person pivots to "I don't know how to focus on myself and not want to help others" right as they're being seen: this is [[deflecting-by-pivoting-to-helping-others]]. Joe names the deflection live. "That's a different thing — you're distracting from the fact that you're being seen right now." The pivot to help-others is the producing-to-be-valued engine kicking back on as the receiving begins to land.

- If the person responds to a compliment with "I tried so hard" or any version of producing-evidence: catch the catch. The person is on their way to letting it in and then pivots to claiming the effort, which is structurally a way to re-route the recognition back into the producing engine. "Good catch — you tried hard, but I'm asking you to feel the care, not to defend the effort." The reflexive defense of effort is itself the deflection of being seen for the care underneath the effort.

- If the person can articulate that they don't want to show the steps: this is the perfectionist refusing to expose the process. The perfectionist standard demands that the process be invisible. The wanting-to-be-seen-for-the-process and the perfectionist-refusing-to-show-the-process are the contradiction the system is locked inside. Joe can name the bind without trying to solve it: you want them to see the process and you cannot show them the process — what does that produce?

- If immigrant parents, struggling parents, or any survival-pressured childhood arrives in the story: Joe takes the childhood condition seriously without pathologizing it. "They were loving, but they were also asking 'is that useful?'" The valuation-by-utility is the inheritance. The producing-as-survival learned in that context is doing what it learned to do. The work is not to blame the parents — it is to feel what the small self needed and did not get and to begin to give that to the present self.

## Coaching Path

The session shape Joe often follows:

1. **Take the sting seriously.** When the person says "overachiever" lands as dismissal, don't argue with the framing. Validate the read. The compliment is incomplete — it credits the output without crediting the cost.

2. **Identify what they wish was seen.** "How can somebody who's not an overachieving perfectionist understand an overachieving perfectionist? How can they get that? How can they see you?" The question surfaces the specific kind of seeing they're hungry for. Usually: the effort, the process, the cost, the survival mode, the fucking hard part.

3. **Find the misvalue underneath.** "So which came first, the misvalue or the perfectionism or the overachievement?" See [[which-came-first]]. Joe is not asking a strict chronology. He is asking the person to feel into the structure that links them. The answer almost always exposes the original wound: the child was not valued for being, only for producing.

4. **Offer the reflective compliment they're actually wanting.** "I really see that you put a lot of effort into being in survival mode." The compliment names the cost, not the output. The compliment is the first one the system can sometimes let in because it does not require buying the story that the person is great. It only requires accepting that the effort is being witnessed. See [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]].

5. **Watch what they do with it.** If they let it hit for a moment and then go to a thought, name that. "Let that hit. You started to let that hit and then you went to a thought." If they pivot to helping others, name that. "You're distracting from the fact that you're being seen right now." If their body tightens, name that. The naming is the work.

6. **Show them how they do it.** The coach deflects the compliment they give the coach. Or the coach physically demonstrates the mm-hmm constriction. "I'm doing that, not because I'm agreeing with you, because I'm showing you how you do it." See [[do-the-deflection-back-to-show-them-how-they-do-it]].

7. **Use the cake metaphor to expose the cost of deflection.** "If I baked you a cake and you went mm-hmm, would I bring you another fucking cake?" The deflector is not just refusing the gift; they are guaranteeing the giver will stop offering. See [[the-cake-metaphor-for-deflected-compliments]] and [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]].

8. **Unpack the achievement-coded compliment into its emotional content.** When the client said "what was the compliment your friend gave you?" and the answer was "this was great, I love this, this is what I needed," Joe restated the same content using the achievement word the client was stung by: "Wow, you are such an overachiever. That means you put a lot of time into this. You cared about this. I see how much effort you put into it. That's what an overachiever means." The unpacking shows the client that the word they hated was actually carrying the same content as the compliment they could receive — they just couldn't translate the achievement-language into being-seen-language. See [[reframe-the-trait-they-feel-stung-by-as-evidence-of-care]].

9. **Get the body to receive.** "Let it tickle you. Breathe. Shake your body a little bit. Let it in a little bit. This is what you wanted. This is what you wanted as a kid." The body has to do the receiving. The head cannot reason its way into being seen. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]] for the somatic move.

10. **Bring in the room.** If in a public Q&A, Joe sometimes asks the audience to put genuine compliments in the chat and instructs the client to actually let them in. The room becomes the receiving practice. The work that started with one compliment from the coach now extends into a flood the client has to let through the channel.

## What This Concern Is Not

- **It is not the same as [[i-cant-receive-compliments-or-love-from-my-spouse]].** That is the marriage-side of the receiving difficulty, where a long-term partner has been chasing and the receiver has had walls up for years. This concern is the achievement-and-recognition form of the same underlying receiving difficulty, but it arrives through a different door: feeling misvalued by achievement-coded compliments, not by feeling chased by a spouse.

- **It is not [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] either.** Approval-seeking is more diffuse — the person is constantly monitoring how they are landing. This concern is more specific: the person has produced something significant, is being recognized for it, and feels unseen by the specific shape of the recognition. The producing has already happened. The deflection happens in the moment of the recognition arriving.

- **It is not [[how-do-i-love-myself]].** That concern presents as a request for technique with a self-aware analysis of dismissing accomplishments. This concern presents as a sting from a compliment with the analysis pointing outward at how the recognition failed.

- **It is not just perfectionism.** The perfectionist drive is part of the structure (see [[perfectionism]]), but the concern here is specifically about the configuration where the perfectionist is producing constantly to be seen and then refusing the seeing — the see-me / don't-see-me push-pull running through the overachievement frame.
