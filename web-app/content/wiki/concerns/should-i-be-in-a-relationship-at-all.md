# Should I be in a relationship at all? / I want to but it's too much work

## What Someone Says

The person is single, often post-divorce or post-breakup, and has been having a recurring internal debate. The voice that wants a relationship and the voice that doesn't have been running on each other for months or years.

- "I have this push-pull. I want to, but then I hear myself say it's too much work."
- "I love relationship, I love sharing my life with someone. But also, I don't have time, it'll take away from the other things I want to do."
- "Should I be in a relationship or not? I keep going back and forth."
- "It's a lot of work, I don't want to do it. Am I going to be able to do that and business and train for sport and like do all that stuff?"
- "I've been single for the last year, and that's where this dialogue is coming up."
- "I tend to do things to extremes. Relationships take everything I've got."
- "I want it to be supportive. I want to not treat it like a burden, like an obligation. Or like something that equals enduring pain."

The framing is symmetric. Both voices are described as having reasons. The person is presenting them as roughly comparable and asking, implicitly, which one to listen to. The deliberation has been running for a while without converging.

## Detection Cues

The signature: a single person presenting a deliberation about whether to enter a relationship, with both sides articulated as legitimate, and with the relationship framed as work, obligation, or burden on one of the two sides.

Surface markers:
- The phrase "too much work," "I don't have time," "it'll take away from."
- A story of having been in a relationship before that was experienced as enduring or heavy. Often a divorce or long breakup.
- A current life full of demanding pursuits (sport, business, training) that the person frames as competitive with relationship.
- The person describes themselves as doing things "to extremes" or as "all or nothing."
- A current difficult relationship with an ex or co-parent that is being treated as evidence about relationships in general.
- The person frames the question as a binary: be in one or don't.

Indirect markers:
- The deliberation has been running for months or longer.
- The person is in their head about it. The body is not in the room with the question.
- The person describes the inner voices but does not describe their somatic experience of the question.
- The "for" voice and the "against" voice are both abstract. Neither has been felt out loud.
- The person describes a strength they have for enduring hard things (the ultramarathon, the demanding job, the long hours) and is unconsciously treating relationship as the next domain to apply that strength to.

This concern is closely adjacent to [[should-i-stay-with-my-ex]] but is structurally different: there is no current relationship to stay in or leave. The person is single. The deliberation is about whether to seek one. The decision-frame is doing the same work — covering an avoided emotional pattern — but the pattern being covered is usually [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]] in its anticipated form, not in its active form.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the should-I-or-shouldn't-I question is rarely a real decision. Three structural patterns Joe typically hears:

1. **The relationship-frame the person is running is endurance.** They are imagining the relationship as a thing they would have to endure, manage, push through. Of course they don't want one — they're not deliberating about a relationship, they're deliberating about a self-imposed endurance project. The actual question is not "do I want a relationship" but "how do I create a relationship that isn't endurance?" See [[relationships-as-test-of-endurance]] and [[reframe-from-whether-to-how]].

2. **The deliberation itself is the self-abandonment.** The voice that says "I want to" gets dismissed by the voice that says "it's too much work" — and that dismissal is the pattern. The person abandons their want every time the inner voice argues against it. The relationship question is in the head because the want, in the body, has been left behind. See [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]].

3. **The caretaking-of-the-ex is still running.** Even though the person is single, the current difficult relationship with an ex (especially around co-parenting) is the active version of the same pattern. They bend to what the ex wants and then get pissed about it. The "relationships are too much work" story is being generated by the configuration they're in with the ex, then generalized to all relationships. See [[decision-question-covers-the-caretaking]].

The work is not to answer the question on its terms. The work is to surface the patterns generating the question and shift them. Once the patterns shift, the question dissolves.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Same presentation, different paths:

- **If the person describes relationships in the language of endurance, burden, obligation:** see [[relationships-as-test-of-endurance]]. The story they're running about relationships is the problem, not the relationship. Joe will explicitly name the frame ("you treat relationships like an ultramarathon") and then ask the reframing question: how do you want to treat them?

- **If the person describes a current difficult relationship with an ex:** see [[decision-question-covers-the-caretaking]]. The active caretaking with the ex is the present-tense version of the pattern. Joe will ask: how do you abandon yourself in that relationship? The answer ("I bend to what she wants and then get pissed") makes the pattern visible.

- **If the person describes both inner voices (the for and the against):** see [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]]. The deliberation between the two voices is the abandonment in real time. The "for" voice keeps getting dismissed. Joe will ask "how do you abandon yourself with the voice in your head" to make it visible.

- **If the person describes a high tolerance for endurance (ultramarathons, demanding work):** see [[use-your-strength-on-the-real-work]]. The endurance capacity is a real skill. Joe will redirect it: use the endurance for staying with yourself, not for enduring the partner's pain.

- **If the person reports being more alive away from relationships:** this is a misread. Joe will distinguish: the aliveness is from the absence of the management pattern, not from the absence of relationship. The relationship done differently would not produce the absence of aliveness. See [[enjoyment-attracts]] (the endurance trajectory in the relationship domain).

## Coaching Path

The deliberation is almost never the unit of work. The path:

1. **Ask both voices what they sound like.** Don't engage with the deliberation at its frame. Surface the "for" voice as fully as the "against" voice has already been surfaced. Both voices need to be in the room. See [[whats-the-for-part]].

2. **Surface the avoided hurt.** "What's the hurt you're trying to avoid, if any?" See [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]]. The hurt is usually the same hurt that produced the previous relationship's deadness — and the person is using the avoidance-of-relationship to manage the avoidance-of-that-hurt.

3. **Name the endurance frame.** If the person describes relationships as work, obligation, burden — say it back. "You treat relationships like an ultramarathon and then a test of endurance? Yeah, I would avoid the fuck out of that." The naming makes the configuration visible. See [[relationships-as-test-of-endurance]].

4. **Reframe the question.** Instead of "should I be in a relationship or not," ask "how do I create a relationship that isn't endurance, that's supportive?" See [[reframe-from-whether-to-how]]. The reframe collapses the binary and points at the actual variable: the configuration the person would bring to the relationship, not whether the relationship exists.

5. **Surface the active caretaking pattern.** Ask about a current relationship where the pattern is running (the ex, the co-parent, the inner voice). "How do you abandon yourself there?" See [[how-do-you-abandon-yourself]]. The answer reveals that the pattern is not about relationship — it's about self-abandonment in any context.

6. **Reframe the work.** "Is this about relationships, or how much of this is just about learning not to abandon yourself?" The work moves out of the relationship domain entirely. The relationship is downstream of the self-abandonment work.

7. **Demonstrate the shift in real time.** Use the live spectrum of presence in the session. "Right now you're abandoning yourself less than at the beginning of the call. I see you here with me in a different way." See [[surface-the-spectrum-of-presence-in-real-time]]. The recognition that abandonment-vs-non-abandonment is a moment-by-moment spectrum gives the person a present-tense practice.

8. **Have them ask the question from the new place.** "Ask the question from that space, from the non-abandonment space, if there even is a question." The person usually discovers that from the non-abandonment space the question dissolves — or that asking the question itself pulls them back into abandonment. Both discoveries are the recognition.

9. **Leverage their existing strength.** If they have endurance capacity (ultramarathons, demanding work), redirect it. "Use what you do for 150 miles on the trail with what you just did here with me." See [[use-your-strength-on-the-real-work]]. The mind's chatter is the same on the trail as in the question; the move is the same; the person already knows how.

## What Joe Does Not Do

He does not weigh the pros and cons of being in a relationship versus being single. The deliberation is the avoidance.

He does not adjudicate whether the person is "ready" for a relationship. Readiness-talk reinforces the endurance frame.

He does not offer advice about dating, attachment styles, or partner selection. The work is not the relationship; it's the self-abandonment pattern that would show up in any relationship.

He does not validate "I don't have time" or "it's too much work" as if those were real constraints. They are the frame, not the truth.

He does not let the person settle for the surface insight without giving them the body-level move. The work has to be practiced in real time in the session for it to be available outside.
