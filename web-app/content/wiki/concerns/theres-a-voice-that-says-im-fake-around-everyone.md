# There's a voice that makes me feel like I'm being fake around everyone

## What Someone Says

The concern is presented as a long-running, multi-year voice that runs across the entirety of the person's social life. It is not domain-specific (work, love, family). It is not an episodic shame attack. It is the constant background suspicion that whatever they are showing other people is not real.

- "This voice I've had in my head that makes me feel like I'm being fake around basically everyone I know."
- "I've had that voice for a really long time, and I don't really like it being there, and it doesn't really help me."
- "I thought to bring the hardest problem to you, the one that I don't really expect can be solved."

Typical signatures:

- The voice is named as a discrete thing. The person already has some distance from it. They are not saying "I am fake"; they are saying "there is a voice that tells me I am fake."
- The voice runs across all relationships. Not "in this relationship I feel fake," but "around basically everyone I know."
- The voice has been around for years. The person has tried many things and the voice has not gone away. The presentation often includes the preemptive disclaimer that the problem may not be solvable.
- The voice does not get described as helpful. The person knows it costs them something. They do not know what to do with it.

## Detection Cues

- The word "fake" or "not real" or "performing" applied to the self in social contexts generally, not specific ones
- A discrete inner voice referenced as "a voice" or "this voice" — already partially objectified
- Long duration framing: "for a really long time," "for years," "I've always had it"
- Preemptive hopelessness about whether the problem can be addressed
- Resistance to fixing presented up front: "the last time someone tried to help me with this, they tried to fix me and I felt pressure to perform being fixed"
- A request that does not really ask for change — more for company in the unfixable

The "I don't expect this can be solved" opening is itself the diagnostic. The person is testing whether the helper will collapse into fix-mode or whether they can be met without that. The opening is a defensive move that is also an invitation.

## What Joe Hears

Two things underneath, simultaneously.

First: the voice is probably saying something true. The voice that calls the person fake around everyone is often [[voice-as-guide-to-unhealed-places]] pointing at the actual social mask the person is operating from. The mask is real. The performances in conversations are real. The voice is correctly perceiving them.

Second: the voice's accuracy has been collapsed with a moral verdict. The person hears "you are being fake" and immediately layers "and that is bad / and you are bad / and this is a problem." The verdict is what makes the voice unbearable, not the perception. See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] for the general form of the move that decouples perception from verdict.

The reframe that does the work: what if the voice is right but it's not bad? The voice is pointing at the mask. The mask is a real thing. The mask being seen through is not a defect — it is what the Buddhists call the veil dropping. The thing the voice is naming is the actual perception of inauthenticity, which is the same thing as the perception of authenticity becoming available. The voice is intuition, doing its job, perceiving accurately. The person has been treating the intuition as an attack because they have been treating the perception itself as the problem.

Underneath the voice's accuracy is the actual material that needs attention: there is a way the person is showing up with people that is not their full self, and there is a part of them that knows it and keeps pointing at it. The path is not to silence the voice. The path is to let the voice be right and then go where it is pointing.

The preemptive "this can't be fixed" is the second piece of intelligence. The person has had people try to fix them. They have felt the pressure to demonstrate they are fixed. They know that being treated as broken does not produce wholeness. The opening is a request to not be put through that again. See [[send-the-message-that-youre-whole]] and [[dont-presume-the-person-is-broken]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person already has some distance from the voice (saying "this voice" rather than "I am fake"): they are partially out of the identification. Joe can work directly with the voice rather than having to dissolve the identification first.
- If the person opens with "I don't think this can be fixed" or some variant: the read is that they have been fix-attempted before and they are protecting against another one. Joe declines fix-mode explicitly and early. See [[dont-presume-the-person-is-broken]].
- If the body responds to the assume-it's-right reframe with sensations (energy moving, weird body feelings): the reframe has landed. The work moves to letting the body have the sensation rather than trying to interpret it.
- If the person says "I need to go away and be with myself": Joe checks whether they are actually NOT being with themselves right now. Often the move toward isolation is a recreation of the very split the voice is naming. See [[point-out-theyre-already-doing-it]].
- If the person worries they will not be able to relate to people from this new state: Joe names that fear of disconnection is a marker of significant growth, not a sign of doing it wrong. See [[fear-of-disconnection-marks-real-growth]].
- If the person reports head-scrambling, slowness, second-delay in speech: Joe normalizes this as the temporary disorientation of operating from a new place. See [[slowness-after-the-drop]].

## Coaching Path

1. **Decline fix-mode explicitly and early.** When the person says "I don't think this can be solved," Joe does not argue, reassure, or promise. He drops the fix-frame: "I have absolutely no interest in fixing you. It assumes you're broken." The explicit refusal is the first move and it is required. Without it, anything else lands inside the same dynamic the person came in protecting against.

2. **Calibrate how active the voice is right now.** "What percentage of that voice was active when you started with the 'I don't think this can be fixed'?" The voice is operating in the room. Naming this lets the work happen with the actual voice rather than with the memory of the voice.

3. **Run the assume-it's-right move.** [[assume-the-voice-is-right-but-not-bad]]: "What if we assume it's right? What if we assume it's right, but it's not bad?" The voice perceiving inauthenticity is not the problem. The reframe collapses the verdict layer that has made the voice unbearable.

4. **Reach for the Buddhist veil framing if it fits.** "When the Buddhists talk about the veil dropping, what do you think they're talking about dropping if it's not that?" The reframe is from "the voice is attacking me" to "the voice is the perception of the veil dropping." The person's intuition has been doing the work of awakening; they have been calling that work an attack.

5. **Stay with the body sensations.** When the person reports weird body feelings (energy moving, something going up), the move is "Great. Enjoy them." No interpretation. No directing. The body is doing the integration; the person's job is to receive it rather than manage it.

6. **Catch the move toward isolation.** When the person says they should go away and be with themselves, [[point-out-theyre-already-doing-it]]: "I don't see you not being with yourself right now. What makes you need to go away to be with yourself?" The person is already in the state they are saying requires solitude. Naming this dissolves the split.

7. **Name the energy-from-resistance mechanism.** When the person says "I'm not trying to do any work to manage this," Joe names what is happening: "The resistance to it is the thing that gives it energy." The voice has been fueled by being fought. Without the fight, it has less to do. See [[resistance]].

8. **Thank the voice.** [[thank-the-voice-when-its-truth]]: "What is it just like to thank the voice that's like, this is all fake?" The voice that has been correctly perceiving the mask is now thanked for the perception. This is distinct from translating-the-care (see [[voice-as-care]]); this is recognizing the voice as accurate intuition and receiving its gift directly.

9. **Pre-empt the social fear.** Often the person worries that operating from this new place will mean losing connection. Joe names this directly: "One of the marks of big movements in ourselves is that we get scared that we won't be able to relate to other people." The fear is a marker of the size of the shift, not evidence of doing it wrong. See [[fear-of-disconnection-marks-real-growth]].

10. **Normalize the disorientation.** If the person reports being slow, scrambled, second-delay in conversation, Joe names this as the expected first phase of operating from the new place. "All that shit will happen until you stop trying to listen to the [old voice] — until you're just listening here." The slowness is not a defect; it is what learning to speak from a new place looks like. See [[slowness-after-the-drop]].

The whole arc can happen quickly — in under twenty turns — when the person is already partially out of the identification and the opening "this can't be fixed" was a protective gesture rather than a deep collapse. The work is to refuse the fix-frame, accept the voice as accurate intuition, and stay with the body sensations as the shift integrates.
