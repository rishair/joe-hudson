# I'm Not Comfortable Sharing My Story

## What Someone Says

The presenting language is built around the word "comfortable." The person has identified that they want to share their personal story — usually some version of personal history, recovery, addiction, illness, or formative experience — and that doing so produces a felt threat in their body even when they intellectually want to do it. The defining feature is the comfort framing: they want to *become* comfortable. Common phrasings:

- "I'm looking to get more comfortable with sharing my story and sharing personal information with people."
- "I've been doing it a bit, and lately I've noticed that it registers as a really big threat in my mind."
- "I'd say I'm at 25% comfort and I'd want to be at 75%."
- "I want to be completely comfortable with it."
- "When I get speaking about what I want to share, I notice that I won't say what I actually want to say."
- "I'm running groups talking about [addiction / recovery / my experience] and I'm sharing my perspectives but I'm not comfortable doing it."
- "I'm doing it, just not comfortably."

The person is often already doing the thing they're presenting as impossible. They've raised their hand, they've signed up for the workshop, they're running the group, they've started the podcast. The concern is not that they cannot do it; it is that doing it produces discomfort they believe should not be there.

## Detection Cues

The concern is recognizable in several configurations:

- Direct presentation: the person names the comfort-with-sharing target explicitly.
- Indirect presentation: the person raises their hand to do a live coaching session in front of an audience, then names being nervous about being seen.
- Through proxy: the person describes a coaching practice, a teaching practice, a creative practice that involves disclosing personal material, and reports that the disclosure is the hard part.
- Through visible discomfort: the person physically demonstrates the discomfort while presenting the problem — they're shaky, lightheaded, dissociated.
- Through the framing: the word "comfortable" is doing structural work in the presentation. The person treats comfort as the prerequisite for sharing.

The decisive cue: the person is doing the thing right now — sharing their story in this coaching session, raising their hand in a public group — while presenting the problem of not being able to share their story. The two are happening simultaneously.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the surface concern is a structural error in the premise. The person has built their entire approach around the idea that becoming comfortable is the path to sharing. The premise is exactly backwards. Comfort is not the precondition for sharing. Discomfort is the conduit.

The concern also surfaces a deeper structure: the person has been taught to hold back their truth, and the holding-back has become the operating mode even when they decide to "share." What gets shared is the comfortable version. The actual truth — the angry version, the angry-at-the-bad-advice version, the angry-at-the-people-who-influenced-them version — stays inside. The reason the sharing doesn't feel like sharing is that the real material is never coming out.

What is being held back is almost always emotion. Specifically: the layers of emotion that were not allowed when the person was young. The pattern is usually that anger went first (anger wasn't allowed) and underneath the unallowed anger is unallowed sadness and underneath the unallowed sadness is unallowed joy. See [[layers-of-unallowed-self]]. The person has been managing all of these in order to present an acceptable version of themselves. When they "share their story," they share the acceptable version. The body knows the acceptable version is not the truth, which is why the body produces discomfort, dissociation, lightheadedness, the out-of-body experience.

The "addiction" thread, when it is present, is not incidental. The pattern that produced the addiction is the same pattern producing the discomfort with sharing now: the person was taught that their own truth was dangerous, that holding-back was the safer move. The addiction was a downstream consequence of the self-suppression. The recovery from addiction was a recovery of the willingness to refuse self-harm. The current discomfort with sharing is the same self-suppression running again — and the same skill that produced the recovery is the skill needed for the sharing. See [[parallel-the-clients-own-survival-skill-to-the-present]].

The deepest implication: holding back the truth is itself self-harm. See [[holding-back-truth-is-self-harm]]. The person is doing harm to themselves every time they speak the comfortable version instead of the actual one. They already learned, in the addiction recovery, that refusing self-harm is the move that produces freedom. The current concern is an invitation to apply the same refusal to the speaking.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The concern branches based on what is visible in the person's body and speech as they present it:

- If the person says they want to be 75% comfortable when they're at 25%: the comfort-framing itself is the structural error. Joe challenges the premise directly. See [[challenge-the-premise-in-the-framing]] applied to comfort. "What makes you need to be comfortable? What if being uncomfortable is the best way to share your story?"

- If the person reports being lightheaded, out-of-body, or dissociated while presenting: read [[trying-to-be-comfortable-instead-of-being-uncomfortable]]. The body is producing the dissociation as the symptom of the wrong operation (trying to be comfortable while in territory that is meant to be uncomfortable). The move is to name the three modes (trying to overcome the discomfort, fully in the discomfort, comfortable and discomfort is not happening) and orient them toward the second. See [[whats-happening-in-your-system-three-modes]].

- If the person starts to speak truth and then visibly clamps down: read [[the-control-after-the-truth]]. They opened the tap, the real material came out, and then control reasserted. Joe names what just shifted ("everybody could feel that shift") and uses the contrast to install the felt difference between truth and control. See [[name-the-shift-everyone-felt]].

- If the person has a history that contained a recovery move (addiction recovery, leaving an abusive relationship, breaking free of a controlling family): read [[parallel-the-clients-own-survival-skill-to-the-present]]. They already have the move. The current concern is the same move applied to a different domain.

- If the person is presenting from a "despondent teenager" posture (the slumped, fuck-you-you're-not-going-to-pick-me energy of a kid whose power was crushed): name what you see, then go underneath. The despondent teenager is the cover for the powerful kid who had the willfulness shut down. The work is to make that suppressed power available.

- If the person smiles when the conversation lands on something alive (Joe mentions his wife and the person lights up): something is intact underneath the despondent teenager. Use the smile as the entry point: "there's something underneath the despondent teenager. Let's go find out what it is."

## Coaching Path

The typical flow from this concern follows this shape:

1. **Challenge the comfort premise immediately.** "What makes you need to be comfortable? What if being uncomfortable is the best way to share your story?" The premise that comfort is the path is the structural error; the whole conversation has to happen outside it. See [[challenge-the-premise-in-the-framing]].

2. **Use the live moment.** The person is sharing their story right now, in this coaching session. There is no need to recreate the situation. Joe pivots immediately: "Give me a one-paragraph story about yourself that's got like a 50% charge." The work happens in the room. See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]].

3. **Run the three-mode diagnostic.** "What's happening in your system right now? Are you trying to overcome the discomfort, are you fully in the discomfort, or are you comfortable and discomfort's not happening?" The diagnostic surfaces which mode is running. See [[whats-happening-in-your-system-three-modes]].

4. **Instruct them to walk into the discomfort and speak from there.** Not from the desire to overcome it. Not from the trying. From inside the discomfort itself. "See what it's like to sit in that discomfort, walk right into the middle of it, and then speak from there." See [[walk-into-the-discomfort-and-speak-from-there]] and the deeper sibling [[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]].

5. **When the truth comes out, name the shift.** "All of a sudden, something just shifted. Everybody could feel that shift." The naming installs the felt difference between truth and control as a recognizable signal the person can use later. See [[name-the-shift-everyone-felt]].

6. **Apply the parallel survival skill.** If the person has a recovery story, name the parallel directly. "What did you have to do to get through the addiction? Refuse anything harmful to you. That's what's happening right now. Holding back your truth is harmful to you." The parallel makes the move feel like home rather than novelty. See [[parallel-the-clients-own-survival-skill-to-the-present]].

7. **Name the layers of unallowed self.** As the truth-speaking opens, the anger arrives first, then sadness, then joy. Name the layers. "Beneath the anger that wasn't allowed is a sadness that wasn't allowed, is a fucking joy that wasn't allowed. It's just layers of you that weren't allowed." See [[layers-of-unallowed-self]].

8. **Install the self-as-oppressor frame.** "You got taught how to be your own oppressor. And you're learning how to break free by telling your truth." See [[you-were-taught-to-be-your-own-oppressor]].

9. **Ask the central question repeatedly.** "What's your truth in this moment?" The question, asked again and again, installs the practice. Each time the person answers, the control re-asserts a little. Each repetition lets them feel the asserting-of-control as a discrete event rather than a constant. See [[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]].

10. **If the speech keeps introducing control, go nonverbal.** "Don't even think that you have to say anything. What's your truth in this moment?" The nonverbal access reveals what the verbal access keeps managing. See [[go-nonverbal-and-let-the-nonverbal-talk]].

11. **Reframe the practice off the stage and into the present moment.** The practice is not in front of the camera or the group. The practice is right now, with the mom, the dad, the friend, the moment. "It doesn't happen in front of a group of people talking about addiction. It happens right now." This removes the loaded performance frame and makes the work available in every moment.

12. **Let the recognition arrive.** The person often discovers in the session that their truth in many of the situations they were presenting was: "I didn't want to be telling my story." The teaching is layered: speaking truth is not the same as performing vulnerability, and saying "I don't want to tell my story" can be the truest version of telling the story.

## Related Concerns

- [[i-get-anxious-in-relationships]] — the broader pattern of anxiety around being seen. The being-seen concern lives at the relational scale; this concern lives at the storytelling/disclosure scale. The structural work is similar (bring the moment into the room, find what is being hidden, name that it's emotion) but the specific terrain is different — this concern has a personal-history disclosure component that the relational anxiety concern does not.
- [[i-want-to-be-authentic-on-stage]] — the performance version of the same structure. A stand-up comic, a public speaker, an author asked to give a reading.
- [[how-do-i-love-myself-and-express-my-emotions]] — the broader concern of having been taught to suppress emotions. This concern is the specific application: when the suppression operates in the storytelling context.
- [[i-cant-forgive-myself-for-hurting-them]] — the related concern of self-betrayal in a relationship, where the self-suppression happened in a specific dyadic context. Both concerns touch the same underlying pattern of holding back the truth as a self-harming default.
