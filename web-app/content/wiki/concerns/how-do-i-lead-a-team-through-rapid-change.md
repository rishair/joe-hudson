# How do I lead a team through rapid change?

## What Someone Says

The presenting concern arrives in several forms, all variations of the same underlying question.

- "How do I lead a team that keeps getting the rug pulled out from under them?"
- "How do I keep up with AI?"
- "How do I run a company that has to change every six months?"
- "How do I keep my team aligned through constant pivots?"
- "The market is different every week and I have to lead through that."
- "I have a guy in revenue who's been selling one thing and now I have to ask him to sell another. I have a CTO who built one thing and now we have to change it. How do I have those conversations?"
- "We just pivoted. We're going to have to pivot again. How do I do this without breaking the team?"
- "How do I keep great talent when the work keeps changing under them?"
- "Everyone in the Valley is telling me to work 996. How do I lead at this pace?"

The variations all point at one thing: the leader is operating in a fast-moving environment, the rate of change exceeds what their team can absorb at normal speed, and the standard leadership playbooks (steady vision, clear strategy, consistent direction) do not match the reality.

## Detection Cues

- The leader mentions AI, exponential change, weekly model releases, or accelerating market conditions
- The leader describes a recent pivot, rename, architecture change, or major repositioning
- The leader is worried about retention in a market where competitors offer wild compensation
- The leader has just laid people off or is about to and is figuring out how to communicate it
- The leader is overwhelmed and worried about projecting that overwhelm to the team
- The leader describes "996" or sleeping-at-the-office or other extreme-hours culture pressure
- The leader uses language like "the rug pulled out from underneath" or "everything keeps changing"
- The leader is wondering whether their company can absorb another major transition
- The leader is asking whether the standard playbooks still work in this environment

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the presenting question is usually one or more of these:

**A nervous system in chronic overwhelm.** The leader has not yet built the personal capacity to absorb the pace they are operating in. The overwhelm is propagating to the leadership team and then to the company. See [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]] for the cascade mechanism. The leader is asking how to manage the team while the deeper need is to develop their own regulation.

**A request for an aligned vehicle.** The leader intuits that the team they have is not structurally ready for the next change. Each pivot has been a fresh fight. The leader is asking, underneath the operational question, how to build the kind of team that does not require a fresh fight each time. See [[alignment-beats-effort]].

**A request for principles that hold under uncertainty.** The strategic answers keep changing. The leader is reaching for something more durable. Underneath the "how do I make decisions in this?" is often "what would let me make decisions in this without having to start over every time?" See [[principles-free-you]].

**A request for the right relationship with overwhelm and FOMO.** The emotions are real. The competitor announcements, the news cycle, the team's anxiety, the customer's expectations. The leader is asking how to operate well in the presence of these emotional pressures. See [[emotional-cycling-as-superpower-in-exponential-change]].

**A request to learn to run at friction.** The leader knows there are conversations they have been avoiding. The pace is making the avoidance more expensive. They are asking, underneath the operational question, how to start having those conversations. See [[run-toward-friction]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching point. Two leaders can present the same concern and Joe goes different directions based on what he reads.

- If the leader is **performing competence** while underneath there is genuine collapse, Joe will name the gap before continuing. The work cannot start until the actual state is in the room.
- If the leader is **in chronic overwhelm** and the team is reading it, Joe attends to the leader's nervous system before attending to the team or strategy. See [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]] and [[slow-down-to-speed-up]].
- If the leader is **avoiding a specific hard conversation**, Joe surfaces the conversation. See [[approach-the-pink-elephant]] and [[run-toward-friction]].
- If the leader is **trying to drive the team faster than the leader's own clarity supports**, Joe redirects from "how do I get them to be faster" to "what is the clarity that would let them move." See [[principles-free-you]].
- If the leader **does not yet have operating principles** that the team can use to make decentralized decisions, Joe moves toward the principle-building work. See [[rollout-principles-in-organization]].
- If the leader **has principles but the team is not living them**, Joe moves toward the structural rollout questions: are the principles sayable in real time? Do people invoke them in meetings? See [[open-the-drawer-as-shared-verb]].
- If the leader is in **FOMO about competitor moves**, Joe redirects from "what are they doing" to "what is my actual game?" See [[emotional-cycling-as-superpower-in-exponential-change]].
- If the leader is **about to communicate a hard change** (layoff, pivot, rename), Joe attends to the author position from which the communication will be made. See [[write-the-email-from-empowered]].

## Coaching Path

The typical flow Joe follows from this concern. Not a script. A map of likely moves with branches.

1. **Joe slows the conversation down first.** Before doing any strategy work, Joe will get the leader into the present moment, often through some attention to what is happening in the leader's body. The conversation cannot help if the leader's nervous system is in fight-or-flight throughout it. See [[slow-down-to-speed-up]].

2. **Joe asks about the actual emotional state under the operational presentation.** Often: "What is it like to be you in this market right now?" or "What's the overwhelm actually like in your body?" The emotional content surfaces before the strategic content can be addressed. See [[decisions-are-emotional]].

3. **Joe attends to the cascade.** The leader's overwhelm is usually being projected to the leadership team and through them to the company. Joe will name this directly, both as diagnostic and as the operating principle the leader will need to work with. See [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]].

4. **Joe redirects toward principles.** When the leader is asking strategic questions that keep getting re-asked, Joe will redirect toward the principles that would dissolve the recurring decisions. See [[principles-free-you]] and [[rollout-principles-in-organization]].

5. **Joe surfaces the unaddressed tensions.** Usually there are friction points the leader has been managing around. Joe will name them and move toward [[run-toward-friction]] as the orientation. The same tensions managed-around are what is producing the felt-overwhelm.

6. **Joe reframes alignment as leverage.** The leader has often been over-investing in effort and under-investing in alignment. Joe will name [[alignment-beats-effort]] explicitly and redirect attention to where the alignment gaps are.

7. **Joe attends to specific high-stakes communications.** If the leader is about to send a layoff email or announce a pivot, Joe will coach the author position. See [[write-the-email-from-empowered]]. The single communication often becomes the cultural inflection point that the rest of the change cycle rides on.

8. **Joe names the developmental opportunity.** Underneath the operational concern is usually a developmental edge for the leader. Joe will name [[company-as-fastest-self-development]] when the leader is ready to hear that the role is the practice, not just the job.

9. **Joe names the structural shift in what matters.** In the AI era, the company that used to require ten thousand people now requires two or three hundred, and what differentiates those few people is not intelligence but wisdom -- the quality of decisions, the quality of relationships, the capacity to hold the team game. The leader's question of "how do I lead through this" is the question of how to build and hire for the [[the-three-games-outer-inner-team]] at the level the small high-stakes team requires. See [[intelligence-vs-wisdom]] for the era framing and [[you-can-hire-anything-except-being-yourself]] for the hiring principle.
