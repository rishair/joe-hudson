# I freeze around authority figures

## What Someone Says

The concern usually arrives as a workplace complaint that has more charge than the workplace explanation can account for.

- "I freeze around people who remind me of my father, and especially in work situations."
- "It happens repeatedly. I can be myself everywhere else and then in this one meeting I shut down."
- "My boss does this thing and I just go cold."
- "I either want to fight myself or I just want to roll over and stay safe."
- "Whenever a man like my father is in the room, I lose access to myself."
- "I become unproductive whenever this particular kind of person manages me."

Sometimes the person has already noticed the parental connection. Sometimes they have not. The fact that the freeze happens with one gender, one personality type, or one specific role configuration is the diagnostic clue.

## Detection Cues

- Disproportionate emotional reaction to a specific category of person (often men like dad, or women like mom)
- The freeze, withdrawal, or rebellion appears with this category and not with others
- The person can name a specific "type" that triggers them (gendered, role-based, age-based)
- Productivity drops when this person is in the room or in the chain of command
- A complaint about "not being seen" by the authority figure, often with strong language ("they don't see me at all," "they don't know how I tick")
- Strong judgments of the authority figure ("wrapped up in themselves," "doesn't actually care")
- Rebellion-flavored language: "I just stop performing for them," "I shut it down"

## What Joe Hears

This is [[childhood-imprinting]] showing up in the workplace. The emotional patterns installed before age 7 do not stay attached to the parents. They transfer onto whatever inherits the parental slot: a boss, a partner, a mentor, money, an institution. The pattern is the shape; the target rotates.

Underneath the freeze is a binary that the person is unconsciously holding: fight or roll over. Both options put the authority in the control seat. Both are responses to a power-over frame. The person has not yet found the third option, which is empowerment alongside the authority rather than against or beneath them.

Also underneath: the very thing the person judges the authority figure for is something they are doing themselves. "They don't see me" is the surface complaint. The deeper layer is that the person is not letting themselves be seen, and is also wrapped up in their own wanting just as they accuse the authority of being wrapped up in theirs.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person is [[in-the-story-im-not-being-seen]]: the complaint is being used as a sure-fire excuse not to risk being seen. Joe will mirror the judgment back: "how is that not true about you?"
- If the person is in the [[fight-or-roll-over-binary]]: the only options on the table are submission or rebellion. Joe will name the binary and look for the third option.
- If the person is [[disassociating-in-the-coaching-room]]: the freeze pattern is happening live, with Joe occupying the authority slot. Joe will name it ("I notice you stop looking at me when you're trying to feel this") and use the live moment as material.
- If the person performs the upset rather than feels it: the rebellion has become identity. Joe will move toward the want underneath.

## Coaching Path

1. Name the pattern. "How do you see them as like your father?" The connection is usually quick.

2. Ask for the evidence. "What's the evidence?" Get specific. This makes the projection examinable rather than absolute.

3. Mirror the judgment. "How is that not true about you?" The pattern the person accuses the authority of is almost always one they are running themselves.

4. Find the three undermined things. Joe maps this concern onto three components that are not being lived: the want, being seen, and empowerment. He works each one in sequence.

5. Locate the binary. "How are you undermining them?" Surface that "stop performing" is the rebellion move and that rebellion puts them in the control seat as much as compliance would.

6. Use the live coaching room. The authority dynamic is almost always running with Joe in the moment. He uses this: "how much of me is fighting you?" "How much distance is between us right now?"

7. Find the third option. Not fight, not submission. Joe pushes toward [[upright-undefended-love]]. Empowerment alongside.

8. Role-play the original scene. Joe takes the authority figure role and the person tries the new stance live. See [[become-the-authority-figure]].

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not validate "they don't see you." The complaint is partially true and entirely useful as a shield. Validating it reinforces the shield.
- He does not coach the workplace conflict tactically. The workplace is the venue, not the issue.
- He does not move quickly to the dad memory. He stays in the present pattern long enough to make it observable, then connects to the origin.
- He does not let "I just want to be seen" stand as the deepest layer. He moves to "what would it take for you to let yourself be seen?"

## A Structural Companion Move

When the freeze is showing up around a boss specifically, the structural reframe [[reframe-boss-as-customer]] sometimes opens a parallel door. The reframe does not resolve the freeze, but it makes the projection observable as projection — the "boss-as-authority" frame is itself an import from the family system. With that visible, the felt sense of being in the pattern (see [[i-cant-trust-my-thoughts-right-now]]) becomes easier to recognize, and the pattern becomes easier to refuse acting from.

## Where This Sits in the Authority-Issues Framework

This concern is the freeze-flavored expression of Position 1 (the Rebel) or Position 2 (the Approval-Seeker) of [[the-four-authority-positions]]. The freeze itself is a body response that often contains both — the rebellion ("I won't engage") and the submission ("I dare not engage"), held in suspension. The work eventually has to identify which position is actually operating in this specific person, because the coaching paths differ:

- If the freeze is the rebel-pattern frozen ("I am refusing to play"), the move is toward [[wants-over-shoulds]] and reclaiming the want underneath the refusal.
- If the freeze is the approval-seeker-pattern frozen ("I dare not say what I see"), the move is toward speaking the truth without fear (see [[name-the-authoritys-fear]]).

See [[authority-issues]] for the broader concept and [[the-four-authority-positions]] for the structural map.
