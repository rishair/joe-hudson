# I have everything and I'm waiting for the shoe to drop

## What Someone Says

The person presents an anxiety that is not about a specific threat but about the goodness of what they already have. The form is often paradoxical: "Everything is great and I cannot enjoy it." The list of good things is real -- healthy kids, a loving partner, a successful business, a home -- and the anxiety is running underneath all of it.

- "I have everything I always wanted and I'm anxious all the time."
- "I'm with my family and it's like I'm missing it."
- "I'm scared that something bad is going to happen."
- "I keep waiting for the other shoe to drop."
- "I don't deserve this. Like, I know I worked really hard, but..."
- "There are people who had the same circumstances I did and didn't have my outcome."
- "I'm just like married to my best friend with three healthy children and I'm so afraid that something bad is going to happen."
- "I throw myself into the to-do list and I don't feel."
- "I have lost the excitement a little bit because I'm scared."
- "Everyone tells me how blessed I am and I can't feel it."
- "I built the dream and I can't be in it."

The concern is often presented with confusion or guilt about presenting it. The person knows they should be grateful. They know their life is enviable. They are asking for help with something that, by external measure, should not be a problem.

## Detection Cues

- The person describes their life in superlatives ("married to my best friend," "three healthy children," "I built a house") and then names anxiety in the same breath.
- The person compares themselves to others who did not get the same outcome ("there are moms with the same birth I had who didn't have the same outcome"). The survivor-guilt flavor of the anxiety.
- The person describes themselves as "throwing myself into the to-do list" or "always doing" or "always busy" as a way of not feeling.
- The person reports anxiety that has no specific named target ("I'm just scared something bad will happen").
- The person reports being unable to enjoy the time with the people they love -- often family time specifically.
- The person reports a felt sense of "missing it" even while present in good moments.
- The person has done significant work to achieve the good life and now cannot rest in it.
- The person uses the phrase "waiting for the other shoe to drop" or some variant.
- The person reports an undercurrent of anxiety about deserving what they have.

The concern often co-presents with chronic to-do-list activity, with chronic project-mode about their partner (see [[making-your-partner-a-project]]), and with the "I don't feel sadness" or "I don't really feel anything" subtype of dissociation.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the shoe-drop anxiety is repressed excitement. The anxiety and the excitement are the same physiological energy. The good life is genuinely exciting; the person has not allowed themselves to feel the excitement of it; the unallowed excitement is showing up as the anxiety. See [[fear-vs-excitement]] and [[say-im-excited-ten-times]].

"What I'm saying is I think you're repressing your excitement, so it's coming out as anxiety." -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

This is the most common shape underneath the concern, but not the only one. The excitement-as-anxiety configuration is the entry point. Underneath the unallowed excitement is usually one or more of:

- **The childhood program of "calm down."** Most people were trained between 0-7 that getting too excited was unsafe (the parent's "calm down, settle, don't get so excited" response). The trained response to excitement is to constrict it. The constricted excitement becomes anxiety. See [[childhood-imprinting]].
- **An identity threat.** Real excitement opens the self and dissolves the bordered identity. The anxiety is the defense against the dissolution. "If you really allow a feeling of excitement, there's less of you. Your identity starts to crack."
- **Deservingness language as a cover.** "I don't deserve this" is often what the system says when it does not have permission to receive the good thing. The deservingness frame is the symptom; the unallowed receiving is the source. See [[what-would-make-it-that-you-dont-deserve-this]].
- **A future-loss anxiety running on the present.** The system is trying to prevent the loss of the good thing by anticipating it. The anticipation is the substitute for being-with the thing. The present moment with the family is being consumed by future-fear about losing the family. See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for why this strategy makes the loss more likely, not less.
- **Avoidance of feeling alone in the good life.** The person built a life that few of their peers built; they are now in a class of one. The aloneness of being the person who has the good life is unfelt; the anxiety is the substitute.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same presenting concern goes different directions based on what is read.

- If the person describes the good life and then immediately reports "throwing myself into the to-do list" or "I keep busy": the read is the to-do-list-as-avoidance configuration. The activity is generating the anxiety it is trying to prevent. The coaching path is to interrupt the activity briefly and find what the activity is keeping the person away from. See [[stop-to-feel-the-anxiety-underneath-the-productivity]] and [[i-keep-getting-stuff-done-but-nothing-changes]].

- If the person describes the anxiety with the language "I'm with my family and it's like I'm missing it": the read is excitement-as-anxiety. The presence is being filtered through the dissociation of the anticipated-loss. The coaching path is [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] applied to the family-presence specifically: "I'm excited about my family" said out loud ten times.

- If the person describes survivor-guilt language ("there are moms with the same circumstances who didn't have the same outcome"): the read is unfelt gratitude that has converted to anxiety about deservingness. The work is to feel the actual gratitude without the guilt. The deservingness question is the misdirection; the unfelt gratitude is the source. See [[what-would-make-it-that-you-dont-deserve-this]] for the question that surfaces the underlying belief.

- If the person describes a project-stance toward their partner ("I'm trying to get him to..."): the read is [[project-language-about-the-partner]]. The anxiety is partly being run through the management of the partner. The coaching path includes both the excitement-as-anxiety work and the recognition that the partner-management is doing the work of not feeling the anxiety directly. See [[making-your-partner-a-project]].

- If the person reports "I don't feel sadness" or "I don't really feel emotions" alongside the chronic anxiety: the read is broader emotional shutdown. The anxiety is the only feeling that has remained available because all the others have been trained out. The coaching path is to make room for the trained-out feelings first, often starting with sadness. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

- If the person reports "I keep doing all the things I read in the books and it's not landing": the read is the intellectual-practice configuration. The work has been treated as a cognitive project. The body has not been let into it. The coaching path is to drop the practice-orientation and let an emotion arrive in the room. The session source is the canonical example -- "Why cannot I just cry on a drop of a hat like I have done 3 times during this podcast?" Joe's response: "I'm giving you this safe space cocoon to do this and I'm asking you the hard questions" -- the safety and the questions are what allow the feeling, not the techniques.

- If the person reports anxiety about a specific big goal they have ("I have this big thing I want to do"): the read is the bigger-room flavor of fear. The fear is excitement about expansion. The coaching path is [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] applied to the big goal. See [[fear-vs-excitement]].

- If the person describes their kids as the teachers showing them what they cannot feel: the read is the family-as-curriculum configuration. The kids are presenting the unfelt emotions of the parent. The coaching path is to let the kids do that work -- "you've got the best teachers in the world, you've got 3 kids, you just have to be with them."

## Coaching Path

The typical flow for this concern:

1. **Convert the anxiety to excitement at the body level.** Use [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] applied to the specific source of the anxiety. If the person says "I'm anxious about being with my family," the practice is "I'm excited about my family" out loud ten times. The somatic shift often arrives in the room.

2. **Name the principle.** The anxiety they are presenting is excitement they are not letting themselves feel. See [[fear-vs-excitement]]. The good life has not been let into the body. The body is running the anxiety as a substitute for the unallowed expansive feeling.

3. **Find the childhood training.** Often "calm down" was the message. The system was trained that excitement is unsafe. The work is now to be the parent who lets the excitement come. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

4. **Address the identity-threat layer.** Real excitement dissolves the bordered self. The anxiety is partly the defense against the dissolution. Naming this allows the person to recognize the anxiety as a defense and not as information about a real threat.

5. **Interrupt the to-do-list-as-avoidance configuration.** If the person uses busyness to avoid the anxiety, the activity needs to be interrupted briefly so the anxiety can be felt. See [[four-causes-of-stress]] for the broader frame -- the busyness is doing the work of not feeling.

6. **Catch the survivor-guilt deservingness frame.** "I don't deserve this" is the symptom of unallowed gratitude. Use [[what-would-make-it-that-you-dont-deserve-this]] or [[turn-the-love-on-yourself-what-did-you-do]] to surface what the system has decided would be required to deserve.

7. **Address the partner-as-project pattern if it is operating.** Often the partner has been recruited into the anxiety as the management target. See [[making-your-partner-a-project]] and [[encouraging-them-agrees-with-their-self-attack]]. The recognition and the four-move replacement ([[own-your-want-instead-of-encouraging-them]]) are part of the work.

8. **Sit with the loneliness of having the good life.** Often unspoken under the concern is the aloneness of being the person who has the good life. The peers who did not get the good life cannot meet the person inside it. The work includes feeling the aloneness rather than displacing it into to-do-list activity.

## What This Concern Is Not

- It is not the same as [[im-so-stressed-out]]. The stress concern is about load. This concern is about the inability to receive what is already there. The interventions differ.
- It is not the same as [[im-not-an-anxious-person]]. The "I'm not anxious" identity is the opposite presentation -- this concern is from someone who is explicitly identifying as anxious.
- It is not the same as [[i-have-to-X-to-be-free]]. That concern is about needing the next thing to be free. This concern is about already having the things and still being anxious. It is the post-acquisition shape of the same engine -- the X-to-be-free engine did not stop when the X was acquired; the anxiety changed costume from "I need to get X" to "I'm scared I'll lose X." See [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] for the money-money-loop example of the same mechanism.
- It is not the same as [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]], though the bigger-room flavor of fear-vs-excitement reframe applies in both cases.

## The Reframe Available Underneath

The concern assumes the goal is to "stop feeling anxious about the good life." The structural truth is that the anxiety is the body's announcement that the excitement has not been allowed in. The goal is not less anxiety; the goal is the excitement the anxiety is the kinked form of. See [[fear-vs-excitement]] and [[kinked-emotions]].

This reframe often lands quickly in the body when the [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] practice is run live. The same situation that produced anxiety thirty seconds ago produces excitement after the practice. The body's confirmation that the reframe is real makes the reframe operable.

The deeper reframe: the inability to feel the good life is downstream of the trained constriction against excitement. The good life keeps being there. The body keeps running the training. The work is on the training, not on producing more good life.

## The Bigger-Room Frame

This concern is one of the cleanest presentations of the second source of anxiety in [[two-sources-of-anxiety]]. The person has stepped into the bigger room -- the dream life, the loving family, the home they worked for -- and they are not letting themselves fill it. The body is producing the activation that matches the size of the room. The person is holding their felt-self small inside the activation. The mismatch is what reads as the chronic anxiety.

"It also comes from that moment when we are doing the thing that we know we're meant to do, stepping into that bigger room, and we're not feeling it all the way." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

The reframe matters because it moves the concern out of the deservingness language ("I don't deserve this") and into the structural language ("the room is bigger than I have been letting myself fill"). The structural language gives the body something to do. The deservingness language is a dead end.

The somatic move that addresses this directly is [[fill-the-room]]: let the felt-self expand to occupy the actual size of the life you are now in. The verbal complement is [[say-im-excited-ten-times]]. Both convert the held activation into accessible excitement. The combination is often the live-in-the-room intervention.
