# I've been trying to change this for years and I can't

## What Someone Says

The presenting form is the report of a long-running, unsuccessful change effort.

- "I've been trying to stop doing this for a decade."
- "I've been trying to change how I eat for years."
- "I've been trying to be more proactive / more forthright / more embodied / nicer / more disciplined."
- "I keep telling myself I should and I never do."
- "Why can't I change?"
- "I want to change in this way and I can't."
- "I've tried everything and nothing has worked."

The hallmark is the time scale (years, a decade) and the loop signature: tries, fails, beats self up, tries again, fails again. The person is not asking how to start; they are asking why the trying has not produced movement.

## Detection Cues

- Time-scale markers: "for years," "for a decade," "since I was twenty," "my whole adult life."
- The behavior is named alongside the duration of the attempt to change it ("I've been trying to X for Y years").
- A loop signature: try → fail → self-criticism → try again. The person can describe the loop.
- The list-format presentation: a person enumerating several things they have been trying to change for a long time. Diet, exercise, who they hang out with, how nice they are, being more embodied, being more forthright.
- "I should" language attached to each item. The change is framed as an obligation the person is failing at.
- The person reports feeling stress when they say the should aloud. Body contraction is observable in the moment of saying it.
- A history of strategies that did not stick: app blockers, accountability partners, programs, courses, books, therapy. The person has invested effort and not seen the change.
- The change item has a moral or identity component ("I should be a better person who does X").

## What Joe Hears

The structural cause is predictable without knowing what the specific change is. The thing has not changed because there is heavy shame holding it in place. The person has been beating themselves up about not doing it. They have been telling themselves they should do it, criticizing themselves for not doing it. The shame is what is keeping them from doing it. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

The exact diagnostic Joe offers in this teaching: "There's something that you've been trying to do for years that you have not done, and I can tell you why you haven't done it without even knowing what it is. You have been beating yourself up for not doing it." -- Joe Hudson, What is Holding You Back?

The loop itself is the engine. The person is not failing to try; the person is trying inside a structure where each new attempt comes from the same shame as the last one, which keeps the loop closed. Each iteration of "I should, I didn't, I'm bad, I should" reinforces the lock. See [[shame-stagnates-emotion]] for the mechanism that makes shame freeze movement, and [[should-as-threat]] for why the should language itself is what produces the rebellion or submission that keeps the cycle running.

The diagnostic move flips the conventional self-improvement frame: the harder the pattern has been to change, the more likely a should is the problem, and adding more should-energy makes it worse. The conventional logic ("I just need more discipline / a better plan / harder accountability") is the trap. See [[joes-26-year-old-list]] for the empirical version of this diagnostic.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same presenting form points different directions depending on what is underneath.

- **If the person can list multiple things they have been trying to change for years:** the work is to surface that every item on the list has a heavy should attached. The list itself becomes the diagnostic. See [[list-things-trying-to-change-for-4-years]] for the writing exercise that produces this.

- **If the person is identifying themselves with the failure ("I'm the kind of person who never finishes things"):** the work is at [[inherent-goodness]]. The identity is the should's residue. The behavior is downstream.

- **If the person is bracing for "the technique that will finally work":** the move is to refuse the search for a better should. The work is not to find a better-engineered version of the same trying. The work is to drop the shame the trying is being run from. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] and the reframe move at [[reframe-the-question-to-the-blocker-emotion]].

- **If the person is afraid that without the shoulds they will collapse:** this is the [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] concern. The fear of laziness is the belief the should is defending.

- **If the person has already done significant work on this in therapy or coaching:** the work is to refuse the credential as evidence and look at what the work has produced. See the mental-health-professional variant of [[i-keep-running-into-the-same-wall]].

- **If the surface item is interpersonal (a relationship pattern, a way of treating someone):** the rumination itself may be producing the behavior. See [[rumination-creates-what-it-ruminates-about]].

- **If the change item is an addiction or compulsive behavior:** the shame is the lock and the substance/behavior is providing relief from the shame. See [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]] and [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]].

## Coaching Path

1. **Refuse the surface frame.** The person is asking "how do I finally change this?" Working on that question on its own terms reinforces the loop. The actual question is different.

2. **Name the loop.** "You try, you don't do it, you beat yourself up, you try again." The naming makes visible what has been invisible because it has been continuous. See [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]].

3. **Produce the diagnostic.** If the person has been trying for a long time, the structural cause is the shame holding it in place. The Adyashanti compression: "Shame is the locks that hold the chains of bad habits in place." See [[shame-drives-behavior]].

4. **Run the list exercise if appropriate.** Have the person write everything they have been trying to change for at least four years, then check off the ones with shame or obligation attached. The pattern usually becomes self-evident: every item on the list has a should sitting on top of it. See [[list-things-trying-to-change-for-4-years]].

5. **Locate the underlying emotion.** Shame is the lock; underneath the lock is the unfelt emotion. The behavior cannot shift while the emotional material under the lock is frozen. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[emotional-inquiry]].

6. **Install the in-the-moment practice.** The first step is just noticing every place the self-shaming is happening and bringing gentleness in at that moment. See [[notice-where-you-shame-yourself-be-gentle]].

7. **Hold the expectation honestly.** The behavior change is downstream of the shame work, not the direct target of it. Promising specific behavior change collapses the work back into the should frame.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- Does not offer a better technique for the change effort. The technique frame is the trap.
- Does not validate the should. "Yes, you really should be exercising more" feeds the lock.
- Does not promise that working on the shame will produce a specific behavior outcome on a specific timeline. The change happens but cannot be scheduled.
- Does not let the work itself become another should. "I should be doing the shame work" is the same engine in a new disguise. See [[gentleness-not-trying-to-be-patient]].
- Does not skip the loop-naming step. The person needs to see the loop they are inside before any work on the loop can land.
