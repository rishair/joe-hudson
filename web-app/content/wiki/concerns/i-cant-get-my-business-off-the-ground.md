# I can't get my business off the ground

## What Someone Says

- "How do I trust myself again, because I feel like I've done it over and over again, that I don't trust myself to do it."
- "I can succeed in other people's businesses, but I don't believe in myself to succeed."
- "I've had like 9 to 10 failed businesses."
- "I would hit a plateau with the main one I started."
- "I was never able to build it past a certain point."
- "I'd hit a plateau."
- "I do hundreds of outreaches a day."
- "They'd come in and they'd go out."
- "I'm not building the clientele to the level that I would want it to."
- "My dad multiple times tried to be entrepreneurial and it didn't work for him."

The shape of the presenting concern is bounded: the person is competent and can execute in other people's businesses (often as an employee, contractor, or in someone else's company). They have repeatedly attempted to launch their own business and have failed to scale past a plateau. The framing arrives as a self-trust problem ("how do I trust myself") rather than as a business-strategy problem. There is usually a parent — often a father — with the same pattern in their history.

## Detection Cues

- The asymmetry: succeeds in other people's businesses, fails in their own
- A specific number of failures named ("9-10 attempts") and a specific failure pattern ("they came and they went," "I hit a plateau")
- High activity / low conversion (hundreds of outreaches per day, low or zero retention)
- The framing as a *trust* problem rather than a tactical problem
- A parent in the history with the same pattern, often named without prompting
- The person can describe the tactical mechanics of their business in detail
- The person cannot describe what it feels like to be with their clients
- Words like "intense," "pushing," "too much" used about their own client engagement style
- The conversation itself displays the pattern — the person tries to push themselves to the right answer rather than connect with what's happening

A near-pathognomonic signal: the person describes pushing on the surface mechanics of the business (more outreaches, more hours, more effort) while describing their actual client interactions as ones where they could not "understand where they were fully."

## What Joe Hears

The presenting concern is "I can't trust myself in my own business." The underneath structure is [[proving-instead-of-connecting]]: the person is running a proving-engine in every customer interaction, and proving pushes clients away. The "I succeed for others" piece is consistent with this — when the person is in someone else's business, the proving-engine is pointed at the boss or the team, not at the clients. The clients receive someone who can actually do the work. When the same person runs their own business, the proving has nowhere else to go. It lands on the clients. The clients leave.

The self-trust framing is a substitute for the structural read. The person believes the problem is internal ("I don't trust myself"). The problem is operational ("I push my clients away by trying to prove myself to them"). The internal frame produces no traction because the work the person actually needs to do is at the level of how they show up in front of clients, not at the level of self-belief work that would precede it.

Underneath the proving-engine is [[value-is-intrinsic-not-earned]]: the proving is being run because the person believes their value has to be earned through demonstration. The demonstration is the proving. The demonstration is what makes them un-trustable as a coach and unable to retain clients.

The inherited piece is often present and important. The father (or other primary modeling figure) often ran the same pattern. The person is not just running their own proving-engine; they are running an inherited proving-engine. The same pushing produced the same business failures one generation earlier. See [[inner-work-as-generational-repair]] for the broader frame.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The branching: which form of the proving engine is operating determines the path Joe takes.

- **If the person is [[proving-instead-of-connecting]] in the room right now:** the read is live. The person is trying to push themselves to the right answer in the session, in the same way they push clients to the close. Joe will use the live demonstration to make the pattern visible. The bridge to their business is built from what just happened between Joe and them.

- **If [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]]:** the person reports they are working on it and the trying itself is the doing of the pattern. Joe will not let the trying continue as a coaching maneuver. He will name the trying and ask what would happen if it stopped.

- **If the person has a domain of mastery (often the activity they sell — fitness training, music lessons, consulting):** Joe will use [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] to show that the pattern they understand in their craft (in their body vs. in their head) is the same pattern operating in their sales. The fitness coach who knows clients get hurt when they get in their head is the fitness coach who is getting hurt in his sales because he is in his head.

- **If a parent with the same pattern surfaces:** Joe will use the [[reframe-the-parents-life-with-the-corrective]] move. "Imagine your dad doing exactly his work but making connection the primary initiative. Where would your dad be today?" The answer the client gives about the parent's life is the answer about their own life. The reframe is loaded with intergenerational permission.

- **If the person is describing the failure as evidence of personal inadequacy:** the read shifts toward [[im-a-failure]] and the verdict-construction work. The 9-10 failures have been organized into an identity-level claim about the self. The claim has to be unwound before the operational work can be received.

## Coaching Path

1. **Receive the framing without taking it at face value.** The person presents a self-trust problem. The first move is to ask about the events. How many failures, what were the businesses, what happened. The investigation produces material the operational read can be built from.

2. **Find the asymmetry.** The person succeeds for others and fails for themselves. The asymmetry is the diagnostic. Same person, same skills, two different outcomes. The variable is not the skill. The variable is what they are doing in the customer relationship.

3. **Surface the operational pattern.** "How did you push them away?" Often the person names it themselves: "By being too intense." The "too intense" is the proving showing up at the client. Mirror back the pattern in the operational language: "You were proving yourself instead of connecting with them."

4. **Find the pattern in the room.** "Are you doing that with yourself right now in this session? Are you connecting with yourself or are you trying to push yourself to an answer?" The live demonstration makes the abstract diagnosis concrete. The person sees themselves doing it.

5. **Use their domain to teach the principle.** If they coach fitness, training, music, surgery — any domain where being in the body is the difference between performing and getting hurt — use that. "What happens when someone is training and they get in their head? They get hurt. How is that any different than what just happened to you?" The teaching is delivered in the language of their existing expertise.

6. **Make them demonstrate the new mode in real time.** Use [[be-my-potential-client-show-me]]: "I'm your potential client. Show me." The person is asked to do the new thing right now, in the conversation. The success or struggle of the demonstration is the data. When they do it, Joe names it. When they fall back into pushing, Joe names that too.

7. **Bring in the parent pattern.** If a parent ran the same engine, ask the alternate-history question: "Imagine your dad doing exactly his work but making connection the primary initiative. Where would your dad be today?" The answer the person gives is the answer for themselves. Permission is generational; the work is generational.

8. **Compress to the principle.** [[proving-yourself-is-agreeing-youre-not-valuable]]. "If you're trying to prove yourself, you're agreeing to the fact that you're not valuable. And then other people agree with you." The principle names what the proving-engine has been costing the person at the level of value, not just the level of business outcome.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not coach the business mechanics. The number of outreaches is not the problem. The conversion ratio is not the problem. The mechanics are operating as well as they can given the upstream pattern. Coaching the mechanics is coaching the symptom.
- He does not affirm the self-trust framing. "Yes, you should trust yourself more" is the wrong move. The self-trust frame produces no traction. The work is at the operational level.
- He does not validate the failure-verdict. The 9-10 failures are real events. They are not evidence the person is unfit for entrepreneurship. They are evidence the proving-engine has been running for 9-10 iterations.
- He does not let the person push themselves toward the answer in the session. Pushing in the session IS the pattern. Allowing it would be allowing the pattern to operate unexamined. Joe interrupts each push and names it.
- He does not pathologize the inherited piece. The parent is not the villain. The parent ran the same engine. The person is not condemned by the inheritance; they are positioned to interrupt it.
