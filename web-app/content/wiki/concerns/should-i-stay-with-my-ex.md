# Should I stay with my ex? / Should I stay in this relationship?

## What Someone Says

- "My husband broke up with me a couple of months ago and we're in a trial separation. I would love some help in figuring out whether I want to stay with this man or not."
- "Should I stay with my ex?"
- "We're in a trial separation. The point of the trial is to figure out whether we should stay together."
- "I love him but I don't know if I should be with him."
- "There's a lot of love between us but there was also so much heaviness."
- "He's willing to do the work and so am I, but I don't know if that's enough."
- "I felt more alive in those 3 weeks breaking up with him and crying on the couch with him than I had in the last 3 years of our relationship."
- "How do I still love him and be joyful? Can I do that?"
- "Help me decide."

The presentation is almost always a request for help deciding. The person rarely opens with the underlying dynamic. They open with the fork: stay or leave. They want the coach to be the thoughtful adjudicator who walks them through it.

## Detection Cues

The clearest signal: a relationship that has gone heavy, an explicit ask for the coach to help decide whether to stay, and language like "trial separation," "I don't know if I want to," "help me figure out whether."

Surface markers:
- Mentions of a trial separation, recent breakup, or unresolved limbo.
- The framing puts the question as "do I want to be with this person?" rather than "what's happening in this relationship?"
- The person reports a stark contrast between the relationship as it was (heavy, depressing) and recent moments (more alive when breaking up than during the marriage).
- Both partners described as "willing to do the work" — and the person treating that mutual willingness as if it should be the answer.
- The other person is described in terms of their state: depression, anger, shame. The speaker's role is described as managing that state.

Indirect markers:
- The person is sitting in the question and has been for weeks or months.
- The deliberation has not converged. Real decisions converge.
- The body has gone flat in the relationship and is more alive away from it — and the person is treating this as a problem to interpret rather than as data.
- Reports of sex going dead, of feeling caged, of the partner having "left" emotionally even before any physical separation.

This concern often arrives stacked with [[i-caretake-people]] — and the caretaking is almost always the active mechanism producing the deadness. Frequently also with [[we-have-built-up-resentment]] and [[i-should-be-doing-this]].

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the stay-or-leave question is rarely a real decision waiting to be made. The work is upstream of the decision.

Two structural patterns Joe typically hears:

1. **The deadness in the relationship is the caretaking, not the partner.** The person has been managing the partner's state — depression, anger, shame, mental health — and the management is what drained the love. They are asking whether to stay with the person, but the actual question is whether they can stop managing him. The relationship may be saveable; the caretaking is not.

2. **The decision is being asked to do what self-love would do for free.** When someone loves themselves, the stay-or-leave question loses its weight. The chips fall where they fall. The decision gets made by itself. See [[self-love-makes-the-decision]]. When the person is sitting in the question, the question itself is the symptom: they are trying to think their way to a conclusion that only the felt sense of self-love can produce.

The reframe Joe makes explicit: "You're trying to manage him into happiness so you can get what?" The answer is almost always: *to get back the person I fell in love with, so I can feel love again.* The caretaking is a strategy to recover the felt love. The strategy fails because [[responsibility-stops-love]]: every act of management is a moment of stopped loving.

The grief usually present underneath: the years already spent in the trade — accepting responsibility for the partner instead of loving them — and not realizing the trade was being made.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Same presentation, different directions depending on what shows up:

- **If the person is asking the coach to help decide:** see [[decision-question-covers-the-caretaking]] and [[treating-it-as-a-decision-when-its-an-emotion]]. Joe refuses to adjudicate. He goes underneath the decision to what self-love would produce, often via a hypothetical: "If you were in a room together for a month and your only job was not to take care of him, what would have to change?" See [[what-would-it-take-for-you-to-not-take-care-of-them]].

- **If the person describes feeling more alive during the breakup than during the relationship:** Joe takes this as the body's already-arrived answer about the caretaking dynamic. He does not treat it as a paradox to solve. The aliveness is data: the caretaking was the cage. He may name what was being held back (joy, silliness, sexual aliveness) and ask what made the relationship the place it could not show up.

- **If the person describes the partner's depression, anger, or shame:** Joe hears the management of those states as the structure of the relationship, not as the problem to solve. He will often pivot to "What would it take for you to be with him and not take care of him?" — making the caretaking, not the partner's state, the variable.

- **If the person says they want to fall back in love:** Joe hears this as a [[surrogate-want]]. The actual want underneath is to feel love. The strategy ("save him so I can fall in love again") is the trade that is killing the love. See [[i-cant-find-love]] for a related framing.

- **If the person is in their head deliberating:** Joe shifts the conversation into the body. "Put your hand on your heart. Love yourself unconditionally for a moment. Now answer the question: does it matter? How much does it matter if you stay with him or not?" See [[move-from-deliberation-to-the-body]].

- **If the person says they cannot do self-love:** Joe doesn't accept that as the answer. "Give me a good attempt." See [[give-me-a-good-attempt]]. The "I can't" is treated as resistance, not as data.

## Coaching Path

The stay-or-leave question is almost never the unit of work. The path:

1. **Refuse the decision-frame.** The person is asking for adjudication. Don't adjudicate. See [[redirect-from-stay-or-leave-to-self-love]].

2. **Surface the caretaking.** Most stay-or-leave questions in long relationships are sitting on top of caretaking. Ask what they would have to face if they stopped taking care of the partner. The answer usually surfaces self-abandonment: "If I were taking care of him, I wouldn't be taking care of myself." See [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]].

3. **Name the strategy underneath.** "You're trying to manage him into happiness so you can get what?" The almost-universal answer: to fall back in love. The realization that the management is the love-killer is the recognition Joe is going for. See [[responsibility-stops-love]] and [[obligation-cuts-off-love]].

4. **Pivot to self-love.** Make the work self-love, not the decision. "All you have to do is love yourself and then the decision gets made." See [[self-love-makes-the-decision]]. The hypothetical: feel unconditional love for yourself for a moment. Now does it matter whether you stay or leave? Usually: it doesn't.

5. **Name what the caretaking has been killing.** The joyful self, the silly self, the sexual self. "Nobody wants to have sex with a kid either, just by the way." See [[nobody-wants-to-have-sex-with-a-kid]]. The caretaking has made the partner into a child to be managed and the speaker into the parent. Sex dies in that configuration. The configuration is what needs to change, not the relationship.

6. **Give the take-home assignment.** "Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall." The work is internal; the relationship outcome is downstream. The person does not need to know whether to stay. They need to know how to love themselves while present with him as an adult.

## What Joe Does Not Do

He does not weigh the pros and cons of staying versus leaving. The deliberation is the avoidance. Playing the role of thoughtful adjudicator reinforces the avoidance.

He does not validate the trial separation as the path to clarity. The trial is often itself the deferral. "How much of the trial is his just giving you an excuse, like just because he doesn't want to deal with the consequences of saying I want a divorce?" The question opens the question of whether the trial is real work or kicked-can.

He does not tell the person whether to stay. The work is not the decision. The work is what produces clean decisions: self-love, an unbraced body, an unconditional present moment. If those are in place, the decision gets made on its own. If they are not, no amount of deliberation will produce a clean answer.

He does not accept "I don't know how to love myself" as the stopping point. The "I don't know" gets pushed through. "Give me a good attempt." The silliness or self-consciousness that arises ("It feels very silly. I'm like, oh my God.") is named as the held-back thing.

He does not let the partner's depression or anger or shame become the explanation. The partner's state is the partner's. The caretaker's caretaking is the work. He may say something like "He's his own man. Clearly. You're not responsible. But if you were, what would you have to face?" — the hypothetical keeps the focus on the speaker's mechanism.
