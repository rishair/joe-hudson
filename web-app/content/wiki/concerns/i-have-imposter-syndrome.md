# I have imposter syndrome / I feel like an imposter

## What Someone Says

- "I have imposter syndrome"
- "I feel like a fraud"
- "I don't know what I'm doing"
- "Everyone else seems to know what they're doing"
- "I'm going to be found out"
- "I shouldn't be here"
- "I just got lucky"
- "I'm faking it until I make it but it's exhausting"
- "How do I get rid of my imposter syndrome?"
- "I keep trying to convince myself I'm not an imposter and it doesn't work"

## Detection Cues

The presenting form is usually a request for reassurance or a strategy. "I keep telling myself I'm qualified but the feeling won't go away." "I have all the credentials but I still feel like a fraud." The person has typically been trying to *convince themselves out of it* using the evidence -- and noticing that the evidence does not actually support the convincing.

Indirect signals: speaking about a role or accomplishment with hedging language ("I sort of run a company"), preemptively pointing out their gaps before anyone else can, attributing success to luck or to other people, anxiety spikes around being observed by people they perceive as the "real" experts.

A specific behavioral signature: the person *avoids the people who could most help them* because admitting what they don't know would expose them. They route around the experts, organize their work to hide gaps, lean away from collaboration where their not-knowing would be visible. The hiding behavior is the tell -- and the hiding itself manufactures more of the imposter feeling, because the more you fake it, the more of an imposter you are.

A specific verbal signature: the person is in a loop trying to argue themselves out of the feeling and is losing the argument. They are reaching for proof that they are not an imposter, and the proof is not arriving.

The concern only attaches to specific kinds of roles. The person reports imposter syndrome about their job, their leadership role, their public position -- but never about being a parent, a friend, a partner. See [[imposter-syndrome-only-attaches-to-performance-identities]]. If the person reports imposter syndrome in domains that look non-performance, look closer -- something about that domain has become performance-graded for them.

## What Joe Hears

The person is trying to win an argument they cannot win, because the underlying claim is true: they do not know what they are doing. Anybody doing anything real does not fully know what they are doing -- that is what makes it real. See [[everyone-doing-something-real-is-an-imposter]].

"If you think you're an imposter, it's because you are, right? None of -- nobody who's doing anything important in the world knows how to do it. They're figuring it out as they go." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

The fix is not to win the argument but to dissolve the frame. The frame says "some people know what they're doing and I am a defective version because I don't." The dissolving move is to ask who in fact does know what they're doing -- and to discover that no one does, and that the discomfort is universal.

Underneath the imposter feeling is a specific kind of fear: the fear of stepping into a bigger room. That fear is the body's signal that something new is being attempted. It is the same energy as excitement, with the breath constricted. See [[fear-vs-excitement]].

There is also a second underneath: the imposter feeling is correct, but in a different way than the person thinks. The person thinks they are an imposter because they are not yet competent. The actual reason they are an imposter is that they are trying to be someone other than themselves. The performance of competence is the imposter act. The vulnerability of admitting what they don't know is the move out of imposter status. See [[trying-to-be-someone-other-than-yourself-is-the-actual-imposter-act]].

The hiding-from-experts behavior is the operational proof of this. By hiding what they don't know, the person becomes more of an imposter, not less. The avoidance of the people who could help is the imposter syndrome generating itself: "the more you're hiding yourself, the more you're faking it, the more of an imposter you feel like you are." See [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]] and [[maintaining-false-persona-costs-more-than-incompetence]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person is in their head trying to win the argument with themselves: Joe goes to [[who-isnt-an-imposter]] -- the diagnostic question that dissolves the frame rather than settling the argument.
- If the person is gripping for someone-who-knows: the underlying want is for someone to tell them they are right, or for some form of perfect to exist that they can reach. Joe names that no such thing exists and lets the ground fall out. See [[perceived-certainty-not-actual-certainty]] and the connection to [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]].
- If the person is treating the feeling as a problem to make go away: the issue is the strategy of management, not the feeling. Joe moves to the felt sense: the imposter feeling is fear of a bigger room, which is the body activating, which is excitement without the breath. The practice [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] is for this.
- If the person is using imposter syndrome to avoid action: Joe may also point at what the imposter feeling is protecting them from feeling. See [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]].
- If the person is collapsed into shame about being an imposter (rather than just naming the experience): the shame is the operating problem, not the imposter feeling. Treat the shame first. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].
- If the person is describing hiding from experts, avoiding asking for help, organizing work to hide gaps: the behavior is generating more imposter feeling. The move is the opposite -- ask for all the help they can get. Vulnerability about not-knowing is the exit. See [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]] and [[ask-for-all-the-help-you-can-get]].
- If the person is in I-I-I-I doubt loops: the doubt is structurally about self-focus, which is structurally close to arrogance. The move is into open-hearted service. See [[doubt-is-self-focus-arrogance-is-self-focus]] and [[open-hearted-service-cures-imposter-syndrome]].
- If the person frames the imposter feeling as a sign they should withdraw or not try: the imposter feeling is actually the scent of where the work is. Stay in. Follow the scent. See [[imposter-syndrome-is-a-scent-you-can-follow-home]].
- If the person seems to think the cure is to "build confidence" by accumulating skill: confidence is not a skill to build. Confidence is the felt-sense of being connected to yourself while doing what you're doing. See [[confidence-is-connection-to-self-while-acting]] and [[confidence-is-running-from-doubt]].

## Coaching Path

The path has two prongs that often run in parallel: the cognitive dissolution of the frame, and the relational/somatic move into vulnerability and open-hearted service. Different clients respond to different entry points.

**Prong 1: dissolve the frame.**
1. Ask the dissolving question: [[who-isnt-an-imposter]]. Let the person try to name one. Watch what happens when they realize the category is empty.
2. State the principle directly: anyone doing anything of consequence does not know what they are doing. An [[expert-is-imposter-with-most-reps]]. The kindergarten frame: it does not bother you that you are a kindergartner if everyone is a kindergartner.
3. Address the desire for someone to tell them they are right. Name that there is no perfect company, no perfect book, no right way. The ground falls out. Stay with that for a beat.
4. Bring in [[only-your-own-judgment-matters]] -- the only judgment that ends up mattering is your own judgment of how you lived.

**Prong 2: redirect from self-focus to open-hearted service.**
1. Name the structure of doubt: doubt is I-I-I-I, self-focus, structurally a form of arrogance. See [[doubt-is-self-focus-arrogance-is-self-focus]].
2. Offer the central question: "How do I do this with an open heart?" The question cannot coexist with imposter syndrome because it relocates attention from self-evaluation to service. See [[how-do-i-do-this-with-an-open-heart]].
3. Distinguish from codependent service ("I have to be valuable to be loved"). The service has to come from empowerment, not from earning love. See [[open-hearted-service-vs-codependent-service]].

**Prong 3: address the hiding behavior directly.**
1. Name that hiding from experts generates more imposter feeling, not less. See [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]].
2. The opposite move: admit what you don't know and ask for all the help you can get. See [[ask-for-all-the-help-you-can-get]].

**Prong 4: address the projection.**
1. If you are an imposter, you are also saying that the person who hired you doesn't know what they're doing. And whoever hired *them* doesn't know what they're doing. See [[imposter-syndrome-projects-incompetence-onto-the-people-who-hired-you]] and [[name-the-projection-in-imposter-syndrome]].

**Prong 5: address the source.**
1. The imposter feeling was installed by conditional validation in childhood -- the "good jobbed" pattern. See [[good-jobbed-as-conditional-validation]].
2. The reward the person was trying to earn through performance is the same reward that becomes available when they admit they don't know everything. The imposter syndrome is a scent that points home. See [[imposter-syndrome-is-a-scent-you-can-follow-home]].

**Prong 6: body and reframe.**
1. Move to the body: the imposter feeling is fear, and that fear is excitement without the breath. Offer [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] as the in-the-moment intervention. Offer [[list-people-you-admire-and-their-mistakes]] as the take-home experiment.
2. Reframe confidence: confidence is not a skill to build but the felt-sense of being connected to yourself while doing what you're doing. See [[confidence-is-connection-to-self-while-acting]].
