# I keep postponing enjoyment / I want to enjoy life now, not in some imagined future

## What Someone Says

This concern arrives with a specific structure: the person knows they want enjoyment, knows it is not arriving, and locates the failure in something about how they relate to time. The future has eaten the present.

- "I want to stop postponing enjoyment into an abstract imaginary future."
- "Tomorrow I'm going to do this, and then I kind of rush through the day to get there."
- "I'll enjoy life when my pains are gone / when this project ends / when I have more money."
- "I keep getting stuck thinking about the next thing."
- "I feel like I oscillate in and out of enjoying myself. I get glimpses of it."
- "I'm running away from what is going on right now even more than I'm chasing what's coming."
- "I can have it for a moment, then I lose it."

Distinct from [[ive-lost-my-joy]]: this person *can* enjoy. They are not flat. They report glimpses, oscillation, brief landings. The complaint is about not staying. They keep leaving the present for the future.

## Detection Cues

- Time-language saturated with future orientation: "when," "then," "after," "tomorrow," "I'll get to."
- The person describes a particular target on the horizon (the pain ending, the project finishing, the money arriving) that will release them to enjoy.
- The body has a rushing signature: see [[the-rush-signature]]. Speech is fast. The person is leaning forward energetically even when sitting still.
- The person can name an experience as enjoyable in the abstract while not actually being in it in the moment.
- A specific tell: when asked "how is that not happening right now?" the person looks confused. The question lands as a category error to them. They have been operating on the premise that enjoyment is a future event.
- The person who reports "I keep getting glimpses" — the glimpses are real, but the framing of glimpse-vs-stable presupposes that what they want is a future stable state rather than this current glimpse repeated.

## What Joe Hears

The structure underneath is [[no-finish-line]] in its present-tense form. The person has installed a finish line called "the moment I can finally enjoy." The finish line is what is keeping the present from being the moment they can enjoy. They are reaching for a future event that, by the structure of the reaching, can never arrive.

This is also [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]] applied to enjoyment: the very act of craving future enjoyment pushes present enjoyment out of reach. The person is trying so hard to get to enjoyment that they cannot land in the enjoyment that is here.

Two things often sit underneath the postponement:
- An emotion the person does not want to be with right now (chronic pain, stress, shame, anxiety). Postponing enjoyment is partly an attempt to postpone *being-with* the present, because the present contains something difficult. See [[trying-to-get-rid-of-the-emotion]].
- A "pulling the brakes" mechanism: the person stops themselves at the threshold of enjoyment. See [[pulling-the-brakes]]. The fear is often of being judged, of being seen wanting, of being too much, of being a burden.

The postponement is not the problem. The postponement is the symptom. The problem is whatever the person is doing to leave the present moment.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

Two people can say "I keep postponing enjoyment" and the coaching path diverges based on what is also present.

- **If chronic pain or chronic stress is present:** the postponement is often an attempted escape from the pain. Joe does not chase the postponement. He moves to the pain itself and asks how the person enjoys *that*. The path runs through what the person is trying to outrun. See [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]].
- **If the person is [[pulling-the-brakes]]:** the postponement is downstream of the braking. The person is stopping themselves before they can land. Joe goes to the brake. What does pulling the brakes look like in the body? When did pulling the brakes get installed? What does the brake protect against?
- **If the person rehearsed for the session ([[rehearsing-how-to-say-it]]):** they will often present neatly framed concerns ("I have two things and they might be the same thing"). Joe notes the rehearsal and uses it: their preparation didn't happen, and here they are, doing the thing they were preparing for. The session itself becomes evidence against the structure they came in with.
- **If shame is present (the "I shouldn't be like this" / "I'm an inconvenience" form):** the postponement is partly an attempt to outrun the shame about whatever is currently true. The work is to feel the shame rather than to fix the underlying condition. See [[shame-as-disconnection]].
- **If the person reports glimpses and oscillation:** Joe will not try to extend the duration. He will ask whether they can enjoy *the oscillation itself*. The framing of "I should be in enjoyment more consistently" is itself a finish line. See [[enjoyment-as-path]] in its iterated form.

## Coaching Path

1. Make the present moment the field. Ask immediately: "how about right now?" / "how do you enjoy yourself right now?" See [[how-is-that-not-here-right-now]]. The question converts the abstract concern into a live experiment.

2. When the person reports the actual thing they are doing instead of enjoying (rushing, running away, fixing, planning the next intervention), ask whether that thing itself can be enjoyed. "Is there anything about rushing through your day that can't be enjoyed?" This is [[enjoyment-as-path]] at its most direct: enjoyment of *whatever is here*, not selection of enjoyable things.

3. Identify the underlying mechanism — the brake, the running-away, the not-being-with. Often this is named by the person in their own metaphor. Stay with their metaphor. See [[pulling-the-brakes]] for the most common version.

4. If pain, shame, sadness, stress, or any difficult feeling is in the present, do not skip it to get to enjoyment. Apply enjoyment to it. "How would you enjoy your shame?" "How would you enjoy this stress?" See [[how-would-you-enjoy-x]]. This is the move people most often resist and most often discover changes everything.

5. If they ask the inverse — "how do I not enjoy X right now?" — listen carefully. The answer names the brake exactly. See [[whats-stopping-you-from-enjoying-x]].

6. Watch for the structure to repeat at each layer. The person who learns to enjoy shame may then postpone enjoyment of stress. The postponement is the pattern, not the topic. Each iteration is the same work.

7. The breakthrough often arrives not as "I am enjoying now" but as a movement *through* the difficult feeling into enjoyment without trying. The "victim" feeling, fully felt, becomes empowerment. The pain, fully met, sits inside spaciousness. See [[running-toward-yourself]].

## When This Is Not the Pattern

- A person who reports no glimpses of enjoyment at all has [[ive-lost-my-joy]], a different concern with a different path.
- A person who postpones decisions and tasks (not enjoyment specifically) is in a different concern. The postponement-of-enjoyment concern has a specific emotional shape: the person knows enjoyment is what they want, knows they keep deferring it, and notices the deferral as a felt structure.
- A person in acute crisis (active grief, recent trauma, depression) may not be postponing enjoyment so much as not yet able to access it. Different work.

## Tone of the Response

Light. Curious. Slightly amused. The reframe Joe brings to this concern is structural: "you're telling me you want to enjoy and you say you're enjoying right now and your head is confused." Naming the contradiction is most of the move. The contradiction tends to dissolve when held up to the light, not when wrestled with.
