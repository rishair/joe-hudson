# My partner is going through something and I don't know what

## What Someone Says

The partner of someone in an awakening process often arrives more disturbed than the person going through it. The presenting language is fear, sometimes wrapped in concern, sometimes wrapped in advice-seeking, sometimes wrapped in irritation:

- "My partner isn't the same person anymore."
- "Something happened and now they're different. I don't know what to do."
- "I think we need to see a psychologist."
- "I want a diagnosis. I want to know what this is."
- "I keep telling them this isn't normal and they need to get help."
- "They had this weird experience and now they keep talking about how nothing is real."
- "They're suddenly so emotional. Or suddenly so flat. Or suddenly so distant."
- "We've been together for years and I don't recognize them."
- "I'm scared. I'm feeling scared all the time and I don't know why."
- "I'm trying to get them back to who they were."

Sometimes the concern is dressed as advice for the partner. Sometimes it is dressed as worry about the relationship. Sometimes it is dressed as a request for professional help. The underlying material is consistent: the person speaking has lost something about how they knew their partner, and they want it back.

## Detection Cues

The AI coach must catch this from the partner's side -- the person speaking is not the one going through the awakening; they are the witness who needs support of a different kind. Detection cues:

- The person describes recent changes in their partner that don't match prior baseline.
- The partner's changes track the symptom set in [[something-weird-is-happening-to-me]] -- perceptual shifts, emotional shifts, behavioral changes, sudden disinterest in things that used to engage them.
- The speaker is requesting diagnosis, professional intervention, or some way to restore the prior state.
- The speaker reports feeling scared, ungrounded, or like they have lost their footing in the relationship.
- The speaker has been a caretaker or rescuer in the relationship, and the role is no longer being received.
- The speaker reports increased irritation or distance toward their partner that they cannot fully account for.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is the structural reality that when one person in a close relationship has an identity shift, the other person's identity has to shift too -- because their identity was partly constituted by the configuration of the relationship.

"If I'm in a close relationship with you and your identity changes, it means my identity has to change a little bit, right? Or a lot. So if our relationship was, I took care of you all the time, and all of a sudden you realize that you don't have any problems, that like the whole idea of a problem has disintegrated in your mind, that's just another thought that you can't believe, and that your essence has never changed, and so what's the problem? And any attack that I give you is my projection onto you. It's not personal. Then what do you have to defend? But I'm used to being the person who's like slightly above you and taking care of you and making sure that you're okay and emotionally being responsible for you. What the hell happens to me? Well, how am I useful?" -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The push to diagnose, the push to get the partner back to who they were, the push to get them to a psychologist -- these are usually the speaker's nervous system trying to restore an old configuration that worked for them. Not because they are bad, but because the loss is real. They have lost a role and they have not yet found the new one.

The other dynamic Joe names: the speaker may be feeling the partner's fear. If the person going through the awakening is in the "what the fuck is happening to me" version of the experience, there is fear in their body. A sensitive partner picks it up. They then have to manage both the partner's fear and their own response to it without a frame for what is happening.

There is also a third dynamic: the spaciousness the partner has dropped into can feel to the speaker like abandonment. "Hey, why are you leaving me? Hey, I'm right here, what are you doing? Like, get back to me."

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If the speaker is in caretaker grief:** their role in the relationship was to take care of the partner's problems, and the problems have stopped requiring caretaking. The grief is real. Underneath is the question: who am I if I am not the one taking care of them? The coaching path is to honor the grief and then look at what other configurations might be possible. Often the caretaker dynamic was load-bearing for the relationship in ways neither person fully wanted.

- **If the speaker is in fear:** name that they are likely feeling the partner's fear in addition to their own. Naming this often allows both to be felt separately. The speaker can then address their own fear (about the unknown, about the partner being lost to them, about the relationship changing) without it being contaminated by the borrowed fear from the partner.

- **If the speaker is pushing for diagnosis:** the diagnosis-seeking is usually the system trying to make the unfamiliar familiar. The coaching path is not to refuse the question of diagnosis -- it is to name what is actually happening (an awakening process) and provide enough framework that the diagnosis-seeking can quiet. See [[normalize-the-experience-its-happened-to-others]].

- **If the speaker is angry at the partner:** the anger is often grief and disorientation wearing a different costume. The coaching path is to slow down underneath the anger.

- **If the speaker has been the more "awake" or developed partner before this shift:** there can be a specific destabilization in finding that the other person has moved past them in a domain the speaker thought was theirs. Address this directly. Awakening is not a competition. See [[awakening-as-natural-development]].

## Coaching Path

The base arc has several distinct moves that coordinate:

1. **Normalize for the witness too.** "This is something that has happened to many people. It is a recognized phenomenon. Here is what it is." The witness needs the same normalization the person going through it needs.

2. **Validate the grief.** The role they had in the relationship -- caretaker, manager, the-more-stable-one -- is not coming back in the same form. That loss is real. It can be grieved without being fought against.

3. **Locate the witness's own fear separately.** "Some of what you are feeling is yours. Some of it may be what you are picking up from them. Let's separate them." This often gives the speaker air.

4. **Teach the witness stance.** See [[be-with-them-without-trying-to-fix-or-diagnose]]. The single most useful thing the witness can do is be present without needing the partner to be different. Hold them. Don't pretend to know what they're going through. Don't try to fix it. Don't make it your project to diagnose.

   "How can you be there with them in the experience the same way that you would be with an infant who is crying or the same way that you would be with somebody who was scared, like a 3-year-old who was scared. Like, how do you be with them in that way?" -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

5. **Address the speaker's own fear directly.** "Which of course requires having that same stance towards your own fear and your own uncertainty and disappointment or sadness that comes up when this shift occurs in your life." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening. The witness cannot do the witness stance from a contracted place. Their own work is to meet their own discomfort the same way they are being asked to meet their partner's.

6. **Reassure that change is the only constant in this period.** Things are going to shift rapidly. The witness's job is not to predict where the changes go but to stay present through them.

7. **Name the long-term pattern.** "I've never seen anybody have a worse life afterwards." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening. Lives change. Sometimes a lot. The change is not predictable from the beginning. But the system tends toward more alignment, not less. The witness usually does not need to fear the outcome, even if the outcome looks dramatic from where they are sitting.

8. **Acknowledge the privilege.** Being present with someone going through this is an unusual position to be in. It also accelerates the witness's own development.

   "If you can just be with somebody and witness that transformation happening, it is an incredibly privileged place to be. And it also quickens your own experience of it." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

## When the Witness Cannot or Will Not Do This

Joe is realistic: not every partner will be able to do this. It is a lot to ask of someone who has no framework for what is happening. The coaching path in that case is to support the witness in not making the inability into a moral failure. The relationship may not survive the transition, and that is not necessarily a sign that the awakening was wrong or that the witness was wrong. Configurations end.

## What Not To Do

- Do not tell the witness to "just trust the process" without giving them the framework. They have a real loss to navigate. The trust comes from understanding, not from being told to trust.
- Do not collapse the witness's role into "be more supportive." They may already be doing everything they can. The work may be on their own fear, not on giving more to the partner.
- Do not let the witness diagnose the partner with the AI coach's help. If the partner has presented to a coach somewhere, the diagnosis is happening in that space. The witness's role is different.
- Do not assume the witness is wrong to want their old partner back. The wanting is honest. It is also unfulfillable. Both can be true at the same time.
