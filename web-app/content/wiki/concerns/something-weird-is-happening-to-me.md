# Something weird is happening to me

## What Someone Says

The presenting language often does not name awakening at all. People come in describing the symptoms and asking what is wrong. The language is alarm. Some common variants:

- "I walked through the grocery store and didn't know what I wanted anymore."
- "I noticed for a while things have been a little bit different and I just didn't see it."
- "I can't see the world the same way anymore."
- "I feel like I'm being breathed instead of breathing."
- "It feels like I'm walking through the landscape and the landscape is walking through me."
- "My visual field has changed -- things look flat, surreal."
- "It feels like I'm being thrown underwater. I can't sleep. I'm shaking through the night."
- "Life doesn't feel personal anymore. What's wrong with me?"
- "I don't know if my brain is working."
- "How do I get this to stop?"
- "How do I get this to stop persisting?" (the inverse of the seeker)
- "What in the fuck is happening to me right now?"

Some people come in panicked. Others come in detached and ask the question with strange calm. Both are reporting the same underlying movement.

## Detection Cues

The AI coach must catch this concern even when the person does not use the word "awakening" -- because most people in it do not know that is what it is. Detection cues:

- Sudden or recent shift in baseline experience that the person cannot return from.
- Perceptual changes: visual field flattening or becoming surreal, feeling of being breathed, feeling that the boundary between self and environment has dissolved or thinned.
- Loss of usual desire or ability to choose ("I didn't know what I wanted at the grocery store"). See [[the-grocery-store-phase]] in [[integration]].
- The person describes the experience as both ordinary and impossible at the same time -- "it's a nothing burger and it's also everything."
- Repetitive negative self-talk has substantially decreased and the person notices this with surprise rather than relief.
- Sleep disruption, shaking, somatic activation -- if the opening is more energetic/gut-based.
- The person is asking how to stop it, how to fix it, or what is wrong with them.
- The person mentions a recent intensive practice, a near-death event, a depression that lifted, a drug experience, a deep loss, or simply that they have been doing emotional/meditative work for years.
- The person is afraid they are going crazy, but the description matches the religious literature on awakening more than it matches any specific psychiatric category. See [[zen-sickness]].

## What Joe Hears

Underneath this concern is almost always an [[awakening]] event in progress. Joe distinguishes [[fog-walker-vs-belt-over-the-head|two recognition styles]]: the gradual realization that things have already shifted (fog walker), and the sudden disruptive event ("what in the fuck"). Either way, the underlying movement is the same: the bounded sense of self has loosened, perceptually or somatically, and the person is now navigating an experience their previous framework does not contain.

The person almost always treats the experience as a problem. The reading underneath: the experience is not the problem. The relationship to the experience is what determines whether this becomes integration or [[zen-sickness]].

"This experience is what you make out of it, just like any experience, right? Two people are going down the roller coaster and one person's like, 'This is great,' one person says, 'This is horrible,' they're gonna have very different experiences on the roller coaster." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The person is also usually scared. Joe is direct: fear is part of the experience for almost everyone going through it, because the bounded self is dissolving and the system reads dissolution as death.

"When your ego disintegrates or your identity disintegrates, there's a fear that comes with that. Like, what I know to be me is going to die on some level." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The fear can be confused for fear-for-safety. It is not. It is fear-for-self-definition. The body has not registered the difference yet.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

The same concern goes different directions based on what is read about how the person is showing up:

- **If they are in resistance to the experience:** the experience is becoming [[zen-sickness]] -- the same underlying movement met with rejection rather than allowing. The coaching path is to normalize the experience and reduce the resistance, not to do anything about the experience itself.

- **If they are in the rigid/contained presentation:** the person typically holds emotion tightly in the body. The disruption tends to feel like an unwanted flood -- sensations they cannot explain or control. The path: physical grounding, attention to feet, a lot of holding. See [[ground-the-body-during-awakening]].

- **If they are in the hysteric/feeling-everything presentation:** the person already feels the universe constantly, picks up emotional energy from rooms and people. For them the opening overwhelms an already-porous system. Same intervention: ground, hold, sensation in the feet, but with even more emphasis on the holding.

- **If they came in through a head-channel opening:** the negative self-talk has gone down substantially. They may not be in distress, just disoriented. The path: normalize and let it integrate. See [[three-paths-of-awakening]].

- **If they came in through a heart-channel opening:** they may be in love-everywhere mode (the [[heart-awakening-disney-phase]]) and just confused about why their life is suddenly suffused with love. The path is also normalization, with awareness that the Disney phase ends abruptly and they will need support when it does.

- **If they came in through a gut-channel opening (kundalini-like):** energetic disruption, sleep difficulty, shaking. This is the most dangerous failure mode -- they may need somatic practitioners (acupuncture, craniosacral, energy work) alongside the conversation. See [[inherent-safety-as-gut-awakening]].

- **If they had a near-death experience or depression-to-recognition:** the integration tends to be longer and harder. Eckhart Tolle's park bench. Byron Katie. Bucky Fuller. The person may be unable to operate normally for months. See [[integration]].

- **If they are using the recognition as an exit from emotional life:** the opening has lifted them above the messy material and they are now dissociated and possibly depressed. The path is harder: [[awakening-can-make-emotional-healing-harder]].

## Coaching Path

The base coaching arc is simple and consistent:

1. **Normalize the experience.** "This is normal. It has happened to many other people. It has a name." See [[normalize-the-experience-its-happened-to-others]]. This single move dissolves much of the distress because the distress is largely produced by the story that "something is wrong with me." Replace the story.

2. **Point at the still-operating life.** The person worries they cannot function. The diagnostic: are you still feeding yourself? Still making your way? Still operating? Almost always yes. The system is whole. Only the identity is shifting, not the capacity.

3. **Address the fear directly.** Name that the fear is about self-definition, not safety. The body has confused the two. Naming the confusion often releases part of the fear.

4. **Ground the body.** For somatic/energetic disruption: attention to the feet, physical holding by a partner or trusted person, slow yoga, walking in nature, deeply sensual experiences. See [[ground-the-body-during-awakening]].

5. **Recommend enjoyment of the sensations.** "Get out of your brain about what it means for the future of you and just notice that there's a lot of freedom happening in your body. There's a lot of opening that's happening in your body. How do you enjoy that? How do you lean back and deeply enjoy that experience?" -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening. This is counter-intuitive but it is the core integration move: enjoying the dissolution speeds the integration.

6. **Address the thoughts as thoughts.** If the person believes they are going crazy, that this isn't safe, that they need to stop it -- treat those as thoughts to see through, not as facts to address. See [[if-mind-can-think-it-it-isnt-it]].

7. **Locate them in [[three-paths-of-awakening]].** Knowing which channel opened tells you what support helps next.

8. **Reassure that it goes into the background eventually.** "This is not the end, whether it's a good end or a bad end. This is not the end." The intensity passes. The recognition does not, but it becomes ordinary, not load-bearing. See [[peace-precedes-joy-after-non-duality]].

The path does not require complex coaching. The single most powerful intervention is normalization by someone who has seen this before and is unbothered by it.

"When they find out it's normal and they see that I'm not bothered by it... then they can get curious about it, and everything shifts." -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Do not diagnose the person with a psychiatric condition just because their description matches depersonalization. See [[zen-sickness]] -- about 70% of online depersonalization stories match awakening literature.
- Do not try to get them to stop the experience. The trying-to-stop is what creates the suffering, not the experience itself.
- Do not encourage them to chase the bliss or peak parts of it. See [[state-seeking]].
- Do not let them build an identity on the experience. See [[dont-identify-as-awakened]].
- Do not give complex theory before grounding. A panicked nervous system cannot absorb the framework. Ground first, frame after.
