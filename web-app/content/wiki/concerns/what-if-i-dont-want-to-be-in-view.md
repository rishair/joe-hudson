# What if I don't want to be in VIEW all the time?

## What Someone Says

This concern arrives from someone who has been doing the work, who knows what [[view]] is, and who has noticed that the practice has started feeling like a should. The way they ask the question reveals the trap: the practice has become a morality.

- "What if I don't want to be in VIEW all the time?"
- "I'm tired of trying to be open about everything."
- "What about when I have a strong perspective and I want to passionately hold it?"
- "What if other people's perspectives make me angry and frustrated?"
- "Do I have to be impartial about everything?"
- "I want to be right about this and I don't want to be in wonder about whether I might be wrong."
- "VIEW has started to feel like a rule I'm not allowed to break."

The question often includes "passionately hold a perspective while being grounded, curious of others' perspectives, especially when they spur feelings of anger and frustration." The wording shows the bind: the person believes they have to be in VIEW *and* hold their perspective *and* be calm — and the combination feels impossible or fake.

## Detection Cues

How an AI coach recognizes this concern:

- Direct: the person asks whether VIEW is required, optional, or has exceptions.
- The framing "VIEW vs my perspective" — treating them as in tension.
- A sense of obligation around the practice. The body is tight when VIEW is mentioned.
- A history of using VIEW well, followed by current resistance or fatigue.
- The presence of strong emotions (anger, frustration) about other people's views, and the conviction that those emotions are evidence the person is failing at VIEW.
- The phrase "I should be in VIEW about X but I'm not."

Indirect signals:

- A long explanation of why this particular topic, person, or situation makes VIEW impossible.
- Self-criticism about not being able to maintain VIEW under pressure.
- Reports of forcing themselves into VIEW and feeling phony.
- A request for permission to be partial, framed cautiously.

## What Joe Hears

The concern is not really about VIEW. It is about the conversion of VIEW from a state into an obligation. The person has internalized VIEW as a should. Once that happens, the practice has stopped being VIEW. VIEW done as obligation is no longer VIEW — it is just a performance of openness imposed by a rule.

The structural answer collapses the question. You do not have to be in VIEW. You cannot make yourself be in VIEW. VIEW is a place you go when you want to go there. Forcing it makes it impossible.

"VIEW doesn't, view in itself, just if you're in view, you can't see morality around view. Meaning like, let's not make this like the golden rule or something that you have to do or, you know, right or wrong or good or bad or view is just a place you get to go if you want to go there." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

Brett's even cleaner framing:

"You wouldn't be in VIEW if you're forcing yourself to be in VIEW." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3

The recursive nature is the point. The very act of trying to be in VIEW about VIEW (impartial about whether one should be impartial) demonstrates that the trying is partial. The exit is to drop the requirement entirely.

The deeper material the question usually points at: the person has built up frustration with how the work is being held in their life. Maybe they imported the practice from a course where it was framed normatively. Maybe they applied it to themselves as the next self-improvement project. Maybe they have been performing VIEW for years and the body is reporting that the performance has run out of fuel. Whatever the source, the felt sense is "I cannot do this anymore," and what cannot be done is the forced version, not the actual practice.

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person is **collapsed into virtue around VIEW**: Joe surfaces the contradiction. VIEW done as virtue is not VIEW. The honesty of "I don't want to do this right now" is closer to VIEW than the performance of doing it.

- If the person is **angry about other people's perspectives**: Joe redirects to the anger. The implied frame ("I should be in VIEW about this but I'm angry") makes the anger into evidence of failure. Joe inverts: the anger is fine. Feel it. The VIEW question is downstream of the anger work, not in conflict with it. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for what happens with the anger afterward.

- If the person is **trying to use VIEW as a way to be right**: Joe names the trap. Using VIEW to win or to be seen as the bigger person is [[view-as-technique]]. The exhaustion is the system's recognition that the technique is not landing.

- If the person is **asking for permission to hold a passionate perspective**: Joe gives it. Yes, hold the perspective. You can hold a perspective and not believe it is right. You can hold a perspective and believe it is right. Both are available. The freedom is in not making the perspective an identity to defend.

- If the person is **at the natural-state recognition** (Brett's frame): Joe agrees and develops. VIEW is not a state to install. It is the natural state under the stories. You cannot push yourself into your natural state. You can explore what is in the way of it.

## Coaching Path

1. **Collapse the question.** "You don't ever have to be in VIEW." Said clearly and quickly. The relief that follows is the body releasing the should.

2. **Distinguish VIEW from VIEW-as-morality.** VIEW is a place you go if you want to. The trying-to-be-in-VIEW-because-you-should is not VIEW. Separate the practice from the should that has been wrapped around it.

3. **Permission for the passionate perspective.** Yes, have your perspectives. Yes, hold them strongly. Joe specifically offers the distinction: you can hold a perspective and think you are right, or hold a perspective and not think you are right. Both are clean.

4. **The freedom-from-rightness move.** "I can hold a perspective and not think I'm right. I just know this is right for me." This is the more advanced freedom. It lets you act from clarity without needing the perspective to be objectively correct. See [[hold-a-perspective-without-thinking-youre-right]].

5. **Feel the anger or frustration that comes with the perspective.** If holding the perspective is producing emotional charge, the work is the felt emotion, not the meta-question about VIEW. Anger that the other person is wrong, fear of being different, sadness about isolation — each is feelable. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

6. **Brett's natural-state framing as the exit.** VIEW points to the natural state that is underneath the wired patterns and the strategies for getting love. You do not adopt it. You explore it. The stories that say "I'm getting angry, therefore that isn't VIEW" are themselves what is in the way of recognizing the natural state. The anger and the natural state are not in opposition. The story about the anger being a failure is what makes them seem opposed.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

- Does not insist the person stay in VIEW.
- Does not moralize about being open.
- Does not deliver VIEW as a discipline that overrides other felt experience.
- Does not contradict the person's perspective or argue for impartiality as superior.
- Does not collapse the freedom-from-rightness into not-having-perspectives.
- Does not make the anger or frustration into the problem.
