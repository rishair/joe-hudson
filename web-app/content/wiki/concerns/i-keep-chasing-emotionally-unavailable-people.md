# I keep chasing emotionally unavailable people / I'm in an anxious-avoidant dynamic

## What Someone Says

- "I keep falling for people who are emotionally unavailable"
- "I'm always the one chasing"
- "They're always a little bit aloof and I'm always reaching"
- "I get hurt because I'm pursuing somebody who pulls away"
- "How do I stop being in an anxious-avoidant dynamic?"
- "Why am I always the one who cares more?"
- "I'm always fighting for their attention"
- "They were so distant and I just kept trying harder"
- "I always end up getting rejected"

The framing usually has a quality of "this is happening to me." The pattern is described as something the person walks into rather than constructs.

## Detection Cues

- Repeated mention of being the one "chasing," "trying harder," "reaching," "pursuing"
- The partners are described in similar terms: aloof, unavailable, distant, hard to read
- The person frames themselves as the available one and the partner as the problem
- Jealousy or neediness is named as a recurring feature
- The person describes adoring people across the room who are visibly into them as "not attractive"
- The person says they have done a lot of work on this and the pattern keeps happening

The deeper signal: the person reports having had this same configuration with multiple partners. The pattern is portable. The partners change; the configuration does not.

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the chasing is a body that equates love with chasing, not love with receiving. The pattern is [[trauma-mapping]] applied to romantic attachment specifically. The person is generating the configuration where chasing is the form of love available. See [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]] for the full description of this configuration.

Joe also hears: the chasing is itself the move that pushes the partner away. The pattern is not "I keep finding distant people." The pattern is "I keep producing the distance." The pursuing, the jealousy, the neediness -- these are not responses to unavailability. They are the inputs that produce unavailability in the partner.

"Jealousy is like that. Jealousy is like, I really want that person, but my jealousy in and of itself is pushing them away, or my neediness is pushing them away." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

This is the empowering move that the person will at first reject: they are not victims of the configuration. They are choosing the configuration. There is something in them that does not want the level of intimacy they say they want.

"I'm actually pushing them away, so therefore I am choosing to push them away. There's something in me that doesn't want that level of intimacy. There's something in me that equates love with chasing, not love with receiving. And so I'm scared of a love where I receive." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- If the person stays in victim frame: they may need [[deconstruct-the-victim-to-release-anger]] before the rest of the work can land. Joe will not move forward as long as the framing is "this is what keeps happening to me."
- If the person can hear "I am choosing this": Joe moves to the underlying emotion being avoided. Often the avoidance is of receiving love, or of the felt sense of being adored ("the abyss"), or of feeling like hot shit.
- If the person describes the emotional unavailability of past partners with disdain or contempt: this is a tell that the receiving stance is itself the thing being avoided. The disdain is the defense.
- If [[trauma-mapping]] is showing up: Joe may go directly to the childhood configuration where love and chasing got wired together. Often this is a parent whose love had to be earned, fought for, or chased.

## Coaching Path

1. **Establish that this is a chosen pattern, not a fate.** This is the harder move. Joe is matter-of-fact about it but does not force it. The person needs to see that they are producing the distance. Without this, they keep looking for the right partner instead of working the configuration.

2. **Identify the unfelt emotion the configuration is avoiding.** Usually this is one of: the felt sense of receiving love, the felt sense of being adored or chased, the felt sense of being empowered ("they can earn me"), or the abyss-like emotion that opens when chasing stops.

3. **Fall in love with the avoided emotional state.** The corrective is not "stop chasing." The corrective is to feel and love the emotional state that comes up when you are the rejected one or the chasing one -- because that is the state the system is using the partner-configuration to keep cycling around without ever fully feeling. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[chase-it-around-the-room]].

   "The way through is to fully fall in love with and accept and look forward to the emotional state that happens when you are rejected or when you are chasing. It's falling in love with that part of yourself is the quickest way to do it." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

4. **If the relationship has ended, grieve it fully.** Joe specifically names grief as the second main path. Not just grief for the partner, but grief for "20 years of your life chasing pretty much your mom or dad's love through the face of a boyfriend or a girlfriend." This is [[grief-as-gateway-to-self-love]] applied to the chasing pattern. See [[mourn-the-breakup-fully]] and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]].

5. **The arc tends to follow:** see the pattern as chosen -> identify what is being avoided -> fall in love with that avoided state -> grieve the years spent in the configuration -> the body stops producing the configuration.

## Related Concerns

This is one of the most-developed forms of the broader [[i-cant-find-love]] concern. Where the chasing pattern is recognizable enough that the person can name it, this concern article is the entry point. Where the person presents the more general "I can't find love" frame without the chase pattern in view yet, start there.

The push-pull structure underneath this concern is the same as the structure underneath jealousy, neediness, hyper-independence, transactionality, and the "I'm not enough" frame. See [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]] for the family of forms. The chasing-of-the-unavailable is one of the most reliable forms because it self-confirms: the chosen partners keep being unavailable, which keeps the cycle running and the avoided emotional state out of reach.

The underlying motivator across all of these forms is [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]]. The chasing keeps the person from the configuration where they would be received, would have to commit, and would face the felt threat of the kind of self-loss they have been protecting against.

## The Two Binary Avoidance Behaviors Inside This Pattern

In the abandonment-mapping form of this concern, two specific avoidance behaviors tend to alternate or co-exist inside the same person. Both are present-time attempts to not feel the abandonment that is arriving, and both produce more abandonment as the predictable result:

1. **The hardening / push-away.** "Fuck you, I don't need you anyhow." The body preemptively rejects to avoid the felt cost of being rejected. The partner reads the hardening as departure and departs.
2. **The grasping / pull-toward.** "What can I do to help? How do I keep you here?" The body becomes useful, accommodating, anticipatory. The neediness this produces is itself unattractive; the management of the partner is itself smothering. The partner pulls away in response.

The two are not different patterns. They are the same pattern wearing two different costumes. A person who has both alternates between them based on the partner or the moment. The recognition that the cold-and-independent self and the needy-and-accommodating self are doing the same work is often the breakthrough — it dissolves the inner judgment of one mode against the other and reveals both as forms of the abandonment-avoidance. See [[joes-abandonment-mapping]] for the worked-out version of these two behaviors in Joe's own history.

The coaching move once these are visible: the work is not to choose between hardening and grasping. The work is to drop both — which is only possible by feeling the abandonment that both were avoiding. The corrective is the same as elsewhere in the pattern: fall in love with the abandonment itself. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].
