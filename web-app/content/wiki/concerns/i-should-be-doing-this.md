# I should be doing this / I shouldn't be doing this

## What Someone Says

This is the concern almost no one names directly. It shows up in the language people use to describe their own lives.

- "I know I should be exercising more."
- "I shouldn't be eating this."
- "I have to call my mom."
- "I keep telling myself I should stop doing X."
- "I've been trying to quit for years."
- "I just need to push through."
- "I should be over this by now."
- "I've been trying to start this business for years and I keep not doing it."
- "I keep telling myself I should exercise/meditate/call/write and it never happens."
- "I keep beating myself up about not doing it and it doesn't change anything."
- "I seem to be on the cusp of everything I could want and hope for, and the patterns of obligation and responsibility just get in the way."
- "Why can't I finish up with this divorce? The same patterns apply at work and everywhere else."

The concern can also surface as a complaint about a bad habit, a missed goal, a project that will not start, or a relationship duty that feels like a weight.

## The 'Can't Finish' Variant

A specific shape of this concern: the person can name what they want, and they have done significant work, and they cannot finish. They are on the cusp of what they want -- the divorce that needs to be finalized, the company that needs to be sold, the move that needs to be made -- and obligation and responsibility patterns keep stalling them at the threshold. The body locates the stuckness somewhere (chest, upper arms, unease). They can describe it precisely. They cannot move past it.

The diagnostic for this variant: ask what someone could say to them that would immediately drop them into the felt sense of obligation. The answer usually names the original conditional-love source ("you ruined my life" was the wife's; the inner critic's version follows the same template). Once the source sentence is in the room, the body locates it and the work can begin. See [[responsibility-stops-love]] for the principle that explains why the can't-finish state happens: the obligation has been substituting for love for so long that the person no longer trusts that love can be the operating mode of the decision.

## The "Everything I Do Is From Obligation" Variant (The High-Functioning Empty Form)

A specific shape worth naming distinctly: the person reports that they are already doing all the things — high achievement on the outside, straight A's, good work, the right behaviors — but everything they do is from "should" and nothing is from desire. The person is not failing to do the right things. They are doing them perfectly. The complaint is that the doing is empty.

The presenting language for this variant:
- "Everything I do is just because that's the right thing to do and not because I want to do this."
- "I do all the things and none of them feel good."
- "I can't find pleasure in what to do next."
- "I'm very high achieving on the outside but I don't actually want any of it."

The diagnostic difference from the "years of trying" variant: in the years-of-trying form, the person is failing to do the should-thing and beating themselves up about it. In the everything-from-obligation form, the person is succeeding at the should-things and reporting that the success is empty. The shame layer is different: years-of-trying carries the shame of failure; everything-from-obligation often carries the shame of being ungrateful for what the person has achieved.

The underlying mechanism is often deeper: the wants are not just buried under shoulds; the channel that carries wants has been sealed by long-term compartmentalization. The person cannot just locate the want behind the should because the want-signal is structurally offline. See [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] for the read this variant often pairs with, and [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]] for the related concern.

The coaching path for this variant differs from the standard shoulds path. Locating the want directly often fails — the person genuinely does not know what they want, not because they have not looked, but because the want-signal is not arriving. The work first has to re-open the emotional channel that wants ride on. See [[joy-as-matriarch]] for the structural mechanism: the wants are part of the family that has been kept out.

The opening move is often the matriarch teaching paired with the wisdom-frame (see [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] compact form): "you very wisely compartmentalized your emotions so that you could survive [the original conditions]." The wisdom-frame is critical because the person presenting this variant is often holding shame about the want-absence ("I should be able to want things") that would only get louder under a directly want-focused intervention.

The arc from there moves through:
1. Honor the strategy that sealed the wants (the compartmentalization was wise)
2. Open the channel by allowing the difficult emotions back through (matriarch's children first)
3. The wants return as part of the family — not as a separate project, but as the natural arrival once the channel is open

## The Productivity-Loop Variant ("I Get in Loops of Guilt About Not Being Productive")

A different shape of the concern surfaces in a continuous-loop form. The person reports that they are caught in a self-reinforcing cycle: they should do the thing, they feel guilty for not doing it, the guilt makes it harder to do, they tell themselves to stop feeling guilty, they don't do it anyway, then they feel guilty about not doing it, and around again. The presenting frame is the loop itself, not any specific should.

The presenting language for this variant:

- "I get in like these loops where I feel guilty for not doing something, even if it's something that would make me happy."
- "I logically know I should stop guilting myself about it, but also that I shouldn't just stay still all the time."
- "It's a cage of shoulds." (Joe's compressed naming of this presenting form.)
- The client often pairs this with a depression component: "I have an emotional depression problem too, which is interesting."

The diagnostic feature of this variant: the client has done enough self-awareness work to see that the shoulding-themselves is the problem, but the meta-recognition has been converted into a new should ("I should stop shoulding myself") that perpetuates the cage. The work has to operate outside the should-frame entirely. Conceptual interventions that the client can convert into more shoulds will fail.

The coaching path for this variant differs from the "years of trying" form. The starting move is not to locate a single specific want behind a single specific should. The starting move is to force the diffuse-loop frame into a single specific should so the work can begin. "Give me a specific. What's the one specific thing you should yourself for the most?" The specificity-step is preliminary and required. Without it, the descent has nothing concrete to operate on.

Once the specific should is in the room, the descent runs through [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]] in its should-as-blocker variant: "If you couldn't feel the should, what would you have to feel?" The underneath layer is usually aimlessness or some adjacent purposeless-state. The descent continues through that layer to whatever is underneath -- often panic, then existential dread. At the bottom of the descent is the freedom the cage of shoulds has been protecting against. See [[purposelessness-is-freedom]].

The depression component, when present, is structurally connected to the loop. The repressed material that the shoulds have been holding down is what produces the depression at the emotional layer. See [[depression-four-levels]] for the framework and [[emotional-leak-mistaken-for-emotional-excess]] for the related read when the depression coexists with chronic small-upsets.

## The "Years of Trying" Variant

A specific variant deserves naming: the person presents a thing they have been trying to do for years and have not done. Start a business. Exercise. Get closer to a loved one. Quit a habit. The content varies but the structural diagnostic does not: they have been beating themselves up about it the whole time. The shame is what has been keeping it in place. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] for the diagnostic in full -- the structural prediction is reliable enough that you can name it before you know what the thing is.

## Detection Cues

Listen for the word *should* in any form. Listen for *have to*, *need to*, *supposed to*, *ought to*. Listen for the language of compliance and the language of failure-to-comply. Both signal the same underlying pattern.

Indirect signals:
- The person describes the same stuck habit they have described before.
- The person frames a goal as an obligation rather than a desire.
- The person describes self-criticism as motivation strategy.
- The person says "I'm just lazy" or "I have no discipline."

## What Joe Hears

Underneath the should is shame. The should is the verbal form of [[shame-drives-behavior]]. The structure assumes the person is bad and must be corrected. As long as the should is running, the bad habit stays in place because the should itself is what holds it there.

The person is also locked in [[rebellion-or-submission-to-should]]. Either response keeps them stuck.

The deeper read: the person has lost contact with what they actually want. The shoulds have buried the wants. See [[the-want]] and [[wants-over-shoulds]].

In the relationship/can't-finish variant, the underlying mechanism is more specific: every act of taking responsibility for the other person has stopped the felt love. The accumulated stoppage is the obligation pattern. The person cannot finalize the ending because the only mode they have ever known with this person is responsibility, not love. See [[responsibility-stops-love]].

## What Joe Looks For (Reads)

- **If they accept the should as the right approach:** the work is to challenge the assumption that self-management produces change. The screw gun analogy: if it strips every screw, you stop using it. Joe will introduce [[should-as-threat]] and the developmental argument (0 to 7 years, no shoulds, fastest learning of life).
- **If they fear what happens without shoulds:** the concern is [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]]. Joe will go to [[inherent-goodness]] and the question of who they think they are underneath.
- **If they are aware the should isn't working but feel stuck with it:** Joe will go to [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] -- the specific move of locating the alive want underneath each dead should.
- **If they are running a meta-should** ("I should stop using shoulds"): Joe will name it. This is one of the few cases where simply seeing the pattern dissolves it.
- **If they say they have reached acceptance but not love:** the read is [[acceptance-without-love]]. The shoulds have been managed down but not extinguished. The work is to feel the gap between accepting and loving.
- **If the shoulds are wrapped around obligation to another person:** the work runs through [[responsibility-stops-love]]. The live demonstration (feel the love → now feel obligated → notice the love disappear) is often the most efficient entry point.

## Coaching Path

1. Name the should as the cause of the stuckness, not the symptom. The person usually has it inverted (they think the bad habit causes them to should themselves; the reverse is true).
2. Introduce the four layers of should-as-threat so the body understands why it has been resisting.
3. Locate the want under the should. "What do you actually want here?"
4. If a want surfaces but feels selfish or unhealthy, distinguish [[wanting-vs-craving]].
5. If no want surfaces, work on permission to want at all. See [[the-want]].
6. The arc moves from "I should fix this about me" to "what do I actually want, and how does it feel to want it cleanly?"
7. For the can't-finish variant: run the live demonstration of [[responsibility-stops-love]]. Then use [[mirror-to-group-then-substitute-i-for-you]] if a group is present, or [[tell-yourself-you-love-yourself-in-a-mirror]] if not. The pivot is from the shoulds to "I deserve love."
8. Offer [[stick-a-fucking-shit-in-front-of-the-should]] as the in-the-moment ongoing intervention. Every should that arises after the session gets cursed.

## When the Shoulds Are Producing Anxiety, Not Just Shame

A distinct branch of this concern: the should-loop is producing chronic anxiety, not just chronic shame about the unmade action. The person reports being anxious all the time and they cannot find a specific thing to be anxious about. The anxiety is the somatic signature of the continuous should-attack.

The two-CEO example in [[two-ceos-at-eighty-hours]] is the canonical illustration. Two high-capacity leaders at the same load -- one constantly stressed and exhausted, one with infinite energy. The difference is the self-talk. The stressed one is running the should-loop continuously. The non-stressed one is either taking action on the should or explicitly re-prioritizing it ("that's important, but it's not as important as this, so I'm gonna do this"). The should-loop is what produces the chronic anxiety, not the load.

The intervention path for this branch differs slightly from the standard shoulds path. The work is not only on the want underneath each should (the standard move) but also on the should-as-anxiety-producer recognition. The compressed version: every unresolved should is an internal threat the body is responding to. Resolving the should -- either by acting on it or by explicitly de-prioritizing it -- ends the threat. The anxiety releases when the loop is closed.

See [[two-sources-of-anxiety]] for the broader framing: the unmade action is the first source of anxiety. The should-loop is the chronic version -- many unmade actions producing many simultaneous threat signals. See [[compare-short-term-to-long-term-consequences]] when the specific avoided action is one that involves speaking truth, and the consequence-comparison is the move that makes the right move obvious.
