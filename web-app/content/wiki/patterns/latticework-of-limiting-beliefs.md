# The Latticework of Limiting Beliefs

## The Pattern

Limiting beliefs rarely operate alone. A single behavior is usually held in place by a structure of multiple beliefs that prop each other up. You work on one. The behavior does not change. The conclusion is often "the work didn't work." The actual situation is: there are six more beliefs holding the structure in place, and the one you worked on was just the surface.

This is why a person can have a real recognition, intellectually see through a belief, and still find themselves doing the same thing. They unhooked one belief from a network of beliefs. The network compensates and re-stabilizes around the missing piece.

"You have one belief, you can see through it, but the behavior doesn't change. But there could be like a latticework of belief systems that are holding that in place, that behavior in place." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## The Layoff Example

A worked example shows how the latticework stacks:

The trigger: your company is doing cutbacks.

- **First belief:** What's happening is wrong. (Felt as constriction.)
- **Second belief:** I don't like what is happening here.
- **Third belief:** Change is hard.
- **Fourth belief:** Change is unsafe.
- **Fifth belief:** I'll be punished. (Often rooted in childhood: every change in the house produced anxiety, yelling, the airport-trip freakout.)
- **Sixth belief:** I'm bad. (The bottom of the stack for many people.)

You can work on "what's happening is wrong" and feel a momentary release. The behavior does not change because the structure underneath is still intact. You have to keep going down the stack.

## The Fractal Quality

The latticework is not finite. It can branch indefinitely, and at each branch a new belief can be uncovered. This can feel like an endless project. It is.

"It is a vast fractal that can go on forever." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

But the fractal is not infinite in a hopeless way. Most chains terminate at a small number of foundational beliefs. The branches lead back to a core. For many people, that core is [[inherent-goodness]] (or rather, the belief in its absence). For others it is "I'm not safe." For others it is the existential fact of existing as a self that requires defending. The fractal is wide but shallow.

"It could all go back to the fact that you exist in the first place, so there's some you that needs to be protected, or I'm bad, or I'm unsafe. Yeah, there are some core ones." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## The Emotional Layer Is Different at Each Level

The beliefs at different layers carry different emotional charges. Surface-level beliefs ("what's happening is wrong") often carry mild annoyance or frustration. Deeper beliefs ("I'm bad," "I'll be alone") carry the heavy emotions: grief, terror, shame.

This means working the latticework is not just intellectual descent. It is an emotional descent. Each layer down opens a different door of feeling that the higher layers were protecting against.

"There might be anger there in one of the belief systems and fear in another belief system. So yeah, that's where the emotional components becomes a really important thing, is like to really understand the emotions that hold these things in place." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

This is why [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] pairs with latticework work. You cannot dismantle a layer of the lattice without feeling the emotion that layer was guarding.

## How to Work the Latticework

The principle: each constriction is an opportunity to find another belief. Do not stop at the first belief uncovered. Stay with the constriction until the next belief surfaces. Stay with that one. Stay with the constriction it leaves until the next one surfaces. Work your way down.

"Each time you feel constriction, there's an opportunity for more freedom in a belief system." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

This is patient work. You are not racing to the bottom. You are letting each layer surface and unhook before going down.

## The Trap of Working Only the Surface

A common pattern: a person works on the surface belief, feels a temporary release, declares the work done. The behavior keeps happening because the structure is still in place. They conclude limiting-belief work does not actually change behavior.

The diagnosis is: you have not yet found the load-bearing belief. The structure is held up by something deeper. Until you find it and unhook it, the cycle continues.

This connects to [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]]: the surface complaint is often a symptom, not the root. The latticework is one way the root hides — behind a stack of intermediate beliefs that all seem like the real issue.

## Why Some People Plateau

A person who has been doing limiting-belief work for years and feels stuck is often working at one layer and not descending. They have become skilled at the surface inquiry and stopped going below. The plateau ends when they let themselves drop into the next layer.

The signal that more layers exist: the behavior keeps returning, even though they have "worked on" the belief multiple times. The behavior is the symptom; the unfelt deeper belief is the cause.

## The Three Keepers Are Usually at the Bottom

[[three-keepers-of-stuck-beliefs]] (I'm bad / It's hard / I'll be unsafe) are usually what the latticework terminates in. When you reach one of these three, you have hit a foundational layer. The work from here is different — you are not unhooking another intermediate piece, you are working on the structural assumption that holds the whole network up.

## Tone

Joe acknowledges the depth without making it daunting. The fractal nature is named as a fact, but immediately accompanied by the reminder that each constriction is an opportunity. The pattern is honest about complexity while keeping the practice approachable.
