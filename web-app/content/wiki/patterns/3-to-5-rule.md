# The 3-to-5 Rule

## Definition

When someone changes their behavior or draws a new boundary, the people around them will test it 3 to 5 times. Each test escalates. The other person ups the ante, using new strategies to pull the changed person back into the old agreement. After 3 to 5 rounds of holding the boundary, the old pattern breaks.

## In Practice

"When you have a new behavior, you're drawing a new boundary, you should expect that the person is gonna treat you like your old self 3 to 5 times, and each time they're going to kind of up the ante on the behavior." -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

Concrete example: a partner who yells. The transformed person says, "I don't want to be with your yelling, but I want to be with you. When you're yelling, I'm going to leave the house. I'll come back in 30 minutes. If we can talk, great. If not, I'll leave again." It takes 3 to 5 repetitions. The yelling partner will try additional tactics to reestablish the old dynamic. After the boundary holds through the escalation, the pattern collapses.

Joe frames this pattern as a gift rather than an obstacle. The testing forces the person to learn to maintain the new behavior under stress, which deepens the [[integration]].

"I'm really grateful that it's built this way, that humanity seems to tend to act in this way, because it really forces us to learn to keep that boundary." -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

## When Someone Experiences This

- "I changed but nobody else changed"
- "I drew a boundary and they got worse, not better"
- "Maybe the change isn't working because everyone is resisting it"
- Temptation to revert because the social pressure feels too intense

The coaching response: this is expected. Name the pattern. Give them the count. Expect 3 to 5 rounds of escalation. The other person is not being malicious; they are responding to the disruption of a known agreement.

## What Follows

About 70-80% of relationships survive the shift. The 20-30% who leave are not a failure; they are choosing a different agreement. People who share the new values show up to fill the space.

"If you transform, there's some people who are going to be like, I don't like the new agreement. Everybody's very scared of it. But it doesn't happen as frequently as you would think." -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- After any significant transformation when the person re-enters their existing relationships
- When someone has drawn a new boundary and faces resistance
- When [[familiar-over-safe]] is at work in the relational system, not just the individual

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Deeper [[integration]] through tested, stress-proven boundary holding
- Relational reconfiguration: some relationships deepen, some end
- Increased confidence in the transformation when the boundary survives testing
