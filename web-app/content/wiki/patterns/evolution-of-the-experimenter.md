# The Evolution of the Experimenter

## The Pattern

Over years of running experiments, the experimenter's relationship to the experiments changes. The activities may look similar from the outside. The internal stance is different at each stage. Three stages are visible in Joe's account of his own development:

**Stage 1: Rebellion (experiments as autonomy assertion).** The reason to experiment is to refuse to take other people's word for things. Authority is suspect. The experiment is the rebel's tool for not being told what is true. The energy is "fuck off, I'm going to find out for myself."

**Stage 2: Internalization (experiments as self-making).** The reason to experiment has shifted. The rebellion is over. Now the experiment is the way the person finds out what is theirs. Not "I refuse to listen to them" but "I am finding out what I actually think, want, and know." The energy is "I am following my own path, distinct from theirs."

**Stage 3: Surrender (experiments as gurus).** The reason has shifted again. The experiments are no longer something the person designs to assert themselves. They arrive. The person notices what they need to learn next and surrenders into the experiment as into something larger than the self. The energy is reverent. The experiments have become the teacher.

"When I started, it was a way to show my autonomy, show my sovereignty. Nobody tells me what to do. I'm a rebel. You're an authority figure. Fuck off. I'm going to learn this for myself. Then it became a way for me to really internalize it and see what was mine. And I'm not living against them or for them. I'm not listening to them or not listening to them. I am just following my thing. And now it's very much like a surrender. The experiment shows up and it's like, oh, that's obviously something I have to do. And I surrender into the experiment almost like it's a, you know, it's a traditional guru. Like my experiments are my gurus on some level." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## Why This Pattern Matters for the Coach

The coach who recognizes which stage a person is in can meet them where they actually are. The same instruction lands differently at each stage.

To the Stage 1 person: "design an experiment" is welcomed because it bypasses the authority they distrust. They will run experiments to prove the teacher wrong, and that is fine. The proving-wrong is the activity that produces the learning.

To the Stage 2 person: "design an experiment" is welcomed because it is the operating mode they have already adopted. They do not need to be sold on it. They need help with specific experiments and refinement of the practice.

To the Stage 3 person: "design an experiment" might land badly because they have moved past the "I design" frame. The experiment is what arrives, not what they construct. For them, the move is to notice what is arriving, not to manufacture something new.

The same word ("experiment") references different things at different stages. The coach who tracks the stage tracks what the word means to this person right now.

## Stage 1: Rebellion

The marker: the person is using experimentation as a refusal of authority. They run experiments primarily to discover that the authority was wrong, partial, or full of itself. The validation is "I figured this out for myself; I did not have to trust anyone."

Joe describes his own Stage 1 in detail. The authority issues were structural: his father was an authority not to be trusted, and that pattern was projected onto every authority figure. The result: no teacher could do right by him. The experiments started as the only way he could trust anything -- by testing it himself.

"For me, it was just that I had really bad authority issues... I didn't trust authority at all. I projected my dad onto every authority figure... And eventually I heard enough teachers say, don't trust me, uh, try it out for yourself, test it... I, I said, okay, that's, that's something I can do. You want to put the power in my hands, that's where I can trust it. And so I started testing everything that I read." -- Joe Hudson

What characterizes Stage 1 work:
- High intellectual debate energy
- Tendency to seek out and test the most provocative teachings
- The experiment often "fails" in ways that confirm the original suspicion
- Reading is suspect; testing is trusted
- The person says "I had to test this for myself" with some heat

This stage is not a flaw. It is the structural opening that lets the person actually engage with material they would otherwise reject. The authority issue produced the experimentation, and the experimentation produced everything that followed. Joe names this directly: it was "by far one of the biggest blessings or fortunes of having authority issues was that I had to test everything."

## Stage 2: Internalization

The marker: the rebellion has quieted. The person is no longer experimenting against the teachers. They are experimenting to find out what is theirs.

The energy shifts from "you can't tell me what to think" to "I am finding out what I think." The same activity (running experiments) is now serving a different function. The authority-protest has receded; the self-discovery has come forward.

What characterizes Stage 2 work:
- Experiments are designed around what the person wants to know, not around what the authority claimed
- The teaching is taken seriously enough to test on its own terms
- The person is no longer prosecuting the teacher; they are investigating the territory
- Reflection becomes more important; the data is being read
- The person says things like "I want to find out for myself" without the heat the Stage 1 person had

The internalization stage is where most of the actual self-knowing work happens. The rebellion is necessary opening; the internalization is sustained work. The person has moved from "I will not be told" to "I am finding out."

## Stage 3: Surrender

The marker: the experiments arrive. The person notices what they need to learn next and surrenders into the experiment as into something larger than the self.

The shift here is significant. In Stage 2 the person still designed the experiments. In Stage 3 the experiments are designed by something the person is listening to -- the migratory path, the call, the next-thing-to-be-learned. The person's job is to notice what is arriving and to say yes.

"Now it's very much like a surrender. The experiment shows up and it's like, oh, that's obviously something I have to do. And I surrender into the experiment almost like it's a, you know, it's a traditional guru. Like my experiments are my gurus on some level. And it's just like, I surrender into that experiment. There is a way in which the self gets seen through in every experiment. And part of that is in that deep surrender." -- Joe Hudson

What characterizes Stage 3 work:
- Experiments arrive rather than being constructed
- The person feels the pull and surrenders into it
- Each experiment includes some seeing-through of the self
- The reverence Joe describes ("almost like a traditional guru")
- The experiments are doing the teaching; the experimenter is the student

See [[migratory-path]] for the underlying mechanism that allows Stage 3 to function. See [[enjoyment-as-surrender]] for the related framing of how the call is followed. See [[surrender-to-ineffable-vs-person]] for the distinction that protects against treating experiments-as-guru as a substitute for the work of surrender to the ineffable.

## The Stages Are Not Strictly Sequential

The stages described in chronological order in Joe's account, but the person does not necessarily move through them once and never return. A person primarily in Stage 3 can fall back into Stage 1 if an authority issue is re-triggered. A person in Stage 2 can be in Stage 3 in one domain (their personal practice) and Stage 1 in another (their relationship to a new teacher).

The stages are also not value-ranked. Stage 3 is not "better." Each stage is doing different work. The work of Stage 1 (asserting autonomy, breaking from internalized parental authority) is real work. Stage 2 is real work. Stage 3 is real work. The pattern is descriptive of how the relationship matures, not prescriptive of where the person should be.

## What Each Stage Produces

**Stage 1 produces sovereignty.** The person can hold their own ground against any teacher. They know what they have tested and what they have not. They do not collapse into approval-seeking around authority figures.

**Stage 2 produces self-knowing.** The person knows what is theirs. The experiments have built a body of validated knowledge specific to this person's life. The person can describe what they want, what works for them, what does not.

**Stage 3 produces openness.** The person no longer needs to design or protect. The experiments arrive. The self gets seen through in each one. The orientation is reverent receptivity rather than active construction.

## The Coaching Move for Each Stage

For the Stage 1 person: ride the rebellion. Do not try to convince them of anything. Suggest experiments and let them prove things wrong or right. The trust that comes from their own testing is the only trust that will stick.

For the Stage 2 person: support the design of experiments. Help with the structure, the time bounds, the reflection. The person is doing the work; the coach is sharpening the tool.

For the Stage 3 person: get out of the way. Help them notice what is arriving. Resist the urge to suggest experiments they did not need. Trust the migratory path.

## Tone

Joe describes this pattern as autobiography. The tone is reflective. He is naming what he can see now, looking back at decades of his own practice. There is warmth toward each stage, including the rebellious one. The Stage 1 Joe was not wrong; the rebellion was the entry. The Stage 3 Joe could not have arrived without the Stage 1 Joe.
