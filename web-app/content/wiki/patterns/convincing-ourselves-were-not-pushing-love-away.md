# The Two Ways We Convince Ourselves We're Not Pushing Love Away

## The Pattern

A person who is pushing love away through chasing, avoiding, or fighting (see [[three-ways-we-push-love-away]]) has a problem: the conscious mind says it wants love. The behavior produces no love. The gap between the wanting and the result has to be explained somehow. Two specific self-deceptions do that job. They make the push-away invisible by attributing the absence of love to something else.

"The weird part is that we have a way of convincing ourselves that we actually want the love and we're not pushing it away. And we do this in one of two ways." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The two deceptions:

1. **It's not the right person.** The absence of love is because the people I've been with were wrong for me. The right person hasn't shown up yet. I just need to keep looking.

2. **Blame.** The absence of love is because of something they did or something I did. The relationship failed because they wouldn't open up, or because I'm too damaged, or because of a specific behavior on one side.

Both deceptions function as covers. Both keep the conscious story intact. Both prevent the structural recognition that the same person, with whatever partners and whatever specific behaviors, is producing the same outcome — and is therefore producing the outcome.

## The Wrong-Person Deception

The wrong-person deception attributes the absence of love to circumstance. "I just haven't met the right person yet." "There aren't enough good people where I live." "I'm dating in a bad demographic." "My career hasn't allowed me to meet the kind of person I want." "I keep meeting people who aren't ready for what I want."

The deception is sustained by a specific maneuver: the person's selection mechanism is reliably producing partners who cannot land. The body is choosing partners who are unavailable, incompatible, or already attached. The conscious mind reads each instance as bad luck. The pattern across instances reveals the selection at work.

"I'm talking to a guy and he is saying how he is 35 years old, hasn't found the love of his life. But he said, 'I know that I can get it, because I've had two relationships that have been really good. And so I know that I can get it. So I just think it's about finding the right person.' And I was like, 'Okay, cool. So what happened to those two people?' And what we discovered was that the two people that he was dating were both significantly younger than him and were at a place in their life where there's not really a capacity to commit." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The diagnostic question that breaks the wrong-person deception: not "what was wrong with them?" but "how much are you actually just attracted to people who are unavailable, that you know on some level are unavailable, and then you have a good relationship but you know it's never gonna actually pan out?" See [[the-35-year-old-dating-younger-women]] for the worked example of this move.

The body's selection of the not-quite-right partner is the push-away in operation. The person is not failing to find love; the person is locating partners whose unavailability guarantees the love will not land. The wrong-person deception is the cover story that lets the selection continue running without being seen.

Adjacent forms of the wrong-person deception:
- The checklist — see [[the-partner-checklist-doesnt-survive-the-actual-relationship]]
- The "I'm not enough" frame — "once I become enough, the right person will arrive"
- The geographic deferral — "the right person isn't in this city / this country / this generation"
- The timing deferral — "the right person will come when the time is right"

Each variant defers the question of why the person is consistently producing the not-finding outcome.

## The Blame Deception

The blame deception attributes the absence of love to a specific behavior on one side of the relationship. The blame can be aimed at the partner or at the self; both function the same way structurally.

**Blame aimed at the partner.** "I've been chasing you for your love and you just don't open up." "I can't get the love I want because he doesn't open up." "I don't get the love I want because they don't stop hassling me, telling me how I have to be." "I don't get the love I want because they're always fighting with me." "I don't get the love I want because they don't see me."

**Blame aimed at the self.** "I don't get the love I want because I yell, I scream, or whatever." "I'm avoidant and that's why I can't keep love." "I'm too damaged for love."

"We're just constantly blaming instead of actually just looking at the pattern, like, oh, I can get the love I want without the thing it was married to as a kid." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

The blame deception keeps the framing local to the specific relationship. The partner's behavior or the self's behavior becomes the explanation. The structural question — that the same person keeps producing the same outcome across relationships — does not get asked because each specific failure has its own specific blame-story.

The blame deception also performs a second function: it gives the person something to do that feels like work but is not the work. Blaming the partner can be sustained indefinitely. Blaming the self can be sustained indefinitely. Neither requires the person to address the underlying [[three-ways-we-push-love-away|push-away pattern]]. The blame is a substitute activity that produces the feeling of engaging with the problem without engaging with it. See [[blame-is-the-abandonment-of-leadership]] for the related principle.

## Why Both Deceptions Work

Both deceptions work because both contain a partial truth. The partner may actually be unavailable. The self-criticism may actually have produced harm. The specific incident the blame is about may actually have happened. The deception is not that the contents are false. The deception is that the contents are operating as cover for the structural pattern.

The wrong-person deception says: this particular person was wrong. It does not say: the pattern of choosing this particular kind of wrong person across many relationships is the push-away. The blame deception says: this specific behavior is the problem. It does not say: the recurrence of behaviors like this in relationship after relationship is the push-away.

The structural question — "what is the same about all of these relationships?" — is what both deceptions are protecting the person from having to ask. As long as each relationship has its own story (wrong person, specific blame), the structural sameness does not become visible.

## What Breaks the Deceptions

Both deceptions break when the structural pattern becomes visible. The break usually requires someone outside the deception to surface it. The person inside the wrong-person deception cannot easily see that all the wrong people share a specific feature that the body is selecting for. The person inside the blame deception cannot easily see that the blamed behaviors recur across relationships and partners.

The coaching moves that surface the structure:

- "What did all of your past partners have in common?" (cuts through wrong-person)
- "How much are you actually attracted to people who are unavailable?" (cuts through wrong-person)
- "If this happened with three different partners, what does that tell you about the pattern?" (cuts through both)
- "What's the same about every relationship that hasn't worked?" (cuts through both)
- "When the same fight has happened with four different people, who is the common factor?" (cuts through blame)

The break is often accompanied by the subconscious grin described in [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]]: the body recognizes that the conscious story has just been pierced. The break opens the work to the underlying [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]] that the push-away has been protecting.

## The Specific Recovery Move

Once the deceptions break, the recovery is not to find the right person or to fix the blamed behavior. The recovery is to address the wired pattern that has been organizing the selection or the behavior. See [[what-was-wired-with-love-needs-untying]] for the actual work. See [[boundary-against-the-wired-pattern]] for the boundary move that operationalizes the separation of love from the wired companion.

The boundary statement that replaces the blame: "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to [chase / be dominated / fight] for your love." The statement is a structural shift from blaming the specific contents to naming the structural condition. The blame says "you did this wrong, fix it." The boundary says "this is the configuration I will not be in anymore." See [[joes-parents-criticism-boundary]] for the canonical worked example of this move with parents.

## The Repeated Pattern That Should Tip You Off

A specific marker: a person who has had multiple relationships end in the same way — the partner left, the partner withdrew, the partner became cold, the partner couldn't commit, the relationship became a constant fight, the spark died — and who can describe each ending with a specific blame-story or a specific wrong-person-story. The multiple ends with the same shape are the signal. The specific stories are the cover.

The coaching question: "Notice that this has happened three times. What might you be doing across these three?" The question is not asked accusingly. It is asked with the recognition that the body is doing something it does not yet see, and the work is to see it.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person who keeps saying "the right one just hasn't come along yet"
- A person whose past relationships ended for specific blame-able reasons but who cannot see the pattern across them
- A person who can describe in detail what their partners did wrong but cannot describe their own contribution to the pattern
- A person whose self-blame about a specific failed relationship is sustained for months or years
- A person who is articulating one of the two deceptions and is open to hearing what is underneath

## Tone

The naming is done with a slight lightness. Joe is not catching the person out; he is showing them something they have been doing that they could not see. The "subconscious grin" moment is part of the delivery. The deceptions are universal enough that the naming does not need to be heavy. The work that follows is heavy; the naming itself is light.
