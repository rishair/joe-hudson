# The 'What Did I Do Wrong?' Response to Aggression

## The Pattern

When someone is aggressive toward this person — either directly mad or passive-aggressively mad — the person immediately and automatically asks themselves "what did I do wrong?" The anger that should have moved outward at the aggressor turns around and lands on the self as self-blame and self-questioning. The pattern fires without conscious choice. Most people running this pattern do not even notice they are in it; they experience the resulting self-doubt as an internal weather pattern rather than as a response to an external event.

Joe estimates the pattern runs in 60% of people. It is not rare. It is one of the dominant strategies humans develop for handling aggression they cannot push back against.

"I was working with a client recently, super successful alcoholic father like me, and learned that every time Dad got mad, she could not get mad back. So the only act of control, of sovereignty she could have, was to say, what did I do wrong? And I can't tell you I think it's at least 60% of the people work this way. Somebody gets mad at them, either aggressively mad at them or passively aggressively mad at them, and they go, what did I do wrong?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

## The Origin: The Five-Year-Old's Only Sovereign Move

The pattern is installed in childhood. A child whose parent gets aggressive cannot say "fuck you" back. The relationship is required for survival. The child has no available outward route for the anger. The only available move that produces any sense of agency is to take responsibility: "what did I do wrong?"

Taking responsibility looks like sovereignty to a five-year-old because it converts a powerless situation into a soluble problem. "If I did something wrong, then I can do something different, and then the aggression will stop." The problem becomes one the child has theoretical control over, rather than one that depends on the unpredictable parent's mood.

The price: the heart has to close to support the move. The natural response to being aggressed against by someone you love is grief and outward anger. Both require the heart to remain open. The "what did I do wrong" move requires it to close, because the open heart would feel the betrayal directly, and the betrayal is unbearable. The closure is protective.

The structure works for the child. It then runs for the next forty years without anyone noticing. The adult is still routing aggression-responses through the same five-year-old's protocol, with the heart still closing in the same way, producing the same self-blame loop now misread as adult self-questioning.

## The Two Variants

Not all aggression-responses route inward. The other variant routes outward:

**Inward variant ("what did I do wrong?"):** The anger goes back at the self. Produces self-blame, doubt, the rumination loop. The person beats themselves up. Joe estimates 60% of people run this pattern.

**Outward variant (immediate counter-anger and rationalization):** Someone gets mad, you immediately get mad back. You rationalize and justify your position. The anger does not turn inward but it also does not move cleanly — it loops into defensive argumentation. Joe places himself in this category.

"If you're the other kind where someone gets mad at you and that immediately makes you angry, that's the other, the other form of it. I fall more into that category. Then you rationalize and you justify." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Seeking Approval

Both variants are the same underlying pattern: the original anger at the aggressor cannot move cleanly at the right target, so it goes somewhere — inward as self-blame, or outward as defensive counter-anger. Both routes require the heart to close. See [[three-forms-of-anger-repression]] for the full taxonomy.

## What Someone Experiences

From the inside, the pattern feels like normal self-reflection. The person experiences themselves as thoughtful, responsible, self-aware. They are not aware that the question "what did I do wrong?" is firing as an automatic response to aggression. They experience it as their own genuine inquiry into their behavior.

The actual experience-stream:

- Someone gets mad at the person (directly or passive-aggressively)
- The person's heart closes (often imperceptibly to the person)
- The anger that should have gone outward at the aggressor turns inward
- The question "what did I do wrong?" starts running
- The person begins listing potential offenses, real or imagined
- The mind generates rationalizations ("I shouldn't have said X, I should have said Y")
- The doubt loop continues for minutes, hours, or days
- The original aggression has been absorbed and converted into self-criticism

Most people running this pattern do not link the doubt to the triggering aggression. They experience the doubt as a free-floating self-questioning. The link to the trigger is invisible. This is what makes the pattern so persistent: the person cannot dissolve a loop they do not see has an external trigger.

## The Exposure Question

A simple diagnostic question exposes the pattern. Joe asks clients:

"How many times can you literally point at somebody who's been aggressive towards you or passive-aggressive towards you and you were like, yeah, I didn't do anything wrong, that's them?"

The client typically cannot find a single instance. Sometimes for their entire adult life. Said aloud, the absurdity surfaces: "So apparently anytime anybody's gotten mad at you, it's your fault."

The structural verdict — "apparently any time anyone got mad at me it was my fault" — exposes the pattern because no rational person believes this. The verdict was never literally believed by the conscious mind; the pattern was running below conscious thought. Once the verdict is named, the gap between what the pattern produces (always-my-fault) and what is actually true (sometimes-their-stuff) becomes visible. The visibility is the first crack in the pattern.

## The Doubt Connection

The pattern is the primary source of chronic self-doubt for many people. The doubt is not a verdict on their actual mistakes. It is the inward-routed anger from accumulated aggression-events that were never processed outward. The pattern keeps running because:

1. Aggression continues to arrive (life keeps producing aggressive people)
2. The pattern keeps routing it inward
3. Each routing adds to the doubt-substrate
4. The doubt is never resolved because it has no terminating condition (you cannot prove you did nothing wrong, especially when you did not actually do anything wrong)

See [[doubt-requires-closed-heart]] for the principle account. See [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] for the parallel mechanism applied to shame more broadly.

## The Connection to Approval-Seeking

The pattern is closely linked to approval-seeking. A child who learned to ask "what did I do wrong?" in response to parental aggression also learned to monitor the parent's state continuously, in order to prevent the aggression in the first place. The monitoring is the early form of approval-seeking. The pattern and the approval-seeking are two operations of the same protective configuration.

This is why the pattern often shows up under the presenting concern of [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]]. The person came in asking about approval-seeking. The deeper structure includes the inward-anger-routing that runs underneath. Both have to be addressed for the structure to dissolve.

## The Exit

The exit is not "stop asking what you did wrong." Asking the question is a symptom of the structure, not the structure itself. The exit is to interrupt the routing.

Three moves work together:

**Open the heart.** When the doubt-loop starts, notice it. Notice that the heart has closed. Put attention in the chest. Soften. Breathe. The doubt cannot survive the opening. With the heart open, the actual data becomes available: "they were angry; that was about them; I do not need to take it on."

**Let the anger move outward.** The original anger at the aggressor (often a parent, often long ago) has been routed inward for decades. The anger is still there. Letting it move outward — in private, on a punching bag, with a coach, in a journal — releases the substrate that has been feeding the doubt loop. See [[how-to-release-anger-out-loud]] and [[get-angry-in-the-car]].

**Install [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]].** The truth-speaking practice rewires the default mode. Instead of asking what you did wrong, you speak what is actually true for you. The act of speaking is the act of self-referencing. The self-referencing breaks the always-it-was-me protocol.

The combination dissolves the pattern. Not overnight. The pattern was installed over years. Dissolving it takes some time. The first sign of progress is usually the first instance of being able to identify that someone else was the source of the aggression — "that was their stuff, not mine." The capacity to do this even once is the wedge.

## When This Pattern Is Operating

Signs the pattern is operating:

- Chronic self-doubt that the person cannot trace to a specific cause
- A history of "successful alcoholic" parents (or any parent whose mood was unpredictable and whose aggression was directed at the child)
- A high-achiever profile combined with persistent self-criticism
- Reports of "ruminating" or "going over things in my head" after any conflict
- The inability to recall a single instance of having been wronged without it being their fault
- The presenting concern is approval-seeking, people-pleasing, or chronic doubt
- The person reports that they "always feel bad" after any interaction with an angry person

## When Someone Experiences This

From the inside:

- A free-floating sense of having done something wrong
- An inability to settle after any conflict
- Replays of the conversation looking for what you did to cause it
- A felt sense of smallness or wrongness after any interaction with an angry person
- The mind generating list after list of possible offenses
- A strong sense that if you could just figure out what you did, you could fix it
- An absence of any felt anger at the aggressor (the anger has been routed away)
