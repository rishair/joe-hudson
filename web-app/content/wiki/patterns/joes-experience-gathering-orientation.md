# Joe's Experience-Gathering Orientation in His Twenties

## What This Names

A specific orientation Joe held from roughly his late teens through his mid-to-late twenties, before the 8-second oneness experience that turned everything into "I am on a path." The orientation: collect any experience you can get into, especially the ones outside the range your background would normally produce. The goal — to the extent there was a conscious goal — was to understand humanity. The actual operative driver was wants, before those wants had been overlaid with shoulds.

This is not framed as a pattern in the negative sense Joe usually uses the word. It is a developmental period that produced the substrate Joe later coached from. Understanding this period is useful because it answers the question that many people implicitly ask about how a person comes to be able to coach across the contexts Joe coaches across.

## The Operational Description

Joe's own summary, in retrospect:

"I was constantly finding the most obscure people, places that I could and understanding them. Like, hanging out with homeless people or hanging out with billionaires. I would find these little niches. I was trying to understand humanity in any way that I could." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The specific instances he gives in one breath: hitchhiking across the country, hitchhiking to Alaska to butcher fish at a cannery (see [[joes-togiak-fish-cannery]]), driving with a Uruguayan hitchhiker into a cowboy bar in the Central Valley (see [[joes-cold-beer-friday-cowboys]]), sailing in Tahiti, living with tribes (at least 6 or 7 in America and 2 or 3 outside America), spending one day with a homeless man named Lucky who had been riding rails for 15 years and the next day with one of the Waltons. He fasted alone in an old hermit's cabin in the woods (see [[joes-woods-fast-stopped-fighting]]). He was constantly putting himself into rooms his background would not have produced.

The clothes were instruments. Joe describes his outfit in this period as deliberately neutral — bowl cut, t-shirt, pants — engineered to let him fit into the corners of any room. Not flashy enough to provoke an artist scene, not square enough to provoke a business room. The neutrality was operational. It let him enter spaces a more visible self-presentation would have closed.

## What He Was Following

Joe is explicit that there was no path in this period. He was not running a self-development curriculum. He was not collecting experiences in service of a future use. He was following his interests, which in his framing means he was following his wants — before the wants had been overlaid with shoulds.

"It's following my interests. I was following my wants, right? Before I had like a way that I had to be." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

This is the key structural point. The wants had not yet been hijacked. The internal voice telling him what he was supposed to be had not yet installed itself. Whatever drew him was what he followed. The collecting was the natural shape of the following.

The interest in this period was humanity. What is human under the cultural costumes. What is shared across every kind of person. What it is to be a person. The early intuition that he would be "a psychologist when you grow up" (something his family told him because of his observable interest in humans) had no formal expression yet. The orientation was the formal expression.

## The Self-Abusive Voice Was Running In Parallel

The clean wants-driven orientation existed at the same time as a brutal internal critic. Joe is precise about both.

The same person who was driving with a hitchhiker to a cowboy bar with open curiosity was also being mercilessly attacked by the voice in his head. There was no winning with the voice. Joe describes a moment in his first solo fast in the woods, at about 25, in an old hermit's cabin he had heard about: he sat there and watched his own brain refuse to give him a win at any move he made. The voice said "you can't fucking win. You gotta try harder." That was every day.

The juxtaposition is structurally important. People often assume that someone with an active inner critic must be paralyzed, or hidden. Joe was not. The critic ran in parallel with an unusually open and adventurous following of his wants. The critic did not collapse the wants; the wants did not silence the critic. Both were operative.

Brett asks how that worked. Joe's answer is the role he was given in the family:

"I was so lucky to be born the black sheep, to be put in the role of the black sheep. So I was the rebel, I was the one who wasn't gonna do the things that everybody was supposed to do." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

See [[black-sheep-role-as-protected-exploration]] for the developed principle.

The black-sheep role provided the structural permission to follow the wants without the family's permission being required. The rebel does not need to consult. The rebel was already not doing what was approved. Once the framework is in place that "I'm the one who doesn't do the expected thing," the cost of doing one more unexpected thing is zero. The orientation Joe lived in his twenties was downstream of the black-sheep position. It was not despite the critic; it was made possible by the rebellion the critic and the family role had jointly constructed.

## The Childhood Roots of the Curiosity Itself

The interest in humanity has at least two roots Joe names.

**First**, growing up in volatile conditions — an alcoholic father in particular — installed the survival skill of reading people very fast. The child who has to read whether the parent who just walked in is dangerous becomes very good at reading humans. The fascination outlasts the original danger and becomes a vocation.

"I don't know particularly, like I can't think of a story as to how that happened. It just was always there for me. And I think if I had to guess, it would be like trying to read my father when, like, part of survival is that when your father walks in the room, he's an alcoholic, you better understand that shit really quick or you're gonna get in trouble." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

**Second**, Joe grew up in Iran, Saudi Arabia, Wales, England, and Switzerland. The exposure to multiple cultures during formative years installed the basic intuition that what people take to be "the way things are" is mostly culture. See [[joes-international-childhood-as-curiosity-root]].

"You go to another culture and you see how you learn so much more about your own culture by seeing it in contrast to another one... And you get to see, oh, all of this stuff that's all choice. That's all culture. All of this stuff, that's humanity." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The cross-cultural exposure plus the volatile-household reading skill plus the black-sheep role together produced a young man whose orientation was: get inside as much of humanity as you can. The orientation found its operational expression in the experience-collecting twenties.

## What This Orientation Produced

The orientation produced two things that compounded later.

**First**, the substrate for cross-context coaching. Joe can coach a CEO and a recovering addict and an artist and a software engineer because he has been inside enough rooms to know what each is dealing with. The substrate is not theoretical. It is the lived consequence of years of putting himself into rooms that produced specific somatic and social literacies.

**Second**, the lived knowledge that wants come before shoulds. Joe coaches people back to their wants ([[wants-over-shoulds]]) with a particular conviction because he lived for years in a wants-first orientation before the shoulds installed themselves. The teaching is not abstract for him. He knows what it is to live from wants because he did it, in a relatively unmixed form, for most of a decade.

He also names that this is "probably really useful because I, I didn't have an agenda." The lack of agenda was the protective container around the experience-collection. The moment an agenda installs itself, the collection becomes curated and the wants get filtered through the agenda. The pre-agenda collection had a different quality. It got into rooms the agenda would have ruled out.

## When This Pattern Comes Up in Coaching

This pattern matters for the coaching work in several places.

**For a young person trying to find their purpose:** the orientation Joe describes is the orientation the work points back toward. The person who is grinding through productivity hacks looking for purpose is typically several layers of should removed from their wants. The corrective is to drop into what would actually be interesting to them right now, regardless of whether it is on the path to anything. See [[i-cant-find-my-purpose]] and [[searching-for-purpose-pushes-it-away]].

**For a person who has been doing the path-and-self-development work for a long time:** Joe's pre-path period is the reminder that the orientation came before the path. The path was not the source of the orientation. The path was a later overlay on a wants-following that had been operative for years before any path was visible. People who have been "on the path" for a long time can lose contact with the pre-path wants the path was supposed to serve.

**For a coach or facilitator developing their range:** the substrate Joe coaches from was built by years of putting himself into rooms his background would not have produced. There is no shortcut to this. The breadth of contexts a coach can hold is set by the breadth of contexts they have been inside of.

**For a person who is too rule-following to follow their wants:** Joe's black-sheep position made the experience-collection possible. The person who is the obedient one in the family does not have the structural permission Joe had. Part of the work is recovering the permission. See [[black-sheep-role-as-protected-exploration]] for the role-structure analysis.

## Tone

Joe tells this in retrospect with affection and slight amusement. He is not glamorizing the period. He is describing what produced who he became. The voice is one of looking back at a younger self with recognition rather than judgment.
