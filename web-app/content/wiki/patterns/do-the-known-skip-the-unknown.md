# Do the Known, Skip the Unknown

## What This Pattern Is

A recurring procrastination signature visible across business, creative, and personal projects: people do everything they already know how to do, then procrastinate on the parts they don't know how to do. The unknown step gets postponed, often indefinitely. Unfinished projects almost always have an unknown step somewhere in the middle of their plan, and the project has stopped at that step.

The pattern is structurally invisible from inside because the person can point to all the work they have done. They have been busy. They have made progress. They just haven't done the specific one thing they don't know how to do — which is exactly the thing the project actually needs.

> "How about — or you see it in people where they'll do everything they know how to do and then they'll do the thing they don't know how to do last, if they do it at all." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

## The Canonical Cases

**The tech CEO and sales.** A technical founder who knows how to build product moves into the CEO seat. Sales and marketing are new territory. The technical founder builds and rebuilds the product, optimizes the architecture, refines the code — and never moves into sales. The company runs out of runway because the part of the work that would have brought in customers was the part the CEO didn't know how to do.

> "Typically what I see is a tech CEO who's really tech-oriented never move into sales. And it can kill companies. Some companies really require that sales or marketing, and it'll kill the company because that's the thing they didn't know that they didn't feel good about, rather than doing that first, which is far more successful because then you know what the customer actually wants because you've tried to sell them it, and then you're building something that's for the customer instead of something that you thought was a good idea for the customer." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The structural failure mode: the product gets built for an imagined customer rather than the actual customer, because the conversation with the actual customer (which would have required learning sales) never happened. The procrastination on the unknown task is what produces the wrong product. The cost of avoiding the discomfort of not-knowing is much higher than the cost would have been to just learn the unknown step.

**The pitch deck with the skipped slide.** Brett's example: pitch decks where the founder shows the problem, the solution, the market, the traction — and skips the step where the solution actually delivers the value. "Gosh, and voilà." The hard part — the part the founder doesn't know how to make work yet — gets glossed over.

> "You see so many pitch decks where it's just like, gosh, and voilà. And you're like, but what about this step right there?" -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now (Brett, naming the case)

The pitch deck is the visible artifact of the pattern. The investor reading it sees the gap. The founder writing it didn't because they were doing what they knew how to do (laying out the narrative) and not addressing what they didn't (proving the mechanism).

**The unfinished side project.** Most personal projects that have been "in progress" for years are stuck at a specific step the person doesn't know how to do. The website where everything is built except the payment integration. The book where every chapter is drafted except the one that requires research the writer hasn't done. The course where every module is recorded except the one that requires teaching a subject the creator hasn't mastered.

> "It makes me wonder how like how many unfinished projects is this the, what's happening behind the scenes, right? Is that people did everything they knew how to do up until the point where they got to the thing that they didn't know, and then they procrastinate on that thing and the project just sits undone." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now (Brett, generalizing)

## Why This Happens

The pattern is a special case of the [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] mechanism plus the [[the-perfectionism-that-stops-you-from-starting]] mechanism applied specifically to unknown territory. The unknown step carries:

- **High importance.** It is often the step that determines whether the project works.
- **No felt competence.** The person has no prior experience that says they can do it.
- **High visibility of incompetence.** Doing the unknown step badly is more obvious than doing a familiar step badly, because the person has no muscle for hiding the gaps.
- **No standard for "done."** The person doesn't know what good looks like in the unknown domain, so they can't tell when they've finished.

All of this loads the unknown step with conditions that produce procrastination. The known steps don't carry these loads, so they get done. The unknown step carries all of them, so it gets postponed.

The procrastination is, in this case, structurally correct in the sense that it accurately signals the person doesn't know how to do this thing. The wisdom in the procrastination is "you need to learn something or you need to bring in someone who knows." See [[procrastination-is-wisdom]]. The failure mode is when the procrastination is treated as a personal defect ("I'm lazy") rather than as the signal it actually is ("there is an unknown step that needs the unknown to be addressed").

## The Inversion: Do the Unknown First

The principle that addresses this pattern, which Joe and Brett name as far more successful: **do the unknown thing first.** The reasoning is structural:

> "Far more successful because then you know what the customer actually wants because you've tried to sell them it, and then you're building something that's for the customer instead of something that you thought was a good idea for the customer." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

Two compounding benefits:

**1. You learn what you don't know.** The unknown step turns into a known step after you do it. The rest of the project then assembles around accurate information rather than around your guess at what the unknown step would have produced.

**2. You discover whether the rest of the project even makes sense.** Often, doing the unknown step first reveals that the rest of the project needs to change. The product is wrong for the customer. The book is for the wrong audience. The course is the wrong shape. Better to discover this before building everything around the guess than after.

This connects directly to [[mit-media-lab-principle]] — go to what you know least about, do the simplest experiment to learn the most about it. The MIT principle is the formal statement of the inversion. The do-the-known-skip-the-unknown pattern is what the MIT principle is correcting.

It also connects to [[first-experiments-should-contradict-assumptions]] — the unknown step is often the place where the project's assumptions are most likely to be wrong. Doing it first surfaces those wrong assumptions while they're still cheap to fix.

## What Coaching Does With This

When the pattern is visible in a project, the diagnostic question is direct: **what is the part of this you don't know how to do?** The answer often surprises the person. They have been so busy with the known parts that they have not noticed they are circling the unknown.

The intervention follows the diagnostic:

- **Name the unknown step explicitly.** "What is the part of this project you don't know how to do?" Get the specific step on the table.
- **Validate that this is the actual procrastination.** The pattern is structural, not personal. The person is not lazy. They are doing what almost everyone does when faced with an unknown step.
- **Apply [[mit-media-lab-principle]].** Move the unknown step to the front. Do the simplest experiment that would reveal what the unknown actually is.
- **Reframe the project around the experiment.** The project's first deliverable becomes "learn what we don't know," not "complete the next known step."

The coaching often has to also address the discomfort of being a beginner at the unknown step. The person who has been a competent expert at the known parts will have an ego-cost to being incompetent at the unknown part. That cost is real but tolerable; the cost of never doing the unknown step is much larger.

## The Organizational Variant

When this pattern operates at the team level rather than the individual level, it produces a specific failure mode: the team's roadmap becomes a list of things everyone on the team knows how to do, with the genuinely hard part either implicit ("and then we figure out distribution") or assigned to no one. The hard part stays unaddressed until a competitor figures it out first, or the runway ends, or the team realizes too late that the product they built is for a customer that doesn't exist.

Leaders who recognize this pattern in their teams can intervene by making the unknown step the first explicit work item, often staffed by the most senior person available, with a budget for learning rather than for execution. The team's culture has to support being a beginner at the unknown step; otherwise no one on the team will volunteer.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They have a project that has been "almost done" for months or years
- They can list everything they've accomplished on the project but the project doesn't actually work yet
- They notice that their work on the project always seems to happen in the parts they already know
- Their pitch deck or business plan has a step that gets glossed over
- They are a technical founder who has built product but never sold any
- They are a creator who has built everything except the part that requires the skill they don't have
- They have hired someone to do the unknown step and that person isn't moving either (the hiring was its own form of procrastination)

## Tone

Diagnostic, slightly amused. Joe and Brett describe this pattern with the recognition that comes from seeing it repeatedly in companies and projects. There is no moralizing — the pattern is structural, not a personal failing — but there is a clear naming of the cost. Companies die from this. Projects sit unfinished for years. The tone carries the seriousness of the cost without blame on the person who is in the pattern.
