# The Deepening Stage of a Relationship Can Feel Like Things Are Getting Worse

## The Pattern

As a relationship goes deeper, more trauma surfaces in both partners. The deeper the love available, the more material the body offers up to be tested by it. From the inside, this can register as the relationship getting worse. From the structural view, it is the relationship doing exactly what it was set up to do.

The pattern has a recognizable shape. The early phase of the relationship felt easy; the partner saw the best in you and you saw the best in them. As the relationship deepens, the partner starts seeing the parts you had been hiding. They start seeing you as your "avoidant dad" or your "controlling mom" or your "project to fix." Each of these reads, from the inside, feels like the relationship has gotten worse. The partner used to love you and now they see you as something broken.

Brett names the pattern precisely:

"There's a way that this can happen where you get deeper into a relationship and you find more and more love for one another. And it seems like things are getting worse on some level. You know, like, like you used to see me as like, I couldn't do anything wrong and you loved all of my quirks. And now you see me as this, like, as your avoidant dad, or you see me as something to fix, like some problem, like I'm just a project." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

The intellectual or emotional read: things are getting worse. The structural reality: you are moving from fighting about dishes to fighting about what is actually real in the relationship, to surfacing the fight that never got to be had in your early life, to finally finding a place where that can be seen and loved.

"You're going from talking about the dishes, from fighting about the dishes, to fighting about what's actually real in your relationship, to actually surfacing the fight that never got to be had in your early life and finally, like, finding a place, a relationship where that can be seen and actually loved in the way that it needs to be loved." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win

Joe agrees with this reading and adds the mechanism.

## The Mechanism: Love Surfaces More Material to Be Tested

When deeper love is available, the body responds by offering up the deeper, more guarded material to see if the love can hold it. This is not the partner being cruel or the relationship failing. It is the trauma seeking the loving witness it never had.

"When you are deeply loving the other person, it is natural that more of their trauma will come up to test it, to see if that love is real." -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight And Win (summarizing what Joe has said)

Joe affirms:

"Sometimes you show up in that loving presence and exactly what does exactly what's supposed to do, it brings all that shit to the surface to be loved, which is the trauma because they're in their trauma and they're like, 'Oh, there's love. I can, I'm safe enough to go into my trauma and be loved here.' And I, and I'm testing it. Both of those things can be true." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The testing has two flavors that are usually both present:

1. **The body is offering up material to see if it can be loved.** The shame, the hidden parts, the held-back truths, the parts that were never welcomed -- they come up because the conditions for them to be welcomed are finally present.

2. **The mind is poking to see if the love is real.** "Are you really gonna fucking love me? Fuck you. You're not gonna fucking love me. Fuck you." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win. The poking is the protective layer testing the safety before the deeper layer trusts it.

Both register on the surface as "the relationship is getting worse." Both are, structurally, the relationship doing the work it was set up to do.

## Why It Feels Like Regression

The misread happens because the lived experience of this stage is unmistakably harder than the prior stage. The fights are about deeper material. The shame in the room is stronger. The trauma is more present. The partner's view of you has shifted from idealized to seen-with-warts.

The internal narration goes: "We used to be so good together. Now it's hard. Therefore something is broken."

The structural reading: "We used to be in surface contact. Now we are in real contact. Therefore the trauma that was hidden in the surface contact has come up to be met. The hardness is the realness."

This is the inverse of the [[late-stage-reversion]] pattern. Late-stage reversion is hard material returning unexpectedly after a long period of ease. The deepening pattern described here is the same dynamic seen structurally rather than as an aberration. The reversion is not a return of old material; it is the next layer of material becoming available because the prior layer has been integrated.

## The Marathon Metaphor

Joe uses a precise image for the pattern:

"It's very much as if somebody is running a long marathon race and they're saying, 'I am tired.' It's like, yeah, that's what happens when you're running a marathon race. That's a good, that's good. That means you're on mile 24, you know, that it means you're, you're making progress. You're there. And so it's very, it's a very similar thing that there's a, there are certain things that are challenging in the fighting that are progress and you can't see it until afterwards." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The tiredness at mile 24 of a marathon is the marker that you are at mile 24. It is also the most painful part of the race. Both are true. The pain does not mean you are failing; the pain is the form the success takes at that stage.

The same logic applies to the deepening relationship. The hardness of the deepening stage does not mean the relationship is failing. The hardness is the form the success takes at that stage. The recognition is not available in the during-it experience; it is available afterwards.

## What Joe Does With This Recognition When Coaching

When a couple comes to coaching distraught about a recent incident, and Joe can see they are in the deepening stage rather than the breaking stage, his internal response is "good."

"I'm working with a couple, or I, I, I'm working with one person in a couple, and they tell me something that happened in the relationship, and they're so distraught, and I'm like, 'Oh good,' I cannot tell you, because I see the progress that they're making, even though it feels like shit in that moment." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The "oh good" is not callous. It is the coach recognizing the marathon-mile-24 quality of what the couple is reporting. The work is to help them see what they cannot see from inside the stage: this is progress, even though it feels like collapse.

The coaching move is not to dismiss the difficulty. It is to reframe the meaning of the difficulty so they can stay in the work rather than concluding the relationship is broken.

## The Connection to the Six-Month Vicious Fights

The clearest example of this pattern in long-form is [[tara-fights-that-destroyed-joes-narcissism]]. Joe and Tara had years of good fighting, then hit a six-month period of vicious fights that felt like total regression. The during-it experience was "maybe the marriage is over." The retrospective recognition, years later, was that those fights destroyed a subtle narcissism Joe had been carrying that no other period of work had been able to touch.

The pattern was the same as Brett describes for shorter timescales. Deeper integration required harder surfacing. The hardness was the integration, not the failure of the integration.

## What This Is Not

This is not a claim that every period of difficulty in a relationship is the deepening stage. Some difficulty is just damage. Some is the [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] pattern. Some is genuine incompatibility surfacing. The deepening read applies when:

- Both partners are committed to the work
- The capacity to stay loving while difficult material surfaces is present (even if intermittent)
- The fights are producing learning, even when slowly
- The partners are integrating, not just recycling

If those conditions are absent, the difficulty is not the deepening pattern; it is something else.

This is not a license to ignore the felt experience. The hardness is real. Naming the structural meaning of the hardness does not make it stop being hard. The coaching is "stay in this, the meaning will become legible later" -- not "this is not actually hard."

## When Someone Experiences This

- A long-term relationship where the early ease has been replaced by difficult fights about deeper material
- The recognition that the partner sees parts of you that were hidden before, and the awareness that this changes the relationship
- A period after significant work where things have gotten unexpectedly hard
- A couple presenting in coaching distraught about a recent incident that, on examination, was actually surfacing material that had been hidden
- The internal recognition that the version of yourself the partner used to see has been replaced by a more accurate, harder-to-hold version

## Tone

Joe says "oh good" with energy when describing his response to couples in this stage. The tone matters. The teaching includes the practitioner's actual recognition that this stage is productive, not just an intellectual claim that it should be reframed as such.

The marathon image is delivered as a steady analogy rather than a flourish. The everyday quality of "yeah, that's what happens at mile 24" carries the de-dramatization of the difficulty.
