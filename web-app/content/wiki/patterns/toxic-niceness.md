# Toxic Niceness in Company Culture

## The Pattern

A company culture where people walk on eggshells around each other, do not want to talk about the hard thing, and substitute politeness for the actual conversation. The culture looks pleasant from the outside. The team gets along. Meetings are calm. There is no visible conflict. The pattern produces, over time, a company that cannot make hard decisions, cannot give real feedback, cannot ship hard truths to customers, and cannot survive a downturn.

The name "toxic niceness" captures the diagnosis: the niceness is not the absence of toxicity, it is itself the toxicity. The pleasantness is what is preventing the work that would produce a functional company.

"Back then, we had -- there was too much of, I would say, like toxic niceness in a way in the company. So walking on eggshells, you know, not wanting to talk about the hard thing. Money was so free and cheap, you know, before that, coming out of COVID. So there was a lot of it just like, you know, works." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

This is the company-scale version of [[nice-vs-kind-from-open-heart]]. The same dynamic that makes a "very nice neighborhood" the place where passive aggression breeds also makes a "very nice company" the place where unaddressed tension compounds. Niceness is not the same as kindness. At scale, niceness becomes the protective coating over the things the team is not willing to face.

## How It Forms

Toxic niceness is usually a downstream effect of conditions that made avoiding hard conversations easier than having them.

**Easy money.** When capital is abundant, the cost of avoiding a hard conversation is low. The team that should be replaced gets kept because there is no pressure to make the call. The strategy that is not working gets continued because no one is forced to confront its underperformance. The customer that is dragging on the team gets retained because the revenue covers the drag. The conditions that should be producing the hard conversations are smoothed over by the abundance.

**Growth obscuring dysfunction.** Companies in rapid growth often look healthy when they are not. The growth covers the operational dysfunctions, the trust failures, the strategic confusion. Everyone is busy enough that no one notices what is being avoided. The niceness becomes the working hypothesis: we are growing, so we must be fine, so there is no need to have the conversation.

**Conflict-avoidant founders.** A leadership team that is itself conflict-avoidant produces a culture in its own image. See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]] and [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]]. The patterns the leader runs internally become the patterns the company runs externally. A founder who avoids hard conversations at home and with friends will avoid them in the company too, and the team will calibrate to the leader.

**HR-orthodoxy weaponization.** Modern HR conventions that emphasize "psychological safety" and "compassionate communication" can be misread as "do not have hard conversations." The original concepts were about making it safe to have hard conversations. The lived practice in many companies has become the opposite: the absence of hard conversations gets relabeled as psychological safety. The misreading produces the toxic-niceness pattern.

## How It Manifests

The pattern shows up in observable behaviors:

- **Feedback in meetings is generic and laudatory.** Real critique only happens in private channels, often as gossip rather than as named feedback.
- **The team never disagrees in front of the CEO.** When the CEO leaves the room, the team has the real conversation. When the CEO is present, everyone agrees.
- **Performance issues are tolerated for years.** The person everyone knows is not performing keeps their role because no one is willing to have the conversation.
- **Strategic pivots are deferred past the point they should have been made.** The company keeps building the thing that is not working because the conversation about stopping it would be hard.
- **Customer pushback is not surfaced to the product team.** Sales softens the customer feedback. Product hears that customers love the product right up until churn surges.
- **The board pack is sanitized.** The board sees the version of the company the CEO is comfortable showing. The hard realities are not in the deck.
- **New hires are confused by the gap between what is said and what is done.** They name the gap, get a soft response, and within months they have either left or learned to perform the same niceness.

## Why It Is Toxic Rather Than Just Inefficient

The niceness is not just slowing the company down. It is actively destroying the trust the company runs on. See [[trust-requires-conflict]]. Trust is built by going through hard conversations together and emerging with the relationship intact. The company that avoids hard conversations cannot build the trust that lets it survive when the conversations are no longer avoidable.

When the downturn comes -- and it always comes -- the toxic-niceness company has no infrastructure for the conversations it now has to have. The layoff conversation, the strategic pivot conversation, the underperformance conversation, the customer-loss conversation. Each one is a first. Each one ruptures relationships that were never tested. The company that survives the downturn is the one that had been having those conversations all along; the toxic-niceness company often does not survive it.

The pattern also degrades the quality of the people the company can keep. The strongest performers want feedback, want truth, want the opportunity to be challenged and to grow. A toxic-niceness culture starves them of all three. They leave. The people who stay are the ones who are comfortable in the niceness, which is often the people who are not strong enough to want the alternative. The talent base erodes structurally.

## The Diagnostic

Several signals indicate the pattern is present:

- The CEO cannot name three things that are currently failing in the company without having to think hard
- The leadership team has not had a conflict in front of each other in the last 90 days
- The performance review process is producing mostly "exceeds expectations" ratings
- The team's exit interviews mention "lack of clarity" or "lack of feedback" repeatedly
- The board is consistently surprised by quarterly results
- The CEO finds out about customer churn from the metrics rather than from the team
- The CEO can name the people who should probably not be at the company but they are all still there

Each individual signal can have other explanations. The cluster, when present together, is the pattern.

## What Breaks the Pattern

The pattern breaks when a specific event forces a hard conversation that the company has been avoiding, and the leadership chooses to use the event as the moment to install a new norm. The layoff. The strategic pivot. The major customer loss. The investor passing on the round. Each crisis is an opportunity to either confirm the toxic-niceness pattern (by softening the conversation the crisis demands) or to break it (by having the conversation cleanly and naming what the avoidance was costing).

Johannes's company broke the pattern through a 30%-layoff event. The layoff itself forced the conversation about strategic clarity. The email that communicated the layoff was written from an empowered place (see [[write-the-email-from-empowered]]) rather than a defensive one. Out of that crisis came an operating principle (Open the Drawer -- see [[open-the-drawer-as-shared-verb]]) that converted the new norm into a daily practice.

"So back then, we had -- there was too much of, I would say, like toxic niceness in a way in the company. So walking on eggshells, you know, not wanting to talk about the hard thing. Money was so free and cheap, you know, before that, coming out of COVID. So there was a lot of it just like, you know, works. And we had a lot of that walking on eggshells in our culture. So out of that very, very difficult decision came the operating principle of Open the Drawer, which is about embracing transparency and embracing also intensity ultimately." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles

The crisis is not optional, but the use of the crisis to install a new norm is optional. Companies that go through layoffs without breaking the toxic-niceness pattern emerge with the same culture, just smaller. Companies that use the layoff as a structural reset come out with a different culture. The leadership response in the crisis moment is the determining variable.

## What This Pattern Is Not

This is not the same as the leader who runs a high-conflict culture and calls it "directness." There are companies whose dysfunction is the inverse pattern -- shouting, blame, fear -- and those companies have their own pathologies (see related patterns on cultures of fear). Toxic niceness is the specific pattern of avoidance; the inverse is the specific pattern of weaponized intensity. Both are dysfunctional. Both are downstream of the leader's own patterns.

This is also not a critique of kindness. Kindness from an open heart is not the same as politeness as protection. See [[nice-vs-kind-from-open-heart]]. The healthy company is kind and direct. The toxic-niceness company is polite but not kind, because politeness is not protecting the recipient -- it is protecting the speaker from the discomfort of saying what is true.

## When Someone Experiences This

You are a CEO who realizes you have not given anyone real feedback in months and the team's performance is sliding. You are an executive who has been avoiding a conversation with a peer for so long that the workaround you built to avoid them has become a department. You are a new hire wondering why everyone agrees in the meeting and then complains afterward. You are a team that just lost a key customer and no one knows what to say about it because the avoidance pattern is so embedded that even the postmortem is performative. You are a board member who only finds out about problems when they have become crises because the CEO has not built the muscle to surface them earlier.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A company is described as having a "pleasant culture" but underperforming
- A CEO is asking why their team will not give them real feedback
- A leader is wondering why performance issues are not being addressed
- A company that just received a hard outside reality (a downturn, a layoff, a major loss) is asking what they need to change
- The team's exit interviews mention "lack of clarity" or "no real feedback" as the reason for leaving
- A founder is asking why their company seems healthy but is not making decisions fast enough
