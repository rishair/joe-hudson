# Highly Logical People Are More Logically Inconsistent

## The Pattern

The folk wisdom is that highly logical, highly intellectual people are more rigorous in their thinking than ordinary people, less prone to contradicting themselves, more careful with their premises. The pattern Joe names runs the other way. Highly logical people are often *more* logically inconsistent than people who are not highly intellectual, and they have *less* capacity to see the inconsistency in themselves.

"People who are highly logical, very, very thoughtful people, often are more logically inconsistent than people who aren't highly intellectual, and they have less of a capacity to see it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself

The pattern is structural, not a knock on smart people. It explains a recurring puzzle: why deeply intelligent people can produce arguments that, on inspection, do not add up — and produce them with full conviction, and not register the gaps.

## The Mechanism

Joe's working theory: highly intellectual people have a harder time emotionally feeling the inconsistency in their own thinking. Logic, when held purely in the head, has no internal alarm for self-contradiction. The alarm for self-contradiction lives in the body — a slight discomfort, a wince, a felt sense of "wait, that doesn't fit." When the head has been doing the work for years without the body as a check, the alarm is offline.

The intellectual believes the conviction is the proof. The body has been suppressed long enough that it no longer fires when the premises clash. The head has become very skilled at producing arguments that feel coherent from the inside even when they are not coherent from the outside.

This connects to [[head-heart-gut]]: when the head intelligence has been enthroned and the other intelligences exiled, the head loses its checks. The other intelligences were not just secondary contributors. They were the ground truth that kept the head honest. Without them, the head can spin elaborate constructions that are sealed off from any reality test.

## How It Plays Out: Joe's CEO Example

Joe describes working with a highly logical CEO who would make great-sounding arguments. Joe would buy into the arguments. Then later he would look back and realize: "that shit didn't make any sense. That shit didn't make any sense at all."

The CEO was not being deceptive. The CEO was fully convinced. The conviction was the structural feature: very convicted to the thought, deeply believed it. From the inside, the argument was airtight. From the outside, with time and distance, it was internally inconsistent.

What made Joe buy in was not the logic of the argument. It was the empathic merger. Joe was in the CEO, not with the CEO. See [[being-with-vs-being-in]]. The CEO's conviction transferred to Joe via empathy. Joe felt the conviction as conviction. The argument did not have to actually hold up; the feeling of it holding up was sufficient for the listener who was inside the speaker.

"I would just buy into these arguments that they were making. And then one day I'm like, wait a second, that shit didn't make any sense." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself

This is the operational mechanism by which highly logical, highly convicted speakers move audiences without producing actually-coherent arguments. The audience is in them, feeling the conviction, and the argument's actual structure does not get processed.

## The News and Public Commentary Application

The pattern is visible in public discourse. Highly emphatic, highly logical speakers — commentators, pundits, public intellectuals — produce arguments that have visible holes, and yet they sweep audiences. The audience is not processing the argument. The audience is being in the speaker, riding the conviction, feeling the certainty as their own.

"You can see it making these completely irrational [arguments] — but everybody's buying in because they're empathetically — they bought in. They're in the other person." -- Joe Hudson, How To Be Empathetic Without Losing Yourself

The corrective is not "fact-check more." Fact-checking happens in the head, and the head has already been bypassed by the empathic transfer of conviction. The corrective is to be with the speaker rather than in them. The body has to stay home. The check is not "is this argument true" but "is this conviction in me actually mine, or is it borrowed from the speaker I am inside of?" See [[is-this-mine]] for the form of that check.

## Detection Cues for the Pattern in Yourself

- You walk away from a conversation with a highly intelligent, highly convicted person feeling fully persuaded, and the persuasion does not survive 24 hours.
- You find yourself agreeing with someone whose argument, when you reconstruct it later, has gaps.
- You consistently feel persuaded by a particular speaker (a CEO, a pundit, a thought leader) and consistently find yourself walking back the agreement later.
- The person you were just persuaded by is also someone whose other arguments have not held up over time, but in the moment the conviction always works on you.
- You can tell intellectually that the argument has issues but you are still buying it. The buy-in is happening at the level of feeling, not at the level of evaluation.

## Detection Cues for the Pattern in Others

- The person produces highly fluent, highly confident arguments and rarely qualifies.
- The person experiences disagreement as a failure of the disagreer to follow the logic, never as a possible inconsistency in their own thinking.
- The person's positions across topics, viewed together, contain contradictions that the person does not register.
- The person has a particular emotional flavor of certainty that does not move when premises are challenged.
- The person's body is rigid when they are making the argument — jaw locked, shoulders forward, no give in the face. The argument is being held with the body as well as with the head, and the body's rigidity is what is keeping the inconsistency out of view.
- When the person hits an inconsistency in conversation, they do not slow down. They produce a bridge sentence that papers over the gap without registering that a gap was crossed.

## What This Implies for Coaching

If the person being coached is the highly logical type with this pattern, the work is not "find their argument and correct it." The work is to get them out of the head and into the body, where the inconsistency-alarm lives. Until the body is back online, the head will keep generating coherent-feeling arguments that are not coherent.

Approaches:
- Move them into the body. See [[staying-in-your-body]]. Body attention is the install path for the alarm.
- Ask them what they are feeling, not what they think. The body data is what they have been suppressing; surfacing it surfaces the inconsistency.
- Name the conviction without engaging with the content. "You are very convicted about this" is different from "you are right about this." The naming returns the conviction to them as a phenomenon to look at, rather than as an argument to be inside of.
- Notice the bridge sentences. When the person papers over a gap, the bridge sentence is the diagnostic. Mark it gently and let them feel the gap.

If you are the listener at risk of buying in, the work is to put attention in your own body while listening. See [[being-with-vs-being-in]] and [[is-this-mine]]. The body anchor is what prevents the empathic transfer of unearned conviction.

## What This Pattern Does Not Mean

The pattern is not "smart people are wrong." Highly logical people are often right about a lot, and the head intelligence is real and valuable. The pattern names a specific failure mode: when the head has been operating without the other intelligences as checks, it becomes capable of producing convicted-but-inconsistent arguments and not seeing the inconsistency. The corrective is integration, not anti-intellectualism. See [[head-heart-gut]] for the integrated frame.

The pattern also does not mean less intellectual people are more consistent. They have their own failure modes — emotion-driven reasoning, in-group bias, magical thinking. The specific claim is narrower: that the head-only person's confidence in their own consistency is structurally overestimated, because the alarm system for inconsistency is not in the head.
