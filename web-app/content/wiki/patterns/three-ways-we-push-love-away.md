# The Three Ways We Push Love Away

## The Pattern

All of the specific forms in which a person pushes love away — jealousy, neediness, hyper-independence, transactionality, the checklist, the not-enough frame, the constant fights, the cold shoulder — collapse into three categories. A person can run more than one. Most people run a primary with a secondary. The three categories:

1. **Chase.** Reach for it, beg for it, work for it, perform for it.
2. **Avoid.** Pull away from it, refuse it, brace against it, treat it as a threat to the self.
3. **Fight.** Battle it, defend against it, power-struggle with it, treat it as something to be won or lost.

"So that's how we do it. We're either avoiding it, we're either chasing it, or we're defending/fighting." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

Each is governed by [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]]. Each is shaped by [[love-misconceptions-inherited-from-childhood]] — by whatever specific texture the early caregiver pairings installed. Each succeeds at its actual job, which is preventing love from arriving in the form the body recognizes as dangerous.

This is the same family-of-forms cataloged in [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]], collapsed to its three structural axes. The push-pull article inventories the specific costumes (jealousy, neediness, hyper-independence, transactionality, the checklist). This article inventories the three underlying postures the costumes are variations of.

## Chase

The chase posture is reach-for-it: I really want you, I'll work for you, I'll perform for you, I'll need you. The chasing produces the push-away because chasing love signals two things to the partner: I do not deserve love (why else would I have to chase?) and I am not loving myself (I am abandoning myself to get you).

"If I get really needy with you and I'm like, oh, will you like me? Would you? I mean, I think you're just the greatest thing ever. Like, something in us is just like, yuck, I don't want that, right? I don't want to take care of you. I don't want to have a loving relationship with you that requires me to abandon myself to take care of you." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

**Specific behavioral forms the chase takes:**
- Looking for praise: needing the partner to constantly affirm
- Demanding time: not feeling good unless I'm getting your attention
- Performing: showing only the version that I think you'll love
- Neediness: a perpetual reaching-for that exhausts the partner
- Self-abandonment to please: doing what you want so you'll stay

**What it signals.** Chasing sends the signal that the chaser does not deserve love. "Why would you have to chase it?" The chasing also signals that the chaser is not loving themselves — and there is something in everyone that recognizes "there's no sense in loving somebody who doesn't love themselves." This intuition is why the chase pushes love away even when nothing visibly wrong is happening.

**The childhood imprint.** The chaser is usually someone whose childhood love had to be earned, performed for, or fought for. Love was not stable; it was contingent. The chase is the trained move that the body learned was the way love came. As an adult, the body keeps running the trained move. See [[love-pattern-mimics-childhood-love-pattern]].

## Avoid

The avoid posture is back-away: if I let this in, everything's going to break. I'll lose control. I'll lose myself. I'll open Pandora's box. The avoidance produces the push-away because avoidance is literally the push.

"If I let this in, everything's gonna, like, I'm gonna lose control. It's gonna be, I'm gonna lose myself. I'm gonna lose control. I'm gonna open up a door. I'm gonna open up Pandora's box. That's the feeling inside of that." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

**Specific behavioral forms the avoid takes:**
- Avoiding domination: every time someone loves me it comes with control, so I refuse the love to refuse the control
- Avoiding smothering: I was loved past the point of having an identity, so I treat closeness as engulfment
- Avoiding obligation: love means I'll have to return it, perform for it, owe something, so I refuse it
- Hyper-independence: needing nothing from anyone as a way of needing nothing from anyone
- Disappearing: physically or emotionally exiting at the moment of contact

**What it signals.** The avoider feels they are protecting their identity. The partner feels they are being refused. The partner reads the back-away as departure and departs. The avoider often does not see this; from inside, the back-away feels like self-preservation rather than push-away.

**The childhood imprint.** The avoider's childhood love often came with engulfment, domination, or loss-of-self. The parent's love required the child to disappear into the parent's needs. The adult body now treats love as a threat to identity. See [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]].

The avoider also wants love. The wanting is not absent; it is hidden under the avoiding. "That person wants love. They really do, even though they're running away from it, they're saying, oh, I really want love, but I'm so scared that I will lose myself in it, that I'll be dominated in it. I have to resist your love or I'm going to go away, I'm going to dissolve."

## Fight

The fight posture is defend-against-it: I have to defend myself, I have to get what I want, I can't buckle into this other person. From the inside it feels like defense. From the outside it reads as a fight. "Okay, let's go."

The fighting produces the push-away because what reads as defense to the fighter reads as combat to the partner. The relationship becomes a power struggle. Both parties brace. Love cannot land in a configuration where every contact is a contest.

"What you're really doing is you're replicating that when you were a kid, love came with a fight. There was always a fight." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

**Specific behavioral forms the fight takes:**
- Constant arguments with the partner about everything
- Power struggles in workplaces, friendships, family
- Picking fights with people who are kind to you
- The "let's go" defensive posture at any disagreement
- Parents in chronic power struggles with small children (see [[parenting-from-power-struggle-replicates-the-love-came-with-fight-imprint]])

**What it signals.** The fighter is fighting to be seen, to be loved. The fighting is the trained move from a childhood where being seen and being loved came through combat. The partner does not get the love through the fight; the partner gets compliance, rebellion, or exit. None of these is love.

"He's not going to get love. He might get compliance, he might get rebellion, but he's not going to get love." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Want Most

**The childhood imprint.** The fighter's childhood love came through power struggles. The parent and child fought; the fights were the way the contact happened; love and fight got wired together. The adult body keeps reaching for love by reaching for the fight. See [[love-pattern-mimics-childhood-love-pattern]].

## Why the Categories Hold

The three categories are not arbitrary. They are the three available structural responses to a love that was paired with something dangerous. The body can move toward (chase), move away (avoid), or stand and resist (fight). There is no fourth basic stance; everything else is one of these three in a specific costume.

The forms within each category are interchangeable. The jealous person and the needy person and the constantly-checking-in person are all running the chase posture in different costumes. The smotherer-avoider and the dominator-avoider and the obligation-avoider are all running the avoid posture in different costumes. The constantly-arguing partner and the power-struggling parent and the rebel-against-authority person are all running the fight posture in different costumes.

The diagnostic move with a person is not which costume — the costume is the surface. The diagnostic is which of the three structural postures is operating, and that points to what was wired with love in childhood.

## Multiple Categories at Once

Most people run more than one. A person may primarily chase but fight when the chase fails. A person may primarily avoid but chase when abandonment is imminent. A person may fight in romantic contexts and avoid in friendships. The configurations are not exclusive.

This is consistent with the multiple-form reality cataloged in [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]]: most people run multiple forms at once. The three-category collapse just makes the structural variants visible at a higher level. The same client running jealousy and the checklist is running chase plus avoid in two different domains.

The chaser-and-love-remover symmetry in [[chaser-and-love-remover-both-experience-death]] is one common cross-posture pairing — chase paired with avoid, where the avoider experiences the chase as death and the chaser experiences the avoid as death. Both stances are real; both serve the same function of preventing the configuration where love would actually land.

## Where the Three Show Up Inside a Person

The three categories also operate inside the person, not just toward partners. Negative self-talk runs the same three postures toward the self:

- **Self-chase:** "I should be more X, I need to be Y, I have to fix Z" — the same neediness directed inward
- **Self-avoid:** numbing, distraction, dissociation, refusing to look at oneself
- **Self-fight:** self-criticism, self-punishment, self-attack, the internal power struggle with the inner critic

The way a person treats themselves is the way they were taught love was. See [[negative-self-talk-reveals-love-imprint]] for the diagnostic mapping that uses self-talk to surface the wired pattern.

This also means the work to stop pushing love away externally is the same work as the work to stop pushing love away internally. The configuration is the same. The address of the work is the felt experience, not the partner.

## The Coaching Move

When a client describes a recurring relationship pattern, listen for which of the three is the primary posture. Not which specific behavior — which underlying stance. The client may describe jealousy, neediness, and over-giving (all chase), or describe smothering, obligation, and disappearing (all avoid), or describe constant fights, defensiveness, and power struggles (all fight). The three-category map gives the structural read.

Then the next move is to surface the wired-with-love pairing the posture is enacting. The chase usually comes from love-had-to-be-earned. The avoid usually comes from love-meant-losing-self. The fight usually comes from love-came-with-combat. The specific work is to separate the love from the wired companion. See [[what-was-wired-with-love-needs-untying]].

The boundary move that operationalizes the separation is [[boundary-against-the-wired-pattern]]: "I love you, I want your love, but I'm not willing to [chase / be dominated / fight] for your love." This is the explicit assertion that the wired companion is not part of what the person is willing to do for love anymore.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person who reports repeatedly producing the same outcome in relationships despite saying they want something different
- A person who can name the behavior they keep doing but cannot stop
- A person in whom the conscious "I want love" is contradicted by behavioral evidence
- The "subconscious grin" moment when the structure is named and the body recognizes the pattern
- A person whose negative self-talk mirrors the relational posture they take with others

## Tone

Joe lays this out clinically — three categories, here they are, here is how each one operates. The clinical tone is what makes the recognition possible. The client can place themselves in the map without it being an accusation. The directness is the kindness; the structural clarity allows the client to see the pattern as architecture rather than as personal failure.
