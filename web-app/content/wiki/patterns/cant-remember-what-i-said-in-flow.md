# Can't Remember What I Said (The Signature of Channeled Work)

## The Pattern

A specific somatic-cognitive signature follows work that comes from the channeling state: the person who did the work cannot remember what they said. They walk out of the talk, the coaching session, the meeting, with very little recall of the actual content. They have a sense of how it felt -- usually good or not-good -- but the words are gone.

Other people who were in the room often remember vividly. They quote things back to the speaker. The speaker hears the quotes and thinks "yeah, that sounds like something I would say" -- but does not actually remember saying it. The quote is recognized as authentic without being remembered.

This is not a memory failure. It is the signature of a particular kind of presence. Memory is something that happens when the conscious mind is processing experience as a record. When the conscious mind is fully in the present, channeling rather than producing, there is no recording happening. There is only the experience. When the experience ends, the record is mostly absent.

"I, I have a hard time remembering words when I'm actually in that state. I remember stories of other people. I'll remember, it's the same. You've seen me do the rapid-fire coaching. I'll remember them as a person and their, like, how they present themselves and the trauma that's in them. But I have a hard time remembering words when I'm actually in that state. I've never known why, but it's nice to hear that it, like, this is something that happens for a lot of people when they're speaking this way or when they're coming from that place." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

## Why This Happens

Tristan offers an explanation Joe affirms: when the system is in heightened presence, often with adrenaline running, the senses are hyperfocused on the present. There is no point for memory in a state like that. Memory is a function of the system that is operating one step away from experience -- noting it for later. The fully present system is not one step away. It is in it. So the recording does not happen.

"It's one of the most common things I hear in the beginning of ultra speaking training is I asked them, how did it go? And they said, I have no idea. I genuinely just don't know what happened. Because it's also heightened if there's -- if you have adrenaline in the moment. So if your senses are heightened, you're even more hyperfocused. And so there's just -- there's no point for memory in a state like that." -- Tristan de Montebello, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

This is not a deficit. It is a feature of the state. The state that produces the best work happens to be the state that does not record the work. The teacher who wants to remember everything they said in a great coaching session is wanting two incompatible things: the state of the channeling and the record of it.

## What Does Get Remembered

Joe describes what does survive the no-recording state: stories of other people, the felt sense of who the person was, the trauma he could read in them, the way they presented themselves. The relational and somatic data persists. The verbal content does not.

This is consistent with how the channeling state works. The body picks up far more information than the conscious mind processes. That information stays. The words that the body produced in response also pass through, but they pass through without being held by the conscious mind, because the conscious mind was not in the position of holder during the exchange.

What also persists is the felt sense of how it went. Joe does not remember what he said, but he knows whether it felt good in his system. Twenty people will tell him a coaching session was the best thing ever and he will think "I don't know, no idea about it" -- but he knows whether he was in the state or not. The state is what he can report on. The content is what others can report on.

## The Practical Problem

A specific problem this creates for teachers: the best insights they produce are the ones they cannot recall to write down later. A coach who has a brilliant two-minute monologue in session, knowing in the moment "this is exactly what I have been trying to figure out for three months," will get to the end of the session and find the insight gone. The fish slipped the net.

"In those moments, it's probably the moment when I have the best insights I ever have all week related to speaking. And there's -- I'm coaching, I'm in it, and somebody asks a question and I'll just -- for some reason everything lines up perfectly and I can give a 2-minute monologue that I think to myself, I'm like, wow, even myself, I'm thinking, man, you -- that was just -- that's exactly what you have been trying to figure out for these past 3 months. And I can say it perfectly. And then when it's over, I'm like, that needs to be a video or something. I need to get it out because it was so well said. And there's no way to reaccess that state in your head afterwards. I'm like, ah, it's, it's gone. Where is it? I, it's like cat trying to catch a dream. So I have to find a way to recreate the state, please." -- Tristan de Montebello, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

Joe offers a practical fix: record your own camera on QuickTime. Do not record the other people; just record you. Then anything you wanted to keep, you can pull. The recording is the workaround for the structural fact that the state does not produce its own record.

The cat-trying-to-catch-a-dream metaphor is exact. Once the state is gone, the content is unreachable. The only way to recapture it is to have recorded it externally while it was happening. Trying to reconstruct it from memory after the fact does not work. The memory was never made.

## The "That Sounds Like Something I'd Say" Recognition

A related sub-pattern: when people quote the channeled work back to the person who said it, the person often recognizes the words as theirs without remembering having said them. "Yeah, that sounds like something I'd say. Or, that sounds smart." There is recognition without memory. The voice is theirs. The content is theirs. The moment of producing it is not retrievable.

This recognition without memory is one of the marks of the channeling state. It points to the body knowing the words were authentic, even though the conscious mind did not record them. The authorship is real; the authoring was outside the conscious mind's view.

## Why This Matters for the AI Coach

This pattern matters for the AI coach in two ways.

First, when a person describes this signature -- not remembering their own coaching sessions, not remembering talks they gave, having to be told what they said by others -- the AI coach should recognize it as a positive marker, not as a deficit. The person is describing the state from which their best work comes. The not-remembering is evidence of presence, not of cognitive decline.

Second, the AI coach should recognize that this pattern often comes with a worry attached. The person who cannot remember their best work often feels destabilized by the not-remembering. They feel they should be able to recall what they said. They sometimes feel like a fraud because they cannot account for the work that other people praise them for. The AI coach can name the pattern as what it is: the signature of the state, not a deficit in the person.

The relief that comes from this naming is often substantial. The person has been holding "I should be able to remember" as a problem and finding it could be reframed as "the state I am working from does not record itself" is a structural shift.

## Variations Across Domains

The pattern shows up across several domains where channeled work happens:

- **Coaching.** The coach walks out of the session without recall of the specific exchanges. Remembers the person, the felt sense, what shifted.
- **Public speaking.** The speaker walks off the stage without recall of what they said, especially when speaking extemporaneously.
- **Podcasting and interviewing.** The host/guest does not remember the specific points made. People tell them later "you said this brilliant thing" and they have to take the speaker's word for it.
- **Group facilitation.** The facilitator does not remember the moves they made in the group, only the felt sense of how the group went.
- **Improvisational music and performance.** Musicians often describe the same thing -- not remembering the specific notes from a flow performance.
- **Writing in flow.** Writers sometimes describe re-reading what they wrote and not remembering writing it, feeling like a different person wrote it.

In each case, the same mechanism: the state that produces the best work is the state that does not record the work. The pattern is not domain-specific. It is presence-specific.

## The Inverse: Remembering Everything

The inverse pattern is also instructive. The speaker who remembers exactly what they said, in order, with all the framing and the transitions, was probably operating from rehearsed content rather than channeled presence. The memory is intact because the conscious mind was the producer. The rehearsed content tends to land flatter than the channeled content does, even when it is technically correct.

This is not always a problem. Some contexts require rehearsed delivery. But the signature is worth knowing about. If a teacher always remembers exactly what they said, they may be operating from a state that has its own ceiling.

The opposite extreme -- always not remembering -- can also be a problem if it indicates dissociation rather than presence. The differentiation matters: presence-amnesia comes with a felt sense of having been in the room, alive, alert. Dissociation-amnesia comes with a sense of having been gone. The AI coach should be able to ask the differentiating question if uncertain: "what was the felt sense of being in that?" Presence will report aliveness. Dissociation will report absence.
