# The Fear Hot Potato

## The Trigger

One person in a group, family, or team feels fear. The fear is intolerable. They cannot bear to feel it themselves. So they hand it to someone else -- through complaint, through urgency, through pressure, through asking the other person to fix the situation, through making the other person responsible for an outcome. The other person now feels the fear. The other person, also unwilling to hold it, throws it back, or throws it to a third party. The fear bounces around the group until it lands on whoever has the lowest threshold for catching it.

The pattern is structurally identical to [[shame-hot-potato]]. Same mechanism, different emotion. Joe names it explicitly using the same metaphor:

"It's like a fear hot potato. I don't want to feel this. And so you throw it at someone else and then they're like, I don't want to feel this. And then, or they, or they're the ones that feel it. And so it's like this fear hot potato, which is a big way people not wanting to feel fear." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

## The Family Form

Joe's primary example is the family. One person ends up holding the anxiety for the whole system. Everyone else organizes around managing that person's anxiety, which lets them avoid feeling their own.

"You see it in families where somebody holds the anxiety for the family and everybody else is like trying to manage the anxiety. I don't want to feel that fucking anxiety. Like it's going to be fine, honey. Or, you know, whatever. Like, well, we're going to get through this and blah, blah, blah, blah." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The reassurances ("it's going to be fine, honey," "we're going to get through this") are not actually for the person holding the anxiety. They are for the people offering them. The reassurer is managing their own discomfort with the anxious person by trying to make the anxious person less anxious. The strategy is rational by the reassurer's lights -- if the anxious one stops being anxious, the reassurer can stop feeling anxious about the anxious one's anxiety -- but it never resolves anything. The system has consolidated the unfelt fear into one carrier; the carrier cannot release it because the system depends on her holding it.

This is the family-level expression of the same mechanism described in [[one-team-member-holds-the-helplessness]]. The team's helplessness consolidates in one person; the family's anxiety consolidates in one person. The carrier is structurally selected because they have the lowest tolerance for not-feeling, not because something is wrong with them.

## The Mechanism of the Throw

The fear is thrown through several channels:

**Complaint as throw.** "I'm scared we're going to lose the contract" said in a tone that requires the listener to take on responsibility for the worry. The listener now carries the fear. The original speaker has had a moment of relief.

**Urgency as throw.** "We need to figure this out right now." The urgency is the speaker's unfelt fear converted into pressure on others. The others now feel the pressure (which is the fear in a different form) and the speaker has externalized it.

**Catastrophizing as throw.** "If this doesn't work, everything falls apart." The catastrophic frame requires the listener to either accept the catastrophe (and feel scared) or fight it (and become responsible for preventing it). Either way, the speaker has handed off the affect.

**Asking for fixing.** "What are you going to do about this?" The fixing-demand transfers responsibility, which transfers fear. The fixer now carries the affect; the asker has been freed of it.

**The reverse throw: false reassurance.** "It's going to be fine." This is the throw used by the reassurer who cannot feel the anxious person's fear. The reassurance lands on the anxious person as a demand to stop feeling, which produces more fear, which the reassurer now has to manage with more reassurance. The throw is in both directions in this dynamic: the original anxious person throws by being anxious; the reassurer throws back by demanding the anxiety stop.

**Helping uninvited as throw.** A specific channel worth naming separately because it does not look like a throw on the surface. The helper feels anxious about an outcome; the helper acts on the anxiety by stepping into someone else's domain to do the thing for them or alongside them; the helper feels relief; the receiver now has the helper's anxiety operating in their space, plus the original task. The throw was the help itself. The disguise is generosity. See [[uninvited-help-hands-them-your-fear]] for the dedicated principle treatment and [[the-helper-trap]] for the full pattern with both sides.

## The Setup That Prevents the Throw

The retreat context Brett and Joe discuss provides the structural alternative. The setup that prevents the fear hot potato in a group: make the fear table stakes. Everyone is there to feel fear. No one is allowed to claim they are not afraid. The shared activation removes the throw-target. There is no one to hand the fear to because everyone is already holding their own.

Brett describes the moment this structure was established:

"By the time we got to the edge of a cliff to either walk the High Line or to jump, the thing that was kind of unique about this is that everyone is feeling some level of fear, and it's completely understandable. It's not like it took some work to figure out that there's actually a visceral fear of annihilation here. It was just, you know, it's table stakes. Here it is, fear of annihilation." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

When fear is named as the shared condition, it cannot be projected. Each person owns their own. The system stops consolidating the fear in one carrier because there is no carrier role -- everyone holds.

This is the structural inversion of the family pattern. The family fear hot potato runs because the family does not have a frame in which "all of us are afraid right now" is sayable. The retreat pattern runs because the frame is exactly that.

## The Visible Signature

How to recognize a fear hot potato is running:

- **One person in the group is markedly more anxious than the rest.** The others speak about "her anxiety" as if it were her property. The anxiety is the family's; the carrier is just where it has consolidated.
- **The group's energy goes into managing one person's worry rather than addressing the underlying issue.** Meetings are spent reassuring the anxious team member rather than examining what they are anxious about.
- **Reassurances are met with more anxiety, not less.** The carrier cannot release the fear by being reassured because the reassurances are throws, not landings. They produce more activation, not less.
- **When the carrier is removed (leaves, recovers, gets fired), the anxiety relocates to the next-lowest-threshold person.** The fact that the carrier role is structural and not personal becomes visible only when the seat empties and a new person fills it.
- **Multiple group members are spending energy on the carrier's affect rather than on their own.** The system runs on the carrier as the affect-bearer.

## The Read That Dissolves the Throw

A specific coaching move available to anyone who learns to recognize the pattern: see fear underneath what looks like other behavior. The angry boss is throwing fear. The over-helpful colleague is throwing fear. The catastrophizing partner is throwing fear. Once the throw is visible as a throw, the throw is much harder to catch.

Joe describes the compassion that opens up:

"Somebody gets angry and I'm like, oh, they're scared. Like, creates so much ease and compassion to be with their anger. Or somebody is really helpful in a way that I'm like, hey, stop. It's like, stop taking away my empowerment. Now I can take care of myself. So much compassion because, oh, they're afraid. And we can all be afraid." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

This is the same principle the retreat made visible at the group level: when you see fear where it actually is, the dynamic stops being personal. The other person's anger is not about you. Their over-helping is not about you. Their pressure is not about you. They are scared and they cannot hold it themselves. The throw stops landing as personal injury and starts landing as recognizable pattern.

See [[read-anger-and-help-as-fear]] for the specific move.

## The Inversion: When the Group Holds Together

The retreat also revealed what the opposite of the hot potato looks like. When a group is structured so that each person can hold their own fear -- with the support of the group's attention -- the energy that would have been spent throwing becomes available for the actual work.

Brett describes the example of a participant who, with the group's support, approached the edge to say no, was told "we love your no," and ended up choosing to jump. The fear did not get thrown anywhere. It got held by the participant, witnessed by the group, and worked through. The action that followed was clean.

The functional inverse of the fear hot potato is what Brett calls "people held each other and were able to ask each other for what they wanted and how they wanted to be held." Each person owning their own fear, each person able to ask for support, no one collapsing into the carrier role. The fear does not vanish; it stops being a hot potato. It becomes what it actually is: a shared condition of being a person doing something hard.

See [[welcoming-fear-as-group-stabilizer]] for the structural alternative.

## In Coaching

When a coachee describes a chronic pattern in their family or team in which "one person is the anxious one," the diagnostic move is to ask what fear the rest of the system is refusing to feel. The carrier is rarely the source. The carrier is the consolidator.

When a coachee describes being the only one in the family who feels the worry, the diagnostic move is to ask what would happen if they stopped carrying it. The answer is usually some version of: "no one would deal with it." The fear that they would have to feel if they stopped carrying it is often the actual fear the system has been outsourcing to them.

When a coachee describes a relationship in which they constantly reassure their partner, the move is to ask whose fear is actually being managed. Often the reassurance is the coachee's strategy for not feeling their own fear about the partner. The reassurance produces more anxiety, which requires more reassurance. The dyad is throwing fear back and forth.

When a coachee describes an angry boss, the move is to redirect from "what did I do wrong" to "what is he afraid of." The redirection does not excuse the boss's behavior. It changes what the coachee is responsible for. The coachee is not responsible for catching the boss's fear; the boss is.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You are the only one in your family who is worried about the thing, and everyone else seems oblivious
- You spend significant energy reassuring a family member or colleague whose anxiety is chronic
- Your partner panics about something, you reassure them, they panic more, you reassure more, the cycle is daily
- A team member is "the anxious one" and the team's meetings are organized around her affect
- You notice that when you stop carrying the family worry, no one else picks it up; the issue just goes unaddressed
- You are an angry boss and your team's productivity has collapsed; you have not yet considered that your anger is your fear
- You are the "responsible one" who holds everything together, and you are exhausted; the holding is the catching

## Tone

Joe names this with a kind of plain recognition. The framing is structural rather than blaming. No one is being a villain. People are doing what people do when they have not been given the practice of feeling fear themselves. The frame is offered as a relief: the family member who has been carrying the anxiety is not crazy; the boss whose anger is constant is not malicious. They are people in a hot potato game that no one designed and that everyone is participating in. Naming the game is most of the work.
