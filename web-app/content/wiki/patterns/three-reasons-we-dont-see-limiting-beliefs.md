# Three Reasons We Don't See Our Limiting Beliefs

## The Pattern

There are three reasons a limiting belief stays invisible to the person holding it. These are not the same as the [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs]] (which describes structural types of belief). These describe the three causes of blindness — why a person can be living inside a limiting belief and not notice it is there.

1. **Logical fallacy.** An error in thinking, an unexamined assumption, a logical step that does not actually hold.
2. **Ignorance.** Not knowing the alternative exists. Never having conceived of a different way.
3. **Consequence avoidance.** Refusing to see the belief because seeing it would require facing something you do not want to face.

"There's a logical fallacy. We just have an error in our thought. We haven't really looked at it deeply enough to see through the belief system. The other one is just ignorance. We don't know that there's a possibility. But the most common one is that there's some consequences involved where we don't want to have to face those consequences. So we keep ourselves in the dark around the limiting belief." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## 1. Logical Fallacy

The belief has a logical hole that has not been examined. Once examined, the fallacy is visible and the belief loosens. Until examined, the fallacy holds the belief in place because the mind is taking a flawed inference as solid ground.

Example: "I have a boss." For some people this is treated as creating a parental-authority structure. Examined, the actual structure is: there is a person who pays for your services, and the relationship is contractual. The leap from "I have a boss" to "I have an authority over my life" is a fallacy. See [[reframe-boss-as-customer]].

The intervention is examination. Apply the inquiry techniques. Look at the inference chain. Find where the step does not hold.

## 2. Ignorance

The person has never encountered the alternative. The belief seems true because nothing in their experience has disproven it. They have not met anyone who lives differently, read about anyone who lives differently, or imagined anyone living differently.

Example: a person who has only ever worked in employee roles and never met an entrepreneur might believe employment is the only safe path. The belief is held in place by the absence of comparable data, not by examined evidence.

"We don't know that there's a possibility. Maybe nobody's ever conceived of the fact that their boss is just one client." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

The intervention is exposure. Find the comparable. Read about, talk to, or imagine the person who does the thing your belief says cannot be done. See [[find-the-comparable]].

## 3. Consequence Avoidance (The Most Common)

The deepest of the three. The belief is held in place not because of an error in thinking or an absence of data but because seeing through the belief would require facing a consequence the person does not want to face. The mind manufactures and maintains the belief precisely to avoid that consequence.

"The most common one is that there's some consequences involved where we don't want to have to face those consequences." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

### How It Works

A person holds the belief "I can't do it." Examined, the belief is questionable. But if it turned out the person could do it, that would mean they might try, and trying means they might fail, and failing means they would have to feel the consequences of being a failure. So the belief "I can't do it" is preferable to the experience of having to feel like a failure. The belief is a defense mechanism.

"If you believe that you can do it, then it also means that you might fail. And you don't want to have to suffer the consequences of feeling like a failure. So it's easier to just believe that I can't do it." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

Another version: "I have a boss, that's safer than being an entrepreneur." Examined, employment is not necessarily safer. But if employment is not safer, then the choice to be an employee is not the safe choice it presented itself as, and the person might have to face their actual relationship with safety, money, and risk. The belief protects against that confrontation.

"I like to work under the illusion that because I have an employer, it's safer than being an entrepreneur. And so I'm going to not want to see those consequences." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

### Why This Is the Most Common

Logical fallacies and ignorance can be addressed by information. New facts, new arguments, new comparables. Consequence avoidance cannot. The person already half-knows the belief is wrong. They are choosing not to see it because seeing it produces unbearable feeling.

The intervention is therefore emotional, not informational. You have to be able to feel the consequence the belief is protecting against. Once the feeling is feel-able, the belief is no longer necessary as a shield. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

This is why [[inquiry-on-limiting-beliefs]] always reaches the question: "what would be scary to let go of?" That question is the doorway into the consequence-avoidance layer.

## Why Distinguishing the Three Matters

Different causes of blindness require different interventions:

- **Logical fallacy** -> intellectual examination
- **Ignorance** -> exposure to alternatives
- **Consequence avoidance** -> emotional work, feeling the underlying feeling

A coach who treats all three the same will frequently miss. Hammering intellectual arguments at someone in consequence-avoidance produces resistance, not insight. Showing comparables to someone who actually has a logical fallacy may not loosen the belief because the comparable does not address the inference error.

The diagnostic is: when the person hears the alternative, what happens? If they say "oh, I never thought of that" — ignorance. If they say "I see your point but it doesn't apply to me because..." — logical fallacy (now visible and being inspected). If they get tense, change the subject, defend the belief harder, or rationalize — consequence avoidance.

## The Quick Hack

For any of the three, the fastest test is to look for the comparable. Are there people who hold the opposite belief and live the consequence well? If yes, the belief is at least partly limiting; the work begins. See [[find-the-comparable]].

"The quickest hack to seeing if you have one of those things is you just look for a comparable in the space. You just say, oh wait, is there entrepreneurs that feel safe? Oh, is there entrepreneurs that are actually more secure financially than me in a job? Oh yes, there are. Therefore, there must be something that I'm not seeing." -- Joe Hudson, How to See Through Limiting Beliefs

## Tone

Joe holds this clinically. The three reasons are observable, distinguishable, and each has its appropriate response. The diagnostic clarity is what makes the pattern useful.
