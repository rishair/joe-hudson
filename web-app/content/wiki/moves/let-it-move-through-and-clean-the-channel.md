# Let It Move Through and Clean the Channel

## The Move

When a compliment, award, recognition, or expression of love arrives, do not deflect. Do not minimize. Do not redirect to others. Do not block. Let the substance move all the way through you. The receiving itself does the work.

The image: a hollow bone. The bone does not add anything. The bone does not produce anything. Its job is to be a clear channel through which something else moves. Each time the bone receives without obstruction, the channel gets cleaner. The deflections are the debris. The receiving is the cleaning.

The figure is Lakota healer Fools Crow, who said he was not doing the healing -- the healing moved through him, and his job was to keep the channel clear.

"It's like Fools Crow talked about. He isn't doing the healing. He's like a hollow bone, and his job is just to keep the bone clear of dirt, and then the healing moves through him, through that channel. His job is just to clean the channel." -- Joe Hudson, Humility (paraphrasing Fools Crow)

The application: every time recognition lands undeflected, ego material gets cleared. The compliment is the cleaning agent.

"Humility is when that compliment moves all the way through you and cleans the dirt out of your channel. It just takes out your ego. That's the feeling of letting it all move through you." -- Joe Hudson, Humility

## The Approval-Seeking Variant of This Move

The same move addresses approval-seeking. The approval-seeker is structurally a person who cannot let compliments in. The chase for approval keeps the receiving channel closed (the chase is the meta-position of monitoring, which is incompatible with the in-body position of receiving). Compliments arrive constantly and are deflected. The hunger never abates.

When this move lands for an approval-seeker, the structural change is immediate. The supply was never the problem; the digestion was. The receiving fills the hunger that the chasing could not fill.

"And the more I allowed myself to feel it, the less I needed it. It was like, I was a person who was eating, but then I was always like not digesting the food. And now I was actually digesting the food and it satisfied my hunger for the very first time." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

See [[eating-but-not-digesting-the-food]] for the metaphor's full unpacking. The inversion is structural: the person who can receive a compliment needs them less than the person who cannot. The chase that was running on hunger ends when the receiving begins.

For the broader concern this move addresses, see [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]]. For the principle that names what the deflection costs, see [[seeking-approval-pushes-people-away]].

## What This Looks Like

A compliment arrives. The body's first impulse is often to deflect: "oh no, no, it was nothing," "the team did all the work," "I just got lucky," "you're being too kind." Notice the impulse. Do not act on it.

Instead, let the compliment land. Let it touch the part of the body where it would otherwise have been deflected. Notice what arises -- often discomfort, often the felt sense of being seen, often a small flash of the identity that wants to manage how it is being seen. Let all of that move through. Receive the compliment as fact, as something happening, as a real input to the system.

The body sensation Joe describes is being touched repeatedly through the channel: "whack, whack, whack, whack." Each unblocked receiving is a touch. Each touch sands away a piece of the defending self.

## The Three Specific Deflection Modes (For Detection)

Three distinct forms of deflection that all serve the same identity-protection function. Naming them as separate forms is useful diagnostically: a client may eliminate one and still be running the others without noticing.

**Verbal denial of the fact.** The most visible form. The compliment is contradicted directly. "Oh no, the dress isn't that nice." "No, I'm not really good at that." "That wasn't anything special." The compliment was that X is true; the deflection says X is not true. The conversation surfaces the disagreement.

**Verbal acceptance with internal denial.** The compliment is met with "uh-huh, yeah" or "thanks" while the mind silently completes the deflection: "oh, they don't really know me." "If they knew the real me, they wouldn't say that." "They only see the surface." The verbal surface has been smoothed but the receiving has not happened. This is the polite-thank-you-with-internal-disbelief failure mode named in [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]] — the form most clients land in after they have learned not to verbally deny but before they have learned to actually receive.

**Attributing motive to the giver.** The compliment is dismissed by suspecting the giver's reasons. "Oh, that person's just trying to sell me something." "They want something from me." "They're being polite." "They have to say that because we work together." The compliment is filtered out before it can land because its source has been disqualified.

"There's a thousand ways where we don't let the compliment in. And even if we do let it in, maybe we only let it in a little bit. We don't actually allow it to move all the way through us and really digest it, really appreciate it. Go, oh wow, that feels really good. Allow ourselves to feel really good inside of the compliment." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Most Want

The three forms map to different identity defenses. Verbal denial defends the claim itself. Internal denial defends against being seen — keeps the giver at a distance that lets the deflector still see themselves as unseen. Motive attribution defends the entire category of compliments as untrustworthy data, which protects the deflector from ever having to receive any of them.

The compounded form Joe names — letting it in "a little bit" but not "all the way through" — is the most common form in clients who have done some work on the more visible deflections. The compliment is received as a polite social move but not allowed to actually reorganize the receiver's felt sense of themselves. The diagnostic for this form: the body's reaction to the compliment is too small. A genuinely received compliment produces somatic movement (warmth, tears, a tickling, a softening). A partially-received compliment produces no body response at all — just a thank-you and a continuation. The absence of body response is the data.

## Let It Tickle You / Shake Your Body (The Body-Movement Cues)

A pair of specific somatic instructions for clients who have a body that has been refusing reception for years. The instructions ask the body to do small movements that interrupt the body's habitual closing-down response and create literal space for the receiving to land.

"Let it tickle you. Breathe, breathe. Let it tickle you." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

"Yeah, shake your body a little bit. Let it in a little bit." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The instructions are precise about scale. *A little bit* — not big movements, not dramatic catharsis. The point is to introduce small movement into a body that has been holding still as a defense. The tickling and the shaking are small enough that they don't activate the deeper defenses that bigger emotional expression would trigger.

The mechanism: the body's default-mode for receiving has been constriction. Constriction is held through small sustained muscle tension. The tickle and the shake disrupt the small sustained tension without demanding a large emotional release. The constriction loosens slightly. The compliment has more room to land.

Pair with breath ("Breathe, breathe") to amplify the loosening. Breath is the most basic movement the body has available. A few conscious breaths often produce the openness that the tickle and the shake then ride on.

## This Is What You Wanted As a Kid (The Inner-Child Frame for the Receiving)

A specific framing Joe uses when the client is in the middle of receiving and starts to back off or to brace against the inflow:

"Yeah, yeah. Let yourself feel it. This is what you want. This is what you wanted as a kid." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The framing links the present-moment receiving to the kid-self that did not get this kind of seeing. The kid-self is still in the body. The kid-self is the one who is going to receive what is being offered. The adult who has been deflecting compliments for decades is also the kid-self who needed someone to see them and did not get it.

The frame is short and operational. It is not asking the client to do childhood-origin processing. It is naming, in the moment of receiving, that the receiving is for the kid-self who is finally getting what they always wanted. The naming often produces tears, often produces shaking, often produces the kind of receiving that has been blocked for years because the kid-self has been waiting.

The frame pairs with the let-it-tickle-you and the shake-your-body cues. The tickling and the shaking are how the kid-self lets the recognition into their body. The adult's framing "this is what you wanted as a kid" gives the body permission to receive in the way the kid would have.

In the source session, this framing arrived in the public-Q&A context where audience members were dropping compliments in the chat. Joe instructed the client to let the compliments tickle her and shake her body. The "this is what you wanted as a kid" lands at the moment when the client says "it's hard" or "I'm shaking right now" — the recognition that the receiving is significant and overwhelming. The frame normalizes the overwhelm as appropriate and links it to the unmet need.

## Bring the Room Into the Receiving (Have Others in the Session Offer Compliments)

A move adjacent to the receiving work in public Q&A sessions: instruct the audience to put genuine compliments in the chat, then have the client practice letting them in one by one.

"Yeah, so anybody who has a genuine compliment or scene of [her] can you put it in the chat? Do them a little slowly if you can, and your job is to actually just let them in. Let it tickle you." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The setup makes the receiving-practice external and live. The compliments arrive as real input from real people. The client has to let each one land somatically. The slowness ("do them a little slowly if you can") is what gives the body time to receive each one rather than swallowing them all at once without digesting any.

The move is a public-coaching variant of the basic receiving practice. The audience becomes the partner in the work. The client gets to practice in volume, in real-time, with the coach watching for whether each compliment is landing or bouncing.

## The "Let It All the Way Down" Cue

A specific in-session refinement: when a recognition has landed somewhere in the body but only partially, the cue is to let it land deeper. The receiving has gotten to the heart but the rest of the body is still holding the deflection. The instruction is somatic: drop the recognition into the part of the body that has been the longest holdout.

"Yeah, let it all the way down. You've got it like at your heart, but like all the — let it all the way down." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The cue is read-specific. The coach has to be tracking where the recognition has lodged. The instruction only works if there is a "down" to go to — that is, if the recognition has stalled somewhere shallow and the body has more depth available. The cue is one form of pacing the receiving: not faster, but deeper.

When the recognition does land all the way down, the body often registers it as overwhelming. The overwhelming is appropriate. "It's not bad" or "that's overwhelming" is the diagnostic of the move landing — not a problem to back off from. The work is to let the channel stay open, not to back away from the inflow. The full recognition is "only like 2% of what's coming your way" — Joe's framing for the size of the love that can arrive once the channel is open. The expansiveness is the work, not the side-effect.

The connection to [[count-the-years-of-showing-up]]: when the substance being received is the duration of a partner's consistent showing-up across years, the "let it all the way down" cue is especially load-bearing. The duration cannot be deflected in the usual ways, so the receiving question becomes a matter of how deep the body will let the recognition go.

## Why Letting the Compliment In Is Scary at the Depth (The Spaciousness Contact)

A specific depth-level reason for why the receiving is resisted, beyond the immediate identity-defense reason. If the compliment is allowed all the way in, it brings the receiver into contact with the part of themselves that is not the deflective identity. The contact is with what was always there underneath all the stories — the indescribable, unhurtable, never-changed thing that the [[what-am-i]] inquiry points at. The contact is often experienced as an abyss opening up.

"That's a lot of what's happening in the compliments is that if you really let the compliment in, if you really feel that appreciation, you get in touch with that big spacious emptiness that's there. You get in touch with the thing that can't be touched no matter how much you try to annihilate it or how much you compliment it. That part that never changes is still there and it's still indescribable and it's just hanging out. And there's nothing you can do about it. It's just there." -- Joe Hudson, You're Pushing Away What You Most Want

This explains a phenomenon that the standard receiving teaching does not always account for: clients who have done significant work on deflection still sometimes encounter a layer of resistance that does not respond to the standard moves. The standard moves address the identity-protection function of the deflection. They do not address the deeper resistance, which is the avoidance of contact with the spaciousness that the receiving makes available.

The spaciousness sounds peaceful in abstract description. In the body of someone whose identity has been constructed around being a specific bounded self, the spaciousness lands as the dissolution of the self. The fear is not just "this compliment will require me to be bigger" but "this compliment will dissolve the bounded me that has been navigating my life." The fear of that dissolution is real. See [[the-abyss-is-where-you-dont-exist]] for the related operational definition.

This connects compliment-receiving work to [[self-realization]] more directly than the standard framing suggests. The receiving practice is not just a corrective for approval-seeking. It is also a direct entry into the inquiry of what you essentially are. Each fully-received compliment opens contact with the part of you that the compliment is not about — the part that was there before any compliment and will be there after any compliment, that the compliment is touching by its passage but is not the substance of.

The implication for coaching: when the resistance to receiving feels disproportionate to the identity-protection account, the spaciousness-contact account may be operating. The corrective is the same — let it move through — but the additional naming can help the client understand what they are actually pulling back from and gives them the option to lean into the contact rather than pull away. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]] and [[what-were-avoiding-is-usually-love]] for related principles about why the avoided territory is often the territory of relief.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not deflect verbally.** "Oh, it was nothing" is the deflection. Even when the words sound humble, they are the defense.
- **Do not redirect.** "Oh, but you're the one who really did it" hands the recognition back. The recognition was for you. Let it land.
- **Do not block somatically.** A tight smile, a quick subject change, a fast "thanks" -- these are body-level deflections. The deflection happens before the words.
- **Do not perform acceptance.** Saying "thank you" while keeping the substance out is still deflection. The polite-thank-you-with-internal-disbelief is the most common failure mode. See [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]].
- **Do not buy the inflation.** Receiving is not agreeing that the inflated version is true. It is letting the actual recognition land. See the distinction at [[receive-the-apology-permeably]].
- **Do not contradict the giver.** "Oh, I'm not pretty -- you're the pretty one" is structurally an accusation that the giver is lying. The compliment cannot land because it has been declared false in the act of being received. See [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]].

## What This Does (Mechanism)

The deflection of recognition is a defense of the smallness-identity. The smallness-identity has been holding the channel in a particular shape. Each receiving slightly relaxes the shape. Over time, the shape relaxes enough that recognition lands by default and ego material clears with it.

The deflection seems virtuous -- "I am not letting this go to my head." Structurally, the deflection is the opposite of what it appears. It is the identity protecting itself by refusing the input that would dissolve it. The receiving is the actual work.

The mechanism Joe specifically names in the approval-seeking context: the compliment, when received somatically, challenges the part of the self that says "I'm not enough." The identity has been constructed on the foundation of being limited. The unblocked compliment is direct evidence to the contrary. The identity has to either deflect the evidence (which keeps the identity intact but produces the eating-without-digesting hunger) or let the evidence in (which dissolves the identity but feeds the hunger). The deflection is the choice to keep the identity at the cost of the nourishment.

"What it's actually doing is taking the part of you that feels limited and it's challenging it. It's making the part of you that says, 'No, I'm not enough.' And it's like, wow, there's evidence to the contrary. And you have to deal with that. And that creates an emotion. So if you feel that emotion, it can process all the way through. And it really literally destroys this egoic part of you that says, 'Hey, I'm limited. I'm not good enough.' And you actually can't believe it anymore if you let that feeling all the way through you. It really shows you who you are instead of what you think you are. And that creates a lot of freedom." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The freedom comes from the identity-shift, not from the compliment. The compliment is the substance that does the dissolving. The receiving is the move that lets the substance do its work.

The work also benefits the person giving the recognition. When the recognition does not land, the giver feels the deflection as rejection or as wasted effort. Brett's framing in the Grammy example: "the people who are giving it feel like, 'okay, well, that was dumb. Why'd we do this? What are we even here for?'" The deflection costs the relationship. The receiving honors it.

## When to Use This Move

- When a compliment or recognition arrives and the body wants to deflect
- When an award or formal recognition is being given
- When a partner expresses love and the impulse is to manage how it lands
- When a client thanks you and the impulse is to minimize
- When your team appreciates your leadership and the impulse is to redirect
- When someone tells you something kind about your work and the response would have been deflection

## What to Say Instead

Often, the move is to say less. A simple "thank you," delivered while letting the substance land, is enough. The work is internal -- the words do not need to do much.

If something more is wanted: name what is actually happening. "That landed -- thank you." Or simply receive in silence with eye contact. The substance has to be allowed to touch you. The words follow that or do not.

## In Coaching

When a client is deflecting recognition repeatedly, the read is identity defense. The intervention is rarely "you should accept compliments better" -- that installs another identity. The intervention is usually:

1. **Name the deflection in real time** when it happens with you. "I notice you just deflected that. What would it be like to let it land?"
2. **Pause and have them try the receiving** in the moment. Slow it down. Let them feel the discomfort of receiving without deflection.
3. **Ask about the body** -- where did the deflection happen? What did the body do?
4. **Trace it back** -- where did they learn that deflection was virtue? Often the answer is childhood training in smallness.

The parallel move for receiving apologies is [[receive-the-apology-permeably]]. The parallel move with partners' love is [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]] (the read) and [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]] (the move).

## What This Produces

- A felt sense of being touched by the recognition rather than deflecting it
- Visible relaxation in the body as the channel opens
- Often, tears or a softening of the face as the substance lands
- Sometimes laughter, sometimes a tickling sensation, sometimes discomfort -- the body's actual response to being fed something it has been refusing
- Over time, the spontaneous capacity to receive without effort
- An increased capacity to give as well -- the channel works in both directions
- A diminishing of the hunger for the next compliment -- the receiving fills the hunger that the chasing could not fill (see [[eating-but-not-digesting-the-food]])
- The dissolution of the "I'm not enough" identity, with the felt sense of freedom that follows

The visible signature of someone who has done this work: when complimented, they let it land. Their face softens. They might say "thank you" simply. They do not perform humility through deflection. They do not perform pride through inflation. They just receive.

Joe's autobiographical account of what receiving feels like once it actually starts working:

"When I let it in, it just like hit me over and over again. Sometimes it made me cry. Sometimes it made me laugh. Sometimes it tickled me, but I let it move all the way through my body. And the next thing I knew, I was like, oh, this is the thing that I've actually wanted that I haven't allowed myself to feel, mostly because I was scared that it would go away or I was scared it wasn't true or I was scared it wouldn't come back. And instead I just allowed myself to feel it." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The fears that drive the deflection are also named here: fear that the recognition will go away (so don't get attached), fear that the recognition is not true (so don't trust it), fear that it will not come back (so don't let it matter). All three fears are addressed by the same move: feel it now, fully, and let what happens happen. The fears do not have to be solved first. The receiving is itself the dissolution of them.

## Tone

Joe delivers this teaching with the body sensation of being touched -- the "whack, whack, whack" image. The tone is somatic, not theoretical. He is describing what receiving feels like in the body, not arguing for it as a moral position.

When he coaches someone live on this, his tone is gentle and slow. He pauses to let the person actually try it. He does not move on quickly. The move requires time in the body. The teaching pace is matched to the felt-sense pace.

## The Inverse Move

The deflecting impulse should be respected as a teacher. The deflection is showing you the location of the identity. When you notice yourself deflecting, you have found the place to work. The deflection is information.

The work is not to suppress the deflection (which would be another defense) but to let it arise and let the receiving happen anyway. Both can be present: the impulse to deflect and the choice to let the compliment land. The choice does not eliminate the impulse. It just does not act on it.
