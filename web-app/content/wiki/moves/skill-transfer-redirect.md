# Skill-Transfer Redirect

## The Move

When a client says they cannot do X (often a business-development or self-promotion task) but you can see that they have already mastered a directly transferable skill, name it. Tell them they already know how. The blocker is not skill. The blocker is willingness to apply the skill to themselves.

The form: "You know how to do this. You teach it. The principles are the same."

The move is a refusal of the "I don't know how to market myself" frame. The client claiming inability is using inability to cover avoidance. Pointing at the skill they already have in a different form collapses the inability story.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a client teaches or has demonstrated mastery in a domain that uses the same skills as the domain they claim to be blocked in.
- Most commonly with coaches, teachers, healers, speakers, and consultants who teach skills they refuse to apply to their own business.
- When the "I don't know how" frame is doing the work of "I'm afraid to."
- After [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] has surfaced what they are avoiding. Naming the skill they already have removes the last cover.

## What It Produces

**Removal of the inability cover story.** The client cannot maintain "I don't know how to market myself" after Joe has pointed out they teach public speaking and marketing uses the same principles. The cover story is removed; the actual blocker (shame, avoidance) is left exposed.

**Often: embarrassment.** "Yeah, that's embarrassing." The recognition that they teach what they don't apply is uncomfortable. The discomfort is data — see [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] for the natural follow-up.

**A specific actionable instruction.** The client now has the recognition that they already have everything they need. Whatever they teach in the mastered domain is what they do in the blocked domain. The instruction is concrete and immediate.

**A reduction in the search for new techniques.** The client has often been looking for the right marketing course, the right coaching program, the right framework. Naming the skill they already have stops that search. They don't need a new technique. They need to apply what they know.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Notice the structural match.** The client teaches public speaking → marketing is structured speaking. The client coaches relationships → asking for clients is relational. The client is a therapist → the marketing channel is empathic understanding of pain points. The match has to be real; do not force a parallel that isn't there.
2. **Surface the want first.** "How do you want to market yourself? Give me three adjectives." The client names what they want to be known for. This produces material from inside the client to use in the next move.
3. **Make the connection explicit.** "I bet you teach those things in your [domain of mastery]." The client almost always confirms.
4. **Name the transfer.** "If you know how to do [the domain of mastery], you know how to do [the blocked domain]. It's the same principles."
5. **Let the recognition land.** The client usually says something like "yeah" or "that's embarrassing." Either is fine — both are signs the recognition has landed.
6. **Normalize if shame arrives.** If the client collapses into "that's embarrassing," refuse the shame frame. "It's human." See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]].

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "How do you want to market yourself? Give me like three adjectives that would make you fucking excited to let people know what you do."
- **Guest:** "Authentic, honest. Yeah, blunt."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, I bet you teach those things in your public speaking."
- **Guest:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so you know it all. You know how to market yourself. If you know how to do public speaking, you know how to market yourself. It's the same principles."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, that's embarrassing."
- **Joe:** "It's human."
- *(Source: How Do I Make Money Doing What I Love?)*

The client has been describing the marketing problem as an external blocker. Joe redirects by surfacing what the client wants to be known for (authentic, honest, blunt), then notes that those are the same things the client teaches in public speaking. The "if you know X you know Y" move collapses the inability claim. The client's "that's embarrassing" is met with "it's human."

## Why It Works

The "I don't know how" frame depends on the blocked domain being foreign and the mastered domain being separate. The skill-transfer redirect closes the gap. Marketing is not a foreign discipline for a public speaking coach — it is public speaking. The teaching the client gives every day in the mastered domain is the teaching they need in the blocked domain.

The move also produces an internal mirror. The client teaches what they cannot do. The skill-transfer naming makes the client see themselves teaching what they refuse to apply. That mirror is uncomfortable in a way that pushes the work forward. The client can keep telling themselves they don't know how to market only as long as nobody points out they teach the relevant skills professionally.

The move is also efficient. Long conversations about marketing strategy, business development, sales funnels, and content calendars are not needed. The client already knows. The work is to remove what's blocking the application of what they know.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly amused. Joe does not deliver the redirect with intensity. The intensity is in the recognition the client has to do. Joe's tone says "this is obvious," which is what makes the obviousness available to the client.

When the client says "that's embarrassing," Joe's "it's human" lands soft. The shame is met with normalization, not validation of the shame frame.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the client has named a domain they are blocked in (marketing, asking for the raise, having the conversation, setting the boundary)
- When the client teaches or has demonstrated mastery in a domain that uses the same skills
- When [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]] has surfaced what they are avoiding
- When the "I don't know how" frame is functioning as cover for "I'm afraid to"
- When [[binary-that-forces-the-avoidance-into-view]] has been deployed and the client has agreed they are avoiding

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] — when the client collapses into "that's embarrassing"
- Toward [[is-the-shame-in-the-room-or-in-you]] — if the shame about applying the skill needs to be tested
- Toward [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]] — what they actually want to be known for in the blocked domain
- Toward [[stick-a-fucking-shit-in-front-of-the-should]] — when shoulds arrive about how to do the marketing/business work going forward
- Toward closure of the session, since the recognition is often complete enough to act on

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not give business advice.** The point of the move is to redirect the client to what they already know — not to add new content. Adding strategy or tactics undermines the move.
- **Do not force a parallel that isn't real.** If the mastered domain and the blocked domain don't actually share skills, the move fails and feels glib. Only use when the structural match is genuine.
- **Do not lecture.** The "it's the same principles" sentence does the work. Long explanations of how the principles map dilute the recognition.
- **Do not respond to "that's embarrassing" with sympathy or further explanation.** "It's human" is enough. Sympathy validates the shame frame; explanation gives the embarrassment more to chew on. The brevity is the soothing.

## The Body-Intelligence Variant: Transfer the Felt Sense From the Mastery Domain Into Life

A specific variant of the move applies when the inability claim is not about a business skill but about felt-sense access. The client says they cannot feel their body or their emotions. They also have a domain of mastery in which body intelligence is operational and visible — sport, music, art, surgery, craft. The structural form of the move is identical: the client demonstrably has the capacity in another context, so the inability claim in this context cannot stand.

See [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] for the dedicated article. The form: surface the felt difference between in-flow and not-in-flow in the mastery domain, name that the in-flow state IS body intelligence, ask the client to do the same internal move in the current context. "If you can play tennis, you definitely can feel your body, or you wouldn't be able to play tennis. The state you get into when you're in flow — like, you're in your body, right, in that state. So just do that, but just be doing it while we're sitting here, not playing tennis." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Feel My Body?

The body-intelligence variant differs from the marketing-variant in one key respect: the mastery domain does not have to teach the same content as the blocked domain. A tennis coach is not teaching emotional access. But the felt-sense the tennis coach uses to play tennis IS emotional access in a different application. The transfer is at the level of the underlying capacity, not at the level of the surface content.

The variant is the entry point for the [[athletes-unlocking-body-intelligence-in-business]] pattern more broadly. Athletes, musicians, dancers, surgeons — anyone whose work is mediated by developed somatic intelligence — has the capacity available for transfer into any other context. The coaching work is to surface its availability.

## Variations

The move adapts to many domains where the client teaches what they cannot apply:

- **Public speaking coach who can't market themselves:** "Marketing uses the same principles as public speaking."
- **Relationship coach who can't have a relationship:** "You teach this. You know how. The skill is the same."
- **Sales trainer who can't sell their own services:** "You sell this skill all day. You're not bad at selling. You're avoiding selling yourself."
- **Therapist who can't ask for the rate:** "You teach people how to ask for what they need. You can do that for yourself."
- **Productivity coach who can't get themselves to act:** "You teach this every day. The principles apply."

The same move also runs in non-coaching domains: the leader who teaches feedback but can't give it to a peer; the parent who teaches kindness but can't speak kindly to themselves; the executive who teaches accountability but can't hold themselves accountable. In each case, the structural match is the lever.
