# You Are Going to Get Annihilated

## The Move

When a client, mid-intensity, reports fear of annihilation ("I'm so afraid I'm gonna— it feels like this fear of annihilation, like I'm going to hurt..."), do not soothe the fear. Affirm it. Then specify what is actually being annihilated — which is the identity, not the self.

The move has two beats:

1. **"Yes. You are going to get annihilated."** The affirmation is fast and direct. The body needs the witness to confirm what the body is already reading. Trying to talk the body out of the annihilation-fear creates a credibility gap — the body knows something is happening, and a witness who denies it stops being trustworthy.

2. **"The identity of being a [whatever] is about to fucking get annihilated."** The specification redirects the fear to its accurate target. The self is not getting annihilated. The identity is. The identity that the client has been organizing around — the train-wreck identity, the broken-identity, the survivor-identity — is what is dying. The actual self is what remains.

"You are. No, you're going to get annihilated." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

"That's right, that's how this works. The identity, the identity, the identity of being a train wreck is about to fucking get annihilated." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

## Why This Move Works

The fear of annihilation, when it arrives during deep emotional work, is accurate information. Something is in fact about to be annihilated. The body is correctly detecting an existential threat — to the structure the person has been operating from.

The standard coaching move at this point is to reassure: "you're safe, nothing bad is going to happen, you don't have to be afraid." This move treats the fear as a misreading. The body, hearing the reassurance, registers that the witness does not understand what is happening. The trust drops. The work stalls.

The affirm-and-specify move treats the fear as accurate and gives it precise content. Yes, annihilation. Yes, this is how this works. The annihilation is the work. The fear is correct. What it has been fearing is also correct — the identity that has been doing the holding is about to come apart.

What the body needs from the witness is not denial of the annihilation. It needs confirmation that the annihilation is survivable, that someone is here who has seen this before, that the identity-death is the point. The affirm-and-specify move delivers all three through tone, content, and timing.

This is the in-session coaching application of [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]]. The principle: offer yourself up to annihilation and the part that cannot be annihilated is you. The move: when the client is at the edge of this principle in real time, do not pull them back. Confirm the edge. Specify what is going over it.

## How to Run the Move

1. **Listen for the annihilation-language.** "I'm scared I'm going to hurt someone," "I feel like I'll explode," "I feel like I'm going to disappear," "I'm scared of madness," "I'm scared I won't come out the other side," "fear of annihilation." These phrases are the body reporting an identity-death-in-progress.

2. **Affirm fast.** "Yes." "You are." "That's right." Do not hesitate. The hesitation reads as the coach also being scared of the annihilation, which doubles the client's fear.

3. **Specify what is dying.** Name the specific identity. "The identity of being a [train wreck/broken person/wreck/etc.] is about to fucking get annihilated." The specificity matters. Generic "your old self" is too abstract; the body needs the specific story-of-the-self that the work is dismantling.

4. **Use the strong word.** "Fucking get annihilated." See [[use-the-strong-word-for-somatic-landing]]. The strong language matches the intensity the body is in. A polite version ("dissolved," "transformed") does not land at the same depth.

5. **Stay with the affirmation if the client doubles down on the fear.** Often the client will say "but I feel like I'll hurt someone." The move is to differentiate: the hurt is to the identity, not to a person. "It's you. You think you're a train wreck. You— that is not going to last if you do this." The hurt the body is anticipating is happening to the self-image, not to anyone in the room.

6. **Pair with a clarifying question if needed.** "What just changed in your body when I said that?" The question invites the body to notice the affirmation has been delivered and registered. The body can then update its reading.

## What to Watch For

The move is landing when:

- The client's body relaxes slightly — not because the fear is gone but because the fear has been seen. Being-seen-in-the-fear is itself regulating.
- The client says "yes" or "I know" or some other recognition that the affirmation matches their experience.
- The client is able to continue with the work rather than stopping at the fear. The fear was the gate; the affirmation unlocks it.
- The eyes get more present rather than more distant.

The move is not landing when:

- The client doubles down on the fear's interpretation ("no, I really am going to destroy something"). The affirmation has not been received; the specification needs to be more concrete or the work needs to slow down.
- The client dissociates — the fear has overwhelmed the system and the affirmation did not arrive in time. Slow down. Use somatic anchors. Come back to the work at a lower intensity.
- The client argues with the diagnosis ("but I'm not really a train wreck"). The specification has named something the client cannot yet hold. Adjust to whatever the actual operative identity is.

## The Critical Distinction: Affirmation Is Not Encouragement

The move is not "yes, go ahead and destroy yourself." The move is "yes, the identity that has been doing this work is about to come apart, and that is the point of the work you came here to do."

If the client is in actual danger to self or others — not metaphorically annihilating the identity, but literally moving toward harm — the move does not apply. The fear in that case is reporting an actual operational risk and needs different handling.

The annihilation-affirmation is for the fear of the felt-experience of identity-death. The signature: the fear is about something the client cannot specify in concrete operational terms ("I'm going to hurt — I don't know who, I don't know how, just hurt"), and the fear arrives at the moment the work is reaching the edge of the existing structure. That is the body reporting identity-annihilation. The affirm-and-specify move is the correct response.

## Variations

**The "wait, wait" anchoring.** In the source session, when the client started to speak past the affirmation, Joe paused her: "wait, wait, wait, you are, you are gonna get annihilated." The pause holds the affirmation in place long enough for the body to take it in. Without the pause the client moves on too quickly and the affirmation does not land.

**The specification without the affirmation.** When the fear is less explicit but the identity-defense is operating, sometimes only the specification is needed: "the identity is about to come apart." This is the move at lower intensity.

**Pair with the "of course" frame.** If after the annihilation has begun, the client surfaces grief about why the identity formed in the first place, the natural next move is [[of-course-you-had-to-be-numb]] or the corresponding "of course you had to be this way" — honoring the original adaptation as the annihilation completes.

## Tone

Direct, fast, certain. The voice carries the same confidence as the affirmation itself. Joe is not asking the client to be reassured by his presence; he is offering the affirmation that the body needs.

The pace is critical. The affirmation must arrive within a second or two of the fear. Hesitation reads as doubt. Doubt destabilizes. The faster delivery sustains the work.

The strong language ("fucking get annihilated") is not aggression. It is precision at intensity. The body needs the words at the same energy-level as the felt experience.
