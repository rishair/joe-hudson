# One-Minute Anger With Eye Contact and Depression Measurement

## The Move

When the [[anger-flips-to-shame-on-arrival]] read has just landed and the client has confirmed the pattern, the move is a compact in-session experiment that lets the client experience the principle [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] in their own body in about ninety seconds.

The protocol has three parts:

1. **Measure.** The client feels the level of depression (or shame, or "the heaviness," whatever the downstream symptom is) in their system right now. They do not have to name the amount; just feel it.

2. **Act.** For one minute, the client performs anger out loud, using the script of refusing the shoulds they were judging themselves for. The client is told they are "just an actor" — the performance does not have to be real. The instruction includes maintaining eye contact with the coach throughout.

3. **Remeasure.** At the end of the minute, the client feels the depression level again. Almost always: it is significantly lower.

The entire protocol takes less than two minutes. The point is the experiential demonstration: the body learns, in the most immediate possible way, that the depression it has been carrying is the unmoved anger, and that one minute of moving the anger outward depletes the depression measurably.

"We're going to just move a little bit of anger, just a little bit. And your job is to right now feel how much depression, what you're calling depression, is in your system right now. Just kind of feel the level. Just, you don't have to say how much, you just have to feel the level. Great. That's it. Now we're going to just — you're just going to do — you're just going to do this in an angry voice and you're make-believing." -- Joe Hudson, This is why you're so hard on yourself

## When Joe Uses This Move

- Immediately after [[anger-flips-to-shame-on-arrival]] has been named and the client has recognized the pattern
- The client has presented with chronic self-judgment, depression, or shame as the surface concern
- The session has confirmed via the [[what-would-you-have-to-feel-if-you-couldnt-judge-yourself-for-x]] question that anger is being suppressed at the moment of arrival
- The client is in a setting where the body can briefly raise its voice (not a public space, not somewhere they will be overheard)
- The client has not yet had an experiential demonstration that moving anger changes the felt state
- A take-home practice ([[get-angry-in-the-car]], [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]]) is going to be prescribed and benefits from being preceded by an in-session demonstration

The move is not used when the client is already in active anger release and just needs to continue; it is used when the client has never (or rarely) experienced moving anger outward and needs the proof-of-concept in their own body before they will trust the take-home version.

## How the Protocol Works

### Step 1: Establish the Pre-Measurement

Joe instructs the client to feel the level of the downstream symptom — most commonly the depression, but it can also be shame, "the heaviness," "the stuckness," whatever the client has been calling the chronic state. The instruction is to *feel*, not to *rate*. No number is required.

The purpose of the pre-measurement is to give the body a felt baseline that can be compared against the post-measurement. Without the baseline, the post-state has nothing to be measured against and the demonstration loses its operational impact.

The pre-measurement also serves a second function: it gets the client into their body before the act of performance begins. A client who has been talking about their pattern from the head is operating in a different register than a client who has just spent ten seconds feeling the depression level. The pre-measurement does the register-shift.

### Step 2: Set the Acting Frame

Joe explicitly names that the client is acting. "You're just going to do this in an angry voice and you're make-believing. So it doesn't have to be real. You're just like acting. You're like a great actor."

The acting frame does two things:

- It removes the requirement that the client feel angry first. The whole problem is that the client cannot access anger; if accessing it were the precondition for the move, the move would not work for the people who need it most. The acting frame says: skip the access. Just do the motion.
- It bypasses the self-judgment about doing anger badly. An actor who is performing does not have to defend the performance as authentic. The performance is the performance. The frame protects the client from the meta-layer of self-judgment about the experiment itself.

The acting frame is consistent with [[treat-yourself-as-actor-of-emotion]] and the broader actor-performance teaching. The one-minute experiment is a high-intensity, time-bounded application of that frame.

### Step 3: Provide the Script

Joe provides the specific script for the one-minute performance. The script is the "I don't have to" refusal of the self-judgments the client has just named:

"You're basically just going to be like, I don't have to do shit. I don't have to fucking prove myself. I don't have to be good enough. I don't have to fucking, like, be great. I don't have to fucking, like, live up to my potential. I don't have to do fucking shit." -- Joe Hudson, This is why you're so hard on yourself

The script has three structural features:

1. **"I don't have to."** The refusal form is the operational opposite of the shoulds. Each "I don't have to" releases the obligation the corresponding should had installed. The script is the verbal form of the energetic refusal.

2. **The shoulds named in the session are used as the targets.** "Be good enough," "live up to my potential" — these were the client's specific self-judgments earlier. Using the client's specific shoulds (not generic ones) lets the body push against the exact shoulds it has been holding.

3. **Free use of swearing.** The "fucking" is in the script for a reason: it permissions the kind of unmodulated speech the body has been forbidden. The body recognizes the permission and uses it. A polite version of the script would underdeliver because the politeness keeps the muscular constraint in place.

The script does not have to be memorized. Joe provides it as a model and the client adapts. The structural feature is the refusal form; the specific words can vary.

### Step 4: The Eye Contact Specification

A distinctive instruction: maintain eye contact with the coach throughout the one minute.

"Keeping eye contact with me while you do it is — will be, like, priceless." -- Joe Hudson, This is why you're so hard on yourself

The eye contact does several things at once:

- It prevents the client from collapsing inward as the anger rises. A client who closes their eyes or looks away tends to fold back into the inward direction the anger has always taken; the eye contact keeps the energy moving outward.
- It uses the coach as an anchor for the outward motion. The anger has been pointed inward for decades; pointing it at the coach (even though the coach is not the target) gives the energy an external destination that the body can register.
- It catches the client if shame tries to recapture the anger mid-performance. The coach can see the moment the anger starts to invert and can keep the client moving with their gaze.
- It demonstrates that the coach is not afraid of the anger and is not going to react to it. The body uses the coach's non-reactivity as data that the anger is safe to release. See [[the-bodys-non-reactivity-as-permission-signal]] conceptually.
- It produces a quality of intimacy that is unusual for anger expression. Most anger is expressed alone, at no one, or at someone the person is fighting. Anger expressed in eye contact with a calm witness is a different experience. The body registers the difference and learns that anger can be expressed in connection rather than in disconnection.

### Step 5: Hold for One Minute

The one-minute duration is deliberate. Long enough to actually do the work, short enough to be tolerable for a client who has not done this before. The minute is roughly the time required for the body to fully commit to the performance after the initial awkwardness.

In the source session, the client's first lines were small and somewhat apologetic ("Hey man, it's fine. You don't have to be good enough all the time because you're a human"). By the end of the minute the script had escalated into more direct refusal ("I only have to keep up with my own"). The first thirty seconds were the body finding the motion; the second thirty seconds were the body using it.

If the client trails off before the minute is up, Joe encourages them to continue. The full minute is the dose. Stopping early underdelivers.

### Step 6: The Post-Measurement

At the end of the minute: "Now feel your depression."

The client checks the felt state. In the source session: "That was fucking 1 minute. 1 minute. Wow." The change is immediate and unmistakable. The client's body felt the depression depleted in real time.

The post-measurement is where the demonstration lands. Without it, the client might experience the one minute as just an exercise; with it, they have the felt evidence in their own body that the chronic state they have been carrying is responsive to outward anger movement.

## What Joe Says After

The closing frame names what just happened, in the simplest possible terms:

"That was fucking 1 minute. 1 minute. Wow. So that's why you judge yourself because you're not letting yourself feel that shit." -- Joe Hudson, This is why you're so hard on yourself

The naming connects the demonstration back to the presenting concern. The client came in asking "Why am I always judging myself?" — and now has a concrete answer in their body: the judging is what is being done in place of the anger. When the anger moves, the judging has less to do, and the depression that was the inventory of unmoved anger drops.

The closing instruction to the client is to let the change continue:

"Yes, let it, let it, enjoy it, and then let your, let your emotions move, and all of this will change really, really quickly." -- Joe Hudson, This is why you're so hard on yourself

## Why This Works

The move stacks three mechanisms:

1. **Embodied learning of the principle.** [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] is true regardless of whether the client believes it intellectually. But intellectual belief does not move shame. The body experiencing the depression-to-relief transition in ninety seconds is what makes the principle actionable. After this, the client cannot un-know that the depression and the anger are linked.

2. **The acting frame removes the access-precondition.** Clients with chronic self-judgment usually cannot find their anger when asked to "feel it." The acting frame lets them perform the motion without first finding the felt state. Often the felt state arrives partway through the performance ([[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] is the broader practice this mechanism appears in).

3. **The eye contact prevents the reinversion.** This is the most distinctive feature. Without the eye contact, the anger can rise during the performance and then quietly reinvert into shame the moment the client looks down, looks away, or closes their eyes. The eye contact keeps the anger pointing outward through the duration of the performance, which lets the depletion-of-depression happen.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not a substitute for the take-home anger practice. The in-session demonstration is the proof-of-concept; the daily practice is what produces the lasting change. After the move, Joe typically prescribes [[get-angry-in-the-car]] or a similar daily form.
- It is not a one-shot fix for chronic depression. The depression that depleted in the minute is the depression that was sitting in the body that day. The chronic pattern that produces the depression will reproduce it unless the underlying anger gets a daily outward channel.
- It is not the same as anger therapy or trauma work. The move is a compact experiential demonstration, not a long process. Heavier work with the underlying anger source belongs in longer sessions or in dedicated trauma modalities.
- It is not the dominating-form-anger demonstration ([[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] §coach-modeled-version). The coach in this move provides the eye-contact anchor and the script, but does not model the anger themselves. The client is the performer throughout.

## Tone

The instructions are delivered briskly, with a touch of playfulness around the acting frame. The brisk pace is protective: a slower, more solemn delivery would activate the client's self-judgment about whether they are doing it "right." The brisk pace says: this is just an experiment, do it, see what happens.

The playfulness around the "you're like a great actor" framing is important. The client has likely never been encouraged to swear and yell at full volume in front of another person; the playfulness signals that this is permitted, even welcomed, and is not a serious or scary thing.

The post-measurement is delivered with quiet wonder ("That was fucking 1 minute. 1 minute. Wow."). The wonder is partly the coach's genuine appreciation of the body's responsiveness and partly a model for the client: this is a thing to be amazed by, not analyzed.
