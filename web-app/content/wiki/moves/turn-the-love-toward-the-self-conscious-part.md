# Turn the Love Toward the Self-Conscious Part

## The Move

When a person, having softened into love for others or awareness of others, reports that self-consciousness has just arrived ("they won't love me," "I want to be something"), have them turn the love they are already feeling toward the self-conscious part itself. Don't try to dispel the self-consciousness. Don't argue with it. Use the love that's already in the room as the medium for welcoming the part that fears not being loved.

"So we're gonna, we're gonna let that self-consciousness be there. I want you to turn that love towards the self-consciousness. So feel the love that you have for all these people, and then just turn it towards the self-consciousness." -- Joe Hudson, The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself

The move is precise. It does not ask the person to manufacture love (which they couldn't). It does not ask the person to love their self-consciousness as a separate operation (which would be too abstract). It asks them to redirect the love that is already activated in the body — love for the people in the room, love for the awareness that just opened — toward the small, scared, self-conscious part that has just shown up.

This is a variant of [[why-dont-you-just-love-yourself-as-you-are-right-now]] and [[turn-the-love-on-yourself-what-did-you-do]], with a specific tactical addition: the target is not "yourself" in the abstract but the *specific protective part* that has just arrived. That precision is what makes the move work when general self-love instructions don't.

## When Joe Uses It

The move arrives at a specific moment in a session — when the person has just opened (softened, found love, found awareness) and the small self has just rushed back in to manage the opening.

The signs:
- The person reports a felt expansion ("I feel love for these people," "I feel awareness")
- Immediately after, they report a contraction ("self-consciousness came in," "I want to be something," "they won't love me," "I'm afraid I'll be nothing")
- The two reports are within seconds of each other
- The contraction has a small-self quality — fear of not being loved, fear of being too much, fear of being nothing

The move is the response to that exact moment. The opening produced the love-feeling in the body; the contraction is the part that needs that love but cannot ask for it; the move closes the loop.

The move is also useful any time a defensive part shows up in a session and a love-feeling has just been activated by another exchange. The love-feeling does not have to be self-generated; it can come from a memory, a person in the room, a recently-named gratitude. As long as the love is felt in the body, it can be redirected.

## What This Move Produces

The most common response is a softening of the self-conscious part. The part stops trying to manage and starts being received. The person may report a felt loosening, sometimes tears, sometimes warmth.

A secondary response is the next layer surfacing — usually the fear that the love won't be received, or the historical wound that made the self-conscious part necessary. "It's afraid that I'll just be nothing." This is the part speaking. The next move depends on what is spoken — sometimes [[you-are-nothing-as-vast-awareness]] is the pointing-move that follows.

A third pattern: the person reports that the love won't go toward the self-conscious part. "It's like the love won't direct there." This is data. The strategy that built the self-conscious part has been built precisely on keeping love away from it ("if I love this scared part, I'll be weak"). The next move is to stay with the resistance rather than push through it.

From the source session:

- **Client:** "Yeah, I just, I feel like this self-consciousness comes in."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So we're gonna, we're gonna let that self-consciousness be there. I want you to turn that love towards the self-consciousness. So feel the love that you have for all these people, and then just turn it towards the self-consciousness."
- **Client:** "It's afraid that I'll just be nothing."
- **Joe:** "You are nothing."
- *(Source: The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself)*

The move surfaced the part's actual fear ("I'll just be nothing"). The fear is the next door. Joe's response ("you are nothing") pivots to the [[you-are-nothing-as-vast-awareness]] pointing-move.

## Why It Works

The self-conscious part is operating on the assumption that it must protect the person from not being loved. It has constructed an entire strategy — be something, perform, manage — around the belief that love is conditional. The strategy works exactly until love arrives unconditionally, at which point the part panics because the strategy's job is being threatened.

The move offers the part exactly what its strategy was designed to seek. The love it has been working to earn is now being directed at it, with no condition, no demand to be something other than what it is. The part doesn't have to be different to receive the love. It can be exactly the scared, self-conscious thing it is, and the love is being given to it.

The part's first response is usually suspicion ("this can't be real, the love is for other people, not for me"). The suspicion is the strategy resisting its own dissolution. If the move is held — if the love continues to be directed at the part without retreat — the part eventually starts to receive. The receiving is what dissolves the part's need to protect.

The structural insight: the self-conscious part cannot be argued out of existence, and it cannot be ignored. The part has to be loved. Specifically, it has to be loved as the part it is, with all its small-ness and fear and grasping. Loved differently than the love it has been chasing — which has always been conditional on being something else.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not redirect to general self-love.** "Now just love yourself" loses the specificity. The move is to love this part, the one that just spoke up, in this moment, with its specific fear.
- **Do not try to dispel the self-consciousness.** Saying "you don't need to be self-conscious, they love you" continues the part's strategy by adding new evidence to the same earning-love system. The part is not soothed by being told it doesn't need to exist.
- **Do not skip the body.** The love has to be felt. If the move becomes a verbal exercise ("I love my self-conscious part"), it is the head doing the work and the part is not receiving. The love is a felt operation — the warmth in the chest, the softening of the throat — being directed.
- **Do not move on if the love won't go.** "The love won't direct there" is the work. Stay with the resistance. Often the resistance is itself a part that needs the love. Joe's move sometimes becomes: "Then turn the love toward the part that won't let the love in."
- **Do not require the part to disappear.** The self-consciousness can stay. It is just being loved while it is there. The disappearing, if it happens, happens on its own.

## Dialogue Example: The Full Arc

From the source session, the move occurs near the end of a sequence and produces a chain reaction:

- **Joe:** "Let's go all the way to the awareness for a minute. Put all of your attention into awareness."
- **Client:** "Feel my, my body feels vibrating. I could cry more."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, you're welcome too."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I feel, I feel a lot of love for these people."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Oh wow. So all this pressure has gotten in the way of your love and your awareness."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I just, I feel like this self-consciousness comes in."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So we're gonna, we're gonna let that self-consciousness be there. I want you to turn that love towards the self-consciousness. So feel the love that you have for all these people, and then just turn it towards the self-consciousness."
- **Client:** "It's afraid that I'll just be nothing."
- **Joe:** "You are nothing."

The move is one sentence. It produces the part's voice ("it's afraid that I'll just be nothing"). The part's voice is the door to the next move. The session continues from there to the pointing-to-awareness work.

The sequence shows the structural logic: open, contract, redirect, surface the fear, point to what the fear has been protecting. Without the redirect, the contraction would have absorbed the opening. With the redirect, the contraction becomes a door.

## Variations

- **When the love is for a child or pet:** "Feel the love you have for [child/pet] and turn it toward the part of you that's scared." See [[turn-the-love-on-yourself-what-did-you-do]] for the experiment-version.
- **When the love is for nature or art:** "Feel that love and turn it toward the part of you that doesn't think it can have this."
- **When the love is for the coach:** "Feel that love between us and turn it toward the part of you that's about to close down."
- **When the self-conscious part is about competence:** "Turn the love toward the part of you that's afraid it's not good enough."
- **When the self-conscious part is about being too much:** "Turn the love toward the part of you that's afraid it's too much."
- **When the part is about being unworthy:** "Turn the love toward the part of you that doesn't think it deserves to be here."

The structural form is constant: identify the live love-feeling in the body, identify the specific defensive part that just showed up, redirect the love at the part.

## Tone

Warm, intimate, gentle. The move is delivered as an invitation, not an instruction. "I want you to turn that love towards the self-consciousness" carries the request as a "want you to" rather than a "do this."

The "we're gonna let that self-consciousness be there" framing is also crucial. The "we" is shared agency — the coach is not asking the client to do anything alone. The "let it be there" gives the self-conscious part permission to exist before the love is directed at it. The permission is part of the welcome.

Joe often pauses after issuing the move. The pause is the room for the redirect to happen. The body needs a moment to find the love and to direct it. Talking through the pause would interfere.
