# Your Job Is to Answer As the Resistance

## The Move

When a client is in resistance — visibly bracing, hedging, going internal, defending against the conversation — invite them to speak AS the resistance instead of about it. The shift is grammatical and structural: instead of "I'm resisting because...", the client speaks from inside the resistance: "I am resisting X. I am resisting because Y."

The setup matters. Before the speaking, the coach grants explicit permission for the resistance to continue. The point is not to change the resistance. The point is to give it voice while it is operating.

"If I'm just going to talk to the resistance, so your job isn't to answer like you, your job is just to answer as the resistance for a moment. What is it that you're resisting? I'm not asking you to change. You can resist as much as you like. I just would like to know what you're resisting." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Ask for What You Want

The structural moves inside the setup:

1. **Name the resistance as the addressee.** "I'm going to talk to the resistance." The resistance is treated as a thing that can be addressed, not a quality the person is doing.
2. **Reassign the speaking role.** "Your job isn't to answer like you, your job is just to answer as the resistance." The client is not asked to introspect — they are asked to voice.
3. **Grant explicit permission.** "I'm not asking you to change. You can resist as much as you like." The permission removes the pressure to fix the resistance, which is what was keeping the resistance armored.
4. **Ask the resistance what it is resisting.** "What is it that you're resisting?" The question is directed AT the resistance, not at the client.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the client has just admitted to resistance in their body ("I feel a lot of heat right now in my body") and the resistance has become the central object of the conversation.
- When the conversation has been progressing intellectually but the body has been bracing, and the bracing has the conversational energy.
- When the client cannot describe the feeling underneath the resistance because the resistance is in the way — but the resistance itself can speak.
- When asking the client direct questions about their feelings produces hedging or "I don't know," but the resistance has a clear, available position.
- When a part of the client (a defense, a refusal, a holding-back) is the actual leader of the moment and addressing the surface self would be addressing the wrong location.

The move is a specific case of the larger principle "address what is actually here," with the recognition that resistance is often what is actually here, even when the conversation is nominally about something else.

## What It Produces

- The client often relaxes slightly when the permission is granted. The resistance was waiting to be told to change, and it has been told it does not have to. The pressure drops.
- The resistance speaks. What it says is usually a direct, terse answer that the client could not have offered from the "I" position. From the source session: "Sadness." One word. The resistance was resisting sadness. The client-as-self could not have surfaced that as quickly; the resistance-as-speaker did.
- The conversation moves to a different layer. Instead of working with what the client thinks about the problem, the work is now happening with what the defense system is actually doing.
- The next move opens up — once the resistance has named what it is resisting, the resistance can be asked what it is afraid of, what it is protecting, what would happen if it stopped. The chain proceeds quickly because the resistance is willing to speak.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the resistance names what it's resisting (e.g., "Sadness"):** Continue speaking to the resistance, not back to the client. "And what makes you resist it?" The continued addressing of the resistance keeps the layer in play.
- **If the resistance names a feeling:** Move toward the fear underneath. "What are you scared will happen if [the feeling] is seen / felt / known?" The resistance is protecting against a feared outcome; surfacing the feared outcome is the next step.
- **If the client slips back into the "I" position ("I don't want to feel sad"):** Don't correct. Just keep addressing the resistance. The client will follow. The shift is energetic, not grammatical — the coach is talking to the resistance, and the client's resistance is talking back, regardless of what pronouns either uses.
- **If the resistance produces a vague answer ("I don't know"):** Treat the I-don't-know as the resistance's response, not as a failure. Stay with it. "Okay, the resistance doesn't know. Or — does the resistance know but not want to say?" The resistance can be invited to speak more directly without being asked to stop being resistance.
- **If the resistance produces an answer the client has not been able to articulate before:** Note the surfacing internally but do not make a fuss. The conversation moves quickly when the resistance is given voice; lingering on individual answers slows the work.

## Why It Works

The resistance is a system that the client has been managing-around for years. The standard moves the client knows are: try to stop the resistance, try to override it, try to understand it. None of those moves engage the resistance as a participant. They all treat it as an obstacle.

This move flips the role. The resistance is addressed as a participant. It is asked what it has to say. The permission is granted to keep doing what it does. With those three structural changes, the resistance becomes willing to speak, because for the first time it is not under attack.

What the resistance says is usually load-bearing. The resistance has been the engine driving the surface behavior; once it speaks, the engine becomes audible. The client did not know what they were resisting — but the resistance knew. Voicing the resistance externalizes information that was previously locked inside the defense.

The somatic substrate: the client often reports body sensations (heat, tension, contraction) that confirm the resistance is in play. The speaking-as-the-resistance move uses those sensations as the access point. The client speaks from where the heat is, where the tension is, where the contraction is. The body, not the mind, is the speaker. The mind can join afterward.

## Dialogue Example

The full move in context, from the source session:

- **Client:** "I feel a lot of heat right now in my body."
- **Joe:** "Okay."
- *(Joe traces the heat back through tennis history — the same kind of resistance that's happening right now. Then:)*
- **Joe:** "Gotcha. If I'm just going to talk to the resistance, so your job isn't to answer like you, your job is just to answer as the resistance for a moment. What is it that you're resisting? I'm not asking you to change. You can resist as much as you like. I just would like to know what you're resisting."
- **Client:** "Sadness."
- **Joe:** "And what makes you resist it?"
- **Client:** "Not wanting to be seen as sad."
- **Joe:** "What happens to you if you're seen as sad?"
- **Client:** "That feels like, um, quite scary."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Ask for What You Want)*

The exchange takes seconds. Each response is short. The shortness is the data — the resistance, when given voice, speaks directly and economically. The client could not have produced this content from the "I" position in this amount of time.

## Tone

Light, almost casual. The instruction is delivered as a small invitation, not as a serious technique. "If I'm just going to talk to the resistance" carries the small permission of "let's try this thing." The casualness keeps the move from becoming heavy, which would re-arm the resistance.

The permission ("you can resist as much as you like") is delivered with a small warmth. The resistance is being told it is not in trouble. The warmth is what makes the permission credible.

The questions that follow ("what is it that you're resisting?" "what makes you resist it?") are delivered with curiosity. The coach is genuinely interested in what the resistance has to say. The curiosity is the field that makes the resistance willing to speak.

## Variations

**The chair-work variant.** Have the client physically move to a different chair to speak as the resistance. The physical relocation reinforces the role-shift. Useful when the client is highly intellectual and needs the body cue.

**The two-voice variant.** Address the resistance, then address the part being resisted, alternately. "What is the resistance saying? What is the sadness saying? Okay, resistance, what do you say to that?" The dialogue between the parts becomes the work.

**The witnessing variant.** "Let's just watch the resistance for a minute. What is it doing? What posture is it in? What is it pointed at?" Useful when the client is too defended to speak from the resistance directly. The watching is a smaller commitment than the speaking.

**The thanking variant.** After the resistance has spoken, thank it. "Thank you for telling me. Thank you for protecting them." The thanks completes the addressing — the resistance has been heard, and the hearing is acknowledged. The thanks is also a signal that the resistance is welcome to keep doing what it does, which prevents the work from becoming a new self-attack on the resistance.

## Distinction From [[talk-to-the-part-that-knows]]

These two moves are complementary but distinct.

- **Talk to the part that knows** is a stance the coach holds: address the seeing-self underneath the storytelling-self. It is about who the coach is talking to.
- **Answer as the resistance** is an explicit instruction to the client: speak from the resisting part. It is about who the client is speaking as.

The first is silent; the second is announced. The first works moment-to-moment in any session; the second is deployed when resistance is the central object.

Both serve the same underlying principle: the client contains multiple parts, and the work happens by engaging the right part at the right time. The seeing-self is the part the coach addresses to do change-work. The resisting-self is the part the coach addresses to do defense-work. Both are legitimate participants.

## Anti-Pattern Risk

The move fails if it becomes performance. If the client is producing "what the resistance would say" rather than letting the resistance actually speak, the move is doing intellectual work, not body-level work. The diagnostic: is the response coming quickly and economically, or is the client constructing it? Constructed responses are the resistance still being managed, not voiced.

The move also fails if the coach tries to argue with what the resistance says. The resistance just told you it's resisting sadness because of fear of being seen. If the coach responds with "but you wouldn't really be rejected for being sad," the coach has gone to argument-with-the-resistance, which re-arms it. The move requires accepting what the resistance says as data, not as a proposition to debate.

The move also fails when run on a client who is not actually in resistance but is hedging or confused or sad. Reaching for the move when the actual energy in the room is something else produces a forced exercise. The move is for when resistance is the active engine.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The client reports body sensations associated with resistance (heat, tension, bracing, contraction)
- The client is hedging or going internal in a way that has the conversational center of gravity
- Direct questions have been producing intellectualized or evasive answers
- A historical pattern of resistance has just been mapped (e.g., the same resistance that shows up now showed up in childhood / sports / earlier work)
- The client is willing to do an experiment

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward asking the resistance what it is afraid will happen if it stops resisting — surfacing the feared outcome
- Toward asking the resistance what it is protecting against — surfacing the underneath material
- Toward [[the-only-way-to-not-be-rejected-is-to-reject-yourself-first]] if the resistance reveals it is protecting against external rejection that is being preempted by self-rejection
- Toward [[find-anything-worth-rejecting]] if the resistance is protecting against being seen with material that turns out not to be worth rejecting
- Toward welcoming what the resistance has been resisting — once the feared material is named, the welcoming move opens up
- Toward [[i-love-the-rejected-part-of-me-let-them-love-it-in-the-coach-first]] if the resistance is protecting a part the client cannot directly love
