# Ask for Help with Specifics

## The Move

The leader who feels alone at the top of an organization (Position 4 in [[the-four-authority-positions]]) has typically been asking for help in ways that ensure they will not receive it. The move is to ask with three components present:

1. **A specific request.** Not "I need help" but the actual thing.
2. **The criteria for what would constitute the request being met.** Not just the deliverable but the conditions that would make it land.
3. **A direct ask of a specific person to take it on.**

Joe lays the move out by contrasting two versions of the same ask:

The vague version (which guarantees the help will not arrive):

"It's not, 'God damn it, why can't you just fucking get the sales numbers up?'" -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The specific version (which actually produces help):

"Wow. I feel like everything I'm doing to get the sales numbers up isn't working, and I really need your help to get the sales numbers up. And what I need to feel confident that the sales number is going to be up is that you have — that you're tracking not just how much sales we're making, but how many steps we're making, incremental steps we're making on a weekly or biweekly basis. So I know that the work is getting done. And so I really need help knowing that we're going to hit our numbers because we're making these incremental steps. And how would you feel about taking that on and making it so I don't have to worry about that anymore?" -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The second ask is longer. It contains real vulnerability ("everything I'm doing isn't working"), a specific operational request (track incremental steps weekly or biweekly), the underlying need being served (confidence that we will hit the numbers), and a direct invitation to a specific person to take it on. This ask can be answered. The first ask cannot.

## Why the Vague Version Fails

The lonely-leader pattern produces a particular failure mode in help-asking. The leader, operating from "nobody can really do this," asks in a way that confirms the operating assumption.

"Often the way those people ask to get their needs met, to help to get helped, will ensure that they don't get it." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The mechanisms:

- **The vague ask sounds like blame.** "Why can't you just get the sales numbers up?" lands as accusation, not request. The recipient defends rather than offers help.
- **The vague ask has no surface to grip.** "I need help" with no specifics gives no one anywhere to start. The default response is to wait for more direction, which never comes.
- **The vague ask hides the actual need.** The leader has not actually said what would make them feel confident. The recipient cannot satisfy a criterion they have not been told.
- **The vague ask preserves the leader's position.** If the help cannot be provided, the lonely-leader narrative is reinforced. The vagueness functions to protect the narrative.

The leader is not consciously doing this. The pattern is structural. The work is not to blame the leader for the bad asking; the work is to install the better asking.

## Why the Specific Version Works

The specific ask has four properties that the vague ask lacks:

1. **Vulnerability that lands as humanness.** "Everything I'm doing isn't working" is the leader's actual state. Naming it openly shifts the relational position from boss-issuing-demand to person-asking-for-collaboration.
2. **Operational handles.** "Track incremental steps weekly or biweekly" is something a specific person can do this week.
3. **Stated criteria.** "What I need to feel confident is..." tells the recipient what would actually constitute help, not just what would constitute task completion.
4. **A direct invitation.** "How would you feel about taking that on?" assigns the work without assuming the answer. The recipient is being asked, not told.

The combination converts the relationship from one in which the leader is alone with the problem to one in which the recipient is now genuinely partnered with the leader on it.

## When To Use This Move

- A leader who is exhausted and reports their team is not stepping up
- A CEO who describes asking for help and not getting it, used as evidence of being alone
- A founder whose first response to overwhelm is to take on more themselves
- Any leader who is running [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] in a form that includes complaint about not getting help

The diagnostic: listen to how the leader has been asking. If the asks have been general (the vague version), the move installs the specific version as the next experiment. If the asks have been specific and still not landing, the work is elsewhere (often in the team's capacity or in whether the leader is actually willing to release the work).

## The Companion Move

This move works best paired with [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]]. The lonely-leader narrative has two halves: "I am alone in this" (which the specific-ask move addresses on the future-facing side) and "the team is not showing up for me" (which the apology move addresses on the backward-facing side). Both halves need work for the position to dissolve. The specific ask gives the team something to step into; the apology gives them recognition for what they have already been doing.

## What Tends to Happen

When the move is delivered cleanly:

- The recipient is often visibly relieved. The vague version has been frustrating them too — they have wanted to help and have not known how. The specific ask gives them an entry point.
- The recipient frequently elaborates on the ask, adding their own ideas. The specificity provides a scaffold for their own competence to engage.
- The relationship between the leader and the recipient shifts. The hierarchy is intact, but the affective field is now collaborative rather than extractive.
- The leader experiences receiving help. Sometimes for the first time in years. This is data the lonely-leader narrative cannot accommodate.

When the move does not land:

- The specificity is delivered with old contempt. The form is right but the tone is still "why can't you" — the help does not arrive.
- The leader cannot actually release the work. They make the specific ask and then immediately add caveats and conditions that re-take the work. The recipient cannot step in.
- The recipient genuinely cannot do the thing. This is real and is the place where the move ends and the personnel question begins.

## What This Move Replaces

The default patterns the lonely leader runs instead of this move:

- **Demanding without specifying.** "Why can't you just do X." The team experiences this as criticism, not invitation.
- **Doing it themselves.** Skipping the ask entirely. Reinforces the lonely-leader narrative and burns the leader out.
- **Asking generally and then doing it themselves anyway.** The ask is performed but the leader immediately takes the work back. The team learns that asks are not real.
- **Resentful silence.** Stewing in not-getting-help without ever asking for it cleanly. Maximum burnout, minimum movement.

Each pattern reinforces the operating assumption. The specific ask interrupts all four.

## Tone

The tone of the specific ask is open, undefended, slightly slowed-down. The vulnerability has to be felt for the ask to land. A rushed or businesslike delivery still produces an unanswerable request.

The pace is part of the move. The vague version is usually delivered fast — the leader is frustrated, the words come quickly, the ask is finished before the recipient has registered what is being asked. The specific version requires the leader to slow down enough to name the actual situation, the actual need, the actual criteria, and the actual person being asked. The slowness is the carrier of the change.
