# Name the Avoidance Cascade From Essence to Addiction

## The Move

The person is asking how to stop a compulsive behavior (porn, procrastination, scrolling). The behavior is downstream of a four-stage cascade: essential self → thinking → shame thinking → behavior. The move is to describe the cascade out loud, with specificity, in a way that gives the person's head a complete explanatory frame.

The naming serves two purposes simultaneously. It is psychoeducation — the person now sees the structure of what is happening. It is also tactical — it gives the head something coherent to chew on, so the body can come back to the felt territory the head was defending against.

The form Joe uses:

"So that's the best part, is that's your — so your first line of defense against what you are essentially is to think, and then the thinking gets so fucking rattled and shame-filled that you need to avoid the shit thinking. And you do that with procrastination, doom scrolling, pornography, blah, blah, blah, blah. There's probably a dozen others. So what you are essentially feels good, but somehow overwhelming, moves to thinking. Moves to self-critical thinking, moves to avoidance of self-critical thinking."

Then immediately:

"Yeah, so back to — I'm giving, I'm giving your poor brain a story so it can shut the fuck up for a minute and we can get back to the real work, which is like, what are you essentially?"

The second part is essential. The naming is explicitly framed as a tactical gift to the head, not as the work itself. The work is the felt contact with essential self. The cascade explanation buys a few seconds of head-quiet during which the body can come back.

## When Joe Uses It

The move fires when:

- The person's head is doing the heavy work of the avoidance in the coaching room
- The person is articulate enough that under-explanation would produce more thinking, not less
- The person has just been asked to feel essential self and has retreated into thinking instead
- The person has asked an intellectual version of the very question that is the cascade ("why would I want to avoid it through procrastination?")
- The naming will land as recognition rather than as instruction

The canonical sequence:

- **Joe:** "Well, show me then. Do it for just 30 seconds. Be with what you are essentially in your whole body for 30 seconds, and let's find out what makes you avoid it."
- **Person:** [tries to feel] "Yeah, I'm also thinking and I'm — yeah, I mean, it's, it's a super hard question. I can feel stuff."
- **Joe:** "So that's the best part, is that's your — so your first line of defense against what you are essentially is to think, and then the thinking gets so fucking rattled and shame-filled that you need to avoid the shit thinking. And you do that with procrastination, doom scrolling, pornography, blah, blah, blah, blah. There's probably a dozen others. So what you are essentially feels good, but somehow overwhelming, moves to thinking. Moves to self-critical thinking, moves to avoidance of self-critical thinking."
- **Person:** "Okay, interesting."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so back to — I'm giving, I'm giving your poor brain a story so it can shut the fuck up for a minute and we can get back to the real work, which is like, what are you essentially? There's your thinking again."

The person's "okay, interesting" is the signal that the head got fed. Joe immediately pivots back to the felt work. The naming was not the conversation; it was a brief detour to satisfy the part of the person that needed to understand.

## What It Produces

When the move works:

- The person experiences a moment of recognition. The cascade explains what they have been experiencing without being able to name. The recognition lands as relief.
- The head's grip relaxes briefly. The cascade-frame is more interesting and more complete than the previous self-shame story, so the head is willing to set the old story down at least for a moment.
- The pivot back to felt work has a small window of openness. The body is more accessible in the seconds after the head has been satisfied.

When the move fails:

- The person treats the cascade as a new piece of content to think about. The head grabs the explanation and uses it as fresh raw material for more thinking. The naming has become a new layer of the avoidance.
- The person says "okay so what I need to do is..." — the [[insight-converted-to-task-in-real-time]] read. The cascade has been received but converted into another task to perform.
- The person seizes the cascade as a self-diagnosis label. "Oh, so I have the cascade." The naming becomes identity content rather than perceptual frame.

The mitigation for each failure mode is the same: immediately re-pivot to the felt work. Do not let the cascade-explanation become the session. The cascade is a doorway, not a destination.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The move requires Joe to do something specific after the naming: not stay in the explanation. The successful execution is the explanation plus the immediate pivot. If the pivot is missed, the head consumes the explanation and the session becomes a conversation about cascades.

Joe's pivot is fast and pointed:

"Yeah, so back to — I'm giving, I'm giving your poor brain a story so it can shut the fuck up for a minute and we can get back to the real work, which is like, what are you essentially? There's your thinking again."

Three things in one move:

1. **Acknowledge the gift.** "I'm giving your poor brain a story." Name the cascade-naming as a tactical move so the person doesn't mistake it for the work.
2. **Redirect to essence.** "Back to the real work — what are you essentially?" Pull the attention back to the felt question. The question is the same one from before; the cascade was a detour.
3. **Catch the next instance of the cascade.** "There's your thinking again." If the head re-engages in the seconds after the redirect, name it. The same cascade is firing in the room in real time, and naming each instance keeps the felt territory open.

## Tone

Direct, slightly amused, plain. The cascade is described as what brains do, not as evidence of dysfunction. Words like "blah blah blah blah" and "the poor brain" keep the explanation from sounding like a clinical diagnosis. The lightness is what makes the cascade receivable as a structural fact rather than as a verdict.

The "shut the fuck up" framing is part of the medicine. It treats the head as a part to be settled, not as the person's whole self. The salty register signals that the head is not in charge of the work and does not have to be managed reverently.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not a teaching the cascade as a framework the person should learn and apply. The cascade is being given as a one-time explanation to enable the live work, not as a model to take home.
- This is not pathologizing the cascade. The cascade is described as what the brain does, not as a disorder. The person is not broken; the brain is doing its job, and the job sometimes produces this particular sequence.
- This is not a substitute for the felt work. The naming is a doorway. If the move stops at the doorway and never goes through, the cascade has been described but the work has not happened.
- This is not appropriate for every addictive-behavior presentation. Some clients benefit from less explanation, not more. The cascade-naming is most useful for articulate, intellectualized clients who need a coherent frame to release the head's grip. For clients who are already body-accessible, the naming can add unnecessary cognitive load.
