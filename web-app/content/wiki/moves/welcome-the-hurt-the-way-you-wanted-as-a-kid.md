# Welcome the Hurt the Way You Wanted It Welcomed When You Were a Kid

## The Move

When a person is in present-moment hurt that has the quality of a recurring childhood hurt — same body sensation, same flavor, same trigger pattern — invite them to welcome the hurt in the specific way they always wanted it to be welcomed as a child. Not generic self-compassion. The specific welcome that was missing.

"Just one second, see what it's like to welcome that hurt in, that in a way that you always wanted it to be welcomed when you were a kid. So you were a kid and you hurt, and your dad would just get angry and your mom would guilt-trip you. But imagine that it — someone just was there to be able to be with you in that hurt in the way that you wanted, and be with that hurt the same way, like, just totally welcome and embrace that hurt." -- Joe Hudson, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

The instruction is precise. It names what actually happened when the hurt arrived in childhood (the dad got angry, the mom guilt-tripped), then invites the person to deliver the alternative — the welcome that the kid needed and did not get. The specificity is what makes the move work. Generic "be kind to yourself" does not produce the same opening.

## When Joe Uses It

The move fits a specific moment:

- The person is in a present-moment hurt that has a familiar quality.
- The hurt was met badly in childhood. The person has named or implied what the bad meeting looked like (anger, dismissal, guilt-trip, ignoring, shaming).
- The person has been resisting the hurt. Often visible: a small held-back quality, a slight bracing, a redirect toward problem-solving.
- The work has located the hurt as the central material. It is not a side issue.
- Prior moves to feel the hurt have hit a wall. The hurt has not been able to land because the welcome has not been available.

The move is **not** used as a generic technique. It is used when the specific welcome that was missing in childhood is what is now blocking the present-moment feeling.

## What the Move Does Structurally

The hurt did not get felt fully in childhood because the field around it was hostile or absent. The child learned to hold the hurt at arm's length because being with it produced more bad meeting (the parent's anger, the parent's guilt, the parent's dismissal). The hurt got partially felt and partially stored, and the storing left a residue that returns whenever the same flavor of hurt arises.

The present-moment hurt cannot land fully because the same field-conditions are running internally. The adult has internalized the parent's response. When hurt arrives now, the adult reflexively does to the hurt what the parent did: gets angry at it, dismisses it, guilt-trips themselves about it. The hurt is still being met badly, now from inside.

The move interrupts this loop by inviting the person to construct a different field-condition explicitly. The construction is imaginative — "imagine that someone just was there to be able to be with you" — but the feeling produced by the imaginative construction is real. The body responds to the imagined welcome as if the welcome were happening, because in a structural sense it is.

Once the field-condition shifts, the hurt can do what it could not do in childhood: be fully felt, become heartbreak, open the heart.

## The Specificity Matters

The move depends on the specificity of what the child needed. "Welcome the hurt" is a generic instruction. "Welcome the hurt the way you wanted it to be welcomed when your dad would get angry and your mom would guilt-trip you" is a specific instruction. The specific version names what was missing and invites it to arrive now.

When Joe delivers the move, he often names what the parent did badly, in just enough detail to make the alternative concrete. This is not blame-naming or processing the parent's failure. It is the construction of the contrast that makes the new welcome possible.

The client constructs their own version of the welcome internally. The welcome is not Joe's; it is the welcome the client always wanted and is now giving. Joe is the witness, not the welcomer. The self-parenting is the work.

## What It Produces

When the move lands, the hurt that has been at arm's length lands fully in the body. The body often shifts visibly: the chest drops, the breath deepens, the face softens. Tears often arrive.

What follows is usually heartbreak. The heart that had been closed against the hurt opens. The hurt becomes love-in-the-form-of-heartbreak. See [[heartbreak-is-the-love-underneath-the-blockers]].

The heartbreak can also land first as terror. Joe in the source session described it: "Looks like it's half terror, half love." The terror is the system's response to letting the heart open after years of closure. The terror and the love are present at the same time. Both get welcomed.

After the heartbreak lands, the person's perception of the original sources (the parent, the situation) usually shifts. The defended state is no longer running. The parent can be seen with more empathy, often as also-scared. See [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] and [[guilt-as-passive-fear]] for the recognitions that often arrive in this softened state.

## What Joe Does With Resistance

The move often surfaces resistance. The person tries to do it but cannot quite let the hurt in. Joe sees the resistance and names it without forcing.

"I still see you resisting it a little bit." -- Joe Hudson, How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner

The naming is gentle. It is not a critique of the resistance. It is an observation that the work has not fully landed yet, with an implicit invitation to allow more.

When the person responds with the body's truth — "it feels like my heart will just—" without finishing the sentence — Joe encourages the continuation. "Yeah, let it do that. Let it do that. All the way. Even more welcoming." The instructions are minimal. The person is doing the work; Joe is removing the barriers to letting it complete.

## Dialogue Example

The full move in compressed form, from the source session:

- **Joe:** Just one second, see what it's like to welcome that hurt in, that in a way that you always wanted it to be welcomed when you were a kid. So you were a kid and you hurt, and your dad would just get angry and your mom would guilt-trip you. But imagine that it — someone just was there to be able to be with you in that hurt in the way that you wanted, and be with that hurt the same way, like, just totally welcome and embrace that hurt. I still see you resisting it a little bit.
- **Client:** It feels like my heart will just—
- **Joe:** Yeah, let it do that. Let it do that. All the way. Even more welcoming.
- **Client:** Yeah, almost feels like terror.
- **Joe:** Yeah. Looks like it's half terror, half love. And now think about your mom and dad, right? Again, you just did that for like a minute or something. How do you see them differently? Not in story, but in—
- **Client:** they're both in terror. Yeah, they're both in, in such insane fear.
- *(Source: How to Love My Parents Without Becoming Their Emotional Prisoner)*

The whole arc takes about a minute. The specific welcome lands. The heart breaks open into terror-and-love. The view of the parents shifts to seeing them as also-terrified. The empathy arrives as the natural product of the opened state, not as a virtue the client was supposed to manufacture.

## Tone

Slow. Soft. The instructions are spoken in the kind of voice that itself is the welcome. The pace gives the body time to find what is being invited.

Joe does not perform tenderness. The tone is matter-of-fact about the move but the delivery is unhurried. The "imagine that someone just was there" is said as if it is a small thing being offered, not as an emotional procedure being prescribed.

When the resistance shows up, the tone does not change. The seeing of the resistance is the same plain tone as the invitation. The plainness is what keeps the work from becoming a performance.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not use this as a generic technique.** The move depends on the specific contrast between what happened and what was needed. Without the contrast, it becomes "be nice to yourself" and produces no shift.
- **Do not narrate the parent's failures at length.** The naming of what the parent did is brief, just enough to create the contrast. Lingering in the parent's bad meeting reactivates the defense.
- **Do not lead the welcome.** The welcome has to be the client's. Joe creates the conditions and witnesses; he does not deliver the welcome on the client's behalf.
- **Do not push past resistance with force.** The "I still see you resisting" is an observation, not a demand. The further invitation ("let it do that, all the way") is offered, not imposed. If the resistance does not move, that is the work for now.
- **Do not skip the body check.** The body has to be tracked throughout. The half-terror-half-love observation came from Joe watching the body, not from a script.
- **Do not redirect to processing once the heart opens.** When the hurt lands and the heart breaks open, stay there. Do not pull back into the story about the family. The opening is the work.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not [[swap-roles-with-the-younger-self]], where the client embodies the younger self and Joe plays the present-self. In that move, the kid speaks; here, the adult welcomes the kid's hurt.

This is not generic inner-child work. The specificity (what was needed in this person's particular childhood) is the active ingredient. Generic inner-child framing without the specific contrast tends to produce abstractions, not the felt shift.

This is not "imagine a good parent." The welcome is not from a hypothetical good parent. The welcome is from the person themselves, now, given to themselves, then. The self is doing the welcoming. The fact that the welcome was never given is what makes it land now.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The work has located a present-moment hurt with a childhood quality.
- The person has named what was missing when the hurt arrived in childhood.
- Previous moves to feel the hurt have hit a wall and the hurt has not been able to land.
- A concern like [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]], [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]], or any childhood-rooted concern is being worked.
- [[parent-criticism-as-self-talk]] or [[guilt-as-passive-fear]] has surfaced what the parent was doing, and the next step is meeting the child-hurt that the parent could not meet.
