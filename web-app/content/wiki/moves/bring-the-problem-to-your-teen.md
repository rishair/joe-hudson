# Bring the Whole Problem to Your Teen

## The Move

When the parent notices something they think needs to change about the kid's behavior — phone use, sleep, schoolwork, anything — the standard move is to address it from above. The parent decides what should change, communicates the new rule, and enforces it.

The alternative move Joe describes: bring the whole problem to the kid as a shared problem. Including the parent's side of it. The parent's dilemma about wanting to be a good parent without nagging. The parent's worry. The parent's uncertainty about what to do. All of it.

"It's my job to make sure that you're not addicted to your phone. Like as a parent, I'm not a good parent and I don't want to nag you. And I don't want, like, you to feel micromanaged by me. So what do we do together to help you have a relationship — the right relationship with your phone that's going to make you the happiest? And how do I support you in that?" -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The structure: name the problem, name the parent's conflict about it, name that the parent does not want to be the manager, ask the kid to help figure it out together.

## What This Move Replaces

The standard parenting moves the bring-the-problem move replaces:

- **The new rule.** "From now on, phones go away at 9 pm."
- **The "have you really thought about" softening.** "Have you really thought about how much time you spend on your phone?"
- **The lecture.** "When I was your age..."
- **The shame-loading question.** "Don't you think you should..."
- **The unilateral consequence.** "If you don't put it down, I'm taking it."

All of these put the parent in the position of the authority handing down the directive, and the kid in the position of the subject who is being managed. The bring-the-problem move puts both of them in the position of two people looking at the same problem.

## Why It Works

The move accomplishes several things the standard moves do not:

**It treats the kid as capable of caring.** The unspoken assumption in most parenting corrections is that the kid does not actually care about the underlying issue (sleep, focus, screen time) and has to be made to care. The bring-the-problem move assumes the opposite — that the kid, when shown the whole picture, has their own felt sense about what they actually want their relationship with the issue to look like.

Esme describes the result with the phone example: "With the phone, like I too don't wanna be on it all the time. Like it doesn't feel good for me. And I notice how much happier and more creative I am when I am like doing, filling my time with other things." The kid's own want, once invited into the conversation, is often aligned with what the parent wanted in the first place. The opposition was an artifact of the way the issue was framed.

**It removes the rebellion structure.** Teens are developmentally inclined to push against external authority. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]]. A unilateral parental directive activates the pushing. The bring-the-problem move skips this entirely because there is no directive to push against. The parent is not asserting authority; the parent is sharing a problem.

**It models being whole with conflict.** The parent who shares their own dilemma — "I have my conflict between being a good parent and not wanting to nag you" — models that one can hold tension honestly without needing to resolve it by exercising power. The kid sees the parent's complexity. The kid learns it is okay to have internal conflict and to name it.

**It produces solutions the parent could not have produced alone.** The kid often knows things about their own situation the parent does not. What actually helps them put the phone down. What does not. What kind of support feels supportive versus controlling. The collaborative solution is usually more workable than the parent's solo solution.

## What Esme Describes Getting From It

The move is in use throughout Esme's parenting. She names the effect:

"It's, it's never like a, you should, or like, why aren't you? You know, like finger pointing. It's more like, this is what I'm noticing. Like, how are you feeling around it? Does it feel good to you? And like when you sit down, you're like, hey, I'm noticing that there's this pattern happening. Yeah, more often than not it becomes like a discussion of like, oh my gosh, I hadn't noticed that. Like, what can we do together to change that pattern? And it's always a joint effort. It's never like a, oh, like I have to do this or I have to change that. It's like, how can I support you in changing that?" -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The structural shift: "I have to" becomes "how can I support you." The kid is not the subject being changed; the kid is the co-author of the change. The motivation is internal rather than imposed.

## The Specific Sentence Structure

The move can be deconstructed into a sentence form the parent can practice:

1. **Name what the parent is noticing.** "I notice that..."
2. **Name the parent's role in it.** "It's my job to..."
3. **Name the parent's conflict.** "And I don't want to nag you / micromanage you / be the bad guy."
4. **Pose it as a shared question.** "So what do we do together to..."
5. **Offer the parent's support as a question.** "How do I support you in that?"

The form keeps the parent transparent about their own state, and explicitly invites the kid as a collaborator rather than addressing them as a subject.

## When This Move Lands

The move lands when:

- The relationship has enough mutual respect that the kid can hear the parent's honest dilemma as honest rather than as manipulation. See [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]] for the structural ground.
- The parent is actually willing to find a solution they did not pre-decide. If the parent is using the move as a way to get the kid to agree to the parent's predetermined answer, the kid will feel the manipulation and refuse.
- The parent can tolerate the kid's solution being different from the parent's solution. The collaborative version may include the kid keeping more phone time than the parent would have allowed, in exchange for clearer agreements about when phones are off. The parent has to be able to live with the negotiated outcome.

## When This Move Does Not Land

The move does not land when:

- The kid is in active rebellion and is going to push against anything from the parent regardless of how it is framed. The work in that case is upstream — repair the relational ground first.
- The issue is one where the parent actually cannot share the decision (safety, abuse, immediate danger). Trying to use the move on a non-shareable decision is dishonest, because the parent will override the kid's input. Better to draw the boundary directly.
- The kid is too young to participate as a collaborator. The move is designed for tweens and teens; younger kids need [[stay-close-and-insist]] instead.

## The Underlying Principle

The move is an application of [[people-want-you-in-it-with-them-not-advice]] — bringing the parent into the problem rather than dispensing the solution from outside. It is also an instance of [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]] — the parent treats the kid the way they would treat a respected colleague: by sharing the dilemma rather than issuing the directive.

The move also tracks with [[parenting-as-coaching-domain]]: the parent is treating the parent-teen relationship the way a good coach treats a coaching relationship. The coach does not solve the client's problem. The coach holds the problem with the client and asks the questions that surface the client's own knowing. The bring-the-problem move is the parenting version of the same posture.

## Connected Concerns

- A parent struggling with screen time, sleep, schoolwork, friends, or any recurring issue with a teen
- A parent who feels they are constantly managing and is exhausted by it
- A parent who wants to repair after a period of micromanaging
- A coachee whose own parents only ever issued directives and is asking what an alternative looks like

## Tone

The tone the parent brings to the move is conversational and undefended. Not preachy. Not strategic. The parent is genuinely admitting that they do not know the right answer and they want the kid's help figuring it out. If the tone is performative — the parent acting like they are bringing the problem when they actually have the answer pre-loaded — the kid will read the performance and the move will fail.

The honesty of the parent's stated conflict is what makes it work. The parent is sharing actual uncertainty, not staging uncertainty as a parenting technique.
