# Look Around the Room — Can You See Anyone Who Is Not Here For You?

## The Move

When working with a client in a group or public setting and the client has just expressed a wish or need for someone to be there for them (often a partner or family member), invite the client to look around the room and actually try to find a face that is *not* there for them.

The instruction:

"It's like, just look at the faces that you can see and see if you can see if anybody's actually not here for you right now. Maybe one. You might be able to find one." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The client looks around. They struggle to find someone who is not there for them. The move surfaces an immediate contradiction to the belief that nobody is there for them. The room is full of people who are present and caring. The "nobody is there for me" story cannot survive direct visual inspection of the room.

The follow-up reframe:

"Yeah, right. Like, that's the weird part, is the whole — maybe not all, but enough of the world is wanting to be there for you." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The reframe does not require all of the world to be there for the person. It only requires enough — and the room demonstrates that enough is already true.

## When Joe Uses It

The move requires:

- The session is happening in a group or public setting (live Q&A, workshop, group coaching)
- The client has expressed a longing for someone (often partner) to be there for them in a specific way
- The client is at risk of holding the "nobody is there for me" story as the load-bearing assumption underneath their work
- The client can actually look around at faces (in-person or in a video-call grid)
- The room is composed of people who are, in fact, present and caring (which is usually true in Joe's settings)

The move does not work in 1:1 sessions. It also does not work in settings where the room is hostile or distracted. It works specifically when the room embodies the contradiction to the client's belief.

## What This Move Does

The mechanism: the "nobody is there for me" belief is being held in spite of evidence. The evidence is right there — people are present, attentive, caring. The belief survives by routing around the evidence. The move forces the routing-around to fail by directing the eyes at the evidence and asking the client to find a face that confirms the belief. They cannot.

The failure of the belief to find its confirming evidence in the room is the work. The client experiences the gap between the story they have been holding and what is actually present. The gap is uncomfortable. The discomfort is the structure of the belief becoming visible.

The reframe ("enough of the world is wanting to be there for you") is the move's completion. It does not claim that everyone is there. It claims that enough are. The "enough" is the level at which the belief should have collapsed long ago. The move surfaces that this level has been met for a long time.

## The Client's Usual Move-Out

A common response: the client looks around, cannot find someone not there for them, then says "the problem is, the people closest to me haven't been there for me." This is the belief's defensive maneuver — it retreats from the strangers-in-the-room evidence and re-asserts itself with the people who matter most.

Joe's response in the source session:

"But, but we're talking about your husband." -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The redirect pulls the work back to the actual partner. The strangers-in-the-room cannot disprove the belief about the husband because the belief about the husband is the actual operating belief. The room-exercise served its function — it demonstrated that "nobody is there for me" cannot be defended as a universal statement. The work then narrows to the actual relationship where the belief is operating.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The client visibly cannot find someone in the room who is not there for them
- The "nobody is there for me" story registers as not entirely true at the universal level
- The work narrows to the specific relationship where the belief is most operative
- The room becomes part of the evidence the client can return to later

If the client deflects ("these people are great, but..."), the move still produces value by making visible that the deflection is the move. The "yes, but" exposes the structure of the belief — it requires a specific person not being there, and it cannot survive without that specific person.

If the client genuinely experiences the room as caring and the belief partially loosens, the work can move into the next layer (the husband, the actual partner, the people closest).

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client softens at the seeing of the room: Joe moves into the next layer of work, often [[count-the-years-of-showing-up]] with the actual partner.

If the client deflects by retreating to the closest people: Joe acknowledges and pivots directly to the work with the specific person. "But, but we're talking about your husband." The deflection is data, not a problem.

If the client says "I can only see a handful, so I won't call anyone out" (i.e., they found someone not there for them but won't name them): Joe lets this go. The point is not to name names. The point is that even with a handful of exceptions, enough of the world is there. The "I won't call anyone out" is itself a soft signal — the client is holding the seeing without weaponizing it.

If the client says "everyone is here for me": Joe affirms and uses the felt sense of being held by the room as a doorway into the work that has not let the holding land.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not a test that the client must pass.** Some clients will find faces they don't trust. That is fine.
- **It is not gaslighting the partner's actual absence.** If the partner has genuinely not been there, the room-evidence does not invalidate that history. The move only contradicts the universal claim ("nobody is there for me"), not the specific claim ("my partner has not been there at X moment").
- **It is not a workaround for the actual relational work.** The room can demonstrate that enough of the world is there. It cannot do the work of letting in the specific person whose love the client has been refusing. The specific work still has to happen.
- **It is not for use in hostile rooms.** If the room is not actually there for the client, the move backfires. Use only in settings where the room demonstrates the caring.

## How This Move Differs From [[apologize-for-not-seeing-the-help]]

The lonely-CEO apology has the client recognize the team's support they have been missing. This move has the client recognize the room's presence they have been missing. The CEO apology applies in 1:1 work where the client describes feeling unsupported despite having a team. This move applies in group settings where the client describes feeling unseen despite the room. Both moves use external evidence to dissolve the isolation-belief. The difference is the venue.

## Tone

Soft, slightly amused, exploratory. The "maybe one. You might be able to find one" is delivered with a small smile — Joe is acknowledging that the client might find an exception, and the acknowledgment makes the move feel non-coercive. The reframe ("maybe not all, but enough of the world is wanting to be there for you") is delivered as a piece of information, not as a correction.

The tone is critical. Delivered with too much certainty ("look around, everyone is here for you!"), the move becomes a demand for the client to feel something. Delivered with too much pressure ("you can't find anyone, right?"), the move becomes coercive. The soft, exploratory tone is what allows the room's actual presence to do the work.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the client is in a group or public setting and has expressed feeling alone or unseen
- When the client has said something like "I want him to be there for me" or "I don't trust people are there for me"
