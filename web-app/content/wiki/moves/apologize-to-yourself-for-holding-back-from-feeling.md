# Apologize to Yourself for Holding Back From the Emotions You Wanted to Feel

## The Move

After the client has apologized to the partner for a relational pattern (see [[apologize-to-partner-without-shame-for-the-distance-pattern]]), invite the matching self-apology. The pattern the client has been doing to the partner — making them chase, blocking the connection, withholding the receiving — has a self-directed twin: the client has been making themselves wait, blocking their own access to the feeling, withholding from themselves the emotional life they actually wanted.

The setup as Joe gives it:

"So you said something to your husband, you apologized. What's the apology you have for yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The client crafts the apology. In the source session, the client's apology to herself was:

"Um, I'm sorry I held you back for so long to just feeling the emotions that you wanted to feel for a very long time." -- Client, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The structure of the apology: acknowledge the holding-back, name the duration, name what was being held back from (the emotions the self wanted to feel).

## When Joe Uses It

The move requires:

- The client has just delivered an apology to the partner (or to another significant other) for a relational pattern
- The pattern has a self-directed twin that the client can recognize
- The session has moved into the territory where self-witnessing is becoming available (often after [[redirect-from-being-seen-by-them-to-seeing-yourself]])
- The body is in an open state, capable of receiving the self-apology somatically
- The work is in the closing arc — the session is approaching a moment of integration

The move is the natural completion of a partner-apology arc. The partner-apology addresses the relational structure. The self-apology addresses the structural cause — the pattern of self-withholding that has been generating the relational distance.

## Why the Partner-Apology Comes First

The order matters. The partner-apology first does several things that the self-apology benefits from:

1. **Externalizes the pattern.** The client sees what they have been doing to the partner. The pattern is now visible as an action.
2. **Demonstrates the upright-apology form.** The client has just done one. They know what the form feels like.
3. **Surfaces the underlying want.** The partner-apology often surfaces what the client actually wanted from the connection. The self-apology can then address what the client has been withholding from themselves.
4. **Builds the emotional charge.** The grief that becomes available during the partner-apology often spills into the self-apology, giving it the emotional weight required to land.

Starting with the self-apology before the partner-apology often produces an apology that does not have the structural insight behind it. The self has not yet seen what it has been doing. The apology becomes vague.

## What the Apology Addresses

The apology addresses the specific self-directed action that has been the structural cause of the pattern. Common variants:

- "I'm sorry I held you back from feeling the emotions you wanted to feel for so long" (the source session's version)
- "I'm sorry I made you wait for connection while pushing it away"
- "I'm sorry I asked you to be small so you wouldn't feel so much"
- "I'm sorry I kept you from letting the love land"
- "I'm sorry I made you do the deflecting for all these years"

The form: acknowledgment, naming the duration ("for so long," "for years"), and naming what was being withheld. The variation depends on what specifically the client has been doing to the self.

The apology does not need to be elaborate. The source session's version was a single sentence. Brevity is fine — the somatic landing is what matters, not the elegance.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The body softens further, often more than during the partner-apology
- Tears often arrive — the grief for the years of self-withholding is significant
- The client experiences themselves as a being who has been doing this to themselves, not just as a being who has been on the receiving end of something done to them
- The self-witnessing capacity that was opened by [[redirect-from-being-seen-by-them-to-seeing-yourself]] is now used in service of the self
- The session can close on a note of integration rather than on a note of partner-receiving alone

The diagnostic: did the apology produce a body shift? If yes, the work has landed. If no, the apology has stayed in the head and the somatic invitation needs to be made more explicit ("how does that feel? Ask your heart.").

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client delivers the apology and the body shifts: Joe acknowledges and rests. The session is at a closing point. Often a quiet moment of presence follows.

If the client delivers the apology with shame: Joe gently re-issues the no-shame instruction. The shame is the old pattern. The upright apology has no shame in it.

If the client cries: Joe stays. The tears are part of the work.

If the client cannot find the apology: Joe sometimes offers a starting point, but does not script. The apology has to come from the client. If the client genuinely cannot find one, the work returns to seeing the pattern more clearly.

If the client says something like "it is scary, but it's good" (as in the source session): Joe affirms with brevity. "Yeah, that's right." The closure is light. The work has happened.

## Dialogue Example

After the partner-apology, the [[count-the-years-of-showing-up]] move, the [[redirect-from-being-seen-by-them-to-seeing-yourself]] move, and the moment of receiving the partner's seeing:

- **Joe:** "So you said something to your husband, you apologized. What's the apology you have for yourself?"
- **Client:** "Um, I'm sorry I held you back for so long to just feeling the emotions that you wanted to feel for a very long time."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. When you look back on the film, if — when you look back on this, you'll get to see this moment where you're literally like a little kid excited by the — you hold it back a little bit, but it's there."
- **Client:** "It is, it is scary, but it's good."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's right."

*(Source: If You Can't Accept Compliments)*

The apology is short. The follow-up is brief. The closing is quiet. The work is done. The session ends on the client's recognition that the new state is both scary and good — accurate description of the post-opening felt-sense.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not for use before the partner-apology has happened.** The pattern has to be visible at the external level first.
- **It is not shame disguised as apology.** "I'm so sorry for being such a bad person to myself" is the deflated identity, not the upright apology. The form has to be empowered.
- **It is not the same as [[apologize-as-daily-practice-for-bad-habits]].** That practice is a daily ongoing apology for an active pattern. This move is a single in-session apology that completes an arc.
- **It is not the same as [[apologize-for-contorting-into-frameworks]].** That move addresses a specific pattern (contorting into someone else's framework). This move addresses a different pattern (holding back from feeling). The structural form is similar; the content is different.
- **It is not the whole work.** The apology is a marker that the work has reached a closing point. The actual integration happens over the days and weeks following the session.

## The Closing-Arc Function

This move usually closes a session. It completes the arc:

1. Concern presents (compliments, distance, walls)
2. Pattern surfaces (the chase configuration, the deflection)
3. Partner-apology delivered (for the pattern done to the partner)
4. Receiving opens (the duration-evidence, the seeing)
5. Self-witnessing redirected on (the change-that pivot)
6. Self-apology delivered (for the pattern done to the self)

The self-apology is the integration point. Without it, the session can leave the client with insight but no felt completion. With it, the session has a somatic landing that the body can return to.

## Tone

Quiet, soft, slightly tender. The "what's the apology you have for yourself?" is delivered as an invitation, not as a demand. The tone after the apology is even softer — the work is done, the body is settling. Joe's follow-up in the source session was a quiet image ("you're literally like a little kid excited by the —") that gave the client a felt-sense reference for the new state. The closing "yeah, that's right" is brief. Less is more at this point.
