# Visualize the Kid Composing Themselves (Use Developmental Absurdity)

## The Move

Ask the client to picture a child playing happily. Wait until the image is vivid. Then ask them to picture that same child composing themselves. The body recoils. The recoil is the move.

The full setup:
1. "What year do you think a kid should start composing themselves?"
2. When the client says "as soon as possible" (the introjected answer), ask the absurdity-revealing version: "Like, a baby should be like, nope, compose yourself? A 2-year-old, compose yourself? A 5-year-old, compose yourself? When is it they're morally obligated to compose themselves?"
3. "I want you to think of like a 5-year-old playing happily, like just visualize a 5-year-old playing happily, joyful, in the backyard. Collecting flowers, making fairy houses, digging holes. Do you have that image?"
4. "Great. And now I want you to visualize that kid composing themselves."

The work happens in the gap between the first image and the second. The first image is alive — the kid playing. The second image is grotesque — the kid containing itself. The body knows the second image is wrong before the mind can defend it.

The client will physically recoil (head pulls back, face winces). Name it: "Your head, you literally went like this. I am really sorry that somebody told you that you needed to compose yourself. That's fucked up."

## When Joe Uses It

After [[composing-in-the-session-itself]] has been named live, after the client has acknowledged that composing themselves is constant and painful, when the client says "as soon as possible" to the question of when a kid should start composing. The "as soon as possible" answer is the data — it tells you the introject is intact and operating without conscious examination.

The move uses the client's own logic against the introject. The client believes children should compose themselves "as soon as possible" — but the body cannot tolerate the image of a child composing themselves. The split between the believed answer and the felt answer is the opening.

The move works particularly well when:
- The client is intellectually composed and articulate. The intellectual move (picturing the kid) bypasses the verbal defenses.
- The client does not have children. If they do, this can still work but the personal stakes around their own children's composure may complicate the move.
- The client has identified that they were told to compose themselves but has not felt the absurdity of that instruction yet.

## What It Produces

The visualization usually produces:

1. **A pause.** The client takes a moment to assemble the image of the kid playing.
2. **A bodied recoil at the second image.** The face changes. The head pulls back or to the side. Sometimes a sound of disgust ("ugh").
3. **A spontaneous expression of grief or anger about the original instruction.** "I hate it."
4. **An opening for the apology.** Joe immediately offers: "I am really sorry that somebody told you that you needed to compose yourself. That's fucked up." This is not a coaching move pretending to be repair; it is sincere. The client has just felt, possibly for the first time, the obscenity of what was done to them.

The move converts an intellectual fact ("I was told to compose myself") into a felt knowing ("what was done to me was wrong"). The conversion is what enables the later moves to land. Without the bodied knowing, the externalization moves ([[tell-me-to-compose-myself]], [[apologize-to-the-other-for-what-you-do-to-yourself]]) just become more exercises to perform.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client recoils visibly, Joe names the recoil and offers the apology immediately. The apology is short and direct. It is not a lengthy therapeutic intervention — it is one sentence that gives the client's body permission to know what it already knows.

If the client does not recoil — they remain analytical, intellectualize the question — the read shifts. The composure is deep enough that the visualization isn't penetrating. Joe will then move to a body-direct move ([[tell-me-to-compose-myself]]) that doesn't rely on visualization.

If the client says "I hate it" or shows other expression, Joe stays with the expression briefly before moving on. The hatred is the alive feeling — it has been frozen under the composure for years and is finally available.

## Tone

Curious and a little incredulous when asking the question. The list ("a baby, a 2-year-old, a 5-year-old") is rhythmic, almost comic — the absurdity builds with each iteration. When the client says "it seems psychotic," the agreement is immediate and uncomplicated: "It is psychotic."

The apology after the recoil is sincere and short. The cursing ("that's fucked up") is structural — it matches the magnitude of what was actually done and prevents the apology from sounding like therapeutic technique.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not skip the playing image and go straight to the composing image. The contrast is the move. Without the playing image first, the composing image is just abstract.
- Do not over-explain after the recoil. The body has just understood. Words at this moment usually re-engage the analytic mind and undo the felt knowing. The short apology is enough; then move.
- Do not let the client off the hook if they intellectualize the playing image ("I don't really know any kids"). Insist on the visualization. The move requires the felt image.
