# Just Go Do the Thing, Stop Asking Permission

## The Move

Once the avoided action has been named, propose a version of it that requires no permission, no qualification, no setup, no waiting. Usually this version involves doing the work for free, doing it for someone available, or doing it in a low-stakes context where nothing depends on it.

The form Joe uses:

"Okay, so how hard would it be right now to be like, I will interior decorate your home for free? Like, you literally just send out an email to all your friends, put it on Facebook, put it on Instagram, put it on whatever, and say, I will interior decorate your house for free." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

Then the close:

"All I'm doing is just getting to the thing that you want to do. Just go do the thing you want to do. Stop asking permission." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The move has two parts. First, produce the specific doable version — small enough, free enough, immediate enough that the standard objections (I don't have clients yet, I don't have a portfolio, I'm not ready) become inapplicable. Second, name what the move is actually doing: stripping the permission-asking out of the picture.

## When Joe Uses This

- After [[whats-the-thing-you-dont-know-how-to-do]] has surfaced the specific avoided action
- After [[i-keep-getting-in-my-own-way]] coaching has worked through the structural and felt layers and needs to close on a concrete take-home
- When the client has been waiting for some external condition to be met before starting (more credibility, a paying customer, a finished website, the right time)
- When the client has been building elaborate apparatus around what should be a simple unfamiliar action
- When the want has been clearly named and the only remaining barrier is the permission-asking frame
- When the avoided action is genuinely small in absolute terms and the apparatus around it is the only thing making it look big

The move arrives in the closing phase of the session, after the structural and felt work has been done. Its job is to convert the insight into a specific small action the client can take within hours, not weeks.

## Why It Works

The permission-asking frame is one of the standard architectures of avoidance. The client believes they cannot do the wanted thing until some condition is met — usually a condition that involves someone else granting them status to do it. The free-work proposal collapses the permission frame by removing what the permission would have been required for.

When the work is paid, the asker has to be qualified, recognized, marketable, ready. When the work is free, the asker has to be willing. Willingness is something the client controls. The move converts an external-permission problem into an internal-willingness problem, and the internal-willingness problem is the real one.

The free-work proposal also collapses the perfectionism architecture. A free service does not have to be perfect. "I'm going to play around with your living room for a bit, see if you like it" sets the stakes at zero. The client can do the work without the importance-loading that has been making the work intolerable. See [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]].

The "just send out an email" form gives the client a concrete first action that requires no preparation. The action takes minutes. Most of the client's apparatus has been built around postponing actions like this — they have been treating an email like a major decision. The casualness of "just send out an email" exposes the apparatus as out of scale.

The naming ("all I'm doing is just getting to the thing that you want to do") makes the move's purpose explicit. The client is not being asked to do anything other than the thing they have already said they want to do. The permission-asking has been the only thing in the way. Naming this directly is what finally allows the client to see the permission-asking as the obstacle rather than as a legitimate stage of the work.

## What It Produces

Almost always: a moment of recognition. The client sees how small the actual action is and how disproportionate the avoidance architecture has been. The recognition is sometimes accompanied by laughter, sometimes by quiet, sometimes by relief.

Often: the client immediately starts iterating on the action ("I could decorate someone's living room as a one-time thing, doesn't have to be permanent"). The iteration is the client's mind solving the action rather than avoiding it. The shift is the move landing.

Sometimes: the client surfaces a new objection. ("But why does that feel so hard?") This is not a failure of the move. It is the next material. The objection often surfaces the felt layer underneath the permission-asking — usually contained excitement or fear of disappointment. The next phase of the work addresses the felt layer. See [[breathe-into-the-emotion-to-enjoy-it-not-to-get-rid-of-it]].

Rarely: the client refuses the free-work frame. The refusal usually means there is a deeper identity-investment in being-paid-as-a-professional that the work has not yet addressed. The next move is to investigate what the paid-from-the-start frame is protecting.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client iterates on the action: Joe stays out of the way. The client is doing the work. Joe might add small qualifications ("and no project too big, too small, happy to do it for free, I need to get my book up, all I require is that I get to take pictures afterwards") that further reduce the friction. The qualifications model the casualness of the offer.

If the client surfaces a new felt objection ("but why does that feel so hard?"): Joe stops and works the felt layer. The move has served its function — it has surfaced what was actually in the way, which was not the action itself but the feeling around the action. See [[now-were-at-the-real-thing]].

If the client reports having heard this advice before: Joe affirms and refuses to re-explain. The "I've heard this before" is itself often the avoidance — engaging with the having-heard-it-before instead of doing the thing. Joe will usually close anyway: "Stop distracting yourself. Yeah, no, you just go put out ads and tell all your friends you'll go decorate. That's all you gotta do."

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "Okay, so how hard would it be right now to be like, I will interior decorate your home for free?"
- **Client:** "Um, well, it would just be about finding the right person for that, or some— finding someone for that."
- **Joe:** "That's what I'm saying. Like, you literally just send out an email to all your friends, put it on Facebook, put it on Instagram, put it on whatever, and say, I will interior decorate your house for free."
- **Client:** "Like, okay, that just opened an idea because it doesn't even have to be like a permanent thing. Like, because in my head I'm thinking, oh, who's going to want to invest that kind of money if I don't have like something to point to as a reference? But it could just be, let me play around with your living room for a bit."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Client:** "See if you like it."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And no project too big, too small. Happy to do it for free. I need to get my book up. All I require, all I require is that I get to take pictures afterwards."
- **Client:** "Just offer free work."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. You want to be an interior decorator? That would be a way that you get to start interior decorating."
- **Client:** "It's just funny you say that because I was having this conversation with someone else and they said how I started it was just offering free services and then I used that as a point of reference and it's like a fake it till you make it kind of thing."
- **Joe:** "All I'm doing is just getting to the thing that you want to do. Just go do the thing you want to do. Stop asking permission."
- **Client:** "But why does that feel like so hard?"
- **Joe:** "It feels like now we're at the real thing."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

The sequence shows the full arc. Joe proposes the doable version. The client resists slightly ("finding the right person"). Joe makes the proposal more specific ("just send out an email to all your friends"). The client iterates ("doesn't even have to be permanent"). Joe adds more friction-reducing qualifications ("no project too big too small, all I require is to take pictures"). The client compresses ("just offer free work"). Joe names what the move has been ("all I'm doing is getting to the thing you want to do, stop asking permission"). The client surfaces the next layer ("but why does that feel so hard?"). The next phase begins.

## Tone

Light, almost incidental, friendly. The proposal is delivered the way you might suggest grabbing a coffee. The smallness of the action is conveyed by the smallness of the tone. If the proposal were delivered with weight ("you need to take this seriously and do the work"), the action would carry the same weight as the imagined paid version, and the move would fail.

The "no project too big, too small, all I require is that I get to take pictures" addition is delivered in the voice of the casual flyer the client would actually write. The voice models the casualness the action requires. Hearing the casual voice helps the client locate that voice in themselves.

The closing ("just go do the thing you want to do, stop asking permission") is direct but not lecturing. The directness signals that the session has arrived at the action and is closing — no more analysis is going to happen, time to do the thing. The directness without harshness lets the close land as an arrival rather than as an interruption.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not advice to be reckless. The proposal is calibrated — free work, low-stakes, available pool of people. Joe is not suggesting the client launch a paid service immediately; he is suggesting the smallest version of the wanted thing that can actually happen.

- It is not a refusal of preparation. Preparation has its place. The move is for the situation where preparation has become indefinite postponement. The proposal is to take one small action while continuing to prepare in parallel.

- It is not [[discipline-starts-engine-then-let-gravity]]. That principle is about the relationship between discipline and surrender across a longer practice. This move is the moment of starting the engine on a specific avoided action.

- It is not a permanent business strategy. The free-work proposal is a starting move. Once the work has been done a few times, the client can charge. The point is to convert the unfamiliar into the familiar, not to commit to giving work away forever.

- It is not for every situation. Some actions are genuinely large and require preparation. The move is for situations where the action has been made artificially large by avoidance architecture and is in fact small.
