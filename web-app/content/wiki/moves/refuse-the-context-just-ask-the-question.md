# Refuse the Context (I Don't Need the Context, I Just Need the Question)

## The Move

When a client opens a session by offering to give Joe context about their life, background, or journey before asking the actual question, Joe declines the context and demands the question directly.

"I don't need the context, I just need the question." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Still Feel Alone

The move is delivered as a polite but firm refusal of the standard coaching opening. Most clients have learned that to ask for help they should first set up the situation. Joe is signaling that this is not what is required here. The question is what is needed. The context can be filled in later if necessary, or it may not be necessary at all.

## When Joe Uses It

The move fires when:

- The client signals an intention to deliver context before asking ("To give some context, my journey...")
- The preamble has the quality of a prepared introduction rather than an unfolding response
- The session is short or has high signal-to-noise demands (live workshops, podcast coaching, time-limited sessions)
- The client's pattern suggests that the context-giving is itself part of how they avoid the question

The move is also part of a specific opening discipline Joe brings to live coaching: do not let the session shape become "client narrates, Joe responds to narration." The session is a coaching session, not a consultation. The question is the unit of work. The context can be inferred from how the question is asked, what the question is, and how the client shows up while asking. Pre-emptive context-delivery often replaces the question rather than supporting it.

The move is not for cases where the context is genuinely needed before the question makes sense. A client whose question depends on a specific detail of their situation may need to deliver that detail. The distinguishing signal is whether the context is being offered in service of the question or as a substitute for it. The latter is what this move refuses.

## What It Produces

The most common immediate response is the client recognizing they have a question and surfacing it more directly. The recognition is usually fast — the client has been deferring the question, and the deferral becomes visible the moment the deferral is not allowed.

Sometimes the client cannot immediately produce the question and has to find it. The finding is itself the work — the client realizes that what they have been carrying is not yet a well-formed question, just a felt sense of trouble. The move accelerates the formulating that would have happened eventually.

Sometimes the client produces a question that is still wrapped in elaboration. Joe will then often follow with [[whats-the-question]] or [[compress-to-one-sentence-with-eyes-open]] to push the formulating further. The context-refusal is often the first move in a sequence that compresses the client's framing down to the actual inquiry.

## Why This Works

The move works because most context-delivery in coaching is a performance, not information. The client is not actually orienting the coach to factual details the coach needs in order to coach. The client is organizing themselves through the speaking, justifying their request for help, establishing their credentials, or preempting certain responses by setting up the framing. None of these are coaching work; all of them eat session time.

The move also works because it inverts the implicit hierarchy of who needs to be brought up to speed. The standard frame is: the coach needs context, the client provides it, then the coaching can begin. Joe's frame is: I will work with whatever shows up; what I need is your question; the context will emerge naturally if it matters. This frame respects the client more, not less — it implies that the client is capable of bringing the actual inquiry without first having to justify it.

The third reason it works: the move teaches the client, in real time, that their context is not what is making them coachable. The client comes in believing that without the right setup, the help cannot be received. The refusal disconfirms this belief. The help can begin right now, with the question, regardless of whether Joe knows anything about the client's background.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client produces a question quickly: Joe takes it and begins the work. The move has done its job — the session has skipped the preamble and entered the coaching directly.

If the client produces something that is still preamble in disguise (an elaborated framing of the situation rather than an actual question): Joe will continue to push for the question. He may use [[whats-the-question]] or [[compress-to-one-sentence-with-eyes-open]] depending on the specific avoidance pattern showing up.

If the client says they don't have a question or were hoping Joe would tell them what to focus on: Joe will redirect. The coaching does not work that way. The question has to come from the client. This is itself a useful conversation — the client's expectation that the coach should produce the inquiry is part of the configuration that often needs to be addressed.

If the client gives a single context-sentence that genuinely orients the question (a specific factual detail that the question depends on): Joe may accept it and move on. The refusal is not absolute; it is calibrated to the function of the context. A one-sentence orientation is different from a several-minute preamble.

## Dialogue Example

The compressed exchange from the source session, showing the move at the very opening:

- **Client:** "I guess my question, or what I'm working through right now, is I'm at this threshold in my life. To give some context, my journey—"
- **Joe:** "I don't need the context, I just need the question."
- **Client:** "Yes. Um, after almost two decades of personal work..."
- *(Source: Why You Still Feel Alone)*

The exchange shows the move's complete arc in three turns. The client signals the context delivery ("To give some context, my journey—"). Joe interrupts with the refusal. The client adjusts and begins formulating differently — though, in this particular case, the new formulation still carries some credentialing ("after almost two decades of personal work"), which Joe will address later as [[credentialing-as-hedge]]. The first cut of the move worked; the next cut waited for the next pattern to surface.

## Tone

Polite, direct, slightly impatient with the avoidance. The politeness is in the phrasing — "I don't need" rather than "you don't need to give." The directness is in not softening the demand. The slight impatience signals that the time is valuable and the client knows what they are actually asking; the framing-up is not necessary.

The move is not dismissive of the client's experience. The implication is not "your context doesn't matter." The implication is "your context will emerge if it matters; what we need first is the question." This is a meaningful distinction. A dismissive delivery would shut the client down; the Joe-version invites them forward.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not [[whats-the-question]]. That move asks the client to produce a question after they have narrated; this move declines the narration before it begins. The two are often used together — this one at the very opening, the other after the client's first attempt at a question turns out to be more narration.

It is not impatient or cold. The move is direct but the directness carries care. The care is in trusting the client to bring what they need without first having to justify needing to bring it.

It is not for every client. A client who is emotionally activated and needs to be met where they are does not need this move — they need a different kind of holding. The move is for clients who are using context-delivery as a way of warming up that is actually a way of avoiding the cold-plunge of the actual question.
