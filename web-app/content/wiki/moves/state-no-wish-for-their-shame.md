# Have Them Say: "There's Nothing in Me That Wants You to Feel Ashamed"

## The Move

In a couples coaching context where a [[shame-hot-potato]] is running, have the partners look at each other and say the sentence out loud:

> "There's nothing in me that wants you to feel ashamed or bad right now."

The sentence is the experiment. What surfaces in saying it is the work.

This is the move Joe used with a couple in his community who were in chronic conflict around a pregnancy. They could see the hot potato intellectually when he named it, but they could not feel it. The sentence is what made it feel-able.

"I was having them do this experiment, which is just look at one another and say, 'There's nothing in me that wants you to feel ashamed or bad right now.' And that was the confrontation needed for them to actually really see it, to really feel it." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## What the Move Surfaces

**First surfacing: "Wait, I don't feel comfortable saying that."** Most people, when asked to say this to a partner they are currently in conflict with, notice immediate resistance. They cannot get the sentence out cleanly, or the body refuses, or they feel they would be lying.

This discomfort is the first product of the experiment. It surfaces the previously-subconscious wanting-the-other-to-feel-shame. The wanting was running anyway. The sentence is what makes it visible.

"The first was like, wait, I don't feel comfortable saying that. So, oh, so you do want them to feel ashamed? What's that getting you?" -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

Once visible, the want can be looked at. Most people, looking at it directly, do not actually want their partner to feel ashamed — they were just unable to feel anything else underneath. Surfacing it lets the underlying material (grief, helplessness, hurt) come forward.

**Second surfacing: "I have to give myself the same grace."** The other half of the discovery. For the sentence to be sayable in a way that feels good, the person has to give themselves the same gentleness they are offering the other. This usually surfaces a precise refusal: "I cannot tell them I don't want them to feel shame without also dropping the shame I'm doing to myself."

"For them to be able to say it in a way that felt good, is they realized that they had to give themselves that same grace, that same gentleness. And so, oftentimes, the thing that's stopping people is their own feeling of shame. If I'm going to feel shame, then you should feel shame. That's the subconscious thought process there." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The two surfacings are the same. The unwillingness to say it to the other is the unwillingness to say it to oneself. The hot potato is fueled by the operator's own self-shame, externalized onto the partner.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not [[welcome-the-shame-without-extracting]]. That move is about the coach welcoming the coachee's shame without asking them to stop feeling it. This move is about partners stating to each other that they do not wish shame on each other. The two moves are complementary but different.

It is not a performative reassurance. The point is not for the partners to feel better in the moment. The point is to use the sentence as a probe — what surfaces when you try to say it tells you what is actually running.

It is not an extracted statement. If a coach demands the sentence as a script, the experiment fails. The instruction is "look at one another and say it" and watch what arrives. If the partner says it from a place of compliance, the discomfort it was meant to surface gets bypassed.

## The Subconscious Logic the Move Exposes

The operating logic underneath the hot potato, made visible by this move:

> "If I'm going to feel shame, then you should feel shame too."

This is rarely conscious. When stated this way out loud, most people reject it instantly: "of course I don't want my partner to feel shame." But the behavior says otherwise. The throws of the potato are the wanting. The sentence is the test.

The exposure is gentle. The coachee is not told "you want your partner to feel shame." They are invited to say the opposite, and they discover from the inside that they cannot, yet. The discovery is theirs. That is what makes it land.

## What Happens After the Move

The coachee usually has several options once the subconscious wanting is visible.

- **Feel what is underneath.** The wanting-them-to-feel-shame is usually covering grief or hurt. The move opens the door to the underlying emotion. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].
- **Address the self-shame first.** If the operator is in shame themselves, no amount of trying not to throw at the partner will work. The self-shame is the engine. The work moves inward.
- **Recognize that wishing shame on a partner does not change them.** [[shame-drives-behavior]] — if anything, shame perpetuates the behavior. The wish for the partner to feel shame is wishing for the behavior to continue. Most people, when this is named, no longer want what they thought they wanted.
- **Try the sentence again.** Often, after the underlying material has been processed, the sentence becomes sayable. Saying it from honesty is itself a small reorganization of the relationship.

## When to Use This Move

- In couples coaching where a [[shame-hot-potato]] is the dynamic
- When intellectual recognition of the pattern has not produced behavioral shift
- When one or both partners can see the dynamic in others but not in themselves
- When the recurring fight has a quality of mutual scoring rather than mutual understanding
- When the work has stalled at the level of "I know I shouldn't but I keep doing it"

## What to Watch For

The diagnostic data is in the body and voice as the sentence is attempted.

- **Does the sentence land cleanly, or is there resistance?** Resistance is data.
- **Is the eye contact sustained, or does the speaker look away?** Looking away usually means the sentence is being said as performance.
- **Does the body relax after the sentence, or stay tense?** Continued tension means the wish is still operating.
- **Does the listener receive the sentence, or deflect it?** A partner who deflects "there's nothing in me that wants you to feel ashamed" is usually unable to imagine the absence of the wanting. This is its own data.

## Connection to Other Concepts

The move depends on [[shame-as-disconnection]] for its reframe: shame is not deserved punishment, it is a disconnection signal. Wishing shame on a partner is wishing for the disconnection.

The move enacts the principle underneath [[seeing-through-essential-wrongness]]: the willingness to not see the partner as essentially wrong is a precondition for not wishing shame on them. The work happens at the level of the belief, not the behavior.

The move is the relational complement to the internal practice of [[shame-as-disconnection]]: the partner is asked to do externally what the coachee learns to do internally — refuse the wish for shame, in self or other.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with calm warmth, not as a confrontation. The setup is "let's try an experiment." The reveal is allowed to land on the coachee as their own discovery rather than the coach's accusation. The move requires the coach to be ungrasped about whether the experiment "works" — the discomfort that surfaces is itself the result, not a failure.
