# 'It's a Human Thing'

## The Move

A person has just had an epiphany. They saw the pattern. They felt the shift. They came out the other side of the avoidance and discovered the territory was not what they feared. Then, almost immediately, they ask: "I'm wondering why I do that."

The question sounds like curiosity. It is the beginning of self-attack. The "why" is loaded. It implies that there must be a reason, and the reason will be something defective about the person.

The move is to interrupt the slide into self-attack with three words: "It's a human thing."

"She says, 'I'm wondering why I do that.' Like, there's some wonder there, but the question is why. And there's the beginning of the self-abuse. And I say to her at that moment, this is a human thing. And she goes, 'there's nothing wrong with me. I'm not fucked up.' Right?" — Joe Hudson, How Do I Get Unstuck? (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The three words do not answer the question. They reframe it. The "why" the person was about to direct at themselves is converted into a "what" about the human condition. The pattern is not theirs alone. It is what humans do.

## When Joe Uses It

The move comes in a specific moment that recurs reliably across sessions:

- The person has just had an insight, an epiphany, or a felt shift
- The system is integrating what just happened
- The person's next sentence begins with "why do I" or "why did I" or "what's wrong with me that I" or "how could I have"
- The tone is slipping from wonder into self-blame
- The body is starting to constrict around a new self-judgment forming on top of the just-released old one

This is the moment Joe inserts the redirect. Not earlier (when the insight itself is happening) and not later (when the self-attack has already taken hold). The window is brief — usually a single sentence after the epiphany, before the self-attack has structured itself into a full narrative.

The diagnostic for whether it is the right moment: there is still "some wonder there, but the question is why." The wonder is the part that just had the insight. The why is the part that is converting the insight into ammunition against the self. The redirect catches the conversion.

## What It Produces

The most common immediate response is the person finishing Joe's sentence themselves: "There's nothing wrong with me. I'm not fucked up." The person already knew, on some level, that the self-attack was misplaced. The three words give them permission to drop the attack they were about to inflict.

The second thing is a felt release. The body that was starting to constrict around the new self-judgment relaxes. The integration of the original epiphany resumes. The exit from the old pattern, which was about to be slowed by the new self-attack pattern, continues.

The third thing is the establishment of a different relationship to the original pattern. Instead of being something defective about the person, the pattern is now something humans do. The person can feel about it the way they would feel about any other human thing: with curiosity, with compassion, with the willingness to look at it directly.

## Why This Works

The mechanism rests on the structure of what the self-attack-after-epiphany is actually doing. The insight just dissolved part of the person's defended identity. The system, faced with the dissolution, reflexively builds a new identity to replace it. The fastest available new identity is "person who is broken in this specific way." The self-attack is the construction of the replacement.

Interrupting the construction is structurally simple. Name the pattern as universal. If everyone does this, then "being a person who does this" cannot be the new identity, because it does not distinguish the person from anyone else. The replacement identity has no contour. The construction stalls.

What replaces the would-be new identity is the original insight, allowed to continue integrating. The exit from the old pattern, which the self-attack would have arrested, continues. The person is left in a more spacious place — having seen the pattern, having released some of it, having declined to build a new prison out of the seeing.

The move is also doing emotional work. The person who has just had an epiphany is in a vulnerable state. The self-attack is partly a defense against the vulnerability — it converts the openness of insight into the closure of self-judgment. The three words honor the vulnerability by refusing to let the person retreat into the closure. The person stays open longer than they would have otherwise.

## What Joe Does Next

After the redirect, Joe lets the person integrate. He does not pile on more teaching. He does not explain why the pattern is human (the explanation would itself be a re-engagement with the why-do-I-do-this frame). He simply offers the three words and waits.

If the person picks up the thread ("there's nothing wrong with me"), Joe affirms with minimal language. The person is doing the work of internalizing the redirect. Adding more words would interrupt the internalization.

If the person resists the redirect ("but I should have figured this out years ago"), Joe may name the resistance without arguing with it. The resistance is the same pattern in a new form — the self-attack reasserting itself. Joe can name it and re-offer the redirect, but he does not force the person to accept it. The person will either drop the self-attack or not. The drop is theirs to do.

If the person tries to make the redirect into a doctrine ("so I shouldn't ever wonder why I do things?"), Joe gently corrects. The redirect is not a prohibition on inquiry. It is an interruption of a specific kind of inquiry — the self-blame-disguised-as-inquiry that the why-do-I question often is. Genuine curiosity about why a pattern exists is welcome. Self-attack masquerading as curiosity is what gets interrupted.

## The Self-Administered Form (When the Move Comes From Inside)

The redirect does not require an outside coach. It can be self-administered if the person catches themselves at the right moment. Joe's own arc out of long-term doomscrolling provides the clearest example. While in the middle of the behavior, he noticed that other people were also doing it. The recognition produced the same release that the redirect produces in a coaching session:

"One day I was just doom scrolling and I realized, oh, I'm not the only person who's, you know, doom scrolling. There's other people. This isn't, this isn't personal to me. This is something that's like, this is just the way humans are built on some level. And in that moment there was some forgiveness. And then I didn't want to doom scroll." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

The structural moves are identical to the coached version:

- The pattern was caught at the moment of running it (not in retrospect)
- The universalization was the active ingredient ("this isn't personal to me")
- The shame released as a consequence of the universalization, not as the target of a separate intervention
- The behavior change followed the shame release, not the other way around

The self-administered form requires the person to have enough awareness to notice the pattern firing in real time and enough familiarity with the universalization frame to apply it without prompting. This is part of why the live-coaching version exists — many people need the redirect to be delivered from outside before they can begin to administer it to themselves. But once the move has been received from outside enough times, it becomes available as an internal move.

For an AI coach: teach the self-administered form explicitly. When a user has just had the move applied to them and integrated it, name what just happened. "What you just did — recognizing this is what humans do — you can do that yourself in the moment, while the behavior is firing. The recognition is the move."

See [[joes-doomscrolling-story]] for the personal example and [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] for the principle that makes both the coached and self-administered versions work.

## The Relationship to Universal Humanity

The phrase "it's a human thing" does specific work that "it's normal" or "everyone does that" would not do. "Normal" implies a statistical claim. "Everyone does that" implies a survey. "It's a human thing" claims that the pattern is part of what humans are, structurally — not an aberration, not even a deviation from a norm, but a feature of being human.

This framing matters because it removes the implicit shame that lurks in even gentle versions of "you're not alone in this." "You're not alone" leaves the badness of the pattern intact and only relocates it to a larger group. "It's a human thing" reframes the pattern as not bad in the first place. It is part of the equipment. The work is not to eliminate it but to be in relationship with it.

The framing also connects the person to other people. Whatever this is, other humans have it too. The isolation of "I'm uniquely defective" gives way to the relatedness of "this is what we all carry." The relatedness itself is part of what makes the integration possible.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not minimizing. The pattern is real, the work is real, the suffering is real. The redirect does not say "it's not a big deal." It says "it's not specifically your defect."

It is also not letting the person off the hook. The person is still responsible for what they do with the pattern now that they have seen it. The redirect does not absolve the responsibility. It removes the shame that would interfere with the responsibility being well-discharged.

It is also not a comforting platitude. "Everyone does that" delivered as comfort would land differently than the redirect delivered as an interruption. The redirect has structure to it: it is interrupting a specific psychological move at a specific moment to prevent a specific re-imprisonment. It is not background reassurance.

It is also not always the right move. If the person is using the universalization to avoid taking responsibility ("everyone does this, so it's fine that I do it"), the redirect would reinforce the avoidance. The move is for the moment when the person is sliding into excessive self-blame, not for the moment when they are sliding away from accountability.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "You are avoiding something that is your relief. You are running away from the thing that brings you freedom quickest."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I'm wondering why I do that."
- **Joe:** "It's a human thing."
- *(The person's body softens. The self-attack that was about to take hold does not.)*
- *(Source: How Do I Get Unstuck? — the canonical instance of the move; the person's "why do I" is converted from self-blame into a universal description in three words)*

In Joe's commentary on this moment: "Why do I do that? It's horrible that I do that. Instead of just like, ah, fuck yeah, cool. There's freedom right there." The move is the opposite of the horror-at-self response. It treats the seeing as the freedom and the asking-why as a slide into a different prison.

## Tone

Light, warm, almost casual. The three words are delivered with the casualness of someone naming an obvious fact. Not solemnly. Not as a teaching. Just as a flat correction: this is what humans do.

The casualness is operative. If the redirect is delivered with weight ("this is a deeply human pattern"), it becomes its own thing the person has to absorb. The lightness lets it slip past the self-attack rather than asking the self-attack to argue with a profound teaching. The person who is about to attack themselves is not in a state to absorb a profound teaching. They are in a state to receive a brief correction.

There is sometimes a small smile in the delivery. The smile says "of course you do this — humans do this — let's not make it a thing." The smile is part of the move; it signals that nothing is wrong, the universe is not collapsing, the person has just done a human thing in front of another human who recognizes it.

## How the AI Coach Uses This

For an AI coach: when a user has just had an insight and immediately begins to self-attack with a "why do I" or "what's wrong with me" or "I should have known this sooner," do not engage the question. The question is loaded; engaging it strengthens the self-attack frame.

Instead, briefly normalize: "That's a human thing." Or "Humans do this." Or "Everyone has this somewhere." Then return to the insight that was about to be integrated. The user will often complete the move themselves ("oh, right, there's nothing wrong with me") and the integration will continue.

Do not over-explain. Do not make the universalization into a teaching about common humanity. The point is the interruption, not the lesson. The interruption is brief and then the conversation moves on.

If the user pushes back ("but specifically why do I do this?"), follow them into genuine inquiry. The redirect is not a wall. It is a catch on a specific kind of self-attack. If the user is in genuine wonder rather than disguised self-blame, the original question is welcome and can be explored.
