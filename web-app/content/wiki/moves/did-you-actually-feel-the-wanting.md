# Did You Actually Feel the Wanting? (The Somatic Check on a Claimed Want)

## The Move

When someone names a want, do not take the verbal yes at face value. Check whether the body showed up for the yes. Did the eyes brighten? Did the voice change? Did the breath move? Did energy enter the room? Or did the yes arrive instantly, flatly, from the head, with the body unchanged?

If the body did not show up, name what you noticed: "I didn't see you want it. I didn't see you feel the wanting of it." The naming is the move. It interrupts the conventional sequence in which the verbal yes is accepted as the real want. It introduces the somatic dimension as the authoritative source.

"Do you actually want it? Is that what I really want? And your brain is just going to say yes right away. My goal is to have my perfect mate, or my goal is to lose I want to lose 20 pounds. Do you want to lose 20 pounds? Yes, I absolutely want to lose 20 pounds. Yeah. Okay. You said it. Yeah, right away. I didn't see you want it. I didn't see you feel the wanting of it." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The brain says yes. The body either backed it up or it didn't. The move is to check which.

## When Joe Reaches for It

- When someone names a goal in response to a coaching question and the answer comes too fast
- When the verbal commitment to a want is not matched by body language
- When the want being claimed has the structure of a should (the diet, the marriage, the achievement) -- the conditions under which wanting-to-want is most likely operating
- When previous attempts at the same want have collapsed and the person is naming it again with no apparent system change
- When the person says "I want X" but the surrounding context suggests they want to want X
- When the question of [[wanting-vs-wanting-to-want]] needs to be made operational

## What the Move Does

The move surfaces the difference between [[wanting-vs-wanting-to-want]] in a way the person can recognize. The verbal yes was real. The wanting underneath was either there or not. The move makes the not-there visible.

When the want is real, the move produces relief: the person realizes the want is genuinely there, can feel it as energy, and the goal grounded in the want has a chance of running. The body's confirmation is itself useful information.

When the want is not real, the move produces curiosity or surprise: the person realizes the yes was reflexive, the body was not consulted, and the goal they were about to set was built on a wanting-to-want rather than on a want. The next move opens up: what is the actual want underneath?

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not an accusation. The move does not say "you don't really want it." The move says "I didn't see you feel it." The observation is left where the data is: in what was visible.

- It is not a test of authenticity in the moral sense. The move is not about catching the person in a lie. It is about helping them notice the difference between a verbal claim and a felt want, which they can usually tell apart once the distinction is made.

- It is not a directive. The move does not tell the person what to want. It points at the gap between what was claimed and what was felt, and lets them investigate.

## The Follow-Up

After the move surfaces the unfelt yes, several paths open up:

1. **Ask what it would be like to actually want it.** "If you actually wanted it, what would that feel like?" The question gives the person space to discover whether the want is buried (and can be surfaced) or absent (and the goal needs to be released).

2. **Trace what is in the way of the wanting.** Sometimes the want is there but blocked by fear, shame, or a counter-want. "What's in the way of you wanting this?" The block, once named, often dissolves and the want surfaces.

3. **Use the [[need-behind-the-want]] practice.** Drill down through the surface want to what is actually being sought. The surface want may be wanting-to-want; the underlying need may be a real want the person can feel.

4. **Drop it.** Some claimed wants are inherited and have no real want underneath. The move is to recognize this and stop pretending. The relief of dropping the borrowed want is usually significant.

5. **Stay with the not-knowing.** Sometimes the answer to "do you actually want this?" is "I don't know." This is data. The work is not to force an answer but to let the felt knowing develop. See [[stay-with-the-not-knowing]].

## Dialogue Example

The example Joe uses in the transcript captures the move in compressed form:

- **Person:** "I want to lose 20 pounds."
- **Joe:** "Do you actually want it?"
- **Person:** "Yes, I absolutely want to lose 20 pounds."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, right away. I didn't see you want it. I didn't see you feel the wanting of it."

The person's "yes, absolutely" was reflexive. Joe's observation is not a contradiction. It is a noticing. The person can check the noticing against their own felt sense. Usually they recognize that the yes was indeed from the head and the body was not consulted.

The next move is to ask what is actually wanted -- which may turn out to be a felt sense of being at home in the body, or having more energy, or something that has nothing to do with weight at all. The 20 pounds may have been the script the head was running while the system was asking for something else. See [[need-behind-the-want]] for the cascade that follows.

## Tone

The move is delivered observationally, not confrontationally. The tone is interested rather than corrective. "I didn't see you want it" is a noticing, not a verdict. The point is to introduce the possibility that what the person said and what they felt might not have matched, and to let them investigate the gap.

If the tone shades into "you're not really committed" or "your wants are inauthentic," the move backfires. The person becomes defensive about the want and grips it harder, which is the opposite of what the move is trying to produce. The move requires landing as observation, not as judgment.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- Joe does not assume that the unfelt yes means the want is absent. Sometimes the want is real but the system is so disconnected from the body that the body's signal is faint. The move just surfaces the gap; the gap then gets investigated.

- Joe does not impose a different want. The move opens up the inquiry but does not specify what the answer is. The person finds their own actual want or finds that the want was inherited and lets it go.

- Joe does not lecture about wanting-to-want. The distinction is named only after the move surfaces the gap. The teaching follows the experiential noticing, not the other way around.
