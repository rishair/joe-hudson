# Reframe From 'Whether' to 'How'

## The Move

When the person presents a binary about whether to do something — should I be in a relationship or not, should I take this job or not, should I have a baby or not — and the binary has been running for a long time without resolution, refuse the binary. Reframe the question from "whether" to "how."

The structural form: "So then what makes it that your mind is thinking about [whether to do X], instead of [how to do X in a way that you actually want]?"

The canonical example from the source session:

- **Joe:** "If you knew that you could create a relationship that was not endurance, that was supportive, would there be a question?"
- **Person:** "No."
- **Joe:** "How much? How? Okay, great. Yeah. So then what makes it that your mind is thinking about, do I want to be in a relationship and not want to be in a relationship, instead of how do I create a relationship that's not an act of endurance, that's supportive?"

The reframe moves the work from a question the binary cannot answer ("yes or no to relationship") to a question that opens onto actual configuration work ("how do I create the configuration I would want"). The binary is dissolved by changing its object, not by adjudicating it.

This move is structurally close to [[reframe-the-question-to-the-blocker-emotion]] (which reframes a behavior-question into an emotion-question) but it operates at a different layer. That move points at the blocker emotion underneath a habit. This move points at the configuration the person actually wants underneath the existence-question. Both moves take the question seriously while changing what the question is about.

## When Joe Uses This

The move requires preparation. Joe does not deliver it cold. The sequence:

1. The person has presented the binary.
2. Joe has surfaced both voices (the for and the against — see [[whats-the-for-part]]).
3. Joe has named the frame the person is running. ("You treat relationships like an ultramarathon." See [[relationships-as-test-of-endurance]].)
4. Joe has asked the configuration question. "How do you want to treat relationships?" The person has answered with what they actually want.
5. Joe then asks the conditional that opens the reframe. "If you knew you could create [the configuration you want], would there even be a question?" The answer is almost always "no."
6. Joe delivers the reframe. "So then what makes it that your mind is thinking about [the binary] instead of [how to create the configuration]?"

Without the preparation, the reframe lands as imposition. The person hasn't yet seen that the binary is bad. With the preparation, the reframe lands as recognition. The person has just verified that the binary is bad and that the configuration question is the question. Joe is just naming what they have already shown themselves.

The move fires specifically when:

- The binary has been running for months or longer.
- Both options inside the binary are unappealing in the person's framing.
- The unappealing-ness of both options is a property of the frame, not of the options.
- The person can articulate what configuration they would want if they could have it.
- The binary is structured as "have this thing or don't have it" rather than as "do this hard thing or that hard thing" (the latter usually needs [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]] instead).

## What It Produces

The reframe usually lands quickly. The person hears it and the binary loses its weight. There is sometimes a laugh — the recognition that the question they have been running for months was the wrong question.

The reframe also opens onto material the binary had been keeping closed. Once the question is "how do I create the configuration I want?" the person can describe the configuration in detail. The detail reveals what is in the way of the configuration. That what-is-in-the-way is the actual work.

In the source session, the reframe surfaced the caretaking pattern with the ex. The man, asked what makes him think about the binary instead of the configuration, immediately said: "dealing with my ex is not a pleasant experience, that whole equation is like relationship equals pain, frustration, fighting." The active pattern producing the bad frame became visible. The work could move there.

The reframe also produces a felt shift in the body. The binary is exhausting because both options are bad; the configuration question is energizing because the answer is wanted. The person often reports the shift directly — a relaxation, a lightening, a sense that "this is the actual question."

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person engages the configuration question directly: Joe explores what the configuration would look like and what is in the way of it. The exploration usually leads back to whatever pattern (caretaking, self-abandonment, control) is the active obstacle.

If the person tries to return to the binary ("but I still don't know if I want a relationship"): Joe holds the reframe. He does not re-enter the binary. He may ask the configuration question again, or ask what would need to be true for the configuration to be possible.

If the person describes the configuration but then says "but I can't have that": Joe asks what makes that not possible. The not-possible claim is usually carrying the pattern that needs to surface. The not-possible is treated as data, not as a fact.

If the configuration the person describes is itself reasonable and they have legitimate practical obstacles: Joe shifts to the practical work. The reframe is not a denial of practical reality. It is a redirection of the work to the actual variable.

After the reframe, Joe often moves to surface the self-abandonment that has been keeping the binary in place. See [[how-do-you-abandon-yourself]]. The binary running is itself usually a form of self-abandonment — the wanting voice gets dismissed by the deliberating voice. Naming the abandonment continues the dissolution of the binary.

## Dialogue Example

After Joe has named the endurance frame and asked how the person wants to treat relationships, and the person has answered (supportive, not burden, not obligation):

- **Joe:** "Okay, I got it. Okay, so, so you — right, so if you knew that you could create a relationship that was not endurance, that was supportive, would there be a question?"
- **Person:** "No."
- **Joe:** "How much? How? Okay, great. Yeah. So then what makes it that your mind is thinking about, do I want to be in a relationship and not want to be in a relationship, instead of how do I create a relationship that's not an act of endurance, that's supportive?"
- **Person:** "And yeah, so the kind of the missing piece of this conversation too is that like dealing with my ex — I have a 14-year-old daughter and she lives with my ex and dealing with my ex is not a pleasant experience. So like, that whole equation is like relationship equals pain, frustration, fighting, people not listening, like that."

*(Source: Why He's Afraid To Commit)*

The reframe lands and immediately surfaces the active material — the ex relationship that has been the proximate generator of the endurance frame. Joe does not need to argue for the reframe. The person verifies it by their own response. The next move ("so in your relationship with your ex, how do you abandon yourself?") flows directly from what the person just brought in.
