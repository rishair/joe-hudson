# What does your heart say, not the mind?

## The Move

When a person has been intellectualizing -- producing thoughtful, articulate analysis of a situation while clearly not in contact with the felt experience of it -- the move is to name what is happening and redirect. "I see the mind, really. But what's your heart say?"

The move has two parts. First, the naming: "I see the mind." This acknowledges the intellectual work the person has been doing rather than ignoring or dismissing it. Second, the redirection: "but what's your heart say?" This is the invitation to a different organ of knowing.

The phrasing matters. "Not the mind" is gentle but firm. The coach is not asking for a richer analysis. The coach is asking for the heart's report, which is a different kind of report.

Variation from the transcript: "I'm curious what your heart says about it, not the mind. I see the mind, really, but what's your heart say?"

## When This Move Comes

- After the person has produced an analytically clean account of a situation that is missing felt resonance.
- After the person has named several thoughts about a topic in succession without dropping into the body.
- After the person has done sophisticated mental work to reframe a fear or feeling and the reframe has not produced a felt shift.
- When the person is articulating what they "should" or "could" think about a situation rather than what they feel.
- When a coach is uncertain whether the head-account is alive or rehearsed and wants the heart's vote.
- After [[lets-go-there]] has produced more analysis instead of arrival.

In the source transcript, the move came after Brett had produced a lengthy, thoughtful intellectual frame about why letting the fear in would help him process it rather than enact it. The frame was correct. It was also intellectual. Tara wanted what was actually true for him rather than what he had thought through.

## What It Produces

Often a different answer than the head was giving. The heart-answer is usually shorter, more immediate, less qualified. It may be a single sentence, a felt sense in the body, or a specific impulse toward a person in the room.

The heart-answer can confirm what the head said, in which case the coach gets information that the head was reporting what was actually present, not constructing a coherent story.

The heart-answer can contradict what the head said, in which case the contradiction is the work. The person now has to sit with two different reports and notice which one carries more weight.

The heart-answer can produce silence, with the person reporting that the heart is not currently saying anything. This is also valuable information. The coach then has the choice of going somewhere else or waiting.

From the transcript, the response to "what's your heart say" was:

"This might be my mind interpreting the heart, but it says like, Alexa chose this in me. It's not like this, this aspect of myself that I have this feeling that I, that I wrestle with or work through this particular flavor of feeling is not an inconvenience to Alexa. It actually is something that she's looking for. And so in feeling that, there's a lot of shame that falls away. There's like constriction that releases." -- Brett Kistler, Marriage, Love, and Death

Three things in this response are characteristic of heart-led answers:

1. The hedge ("This might be my mind interpreting the heart") -- the person is doing the work of checking which source the answer is coming from. The coach can stay with the answer despite the hedge.
2. The relational nature of the answer -- the heart-answer is about contact with another person, not abstract reasoning.
3. The somatic confirmation -- shame falls away, constriction releases. The heart-answer produces body movement. The head-answer rarely does.

## What the Coach Does Next

If the heart-answer produces felt shift, stay with it. Use [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]] to let the shift complete.

If the heart-answer produces a turn toward a person in the room, surface that movement: "Can you share that?" "Tell them what your heart is saying." See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]].

If the heart-answer contradicts the head-answer, name the contradiction without picking sides: "So your mind is saying X and your heart is saying Y. What happens when you notice both?" The work is for the person to live with both reports rather than to immediately resolve them.

If the heart-answer is "I don't know," the coach can use [[can-you-hang-out-with-it]] applied to the not-knowing: "Can you hang out with the not-knowing? What's it like to not have an answer?"

If the head immediately interprets the heart-answer (the "this might be my mind interpreting the heart" move), the coach can stay with the original answer rather than the interpretation: "Yeah. And what did the heart say before the mind started interpreting?" This is gentle and keeps the heart-channel open.

## Tone

Soft, curious, not pressing. The move is not a challenge to the head-account. It is an invitation to a different channel. The tone is the difference between "stop thinking" (which would land as criticism) and "I want to hear from a different part of you" (which lands as invitation).

The naming "I see the mind" before the redirect is part of the tone. The coach is acknowledging what the person has been doing, not dismissing it. The "really" in "I see the mind, really" is the acknowledgment that the mind work has been visible, valuable, worth seeing.

The redirect "but what's your heart say?" lands as a friendly second question rather than a correction.

## Why "Heart" Rather Than "Body"

The coach could ask "what does your body say?" instead. Both work. "Heart" carries a specific connotation that "body" does not: the relational, felt, value-laden response. "Body" can produce a body-scan ("I feel tightness in my chest"). "Heart" produces a relational-emotional report ("I feel chosen by her"). Both are useful. The choice depends on what the coach is reaching for.

In the source transcript, the question was about whether Brett's intellectual frame about death-as-gift was real for him. "Body" might have produced "my chest feels tight." "Heart" produced "Alexa chose this in me." The heart-frame got at the relational fact the mind-frame had been circling.

When the person is in their head about a relational question, "heart" is usually the right pointer. When the person is dissociated from the body in a more global way, "body" or specific somatic prompts may be the better pointer.

## Connected to [[head-heart-gut]]

This move is one specific application of the broader [[head-heart-gut]] framework. The framework names three intelligences: head, heart, gut. The move "what does your heart say, not your mind" is the operational version of redirecting from one intelligence to another in a live session.

The move can be generalized:
- "What does your gut say, not your mind?" when the person needs the body-level wisdom of orientation, action, or impulse.
- "What does your head say, not your heart?" when the person has been swept by feeling and needs the analytic check.

The directionality depends on what is currently dominant and what is being missed.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** [extensive intellectual frame about how letting fear in helps prevent the feared outcome] "...you process that data so that it is included in your--"
- **Coach:** "right, so then you're not acting out on it. And I'm curious what your heart says about it, not the mind. I see the mind, really, but what's your heart say?"
- **Person:** "This might be my mind interpreting the heart, but it says like, Alexa chose this in me... in feeling that, there's a lot of shame that falls away. There's like constriction that releases."
- **Coach:** "Beautiful. But there's something in me that moves when I -- there's like a heart opening, softening."
- *(Source: Marriage, Love, and Death. The intellectual frame was correct but inert; the heart-redirect produced the relational truth and the somatic shift.)*
