# Use the Strong Word for Somatic Landing

## The Move

When naming a dynamic that the client has been minimizing, use the strong word that names it precisely rather than the soft word that names it politely. The strong word forces the body to engage with the recognition. The soft word lets the body skip the recognition.

A parent who criticized constantly is "verbally abusive," not "demanding." A childhood where love was conditional on performance involved "abuse," not just "high standards." A relationship where a partner withholds love when displeased involves "emotional manipulation," not "communication issues." The choice of word determines whether the body lands the recognition or whether the cognitive layer absorbs it without somatic consequence.

The principle: the body does not respond to soft synonyms. The body responds to precise naming. If the dynamic was structurally abusive, the body needs the word "abuse" to land the recognition. Calling it "approval-seeking parenting" preserves the polite frame and prevents the recognition.

"If you use a word like approval, it doesn't actually hit the system in a somatic way." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

## Why This Works

A soft word is a permission slip for the body to keep the dynamic at arm's length. "She wanted my approval" is something the body can hold without changing. "She was abusive" is something the body has to actually contend with. The contending is the recognition. Without the contending, no recognition happens, and the work cannot proceed at the level the work needs to.

The body's threshold for engagement is calibrated to the language. Words below the threshold get absorbed without effect. Words at or above the threshold create the friction that lets the recognition land. The strong word puts the recognition above the threshold.

This is also why the same dynamic, named once with the soft word and once with the strong word, produces different sessions. The soft-word session stays cognitive. The strong-word session has a chance of becoming somatic.

## Joe's Personal Example

Joe gives a personal example of why the soft word fails:

"I remember for instance, when I was in a therapy session with Tara early on in our relationship, and our therapist was like, this is verbal abuse. And both of us were like, nah, it's not verbal abuse. And it's until we could admit to ourselves that it was abuse that, that we could feel it, own it, change it." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The example contains the whole pattern. The therapist used the strong word. Joe and Tara resisted it. The resistance was a measure of the word's accuracy — the body was contending with the recognition the soft word had been letting slide. Only when they could admit to themselves that the word was right did the felt recognition land and the change become possible.

The structure: strong word offered → resistance → working through the resistance → admission → felt recognition → change. Without the strong word, no resistance, no admission, no recognition, no change.

## What Counts as "Abuse" in This Usage

Joe expands what the word covers to prevent the move from being heard as melodramatic:

"Criticizing a kid all the time is abuse. Expecting perfection out of a kid is abuse. And until you can see it and own it and feel it, it's going to be a long haul to actually change that pattern." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The definition expands the word's reach to structural patterns most people would call something else — chronic criticism, perfectionist expectations, conditional love. The expansion is not a watering-down of the word; it is recognition that these patterns are abusive in their structural effect on the child, regardless of whether they meet the legal or popular definition of "abuse."

This expansion matters because the soft-word habit is built on the cultural narrowness of words like "abuse." Many adults will not call their childhood "abusive" because abuse, in their cultural imagination, requires hitting or yelling. The expansion lets the word do its work — to name the structural pattern that installed the survival strategy — without requiring the cultural threshold to be met.

## The Move as Care, Not Provocation

The strong word can sound provocative. The move is not provocative for its own sake. It is precise. The precision is what produces the landing. If the dynamic was not in fact abusive, the strong word would not land — the body would recognize the inaccuracy and the word would fail to produce the recognition. The word lands when it is true, even if it is also strong.

The care-based intention is part of what makes the move different from harsh judgment. The coach is not attacking the client's parents. The coach is naming a structural reality so the client can have access to the recognition that has been gated by polite language. The motive is the client's freedom, not the parents' prosecution.

This is why Joe explicitly notes that he does not make the abuser wrong:

"I don't make abuser, the abuser wrong. Like there's no wrong, doesn't solve the problem. Acknowledgement of it solves the problem." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The acknowledgment is the work. The strong word is the instrument of the acknowledgment. The wrongness conversation is a separate conversation that the strong word does not have to enter.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The strong word produces one of several responses. The coach reads which and adjusts:

**The body recognition lands (face shifts, eyes change, something arrives somatically):** the work proceeds on the basis of the now-landed recognition. The coach does not need to re-emphasize the word.

**The body resists (face composes, eyes glaze, intellectual rebuttal arrives):** the resistance is itself the data. The coach can name the resistance as the structure ("notice what just happened when I said that word"). The resistance often becomes the next layer of work.

**The client accepts verbally but the body doesn't engage:** the verbal acceptance is compliant rather than felt. The coach can name the gap ("the words are saying yes but I don't see the body landing it") and invite the felt engagement.

**The client refines the word:** the client offers a different precise word that fits their experience more exactly. This is the move working well — the strong word opened the door, the client's body offered the more accurate version. The coach takes the refined word as the working language.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not a license for verbal cruelty. The move requires precision and care. Using strong words to wound rather than to land recognition produces the opposite of the move's purpose.

It is not the claim that all soft language is wrong. Soft language has its place — when the body has already landed the recognition and the work is in the warmth of the recognition rather than the friction of arriving at it. The move is specifically for the moment of recognition.

It is not the claim that the strongest possible word is always the right one. The word has to be accurate. "Torture" applied to chronic criticism would not land because it overshoots. The right word is the strongest accurate word, not the strongest possible word.

It is not the claim that the client should use the same word in conversation with the relevant family members. The strong word is for the inner recognition. Whether and how the client communicates about the dynamic externally is a separate question.

## Tone

Precise and unflinching, but not cruel. The word is delivered as accurate description, not as accusation. The unflinching quality comes from the coach's commitment to the precision; the non-cruelty comes from the care underneath. The combination is what makes the move land as care rather than as attack.
