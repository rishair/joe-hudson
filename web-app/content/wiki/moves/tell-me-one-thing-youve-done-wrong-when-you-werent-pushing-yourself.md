# Tell Me One Thing You've Done Wrong When You Weren't Pushing Yourself

## The Move

When the person has answered [[what-makes-you-push-yourself]] with "I'm afraid of doing something wrong" (or any variant of "I push so I don't mess up"), invert it. "Tell me one thing you've done wrong when you weren't pushing yourself."

The person will pause. Usually they cannot produce an example. Then the second beat: "Now think about all the things that you think you've done wrong and how many of them are happening while you're pushing yourself."

The structural reveal: all of them. The pushing was being run to prevent "wrong." The pushing IS the source of "wrong." The thing the system was using as protection is the thing producing the harm.

## Why This Works

The fear-of-doing-wrong drives the pushing. The pushing is offered to the self as the solution to the fear. The person has never tested the underlying claim — that the pushing actually prevents wrong. They have only experienced the fear and the pushing, never the absence of both.

The inversion exposes the untested claim by asking for the evidence. The evidence does not exist. When the person searches their memory for "wrong I did when I wasn't pushing," they cannot find it. The wrongs they remember are all in the pushing state.

This is not a coincidence. The pushing is what produces the dissociated, disconnected, contradictory, performative state in which the person does the things they later judge as wrong. The pushing IS the dissociation. The dissociation IS what makes the actions land badly. The whole circuit was being maintained by the belief that the pushing was the cure.

See [[trying-prevents-the-feeling]] for the structural account at the level of feeling. This move is the structural account applied to action: the trying prevents right action the same way the trying prevents the feeling.

## What This Move Produces

The recognition usually arrives whole and fast. "It's me, all of them." The person sees that every wrong they've been worried about has happened while the pushing was operating. The self-protective device was the harm.

The recognition is not yet the dissolution. The person still has the pattern installed. But the foundation of the pattern — the belief that pushing prevents wrong — has just been removed. The pattern can now be worked with from a different angle. The pushing no longer has the justification it was running on.

This is the typical signature: the person sees it, says some version of "all of them," and then there is often a pause or a small smile of recognition. The smile is not the work being done; it is the body registering that the structure has been seen.

## What Joe Does Next

After the recognition, Joe usually returns to the original frame. "Now think about all the things you think you've done wrong and how many of them are happening while you're pushing yourself." Then he often re-issues [[come-to-me-without-contradiction]] or asks the person again to drop into the body.

The recognition is not the end. It is the beginning of the dissolution. The work continues — the pushing is still installed, the person will still push by default, the body has not yet learned that pushing isn't safety. But the intellectual claim ("pushing prevents wrong") is no longer available. The next time the system tries to push, the person can notice and recall that the pushing is the wrong-maker.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not soften the inversion.** "Maybe pushing yourself sometimes leads to mistakes" is not the move. The move is specific and absolute: name one thing you did wrong when you weren't pushing. The absoluteness is what produces the recognition.
- **Do not let the person work to find a counterexample.** Sometimes the person will try to satisfy the inversion by producing a "wrong I did when I wasn't pushing." Often this is not actually a non-pushing example — the person was pushing, just less visibly. Gently redirect: "Were you pushing then?" The pushing is usually present; the person has just been calling it something else.
- **Do not explain why this works.** The recognition is in the body. The explanation will pull the person into the head and dissolve the somatic insight. Let the recognition land. Move on.
- **Do not use this move when the answer wasn't "fear of doing wrong."** This move is for that specific answer. Other answers to [[what-makes-you-push-yourself]] get other follow-ups. The match has to be precise.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "What, what makes you push yourself?"
- **Client:** "So afraid of doing something wrong."
- **Joe:** "Tell me one thing you've done wrong when you aren't pushing yourself. You're pushing yourself right now. Question?"
- **Client:** "Feels like I haven't done anything wrong and I haven't pushed myself. Like nothing's coming to mind."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Now think about all the things that you think you've done wrong and how many of them are happening while you're pushing yourself."
- **Client:** "It's me, all of them."
- **Joe:** "How can that be? And again, I'm just going to ask you, like, come to me with no contradiction, like without a mask."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Stop Trying So Hard)*

The "How can that be?" after the recognition is the bridge back to the live work. Joe does not let the recognition stay intellectual. He uses it as the leverage for the next invitation to drop the mask.

## Tone

Direct, clear, slightly playful. The inversion is delivered as a clean ask, not as a gotcha. The "you're pushing yourself right now. Question?" is a small in-the-moment confirmation that the live pushing is also part of the data — the asking-of-the-question is itself a non-pushing-and-no-wrong moment, while the trying-to-answer is more pushing.

The follow-up after the recognition is matter-of-fact. "How can that be?" delivered with curiosity rather than triumph. The recognition is the person's, not the coach's. The coach is the witness, not the producer.

## When to Use This Move

- After [[what-makes-you-push-yourself]] when the answer is some version of "afraid of doing wrong"
- When the person has identified the pushing/trying as a way to prevent error or failure
- When the person is in an active loop of pushing themselves, judging themselves for "wrong" things they did, and then pushing more
- When the underlying belief "if I don't push, I'll mess up" needs to be tested against the evidence
