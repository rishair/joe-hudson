# Ask How They Could Do the Same Thing and Gain Energy From It

## The Move

The person reports a task or category of work that drains them. The coaching reflex would be to ask whether they could stop doing it, delegate it, or do less of it. Joe's move is different: assume the work has to happen. Ask how it could be done in a way that gives energy rather than takes energy.

The framing matters. The question is not "how do you do this with less effort." The question is "how do you do this and end up with more energy than you started with."

The question reaches for a different category of solution. Most efficiency moves are about reducing cost. This move is about flipping the sign on the cost: from energy-loss to energy-gain.

## When Joe Uses It

- When someone is in [[im-burned-out]] and reports specific tasks that drain them.
- In groups of executives, where each person hears the others answer the same question for their own draining task -- the cross-pollination produces an explosion of options.
- When [[urgency-as-motivation]] has been the engine and the person is starting to see that adrenaline is not sustainable.
- After establishing that the person is operating from the energy-management frame rather than the time-management frame. See [[energy-management-over-time-management]].

## The Group Version (Where the Move Is at Its Strongest)

Joe describes running this with a group of CEOs:

"I just worked with a group of CEOs and we did this exercise where I said, tell me the thing that takes the most adrenaline, that takes the most out of you, and we'll all ask them how, what questions about 'alternatives, alternative ways of doing it that actually feed you.' And every single one of them saw this breakthrough of like, 'Oh, I'm doing it in a way that takes my energy. I could be doing it in a way that gives me energy.'" -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

The structure:
1. One CEO names the most energy-draining task.
2. The other CEOs ask how-and-what questions about alternative ways to do it that would feed energy back. See [[how-what-questions]].
3. The constraint: each alternative must produce the same business outcome and give the CEO energy.
4. Inside five minutes, twenty alternatives surface.

The breakthrough is not the specific alternatives. The breakthrough is the realization that the energy-loss framing was a chosen one, not a structural one. The same task could be done with a different relationship to it.

"And so they would create these urgencies and then they'd be motivated, but all it did was make him more and more and more burnt out... There was literally infinite solutions, at least 20 inside of 5 minutes from the other CEOs who had all figured out some way to do that thing in a way that they enjoyed it." -- Joe Hudson, The Burnout Cycle

## What It Produces

- Recognition that the energy cost was a feature of how the work was being done, not of the work itself.
- A concrete list of alternative approaches generated by people who have figured out something similar.
- A shift in the operating frame from "I have to push through this" to "there is a way to do this that gives me back energy."
- For groups: cross-pollination that no individual could produce alone.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person can name an alternative: have them try it. The frame change without behavior change does not stick.

If the person draws a blank: ask other people. The blank is usually evidence that the person has been alone with the problem too long. Other people who have done similar work have found ways the person has not seen.

If the person says "there isn't any other way to do it": this is usually a sign of identification with the current way. The move is to question the identification. "If you started this company tomorrow, would you set up this task this way?"

If the person produces an alternative and immediately says "but I can't do that": Joe will examine the can't. It is usually a should masquerading as a structural constraint. See [[wants-over-shoulds]].

## Tone

Curious, generative, slightly playful. The question is asked in the spirit of "let's see if there's a way." Not in the spirit of "you should be doing this differently." The person being asked has to feel that the questioner believes the alternative exists.

In group settings, the energy is collaborative -- everyone wants the answer, everyone is suggesting things. The group's belief that an alternative exists is contagious.

## Dialogue Examples

- **CEO:** "I have to do a weekly sales meeting and I hate it. It drains me for the rest of the day."
- **Joe:** "Okay. What would have to be true for you to look forward to that meeting?"
- **CEO:** "I'd have to actually like the people. I'd have to feel like we were building something."
- **Joe:** "And what would change in how you run the meeting if you organized it around that?"
- **CEO:** "...I'd probably start with what people are excited about. Not the numbers."
- **Joe:** "Try that next week."

## Reads That Trigger This Move

- The person names a recurring task they have to do that drains them.
- The person frames the drain as inevitable ("that's just how it is").
- The person is in or approaching burnout.
- The person operates as an executive or founder and has authority to change how the task is done.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't use this to delegate away the underlying emotion.** If the task is draining because the person is avoiding a feeling, no operational reframe will fix it. The avoided feeling needs to be addressed. See [[burnout-cycle]] for the three-layer model.
- **Don't let "find a way that gives energy" become a new should.** The move is exploratory. If the person can't find a way, that is also fine. The framing alone is useful.
- **Don't use this with someone in deep collapse.** For the bathrobe-on-the-couch phase, the move is rest, not reframing. See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]].
