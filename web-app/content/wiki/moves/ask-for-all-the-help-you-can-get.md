# Ask For All the Help You Can Get

## The Move

The direct behavioral reverse of [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]]. The person who has been routing around the experts, organizing the work to keep gaps invisible, performing competence in front of those who could see through it — does the opposite. Show up, name what you don't know, ask for all the help you can get.

"What would it have been like if I just showed up and I was like, yep, I have literally never done this before. And that's, this particular thing we're doing is new to me. And I'm gonna do it... And I'm gonna ask for all the help that I can get." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The move has three components:

1. **Show up.** Be physically and relationally present with the people you have been routing around.
2. **Name the not-knowing.** Say out loud what you don't know. Specifically. Not "I'm a bit new" but "I have literally never done this before."
3. **Ask for all the help.** Not strategic, partial, face-saving help. All the help. The framing "all the help you can get" is important — it forecloses the negotiation about which kind of help you can accept and still maintain the front.

## When to Use It

This is the operational move for the moment when the imposter feeling is firing and the hiding strategy is being activated. The move uses the imposter feeling as the signal that the help-ask is needed, rather than as the signal that more hiding is needed.

Specifically:

- When the client is about to show up to a project, role, or situation where they don't know what they're doing
- When the client has been working alone on something where there are obvious experts available
- When the client is about to walk into a meeting they have been performing competence for
- When the client has identified a specific person they have been avoiding because admitting their gap would expose them
- When the client has been organizing work to hide a gap and is exhausted from the organizing

The move is also useful as a prophylactic at the start of any new role or project. Asking for all the help at the beginning prevents the hiding loop from getting started. The role becomes "person who is here to learn and contribute" rather than "person who is here to perform expertise."

## What It Produces

The expected outcome from the inside is catastrophic exposure. The actual outcome is almost always the opposite:

- The expert responds to a direct "I don't know this, I would love your help" with help. Often with relief that they don't have to pretend the person knows.
- The relationship deepens because vulnerability is the precondition for trust. See [[vulnerability]].
- The work improves because the inputs from the people who actually know are now available.
- The imposter feeling drops because the hiding has stopped — the loop's input is gone. See [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]].
- The cognitive bandwidth previously consumed by the persona returns. See [[maintaining-false-persona-costs-more-than-incompetence]].

The non-expected outcome: the person discovers they were already expending more energy hiding than the asking would cost. The "ask for help" felt like a huge expense from inside the hiding; from outside the hiding, it is structurally cheaper than the strategy it replaces.

## The CEO Form

The same move at the executive scale: "Here's what I don't know. Here's what I do know. What do you all know and don't know? How do we solve this problem?"

"Great leadership is, here's what I don't know. Here's what I do know. What do you all know and don't know? How do we solve this problem that feels like the problem that we need to solve or is the problem that we need to solve?" -- Joe Hudson, What To Do About Imposter Syndrome

The leader who does this gets the inputs, builds the team alignment, and stops running the imposter loop. The leader who does not — who has to pretend to know everything — gets none of these. The vulnerability is operationally more competent than the performance.

See [[leader-says-what-they-dont-know-builds-alignment]] for the leadership-specific application.

## What Joe Does With It

When prescribing this move, Joe attaches a few qualifiers:

- **Specificity.** The ask should be concrete, not vague. "Can you help me figure out X" rather than "I might need some help at some point." The vagueness is often the hiding sneaking back in.
- **Not strategic.** The ask is not "ask in a way that doesn't expose how much you don't know." The point is to expose it. Strategic asks preserve the front; they don't break the loop.
- **All-the-help, not just-enough-help.** "Just enough help" is the form of asking that lets the person maintain most of the front. The "all the help" framing forces the front fully down.

When the client resists ("but if I ask for that much help they'll think I'm not qualified for the role"):

- Name the loop: the not-asking is generating the feeling of not being qualified. The asking is the way out.
- Test the catastrophic prediction. What actually happens when people ask for help? Most of the time, the answer is "the other person was glad to be asked." The prediction of catastrophe is the imposter feeling generating its own evidence.
- Use the BASE jumping example. Joe's own avoidance of the experts produced the worse outcome — the production value suffered. The hiding does not protect; it harms. See [[base-jumping-commercial-as-imposter-syndrome-example]].

## Tone

Practical, almost mundane. The move is not heroic ("be brave and ask"). It is mechanical: the loop is generated by hiding; the loop breaks by asking. The first ask is the hard one because the imposter feeling is loudest when the hiding strategy is being violated. After the first, the strategy starts to disconfirm itself.

The coach delivers this move as available right now. Not "next time you're in this situation, try asking for help." Just "who is the person you have been avoiding asking? What is the question you have been pretending to know the answer to? Call them today."
