# Name the Deflection As It's Happening

## The Move

When the client deflects a compliment, a piece of recognition, or any moment of being-seen, the coach names the deflection in the same beat that it is happening. The naming is short, precise, and refuses to let the deflection complete unseen.

Three live forms from the source session:

"Let that hit. You, you started to let that hit and then you went to a thought." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

"That's a different thing that you're distracting from the fact that you're being seen right now. I see that you've been in survival mode, and it's hard, and you put a lot of effort into it, and you're deflecting me." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

"So this is what happens when you're actually seen." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

Each version is a real-time observation. The deflection happened, the coach saw it, the coach named it, the recognition is back on the table. The move keeps the receiving alive by refusing to let the deflection close the channel without being noticed.

## The Three Sub-Forms

The move has at least three shapes depending on what the client just did:

**1. "You started to let that hit and then you went to a thought."** Use when the client's body briefly received the recognition and then the mind intercepted and produced a thought (often an analytical thought, a question, or a self-deprecating reflection). The naming separates the body-reception from the mind-pivot.

**2. "You're distracting from the fact that you're being seen right now."** Use when the client pivots to a different topic (helping others, an analytical question, a different concern). See [[deflecting-by-pivoting-to-helping-others]] for the specific case of the helper-pivot.

**3. "This is what happens when you're actually seen."** Use when the client is showing a cluster of deflection behaviors (mm-hmm, lips tightening, eye drop, subject change) all at once. The framing names the cluster as the predictable response to being seen — not as a failure but as the structure firing.

The three are complementary, not exclusive. A single deflection often involves a body-pause, a mind-pivot, and a topic-shift all in sequence. The coach can name whichever component is most visible or most useful in the moment.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The client's awareness arrives in the moment of the deflection rather than after the deflection has closed
- The client recognizes the deflection as something they did, not something that just happened
- The client's body has the chance to re-open the channel if they want to
- The deflection itself becomes part of what is being seen — the client is now being seen *for the deflection*, which is sometimes the move that lets the original recognition land
- The client often says some version of "I get good at that" — naming the long practice of the move

If the move bounces, the client may say "yeah, you're right" intellectually without re-opening the channel. In that case, the coach can move to [[do-the-deflection-back-to-show-them-how-they-do-it]] or [[live-naming-the-physical-constriction-of-non-reception]] to make the deflection visible at a more visceral level.

## When Joe Uses It

The setup:

- The coach has just offered a compliment, recognition, or piece of seeing
- The client is in the middle of deflecting (the deflection has not yet completed)
- The deflection is visible to the coach — either as a body-shift, a thought-pivot, or a topic-change
- The session is in a working state where the live naming will be experienced as helpful rather than as criticism

The move is one of the most-used moves in compliment-receiving and being-seen work. It is the basic intervention that keeps the receiving alive in a session where the client's default is to close the channel.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client lets it back in: Joe re-offers the recognition. The original compliment, re-offered with the deflection now named, often lands deeper because the client is more present.

If the client says "I get good at that": Joe affirms the recognition and may add a body-level observation or a cost-of-deflection teaching (see [[the-cake-metaphor-for-deflected-compliments]]).

If the client deflects the naming itself ("I don't think I was deflecting"): Joe stays calm and re-describes what he observed without arguing. Often the client can see what Joe is describing once it has been described twice.

If the client gets emotional: Joe stays with the emotion. The naming of the deflection often surfaces grief at how long the deflection has been operating.

## Dialogue Examples

After Joe offered the effort-compliment about survival mode:

- **Joe:** "I really see that you put a lot of effort into being in survival mode."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I think it hit."
- **Joe:** "Let that hit. You, you started to let that hit and then you went to a thought."

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The client started to let it hit and then went to "I think it hit" — a thought *about* the hit rather than the hit itself. Joe names the going-to-the-thought as the deflection move and re-instructs the body to receive.

After the client pivoted to talking about helping others:

- **Client:** "I don't know how to focus on myself and not want to help others."
- **Joe:** "That's a different thing that you're distracting from the fact that you're being seen right now."

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The pivot to help-others is the deflection. Joe names the pivot as distraction and returns the focus to the being-seen that was happening when the pivot started.

After a cluster of deflection behaviors:

- **Joe:** [Offers recognition; client responds with mm-hmm and a flat affect.]
- **Joe:** "So this is what happens when you're actually seen."
- **Client:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Why the fuck do I want to see you if you're just blowing me off like this?"

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The "this is what happens when you're actually seen" frames the cluster as the predictable response. The follow-up makes the cost explicit using the same channel as [[the-cake-metaphor-for-deflected-compliments]].

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not criticism.** The naming is observational. "You started to let that hit and then you went to a thought" is a description, not a judgment.
- **It is not a one-time intervention.** The deflection will fire again. The naming will need to happen again. The repetition is part of the work.
- **It is not the same as [[do-the-deflection-back-to-show-them-how-they-do-it]].** That move performs the deflection back at the client. This move names the deflection as it's happening. The two can pair: name first, perform if naming doesn't land.
- **It is not the same as the read [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]].** That read is the diagnostic — what the coach sees. This move is what the coach does with what they see.

## The Critical Timing

The naming has to happen in the same beat as the deflection. If the coach waits too long, the deflection has already closed and naming it after the fact has less impact. The skill is recognizing the deflection in flight and naming it before the next sentence.

A common timing failure: the coach lets the client finish a long deflection ("yeah, I think it hit, but I should probably learn how to help others more because I'm always so focused on myself and it's hard for me to..."), then names the deflection at the end. By then, the deflection has compounded across multiple sentences. The naming is now a longer intervention with less specificity. Better to interrupt mid-sentence: "wait, you started to let it hit and then you went to a thought."

## Tone

Direct, slightly warm, observational. Joe's "you started to let that hit and then you went to a thought" is delivered as a noticing, not as a catch. The voice does not rise. The energy stays steady. The client is being shown what happened, not corrected for what they did wrong.

The "this is what happens when you're actually seen" carries a slightly more provocative tone — it names the predictability of the deflection without making it personal. The framing implies "this is the structure firing, not you failing."

The "you're deflecting me right now" carries the most directness. It can land harder than the other forms and is best used when rapport is solid and the client can metabolize the direct naming without collapse.
