# Live-Name the Physical Constriction of Non-Reception

## The Move

When a client deflects a compliment with a small body-level constriction — a tightening around the mouth, a slight head pull-back, a closed throat — the coach names the constriction in real time as a physical event. The naming is precise about location and movement and refuses interpretation. The client is being shown what their body just did.

"Like, you're sitting here talking to me, and then I give you a compliment, your lips tighten, and you go, mm-hmm. It's like you're physically constricting to not let in my compliment." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The structure of the observation:

1. Set the scene briefly. "Like, you're sitting here talking to me..."
2. Name the specific stimulus. "...and then I give you a compliment..."
3. Name the specific body event. "...your lips tighten, and you go, mm-hmm."
4. Name what the body is doing. "It's like you're physically constricting to not let in my compliment."

The four-part structure makes the deflection visible at three levels: the stimulus (the compliment), the body response (lips tighten, mm-hmm), and the function of the response (constricting to not let in). The client now has a body-event linked to a stimulus linked to a function — the data they need to recognize the move from inside next time.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The client says some version of "that's so true" or "I didn't know I did that"
- The client's body becomes available to them as a source of information about the deflection
- The client can now feel the lips-tightening as a body-event rather than experiencing it as a fact of reality
- The recognition of the body-event often softens the body's grip in the same moment — once the body is being seen doing the move, the move's automaticity loosens
- The client is positioned to do the receiving practice with conscious access to the move that was previously blocking it

If the move bounces, the client may experience the observation as criticism of their body. The coach has to read the rapport and ensure the observation is delivered as data, not as evaluation.

## When Joe Uses It

The setup:

- The client has just deflected a compliment
- The deflection had a visible body-component (mouth tightening, head tilt, eye drop, throat closure, jaw clench)
- The verbal naming alone has not produced full recognition (see [[name-the-deflection-as-its-happening]])
- The client is in a state where body-level observation will be metabolized rather than experienced as exposure
- The work needs to move from "you deflect" to "your body does this specific thing when it deflects"

The move is most powerful when the body-event is small and habitual — something the client does many times a day without noticing. The naming of the small habitual move is what makes the pattern visible. The client cannot stop a move they cannot see.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client says "that's so true": Joe affirms and sometimes adds another layer. "Or being seen" — naming that the constriction is not just to deflect compliments but to deflect the broader experience of being-seen.

If the client touches their own mouth or feels the lips tightening in the moment: Joe stays with the body awareness. The body has just become available to the client as observable. The next move is often a body-level receiving instruction ([[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]).

If the client gets defensive ("I don't think I did that"): Joe re-describes what he saw without arguing. Sometimes a second compliment will re-produce the body-event and the client can feel it the second time.

If the client cries: the recognition of the body's deflection has surfaced grief at how long the deflection has been operating. Joe stays with the tears.

If the client asks "how do I stop doing that?": Joe redirects from stopping to noticing. The work is not to suppress the body's move (which would install another wall) but to notice it and let recognition land anyway. The body's move and the recognition can both happen at once.

## Dialogue Example

After Joe did the mm-hmm deflection back at the client and clarified it was a demonstration:

- **Joe:** "Like, you're sitting here talking to me, and then I give you a compliment, your lips tighten, and you go, mm-hmm. It's like you're physically constricting to not let in my compliment."
- **Client:** "That's so true."
- **Joe:** "Or being seen."
- **Client:** "Yeah. Mhm. Yes. Yeah, my friend was like, just say thank you, right? You'll say thank you. And I have such — I feel so complex saying thank you, which is weird, right?"

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The exchange shows the move's full arc. Joe names the specific body event (lips tightening, mm-hmm). The client's "that's so true" is the recognition. Joe extends the observation to the broader frame (constricting to not let in being seen, not just compliments). The client connects the body-event to her own previous attempts to address the pattern through the surface fix (just say thank you). The deeper structure — the "complex feeling about saying thank you" — surfaces as a result of the body-level recognition having landed.

## Why Naming the Body Matters

The deflection of compliments is usually described as a thought or a behavior. The client thinks "no, that's not really true" or behaves by saying "oh, you're being too kind." These descriptions miss the body-level move that is happening underneath the thoughts and behaviors.

The body is where the deflection actually lives. The lips tighten before the mm-hmm. The throat closes before the words come out. The jaw clenches before the deflecting thought arrives. The body's move is the cause; the verbal deflection is the effect.

Naming the body-level move gives the client access to the cause. Once they can feel the lips tightening, they can choose what to do with that tightening — they can let it happen and still let the compliment land, or they can soften the lips and let the body open. The verbal-level work without the body-level work is doomed because the verbal is downstream of the body.

This connects to the broader principle that the head is the last thing to update and the body is the last thing to release — see [[mind-unsees-first-body-last]]. The body has to be addressed directly. The naming of the body-event is the first step.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not criticism of the body.** The naming is observational. The body is doing exactly what it has been trained to do. The recognition is for the body, not against it.
- **It is not interpretation.** The coach is naming what is observable, not what it means. "Your lips tighten and you go mm-hmm" is observable. "You're afraid of being seen because of your childhood" is interpretation. The latter belongs in a different phase of the work, if at all.
- **It is not the same as [[body-cues-of-shame-about-being-seen]] as a read.** That read is the diagnostic — what the coach sees and what it tells them. This move is what the coach does with the diagnostic.
- **It is not the same as [[body-contradicts-the-words]].** That read catches the contradiction between affect and content (smiling while describing fear). This move catches the body's deflection move itself (the lips tightening as the compliment lands).
- **It is not a substitute for the receiving work.** The move makes the body's deflection visible. The receiving still has to happen. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]].

## What to Observe

Common body-events that signal the deflection of a compliment or recognition:

- **Lips tightening** — the most common; the mouth seals against the inflow
- **Mm-hmm** — the flat acknowledging sound delivered without the body actually receiving
- **Head tilt down** — the chin drops, often paired with eyes dropping; signals shame about being seen
- **Quick subject pivot** — the body twists slightly as the topic changes
- **Hand to mouth** — the hand covers the mouth as a physical wall
- **Jaw clench** — the jaw locks before the deflecting thought arrives
- **Shoulders tighten** — the upper body braces against the inflow
- **Eye drop to the right** — common in shame-about-being-seen; see [[body-cues-of-shame-about-being-seen]]
- **Throat closure** — the voice gets flatter or higher as the throat constricts
- **Tight smile** — the face performs reception while the body refuses

The coach has to be tracking these in real time. The capacity to name them depends on the capacity to see them.

## Tone

Observational, direct, slightly tender. Joe's "your lips tighten and you go mm-hmm" is delivered as a noticing, not as a catch. The voice stays steady. The energy is curious about what the body is doing, not judgmental of the body for doing it.

The "It's like you're physically constricting to not let in my compliment" is the function-level naming. This part is delivered with slightly more weight — the function-naming is the substance of the observation. The client needs to understand that the body's move has a purpose.

When the client recognizes the body-event, Joe softens slightly and stays with the recognition. He does not move on quickly. The body needs time to update.
