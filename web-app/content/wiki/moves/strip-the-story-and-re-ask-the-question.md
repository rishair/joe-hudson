# Strip the Story and Re-Ask the Question

## The Move

The person presents a question wrapped in a self-story — usually a story of being bad, being broken, being one's own obstacle. The move is to acknowledge the question, name the story explicitly, and ask the person to re-ask the same question without the story attached.

The form Joe uses:

"If I could just stop that story, you couldn't — not that you — it's bad, but if I just like clicked my fingers and that story stopped, it would probably help."

"Yeah. Okay, so let's try the question again without the story, without the story inside of you. So just how, if that story couldn't exist inside of you, what would you ask me?"

Three components matter:

1. **Hypothetical, not corrective.** The story is not framed as wrong or as needing to be deconstructed. It is framed as a thing that, hypothetically, could be removed. The hypothetical makes the strip possible without triggering defense.
2. **Same question, different wrapper.** The person is not asked to produce a new or deeper question. They are asked to ask the same question without the story. The instruction makes clear that the question itself is fine; the wrapper is what's getting in the way.
3. **Stay live.** "Just like live without the story for a moment." The strip is not an intellectual exercise. It is a momentary stepping-out-of the story to access the question that exists underneath.

## When Joe Uses It

This move fires when:

- The person opens with a question that contains a self-shame story embedded in it
- The story is doing real work in the presentation (it is not incidental phrasing; it is structuring the question)
- The presenting question is impossible to answer because the story has pre-judged any answer
- The story is itself part of the cascade the person is asking about — they are asking how to stop being the way the story already says they are

The canonical example:

- **Person:** "How do I stop procrastinating and how do I stop watching porn? I've been trying it for my whole life. I've tried it really, really hard. I feel so much shame. I've gotten better at not feeling as much shame for it, but I still feel shame. And I'm so good at making myself unhappy. Other people or other circumstances in my life don't have as much impact on my happiness as I have on myself because I, I don't act the way I want to act in so many circumstances of my life."

The question "how do I stop procrastinating" is wrapped in: I've been trying my whole life / I feel so much shame / I'm so good at making myself unhappy / I'm responsible for my own happiness and failing at it. Each of those is a self-story. The combined wrapper makes the question impossible to enter on its terms — any answer Joe gives will be filtered through the wrapper that already says the person is the kind of person who can't do this.

Joe's move:

- **Joe:** "Yeah. If all that story stopped, what would happen to your procrastination and your — let's just do procrastination for a moment."
- **Person:** "That whole story stops."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that, that story you just expressed, it goes on in your head all the time. Yeah."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I'm pretty sure."
- **Joe:** "If I could just, if I could just stop that story, you couldn't — not that you — it's bad, but if I just like clicked my fingers and that story stopped, it would probably help."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I would probably not think about it as much, and I would, I would be more natural, and I would pursue my goals better, and I would probably just — it would help, I think."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Okay, so let's try the question again without the story, without the story inside of you. So just how, if that story couldn't exist inside of you, what would you ask me?"

## What It Produces

When the strip works, the person produces a different question — almost always a more fundamental one. In the canonical session, the question "how do I stop procrastinating?" became, with the story stripped, "how do I find my potential?" and then, with one more pass, "how do I become myself?"

Each pass surfaces a question with less defended self-character. The original question asks about a behavior. The stripped question asks about being.

What does not happen: the person re-produces the same question. If the person re-asks "how do I stop procrastinating?" without the story, the question is now in clean form and can be worked. Often the question itself dissolves in the stripping. The behavior was a placeholder for the deeper question; with the story gone, the deeper question becomes available.

A common failure mode: the person tries to think their way to a new question. They go to their pre-written list, their notes from the last session, their head's idea of what a better question would be. Joe redirects:

- **Person:** "I've actually already written down a different question, which might be the one then."
- **Joe:** "No, don't do that. Okay. Because then you're going into your head. Just like live without the story for a moment. I know it's a challenge. Live without the story for a moment and just tell me what the question is."

The instruction is precise: live without the story right now, then say what the question is. The question has to come from the moment, not from preparation.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The new question becomes the question the session works on. The original framing is allowed to fall away. The coaching does not return to the surface behavior unless the body brings it back.

If the new question is something like "how do I become myself?" — Joe pivots to the felt territory of essential self. He does not give the new question content; he gives it presence. See [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] and [[what-am-i]] for what typically comes next.

If the new question is structurally identical to the old one but cleaner — Joe takes that as the contract and works it without the wrapper. The work is easier because the pre-judged frame is no longer in the way.

If the person can't find a new question — Joe stays with the felt experience of trying to ask without the story. The trying itself is feelable, and the felt experience is often where the work begins.

## Distinction from "What's the Real Question?"

This move is structurally distinct from the [[whats-the-real-question]] move, even though they look similar on the surface.

- **What's the real question?** is used when the opening was a contextual report without a question, or when the opening contains a different question than the one the person came in for. The move surfaces a question that was hidden underneath the framing.

- **Strip the story and re-ask the question** is used when the opening contained a question that was wrapped in a self-shame story. The same question gets re-asked with the wrapper removed. The question itself was visible; the wrapper was preventing it from being workable.

The difference matters because the moves go to different places. "What's the real question?" can produce a question that is structurally different from the opening. "Strip the story" usually produces a deeper version of the same question, with the self-character pre-judgments removed.

A useful diagnostic for which move to use: if the person's opening contained a clearly stated question that you could write down in one sentence, and the surrounding material was a self-story about being bad at the thing the question is about — the strip-the-story move is the one. If the person's opening was a long contextual report that ended without resolving into a question — the real-question move is the one.

## Tone

Warm, slightly humorous, deliberately tentative. Joe uses hypothetical framing ("if I could just clicked my fingers") and adds qualifiers ("not that you — it's bad") that signal he is not attacking the story. The hypothetical lets the strip happen without the person needing to defend the story they just expressed.

The pace is slow. The person needs time to actually leave the story. A fast re-ask produces a fast re-engagement of the story. The instruction "live without the story for a moment" requires a moment — Joe waits for it to land before asking again.

When the person tries to bypass the live work by going to pre-written material, Joe's redirect is gentle but firm: "No, don't do that." The firmness is what prevents the move from becoming another intellectual exercise.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not "your story is wrong." The story might be true. The point is not its truth value. The point is that it is making the question impossible to work on.
- This is not "let's deconstruct your limiting beliefs." Deconstruction would re-engage the head. The strip is a felt removal of the story for the duration of one question, not a cognitive analysis of why the story is wrong.
- This is not "ignore your suffering." The suffering inside the story is real. The move temporarily sets it aside so the question can be asked cleanly. The suffering will be worked on later — through the question that surfaces, not through the story that was wrapping it.
- This is not [[whats-the-real-question]]. See the distinction above.
