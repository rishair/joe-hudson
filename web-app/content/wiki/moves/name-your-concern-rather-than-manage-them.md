# Name Your Concern Rather Than Manage Them

## The Move

When the parent (or partner, or friend) notices an impulse to manage someone — to instruct, correct, nag, remind, micromanage — they instead name the concern directly as their own. The concern stays with them. The other person is told about it, not made responsible for resolving it.

The structural difference:

- **Management:** "You need to wear your jacket. Did you bring your jacket? Why didn't you bring your jacket? You're going to get cold." (The parent's concern about the kid's comfort has been converted into a series of instructions and corrections aimed at the kid.)
- **Concern-naming:** "I notice I'm getting a little anxious about you getting cold. I don't want to manage you about it. Just letting you know what's going on with me." (The concern is named as the parent's. The kid is informed but not made responsible for resolving the parent's anxiety.)

The move is Joe's alternative to nagging in his own household:

"With my girls, if there's something that I feel like, oh my gosh, I need to manage this thing, I'll talk to them about that. I'll just say, 'Hey, I'm concerned about this thing and I don't want to manage you because managing you sucks.'" -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

## When to Reach for It

- The parent notices an urge to nag, instruct, or correct
- The parent has a real concern about something the kid is doing (or not doing)
- The relationship is starting to feel friction over the small things
- The kid is starting to react against the constant low-grade correction
- A teen has stopped responding to instructions and the parent is wondering what to do
- A partner notices they are starting to manage their spouse's behavior
- A friend notices they are starting to lecture their friend about choices

The move is for the moment of recognition of the impulse to manage. The impulse arrives. The recognition is the cue to substitute the concern-naming for the management.

## What Makes the Move Work

Several structural features:

**The concern stays with the parent.** The kid is not asked to fix the parent's anxiety. The kid is informed about it. The information is theirs to do with what they will. This refuses the standard structure where the parent transmits their anxiety as the kid's job.

**The honesty about the impulse is included.** "I don't want to manage you because managing you sucks." This is not the parent suppressing the management urge and pretending it is not happening. The parent is telling the kid: this is what I notice in myself, here is what I am choosing to do instead. This models awareness rather than performing composure.

**The transparency invites the kid to be a partner in the relationship.** Once the kid knows the parent is worried, the kid can engage with the worry as a fellow adult about a real concern. They might say "yeah, I'm planning to do it this weekend" or "yeah, I see what you mean" or "actually here's what's going on for me about that." None of these conversations are available when the parent is in management mode, because management does not invite response — it dictates behavior.

**It preserves the kid's autonomy.** The kid still gets to decide what to do. This is critical for the teen years specifically, where the kid is in the process of becoming their own authority. See [[teach-teenagers-to-be-their-own-authority]]. Management overrides autonomy. Concern-naming preserves it.

**It models a more functional relational stance.** The kid learns that adults can name their concerns honestly without converting the concerns into demands. This is a relationship skill they will use everywhere. The standard management-driven household teaches that worry must be transmitted as instruction. The concern-naming household teaches that worry can be a piece of honest information shared between people.

## The Empirical Confirmation: Both Sides Hate the Nagging

Joe's own confirmation that the move works comes from a conversation he had with his daughters when they were 6-7 years old:

"I sat down and I was like, 'I hate nagging you. It sucks. It feels like crap in my system to nag you.' And they were like, 'Yeah, it feels like crap to be nagged.' I was like, 'Yeah, I still have a memory of being nagged by my mom. I'm like, yeah.' And we'd literally, even at that young age, [say] like, 'How do we live this life together without me nagging you, but me still teaching you what I need to teach you? Because that's my job as a parent.'" -- Joe Hudson, Some Families Feel Like Home & Others Feel Like an Obligation

See [[joe-and-daughters-on-nagging]] for the example article.

Two things are operating in this conversation. First, both sides admit the nagging feels bad — the nagger and the nagged both hate it. The dynamic persists not because anyone likes it but because it has not been examined. Second, both sides become partners in designing the alternative. The kids are 6-7 years old and they are co-designing the household. The concern-naming move is part of the larger pattern of treating the kid as a partner rather than as a subject.

## What the Move Is NOT

**Not a stealth technique to get the kid to do what you wanted them to do anyway.** If the parent is naming concerns and still has the expectation that the kid will do exactly what the parent would have prescribed, the move has degraded into management with a different surface. The concern-naming has to actually accept that the kid may not do what the parent would have done. If that is not acceptable, the parent has not actually let go of the management — they have just dressed it up.

**Not the same as never giving instructions.** Some things genuinely require instruction (a young child near a busy street, a teen learning a dangerous task for the first time, an actual emergency). The move is for the chronic low-grade management that fills most of the conversation in management-heavy households, not for the legitimate moments of instruction.

**Not the same as venting.** "I'm so worried about you" delivered with anxiety, repeatedly, while the kid is trying to do something is its own form of management — the kid is being asked to absorb the parent's emotional state. The move requires the parent to actually be okay with what they are doing. The concern is named and then left there. If the parent cannot leave it there, the concern is still being weaponized.

**Not a substitute for the inner work.** The impulse to manage often comes from the parent's own dysregulation. See [[dont-micromanage-your-kid]] for the structural source. The move addresses the in-the-moment expression of the impulse but does not address the underlying anxiety. Doing both is the full work.

**Not the same as performing the move.** The concern-naming has to be honest. A parent who says "I'm concerned about this and I don't want to manage you" while subtly transmitting "but I am going to keep bringing it up until you fix it" is doing management with extra steps. The kid reads the field, not the words. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]].

## The Variant: Naming the Limit of the Move

Sometimes the parent does need to draw a line. The concern-naming move is not for those moments. For actual limits, see [[draw-boundaries-with-kids-to-let-anger-arise]] and [[stay-close-and-insist]]. The distinction is between things the parent has a concern about (which the kid can choose to act on or not) and things that are actual limits (which the parent will hold regardless).

Mixing these up is a common failure mode. The parent treats a real limit as a concern (and the kid blows past it because nothing is actually being held). Or the parent treats a concern as a limit (and the kid experiences it as management). The move requires the parent to be honest with themselves about which is which.

## Tone

The naming of the concern is delivered without intensity, without anxiety, without the unstated demand that the kid fix it. The honesty about not wanting to manage is delivered with humor and warmth — "managing you sucks" is offered as a shared observation, not as a complaint. The kid feels: this is real information, my parent is being honest with me, I am being trusted.

The opposite tone — the concern delivered with weight, the "I don't want to manage you but I really need you to" tail — collapses the move back into management. The lightness is structural to the move's effect.

## What It Produces

- The kid registers the concern without being defensive
- The kid often addresses the concern on their own timeline, in their own way, without the parent's pressure
- The friction over the small stuff goes down
- The relationship has more room
- The kid develops the skill of naming their own concerns honestly because they have been modeled
- The parent has less of the energy-drain that the chronic management produces
- The teen years can be conducted without the parent constantly trying to control behavior, which prevents the suffocation that produces the rebellion (see [[suffocating-kids-forces-rebellion-as-only-self-route]])
