# Normalize the Experience — "This Has Happened to Many People"

## What Joe Does

When someone arrives in the disruptive phase of an awakening (see [[fog-walker-vs-belt-over-the-head|the belt-over-the-head pattern]]), Joe's primary move is normalization. He names what is happening as a known phenomenon, says that it has happened to many people, indicates that he is unbothered by it, and offers the framework that explains it.

The move is structurally simple. Its operational power is in the unbothered tone of the person delivering it. The person experiencing the disruption is scared. The single most effective intervention is the presence of someone who has seen this before, recognizes it, and is not alarmed.

"For mentally, it's really important for them to know, oh, just notice you're still operating... So you're still operable, you're still feeding yourself, you're still able to make your way in the world. And the job is to just notice how it still works." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

"For those folks, they need like a lot of ground, a lot of holding. They need... it's really important for them to realize like, oh, this is normal. This happens. I can point to 500 other people that it's happened to, and it's a known thing. Maybe not by everybody, it's a known thing." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The move comes from a long tradition. Joe is operating in the lineage of practitioners who have always known that the primary intervention for someone in zen sickness, kundalini disruption, or accidental awakening is normalization. The intervention is documented in Zen monastic traditions, in kundalini yoga teaching, in non-dual lineages. It is not Joe's invention. He is using it because it works.

## When Joe Uses It

The move applies whenever someone is:

- In a disruptive awakening event ([[something-weird-is-happening-to-me]]).
- Describing perceptual or somatic changes they cannot explain and that they fear are pathological.
- Asking "how do I get this to stop?"
- Convinced they are going crazy or losing their mind.
- The partner of someone going through this and convinced the partner needs psychiatric intervention ([[my-partner-is-going-through-something]]).
- In a long integration process and worried they are doing it wrong.

It is the first move when the disruption is in progress. Other moves (grounding, framework, working with specific identifications) follow. The normalization comes first because a panicked nervous system cannot absorb anything else.

## What the Move Sounds Like

Specific instantiations from Joe's actual practice:

- "This is normal. This happens."
- "I can point to 500 other people that it's happened to."
- "It's a known thing. Maybe not by everybody, but it's a known thing."
- "What you're describing matches what religious literature has been describing for thousands of years."
- "This has a name. It's called [zen sickness / kundalini awakening / depersonalization-as-awakening]."
- "When you find out it's normal and you see that I'm not bothered by it, then you can get curious about it."

The phrasing matters less than the tone. The phrasing carries the framework; the tone carries the unbotheredness.

## What Produces the Result

Two things together:

**The framework:** The person has been operating without a framework that explains what is happening to them. Their available frameworks (medical, psychiatric, "I must be broken") all generate alarm. A new framework (awakening, integration, normal stage of development) generates a different relationship to the same experience.

**The unbothered tone:** The person's nervous system is reading the coach's nervous system for whether the situation is an emergency. If the coach is alarmed, the situation is an emergency. If the coach is unbothered, the situation is not an emergency. The coach's state is the operative intervention. The words are downstream.

Joe is explicit that the unbothered quality is the part that does the work:

"When they find out it's normal and they see that I'm not bothered by it... then they can get curious about it, and everything shifts." -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

The two together produce a different felt experience of the same underlying event. The person who had been experiencing a terrifying breakdown of self begins experiencing the same loosening as an integration to be welcomed.

## The Critical Diagnostic

The move only works if the coach can deliver it from an actually-unbothered place. Performed unbotheredness does not work; it reads as performance, and the nervous system that was reading for safety registers the performance as something to be alarmed about.

The diagnostic for whether the coach can deliver this move: does the coach have a felt history of seeing this experience before, and a felt confidence that the experience is workable? If yes, the move can be delivered authentically. If no, the move will not land and may make things worse.

For coaches who have not personally seen this many times: be careful. Either gain that experience or refer. The move requires the felt confidence; the words alone are not the move.

## Variants by Channel

The framework offered should match the channel through which the awakening landed. See [[three-paths-of-awakening]] for the channels.

**Head channel:** "What you're describing is the recognition that the thinker is not the actual self. This happens to people who have done sustained inquiry or meditation. The disorientation is the system reorganizing. It passes. The recognition stays."

**Heart channel:** "What you're describing is the heart opening past the bounded-self version of love. This sometimes produces a sustained love-everywhere phase. See [[heart-awakening-disney-phase]]. The intensity passes. The capacity to love stays."

**Gut channel:** "What you're describing is the nervous system shifting out of the chronic me-against-the-world organization. The shaking, the sleep disruption, the energetic activity -- these are recognized signs. They benefit from grounding work and sometimes from somatic practitioners. See [[ground-the-body-during-awakening]]."

For the partner of someone going through it, the normalization is the same but framed for the witness. See [[my-partner-is-going-through-something]] and [[be-with-them-without-trying-to-fix-or-diagnose]].

## What Not to Pair It With

Do not pair normalization with:

- "And you should be doing X" -- the normalization is itself the intervention. Adding instruction crowds it.
- "But you should still see a psychiatrist just in case" -- this re-installs the alarm the normalization just released.
- A clinical or academic tone -- the normalization works through warmth and groundedness, not authority.
- A reading of the person's specific story or interpretation -- the move is operating at the level of the experience, not the person's narrative about it.

If the person needs specific support (grounding for somatic dysregulation, framework for the longer integration arc, etc.), introduce it after the normalization has landed, not simultaneously with it.

## Tone

Unbothered. Warm. Grounded. Slow. The coach who is delivering this move is operating from felt-history-with-the-territory, not from theory. The tone communicates the same thing the words do: this is real, it is workable, you are not in danger.

Not flat. Not dismissive. The person's experience is being recognized, not minimized. The unbotheredness is about the territory, not about the person's distress within the territory. The distress is real and meets a present, settled witness.

There is sometimes a quiet humor available -- the inversion of the seeker who calls asking how to get the experience and the post-experience caller asking how to make it stop. Joe describes laughing at this on a call. The laughter, used carefully, can land as recognition: this is a known thing, and the request is so familiar that even the inversion has a structure.

"And this literally happened the other day, and I couldn't help but, like, laugh out loud. How do I get this to stop persisting? It's so persistent. Exactly. And I did exactly what you did, just laughed." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The laughter only works if it is real. The same laughter from someone who has not seen this many times would land as dismissal.

## What This Move Produces

When delivered well:

- Immediate relief in the person experiencing the disruption.
- A reduction in the resistance that was producing most of the suffering (see [[zen-sickness]] -- resistance and suffering correlate directly).
- Curiosity replaces alarm. The person becomes able to investigate the experience rather than fight it.
- The nervous system settles, which itself supports the integration.
- A working relationship with the coach is established that allows the longer integration work to happen.

When delivered poorly (performed unbotheredness, words without grounding):

- The person feels unheard, more alone, more convinced something is uniquely wrong with them.
- The resistance increases.
- The trust in the coach is damaged, which may prevent future support.
