# Show Me What Aggressive Looks Like in Your Mind

## The Move

When the person reports they are holding back because doing the thing would be "aggressive," "too much," "selfish," "inappropriate," or any other category-label that is treated as obvious, ask them to demonstrate what the forbidden version looks like. "Show me what aggressive looks like in your mind." The person produces a sample. The sample is almost always wildly tamer than the category-label would suggest. The disproportion is now in the room and can be worked with.

The structure is short:

1. Person uses a prohibitive label ("I would be aggressive," "I would be too much," "that would be selfish").
2. Coach asks for the demo: "Show me what aggressive looks like in your mind."
3. If the person describes it abstractly ("expressing anger"), push for the concrete: "No, I mean do it."
4. If the person hesitates with "it would be inappropriate," remove the stakes: "Everybody knows you're just acting it out. You're not actually — so just show me."
5. The person produces a sentence or motion.
6. Calibrate against the actual room. "This doesn't seem aggressive so far." Or: "I am definitely far more aggressive in this moment than you were in that moment."

The whole sequence takes one to two minutes. The work is done because the projection has been surfaced and contrasted with the actual room.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a person reports being unable to do something because the thing would be "aggressive," "too much," "selfish," "indulgent," "rude," or any category-label that is being treated as obvious.
- When the person's verbal restraint and the coach's read of the person's actual energy are wildly mismatched. The person is describing a wild animal they are containing; the coach sees a soft-spoken person describing the situation.
- Early in a session when the labeling is doing the work of preventing the actual exploration. The demo surfaces the labeling so the labeling can be questioned.
- When the person is asking how to do X without doing the labeled thing. The labeled thing usually does not exist as the person describes it. The demo shows this.
- When [[direct-felt-as-aggressive]] is the operating read.
- When the calibration error is so large that the person cannot specify what the labeled behavior would even look like in concrete form.

## What It Produces

**The person produces a tame sample.** "Why did you pick me for this. I can't believe you just picked me for this. I'm not ready for this. I can't handle this." The sample is direct expression of a difficult feeling. By no observer's standard is this aggressive. The person, however, has been treating this kind of speech as the forbidden zone.

**The disproportion becomes visible to the person.** Once the sample is in the room, the person can be asked to compare. The coach offers their own demeanor as the comparison: "I'm being more direct than you were just now. Do you experience me as aggressive?" The person almost always says no. The calibration starts to recompose in real time.

**The category-label becomes negotiable.** Before the demo, "aggressive" was a fixed boundary the person was operating inside. After the demo, "aggressive" is a category whose contents are now being examined. The person can no longer use the label as the unquestioned reason for holding back.

**The shape of the actual prohibition becomes visible.** Often what the person calls aggressive is "saying what I feel," "asking for what I want," "naming a frustration." The demo reveals that the prohibition is on direct expression itself, not on aggression. This reframes the work.

**Permission to do the demo lowers the prohibition's force in the body.** Even doing the hypothetical version once unsticks the body. The motion has been made. The body now has a reference for what it would be to do the thing. The next time the actual situation arises, the body has the shape available.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person produces something genuinely aggressive:** rare, but it happens. Treat it as the actual aggression it is. Do not pretend it is benign. The work then becomes "where is this aggression actually pointed and what is it about" — different work from the miscalibration work this move is for.

**If the person produces something tame and continues to insist it is aggressive:** the calibration error is severe. Do the contrast move directly. "Watch me. I'm going to say something more direct than what you just said." Demonstrate something obviously more direct. Then: "Did that feel aggressive?" The person almost always says no. The contrast does the recalibration that argument cannot.

**If the person describes it abstractly rather than performing it:** push for the concrete. "No, I mean do it. Say it. Move it." The abstract description preserves the prohibition. The concrete performance breaks it.

**If the person says "it would be inappropriate":** remove the stakes with the acting frame. "Everybody knows you're just acting it out. This isn't real. You're not actually — so just show me what in your mind you think that you never do." The acting frame gives permission. The body can produce a sample because the sample is not the thing.

**If the person smiles or laughs during the demo:** the smile is data. The body is recognizing that the imagined-prohibition is smaller than the suppression has been treating it as. Name it lightly: "I see a smile. The body knows this isn't as serious as you've been treating it." See [[the-smile-of-recognition]].

**If the demo lands and the person is ready to keep working:** move to the next step — usually inviting the person to do the actual expression they have been holding back, with the new calibration. "Now tell me what you actually want to say. Not the demo. The real thing." See [[claim-the-disowned-identity-aloud-ten-times]] for the related move that operates after this one has loosened the prohibition.

## Dialogue Example

The opening of the source session:

- **Person:** "How do I not hold back, and how do I express myself without feeling like I'm being aggressive? It feels to me like I'm being aggressive."
- **Joe:** "Show me what aggressive looks like in your mind."
- **Person:** "Uh, expressing anger."
- **Joe:** "No, I mean do it. Let me, let me, like, in your mind, what's aggressive? Um, this doesn't seem aggressive so far."
- **Person:** "It's, it's not. I'm stuck because I'm holding back what, uh, it feels inappropriate to let out. Aggressive?"
- **Joe:** "Well, everybody knows you're just acting it out, right? This isn't — you're not actually — so just show me what in your mind you think that you never do. Everybody knows you never do it. In your mind, what is the —"
- **Person:** "I mean, like saying something like, why did you pick me? I can't believe you just picked me for this. Like, I'm not ready for this. I can't handle this."
- **Joe:** "Gotcha."
- **Person:** "Something like that."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so I'm aggressive all the time."
- **Person:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "And when you look at me, when you look at me doing the thing that I'm doing right now, this is aggressive."
- **Person:** "That's not — that doesn't feel aggressive."
- **Joe:** "Okay, I, I am definitely far more aggressive in this moment than you were in that moment."
- **Person:** "Yes, it's, uh, I'd say you, you're being direct, and that is where — that's where I get sort of hung up. Being too direct feels, feels aggressive internally for me."

The structural details:

- Two refusals of the abstract ("expressing anger") before the concrete sentence arrives.
- The "everybody knows you're just acting it out" frame removes the stakes that were preventing the demo.
- The immediate calibration move ("I am definitely far more aggressive in this moment") converts the demo into a contrast.
- The person's correction — "you're being direct" — reveals the actual content of their category. "Aggressive" was their word for direct.

## Tone

Light. Curious. Slightly playful. The "this doesn't seem aggressive so far" is delivered with genuine puzzlement, not as a setup. The "everybody knows you're just acting it out" removes the stakes through the acting frame.

The move would not work if the coach treated the prohibition with reverence. If the coach said "I understand, this is hard, just try if you can," the prohibition would be reinforced. The lightness is the point: the prohibition is being treated as a thing whose contents can be examined, not as an obvious boundary that must be respected.

The coach's posture during the demo matters. Lean in. Watch. Be interested. The demo is data. The person is providing information about how their internal world represents the forbidden zone.

## Variations

**The selfish variant.** "Show me what selfish looks like in your mind." Same structure for the moral-category prohibition. The person describes what they think is selfish; the description is almost always tame; the demo surfaces the disproportion. Pairs with [[am-i-being-selfish]].

**The too-much variant.** "Show me what too-much looks like in your mind." For the person who reports being afraid of being "too much." Same structure. The sample is usually a request, a feeling expressed, a need named. None of it is actually too much.

**The mean variant.** "Show me what being mean looks like in your mind." For the person worried they would be cruel if they expressed honestly. The sample is usually direct honesty with one note of frustration. Not cruel.

**The inappropriate variant.** "Show me what inappropriate looks like in your mind." For the person worried about boundary violations in expression. The sample is usually a feeling named clearly, sometimes with a profanity. Almost never actually inappropriate.

In all variations the underlying move is the same: surface the projected version, contrast it with what would actually be appropriate or strong, and let the disproportion do the recalibration.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't argue with the prohibition before the demo.** Telling the person "that's not aggressive" without the demo just becomes a debate. The demo lets the person see for themselves.
- **Don't accept the abstract description.** "Expressing anger" is the prohibition speaking. Push for the concrete sample. The concrete sample is what the prohibition is actually protecting against.
- **Don't use the demo as proof.** The point is not to prove the person wrong. The point is to surface the projection so the person can examine it. Treating it as proof recreates the dynamic the prohibition was protecting against (someone being right at them).
- **Don't skip the contrast.** The demo alone is not the move. The contrast — "this is what you call aggressive; here is what I am doing right now" — is what makes the recalibration possible.
- **Don't move on too fast.** Let the person sit with the contrast. The recognition that their category has been doing this much work needs a beat.

## When Not to Use This Move

- When the person is in acute distress and the demo would land as more pressure. The move requires enough regulation to play the acting frame.
- When the prohibition is protecting against actual past abuse the person experienced. The "demo" can re-trigger if the prohibition is doing legitimate trauma work. In that case the work is different — see [[trauma-is-present-experienced-as-past]] and the work goes through the trauma frame, not the calibration frame.
- When the person's read of "aggressive" is accurate and they actually would do something harmful. In that case the prohibition is doing its job. Surfacing it and asking them to demo it could remove the brake on harm. Verify the calibration error before running this move.
- When the coach has not built enough trust for the playful frame to land. Early in a session, "everybody knows you're just acting it out" needs the person to trust that the coach is not setting them up.
