# Parallel the Client's Own Survival Skill to the Present Work

## The Move

When the person has a past survival situation they made it through — addiction recovery, leaving an abusive relationship, breaking free of a controlling family, surviving illness, getting out of a destructive job — ask them what specific move got them through. Then point out that the present work is the same move applied to a different domain.

The form, from the source session:

- **Joe:** "What did you have to do to actually get you through the addiction?"
- **Person:** "I refused to do anything that was harmful to me."
- **Joe:** "Great. So that's what's happening now. Holding back your truth is harmful for you."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The structure is three beats:

1. **Surface the past survival skill.** Ask what specific move got them through. The person names it themselves.
2. **Apply the move to the present.** State directly that the current work is the same move. The present-action is reframed as a familiar move rather than a novel difficulty.
3. **Make the cost of not doing it explicit.** Name what counts as self-harm in the present. The previous "no" to harm now has a present-tense target.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person has a known past survival situation (addiction recovery, abusive relationship recovery, illness recovery, recovery from a controlling family or cult, etc.) and the present work parallels the structural move that got them through.
- When the person is treating the present work as a novel difficulty they don't know how to handle, and the truth is they've handled the same structural move before.
- When the present work is held back by a sense that the person doesn't have the capacity for it — the parallel reveals they already have demonstrated capacity for exactly this kind of move.
- When the move that got them through their past survival situation is the same shape as the move that would resolve the present concern. Common parallels:
  - Refusing self-harm in addiction recovery → refusing self-suppression in speaking truth
  - Leaving an abusive relationship → leaving the internal abuser (inner critic, self-attack)
  - Breaking from a controlling family → breaking from the internalized parent voice
  - Recovering from illness → trusting the body's signals in present somatic work
  - Surviving violence → trusting one's gut sense in current situations
- When the past survival skill is something the person is proud of or recognizes as their own achievement. The pride/recognition is part of what makes the parallel work; the person already trusts the skill.

The move does NOT apply:

- When the past survival situation hasn't been integrated and bringing it up would re-traumatize. The move assumes the past survival has been worked through enough to be a resource.
- When the parallel is forced. Some past survival skills don't actually map to the present work. The move works because of structural similarity, not because Joe wants the person to feel powerful.
- When the person hasn't yet named the skill that got them through. The asking-them-to-name-it is part of the move; if Joe names it for them, the move loses its leverage.

## What It Produces

- **Recognition that the capacity is already there.** The person had been operating on the implicit frame that they need to develop a new capacity to do the present work. The parallel reveals the capacity has been operating since they made it through the past situation. The frame shift removes the novel-difficulty load.
- **A felt sense of home base.** The past survival skill has a felt signature — the person knows what it feels like in their body to refuse self-harm or to leave or to choose themselves. Naming the present work as the same move makes the felt signature available in the present.
- **The present-action becomes recognizable.** Before the parallel, "speaking truth" was an abstract instruction. After the parallel, "speaking truth is refusing self-harm" is a familiar move with a body-memory attached.
- **Self-trust expands across domains.** The person had been trusting themselves in the recovery domain but not in the present domain. The parallel transfers the trust. If they could trust themselves to refuse harm in the worst situation, they can trust themselves to refuse harm in this one.
- **The cost of not doing the work becomes visceral.** Naming the present as "holding back your truth is harmful for you" reframes the cost in the language the past-survival skill recognizes. The cost is no longer abstract ("you'll feel uncomfortable"). The cost is the language that already mobilizes the person to act ("this is harmful").

## What This Move Is NOT

- It is not [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]]. That move uses the childhood-installed pattern as the diagnostic question for the present. This move uses the adult survival-skill as the template for the present action. The two moves often work together — the childhood pattern explains why the present is hard, the survival skill provides the template for the move.

- It is not "you can do hard things because you've done hard things before." That framing is general motivation. This move is specific: the *same structural move* is being applied to a different domain. The specificity is what makes the move work; without it, the parallel becomes generic encouragement.

- It is not flattery. The move is not "look how strong you are." It's "the move you already know how to make is the same move you need now." The framing keeps the focus on the move, not on the person's identity.

- It is not [[the-fear-is-the-same-as-the-old-fear]] or [[apply-the-clients-stuck-place-to-understand-the-other]]. Those moves use the parallel for understanding (of the past, of the other person). This move uses the parallel for action (in the present).

## The Sequence

1. **The past survival situation is in the room.** Either the person has mentioned it, or it's known from prior context. The move requires the past situation to be accessible without being the focus of the session.

2. **The present work has been identified.** The person knows what they're trying to do — speak truth, set a boundary, choose themselves over the people-pleasing reflex, etc.

3. **The parallel is observed.** Joe notices the structural similarity between the past survival skill and the present work. This is the diagnostic — without the actual structural similarity, the move doesn't apply.

4. **The question surfaces the skill.** "What did you have to do to actually get through [the past situation]?" The person names the skill in their own words. This is the operative step. Joe doesn't tell them what got them through; they tell him.

5. **The parallel is named directly.** "Great. So that's what's happening now. [Naming the present action] is the same move." The naming is unambiguous. The two situations are explicitly being treated as the same structural move.

6. **The cost of not doing the move is made explicit.** The language the past-survival skill recognized (harm, self-harm, betrayal, danger) is applied to the present. This activates the move's underlying motivation.

7. **The person is invited to test it.** Often Joe will use the parallel as the basis for a present-tense experiment: do the move now, in the room. The parallel makes the move feel possible; the in-the-room experiment makes it real.

## Dialogue Example

The canonical exchange from the source session, after the woman has had her first truth-emergence and Joe wants to make the move feel like home base:

- **Joe:** "So just, you've, you've suffered the addiction. Is that correct?"
- **Person:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Great. So notice that there's a parallel happening. What did you have to do to actually get you through the addiction?"
- **Person:** "What did I do?"
- **Joe:** "After, after all you, all the bad advice you played with, blah, blah, blah, blah, blah. What was the actual movement that was like, whoo, that brought you here today?"
- **Person:** "I refused to do anything that was harmful to me."
- **Joe:** "Great. So that's what's happening now. Holding back your truth is harmful for you. It literally, it looked like you opened the tap and you came out when you started saying, I think there's stupid things that people tell you to blah, blah, blah, blah, blah. Like, it was like, boom, you were, you were fucking with us. All of a sudden I was with you. I wasn't with your mask. I wasn't with the trying. I wasn't with the trying not to try. You're just right there. You stopped doing harmful things to you in addiction. I assume like you popped out like the same way."
- **Person:** "Yeah, yeah, I, I did. Um, and it wasn't taken well by many people around me."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The dialogue shows the full structure. Joe surfaces the past survival situation, asks for the specific move that got her through, receives her own articulation of the move ("I refused to do anything that was harmful to me"), parallels it directly to the present ("so that's what's happening now"), names the present-action in the same language ("holding back your truth is harmful for you"), and confirms the body-evidence that just happened in the room (the tap opening was the same move she made in recovery).

The "it wasn't taken well by many people around me" response is also significant. The person, having recognized the parallel, immediately brings forward the side-effect she already knows from recovery: the same move that frees you sometimes upsets the people around you. The recognition is doing its work — she's now operating from inside the recovery-skill frame, and she's accessing what she already knows about that frame.

## Why It Works

The move works because it transfers a known capacity across domains. Most people compartmentalize their capacities — they have a skill they trust in one area of life and treat themselves as incapable in another, even when the underlying move is the same. The compartmentalization is usually unconscious; the person isn't holding two contradictory beliefs, they just haven't applied the skill across domains.

Naming the parallel surfaces the compartmentalization. Once the person sees that the present work is structurally the same move they already know, the capacity transfers. The move that felt impossible before now feels familiar.

The move also works because past-survival skills carry felt-body memory. The person knows in their body what it feels like to refuse harm, or to leave, or to choose themselves. Activating the felt memory makes the move available now — not as an idea, but as something the body remembers how to do.

There's a third mechanism: the past-survival skill has already had a victory. The person knows it works. When the present-action is named as the same move, it inherits the victory. The present-action stops feeling like a new bet and starts feeling like the move that has already produced freedom once.

The "harm" framing matters. The past-survival skill was activated by the recognition of harm. Applying the "harm" language to the present situation activates the same mobilization. "Holding back your truth is harmful for you" is not just a description; it's a trigger for the move that the body already knows how to make in response to harm.

## Tone

Direct, warm, slightly amazed. Joe is not selling the parallel; he's pointing it out. The amazement is at the fact that the person already has the move and didn't know. "Great. So that's what's happening now" — the great is genuine, the so-that's-what's-happening is the recognition delivered as a found-fact rather than a clever insight.

The naming of the present-as-harm ("holding back your truth is harmful for you") is delivered with weight. The weight matters because the past-survival skill responds to weight. A casual "your truth-holding is, you know, a little harmful" would not activate the same response. Joe gives the present-harm the same standing as the past-harm.

When the person confirms the parallel ("yeah, yeah, I did"), Joe extends the parallel by naming what's already happened in the room ("it looked like you opened the tap and you came out"). The extension keeps the parallel from being abstract — the present action has already occurred, the past skill has already shown up, the work is to keep going.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not name the parallel before asking the person to name the skill.** If Joe says "you survived addiction by refusing self-harm, and that's what you need to do now," the move loses leverage. The person's own articulation of their skill is the operative step. Joe's articulation, even if accurate, is just another instruction.

- **Do not force a parallel that isn't structurally there.** Some past survival skills don't map to the present work. Forcing the parallel produces a feel-good moment that doesn't translate into action. The move works only when the structural similarity is real.

- **Do not use the move as flattery.** "You're so strong, you got through that, you can do this too" is encouragement, not parallel. The move requires specificity: the *same move*, applied to a different domain. Without the specificity, the move loses its operational power.

- **Do not use the move when the past survival situation is unintegrated.** If the person hasn't worked through the past, bringing it up as a template can re-activate the trauma rather than activate the skill. The move requires the past to be accessible as a resource, not a wound.

- **Do not pile multiple parallels on top of each other.** One clean parallel is the move. Multiple parallels become a lecture. Trust the one parallel to do its work.

- **Do not move on too quickly.** The recognition that the parallel applies needs time to land in the body. Joe gives the person space after naming the parallel; the body-recognition is what makes the move stick.

## Variations

The same structure adapts across many past-survival domains:

- **For someone in recovery from an abusive relationship:** "What did you have to do to leave them? — I had to listen to my own gut over what they were telling me. — Right, so that's what's happening now. Listening to the inner critic instead of your gut is the same dynamic."

- **For someone who broke from a controlling family/cult/religion:** "What got you out? — I trusted what I saw with my own eyes. — Great. So that's what's happening now. The doubt that's keeping you in this pattern is the same doubt that wanted to keep you in the family."

- **For someone who recovered from a serious illness:** "What got you through? — I made my body the priority over what other people wanted from me. — Great. So that's what's happening now. The body's signals about this job/relationship are asking for the same priority."

- **For someone who survived violence:** "What kept you safe? — I trusted my gut. — Right. So in this current situation, your gut is telling you the same thing it told you then, and listening is the same move."

The structural test for whether the variant works: the specific named-move from the past must be the same move the present situation calls for. Without that, the parallel is rhetorical, not operational.
