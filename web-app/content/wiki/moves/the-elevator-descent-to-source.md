# The Elevator Descent to Source

## The Move

When the person has habitually ridden their attention up into the head — into thinking, figuring, analyzing, monitoring — the move is to direct their attention down. Past the head. Past the chest. All the way down. The destination is source: the place inside the person that is below cognition, below the meta-monitoring, below the figuring. The place the creating actually comes from.

The metaphor that organizes the move is an elevator. The person has been riding the elevator up to the top floor — what Joe calls the penthouse, the head. The instruction is to bring the elevator all the way back down. Not to the lobby. Not to the basement. To the place there is no further down to go.

The verbal forms:

- "Let's do a quick trick on that. Let's bring the elevator all the way down. All the way down. Nope, past there. All the way down. Don't try to figure it out, just feel."
- "All the way down. How do you not create art from here?"
- "Yeah, go all the way down. Look at it. Oh, look at it."

> "The elevator just keeps going to the penthouse. Gotta get rid of that button."
>
> "Yeah, so let's do a, a quick trick on that. So what's really sweet about you is that you see that, but, but let's get — let's bring the elevator all the way down. All the way down. Nope, past there. All the way down. Don't try to figure it out, just feel. All the way down. Look at that. Immediately you went to that place where you make art." -- (Source: 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled)

The client herself reaches for the metaphor first ("the elevator just keeps going to the penthouse"). Joe takes the metaphor and uses it as the access. The elevator was hers; the direction of the descent is his addition.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person has demonstrated they live in the head and the work requires the body. The artist whose art happens in flow but who reports being "always in my head" — the structural mismatch is the diagnostic.
- When the person has had the recognition (the conversation has surfaced the pattern) and now needs an experiential anchor that is not cognitive. The descent provides the somatic landing that the recognition needs to integrate.
- When the person reaches up — into figuring out, into asking how, into reaching for a technique — and the reach itself is the obstruction. The descent is the directional opposite of the reach.
- When the person uses an elevator metaphor or any vertical metaphor about their own attention. The metaphor is already there; the move uses what is available.
- When the person is creative or knowledge-work-oriented and has built a sophisticated head that runs the show, and the work being asked of them requires somewhere the head cannot get to.
- When the person says "I leave my body" about the flow state and is currently above their body in the conversation. The descent re-enters the body from a new direction: not upward through trance, but downward through felt sensation.

The move is most appropriate when the person is already articulate, head-dominant, and able to track abstract instruction. It is less useful for someone who is already dropped in and would only be re-dissociated by being directed somewhere. The diagnostic: is the person currently above where they need to be? If yes, the descent is available. If they are already below, the move would scatter them.

## What It Produces

A felt drop. When the descent lands, the person's face changes. The eyes soften. The shoulders drop. The breath deepens. Joe will often see the change before the person reports it: "Immediately you went to that place where you make art."

Common responses:

- **Immediate arrival.** The person descends and finds themselves somewhere they recognize. The artist whose creating happens in flow descends and finds the place creating happens. "It's the source."
- **An interrupted descent.** The person starts to drop and the head catches them on the way down. See [[interrupting-your-own-descent]]. The descent has to be redone several times. Each redoing teaches the body the direction.
- **A new question.** "Is there a trick?" The trick question is the head reaching back into control. See [[insight-converted-to-task-in-real-time]]. The descent was the trick; calling it a trick is the head trying to install it as a technique to manage with.
- **A reach to hold on.** The person finds the descent and immediately tries to grip it. "Don't try to hold on to this thing. Art isn't something you can hold on to. You're trying to hold on." The holding-on is the head returning. The release of the grip is the second descent.

## What Joe Does Next

The move is rarely done once. The pattern of the head reaching back is durable, so the descent is repeated.

- **If the person arrives:** stay there. Have them turn from the source toward the work. "Do me a favor, turn — from keeping that consciousness, turn to the piece of art wherever it is that has been the longest sitting there." The descent provides the connection; turning to the work uses it. The art the person has been unable to enter becomes available from below.
- **If the person starts the descent and then surfaces with an answer:** see [[interrupting-your-own-descent]]. Name what just happened. "You sank then you spoke." Direct them down again. The pattern of the answer-interrupting-the-descent is itself information.
- **If the person reaches for a technique:** refuse the technique frame. "I mean, how is that not a trick that you just learned?" The technique-frame is the head's way of installing the experience as a thing to do, which is the opposite of the experience itself.
- **If the person tries to hold on:** name the grip. "Don't try to hold on to this thing. Art isn't something you can hold on to. You're trying to hold on." The grip is the head's last attempt at control. The release is the deepening of the descent.
- **If the person sees and recognizes:** "Yeah, there you go, there it is again." The naming reinforces what just happened so the person can recognize it on their own next time.

## The Recognition vs. Holding Distinction

A crucial sub-move within the descent. Once the person finds source, they have two options: recognize that source is always there, or try to grip the felt-state. The first is sustainable. The second is the same pattern that has been preventing the work for years.

> "Your movement is to try to get out of here instead of to go there. And there is always there, so you don't even have to go. You just have to recognize. So let's do that again. Recognize there." -- (Source: 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled)

Source is not a state to be achieved and held. It is a place that is always present, which only becomes available when the head stops blocking the descent. The work is not to maintain the descended state. The work is to notice when the head is blocking and to drop.

The "hold on" reflex is the misreading. The person interprets the felt arrival as a state they got to and now have to keep. The interpretation produces the grip, which produces the loss of the state, which produces the reach to regain it. The cycle is the head's way of converting an experience into a project. Joe refuses the project frame: "Art isn't something you can hold on to."

## Why It Works

The mechanism is structural. The person who lives in the head has built a sophisticated upstairs that runs the show. The downstairs (body, feeling, source) is intact but rarely visited. The block on the creative work is downstream of this: the work happens downstairs, and the person has been trying to do it from upstairs for years.

The elevator metaphor uses the person's own spatial intuition about their attention. Up is the head. Down is the body. The instruction "all the way down" is precise: not "into your body" (which is abstract) or "be present" (which is vague), but a specific directional vector the person can follow with their attention.

The "past the penthouse" specification matters. The person who tries to descend often stops at a middle floor — the chest, the heart, the upper belly. The middle floor is still the head's territory, just slightly lower. "All the way down, past there, all the way down" pushes through the middle floors to the place there is no further to go. That place is source.

The "don't try to figure it out, just feel" addendum cuts off the head's last route. The head would try to figure out how to descend, which is itself the riding up. The instruction substitutes feeling for figuring. Feeling is the operation that happens in the body; figuring is the operation that happens in the head. Telling the person which operation to perform routes them around the pattern.

## What This Move Is Not

- Not dissociation. The descent is not leaving the body; it is going deeper into it. The artist who has been "leaving her body" in flow may be confused by the instruction at first — she has only known the body-exit, not the body-deepening. The clarification: descend is downward into sensation, not upward into trance.
- Not relaxation. The descent is not "calm down" or "take a breath." It is a specific reorientation of attention to a specific place. A person can be relaxed and still in the penthouse; a person can be tense and still descended. The relevant variable is the location of attention, not the level of arousal.
- Not a one-time intervention. The descent has to be made repeatedly because the head reaches back. The work over time is to make the descent more available, not to achieve a permanently descended state.
- Not specific to creativity. The move can be used whenever the person is head-dominant about a work that requires the body. The artist case is the canonical use because the mismatch is most visible there, but the same move applies to anyone whose work requires somewhere the head cannot get to.

## Tone

Direct, gentle, paced. Joe slows down for the descent. "All the way down. Nope, past there. All the way down. Don't try to figure it out, just feel." The instructions are short and stacked. The pauses between them give the person time to actually follow the direction.

The "nope, past there" is corrective without being critical. The person stopped at a middle floor; Joe names that the destination is further. The "nope" is light; it is not a rebuke.

When the person arrives, Joe matches the descended state. "Yeah, there you go, there it is again." The matching is what allows the descent to land somatically — the coach descended with the person and is reporting from there.

## Dialogue Example

- **Client:** "I would like that, actually. It's time. I, I like to get lost, actually. Yeah, yeah, I'm in there. The elevator just keeps going to the penthouse. Gotta get rid of that button."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so let's do a, a quick trick on that. So what's really sweet about you is that you see that, but, but let's get — let's bring the elevator all the way down. All the way down. Nope, past there. All the way down. Don't try to figure it out, just feel. All the way down. Look at that. Immediately you went to that place where you make art."
- **Client:** "It's the source."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Your movement is to try to get out of here instead of to go there. And there is always there, so you don't even have to go. You just have to recognize. So let's do that again. Recognize there. Ah, fuck, don't think. Just—"
- **Client:** "I— Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Okay, great. And then all the way down. How do you not create art from here?"
- *(Source: 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled)*

The exchange takes under two minutes. The move is repeated twice in this short space, with the second repetition reinforcing the first. The "how do you not create art from here?" follow-up uses the descended state as the new platform: from source, the creating is no longer blocked.
