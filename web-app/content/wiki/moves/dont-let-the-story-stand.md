# Don't Let the Story Stand

## The Move

When the client offers a self-diagnosis as the explanation for their problem — "I'm scared of success and failure," "I'm a procrastinator," "I'm a perfectionist" — the move is to refuse to accept the self-diagnosis as the working premise of the session. The story is the thing that has been keeping the person stuck. Treating the story as data to be worked with reinforces the trap. Treating the story as a thing to be deconstructed opens the work.

The client believes the story. The story has been told to themselves dozens of times a day for decades. Believing it has not produced freedom. What would make the next iteration of believing it produce freedom? Nothing would. The coach's job is to not collude with the story.

> "So that's his story. I'm not gonna let that story particularly stand because he's been telling himself he's scared. Most likely he's been scared of success and failure for a long time. Like that's something that he read somewhere or saw something and maybe he's even worked through it a couple of times. I'm not gonna let that story stand because again, the story is the thing that's keeping him trapped in the situation. Definitely want to deconstruct it." -- Joe Hudson, Overcoming Procrastination (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The premise of the move: the consciousness that got you into the problem is not the consciousness that's going to get you out of the problem. If the person is offering a familiar self-description, the familiar self-description is built from the same consciousness as the problem. It cannot deliver the exit.

## What "Letting the Story Stand" Looks Like (and Why It Fails)

A coach lets the story stand when they:

- Take the self-diagnosis as the working frame ("okay, so you're afraid of success and failure — let's explore that")
- Build the session around the language the client brought ("what does success mean to you?")
- Validate the framing by inquiring inside it ("when did you first notice this fear?")
- Offer interventions designed to address the named problem ("here's a tool for the fear-of-success")

Each move feels like good coaching. Each move is letting the story stand. The session becomes an inquiry inside the cage. The cage is the story; the inquiry is being conducted by the cage; the cage will not open from within.

The failure mode: the client leaves the session having explored their fear-of-success more thoroughly than they had before, with no actual change in the procrastination. The story has been polished. The trap has been deepened.

## How the Move Operates

The mechanics are usually one or both of:

**1. Refute the story with obvious counter-evidence.** The story claims a generality the data doesn't support. The person says they're a procrastinator. They are not procrastinating on 98% of things in their life. The person says they're scared of being overwhelmed. They are overwhelming themselves all the time through the procrastination. The contradiction the body cannot ignore is the lever. See [[you-procrastinate-on-2-of-100-things]] and [[bound-the-behavior-find-where-it-doesnt-happen]] for the specific forms this takes.

**2. Decline the framing offered by the language.** When the client says "I oscillate between fear of success and failure," the coach does not pick up that frame. Instead, the coach goes underneath it — asks what actually happens, what specifically, when, with what — and stays at the structural level the story has obscured. The story names a feeling; the coach asks for the situation. The story names an identity; the coach asks for the behavior. The story names a duality (success/failure); the coach asks where the procrastination actually happens and where it doesn't.

The first form is more confrontational. The second is more lateral. Both produce the same effect: the story stops being the operating premise of the conversation.

## The Diagnostic Sign That the Move Is Landing

When the story has been pierced, the client's response is often a brief inability to make sense of what was said. The brain that has been running the story for years cannot immediately process the contradiction. The person says "wait, what?" or goes silent or laughs at the absurdity. This is the [[the-404-the-productive-unknown]] arriving at micro-scale, in real time, in the middle of a sentence.

> "What I said was entirely logical and entirely obvious. He said, I'm scared of success because it'll overwhelm me. How much does your procrastination overwhelm you? It overwhelms me a lot. So you're not scared of overwhelm. You're doing it to yourself all the time. But his whole brain just went, what the— like, he couldn't make sense of it. That's where I know I'm starting to pierce the story. Because the story is so embedded, and I'm like, when his brain can't see that obvious thing right away, he will in a second see it, but when he can't see it right away, that's my knowing, oh, he's broken through the story. Like, the story is now no longer at like this solid fortress of stuckness. It is now starting to crack open." -- Joe Hudson, Overcoming Procrastination (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The brief blankness is the marker. The story is no longer a solid fortress. Something has moved. The next moves can land on different ground than before.

## The Watch-For After the Move Lands

Once the story has been pierced, the brain looks for the next story. The first story is gone; the system requires a story to function; a new one will be manufactured. See [[brain-manufactures-next-problem-to-stay-on-map]].

In the breakdown session, the client moves from "fear of success and failure" to "perfectionism is also at play" in the next few exchanges. The move from one story to another looks like progress. It is the same trap with a different label.

The coach who does not see this lets the second story stand. The coach who sees it refuses the second story the same way they refused the first. The deconstruction has to operate on whatever story the system manufactures, not just the story the person walked in with.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not dismissing the client's experience. The feelings the story names are real. The fear-of-success and fear-of-failure are real felt states. The procrastination is real behavior. The refusal is not of the experience but of the story-frame the person has wrapped around the experience.

It is not arguing with the client. The move is structural, not adversarial. The coach is not trying to convince the client they are wrong about themselves. The coach is showing them a contradiction in the story and letting the contradiction do the work.

It is not the same as [[dont-presume-the-person-is-broken]], though they are siblings. That anti-pattern is about declining to coach from the premise that the person needs fixing. This move is the operational form: even when the client offers the broken-self framing, the coach refuses to organize the session around it.

It is not [[deconstruct-the-victim-to-release-anger]], though it shares a lineage. That move surfaces the victim posture to free the anger; this move refuses the self-diagnostic frame to free the work. The two can show up in the same session.

## When to Use It

- When the client opens with a familiar, rehearsed self-diagnosis ("I'm a perfectionist," "I'm anxious," "I'm a procrastinator")
- When the language is generality-talk rather than specifics ("I always," "I never," "this is who I am")
- When the story has the smooth quality of having been told many times
- When the diagnostic question "where don't you have this problem?" would produce immediate evidence that contradicts the generality
- When the felt sense in the room is that the story is the trap, not the symptom

The move is less useful when the person is in active overwhelm, when the felt material is so close to the surface that any cognitive intervention would short-circuit it, or when the story is actually accurate at the structural level the person is naming. Calibrate to the room.

## What Joe Does Next

After the story has been pierced and the brief 404 has arrived, the next move is usually some version of: ask what the actual situation is. Bound the behavior ([[bound-the-behavior-find-where-it-doesnt-happen]]). Get into specifics. Find what the story was obscuring. The deconstruction opens the territory; the next moves explore it.

When the brain manufactures the next story, repeat the move. The work is iterative. Each story is a layer; each piercing produces a small 404; each 404 makes the next layer accessible. The session moves through the layers until the felt material underneath the stories becomes available.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I oscillate between fear of success and failure."
- **Joe:** [internally: I'm not gonna let that story stand. He's been telling it for decades. Believing it hasn't produced freedom. Why would another round of believing it produce freedom?]
- **Joe:** [aloud] "The fear of success is that I'll become overwhelmed."
- **Person:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "How overwhelmed are you with the procrastination?"
- **Person:** "Considerably so."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so it's not overwhelm you're scared of, you're doing it to yourself all the time."
- **Person:** "How do you mean?"
- *(The brief inability to process is the story cracking. — Source: Overcoming Procrastination Breakdown)*

The whole sequence takes under thirty seconds. The story is named, the contradiction is surfaced, the contradiction lands as an inability-to-process. The story is no longer the fortress it was.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, not confrontational. The move sounds confrontational in transcript form but is delivered as logical observation. The coach is not attacking the client; the coach is pointing out that the math doesn't add up. The warmth comes from underneath — this is being done because the client is stuck, and the story is the cage.
