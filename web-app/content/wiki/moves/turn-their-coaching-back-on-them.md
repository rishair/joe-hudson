# Turn Their Coaching Back On Them

## The Move

When a client who is a coach, teacher, parent, leader, or trainer hedges or qualifies in a way they would never accept from someone they work with, ask them what they would say to that person. The client has to deliver the move themselves. The client knows the right answer; the question forces the answer to be spoken to themselves rather than received from Joe.

The form: a question that names the role-reversal directly.

- "If you had somebody say to you when you're teaching them — you teach tennis, is that right? — and they were like, 'oh yeah, I can do that, I guess.' What would you say to them?"

The client answers with their professional voice. The professional voice contains the teaching the client needs at that moment. The teaching that came out of the client's mouth is harder to deflect than the same teaching offered by Joe.

The move is a specific application of [[skill-transfer-redirect]] applied not to a skill the client teaches but to a teaching move the client makes. The structural form is the same: the client demonstrably has the capacity in another context, so the inability claim in this context cannot stand.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the client is a coach, teacher, parent, leader, or trainer and is hedging in a way their professional role would not allow.
- When the client uses a qualifier ("I guess," "kind of," "I think maybe") at the moment the work has just landed and the hedge is being used to soften it back into nothing.
- When the client has just demonstrated a capacity and is then disclaiming the capacity.
- After [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] has established the transfer and the client's response is "I can do that, I guess."
- When the client teaches something professionally that would address the exact hedge they just produced.
- When direct argument with the hedge would put Joe in an authority position; the move keeps the authority on the client.

The move requires the client to have a professional role of coaching, teaching, or directing others. Without that role, there is no "what would you say to them" to ask.

## What It Produces

**The teaching comes out of the client's mouth.** Whatever Joe could have said about not hedging, the client says better. The client has been delivering that teaching to students for years. The professional voice is more authoritative for this client than any external voice could be.

**The hedge dissolves in the speaking.** Once the client has said "Don't guess, just do it," the hedge they just made ("I can do that, I guess") is incompatible with their own teaching. The hedge cannot survive the contradiction.

**A small flash of recognition or laughter.** The role-reverse is usually visible to the client as Joe is asking it. They see the trap before they answer. The answering still happens because the question has been asked, but the client also sees themselves being caught by their own teaching. The seeing is part of the work.

**A reduction in the gap between what the client teaches and what they apply to themselves.** The move surfaces the gap and closes it in a single exchange. The client cannot continue holding the hedge as a private allowance once they have publicly stated that they would not accept the hedge from a student.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Catch the hedge.** The client uses a qualifier at the exact moment the work has landed or is about to land. "I can do that, I guess." "Yeah, kind of." "I think I see it."

2. **Confirm the professional role.** "If you had somebody say to you when you're teaching them — you teach tennis, is that right? Part of it is you teach? Yeah." The confirmation is brief; the role just needs to be on the table.

3. **Set up the mirror.** "And they were like, 'oh yeah, I can do that, I guess.' What would you say to them?" The exact words of the client's hedge are repeated back inside the scenario. The mirror is precise.

4. **Wait for the client's answer.** The client almost always starts answering the surface question first ("great, great"), then gets pulled to the hedge. Joe redirects to the hedge specifically: "And what would you say to the 'I guess' part?"

5. **Let the answer land.** The client delivers the teaching. "Don't guess, just do it. If you feel like you can do it, go do it. That's it. Like, you can't guess." Joe receives the answer with a short affirmation. "Okay. All right. It's literally just like tennis. Okay."

6. **Bridge back to the work.** The client's professional teaching has just been spoken into the room. The next move uses that teaching as the active frame. The client has delivered the instruction; the instruction now applies to them.

## Why It Works

The move works because the client is the most credible authority on the topic the client teaches. Whatever Joe could say about not hedging, about committing to the action, about trusting the body's knowing — the client has been saying versions of those things to students for years. The professional voice carries more weight for this client than Joe's voice does.

The move also works because the role-reverse is short-circuiting a defense. The hedge is doing protective work: it allows the client to acknowledge the recognition without fully committing to it, which keeps the identity "I'm someone who can't do this" intact in case the commitment fails. The role-reverse asks the client to step out of the identity for thirty seconds and into the role of the teacher who is talking to someone holding that identity. From the teacher's seat, the hedge looks different. It looks like exactly what the teacher would push past in a student.

The move is also a recognition-amplifier. The client has already had the recognition (that is what the hedge is hedging). The role-reverse holds the recognition in place by surrounding it with the client's own professional commitment to its truth. The recognition becomes harder to discount.

## Dialogue Example

The guest was a tennis coach who had just briefly demonstrated being in the flow state during conversation and then hedged.

- **Joe:** "Yeah, it's that easy. It's just you're playing tennis in life."
- **Guest:** "I can do that, I guess."
- **Joe:** "If you had somebody say to you when you're teaching them — you teach tennis, is that right? Part of it is you teach? Yeah. And they were like, 'oh yeah, I can do that, I guess.' What would you say to them?"
- **Guest:** "Great, great. Well, the I guess part, like, if they said I could do it, great."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And what would you say to the I guess part?"
- **Guest:** "Don't guess, just do it. If you feel like you can do it, go do it. That's it. Like, you can't guess."
- **Joe:** "Okay. All right. It's literally just like tennis. Okay."
- **Guest:** "Play tennis in life. All right, that sounds fun to me."

*(Source: How Do I Feel My Body?)*

The exchange takes under thirty seconds. The guest answers the surface question first ("if they said I could do it, great") and Joe immediately redirects to the hedge specifically ("what would you say to the I guess part?"). The redirect prevents the move from being deflected. The guest then delivers the full teaching to himself: "Don't guess, just do it. If you feel like you can do it, go do it. That's it. Like, you can't guess." Joe's "okay, all right" is enough — the teaching has been delivered, and the session can close with the guest's own framing carrying him out: "Play tennis in life. All right, that sounds fun to me."

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the client answers the surface question without addressing the hedge:** name the hedge specifically. "What would you say to the I guess part?" The redirect is precise — the client cannot answer about the surface and skip the qualifier.
- **If the client gives a short version of the teaching:** receive it briefly and bridge to the present. "Yeah. It's literally just like tennis." The bridge maps the client's teaching onto the current situation.
- **If the client deflects the role-reverse ("well, I wouldn't say that to a student because..."):** the deflection is the work. The client is now defending the hedge against their own professional standards. Often the next question is whether the defense holds up: "Why would you treat yourself differently than you'd treat them?" See [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] for the related move.
- **If the client laughs at the trap:** good. The laughter is the recognition arriving. Let it land. Move forward.

## Tone

Curious, slightly playful. The setup question is delivered casually — Joe is not setting up a confrontation. The professional reference ("you teach tennis, is that right?") is genuinely confirming a fact, not building a trap. The trap is visible to anyone who looks at the structure, but the delivery is light enough that the client does not feel cornered.

The redirect ("what would you say to the I guess part?") is the moment of small precision. The tone shifts slightly — not harder, just more specific. The client cannot answer around the hedge anymore. But the precision is gentle. There is no edge in Joe's voice.

The receiving of the client's answer is short and warm. "Okay. All right. It's literally just like tennis." No commentary on how the client just coached themselves. The teaching has been delivered; commentary would draw attention to the move and weaken it.

## Variations Across Roles

- **For a coach who hedges about applying their own coaching:** "If a client said that to you, what would you say?"
- **For a therapist who minimizes their own feelings:** "If a client described what you just described, what would you reflect back?"
- **For a parent who second-guesses a decision:** "What would you tell your kid if they second-guessed themselves that way?"
- **For a leader who softens what they actually mean:** "If someone on your team gave you that answer, what would you do?"
- **For a teacher who can't commit to a position:** "If a student handed you that as their answer, what would you tell them?"

The structural form is identical: name the role, reflect the hedge into the scenario, ask what the client would say. The client delivers the teaching.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A client who is in a teaching, coaching, parenting, or leadership role.
- A hedge that arrives at the moment of recognition — a qualifier that softens the recognition back into deniability.
- After [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]] when the client says "I can do that, I guess" rather than "yes, I can do that."
- After [[skill-transfer-redirect]] when the client acknowledges the skill transfer but qualifies the acknowledgment.
- When the client has just demonstrated a capacity and is then qualifying the capacity.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward closure of the session — the client's own teaching often provides the natural ending frame.
- Toward [[skill-transfer-redirect]] for the broader principle the move is operating within.
- Toward [[stick-a-fucking-shit-in-front-of-the-should]] if the qualifier returns later in the session in a different form.
- Toward [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] if the client tries to defend the gap between what they teach and what they apply to themselves.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not explain the move.** "I'm using your own teaching against you" or "you would never accept that from a student" undoes the move. The client has to deliver the teaching themselves. Explanation puts Joe back in the authority seat.
- **Do not extend the role-reverse beyond the hedge.** The move is short and surgical. A long conversation about how the client treats themselves differently than students is a different move and tends to land as therapy rather than as a redirect. Use the move for the hedge; use other moves for the broader gap.
- **Do not use the move on a client who does not have a teaching or coaching role.** The "what would you say to a student" question requires there to be a student. Without one, the move has no structural footing.
- **Do not respond to the client's teaching with admiration.** "That's a great teaching" makes the move about the quality of the teaching rather than about its application to the client right now. A short receiving ("yeah") is enough.
