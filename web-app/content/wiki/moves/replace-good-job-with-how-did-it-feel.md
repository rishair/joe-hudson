# Replace "Good Job" With "How Did That Feel?"

## The Move

When a child does something -- finishes a painting, solves a problem, plays a game, performs a chore, or even engages in something the parent finds problematic (doom-scrolling, throwing a tantrum) -- the standard parental response is some version of "good job" / "bad job" / "you did well" / "you did poorly." This response trains the child to evaluate their actions against external metrics for external approval.

The alternative move: ask the child about their own experience of doing the thing. "How did it feel to paint that painting?" "What was that like for you?" "What's going on in your system right now?" "What makes you want to feel that way?" The questions redirect the child's attention from "did I do well by the metric" to "what was my experience of this."

"If we're working with our kids, especially when they were younger and they did a painting, we weren't like, 'Good job!' We said, 'Oh, how did that feel to paint that painting?' So that they could reflect, so they could see their own experience and relate to their own experience. If they were doom-scrolling, it'd be like, 'Hey, how do you feel doom-scrolling? Like, what's going on in your system? What does that feel like?' What makes you want to feel that way? It wasn't bad job, right?" -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

## What This Produces in the Child

The child trained with this move develops several capacities that the good-jobbed child does not:

- **Internal evaluation.** The child learns that their own experience is information worth attending to. They develop the habit of asking themselves "how did that feel?" -- which becomes the foundation of internal compass as an adult.
- **Worth not conditional on output.** Love and attention from the parent does not arrive contingent on producing approved outputs. The child can relax into being-loved-for-existing because the love is not the prize for performance.
- **Self-reflection as default.** Asking "how did that feel?" becomes habitual. The child can be in their own experience without needing to leave themselves to track external grading.
- **Curiosity about own behavior.** Even uncomfortable behaviors (doom-scrolling, lashing out) become objects of curiosity rather than shame. "What makes me want to feel that way?" is a structurally different question from "why am I bad?"

The result: the adult version of this child does not develop imposter syndrome at the rates the good-jobbed adult does. They have an internal compass that does not require performance metrics to function. When they enter performance-graded domains, the not-knowing feels neutral rather than threatening. The conditional-worth structure that produces imposter syndrome was never installed.

See [[good-jobbed-as-conditional-validation]] for the alternative installation this move interrupts.

## What This Is Not

The move is not "withhold praise to make the child more self-sufficient." That would be a different move with a different mechanism. Praise can still happen; the structural shift is from "good job" (which grades the output against an external metric) to engagement with the child's own experience of what they did.

The move is also not "be neutral about everything." If the child does something that genuinely lands as delightful or moving for the parent, the parent's authentic response is welcome. The structural problem is the conditional-validation pattern of praise-as-love-currency, not the parent's authentic appreciation. Authentic appreciation is part of the relationship; conditional validation is a control structure.

The move is also not specifically about praise. It applies just as much to discipline. "Bad job" with the same conditional structure produces the same installation in reverse. The redirect to "what's going on for you?" works in both directions -- it does not optimize for approval-seeking and it does not optimize for shame-avoidance. It optimizes for the child's relationship to their own experience.

## When To Use It

- For any age child, with age-appropriate language.
- After a child completes a task or activity, instead of "good job" or "well done."
- When a child is engaging in a behavior the parent might be tempted to criticize (doom-scrolling, tantrums, withdrawal).
- After a child performs in a graded context (a test, a game, a recital) -- the question redirects from the external grade to the internal experience.
- When a child reports something that happened, instead of immediately evaluating ("that was good / bad") or solving.

The move generalizes well. Once installed as a habit, it becomes the parent's default response to most situations, and the child internalizes the pattern.

## How To Use It

The question has to be asked with genuine curiosity, not as a technique. If the child senses that the parent is asking "how did that feel?" as the new approved-response that they are supposed to give a specific answer to, the move collapses back into the conditional-worth structure with new wrapping. The question is sincere; the child's answer is genuinely interesting to the parent; whatever the child reports is welcome.

A useful sub-move: when the child gives an evaluative answer ("it was good" / "it was bad"), ask one more level. "What was good about it?" "What made it feel good?" The follow-up redirects from the evaluation back to the experience, training the child to notice what was actually happening for them rather than producing a verdict.

If the parent does not have access to their own internal experience, this move is hard to do authentically. The parent's own curiosity about experience has to be there for the question to land as a genuine question. The parent's repair work on their own conditional-worth installation often has to precede or accompany the move with their children.

## When This Is the Move in Coaching

The coach reaches for this move with parents who:

- Are working on their own imposter syndrome and want to interrupt the cycle for their kids
- Are noticing themselves "good jobbing" their kids and want an alternative
- Have absorbed the [[good-jobbed-as-conditional-validation]] pattern and want the practical action
- Are asking how to raise children who do not develop conditional-worth structures
- Are working with [[parenting-as-the-most-effective-change-agent]] as a frame

The move is also useful as a self-coaching practice for the adult who was good-jobbed. The adult can apply the same question to themselves -- after any activity, ask "how did that feel for me?" instead of asking "did I do that well?" The practice gradually rewires the conditional-worth structure that the good-jobbing installed.

## Connection to Internal Compass Work

This move is the parenting version of much of the deeper work Joe does with adults: relocate evaluation from external metrics to internal experience. The adult version is harder because the conditional-worth structure has been operating for decades. The child version is structurally easier because the alternative is being installed in real time without having to undo a prior installation.

The intergenerational leverage is high. A parent who interrupts the cycle for their child changes the adult that child becomes. The compounded effect over a generation is the [[parenting-as-the-most-effective-change-agent]] dynamic. The single move "how did that feel?" instead of "good job" is one of the highest-leverage interventions available to a parent who wants the change.

## Tone

When demonstrated, Joe says this almost lightly. The move is small. The parent does not need to make a production of it. The smallness is part of why it works -- the child does not register a behavioral change in the parent's responses; they just notice that the parent is genuinely interested in what was happening for them.

The coach also delivers this move lightly to the parent client. The instinct is to make the move into a parenting technique to be applied perfectly. The technique-frame collapses the move back into the conditional-worth structure (the parent is now trying to perform the "good parent" version, the child senses the performance, the relationship suffers). The lightness preserves the structural shift that makes the move work.
