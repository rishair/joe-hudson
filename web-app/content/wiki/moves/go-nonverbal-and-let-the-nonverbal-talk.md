# Go Nonverbal and Let the Nonverbal Talk

## The Move

When the verbal-answer access keeps reintroducing control after a truth-question is asked — the question opens something, the answering closes it again — remove the verbal requirement. Ask the truth-question again, but tell the person they don't have to say anything in response. Let the felt answer be the answer.

"So go nonverbal for a minute and let the nonverbal talk. I know that's weird to say, but like, I'm going to say, what's your truth right now in this moment? And don't even think that you have to say anything. So what's your truth in this moment?" -- Joe Hudson, What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?

The structure is three beats:

1. **Name what's being asked.** "Go nonverbal for a minute and let the nonverbal talk." The framing primes the person to receive the question differently than they have been.
2. **Acknowledge the strangeness.** "I know that's weird to say." This pre-empts the person's confusion about how to answer, and removes the requirement to figure-it-out before doing it.
3. **Ask the truth-question with no verbal requirement.** "Don't even think that you have to say anything. So what's your truth in this moment?" The question is the same. What's removed is the obligation to answer in words.

The move reveals that the verbal access has been the channel through which control was re-engaging. Removing the verbal access removes the channel. The truth, when it arrives, arrives in the body — visibly, often dramatically — without the person having to organize words around it.

## When Joe Uses It

- After [[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]] has been asked several times and the [[the-control-after-the-truth]] pattern is running consistently. Each verbal answer is showing the same dynamic: an opening, then a re-control as the words form.
- When the person can feel the opening when the question is asked but consistently loses it in the speaking.
- When the verbal-answering has been observed to be the location where the management-mode lives. The talking is where the control is. The non-talking is where the truth is.
- When the work needs to access truth that hasn't yet been verbalized and may not yet be verbalizable. The nonverbal channel can deliver material the verbal channel cannot.
- When the person reports a felt sense that doesn't have words yet. The instruction to go nonverbal honors the felt sense as a valid answer in its own form.
- When the person is heavily verbally-oriented (intellectual, articulate, prone to managing through speech) and the work needs to drop below the verbal apparatus.

The move does NOT apply:

- When the verbal channel is producing truth and the answers are landing. If the verbal access is working, there's no reason to remove it.
- When the person needs the verbal articulation for the work to do its job. Some material needs to be spoken to be metabolized; nonverbal-only access leaves it inaccessible.
- When the person has not yet had any truth-emergences in the session. The move presumes the verbal channel has been tested and is showing the control-pattern; without that test, the move is a non-sequitur.

## What It Produces

- **An immediate access to the body without the management apparatus engaged.** The verbal apparatus had been doing double duty — producing answers and managing how the answers landed. Removing the verbal requirement removes the management. The body can deliver its truth without the management filter.

- **Visible body-truth.** The person doesn't speak but the body speaks. Joe in the source session could read the answer from across the conversation: "so much power you are holding back. Holy fuck, you must have been just like this willful, powerful little girl." The truth was in the body — in the posture, the eyes, the energy. The coach can name what the body is delivering.

- **Surprise at what comes through.** The person often discovers a felt-truth they didn't know they had. The nonverbal channel doesn't filter through the head's edits, so what arrives is sometimes startling — power, anger, grief, joy that the head was managing down.

- **The wordless answer becomes available as a fact.** Once the body has answered nonverbally, the answer is no longer the head's theory. It's the body's report. The person can then verbalize from that report, and the verbal version carries weight the head's earlier versions did not.

- **The re-control loses its primary channel.** The control had been using the verbal apparatus. With the verbal apparatus paused, the control has nowhere to engage. The truth has space to be present without immediate reflexive management.

## What This Move Is NOT

- **It is not silence as a coaching technique.** Some coaches use silence as a generic move to let the person sit with something. This move is more specific: the silence is a re-asked truth-question with the verbal requirement removed. The question is still being asked; only the answer-form has changed.

- **It is not [[stay-with-it]] or generic "sit with the feeling" instructions.** Those moves ask the person to stay with a felt state. This move asks the person to let the felt state be the answer to a specific question. The questioning frame is what differentiates this from broader sit-with-it work.

- **It is not [[answer-from-inside-the-feeling-not-outside-it]]. That move asks the person to verbally answer a truth-question while in the felt-state. This move asks the person to answer the truth-question without speaking at all. The two moves can sequence — try the answer-from-inside, then if the verbal is still reintroducing control, go nonverbal.

- **It is not [[ask-the-body-the-question]]. That move directs the question to a specific body-part. This move directs the question to the whole-body-without-verbal-output. The mechanism is similar (bypass the head) but the address is different.

- **It is not [[interrupting-your-own-descent]]. That read identifies the answer that interrupts the descent. This move pre-empts that interruption by removing the answer-requirement altogether. The two work together — if the descent keeps getting interrupted by the verbal answer, removing the verbal requirement removes the interruption.

## The Sequence

1. **The verbal-answer access has been tested and is producing the control-after-truth pattern.** Several iterations of [[whats-your-truth-in-this-moment]] have produced openings followed by re-controls. The pattern is clear.

2. **The framing is delivered slowly.** "Go nonverbal for a minute and let the nonverbal talk." The slowness lets the person actually relocate the listening before the question arrives.

3. **The strangeness is acknowledged.** "I know that's weird to say." This pre-empts the head's attempt to figure out how to do it. The acknowledgment removes the need to solve the instruction before receiving it.

4. **The truth-question is re-asked with the verbal requirement removed.** "Don't even think that you have to say anything. So what's your truth in this moment?" The question is identical to the verbal version. What changes is the permission to not answer in words.

5. **The silence is held.** Joe does not fill the silence. He lets the body do its work. The silence may last a long time. The body knows when it has done what it needed to do.

6. **What the body delivers is named by the coach.** The person may not yet have words for what just happened. The coach can name what was visible. "So much power you are holding back" or whatever the body delivered. The naming installs the body's answer as a real event.

7. **The verbal channel can resume when the body is ready.** Often after the nonverbal answer has landed, the verbal channel opens differently — the words that come now carry the body's truth rather than the head's management. The verbal-vs-nonverbal split that was producing the control dissolves.

## Dialogue Example

The canonical exchange from the source session, after several rounds of the verbal answer producing the control-after-truth pattern:

- **Person:** "I'm confused. I'm recognizing something about the movement from being embodied... using nonverbal as a communication to verbal, that there's some juncture."
- **Joe:** "So go nonverbal for a minute and let the nonverbal talk. I know that's weird to say, but like, I'm going to say, what's your truth right now in this moment? And don't even think that you have to say anything. So what's your truth in this moment? So much power you are holding back. Holy fuck, you must have been just like this willful, powerful little girl. Holy fuck. I can't imagine you. Did you play some sort of sport?"
- **Person:** "I should have."
- **Joe:** "Oh my God, you should have. Oh my God. You just did it, by the way. You just spoke from that place. All you said was, I should have, but it was all there."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The progression: the woman herself has noticed the juncture between nonverbal and verbal — she's identified the location where control engages. Joe takes the cue and removes the verbal requirement. The question is asked. The body answers (visibly, dramatically) — power held back, willfulness, a different kind of person underneath the despondent teenager. The person speaks a small fragment ("I should have") and it comes from the new place — see [[name-the-shift-everyone-felt]] for what Joe does with that small verbal arrival.

The interesting structural detail: the person did not have to say anything when the question was asked nonverbally. The body did the answering. The verbal speech that arrived later ("I should have") was the verbal version of what the body had already answered. The verbal speech carried the body's truth because the body had already given the answer first.

## Why It Works

The move works because for many people, the verbal apparatus is where the management lives. The head has been trained to filter what gets spoken. When the head is allowed to filter (which it does any time speech is required), the filter runs and the management engages. Removing the requirement to speak removes the filter's job.

The body, given the floor without the verbal apparatus, can deliver material the verbal apparatus would have filtered. The body's signals are pre-verbal: posture, energy, breath, eye-quality, micro-movements. These signals carry information the head can't fully manage even when it wants to. The body's truth arrives more whole than the head's would.

The move also works because it surfaces the location of the control. Many people don't know their control lives in their speaking. They experience their truth-withholding as a general state ("I just can't be vulnerable") rather than as a specific operation ("the speaking is where my management runs"). The nonverbal move makes the location visible — the person can feel that the verbal channel was carrying the control, and the felt-distinction becomes available for them to work with.

The third mechanism: the move installs a new operating mode. The person discovers that not-saying-anything can be a complete answer to a truth-question. The discovery expands their repertoire. Now the person has two ways to answer the truth-question — verbal and nonverbal — and can choose based on what each moment needs.

## Tone

Slow, gentle, slightly conspiratorial. The "I know that's weird to say" carries warmth — Joe is letting the person in on the move's strangeness, which makes the person less likely to brace against it. The strangeness is normal; the strangeness is part of how this works.

The asking of the question is even softer than the verbal version. "What's your truth right now in this moment?" — said quietly, without pressure, with no expectation of immediate verbal response. The slowness gives the body time to organize its answer.

The acknowledgment of what the body delivers is delivered with weight and a kind of awe. "Holy fuck, you must have been just like this willful, powerful little girl." The weight is appropriate because what just arrived nonverbally was significant; the awe is the response of being-witness-to it.

When the person finally speaks (often a small fragment), the recognition is immediate. "You just did it, by the way." The naming marks the small verbal arrival as the same thing the body just delivered. The verbal-and-nonverbal channels are reconnected through the naming.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not use the move as a first resort.** The verbal channel needs to be tested before the nonverbal move is offered. Premature use of the move skips the diagnostic data that makes the move land.

- **Do not fill the silence.** The silence is the work. If Joe (or the coach) fills the silence with explanations or follow-up questions, the move loses its operative quality. Hold the silence.

- **Do not interpret the body's answer prematurely.** What the body delivers needs to be received first, named second. Interpretation that gets ahead of the person's own felt-sense of the answer can install the coach's reading over the person's own.

- **Do not require the verbal articulation immediately after.** Some people will want to verbalize what just happened nonverbally; others will need time. Don't push for the verbal version. It will come when it comes.

- **Do not use the move repeatedly within a session.** The move has its leverage from being a deliberate departure from the verbal mode. Using it many times within a session dulls the contrast and makes the move feel like a general technique rather than a specific intervention.

- **Do not assume the body's answer matches what the coach expected.** The body sometimes delivers something the coach didn't anticipate. Receive what's there, not what the coach was hoping for.
