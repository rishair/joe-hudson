# Drill Responsibility to Thoughts You Can't Control

## The Move

When a coachee is using "I'm responsible for X" as a self-blame engine, ask them to justify the responsibility. Follow the chain of justification recursively — "what made it that?" / "what made that?" — until the chain lands at a thought, an emotion, or a state the coachee cannot consciously control. Then make the observation explicit: "Cool. So choose to stop having that thought." The coachee, unable to comply, has demonstrated that the entire chain of responsibility they were running on themselves traces back to something they could not have controlled.

The move dissolves the self-blame architecture by walking the coachee through the logic of their own claim. They started by saying they were responsible for not making money. By the end of the drill, the same coachee is saying "I can't choose what thought to have next." The two statements are incompatible. The drill makes them notice the incompatibility themselves.

## When Joe Uses It

The trigger is the coachee asserting responsibility for an outcome and using that assertion as the engine of self-criticism. Markers:

- "It's my fault" about an outcome that has multiple causes
- "I should have" applied to a result that was downstream of many uncontrollable factors
- "I'm responsible for" used as a verdict, not as an operational stance
- The coachee identifies as "their biggest critic"
- The self-blame is unmoved by the standard counter-arguments

The move is especially useful when the coachee has been intellectually exposed to the idea that self-blame is unhelpful and has nevertheless continued to run the self-blame. The drill is a cognitive demonstration, which the cognitive coachee's defenses can engage with. The drill ends in a place the defenses cannot defend.

## How Joe Delivers It

The drill follows a predictable shape. Each link in the chain is named, and the coachee is asked what made the previous link the case.

The Bobby session demonstrates the full drill:

- **Joe:** "So prove to me that you're responsible. We'll try it. We'll like track it right down to its root. You're responsible for not making money because of what?"
- **Bobby:** "I'm not trying hard enough."
- **Joe:** "You're not trying hard enough, that's great. And what makes it that you didn't try hard enough?"
- **Bobby:** "Uh, there's a million different ways to attract clients and you've, you've tried 4 out of the million ways. So get on board."
- **Joe:** "And what made it that you only tried 4 instead of 10 or 20?"
- **Bobby:** "Time and bandwidth."
- **Joe:** "Time and bandwidth. And wait, so you're responsible for time?"
- **Bobby:** "No."
- **Joe:** "Okay."
- **Bobby:** "Well, I'm responsible for how I spend my time."
- **Joe:** "Okay. And how you spend your time is based on what your thoughts or your— like, what— how does— how do you— how do you decide how to spend your time?"
- **Bobby:** "How I— how I manage my calendar."
- **Joe:** "What makes you— how do you determine how to manage your calendar?"
- **Bobby:** "Priorities."
- **Joe:** "How do you determine priorities?"
- **Bobby:** "I choose it."
- **Joe:** "Choose it. Okay, cool. So if you have choice, then you could be held responsible, right?"
- **Bobby:** "Correct."
- **Joe:** "Cool. So choose to stop having thoughts."
- **Bobby:** "I can't though."
- **Joe:** "But yeah, exactly. You can't even choose what thought to have next. So your whole prioritization scheme that led to the time management, that led to you not doing 6 and doing 6 instead of 4 or whatever down that line, all comes from a thought that you can't control."
- *(Source: Money Worries, Identity, and the Trap of Being 'Good')*

The drill takes about ten exchanges. By the end, the coachee has reached the impasse themselves. Joe does not have to argue. The structure of the coachee's own justification has produced the conclusion.

## The Catch: "Choose to Stop Having Thoughts"

The pivotal move in the drill is the inversion question: "Cool. So choose to stop having thoughts." Or: "Choose to not have that next thought."

The question can only be answered one way. The coachee cannot choose what thought arrives next. Their whole chain of responsibility is built on the premise of choice; the inversion question shows that the choice does not exist at the level where it would need to exist for the self-blame to be coherent.

The "cool" is part of the move. It signals that the question is being asked in good faith — not as a gotcha, but as the natural next step in the drill. The coachee can engage with it without defensiveness because the tone is collaborative.

After the impasse is reached, Joe makes the observation explicit: "So your whole prioritization scheme that led to the time management, that led to you not doing 6 and doing 6 instead of 4 or whatever down that line, all comes from a thought that you can't control." The summary lands the point that the drill made. The self-blame architecture has been built on a foundation of agency that the coachee has just demonstrated they do not have.

## Why This Works (And Where It Stops Working)

The drill works because it engages the cognitive faculty the coachee is using to construct the self-blame. The same faculty, asked to justify each link, eventually produces a link it cannot justify. The cognitive coachee is much harder to argue out of self-blame than they are to follow into the contradiction of their own logic.

The drill stops working when the coachee uses the recognition to construct a new responsibility ("I'm responsible for the fact that my brain is telling me I'm responsible for shit"). Joe names this directly: "You've just gone meta." The recursion has produced the next layer of the same pattern. The drill alone cannot prevent this; it has to be paired with [[name-the-pattern-of-converting-insight-to-task]] to catch the meta-conversion.

The drill also stops working if the coachee accepts the conclusion intellectually but the body doesn't release. This is common in highly intellectualized coachees. The cognitive case is granted; the felt-state of self-blame continues. The pairing here is with [[imagine-you-are-not-responsible-thought-experiment]], which produces the somatic case alongside the cognitive one.

## What This Move Does Not Do

The drill does not remove operational responsibility for the coachee's life. It removes the architecture of self-blame that was built on top of operational responsibility. After the drill, the coachee still has to feed themselves, pay rent, do their work, and care for their relationships. What they don't have to do is run themselves as the guilty party for everything that has ever gone wrong, because they have just demonstrated that the guilt-architecture requires a level of agency they don't actually have.

The drill also does not produce a permanent shift on first deployment. The self-blame engine has been running for decades. One drill produces an opening. The opening will close. The drill will need to be deployed again. The body's threshold for tolerating the no-self-blame state rises with repetition.

## Tone

Collaborative and slightly playful. The drill is not a courtroom interrogation; it is a curious walk through the logic of the coachee's own claim. Joe's tone in the Bobby session is "let's try this" — an invitation to investigate, not a setup to expose a flaw. The playfulness keeps the drill from feeling adversarial, which is what allows the coachee to keep producing the next link in the chain rather than getting defensive and refusing to engage.

The "okay, cool" punctuation between links is a tone marker. It signals "yes, that makes sense, what's the next link?" — not "aha, gotcha, you contradicted yourself." The coachee can keep moving because there is no shame on offer for each link.

## When Not to Use This Move

For coachees who are externalizing responsibility, this move can collude with the externalization. They are blaming the boss, the parent, the system; the drill could give them more material for that. The opposite move (helping them see and own their part) is what fits there. The diagnostic: this move is for over-internalizers, not for under-internalizers.

For coachees in active dysregulation, the drill requires more cognitive bandwidth than is available. Resource first, drill later.

For coachees whose self-blame is functioning as a tether to a relationship or person they have lost (see [[self-blame-as-tether-to-the-lost]]), the cognitive drill misses the actual function the self-blame is performing. The work there is at the relational level, not the logical level.
