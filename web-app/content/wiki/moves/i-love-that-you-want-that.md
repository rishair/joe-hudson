# I Love That You Want That

## The Move

When a partner names a want that you cannot or do not want to give, the move is to receive the wanting itself with warmth, separate from the question of whether you will give the thing. The wanting is honored. The action is negotiated separately.

"I love that you want that, even if it's like I'm not gonna give that to you right now. But I honestly love that you wanna go to the movies tonight. That does not work for my schedule. I love that you want that. I'm excited to hear that you want that." -- Alexa Kistler, Ending The War With Your Wants

The move is small in form and large in effect. It tells the partner: your want is welcome here, even when this particular request does not land. Their wanting is not the thing you have to manage, refuse, or punish. It is just their wanting, and you receive it with warmth.

## What This Resolves

The default response to a partner's request you cannot meet is some version of:

- Apologetic refusal ("I'm sorry, I can't, I have work")
- Defensive refusal ("I can't, you know I have work, why do you keep asking")
- Resentful compliance ("Fine, I'll go, but I have work to do")
- Counter-attack ("You always want to go to the movies")

All four positions place the partner's wanting itself as the problem. The refusal is necessary, but it lands as a refusal of the wanting, not just of the action. The partner learns to want less, or to want more carefully, or to stop saying what they want.

The "I love that you want that" move separates the wanting from the action. The wanting can be welcomed even when the action cannot be given. The partner's want-naming muscle is reinforced rather than punished. The relationship retains the want-flow that keeps it alive.

## The Mechanics

The move has two components, in order:

1. **Receive the wanting.** "I love that you want that." "I love that you want to go to the movies." Said with actual warmth, not performed warmth. The body has to register that the partner's wanting is genuinely received.

2. **Then address the action separately, if needed.** "That doesn't work for my schedule tonight." "I can't tonight, but maybe Friday." "I don't want to go, but I'm glad you want to." The action question is its own question, handled cleanly, with no contamination back into whether the wanting was acceptable.

The order matters. If the refusal lands first and the receiving lands second, the receiving feels like a softener for the refusal — a manipulation, not an actual welcome. If the receiving lands first and the refusal lands second, the receiving is the primary message and the refusal is the secondary information.

## The Joe Caveat

Joe notes that Alexa's version of the move is "a step farther than is necessary." The minimum version is just being okay with the want without resentment:

"Oh cool, you wanna go to the movies? That's awesome. Like, you know, like, fantastic." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The minimum version skips the explicit "I love that you want that" and just receives the wanting as fine, normal, welcome. Either version works. The expanded version makes the welcome explicit, which is sometimes what is needed — especially for a partner who has been trained to expect their wanting to be punished.

Both versions are protected against the resentment-cascade by the same mechanism: the wanting is not treated as a demand. It is treated as information about the partner's experience, which is its own thing, separate from whatever the listener does with it.

## What This Move Prevents

A specific failure mode the move prevents:

"Well, I don't want to go to the movies, but they want to. And if they don't, then they're going to be resentful for me because I don't want to go to the movies. Okay, I'll go to the movies." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The above is the trap of taking responsibility for the partner's happiness with the partner's wanting as the trigger. The partner wants something, and the listener feels obligated to satisfy the wanting to prevent the partner's resentment. The result is going to the movies you do not want to see, with someone you are now resenting for "making" you go.

The "I love that you want that" move exits the trap by separating the wanting from the obligation. The partner's wanting is welcome. The action is the listener's choice. The listener can choose freely because the wanting is no longer a demand requiring management.

See [[dont-take-responsibility-for-anothers-happiness]] for the broader principle this move operationalizes in the want-receiving direction.

## The Reciprocal Form

The move has a self-directed parallel for the want-namer: when you state a want that the other person cannot meet, the cleanness of the want-statement determines what the partner can do with it. A want stated with grasping ("I really need you to go") forces the partner to manage the grasping. A want stated clean ("I want to go to the movies tonight") gives the partner room to receive it cleanly.

See [[asking-with-shame-invites-refusal]] for the failure mode of unclean asking. The clean ask makes the "I love that you want that" response available to the listener. The unclean ask does not.

## Why This Builds the Relationship

The relationship that punishes wanting loses its want-flow. Both partners start hiding what they want to avoid the punishment. The hidden wants then come out sideways (see [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] for the organizational form of the same dynamic). The relationship becomes a place where neither person knows what the other actually wants, and both are operating on guesses and projections.

The relationship that receives wanting cleanly keeps the want-flow. Both partners can say what they want without bracing for punishment. The wants get heard. Some get met, some do not, but the saying and the hearing are not the variables in jeopardy. The relationship becomes a place where what is true keeps getting said.

The "I love that you want that" move is a small daily action that keeps the relationship on the second track instead of the first.

## What This Is Not

- Not "always say yes to your partner's wants." The move explicitly separates the receiving of the want from the giving of the thing. The thing can be refused; the want is still welcomed.
- Not "perform warmth you do not feel." The move requires the receiving to be actual. A performed "I love that you want that" with internal resentment underneath is read as a manipulation and produces the opposite effect.
- Not a way to control your partner. The move is a way to receive your partner cleanly. If it is being deployed to produce a specific response from them, it has become its opposite — a sophisticated form of management.
- Not just for romantic partners. The move works the same way with children, friends, family, and colleagues. Anyone who states a want is in the same position; anyone receiving it has the same choice.

## When To Use This Move

- Whenever a partner names a want you cannot or do not want to meet
- When a child says they want something you are not going to give them
- When a colleague asks for something you have to decline
- When you notice yourself about to refuse a wanting with frustration
- When you sense your partner has stopped naming wants because the wantings keep getting refused or punished
- Especially when you have just refused a series of wants and the relationship is starting to feel strained

## Tone

Warm, light, not effortful. The move only works if the warmth is actual. A heavy or studied "I love that you want that" reads as performance and produces the opposite effect. The aliveness of the receiving is the carrier of the move.
