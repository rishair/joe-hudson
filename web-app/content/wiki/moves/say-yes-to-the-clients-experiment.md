# Say Yes (Fully) to the Client's Experimental Request

## The Move

When a client takes the risk of asking for a specific experiential thing in the session — "would you be the parent who tells me it's okay?" "could you love my pain?" "would you let me be a victim for a little bit?" — Joe says yes. Not partially. Not with a frame around it. Not with an explanation about how this isn't quite how coaching works. Yes, and then delivers the thing requested with full presence.

This is a coaching move with a specific shape. The person is exposing a want they have rarely or never voiced. The request itself is the breakthrough. The coach's job in this moment is not to interpret the request or coach the person about asking. It is to receive the request and deliver. The transformation lives in the experience of having the want met, not in talking about wanting it met.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person has been pulling the brakes on a specific want and they finally name it.
- When the want is something the coach can actually provide — presence, witnessing, agreement to a role, willingness to receive an emotion.
- When the person has been described as "afraid to come across as a victim" and they ask to be allowed to be one.
- When the person frames the request as an experiment: "I want to try something."
- When refusing the request would re-enact the very pattern the person came in with.

The move does NOT apply when:
- The request is for the coach to take responsibility the client needs to hold.
- The request would compromise the coach's role or the work's integrity.
- The request is for the coach to confirm a limiting belief.
- The request is for advice the coach genuinely doesn't have.

The discrimination matters. The move is "yes" when the request is for an experience the coach can give. It is not blanket compliance.

## The Specific Form: Yes Plus Embodiment

Joe does not just verbally agree. He physicalizes the yes. The phrasing he uses:

> "Oh yeah, absolutely, I can do that. Yeah, but you got to look at my face. You got to see that I actually do love your pain and anguish. And for me, it's not even holding space. It's a privilege and an honor to be with it."

Three moves inside the yes:
1. Direct verbal agreement, said immediately. ("Oh yes, oh yes.")
2. A specific instruction to receive: "you got to look at my face." The receiving has to be embodied or the giving doesn't land.
3. Elevation of the framing: not "holding space" (which is therapist-speak) but "a privilege and an honor." The reframe communicates that the client is not asking for a difficult favor; they are offering the coach something precious.

The third element matters most. Many coaches will agree to do the thing while subtly communicating that it is hard or unusual or a stretch. That subtle reluctance is what makes the experience fail to land for the client. The move only works when the coach genuinely receives the request as a gift.

## What It Produces

The thing the person has been bracing against for years happens, and they survive it. They get to be a victim for a moment, and the world does not collapse. They get to express their anguish, and they are not abandoned. They get to be loved in the very state they were sure was unlovable, and the love arrives.

What follows is usually:
- A breath out of the body.
- Tears, sometimes for the first time in a long time about this specific thing.
- A spontaneous arrival of gratitude or another positive emotion that the person had no plan to feel. (See [[running-toward-yourself]] — the feeling, fully met, moves to its opposite.)
- An "oh, fuck" or similar — the recognition that this was possible the whole time.
- A specific physical signature: the body comes online in a way it hadn't been.

> "And at the same time, I feel so grateful that I get to be here at all and I get to be here with you right now. And it's -- I'm so grateful for that."

The gratitude was not asked for and not performed. It arrived because the victim-feeling was fully allowed.

## What Joe Does Next

- Names what just happened. "Notice what just happened. Notice that when you allow that feeling of victim all the way in and all the way through, it moves to enjoyment."
- Connects the experience to the structure they came in with. "We're scared that if we allow the feeling of the victim to be fully there, that we'll end up being a victim forever. Yeah, but what it actually just did is empower you."
- Extends the principle outward. The same move the client just received can now be given to others: "And so most likely something's happening in you where you're feeling your wife's anguish and feeling responsibility, so you can't fully just be there with her in it. And if you can be there fully with her in it, this is what's on the other side for her too."

The session does not end with the yes. The yes is the experiential payload that makes the principle understandable. Joe then helps the client convert the felt experience into something they can do with others.

## Why Refusing Would Re-Enact the Pattern

A specific dynamic: the client in the chronic-pain session had spent his life learning that expressing his pain would make him a burden. If the coach, when explicitly asked to love the pain, hedged or qualified, the moment would teach him the same lesson again. The hedging would be received as "even here, even when I ask, my pain is too much." The pattern would be confirmed in the very place that could have broken it.

The yes is therefore not a stylistic preference. It is structurally necessary. The work happens in the receiving of the want. Refusing the want, however gently, prevents the work.

This is why the move requires the coach to actually mean it. A performed yes feels like a hedged yes. The body knows the difference. The coach has to be the kind of person who is actually willing to love this person's pain, not the kind of person who performs willingness as a technique.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "Because, um, you always say that the vulnerable thing is to say the scary thing."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Person:** "And so the scary thing that's true, would be if, if you would feel comfortable, if you felt like you could hold space."
- **Joe:** "Oh yes, oh yes."
- **Person:** "And say that you, you love my pain and my anguish and let me be a victim for a little bit."
- **Joe:** "Oh yeah, absolutely, I can do that. Yeah, but you got to look at my face. You got to see that I actually do love your pain and anguish. And for me, it's not even holding space. It's a privilege and an honor to be with it."
- **Person:** "It fucking sucks to be in pain all the time, and I hate it."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Ah, fuck. I hate it for you. I'm not even experiencing the pain. Thanks, man. Yeah."
- **Person:** "Oh, fuck."
- **Joe:** "Oh yeah."
- *(Source: How Do I Stop Postponing My Enjoyment?)*

The "Oh yes, oh yes" arrives before the person has finished the request. Joe knows where the request is going and meets it preemptively. The speed of the yes is part of what makes it credible.

The "Yeah. Ah, fuck. I hate it for you" is Joe receiving the person's anguish without flinching. He does not soften the suckiness. He matches it. The matching is what makes the witnessing real.

## Tone

Open, warm, unhesitating. There is a specific energy: no surprise that the person asked, no praise for asking, no hedging in the giving. The yes is delivered as if it is the most natural thing in the world. The naturalness is what allows the person to actually receive.

A specific somatic feature: Joe's face. He explicitly instructs the client to look at his face. The face is communicating the yes as fully as the words. Coaches who can only deliver this move verbally are losing most of the medicine.

## Anti-Pattern: Hedge Words That Kill the Move

Watch for these almost-yeses that fail:
- "Yes, I can hold space for that." (Therapist-speak. Distances the coach from actually doing the thing.)
- "Sure, that's okay." (Permissive but not present. The client is not asking for permission; they are asking for the thing.)
- "Of course, that's totally valid." (Validating the request instead of fulfilling it.)
- "I love you anyway." (See [[dont-add-anyway-to-love]]. The "anyway" makes the loved thing the bad guy.)
- "Let me know if you'd like to try that." (Punting the move back to the client.)

The test: does the response answer the request or talk about the request? Answering is the move. Talking about it is the failure mode.
