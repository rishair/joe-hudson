# Be With Them Without Trying to Fix or Diagnose

## What Joe Does (Or Teaches the Partner to Do)

When someone's partner is going through an accidental awakening, the partner's instinct is to fix it, diagnose it, or get the partner back to who they were. Joe teaches a different stance: be with them, hold them, let the experience be what it is, do not pretend to know what is going on.

This is the witness stance. It is closer to how you would hold a crying infant or a scared three-year-old than to how you would handle a sick adult who needs intervention.

"If you are going, if you're with somebody who's going through this, the important thing is to be with them. It's gonna change, it's gonna change really rapidly, especially at first. You don't, like, don't pretend to know that you know what's going on for the other person or that you have to deal with it. It's like, how can you be there with them in the experience the same way that you would be with an infant who is crying or the same way that you would be with somebody who was scared, like a 3-year-old who was scared. Like, how do you be with them in that way?" -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The move has a felt shape. The witness is present, attentive, holding, soft. The witness is not trying to interpret, fix, or restore. The witness is making themselves available without making themselves responsible for the outcome.

The phrasing Joe offers as model: "Nothing needs to change. I'm right here. I know that everything's gonna shift. So let's just -- this is -- haven't -- it's not something I've experienced, but I've heard that it's quite common. So let's just hang out and I'll hold you. I'll love you through this experience. And everything's gonna change."

## When the Move Applies

The move applies when:

- A partner is going through an awakening process and the speaker is the witness.
- A friend, parent, or close other is going through it and the speaker is the close-other.
- The witness is asking "what do I do?" or "how do I help them?"
- The witness has been doing something else (advising, diagnosing, pushing for intervention, getting angry, withdrawing) and the work is to redirect into the witness stance.
- The relationship is solid enough to bear the witnessing -- if it is not, this move addresses the wrong layer; the relationship's stability is the prior work.

It does not apply when:

- The witness is themselves the one having the experience -- in that case the moves are different (grounding, normalization for self, etc.).
- There is a genuine acute psychiatric emergency that is not awakening (psychosis with self-harm risk, etc.). The witness stance is for an awakening process; an acute mental-health crisis needs other intervention.
- The witness's role in the relationship was abusive and the partner's shift is exposing that. In that case the work is more complex and the witness stance may be a way of avoiding accountability.

## What "Being With" Actually Means

Specific operations the witness can do:

**Physical presence.** Hold them. Sit close. Be available to be touched. The body of the person going through the disruption is in unfamiliar territory; the presence of a familiar body is grounding.

"Like literally physically being held is really good. Like having, if you have like a wife, lover or something, husband, like, can you just hold them?" -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

**Verbal acknowledgment without interpretation.** "I'm right here. I love you. I don't know what's happening but I'm with you." Not "you're going crazy" and not "this is awakening and here's what it means." The witness is acknowledging the experience without taking a position on what it is.

**Normalization in passing.** "I've heard this is quite common." This is the witness's version of [[normalize-the-experience-its-happened-to-others]] -- offered briefly, in passing, without becoming a lecture.

**Steadiness.** The witness's own state is doing as much work as their actions. If the witness is panicking internally, the partner picks that up. If the witness is settled, the partner picks that up. See below on the witness's own work.

**Non-fixing.** The witness does not propose interventions. Does not push for professional help unless there is a genuine emergency. Does not push for the partner to talk about it more, or talk about it less. Does not try to engineer the next state.

## What the Move Excludes

The witness is specifically not doing:

- Diagnosing the partner with a psychiatric condition.
- Pushing for the partner to "snap out of it" or "be normal again."
- Pretending to understand what the partner is experiencing.
- Treating the experience as a problem to solve.
- Making the experience about the witness's own discomfort with the partner being different.
- Withdrawing emotionally because they don't know what to do.
- Going through every framework they can find to figure out what is happening.
- Becoming the partner's therapist or coach in a structured way.

The default operations of caring -- fixing, diagnosing, advising, intervening -- are precisely what does not work here. The witness has to actively resist the default operations and substitute something simpler.

## The Witness's Own Work

The witness cannot do this move from a contracted place. If the witness is in their own fear, their own grief, their own destabilization, they will leak it into the holding. Joe is direct about this:

"Which of course requires having that same stance towards your own fear and your own uncertainty and disappointment or sadness that comes up when this shift occurs in your life." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The witness's job is partly to do the same work toward themselves that they are being asked to do toward the partner. Their own fear, uncertainty, sadness, disappointment, grief -- these arise. The witness does not have to make those go away. They have to be met with the same allowing they are extending to the partner.

This is one of the structural reasons being-the-witness is itself developmental work. It is not a passive role. It asks the witness to develop their capacity to be with discomfort -- their own and their partner's -- without trying to manage either. The capacity that develops in the witness is the same capacity that supports awakening in general.

"It's not just like -- and don't -- it's not like, how do you take care of them in it? It's, this is how do you take care of yourself in it? And if you can just be with somebody and witness that transformation happening, it is an incredibly privileged place to be. And it also quickens your own experience of it." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

## Why This Move Quickens the Witness's Own Development

Watching someone go through an awakening process opens a possibility in the witness. See [[contact-with-greatness-shows-whats-possible]]. The witness sees that the territory is real and is being navigated in their actual home. Their own system picks up the possibility without instruction.

The witnessing also asks the witness to develop the same capacity the partner is developing -- the capacity to be present with the dissolution of familiar configurations without trying to grab them back. The work the witness does on themselves to be able to stay present is itself the work that produces development.

This is one of the structural reasons partners often have their own openings during or after their partner's awakening. The witnessing is not a passive role; it is its own form of practice.

## The Hard Case: Partner Cannot Do This

Joe is realistic that not every partner will be able to do this move. It is a lot to ask of someone who has no framework, no preparation, and may have a long history with the partner that is now being reorganized.

"I don't expect that a lot of people will do it or be able to do it or even end up listening to this, but if you happen to be one of those lucky people, that is the stance that'll make the whole thing so much easier for you and for them." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

When a partner cannot do this move, the coaching work shifts. The partner may need to do their own work first. The relationship may need other forms of support. The person going through the awakening may need to find the holding elsewhere. None of this is failure; it is the realistic shape of how this work intersects with actual relationships.

## What This Produces

When the witness can do this move:

- The person going through the awakening has the safe container the integration requires.
- The fear component (which is the primary producer of suffering during awakening) reduces faster.
- The relationship transforms with both partners rather than breaking under the strain.
- The witness develops their own capacity for presence, which often opens their own process.

When the witness cannot do this move:

- The person going through the awakening has to navigate it without their partner's support, often in isolation or with the partner as a stressor.
- The integration takes longer and is harder.
- The relationship may not survive the transition.
- The witness may continue their own development through other paths, or may not.

## Tone

Soft. Steady. Spacious. The witness is making themselves into a low-noise environment for the partner to be in. Their state communicates: nothing is wrong, you are safe here, I am here, I am not going to push, I am also not going to leave.

The tone is closer to how a parent holds a frightened child than to how a doctor handles a patient. It is intimate, embodied, and quiet.
