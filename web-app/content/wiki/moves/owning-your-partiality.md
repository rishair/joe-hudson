# Owning Your Partiality

## Definition

Stopping mid-conversation to name your own agenda. This is both a return to [[impartiality]] and an act of [[vulnerability]]. It interrupts the partial dynamic by making the hidden want visible.

## What It Sounds Like

- "I'm sorry, I notice that I really want to be valuable to you, and that's not the best way that I can respect you or this conversation. I apologize for that."
- "I noticed that I'm giving you advice. I'm trying to fix you. How do you feel about that?"
- "Oh, I want for you to be different than you are, and I apologize for that."

## When To Use It

- When you notice yourself steering a conversation toward a particular outcome.
- When the urge to give advice, fix, or lead someone to your conclusion arises.
- When the conversation feels like it has become about you being right or helpful rather than about the other person.

## What It Produces

The move disarms the dynamic immediately. It breaks the pattern of partial conversation and often produces relief in the other person. If asked mid-conversation, "I notice I'm trying to fix you, how do you feel about that?", most people will say "yeah, please don't."

"You think about that for a second. Most people, most of the time are going to say, yeah, please don't. And yet we're freaking doing it all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3

Owning the want also reduces its grip. Naming "I want you to be different" makes the want visible, and visible wants lose their compulsive quality. See [[wants-vs-partiality]].

## Dialogue Examples

**The investor meeting (Mikey Siegel story):**
Joe was meeting Mikey Siegel when Joe was an investor. They were having a nice connection. Then Mikey shifted into pitch mode. After about a sentence, Joe said: "I noticed my entire body is getting tense right now." He had no idea what the consequence would be. The moment produced a deep level of connection. Their friendship has lasted years, and the benefits far exceeded whatever Mikey was originally pitching.
*(Source: Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3)*

**The fight with his wife:**
Early in Joe's marriage (year 4 or 5), his wife came home furious that he had skipped an event important to her. She was yelling. Joe wanted her to stop punishing him, to see him as loving, to stop being angry. Then something clicked: "It's okay for her to be just the way she is." He let her be angry without trying to change it. The anger lasted three or four minutes. She got angry at him for not reacting. That lasted about a minute. She needed some alone time. It was not a quick fix. But for Joe, it was freedom: "I can love her just as she is, no matter what's happening." -- Joe Hudson, Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3
*(Source: Impartiality -- Connection Course Series #3)*

## Tone

Gentle, matter-of-fact, slightly self-deprecating. Not performative self-criticism. Not fishing for reassurance. A simple honest observation shared out loud.
