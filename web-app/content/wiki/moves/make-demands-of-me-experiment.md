# Make Five Demands of Me

## The Move

After the client has touched the dominating-anger form ("I am going to dominate, I am not going to be dominated"), the anger often collapses the moment the client tries to direct it at a person. The collapse signals that the anger is bumping into need-suppression: the client has not been allowed to make demands of anyone, and the dominating anger requires the directed-at-someone structure to land.

The move: have the client make aggressive demands of you, the coach. Five demands. Or ten. The framing matters: it is not personal, you (the coach) are not going to do them, the demand itself is the exercise.

The form Joe uses:

1. **Catch the moment the dominating anger collapses on contact with another person.** The client is moving in dominating anger, then says something like "I have something to say, you're going to listen" — and the body drops the energy as soon as a real "you" is in the sentence.
2. **Name what just happened.** "What just happened is, as soon as you made a demand of somebody else..." The naming makes the collapse visible.
3. **Set up the experiment.** "Do me a favor, make, in an aggressive tone, make ten demands of me. It's not personal, I'm not going to do them. Just make — or make five demands of me."
4. **Watch what happens.** The client almost always finds this much harder than expected. The struggle is the diagnostic. The number, the difficulty, the inability to find demands at all — each is data about the need-suppression layer.

In the canonical session:

- **Joe:** "Make, in an aggressive tone, make 10 demands of me. It's not personal, I'm not going to do them. Just make — or make 5 demands of me."
- **Client:** "Oh, uh, gosh, see, that's hard. Um, yeah, okay, Joe, give me an answer, tell me what I need to do, um, understand me, um, uh, ah, see, this is harder than I thought it was going to be. Uh, I can tell the world this, but like telling you something is much harder, like the symbol of I need something. I have needs. Oh wow, I have needs, Joe."
- *(Source: Why Am I Stuck In Life When I Know My Patterns?)*

The client's discovery in real time: "I can tell the world this, but like telling you something is much harder." The world-directed anger had cover. The person-directed demand exposes the need.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the client has accessed dominating anger toward an abstract circumstance but the energy collapses when a real "you" enters the sentence
- When the read is [[need-suppression-from-childhood-unsafety]] — the person learned it was not safe to have or to share needs
- When the client is a self-reliant type ("I have to do it all myself") and the dominating anger keeps slipping back into victim-anger because there is no permission to demand
- When the client's anger reliably runs as "tired of it" (victim) rather than as "you will give me" (dominating), and the dominating form needs an explicit container to find shape
- When the work is on [[i-have-to-do-it-all-myself]] or [[how-do-i-love-myself]] (the "I ignore my needs" variant) and the move needs an in-session demonstration that the need exists
- After the dominating anger has been modeled (see [[victim-anger-vs-dominating-anger]]) and the client needs a concrete target other than the original circumstance to practice it

The move presupposes some access to anger has already opened. If the client has not begun to mobilize, the move is premature; the prior move is to release victim-anger first or to use [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] to start the engine.

## What It Produces

Four common outcomes, in roughly this order of recognition:

**1. The demand collapse.** The client cannot find demands. The body wants to please instead. "Give me an answer." "Tell me what I need to do." "Understand me." These are not demands — they are appeals. The client may not initially register the difference.

**2. The recognition.** Somewhere in the attempt, the client realizes that what they are producing is not what was asked for. "Oh wow, I have needs." The recognition is the work. The move is not designed to produce successful demands; it is designed to surface the structural fact that the demands cannot be found.

**3. The retreat.** Often immediately after the recognition, the client retreats into self-reliance: "I have needs and I'm not gonna share them with you because I've got this. I'm on my island. It's all right. You go do you and I'm gonna figure this out on my own." The retreat is also data — it reveals the survival mechanism that took over when needs became unsafe.

**4. The opening question.** With the structure now visible, the natural next move is "what's the need?" — asked as an actual inquiry rather than as a request for content. The need that surfaces is usually the one the dominating anger was the form of, and is also the one whose suppression has been keeping the work stuck.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the client finds demands easily:** the read of need-suppression may be wrong. The work is something else. Adjust.

**If the client struggles to find demands (most common):** name the struggle as the finding. "You have needs and needs, right? And you don't share them. You don't even share them with yourself." The not-sharing-with-self is the deepest layer; the move has surfaced it.

**If the client retreats into self-reliance:** let the retreat happen without interrupting. The retreat is showing the historical survival move. Name it once it has played out: "Yeah. You have needs and needs, right? And you don't share them. You don't even share them with yourself." Then move to the inquiry about what the need is.

**If the client locates the need and intellectualizes it:** redirect to feeling. The need-vocabulary can become the next defense if the client has framework-language for needs. The point is the felt need, not the named need.

**If the client locates the need and the body shifts:** the work has reached the substrate. The follow-up is to stay with the need as a felt thing, then connect it to the original presenting concern. The need was the anger's destination.

## What This Move Is and Is Not

It is not the same as [[invite-the-aggression-out]]. That move converts existing passive aggression into direct aggression. This move surfaces the absence of demands when there is no aggression — passive or active — toward anyone specific because the demand-channel was never installed.

It is not [[dont-weaponize-the-make-it-active-move]]. That anti-pattern warns against telling a partner or team member to be aggressive at you outside the coaching frame. This move is contained within a coaching session, with the coach as the deliberate target, with the explicit setup that the demands will not be granted.

It is the dominating-anger application of [[victim-anger-vs-dominating-anger]]. The distinction names that both forms are required for clarity. This move is the in-session practice for the form most people find harder, with the structural twist that the difficulty itself is diagnostic.

## Why It Works

Demands require two things the self-reliant or compliance-trained person has had blocked: the felt sense that one is allowed to want something from another, and the felt permission to direct energy at that other person. The childhood install — "it wasn't safe to have needs" — closes both. The result is a person who can express anger about the world ("I'm tired of this," "this shouldn't be like this") but cannot form the sentence "you will give me X."

The artificial container of the experiment removes the consequences. The coach is not going to do the demands. The client knows this in advance. The relational stakes are zero. And the client still cannot do it. The inability with zero stakes proves that the block is not situational; it is structural. The structure is what the work then addresses.

The experiment also gives the body a chance to discover what demanding feels like. Most clients have never made an aggressive demand of anyone in their adult life. The somatic motion is unfamiliar. Even one or two demands successfully produced (even haltingly) installs a small new template the body can build on.

## The Diagnostic Layer

The move has informational value even when no demands successfully form. Specifically:

- **Time-to-first-attempt:** how long does it take the client to even try? Long delay = deep block.
- **Quality of the attempts:** are they actually demands, or are they appeals dressed as demands? "Understand me" is an appeal. "Give me $1000 right now" is a demand.
- **Energy of the attempts:** is the body mobilized while making them, or is the body apologetic? Apologetic body = the demand has not actually been made.
- **What replaces demands:** what does the client produce instead? Pleading, justifying, explaining, retreating — each points at a different historical survival adaptation.

The data from the attempt is the work for the next several minutes of the session.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence showing the move and its outcome:

- **Joe:** "There it is. Oh, and what just happened is you — as soon as you made a demand of somebody else —"
- **Client:** "yep, yep, because this is a relational trauma. Fuck this."
- **Joe:** "yeah, so do me a favor, make, in an aggressive tone, make 10 demands of me. It's not personal, I'm not going to do them. Just make — or make 5 demands of me."
- **Client:** "Oh, uh, gosh, see, that's hard. Um, yeah, okay, Joe, give me an answer, tell me what I need to do, um, understand me, um, uh, ah, see, this is harder than I thought it was going to be. Uh, I can tell the world this, but like telling you something is much harder, like the symbol of I need something. I have needs. Oh wow, I have needs, Joe. No, that's, that's okay. I have needs and I'm not gonna share them with you because I've got this. I'm on my island. It's all right. You go do you and I'm gonna figure this out on my own."
- **Joe:** "Right."
- **Client:** "Which is why I almost didn't raise my hand here too, because I'm like, what am I doing being all visible?"
- **Joe:** "Yep. Yeah. You have needs and needs, right? And you don't share them. You don't even share them with yourself."
- *(Source: Why Am I Stuck In Life When I Know My Patterns?)*

The exchange shows all four common outcomes: the collapse (cannot find demands, produces appeals), the recognition ("Oh wow, I have needs"), the retreat into self-reliance ("I'm on my island, I'm gonna figure this out on my own"), and the opening for the inquiry about what the need is. Joe's role is small — set up the experiment, hold the space, name the structure at the end. The client's struggle does the work.

## Tone

Light and matter-of-fact in the setup. The disclaimer ("it's not personal, I'm not going to do them") is delivered without ceremony — too much ceremony makes the move feel like a test and the client braces. The casualness allows the client to attempt without performance pressure.

Patient during the struggle. Joe does not rescue the client by suggesting demands or by softening the ask. The struggle is the work.

Direct and slightly warm at the naming. "You have needs and needs, right? And you don't share them. You don't even share them with yourself." The phrasing makes the structural fact visible without making it shameful.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not insist the demands be granted.** The setup specifies the coach will not grant them. Granting them undermines the structure — the client now has reason to manage the demand for outcome, and the experiment becomes performance.
- **Do not pre-soften the move.** "I know this is going to be hard" makes the difficulty an obstacle to overcome rather than the data the move is collecting. Let the client discover the difficulty.
- **Do not suggest demands to the client.** If the client cannot find demands, that is the finding. Suggesting "demand more attention" or "demand respect" replaces the client's content with the coach's content, and the discovery is lost.
- **Do not let the client substitute a polite request for a demand.** "Could you please understand me?" is not a demand. Name the difference. "That's a request. Make a demand."
- **Do not move on too quickly when the client succeeds with one or two demands.** The successful demand is a small new template; spend a beat letting the body register what it just did.
