# The Happy Money Game

## The Move

For wealthy clients (often billionaires or near-billionaires), the standard pricing structure does not work as coaching. The fee is too small to matter, the relationship can be bought, and the money loses its function as a vehicle for transformation. The Happy Money Game is the structural alternative: an exchange of proposals where the pricing itself is designed to address the specific pattern the client is working on.

The base structure has two parts:

1. **Reciprocation as the entry condition.** "I will coach you for one session. You have to reciprocate in some way. I don't care if you give me a million dollars or you put a million dollars into my kids' college fund, or if you give a dollar to a homeless person. Don't care. Just want you to reciprocate in a way that feels right for you." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

2. **Proposal exchange to determine ongoing terms.** Both parties propose terms. The winning proposal is the one that works for both people. The negotiation itself is part of the coaching.

The move treats money as one more instrument in the work, not as a separate transactional layer. The fee structure becomes the coaching, not just the precondition for it.

## How the Pricing Is Designed for the Pattern

The two examples Joe describes show how different the designs can be when the design serves the client's specific pattern.

**The client who could buy anything.** A wealthy client whose pattern was that he bought everything; he had had money so long that he could not trust whether anyone was with him for him or for the money. The proposal Joe accepted: "I will not take any money from you." The relationship could only have the trust this client needed if Joe was structurally unbuyable.

"With one of those two people, our financial agreement was, I will not take any money from you. Refuse to take any money because that person's particular situation was that they bought everything. They had money and they had had it for so long that they could buy anything. And so the only way that the relationship could have trust for me was that he couldn't buy me." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

**The client who could not commit.** A brilliant but scattered client whose pattern was difficulty completing commitments. The proposal Joe accepted: an exorbitant fee, with the fee doubling if the client did not do the work, and a 5-minute daily recording for 30 days of telling himself he loved himself; the fee tripling if the recording did not happen.

"You're gonna pay me an exorbitant amount of money, and then if you do not do the work, then you're gonna pay me twice the money. And you're going to send me a recording, 5-minute recording, every day for 30 days telling me that you love yourself. And if you don't do that, then you're gonna pay me triple the money." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

The fee structure made the client's growth path the only winning path. The client cared about winning, measured by money. The game was constructed so that the only way to win the money game was to do the work.

"I was playing against this particular person's thing of like winning. He was incredibly smart. He loved winning. So I set up a situation where the only way he could win is to do the work." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

## Why This Works (And Why Standard Pricing Does Not)

For most clients, a standard hourly fee creates an exchange that both parties can feel. See [[money-must-flow]] on calibrating the exchange so both parties contribute. For wealthy clients, the standard hourly fee fails this test. The money is too small to register as the client's contribution. Without felt exchange, the client does not have the same internal stake in the work.

The Happy Money Game corrects for this by making the fee structure something the client has to actually engage with, not just pay. The proposal exchange forces the client to think about what would make the relationship work. The unusual terms (no money, or escalating money) keep money from being the easy default.

The move requires that the coach has done their own work on [[money-is-impersonal]]. A coach who is still personal about money will be unable to refuse the million dollars or propose the tripling fee without contamination from their own charge. The move is only available from impersonal money.

## What Joe Was Optimizing For

Across both examples, the optimization function was the same: how do I use money as one more tool to help this person transform? Not how do I get paid. Not how do I price my time. The fee structure was designed backward from the transformation question.

"Money just became this tool of like, how do I use money as one more tool to help people in their transformation, which is what I cared about, which is what was in alignment with me. And that's when money -- I could not do that until that moment of seeing through money and what it was and what I had made out of it, that like the ultimate projection for me around money." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

This is what makes the move different from creative pricing as a marketing technique. The fee structure is part of the coaching. The negotiation is part of the coaching. The agreement is part of the coaching. Each element is designed to address the specific pattern the person is working on.

## When the Move Is Available

The move requires several conditions:

- The coach has done their own work on money (see [[money-is-impersonal]]) such that they can design without their own projection contaminating the design
- The client has unusual wealth such that standard pricing fails to create exchange
- The client's pattern can be addressed through the fee structure (commitment issues, trust issues, control issues, winning orientation, etc.)
- The coach has the relational authority to propose unusual terms without it landing as gimmick

Without these conditions, the move becomes a stunt. The fee structure has to actually serve the work. If it does not, it becomes another way for the coach to feel clever rather than another instrument for the client's transformation.

## What Replaced This Move Later

Joe notes that in his current practice, he no longer plays the Happy Money Game with individual clients. The game he is playing with money has scaled up: how do I use money structurally so that the work is available to as many people as possible? The MasterClass, the broader business, the reinvestment of revenue into community and access -- these are the scaled form of using money as an instrument for transformation.

"It's like there's more and more revenue every year, but not really any more net. It's more and more gross because we're just taking all the money that we make and put it into ways of like, you know, supporting the community or growing the capacity for people to find it." -- Joe Hudson, Much Ado About Money

The move is still valid for the right context. It is no longer the primary form Joe uses for the same intention.

## What This Move Reveals About Pricing in General

The Happy Money Game is the extreme case that surfaces a general principle: the fee structure for transformative work should be designed for the transformation, not for the market rate. Different clients need different fee structures to make the exchange real. Uniform pricing serves administrative simplicity. It often does not serve the work.

This is the lived form of the tiered-pricing logic in [[money-must-flow]]. The general principle is that the fee should be calibrated to make the exchange real for the specific client. The Happy Money Game is what calibrated exchange looks like for the wealthiest clients.
