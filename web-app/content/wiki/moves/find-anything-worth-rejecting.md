# Find Anything Worth Rejecting

## The Move

After the client has admitted, recognized, or been shown that they are rejecting themselves, ask them to find the evidence. Not in the abstract — in their body, right now. What inside them actually deserves rejection?

The move is a body-scan request. The client is asked to look inside and identify what would warrant the rejection that is currently underway. The expected — and almost universal — finding: nothing. Or more precisely: the search itself produces a felt absence, and the absence is the data.

"So here's a weird one. I want you to look inside yourself and see if you can find anything worth rejecting. Anything." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Can't Ask for What You Want

The instruction is delivered with the qualifier "weird one" — flagging the structural strangeness of the request. The strangeness is part of the move. The client has been operating as if there is obviously something inside that deserves the rejection. Asked to specify what, the answer does not arrive. The non-arrival is the work.

## When Joe Uses It

- Immediately after [[the-only-way-to-not-be-rejected-is-to-reject-yourself-first]] has landed and the client has acknowledged the self-rejection. The principle names the mechanism; this move tests the basis.
- When the client has been doing self-rejection long enough that they take it as obviously justified ("I should reject this part of me, it's bad / weak / wrong"). The move surfaces that the obvious-justification cannot actually be located.
- After parts work has surfaced a rejected part (sadness, resistance, anger, ambition, the victim) and the rejection of the part is about to be addressed.
- When [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]] has been named and the client is about to be offered welcoming as the alternative.

The move is not used to argue. It is used to invite the client to look. The looking is the move. If the client looks and finds something they think is worth rejecting, that finding is the new data — and the work continues with that material rather than against it.

## What It Produces

- A felt search inside the body. The client closes their eyes or goes internal. The search is somatic, not logical.
- A felt absence. Most clients cannot find what would justify the rejection. The mind can produce candidates ("my sadness, my fear, my needyness"), but when scanned for what actually warrants the rejection, the candidates do not hold up.
- An uncomfortable noticing. From the source session: "Anything worth rejecting? Yeah. I, I don't know. I Doesn't really feel great." The doesn't-feel-great is the precise data — the search has found that there is nothing solid to reject, and that absence is uncomfortable because the self-rejection has been organizing the client's behavior for years.
- A natural opening to welcoming. The absence of a target makes the alternative (welcoming) suddenly available. If there is nothing inside that deserves rejection, then the welcoming is not a stretch — it is what remains when the rejection has no object.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the client cannot find anything:** Move to welcoming. "So then welcome it. Welcome the whole thing. Just for like 1 minute, see what it's like to welcome yourself completely." The welcoming is the alternative made available by the empty search. See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]] and [[loving-all-parts]].

- **If the client reports "it doesn't feel great" or similar discomfort:** Treat the discomfort as confirmation, not as a problem to fix. The discomfort is the gap left by the absent target. Don't talk the discomfort away. Move directly to welcoming the discomfort too. The discomfort is part of the whole-thing being welcomed.

- **If the client offers a candidate ("my sadness," "my anger," "the weak part"):** Address the candidate with the parent move it requires. If the candidate is a feeling, welcome the feeling. If the candidate is a part, use [[loving-all-parts]] or [[i-love-the-rejected-part-of-me-let-them-love-it-in-the-coach-first]]. The candidate's appearance is information about what the client has been organizing the self-rejection against — that material can now be worked directly.

- **If the client offers a story candidate ("I'm a bad person," "I deserve it because of X"):** Surface that the story is the rejection, not the basis for it. The story is what self-rejection sounds like, not what justifies it. See [[reject-all-the-stories]] for setting the story aside to do the work underneath.

- **If the client tries to intellectualize ("I think I have some things I'd want to work on"):** Redirect back to the body-scan. "Don't think about what you'd want to work on. Look inside right now and see if you can find anything that deserves rejection — anything that would warrant the way you've been treating yourself." The work-on framing is a different question than the deserves-rejection question.

## Why It Works

The self-rejection has been operating without ever being asked to defend itself. The client has been performing the rejection because it has been the operating system, not because they have an articulable case for it. The move asks for the case. The case turns out not to exist, or to dissolve when examined.

This is the same mechanism as [[surface-the-rejection-then-demand-its-evidence]] in the projection domain. The reader is asked to look at the room for evidence of what the rejection claims. The looking finds the absence. The absence breaks the spell.

The somatic substrate matters. The move asks for a body-scan, not a mental inventory. The body's response to the search is the data — the "doesn't feel great" of finding nothing solid, the slight settling when the search comes up empty, the opening when the welcoming becomes available. The body can tell the truth that the mind has been arguing with.

There is also a structural truth being demonstrated: self-rejection is content-less. It is a defense pattern, not a verdict on actual material. The client has been treating the rejection as if it were warranted by what is inside them. The scan shows that the rejection is operating without a target. Once seen, the rejection can be put down because it has nothing to do.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, the move follows the principle landing:

- **Joe:** "Check it out. The only way in life to not get rejected is to make sure you reject yourself first. There's the sadness. So here's a weird one. I want you to look inside yourself and see if you can find anything worth rejecting. Anything."
- **Client:** "Anything worth rejecting? Yeah. I, I don't know. I Doesn't really feel great."
- **Joe:** "So then welcome it. Welcome the whole thing. Just for like 1 minute, see what it's like to welcome yourself completely."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Ask for What You Want)*

The exchange is short. The move does not require elaboration. The instruction, the search, the empty-finding, the redirect to welcoming. The brevity is part of why it works — there is no place for the mind to construct a defense of the self-rejection.

## Tone

Curious, light, slightly playful. The "weird one" qualifier signals that the move is unusual but not heavy. The instruction is delivered as an invitation to look, not as a demand to prove. The coach is not preparing to argue; the coach is asking the client to do the looking themselves.

If the client comes up with a candidate, the coach does not get firmer. The coach receives the candidate ("okay, so there's the sadness — what makes the sadness deserve rejection?") and continues the inquiry. The tone stays light because the inquiry is not adversarial — it is genuinely curious about what the self-rejection is supposedly about.

When the client reports the doesn't-feel-great, the coach does not soothe. The discomfort is part of the work. The coach moves to the next step (welcoming) without dwelling on the discomfort, because the welcoming is what addresses the discomfort.

## Variations

**The body-part variant.** Instead of looking inside generally, scan specific parts: "scan your body for any part that deserves to be rejected." See [[scan-body-for-what-cant-be-loved]] for the related move that goes after specific parts rather than the general scan.

**The list-then-test variant.** Have the client list everything they think deserves rejection. Then go through the list one by one and ask what makes each item deserve rejection. The list rarely survives the testing.

**The framework-mirror variant.** Use the client's own framework against the rejection. If the client believes in unconditional love, ask why they are excluding themselves from it. If they are a parent, ask whether they would reject these qualities in their child. See [[mirror-the-clients-own-framework-back]].

**The post-rage variant.** After [[i-love-the-rejected-part-of-me-let-them-love-it-in-the-coach-first]] has moved the wall down, the body-scan can find that the part the wall was protecting is now loveable. The empty-search produces the same opening to welcoming.

## Anti-Pattern Risk

The move fails if the coach turns it into an argument. The instruction is "look," not "I will prove there is nothing." If the coach is trying to win the argument that nothing deserves rejection, the client will defend the rejection. The move works only when the client does the looking and the looking does the work.

The move also fails if the coach treats a candidate ("my sadness deserves it") as a problem to dispute. Disputing the candidate makes the candidate stronger. The candidate is information. Work with the information.

The move also fails when run too early — before the self-rejection has been named, before the client is willing to look. Run too early, it lands as "you should love yourself" which is the very should that the work is trying to dismantle.
