# Coach as Sonar: Ping the Person and Listen for What Comes Back

## The Move

The coach makes a move — a question, a challenge, a reflection, a provocation. The coach does not make the move because they have decided the coachee needs to go in a specific direction. They make the move to send out a ping. Then they listen for what comes back. The bounce — the coachee's response, their body, their tone, what they say or do not say — is the information that tells the coach where to ping next.

The metaphor: sonar. A submarine sends out a sound. The sound bounces off whatever is out there. The shape of the bounce tells the submarine what is around it. The submarine does not decide in advance what is out there. It pings, listens, and follows the picture as it builds.

Joe describes his coaching method in exactly these terms:

"I'm definitely — pattern recognition is happening. But what the other thing that's happening is I'm really letting them lead because somehow or another what I've noticed is, and it might not be the most efficient path, but it is a more efficient path than the other ones I've run across, which is to — that person knows where they need to go next better than I do." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

"It can be very aggressive looking from the outside, but it's — I'm pinging against them and like listening to the sonar that comes back. To say, oh, this is where they're ready to move." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

## Why the Move Looks Aggressive From Outside

A specific feature of the move that is worth naming: it can look aggressive from outside, especially compared to a more reflective, validating coaching style. Joe will pose a sharp question, hold a long uncomfortable silence, challenge a framing, make a provocative reframe. To an observer, this can read as the coach pushing hard. From inside the move, the coach is doing none of that. They are pinging to see what comes back.

The signature: the coach is not invested in any specific response. They are not trying to get the coachee to agree with the framing, accept the reframe, or move in the direction the question implied. They are watching what the question produces in the coachee's system and using the production as the next data point. If the question produces a defensive contraction, that is information. If it produces a sudden breath of relief, that is information. If it produces a long silence followed by tears, that is information. Each of these tells the coach what to ping next.

The aggressive surface is the function of the sharpness of the pings, not the function of the coach's investment in the answer. A diffuse, vague question produces a diffuse, vague bounce. A sharp specific question produces a sharp specific bounce. The coach uses sharp pings because they produce more legible bounces. The legibility of the bounce is what makes the next move possible.

## Why This Differs From Leading

A coach who is leading has a destination in mind. They are using questions and reflections to steer the coachee toward the destination. The coachee may resist; the coach reformulates; the coachee may evade; the coach circles back. The coaching session is a transit between the coachee's starting point and the coach's destination.

A coach who is pinging-and-listening has no destination in mind. They have skill, intuition, pattern recognition, and a deep familiarity with how human transformation works. They have the empty cup (see [[empty-cup-parable]]). What they do not have is a place they are trying to take the coachee.

The reason this matters is operational. The coachee knows where they need to go better than the coach does. The coachee's own migratory path (see [[migratory-path]]) is the curriculum. Following the coach's destination is, at best, an approximation of what the coachee actually needs. At worst, it is a derailment. The coachee's bounce, attended to closely, points more reliably at the next step than the coach's best inference.

"That person knows where they need to go next better than I do." -- Joe Hudson

This is a deep methodological claim. It contradicts the standard image of the expert coach who has analyzed the situation and is now skillfully maneuvering the coachee toward the resolution. Joe's stance is the opposite: the expert work is in the listening, the pinging, the willingness to be led by what the coachee's system reveals. The coach is the instrument; the coachee's path is the music.

## The Pattern Recognition Layer

The move is not pure passive responding. The coach has, underneath the listening, a great deal of pattern recognition. They know what they are pinging for. They know what kinds of bounces signal what kinds of internal states. They know which patterns tend to follow which other patterns. The pattern recognition is what makes the pinging skillful rather than random.

"I'm definitely — pattern recognition is happening." -- Joe Hudson

But the pattern recognition serves the listening, not the leading. The coach uses pattern recognition to know which ping to send next, not to know where the coachee should end up. The pattern recognition narrows the field of possible next moves; the coachee's bounce selects from the narrowed field.

This is also why pure technique-extraction from coaching videos fails (see [[dont-treat-coaching-videos-as-how-to-coach]]). The visible move is the technique. The invisible work is the pattern recognition that produced the technique as the right ping at that moment, and the listening that responded to the bounce. Copying the move without the underlying work produces a coaching imitation that does not actually function.

## The Required Internal State

The move is structurally impossible without a specific internal state in the coach. The state is the emptied state described in [[empty-cup-parable]]. The coach has to not be invested in the answer. If they are invested, the bounce gets distorted in their reception — they hear what they wanted to hear, or they hear the absence of what they wanted to hear, rather than hearing what was actually there.

Joe names the internal state directly:

"There's a deep impartiality in me, even though it doesn't look like it, where I — I don't think I need to get that person anywhere. I'm not there to help them. This isn't — there's just, there's not a lot of I in the equation. So oftentimes at the end of it, I don't remember what happened. I don't know if it went well, and I don't care." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

Each phrase points at a specific feature of the state:

- **Deep impartiality.** The coach has no preference about the outcome. See [[impartiality]].
- **Not needing to get them anywhere.** No destination in mind. See [[no-attachment-to-the-outcome]].
- **Not there to help them.** The helping frame is itself an investment in a specific outcome (their being helped). The frame fills the cup. Removing it empties it.
- **Not a lot of I in the equation.** The coach's self is not in the conversation in a way that competes with the coachee's self. The coach is present as instrument, not as participant.
- **Doesn't remember afterward.** The coach was not processing during the session — they were attending. The absence of processing means the absence of formed memories. The not-remembering is a signature of having been fully present.
- **Doesn't care if it went well.** The judgment "did it go well" requires a standard. The coach has no standard, so the question does not arise.

Each feature of the state is a feature of the emptied cup. Each is what makes the pinging-and-listening possible. A coach without this state will still be doing the pinging (they will still be asking questions), but they will not be doing the listening, because the cup is too full to receive what the bounce contains.

## The Empathic Receiving

The move includes a specific somatic-empathic component. The coach does not just hear the coachee's verbal response. They feel, in their own body, what is happening in the coachee's body. The bounce includes felt-sense information that is not in the words.

"Deeply empathetic. I'm feeling the thing they're feeling. So oftentimes if I'm working with somebody and I'll say, 'yeah, what's going on in your gut?' I can't even see it. But because I'm so empathetically in with them that I'm actually able to feel it. I feel it through my own body, through my own system." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The mechanism: the coach's body is receiving the coachee's state. The coach's question about what is going on in the coachee's gut is not a probe based on visual evidence. It is the coach naming what their own body is registering and asking the coachee to confirm or correct.

This is the part of the move that emerges from the self-work described in [[dont-chase-coaching-or-psychic-skills-directly]]. The coach who has spent years working through their own somatic terrain recognizes the same terrain in the coachee's body, because the same signatures are present in their own. The empathic receiving is the operational form of that recognition.

## The Vulnerability in the Asking

A small but operationally important feature: the coach is vulnerable in the asking of the question. Joe describes literally puckering when a question is about to come that he knows is the right one.

"I'm vulnerable in the listening and in the asking of questions. I'll often ask questions where I literally pucker to ask the question, and I usually know that that's a really good question. There's feeling of vulnerability." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The vulnerability is the diagnostic. A question that does not produce vulnerability in the coach is usually a question the coach already has an answer to — a leading question, a checking question, a confirmation. A question that produces vulnerability is one the coach genuinely does not know the answer to. The not-knowing is what makes the ping clean. The clean ping is what produces the legible bounce.

This is also why the move requires courage. The coach who is willing to ask only the safe questions stays on the surface. The coach who is willing to ask the questions that make them vulnerable goes deeper, because their willingness to be vulnerable is what permits the coachee to be vulnerable in return.

## What the Move Looks Like in Practice

A composite of the operational sequence:

1. The coach pings — asks a question, makes an observation, holds a silence, names a felt sense.
2. The coachee responds — verbally, somatically, with a shift in tone, with a defensive contraction, with a sudden breath, with tears, with words.
3. The coach receives the bounce — fully, in their own body and mind, without immediately deciding what to do with it.
4. The bounce reveals where the coachee is currently ready to move.
5. The coach designs the next ping in service to that readiness.
6. Repeat.

The whole sequence happens fast. Often within a single sentence the coach has pinged, received, and is already pinging again. From outside it looks like fluid expertise. From inside it is just pinging and listening on a fast cycle.

## When the Move Fails

The move can fail in two specific ways:

**The coach decides what they heard.** Instead of receiving the bounce, the coach interprets it through their existing framework. The interpretation replaces the bounce. The next ping is in service to the interpretation rather than the bounce. The coaching session drifts away from the coachee's actual path and onto a parallel path that exists in the coach's mind.

**The coach has an agenda.** The coach is invested in a specific outcome — the coachee seeing something specific, accepting a specific framing, taking a specific action. The investment fills the cup. The bounce is heard through the investment. The pings become attempts to produce the desired outcome rather than attempts to surface information.

Both failures collapse the move back into leading. The coach has resumed deciding where the coachee should go. The pinging-and-listening surface is preserved but the underlying mechanism is now driving rather than receiving.

The internal check: am I curious about what is actually happening in this person? Or do I have a sense of where this should go and am I working toward it? If the second, return to the work of emptying the cup. The move is not available without the emptiness.

## When to Use This Move

The move is not a specific intervention to deploy at specific moments. It is a coaching stance, an underlying mode. The move is the default operating mode of skilled coaching once the coach has done enough of their own self-work to be able to hold the emptied state reliably.

Specific moments where its presence is especially visible:

- When a coachee presents a problem and the coach resists the urge to immediately reframe or solve.
- When a coachee says something the coach disagrees with and the coach receives it without correcting.
- When a long silence develops and the coach does not fill it.
- When the coachee starts to cry and the coach stays with the crying rather than trying to console.
- When the coachee asks "what should I do?" and the coach asks a question back rather than answering.
- When the coach asks a question that surprises them as they ask it — the coach's own surprise is the signature that the ping arose from the listening rather than from a script.

## Tone

The tone of the move is paradoxical: simultaneously deeply present and entirely impersonal. The coach is fully there with the coachee, attending to them with all available attention. The coach is also entirely free of the personal investment in how the session goes. The combination produces a tone that is intimate without being attached, intense without being aggressive, warm without being soft, sharp without being cold.

The tone is what people often describe as feeling "seen" by Joe even when he has said very little. The seeing happens because the cup was empty enough for the coachee to actually enter, and the empathic receiving was sensitive enough to register what arrived.

- When the coach is being asked how they do what they do.

- When the coachee is over-relying on the coach's direction and the move back to their own knowing is needed.
- When teaching others how to coach and the temptation to teach techniques is rising — the move is the underlying capacity the techniques are expressions of.
