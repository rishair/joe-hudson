# Feel the Shame, Then Feel the Exact Opposite

## The Move

When the person has identified an antidote intellectually ("love," "support," "acceptance") but the antidote is operating at the conceptual level, take it to the body. Have them feel the shame somatically. Then have them produce the somatic opposite — not by thinking about it, by feeling it.

The form Joe uses:

1. **"Stop, get out of your head for a second."** Bring attention from the head to the body.
2. **"Feel the shame in your body."** Have them locate it somatically. Often the chest, the gut, the throat.
3. **"Now, on a completely somatic level, I just want you to feel the exact opposite of that feeling."** No thinking, no conceptualizing. Feel the opposite.
4. **Watch the body.** Something happens. A breath, a softening, a small expansion. Name it.

In the canonical session:

- **Joe:** "Just stop, stop for a second. Get out of your head for a second. Go to your heart. Go to your gut. Right? ... So what's the antidote to shame? What do you do when you've got like the shame hangover that you currently have?"
- **Client:** "Support, love. Yeah, yeah, acceptance."
- **Joe:** "Let's do love here first. Let's do a visceral thing in your body. Feel the shame in your body. You hurt this woman, she left. And now, on a completely somatic level, I just want you to feel the exact opposite of that feeling."
- **Client:** "That's hard."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, you started, man. I saw it. Your chest raised. You started to feel that love. I saw it."
- *(Source: Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?)*

The "that's hard" response is normal and is data. The body has not fully produced the opposite yet, but it has started. The coach watches and names the start. The naming gives the body permission to continue.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the person has named the antidote intellectually ("love is the answer") and is still in the shame somatically. The intellectual answer alone does not move the body.
- When [[i-cant-forgive-myself-for-hurting-them]] is the presenting concern and the conceptual work has surfaced but the embodied work has not.
- When the person reports being a "shame addict" or recognizes the [[addiction-to-shame]] pattern and needs a felt reference for what the alternative actually is in the body.
- When the session needs to demonstrate that the antidote is available right now, not just describable as a future state.
- When [[shame-blocks-connection-in-real-time]] is the live read and the move needs to interrupt the shame somatically rather than discursively.

The move presupposes the client can locate shame in the body. If they cannot, the prior move is locating shame somatically — see [[ask-the-body-the-question]] or [[scan-body-for-what-cant-be-loved]].

## What This Move Is and Is Not

It is not the [[toggle-between-opposite-emotions-then-hold-both]] practice, which uses memories to anchor each emotion and cycles between them. This move is faster and more direct: locate the current shame in the body, then produce the somatic opposite without an anchor.

It is also not the [[feel-the-searching-then-feel-the-opposite]] move, which applies to abstract pursuits like searching for purpose. The current move applies to a specific emotion the person is currently experiencing in the body.

It is closely related to [[show-me-the-bracing-and-show-me-the-opposite]] — same somatic A/B logic, applied to an emotion rather than a posture. The shame is what bracing is to the body — a sustained state. Asking the body to produce its opposite is the move in both cases.

## Why It Works

The mind has a concept of love. The body has a felt experience of love. The two are not the same. When the body is in shame, the conceptual love is unreachable because the body has been organized around the shame for hours or days. Asking the body to imagine the opposite would invoke the concept again. Asking the body to *produce* the opposite somatically bypasses the conceptual layer.

The body has the capacity. The body has produced love before. The somatic memory of love is available. The instruction "feel the exact opposite of that feeling" gives the body permission to dip into that memory — not as a mental image but as a felt state.

The coach's job is to watch for the start of the opposite arriving and to name it. The naming validates the small movement the body produces. Most people, in this move, produce a slight start of the opposite and then catch themselves — the shame reasserts. The coach's naming ("your chest raised, you started to feel that love") makes the small movement count and invites the body to continue.

## The Permission-to-Cry Add-On

In the breakup session, once the love started to arrive, the client began to compose himself against tears. Joe added a specific instruction:

"You're allowed to cry. That's part of love. So what you're going to want to do right now is shame yourself for crying in front of all these people." -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?

This is not a separate move. It is a continuation of the somatic A/B. The tears are part of the somatic opposite of shame. The composing is the shame reasserting. Naming the shame-about-to-fire-again is what allows the body to continue producing the love-state instead of the composed-state. See [[composing-in-the-session-itself]] for the read this permission addresses.

## The Use-the-Room Add-On

After the somatic start of love has been produced, the move can be extended by anchoring to the room. The client is on a call with people present.

"Fuck that. Look at everybody. Look at me and, and love me. Love yourself. This is the antidote to your shame addiction. Yeah, see that I'm loving you. Look at the other faces, see that they're loving you right now, or in awe of you, or check them out." -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?

This is [[use-the-live-group-as-love-field]] applied to deepen the somatic opposite. The body has started producing love; the room provides the field for it to expand into. The combination — somatic opposite plus group amplification — is what lands the experience as more than a momentary spike.

## What Not To Do

- **Do not let the person stay in their head.** "Get out of your head" is a real instruction. If they start explaining what love is or why it is hard to feel, redirect to the body.
- **Do not let the antidote be the wrong one.** The antidote must be the actual somatic opposite of the shame, not a coping mechanism. "Distraction" is not the antidote. "Productivity" is not the antidote. Love, support, acceptance — the ones the client names — are antidotes because they are the somatic opposites.
- **Do not push past the resistance.** If the client says "that's hard," name what you see ("but you started, your chest raised"). Do not force the body to do more than it is doing. The naming gives permission; the forcing closes the door.
- **Do not skip the watching.** The move depends on the coach actually seeing the somatic change. If the coach is not watching the body, the naming becomes generic and the body does not register the permission.
- **Do not treat the spike as the destination.** The brief moment of the opposite is the start, not the conclusion. The move opens the door; the practice (see [[15-minutes-of-self-love-physical-therapy-for-the-heart]]) is what builds the muscle.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "What's the antidote to shame? What do you do when you've got like the shame hangover that you currently have?"
- **Client:** "Support, love. Yeah, yeah, acceptance."
- **Joe:** "Let's do love here first. Let's do a visceral thing in your body. Feel the shame in your body. You hurt this woman, she left. And now, on a completely somatic level, I just want you to feel the exact opposite of that feeling."
- **Client:** "That's hard."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, you started, man. I saw it. Your chest raised. You started to feel that love. I saw it. Yeah, so let's just call it love and just love yourself for a minute. Love her. Yeah, you're allowed to cry. That's part of love."
- *(Source: Why Can't I Move On After My Breakup?)*

The exchange shows the full move: ask for the antidote, locate the shame in the body, ask for the opposite, watch, name the start, continue, give permission for the tears that the opposite is producing. The whole sequence is under a minute. The client moves from "that's hard" to actually feeling the love within the next exchange.

## Tone

Active. Joe is doing the move with the client, not narrating it from outside. "Let's do a visceral thing." "Stop. Get out of your head." The tone is that of a trainer in a session, not a teacher describing technique. The pace is fast enough to prevent the mind from intervening.

When the body starts to produce the opposite, the naming is celebratory but not theatrical. "Yeah, you started, man. I saw it. Your chest raised." The validation is specific (the chest, the raise) and direct. The specificity lets the body register what just happened so it can do it again.
