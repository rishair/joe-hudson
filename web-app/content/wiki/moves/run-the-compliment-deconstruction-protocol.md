# Run the Compliment-Deconstruction Protocol

## The Move

A three-step protocol the coach runs in session, announced in advance, that trains the client to receive a compliment by using their mind to dissolve the dismissal their mind would otherwise produce.

The structure announced explicitly before running it:

"Okay, so I'm going to compliment you, you're going to tell me what your mind says, and then you're going to deconstruct the mind. Okay, that's the steps we're going to take." — Joe Hudson, Why Your Boss Never Sees You

The three steps:

1. **Coach offers a genuine compliment.**
2. **Client articulates what their mind says to dismiss it.**
3. **Client deconstructs the dismissal using their own mind.**

The protocol is run once or twice in session as a demonstration. The client takes it home as a portable practice. The whole sequence takes about thirty seconds per cycle.

## When Joe Uses This

The setup requires:

- The client has been identified as a deflector of compliments
- The client has named a not-enough belief or similar underlying structure powering the deflection
- The client is intellectually strong — the mind they are being asked to redeploy actually has the capacity to deconstruct its own outputs
- The principle [[mind-deconstructs-itself]] has been established (or is being established by the running of this protocol)
- The session has already done at least one round of the receiving work less formally and the client is ready for a structured practice they can take home

The protocol is for the client whose problem is not "I want to receive but the channel is rusty" but rather "I want to receive and my mind immediately produces an argument against the receiving." The protocol gives the mind a job that uses its strength while disabling its sabotage.

The protocol is also for the client who needs an explicit, repeatable structure to take with them. Some clients need a vague invitation to "just try receiving." Others need a numbered sequence. The protocol serves the second type.

## What It Produces

When run cleanly, the protocol produces a small but unmistakable somatic shift on the deconstruction step. The client articulates the mind's dismissal, then articulates the deconstruction, and as the deconstruction lands the body softens. The compliment that was being deflected lands somatically because the deflection mechanism has just been disabled by its own logic.

In the source session:

- **Joe (Step 1):** "I really am enjoying our conversation, and I fucking love the way your mind works."
- **Client:** "Thank you so much. No one had said that."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, actually letting it in."
- **Joe (then setting up the next round):** "So normally, if I give you a compliment like this, what would your mind say to not feel that?"
- **Client (Step 2):** "I would simply say that I haven't proven anything to get this compliment."
- **Joe:** "Great. Now let's use your mind to deconstruct it."
- **Client (Step 3):** "I don't need to prove to have a compliment."
- **Joe:** "Exactly. Look, there you are feeling it again."

*(Source: Why Your Boss Never Sees You)*

The client's deconstruction ("I don't need to prove to have a compliment") was produced by the same mind that, two seconds earlier, had said "I would simply say that I haven't proven anything to get this compliment." The mind that had been firing the dismissal had also fired the dissolution. The body felt the second one. The compliment landed.

After two rounds, the client reported: "I can feel a lot of tension getting out of my body. I can feel that little shakiness." The protocol had moved the system. The shakiness is the discharge of the held tension of the long-running deflection.

## Why This Works

The protocol works because it gives the mind two roles in sequence: prosecutor and defense. The mind that has been only prosecuting the client's worthiness (the prosecutor's job: "you haven't earned this, they're just being nice, you don't deserve it") is now also asked to defend it (the defense's job: "find where the prosecution is wrong, find where the dismissal is not true"). The same mind, redeployed, does the second job competently.

This is the operational form of [[mind-deconstructs-itself]] applied specifically to the receiving difficulty. The principle is: the mind's best job once presence is available is to deconstruct its own outputs. The protocol implements the principle as a three-step sequence the client can run on demand.

The compliment-deconstruction protocol also breaks the implicit assumption the deflector has been operating under: that the mind is on the deflection's side. The protocol demonstrates that the mind is not on a side. The mind does whatever job it is given. If given the job of dismissing, it dismisses. If given the job of dissolving the dismissal, it dissolves the dismissal. The client's relationship to their own mind shifts from "my mind keeps doing this thing I don't want" to "my mind does what I deploy it to do."

The third reason the protocol works is that it makes the dismissal articulable. Before the protocol, the dismissal is doing its work pre-consciously. The client cannot grab it because they cannot see it. The protocol's Step 2 ("tell me what your mind says") forces the dismissal up to the surface where the conscious mind can then take it as the target for Step 3. The dismissal that has been operating in the dark gets put on a table where it can be examined.

## The Meta-Frame Matters

The protocol must be announced before it is run. The announcement is not optional. The announcement does several things at once:

- It tells the client what is about to happen, so the client can participate consciously rather than just react
- It signals that what is coming is a teaching moment, not a real test of worthiness — the client does not have to defend against the compliment as a maneuver
- It removes the surprise that would otherwise produce the immediate reflexive deflection
- It frames the coming exchange as collaborative work, not as the coach trying to make the client feel something

"Okay, so I'm going to compliment you, you're going to tell me what your mind says, and then you're going to deconstruct the mind. Okay, that's the steps we're going to take."

The "okay" at the end is the consent check. The client has time to nod or push back before the compliment arrives. This is structurally different from the surprise compliment that catches the client off guard. The surprise compliment surfaces the deflection. The announced protocol trains the receiving.

Both are useful coaching moves at different points. The surprise compliment is for diagnosis. The announced protocol is for practice. Conflating them — running the protocol as a surprise — produces neither outcome cleanly.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If Step 2 produces a clear dismissal: Joe affirms it, then moves to Step 3 with "great, now let's use your mind to deconstruct it." The "great" matters — it tells the client that surfacing the dismissal is itself success, not failure. The deflection that has been a problem is now data.

If Step 2 produces no dismissal because the client is genuinely letting it in: Joe names this and stays with the receiving. The protocol is for the deflective configuration. If the configuration is not present in this moment, run the receiving instead.

If Step 3 produces a deconstruction that does not land: Joe asks for a stronger version. The deconstruction has to actually dissolve the dismissal, not just say words against it. "I don't need to prove to have a compliment" lands because it is structural — it asserts a principle that, if true, makes the dismissal false. "I'm probably good enough" does not land because it concedes the prosecution's frame.

If Step 3 produces a deconstruction that does land and the body softens: Joe names the body's report. "Exactly. Look, there you are feeling it again." The naming anchors the somatic shift to the protocol. The client now has a felt experience of the protocol working, which makes the take-home version more usable.

If the client reports tension exiting the body after a round or two: Joe immediately moves to a discharge practice. In the source session, Joe asked the client to say "I'm excited" ten times to ride the shakiness through into the body fully. See [[say-im-excited-ten-times]].

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence in compressed form:

- **Joe (meta-frame):** "Okay, so I'm going to compliment you, you're going to tell me what your mind says, and then you're going to deconstruct the mind. Okay, that's the steps we're going to take."
- **Joe (Step 1):** "I really am enjoying our conversation, and I fucking love the way your mind works."
- **Client:** "Thank you so much. No one had said that."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, actually letting it in."
- **Joe:** "So normally, if I give you a compliment like this, what would your mind say to not feel that?"
- **Client (Step 2):** "I would simply say that I haven't proven anything to get this compliment."
- **Joe:** "Great. Now let's use your mind to deconstruct it."
- **Client (Step 3):** "I don't need to prove to have a compliment."
- **Joe:** "Exactly. Look, there you are feeling it again."
- **Client:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "Right. So what you're doing is you're using this brilliant mind of yours to try to figure out yourself. Pivot that to using that brilliant mind of yours to deconstruct your mind so that you can know yourself."

*(Source: Why Your Boss Never Sees You)*

The exchange demonstrates the protocol in operation and closes with the take-home framing: pivot the mind from figuring-out-self to deconstructing-mind. The protocol is the practice form of the pivot.

## Why the Take-Home Framing Matters

The protocol is most useful when the client takes it with them. The take-home framing has to be portable: short enough to remember, structured enough to be runnable in the moment, generic enough to apply to any compliment.

Joe's portable framing: "Use your brilliant mind to deconstruct your mind so you can know yourself."

The framing is short. The framing inverts the standard self-improvement use of the intellectual mind (figure yourself out) into the deconstructive use (dissolve what is in the way of presence). The framing gives the client both why and how. The "why" is "so you can know yourself" — the deconstruction is in service of self-knowledge, not in service of suppressing thought. The "how" is "use the mind to deconstruct the mind" — the operational instruction.

The protocol is what the framing means in practice. Run the protocol once, take the framing home, run the protocol on any future compliment that does not land. The portability is the design.

## Tone

Direct, instructional, slightly playful. The meta-frame is delivered conversationally — "okay, so I'm going to compliment you" lands like a teacher explaining the next exercise, not like a clinician administering a protocol. The compliment that follows is genuine, specific, and chosen for the actual person in the room. The follow-up questions are crisp and short. The affirmations along the way ("great," "exactly," "there you are feeling it again") are quick, not effusive.

The tone keeps the protocol from feeling mechanical. The protocol has a clean three-step structure, but the running of it is improvised and warm. The structure is for the client's mind. The warmth is for the client's body. Both have to be present for the receiving to actually happen.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not [[use-recent-deflection-to-test-receiving-live]].** That move uses the client's recent deflection of the coach's compliment as a teaching moment by having the coach deflect the client's compliment in return. This move is structural training in receiving via a repeatable protocol. They can be used in sequence.

- **It is not [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]].** That anti-pattern names what deflection costs the giver. This move addresses the deflection mechanically by giving the mind a different job. The anti-pattern is the diagnosis; this protocol is one form of the treatment.

- **It is not a thinking exercise.** Step 3 is intellectual but the test of success is somatic. The deconstruction has worked if the body softens. If the body does not soften, the deconstruction was performed but did not land. The protocol is somatic with intellectual scaffolding, not intellectual with somatic decoration.

- **It is not [[apply-how-is-that-not-true-to-every-thought]].** That practice asks the universal question of any thought as it arises. This protocol is specific to compliments and runs as a three-step sequence with a coach-offered prompt. They share the underlying principle ([[mind-deconstructs-itself]]) but operate at different scales.
