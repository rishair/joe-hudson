# Open the Heart to the Other Person

## The Move

The person has frozen, is freezing, or will freeze the next time they are seen. They are in the meeting, in the conversation, in the room, with the partner. The Eye of Sauron is on. They need a concrete, single, take-home move they can do in the moment when the limbic system is mostly offline and only "a little tiny part" is still active enough to hear an instruction.

The move: open your heart to the other person. Not to the situation. Not to yourself first. To the specific human you are scared of being seen by.

The full instruction:

> "Open your heart to that person. So stop objectifying them. Recognize that you've already cut yourself off from them. You're judging them. You're basically saying in that moment, you're going to attack me. You're a bad person. You only care about yourself. You can't see me. And you're totally self-obsessed and focused on you. That's what you're saying about the other person. So you've already like fully disconnected and you're worried about what they're thinking about you, but you're basically thinking all those things about them." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen and the Strange Way Out

The instruction has two parts. The first part is the move itself ("open your heart to them"). The second part is the diagnostic that makes the move land: you have already objectified them; the worry about what they think is itself the disconnection; the move is to undo the disconnection you have already performed without noticing.

## When Joe Uses It

This is the closing move when the presenting concern is fear of being seen and the work has produced enough recognition that a take-home practice is needed. Joe brings it in when:

- The person has surfaced that they freeze in meetings or social situations and wants a concrete thing to do.
- The Eye of Sauron architecture has been named or is implicit, and the person needs the operational antidote.
- The work has gone deep enough that the person can hear the move structurally, not just as "be nicer to them."
- The session is closing and the person is asking, in some form, "what do I do next time?"

The move can also be brought in mid-session if the person is freezing live in the coaching room. The same instruction works: open your heart to the coach right now. The freeze becomes the immediate laboratory.

The move presupposes the person has at some point experienced an opened heart. If they have not, the prior work is to find one moment when they have felt connection to someone and let that be the somatic reference. The instruction is not "manufacture an emotion you have never had." It is "do, deliberately and in this moment, the thing your body already knows how to do in other moments."

## What It Produces

The most common immediate report: a dissipation of concern about what the other person thinks. Not because the thinking stops, but because it stops mattering. The attention has relocated. The slot the worry was occupying is now occupied by the connection.

"If I choose to be connected with you, it doesn't really matter if you're connected to me, from my experience, right? If I'm connected to you and you can — like, anybody who's listening to this right now can just do it. You can just say, okay, like, deeply connect with Brett. Brett is like not connecting with you right now. Brett's connecting with me. But like, connect with Brett and notice how much less you give a shit what Brett thinks about you, because there's — and how much that connection doesn't really require Brett." -- Joe Hudson, The Fear of Being Seen and the Strange Way Out

The structural inversion is what makes the move work. The pattern said: I need them to see me well, which requires monitoring how they are seeing me. The move says: I am connecting to them, which makes the monitoring unnecessary. The connection is what the monitoring was indirectly seeking, and the connection is now available without the seeking.

Other reports:
- The nervous system settles. The throat unclenches. The body softens.
- Wonder arrives in the place that was occupied by fear. "What is going on with them?" replaces "what are they thinking about me?" See [[wonder]].
- The person can ask the other a question they had not been able to ask. The blocked speech becomes available.
- The realization that the person across from them is, often, also scared. The shift makes the other person's humanity visible in a way the fear blocked.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **When the person says it works but only when the other person seems friendly:** that is not what the move is. The move is opening the heart regardless of what the other person is doing. "It doesn't mean that I'm not going to draw boundaries. It doesn't mean I'm going to take abuse. It doesn't mean that I'm going to be like a punching bag for somebody. But it does mean, oh, right, I'm going to actually stay connected with you." The opening is unilateral. It does not depend on the other's reciprocation.

- **When the person worries that the other will exploit the openness:** name the protection inside the worry. The protection is the same Eye-of-Sauron pattern, now applied to the move that was supposed to dissolve it. The boundary is not the opposite of the open heart; the boundary is what the open heart can do while remaining open. See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] and [[heart-opening-needs-boundaries]].

- **When the person reports it makes the situation worse:** check the read. Often what the person calls "worse" is the felt arrival of the emotion the freeze was preventing. The freeze was muting the body; the open heart unmutes it; the unmuted body has feelings. See [[the-pulled-back-from-part-is-the-unloveable-part]].

- **When the person asks how to remember to do it in the moment:** anchor the move to the felt sense of the Eye-of-Sauron firing. The freeze itself becomes the cue. The recognition "I am freezing" is the prompt to open the heart. The pattern's signal becomes the trigger for the antidote.

- **When the person reports the opening of the heart increased the wonder for the other person:** stay with that. The wonder is the rest of the architecture. See [[the-desire-to-be-seen]] for why the bidirectional mechanism (seeing them lets you be seen) is the deeper move.

## The Mechanism

The mechanism has three components that work together:

**First, the disconnection diagnostic.** Before the move can be done, the person has to see that the fear-of-being-seen state is already a disconnection. They are not protecting themselves against the other person; they have already left the field of contact with the other person. They are over here, in their head, running the Eye of Sauron. The other person is over there, being imagined as the threat. The "connection" never started.

This is the part of the move that is most counterintuitive. The person believes they are in contact with the other and trying to manage how the contact is going. The diagnostic says: there is no contact. There is only management. The move is to start the contact.

**Second, the relocation of attention.** Opening the heart to the other moves attention from "what are they thinking about me?" to "what is happening with them?" The same attentional bandwidth that was running the worry is now running the connection. The slot is occupied. The Eye of Sauron has no fuel.

This is structurally the same mechanism as [[how-do-i-do-this-with-an-open-heart]] for imposter syndrome. The redirect from self-focus to other-focus is what dissolves the doubt loop. The fear-of-being-seen version is the relational specialization.

**Third, the connection-as-safety inversion.** The default protective frame says: close to stay safe. The move says: open to stay safe. The opened heart is harder to seduce, harder to capture, harder to hurt in the way the fear is anticipating, because the system is no longer running the surface that the hurt was supposed to land on. See [[pour-the-love-at-the-threat]] for the more extreme version of the same inversion applied to narcissism.

## What This Move Is Not

- **Not "be nice to them."** Niceness is a social performance. The move is an internal reorientation. The person can be perfectly nice while running the Eye of Sauron full blast. The move is what changes underneath.
- **Not forgiveness or compassion for past harms.** The move is in this present moment, toward this present person, regardless of history. Forgiveness work is a different thing. See [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]].
- **Not a boundary-collapse.** The move is fully compatible with boundaries. "It doesn't mean I'm going to take abuse." The opened heart can still say no.
- **Not "let them in" or "trust them."** The move is what you do, not what you grant them. It does not require any change on their part.
- **Not a thinking move.** The move is somatic. The instruction is to open the heart, not to think positive thoughts about the person.

## The Companion Move: Open the Heart to Yourself

The same source episode gives two parallel moves: open your heart to the other person, and open your heart to yourself. The two are alternatives, not opposites; either one breaks the Eye of Sauron, and each is the better choice in different configurations.

The other-directed move is the better choice when the person is in the room with the other and freezing. The self-directed move is the better choice when the person is alone with their inner critic or organizing their life around the imagined judgment of others.

The self-directed version is named separately in [[open-the-heart-to-yourself]] and uses the question "what is it that I need?" as its operational form. The other-directed version is this move.

Both move attention out of the Eye of Sauron and into a warmer place. The choice between them is which warm place is most accessible in the moment.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "What do I do? Next time I'm in a meeting and I freeze, what's a thing?"
- **Joe:** "Open your heart to that person. Stop objectifying them. Recognize that you've already cut yourself off from them, you're judging them. You're basically saying in that moment: you're going to attack me, you're a bad person, you only care about yourself, you can't see me, and you're totally self-obsessed and focused on you. That's what you're saying about the other person. So you've already, like, fully disconnected and you're worried about what they're thinking about you. But you're basically thinking all those things about them."
- **Person:** [pause, recognition]
- **Joe:** "And look — they don't seem to give a shit."
- *(Source: Fear of Being Seen)*

The compressed exchange shows the structure of the teaching: name what the person is unconsciously doing (the projected judgment), make the symmetry visible (you are doing to them what you fear they will do to you), and offer the reversal (open your heart instead). The slight irreverence ("they don't seem to give a shit") punctures the seriousness with which the person has been treating the imagined judgment while honoring the realness of the freezing.

## Tone

Practical, almost matter-of-fact. The move is not delivered as a spiritual instruction. It is delivered as a concrete intervention for a specific failure mode. The framing tends to be operational: "next time you freeze, this is the thing." The person does not need to believe in the move beforehand; they need to try it in the moment and report what happens.

Joe sometimes pairs the instruction with a live demonstration: "anybody who's listening to this right now can just do it." The live test is part of the teaching — the move is verifiable in real time, not a thing the person has to take on faith.
