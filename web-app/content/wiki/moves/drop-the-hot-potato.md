# Drop the Hot Potato

## The Move

When a [[shame-hot-potato]] is running, the move that ends it is to stop throwing it back. Receive the throw. Feel the shame the throw was intended to install. Process what is underneath. Then listen to the other person, ask actual questions, and stay impartial.

The dynamic requires both throwers. Removing one stops the game.

"Drop the hot potato. Feel the shame, process the emotions that are underneath it and listen to the person and see what's true for them and what's going on. And ask view questions and be impartial, like that can solve a hot potato inside of an organization or a marriage all the time." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

## The Four Components

The move is one action with four named components, all of which are required.

**1. Feel the shame.** The throw was designed to install shame. Let it land. Do not deflect with defense, counter-attack, or argument. The shame is the material to work with. Without feeling it, no other component lands.

**2. Process the emotions underneath.** Shame is a [[blocker-emotions]] layer. Under it: grief, anger, helplessness, hurt. The processing is what releases the stagnation. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

**3. Listen for what is true for them.** The other person has a truth. From inside the hot potato, only their attack is visible. From outside, their fear, their hurt, their version of the events are visible. The shift to actual listening is what reveals the truth.

**4. Ask VIEW questions, be impartial.** [[how-what-questions]] from a stance of [[impartiality]]. No agenda to win, no agenda to fix. Open-ended exploration of what is actually happening. See [[view]] for the framework.

The four happen roughly sequentially but compress in practice. The whole thing can occupy thirty seconds in a conversation. The result, when it works, is that the conflict has nothing left to feed on.

## Why One Person Is Enough

The hot potato requires both people to throw. Each throw makes the other person want to throw back. If one person stops throwing, the other person's throws have nowhere to land. The game stops being a game.

"In a political conversation or a business conversation, if the shame hot potato is going back and forth, it only requires one person to stop it." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

The other person may continue throwing for a while. The momentum can take time to dissipate. But without the return throw, the dynamic runs out of energy.

## What This Move Is Not

**It is not capitulation.** Dropping the hot potato does not mean agreeing that you were wrong. It does not mean accepting the other person's frame. It means stopping the shame-installation game. The truth of what happened can still be examined, just not in the shame frame.

**It is not silent submission.** Listening, asking questions, being impartial — these are active stances, not the absence of stance. The person dropping the hot potato is doing more, not less. They are doing the work that the throwing was substituting for.

**It is not bypass.** The shame is felt, not skipped. The grief or anger or helplessness underneath is processed, not numbed. The move is harder than throwing, not easier.

**It is not a manipulation tactic.** If "drop the hot potato" is used as a technique to win the conflict, it becomes another throw. The move only works when the person doing it is actually in the stance described, not performing it.

## The Marriage Version Requires Both

Joe makes a sharp distinction for marriages: the move works in workplace and political contexts unilaterally, but a marriage where only one partner can do this is not a marriage Joe would recommend entering. The whole point of marriage in Joe's frame is shared growth. A marriage where the hot potato runs forever because the other partner cannot meet the work is a marriage stuck in the dynamic for the life of the relationship.

"In a marriage, you don't — I wouldn't suggest being married to somebody who can't get there. Like, I think the whole thing about marriage is wanting to grow and learn together." -- Joe Hudson, The Shame Hot Potato

This is not a recommendation to leave. It is a recommendation to look honestly at whether both people are willing to do this work. See [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]].

## The "Seen In An Hour" Lever

A specific in-the-moment formulation that makes the move accessible when the urgency to be seen is high.

"If you can drop that experience right now, just for a short period of time and say, 'I don't need to be seen at this moment, I can be seen in an hour. But in this moment, I don't need to defend anything. I can just actually listen and find out what their reality is and see that truth.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The lever works because most of the felt urgency in the moment is "I need to be seen right now or I never will be." The temporal release of the urgency — "I can be seen in an hour" — does not require giving up on being seen, only on being seen in this minute. The body can usually metabolize a one-hour wait. It cannot metabolize a permanent loss.

The paradox: holding tightly to being-seen-right-now pushes the seeing away. Releasing the urgency creates the conditions in which the other person, having felt heard, often starts wanting to see you. See [[i-dont-need-to-be-seen-right-now]] for the full move and [[ill-be-seen-in-an-hour-not-now]] for the variant phrasing.

"When they feel heard, then oftentimes they're at that point wanting to see you. And so if you hold on tightly to trying to be seen, you're going to push being seen away." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

## When the Other Person Cannot Receive It

A common failure mode: the move is attempted with someone who is currently incapable of seeing. The drop happens. Nothing comes back. The practitioner concludes the move did not work.

The reframe: when the other person cannot see you, the move shifts. The drop still happens. The listening still happens. But the path to peace is no longer through being seen by them — it is through seeing yourself.

"If somebody else can't see you and you recognize that, like, they're not capable of seeing me right now, the only option for you to get to peace is to see yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

This is the variation of the move for the case where the other party is too triggered, too defended, or too incapacitated to do the corresponding work. See [[see-yourself-when-they-cant-see-you]] for the full move. The core insight: the move does not require the other person to do anything for it to work. It works on the practitioner's nervous system first. What the other person does is downstream.

## What Tends to Happen

When this move actually lands, the conflict deflates with surprising speed. The other person may continue throwing for a beat or two. Then their throws stop hitting anything and the throwing slows. The conversation can become an actual conversation. The thing that the fight was supposedly about can be examined.

In a team setting, a leader who drops the hot potato by taking responsibility without taking shame ("we're not going to spend time figuring out who's to blame; we're going to figure out how to make sure this doesn't happen again") often shifts the whole team's relationship to the issue. See [[fault-vs-responsibility]].

In a marriage, the first time this lands cleanly, the partner often experiences a noticeable change in the emotional weather. The same conversation, without the hot potato, often resolves quickly.

## What Tone Is Required

The move requires a particular felt-stance that cannot be faked.

- **Non-defended.** Not braced. Not in counter-attack mode. The body is open. See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]].
- **Genuinely curious about what is true for them.** Not performing interest. Actually wondering what they see.
- **Impartial about the outcome.** Not trying to win, not trying to lose, not trying to make them feel anything in particular.
- **Willing to feel shame.** The shame the throw installed is going to arrive. The capacity to be with it is the precondition.

If any of these is missing, the move slides into something else. Performed listening with internal counter-argument is still a throw. Asking questions to corner them is still a throw. The stance is the move.

## In Coaching

When a coachee describes a recurring fight, the move is to recognize the [[shame-hot-potato]] structure and introduce the option of dropping it. The coachee usually has years of practice at the throwing form and no practice at this form. The coaching may need to work through:

- What the coachee is throwing (often it does not feel like throwing to them — it feels like defense, or like reasonable disagreement)
- What shame the coachee is unwilling to feel
- What emotion is underneath the shame that the throwing is preventing
- What the other person's truth might actually be

The move may need to be rehearsed in coaching before it can be deployed in life. See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]] and related practices for how the rehearsal can happen.

## When to Use This Move

- Any conflict diagnosed as a [[shame-hot-potato]]
- After a coachee recognizes that the fight has the same shape every time
- When defensiveness is the dominant signal in a relationship dynamic
- In team contexts where blame is doing the work that problem-solving should be doing
- In an internal conflict where two parts of the self are throwing shame at each other (the move can be applied to the internal hot potato as well)

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A coachee describes a recurring fight or chronic team conflict
- A coachee names blame as the active dynamic
- A coachee reports defensiveness in self or other
- A coachee is in a political or societal conflict and is suffering from it
- A coachee has tried "winning" the argument and finds the relief does not last

## Tone

Calm, grounded, almost light. The move is not heavy. It is the simple act of not playing a game that nobody actually wants to play. Dropping the potato is, in the felt sense, a relief.
