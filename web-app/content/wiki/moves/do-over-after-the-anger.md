# The Do-Over After the Anger

## The Move

When something happens between you and your kid (or partner) that triggers a hit of anger, the move has three parts in sequence:

1. **Pause.** Hold up a finger, name what is happening: "Hold on one second."
2. **Move the body charge.** Step aside briefly and let the anger sequence through. Jump, shake, yell, press the wall, whatever the body wants. Sometimes thirty seconds is enough.
3. **Come back and propose the redo.** "Okay, let's try that again. That really hurt. I know you didn't mean to hurt me. Let's make eye contact before we throw things to each other in the kitchen."

The do-over is not an apology and it is not a punishment. It is a reset that names what just happened, names what the request is going forward, and re-enters the interaction from clarity rather than charge.

## Tara's Example (Esme and the Throwing in the Kitchen)

The canonical telling comes from Tara about a moment cooking with her daughter Esme, who threw something that hit Tara unexpectedly in the face.

"I got really triggered and I got really angry. And I knew it was an accident logically, but I was like, I had heat in my body. And I was like, 'Hold on one second. I just have to do a little dance.' And I — ah! Woo-wee-ah! Finally, I'm like, motherfucker, motherfucker, that hurt... I could come back and say, okay, let's try that again. Like, that really hurt. I know you didn't mean to hurt me. Let's make eye contact before we throw things to each other in the kitchen. So I could — I could not respond from anger." -- Tara Hudson, The Path To Healthy Fighting

The structure is visible in the example:
- The pause is "hold on one second."
- The discharge is the dance, the cursing, the body movement (private enough that the kid is not in the middle of it).
- The do-over is "let's try that again" + naming the hurt + acknowledging the kid did not mean to + a clear request for what to do differently next time.

## What Each Part Does

**The pause.** The pause buys the body the moment it needs to not respond from the triggered state. Without the pause, the next thing that comes out is whatever the trigger produces, which is almost always damaging. The pause is the choice point. See [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]] for what becomes available only after the body has moved through.

**The discharge.** The body has a charge in it. The charge does not go away on its own in the time the interaction has. Either the charge moves (privately, quickly), or it goes into whatever gets said next. The discharge is the alternative to the leak. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the longer-form version and [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]] for what happens without it.

**The do-over.** The do-over re-enters the interaction without the charge. The request is clean. The acknowledgment is clean. The interaction can actually take a different shape now because the body is not still in the original hit.

## Why "Let's Try That Again" and Not Just "Don't Do That"

The do-over frame is different from a correction. A correction stops at "don't do that." The do-over rewinds the interaction and replays it the way it could have gone. The kid gets to participate in the replay, not just receive a verdict.

The specific phrasing "let's try that again" makes the kid a partner in the redo, not a recipient of a rule. They see what to do, not just what not to do. They get to do the new shape of the interaction with the parent in real time, which is how the new shape actually gets installed in the body. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]].

## The Move Inside the Pancake Story

Joe's version of the same move appears in the pancake story (see [[joes-pancake-anger-story]]). The discharge — "I'm angry, I'm angry, I'm angry, I'm angry" with jumping — is followed by the clean request: "Hey, can you let me focus for a minute until breakfast is ready?" The request is the do-over. The interaction restarts, this time with the actual ask the situation needed.

The two examples are structurally identical:
- Trigger → pause → body discharge → clean request that asks for what the situation actually needs.

Both examples also illustrate the principle in [[emotions-are-neutral-bad-because-of-use]]: the anger does not have to be a weapon at the kid. It can move through the parent's body and then the parent can come back to make a clean ask.

## What This Move Requires

The move only works if the discharge step actually happens. If the parent skips the discharge and tries to do the do-over while still charged, the do-over carries the charge in the tone. The kid feels the charge regardless of the words. The interaction restarts with the same energy that caused the problem.

This is why the practice of solo discharge (see [[get-angry-in-the-car]]) is upstream of this move. If the parent has no regular practice of moving anger privately, the in-the-moment discharge often does not complete because the held charge in the system is larger than the immediate hit. The bigger backlog comes up and there is no time inside the immediate interaction to move all of it. See [[initial-anger-release-wave]] for what to expect when the backlog starts surfacing.

## With Adult Partners

The same structure works between adults. The hit happens. One person says "hold on, I need a minute" — see [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]]. They go discharge. They come back. They name what happened and propose the redo: "I want to try this conversation again. What I actually want to ask is..."

The phrasing in the adult version is less likely to be "let's try that again" (which can sound infantilizing to a partner) and more likely to be "can we restart that conversation?" or "I want to come back to that with a different tone." The structure is the same; the language adapts to the relationship.

## Why This Move Matters in Parenting

Without the do-over, the choices in the moment of trigger are:
- React from the anger (which damages the kid and the relationship).
- Suppress the anger (which produces [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]] later, often at the kid in a delayed form).
- Walk away with no return (which can land as abandonment and leaves the original interaction broken).

The do-over is the fourth option. It does not require the parent to not have anger; it does not require the parent to perform calm; it does not require the parent to leave the relationship intact through suppression. It uses the anger by moving it through the body, then it uses the clarity that follows to make the interaction the one it needed to be.

## The Kid Learns Several Things at Once

When the parent does the do-over, the kid receives several lessons in one short sequence:
- Anger is a thing that moves through the body, not a thing that gets done to me.
- The parent can be triggered without the relationship being threatened.
- There is a way to come back after a hard moment.
- I am not the cause of the anger and I am not responsible for managing it.
- The interaction can be re-done. Mistakes are repairable.

These lessons are structural. The kid does not learn them by being told; the kid learns them by watching the parent enact them. See [[model-emotional-movement-for-your-kids]].

## Tara's Origin: The Patty Wipfler Pre-Announcement

The do-over move is downstream of a related practice Tara learned from Hand in Hand Parenting (specifically the founder Patty Wipfler — see [[hand-in-hand-parenting]]). The pre-announcement to the kid sets up the do-over so that when it happens, the kid is not surprised by the parent's discharge.

"I remember saying it to Esme like, 'Oh, if I ever get angry it's nothing to do with you; it's my job and this is how I'm gonna take care of it. I'm gonna go into our bedroom and I'm gonna press on this wall and you might hear me make funny sounds like wah-wah or argh.'" -- Tara Hudson, The Path To Healthy Fighting

The pre-announcement is the upstream piece that makes the in-the-moment discharge readable to the kid. Without the pre-announcement, the kid sees the parent step away and discharge and may interpret it as scary, as their fault, as abandonment. With the pre-announcement, the kid sees the parent step away and recognizes "this is the thing they told me about; this is how they take care of their anger; this is what care looks like."

Tara reports: "I remember doing that one or two or three times and like just, parenting got a lot easier after that. Before that, I found myself losing my temper, having like a short fuse with the kids... And when I learned that hack that I could go and move anger, then it was like, I'd actually do that once and I'd be settled for a week. Like untriggered, unfazed, like things could get thrown at me and I'd be like, 'Okay, that didn't work. Let's try a do-over.'" -- Tara Hudson, The Path To Healthy Fighting

The frequency note is instructive: the regular discharge meant the in-the-moment hits did not accumulate. The do-over was available because there was no backlog charging each incident with more than its own weight.

## Tone

The do-over is delivered light. Not heavy. Not therapeutic. Not an event. "Let's try that again" — said with warmth, no lecture, no extended explanation. The lightness is what makes the move repeatable. A heavy do-over teaches the kid that this is a serious moment of correction. A light do-over teaches the kid that interactions can be redone, mistakes are okay, the relationship is robust.
