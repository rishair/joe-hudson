# Mirror Back the Recursion of the Fix Question

## The Move

When a person asks "how do I stop X?" where X is itself a pattern of self-pressure or self-management, do not answer the question. Restate the question in a form that makes its recursive structure audible.

The form: "Let me share with you how my mind works. Here's how my mind works. My problem is that I [X] and I [oppose-X-by-doing-X-again]. So how do I [X] to stop [X-ing] to [oppose-X]? That's what I hear."

The move converts the person's question into a sentence the person cannot ignore. The recursion is now in words. The person can hear the loop they have been inside of for years. The loop's authority over them depends on its invisibility. Once the loop is audible, the loop loses its grip.

This is the live, in-session move that operationalizes [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]]. The fix-trap principle says: don't answer the how-do-I-stop question. This move is the affirmative replacement — what to do instead of answering.

## When Joe Uses It

When a person presents a long-running pattern of self-pressure or self-management and asks how to stop. The signal is:

- The presenting question is "how do I stop?" or "how do I get out of this bind?"
- The pattern named has two opposing halves (pressure-rebellion, push-collapse, force-give-up) that are both running inside the person
- The pattern has resisted previous attempts to change it, often for years
- The asking has a quality of "tell me what to do" that the body subtly tightens around even as it asks

The move is most useful at the opening of a session, in the first two or three exchanges, before the person has gotten further into their analysis of the pattern. Once the analysis is running, the recursion is less visible because the person has rehearsed the framing many times.

## What This Move Produces

The most common response is a one-word "Yeah" with a small laugh or breath out. The body recognizes the recursion before the head finishes processing the words. The recognition is itself a small exit from the loop.

The next move is the inversion question: "How do you think that's going to work?" The person says some version of "not really, not going to work." The "not going to work" is the person dropping the question. The dropping is what makes the next move possible.

If the person tries to defend the question ("but I really do want to know"), the move has not landed yet. Joe will often repeat the recursion in a slightly different form, or stay silent and let the recursive sentence keep working on the body.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, the woman opens with "I put pressure on myself and rebel against it. How do I stop? How do I get out of this bind?" Joe's move:

- **Joe:** "I want to share with you how my mind works. Here's how my mind works. My problem is that I put pressure on myself and I rebel against myself. So how do I put pressure on myself to stop putting pressure on myself to rebel against myself. That's what I hear."
- **Client:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. How do you think that's going to work?"
- **Client:** "Uh, not really. Not going to work."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Then what's the solution? If you're pushing against the wall, how do you stop?"
- **Client:** "Let go. Stop."
- *(Source: The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself)*

The move took one exchange. The recursion was named, the person heard it, the question dissolved. The "how do you stop pushing against a wall" inversion follows immediately as the door to the next part of the work.

The conversational form Joe uses ("I want to share with you how my mind works") is friendly. The recursion is not delivered as a gotcha. It is delivered as: "I think this is what I'm hearing inside your question, want to see it together?" The framing keeps the person from feeling caught and lets them join in the seeing.

## Why It Works

The fix-question has structural authority because the person believes it is a real question with a real answer they have not yet found. The belief depends on the recursion being invisible. The person is inside the loop and inside the question; from inside both, the question looks like a path to outside.

Stating the recursion places the person briefly outside it. The sentence "how do I X to stop X-ing to oppose X" cannot be said inside the loop. Once said, the question is exposed as the loop talking. The person now sees they have been inside a loop, not on a path.

The seeing produces a small movement: the body stops bracing for the answer it was demanding. The bracing was part of the pressure. The pressure has lost a small amount of fuel. The session now has room to ask a different question.

The move also models the operation. The coach is not pressuring the client. The coach is reflecting. The reflection is itself an example of the soft response to a pressure-question. The client gets a felt sense of what the response looks like in someone else, which gives them a template for finding it in themselves.

## What Joe Does Right After This Move

The mirror is the opening. The follow-up is critical. Two things typically happen next:

1. **"How do you think that's going to work?"** This converts the recognition into a felt question. The person consults their experience, finds the answer is "it doesn't," and the question is dropped not as a defeat but as a release.

2. **"If you're pushing against the wall, how do you stop?"** This is the inversion to the opposite operation. The person finds the word — "let go," "soften," "stop." The found word is the next door. The next door opens onto [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-you-soften]].

The two follow-ups together pivot the session from analysis-of-the-pattern to felt-exit-from-the-pattern in under a minute. The speed is part of the move's effectiveness — the loop does not get a chance to reorganize.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not deliver the mirror as a gotcha.** "See, you're doing the pattern right now" sounds like the pressure the client has been living under. The framing has to be collaborative — "here's what I'm hearing" — not accusatory.
- **Do not over-explain the recursion after stating it.** The sentence does its own work. Adding "and that's the trap" or "so you can see why that doesn't work" interferes with the body's recognition.
- **Do not skip this move and go straight to "let's feel what's underneath."** The fix-question has to be dissolved before the underneath becomes accessible. If the question is still running, the underneath is being held away by the question.
- **Do not require the client to articulate the recursion themselves.** Some coaches will ask "do you hear the loop in your own question?" That puts the client back in the head. The coach states the recursion; the client just has to hear it.
- **Do not relent and answer the original question if the client presses.** "Yeah, but really, how do I stop?" is the pattern intensifying. Re-mirror it: "How do I stop the thing that has me pressing for the answer about how to stop?"

## Variations

- **For long-running addictive patterns:** "So I keep doing X, and I keep beating myself up for doing X, and now I want to know how to beat myself into not doing X. Is that what I'm hearing?"
- **For chronic self-judgment:** "So I judge myself, and I judge myself for judging myself, and you want to know how to judge yourself into not judging yourself?"
- **For chronic fixing:** see [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]] — "how do I stop fixing" is the question, mirrored as "how do I fix the fixing?"
- **For chronic people-pleasing:** "So I please everyone, and I'm exhausted, and now I want to please you by figuring out how to please everyone less. Is that what we're doing?"

The structural form is always the same: take the verb of the pattern, restate the request using the same verb, and let the recursion be audible.

## Tone

Warm, light, slightly playful. The "let me share with you how my mind works" carries some self-deprecation — the coach is including themselves in the recursion rather than pointing at the client. The smile is in the voice. The mirror lands soft, not sharp.

Joe often slightly speeds up the recursive sentence so it lands as a tongue-twister. The tongue-twister quality is part of the effect — the sentence is hard to follow, which makes the loop's absurdity audible.

The follow-up "how do you think that's going to work?" is delivered without sarcasm. It is a real question. The client's answer matters; the laughter that often comes with it is the body's recognition, not the coach's mockery.
