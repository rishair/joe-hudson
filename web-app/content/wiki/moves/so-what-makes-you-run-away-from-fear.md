# So What Makes You Run Away From Fear?

## The Move

When the person has just surfaced or admitted to being scared, do not move toward strategies for resolving the fear. Move toward the avoidance itself. Ask: "What makes you run away from fear?"

The question shifts the inquiry from the content of the fear ("what are you afraid of?") to the relationship with the fear ("why are you treating it as something to flee?"). This is a different conversation. It puts the fear-flight pattern itself on the table.

"So what makes you run away from fear?"

Variations:
- "What makes you run away from it?"
- "What's the move you make when fear shows up?"
- "Why do you treat fear as the thing to avoid?"
- "What makes fear unwelcome in your system?"

## When To Use It

The person has admitted to being afraid. They have not yet stopped running from the fear. They are still in the position of "fear is bad and I need to figure out how to stop being afraid."

Use the move when:
- The person describes contradictory fears (scared either way) and is asking for a strategy to escape.
- The person has just named fear as the operating force and is reaching for a way out.
- You want to interrupt the assumption that fear is a problem to solve rather than a feeling to feel.
- The avoidance pattern needs to become visible before the feeling work can happen.

Do not use the move when:
- The person is already in contact with the fear and feeling it. The question would interrupt the feeling.
- The person is genuinely in danger and fleeing is the appropriate response.
- The person cannot yet identify that fear is what they are running from.

## What It Produces

The question often produces a pause. The person has not been asked this before. They have been asked what they are afraid of, but they have not been asked why they are running from it.

Common responses:
- A description of the imagined consequences of feeling fear ("if I feel it, I'll fall apart").
- An identification of an inherited message about fear ("I was taught fear is weakness").
- A recognition that they don't know — they just run.
- A sudden contact with the fear itself, which arrives the moment the running is named.

The fourth response is the most common and the most useful. The act of asking why they run from fear often dissolves the running, because the running has been operating below awareness. Once named, it loses some of its automaticity.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person describes the imagined consequences, Joe often invites them to test the consequences by actually feeling the fear right now. "Feel the fear right now in your system" — see [[name-the-fear-loop-directly]]. The imagined consequence is usually worse than the actual experience.

If the person identifies an inherited message, Joe often goes to the source ("where did you learn to turn your heartbreak into fear?"). This opens the childhood imprinting layer. See [[childhood-imprinting]].

If the person says they don't know, Joe stays with the not-knowing. The not-knowing is data. It often produces a body shift — the eyes change, the breath shifts — and the fear becomes accessible.

If the person makes contact with the fear, the move has already worked. Stay with the fear. The next moves are about being in it, not about analyzing the running anymore.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** (after Joe has named the fear loop) "Yeah, yeah."
- **Joe:** "So what makes you run away from fear? Like, feel the fear right now in your system. Oh wow, did you — what you — yeah, what just happened there? I saw sadness. Was that sadness that I saw?"
- **Person:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "All this fear to stop the heartbreak. What is it like to just invite that heartbreak? It's such a wonderful thing."
- *(Source: How Do I Live Up To My Potential?)*

The question "what makes you run away from fear" was paired with an immediate invitation to feel the fear in the body. The combination produced an instant body shift — sadness arrived in the place fear had been. Joe named the sadness, and then named what the running was protecting against: the heartbreak.

The move's power is in this conjunction. Asking about the running while inviting the feeling produces what asking alone often doesn't.

## Tone

Curious, level, not accusatory. The question is genuinely curious — what does make you run? — not rhetorical. The energy is "I see you running, I'm interested in why, let's look at it together."

A wrong tone makes the question sound like an accusation ("why are you avoiding your feelings?"). The right tone is open inquiry. The person is more likely to look at the running honestly when the looking is not framed as fault-finding.

## Why This Works

Most fear-coaching focuses on the content of fear: what are you afraid of, why are you afraid of it, what would happen if the feared thing happened. This is the right work sometimes. It is not the work when the person is not yet willing to feel the fear at all.

A person who is running from fear cannot do useful content work on the fear, because they are not in contact with it. The work of asking "what are you afraid of" produces stories about fear, not contact with fear. The stories are made up by the part of the system that is fleeing.

The move "what makes you run away from fear" interrupts the fleeing by making it visible. Once visible, it has less authority. The person can begin to relate to the running as a choice rather than as a fact. From there, the next choice — to stop running and feel — becomes available.

## The Deeper Layer: What the Running Is Protecting

Often the running from fear is itself protecting against something deeper — usually heartbreak, sadness, or shame. The fear is the surface emotion. The deeper emotion underneath is what the running is actually managing.

When Joe noticed the sadness arrive the moment fear was invited, he named the structure: "all this fear to stop the heartbreak." The fear was not the primary thing. It was the agent protecting against the heartbreak. The running from fear was, more deeply, running from the heartbreak.

This is the structure described in [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] applied to fear: fear is often the cover, and beneath it is the more vulnerable emotion. The move "what makes you run from fear" opens the door not just to fear but to whatever fear is hiding.
