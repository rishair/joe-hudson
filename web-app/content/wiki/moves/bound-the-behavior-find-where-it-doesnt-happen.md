# Bound the Behavior — Find Where It Doesn't Happen

## The Move

When someone presents a behavior they want to stop — procrastinating, judging, criticizing, scrolling, eating, drinking — the reflex move for most coaches is to ask about the behavior. When does it happen, why does it happen, what makes you do it.

The contrasting move: ask where the behavior does NOT happen. Find the domains where the person is functional, free, or unaffected. The contrast between the affected and unaffected domains is almost always more diagnostic than any depth-probing of the affected domain.

> "You procrastinate a lot on what? Because there's, there's shit you don't procrastinate on. For instance, you didn't procrastinate on this call, putting your shirt on in the morning. So what is it that you procrastinate on?" -- Joe Hudson, Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes

The move is short. Two questions. But the questions reframe the entire conversation: the behavior is not a global character trait. It is bounded to specific domains. The boundary is the data.

## When to Use It

- The person presents a behavior as a global pattern ("I procrastinate a lot," "I'm so judgmental," "I always overeat")
- The presentation carries a self-character framing ("I'm just the kind of person who...")
- The behavior is described without specific domain attached
- The person seems to have given up on changing the behavior, treating it as inherent
- The behavior has run for a long time and the person has tried many things at the behavior level
- A discipline framing or willpower framing is on the table

The move is especially useful at the opening of a session. It immediately repositions the conversation from character-level work (which often becomes recursive shame) to structural-level work (which is actionable).

## What It Produces

The first response is often surprise or mild defensiveness. The person had not framed the behavior as bounded. The framing "I procrastinate" felt complete. Asking "what do you not procrastinate on" disrupts the completeness.

After the surprise, the person usually produces a quick answer. They can name what they do not procrastinate on faster than they can analyze why they procrastinate when they do. The answer is data the person had access to but had not collected.

Three things tend to follow:

1. **The boundary surfaces.** "I don't procrastinate on work for clients. I don't procrastinate on tasks for my kids. Just on my own stuff." The boundary turns the diagnostic into a contrast.

2. **The mechanism becomes visible.** Why is the behavior present here and not there? The answer is usually visible once the contrast is named. In the website case, the procrastination happened only at the intersection of self-care and money — the most loaded domain in the man's life.

3. **The character framing dissolves.** The person sees they are not "a procrastinator" globally. They are someone who procrastinates on a specific category. This shifts the work from "fix yourself" to "find what is happening in this specific category."

## How Joe Sequences It

The move usually opens with a question framed as factual interest in specificity, not as a diagnostic challenge:

- "You procrastinate on what?"
- "What are you procrastinating on right now?"
- "Tell me a specific case."

If the person stays general, the move sharpens:

- "But not on everything?"
- "What do you not procrastinate on?"
- "You didn't procrastinate on [this specific thing right now], so where does it happen and where doesn't it?"

The naming of a current counter-example matters. The person is in front of you, currently engaged with the session. They did not procrastinate on coming. That is data. Naming the data makes the boundedness undeniable.

Then the move drives to the boundary:

- "So the only place this happens is X?"
- "So the procrastination is at the confluence of [Y and Z]?"
- "Is that accurate?"

The synthesizing question turns the contrast into a named domain. Once the domain is named, the coaching can move to what is specific about that domain.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I procrastinate a lot."
- **Joe:** "You procrastinate on what?"
- **Person:** "Um, work stuff."
- **Joe:** "Again, so not all your work stuff?"
- **Person:** "No."
- **Joe:** "So what's the stuff that you procrastinate on in work?"
- **Person:** "Well, you could call it side hustle, side project, something of my own."
- **Joe:** "So it's the confluence of something that takes care of yourself and money where you procrastinate."
- **Person:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "Taking care of somebody else and money — do you procrastinate?"
- **Person:** "No, I do what I need to do for sure."
- **Joe:** "So the only place you're procrastinating is where money and taking care of yourself happens? Is that accurate?"
- **Person:** "Yes."
- *(Source: Watch Him Dissolve Procrastination In 11 Minutes)*

The opening moves are all bounding. Joe is not yet investigating the procrastination. He is making the procrastination's domain visible. By the time he is six turns in, the domain has been precisely identified as "self-care and money," and everything that follows happens in that named domain.

## Why This Beats Asking About the Behavior Directly

Asking "why do you procrastinate?" pulls the person into the felt territory of the behavior. They produce explanations — fear, perfectionism, low motivation, ADHD, etc. — that may or may not be accurate but are all in the language of the person's existing self-story. The conversation tends to reinforce the self-story.

Asking "where don't you procrastinate?" pulls the person outside the behavior. They have to look at their life from a different angle. The angle reveals structure they had not seen. The structure is the actual data; the self-story is downstream of the structure.

The move also short-circuits one of the most common defenses: the global self-character claim. "I'm just a procrastinator" cannot survive a serious answer to "what don't you procrastinate on?" The claim collapses into the bounded reality, and the bounded reality is where the coaching can do work.

## What This Move Does Not Do

It does not invalidate the person's experience of the behavior. The procrastination is real; the suffering is real. The move just locates the behavior precisely instead of letting it operate as a global verdict.

It also does not always work. Some behaviors are genuinely global, and the answer to "where doesn't this happen?" is "nowhere." In those cases, the move surfaces a different reality — that the person is dealing with a generalized pattern, often the [[how-do-i-stop-watching-porn-and-procrastinating]] essential-self-avoidance cascade. The move is diagnostic regardless of the answer. The answer determines the path.

A useful diagnostic table:

- **Behavior is bounded to specific high-stakes domain** → [[the-perfectionism-that-stops-you-from-starting]] read, [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] mechanism
- **Behavior is global across many domains** → [[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]] cascade, [[how-do-i-stop-watching-porn-and-procrastinating]] path
- **Behavior is bounded but to a domain involving authority or approval** → [[confidence-only-where-approval-isnt-needed]] read

## Variants for Other Behaviors

The move generalizes beyond procrastination:

- For judgment: "Who don't you judge?" "Are there people you find yourself not judging?"
- For overeating: "When do you not overeat?" "What meals don't this happen at?"
- For criticism: "Who do you not criticize?" "What domains of work don't you criticize yourself in?"
- For scrolling: "When do you not scroll?" "What activities completely prevent the scroll?"
- For anger: "Where doesn't this anger arise?" "Who don't you get angry at?"

The pattern is the same. Find where it doesn't happen, contrast with where it does, name the boundary, find what is structurally different about the bounded domain.

## Tone

Curious and matter-of-fact. The move is not delivered as a gotcha or a challenge to the person's framing. It is delivered as genuine investigative interest. Joe is not arguing that the person is wrong to think they procrastinate. He is interested in the specifics, because the specifics will tell both of them something.

The lack of dramatic delivery is itself part of the move. The person is often expecting a heavier engagement with their problem. Getting a series of clarifying questions instead is its own message — the problem is more bounded than the person had treated it as. The treatment can be lighter than they had imagined.
