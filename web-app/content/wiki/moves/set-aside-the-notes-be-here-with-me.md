# Set Aside the Notes and Be Here With Me

## The Move

When the person opens the session with a prepared question, reads from notes, or is visibly taking notes during the call, ask them — as a favor, in the first or second turn — to set the preparation aside and just be here.

The move has three components delivered in one breath:
1. A frame that justifies setting the notes down without confrontation ("this will be recorded").
2. A favor-frame that softens the request ("can you do me a favor").
3. An explicit statement of the alternative posture ("just be fully here with me").

"This will be recorded, so can you do me a favor and not take any notes and just be fully here with me?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The move is the first instance of the work the session will do. The person who came in with a fixing or controlling pattern is being asked to drop the fixing/controlling stance in the first sentence. The rest of the session will repeatedly return to this same operation in different forms. The opening sets the frame.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person reads or delivers a clearly rehearsed opening statement.
- When the person says they have been taking notes during the call to formulate the question.
- When the person says they have been "figuring out how to ask this" or "working out how to describe it."
- When the person has a pen and paper visible and is actively writing while talking.
- When the presentation is too polished — too complete, too compressed, too articulate — for real-time speech.
- When the question is delivered with eyes on a page instead of on the coach.

See [[reading-from-notes-or-script]] for the broader read this move serves. The move is the response to that read in its specific form (notes are physically present); when the preparation is internal rather than written, the move shifts to refusing the intellectual frame more broadly.

## What It Produces

The person sets the notes down. The energy in the room shifts. The shift is small but real — the person is now present in a way they were not 10 seconds ago. The protection that the preparation was providing has been removed.

Often the person becomes slightly unsteady. The preparation was scaffolding. Without it, they have to find the question fresh, in real time. The unsteadiness is the work beginning. Joe receives the unsteadiness as the entry point, not as a problem.

The session now has air. The conversation can move in directions the preparation had foreclosed. The coach can respond to what is actually happening rather than to what the person had planned to present.

## What Joe Does Next

The immediate follow-up is usually a compression request: take the prepared formulation and put it in one sentence. "If you put it in a sentence, what would you say?" The compression continues the same operation. The over-prepared question has too many parts; the single sentence forces the person to find the one thing that actually matters.

Once the single sentence is delivered, Joe responds to that. The session is now in real time. The work begins from there.

If the person continues to deliver pre-formulated content even with the notes set down, the move has not fully landed. The repetition of the request can be done lightly: "stay with me." Or the move can pivot to an explicit naming of the pattern as it is now showing up in real time. See [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]].

## Why the Favor-Frame Matters

The request could be delivered as an instruction ("stop taking notes"), as a coaching observation ("I notice you're using the notes as a defense"), or as a rule ("we don't take notes in these sessions"). All of these would land differently than the favor-frame.

The favor-frame ("can you do me a favor") inverts the power dynamic. Instead of the coach correcting the client, the client is doing the coach a kindness. This makes the request easy to accept. The person does not have to defend the note-taking; they get to graciously honor a request.

The instruction-frame would activate defensiveness. The observation-frame would convert the moment into analysis. The rule-frame would feel arbitrary. The favor-frame is the form that gets the notes down with the least friction.

The "this will be recorded" addendum is the matter-of-fact rationale. The person doesn't have to wonder why they shouldn't take notes; the recording answers the question. The rationale is also true — coaching sessions are typically recorded — so the move is not manipulative; it is using a real fact to make the request reasonable.

## Why "Be Fully Here With Me" Matters

The alternative posture has to be named, not just implied. If the request were only "stop taking notes," the person would not know what to do with their attention instead. The "be fully here with me" gives the destination. The person can now drop the notes into something — into here-with-the-coach — rather than just dropping them into nothing.

The "with me" is also important. The coach is not asking the person to be here alone. The coach is going to be here too. The here is shared. This is the same companionability that runs through [[lets-go-there]] and other Joe-presence moves.

## Tone

Friendly, casual, slightly warm. The favor-frame is delivered the way you would ask a colleague to close the door. There is no gravity, no significance loaded onto the moment. The lightness is what makes the request immediately accept-able.

The pacing is slow but not significant-slow. The whole move is one sentence delivered in five seconds. The brevity matters. A long elaboration would convert the move into a teaching moment, and the moment is not for teaching; it is for clearing the way.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not a rule. The coach is not enforcing a no-notes policy. A different session with a different client might leave the notes alone.
- It is not a coaching insight. The coach is not pointing out that the note-taking represents the pattern. The pointing-out, if it happens at all, comes later. The first move is just to clear the obstacle.
- It is not a power move. The coach is not establishing dominance over the client. The favor-frame is the opposite of dominance.
- It is not interpretation. The coach is not making the note-taking mean something. The note-taking just needs to be set down so the work can happen.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Guest:** *(delivers a polished four-sentence presentation of her concern, eyes occasionally glancing down)*
- **Joe:** "Are you reading from a card?"
- **Guest:** "No, I've been taking notes the whole time we've been on the call. This is— people have been asking their intellectual questions. I'm like, okay, how do I describe it differently? Or how do I—"
- **Joe:** "Could you— so this will be recorded, so can you do me a favor and not take any notes and just be fully here with me?"
- **Guest:** *(sets aside the notes)*
- **Joe:** "And see if I understand the question. It's, I'm always trying to fix shit and I get upset if people don't let me. Is that— how do I handle that? If you put it in a sentence, what would you say?"
- *(Source: Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?)*

The sequence is under 30 seconds. By the end, the notes are down, the question has been compressed to a single sentence, and the work has begun.
