# Catch 'I feel like' as Not a Feeling

## The Move

The person uses the phrase "I feel like" followed by a thought, a story, or an outcome — not an emotion. Catch the phrase directly. Name that what they said is not a feeling. Ask for the actual feeling.

The move is a single sentence delivered immediately after the not-a-feeling lands.

"That's not a feeling. You said 'I would feel like.' That's not a feeling. What's the feeling you would feel?"

Variations:
- "That's a thought, not a feeling. What's the feeling underneath?"
- "I'm tracking what you said but 'I feel like' is a thought. What's the actual feeling?"
- "What you said is a story about how you'd be. What's the felt sense?"

## When To Use It

The person has been asked what they feel. Their answer begins with "I feel like" and then continues into:
- A prediction ("I feel like I would have failed")
- A judgment ("I feel like I'm being selfish")
- A description ("I feel like I didn't do enough")
- A story ("I feel like everyone is going to be disappointed")

None of these are feelings. They are thoughts in feeling's costume.

Use the move when:
- Coaching toward the felt sense of fear, sadness, anger, or grief and the person keeps producing thoughts instead.
- The person is in their head trying to articulate the feeling and never quite getting to it.
- The body is showing emotion (chest constriction, eyes wet, jaw tight) but the words are still in thought.

Do not use the move when:
- The person is in the early stage of a session and is still surfacing the topic. The catch lands well only after the territory has been established.
- The person is genuinely using "feel like" in the colloquial sense and the move would derail something useful that is happening.

## What It Produces

The person usually pauses. The mind realizes it cannot give the answer it was about to give. There is a moment of confusion where the speaker has to look inside the body rather than reach for a sentence.

Three common responses:
1. **The feeling arrives.** "Fear." "Sadness." "Anger." A single word, sometimes with the body shifting as it is named.
2. **More thoughts.** The person tries a different "I feel like" or "I think." The move can be repeated lightly. The repetition is teaching.
3. **A blank.** The person says "I don't know" or "I can't tell." This is data, not failure. The next move is to ask what they notice in the body rather than what they feel.

## What Joe Does With the Response

When the actual feeling arrives, stay with it. Do not move on. The whole session often pivots on this one word being named. "Fear" out loud changes what is happening in the room.

When more thoughts come, gently catch again. The repetition installs the distinction. The person learns, in real time, the difference between a thought about a feeling and a feeling itself.

When a blank arrives, redirect to the body. "Where do you notice anything in your body right now?" The body has the feeling. The mind is trying to translate. Bypass the translator.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I would feel like I did not excel at the things that I could do well."
- **Joe:** "That's not a feeling. You said, 'I would feel like.' That's not a feeling. What's the feeling you would feel?"
- **Person:** "I fear."
- **Joe:** "You would feel fear."
- **Person:** "I fear that I would not live my life to the fullest."

Notice the move is delivered twice, lightly. The first catch produces the word "fear." Joe reflects it back ("you would feel fear") to anchor it. The next sentence the person speaks contains both the felt emotion and the thought it was wrapped in.

## Tone

Direct, light, unmoralizing. The catch is not a correction. It is a precise naming of what just happened. The energy is "let me help you find what you actually meant" rather than "you said it wrong."

The lightness matters. Heavy delivery turns the catch into shame about not feeling correctly. Light delivery makes it an invitation to look more closely.

## Why This Works

The English phrase "I feel like" has drifted into general use as a softener for "I think." Many people use it without noticing they have left the body. The mind produces "feel like X" sentences as if it were producing emotions, when it is actually producing thoughts about how it imagines emotions would be.

The body cannot follow "I feel like." There is no felt sense of "I would not live up to my potential." There is a felt sense of fear, sadness, helplessness, dread. The "feel like" sentence is a screen between the speaker and the actual emotion.

Catching the screen lets the speaker walk past it. Once past it, the actual emotion is often immediately available.
