# Reframe the Trait They Feel Stung By as Evidence of Care

## The Move

When a client has named that a particular achievement-coded word (overachiever, perfectionist, creative, warrior, high-performer) stings, the coach uses that exact word later in the session but unpacks it into its underlying content: care, effort, time invested, attention paid.

In the source session:

"Wow. You are such an overachiever. Cause that means you put a lot of time into this. You cared about this. I see how much effort you put into it. That's what you're an overachiever means." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The structure of the move:

1. Use the exact achievement-word the client felt stung by
2. Immediately translate it into care-language ("cause that means you put a lot of time into this")
3. Name the effort and attention ("you cared about this")
4. Make the underlying content explicit ("I see how much effort you put into it")
5. Re-attach the meaning to the word ("that's what you're an overachiever means")

The move re-engineers the meaning of the word in the client's hearing. The word that had been heard as dismissive becomes the word that carries the seeing the client was actually hungry for.

## Why This Move Works

The client's sting at the achievement-word comes from the gap between what the word literally says and what the client needed to hear. "Overachiever" credits the output. The client needed credit for the cost, the care, the time. The word felt dismissive because it skipped the underneath.

The reframe closes the gap by making the underneath explicit. The compliment-giver who says "you're such an overachiever" usually does mean some version of "you cared about this and put effort into it" — but they leave the meaning implicit. The implicit meaning does not land because the deflection-engine intercepts the surface word.

When the coach unpacks the word in real time, the client gets to hear what the word was always pointing at. The next time someone says "overachiever," the client has the translation available. The word can land because its content has been made visible.

## When Joe Uses It

The setup:

- The client has named a specific achievement-coded word that lands as sting rather than as compliment
- The session has done some work on what the client wishes was being seen instead (usually: the effort, the cost, the care)
- The client has had at least one experience of a non-achievement-coded compliment landing (e.g., the effort-compliment from [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]])
- The coach can use the achievement-word naturally as part of a real compliment, not as a forced demonstration

The move is most powerful as a closing or near-closing move in the receiving-work. The client now has the effort-compliment as the felt reference for what real seeing feels like. The reframe of the achievement-word re-attaches that felt reference to the language the client encounters every day.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The client says some version of "I don't know why I was getting offended by these types of words"
- The achievement-word the client felt stung by becomes available to be heard differently in future situations
- The client now has a translation that they can apply to other achievement-coded compliments they receive
- The work has moved from in-session receiving to portable receiving capacity

If the client deflects this compliment too: the deflection-engine is still firing on the achievement-language. The coach can then pair this move with [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]] more times before re-offering the achievement-word, or with [[live-naming-the-physical-constriction-of-non-reception]] to make the deflection visible at the body level.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client says "I don't know why I was getting offended": Joe affirms the recognition and may name the previous structure ("because you didn't want to feel this thing that I'm asking you to feel"). The recognition that the offense was the deflection-engine firing, not a reaction to the word itself, deepens the work.

If the client deflects again: Joe re-offers the compliment with more emphasis on the underneath. "Hey. I see how much care that you put into things." Sometimes the achievement-word is too loaded and the unpacking has to be re-done without the word.

If the client lets it land partly and then catches themselves trying ("oh that tried so hard"): Joe affirms the catch and names the catch. "Good catch — for a perfectionist, you're really not being perfect at receiving compliments." The light reframe lets the client laugh and continue receiving.

If the client cries: the recognition that the words people have been using were always pointing at the care has surfaced grief. Joe stays with the tears.

## Dialogue Example

After the client talked about a friend's compliments ("what you made was great, this was great, I love this, this is beautiful, this is what I needed") and how she couldn't receive them:

- **Joe:** "And cool. I'm gonna, I'm gonna say that to you in another way. Wow. You are such an overachiever."
- **Client:** "Thank you. I'm learning."
- **Joe:** "Cause that means you put a lot of time into this."
- **Client:** "Sure."
- **Joe:** "You cared about this. I see how much effort you put into it. That's what you're an overachiever means."
- **Client:** "Okay."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Client:** "I don't know why I was getting offended by these types of words."
- **Joe:** "Like, because you didn't want to feel this thing that I'm asking you to feel."

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The exchange shows the move's full arc. Joe uses the exact word the client had named as the source of sting ("overachiever"), then unpacks it step by step into care-language. The client's "I don't know why I was getting offended by these types of words" is the recognition. Joe then names the previous structure: the offense was the deflection-engine firing because letting the word in would require letting the underneath in.

## The Pairing With the Catch-the-Catch Move

A specific variant: when the client tries to defend the effort after the reframe ("oh that tried so hard"), the coach can light-touch the catch:

"Good catch."

Or use the client's own self-concept to flip the deflection:

"For a perfectionist, you're really not being perfect at receiving compliments." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

This is its own micro-move within the broader receiving-work. The playful use of the client's own perfectionist identity short-circuits the deflection without shaming it. The client can laugh and continue receiving.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not lecturing the client on what people mean.** The reframe is a real compliment that uses the client's own framework. It is not "let me explain what people are really trying to say."
- **It is not insincere.** The coach has to actually see the care and effort in the client's work. If the coach is just performing the reframe to make a point, the client will feel the performance and the move will bounce.
- **It is not for every achievement-word.** Some words ("workaholic," "obsessive") may not be available for this reframe because they carry too much negative weight. The move works best with words that are usually meant as compliments but land as dismissive.
- **It is not a substitute for the effort-compliment.** The effort-compliment [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]] usually has to land first. The reframe then re-attaches the same content to the achievement-language.

## Tone

Direct, slightly bold, with the achievement-word delivered with weight. Joe's "Wow. You are such an overachiever" is delivered emphatically, with the same energy a sincere admirer would use. The boldness is what allows the unpacking that follows to feel like the actual meaning of the word, not a softening of it.

The unpacking ("cause that means you put a lot of time into this") is delivered more matter-of-factly, like explaining what the word obviously means. The casualness signals that the meaning was always there — the coach is just naming it out loud.

The closing "that's what you're an overachiever means" is delivered with conviction, re-attaching the meaning to the word with finality.
