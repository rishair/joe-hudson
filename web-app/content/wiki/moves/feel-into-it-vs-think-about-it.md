# Feel Into It vs. Think About It

## The Move

Ask the same question two ways, in immediate succession, with different instructions about which faculty to use. First, "think about it." Then, "feel into it." Or the other way around. Make the contrast vivid by asking explicitly within the same minute. The gap between the two answers is the data.

The instruction format:
- "When you feel into [X], what is it?"
- "Don't think about it. You're thinking about it. Feel into it."
- "Yeah, but when you think about it —"
- "When I think about it, I could write a fucking book on it."

The discovery, almost always: thinking produces a lot, feeling produces something different (or nothing locatable). The contrast itself is the work.

"Yeah, so when you feel into it, nothing's wrong with you... When I like feel into the truth of it, nothing's wrong with me... Yeah, but when you think about it —" — *I Have To Do What Others Want*

## When Joe Uses It

- When a client is producing a story and the story is operating as a cover for the felt experience.
- When a question has been asked and the person is visibly thinking about the answer rather than feeling into it.
- When shame, fear, or another blocker emotion is operating in the head and not in the body, and the gap needs to be made visible.
- When the person says "I don't know how I feel" — usually they have not been asked to feel into it as distinct from thinking about it.
- When intellectualization is the defense and the person is being articulate as a way of staying away from the body. See [[disassociating-in-the-coaching-room]] and [[intellectual-process-with-no-somatic-component]] for the reads that trigger this move.

## The Operating Sequence

1. **Ask the question.** "What is wrong with you?" / "What's wrong with money?" / "What do you want?"
2. **Notice the response.** If it's a story, an explanation, a "well, I guess..." — the person is thinking.
3. **Issue the instruction.** "Don't think about it. You're thinking about it. Feel into it."
4. **Wait for the body answer.** This often takes several seconds. The body is slower than the head.
5. **Then ask the other way.** "Yeah, but when you think about it —"
6. **Let the contrast land.** "Exactly." The gap is the data.

The "exactly" at the end is important. Joe is not declaring one answer right and the other wrong. He is naming that the person has two different answers depending on which faculty they consult. The two-answer fact is what unlocks the next move.

## What It Produces

A live demonstration that the thinking and the body do not agree. The person discovers they have been carrying an intellectual claim about themselves (or about money, or love, or work) that the body does not corroborate.

The discovery is somatic and immediate. It is not the result of inquiry or deconstruction. It is the result of being asked the same question twice, with different access instructions, and noticing the gap. The body's answer is usually quieter, simpler, and harder to articulate — but unmistakably different.

The gap also delegitimizes the thinking-version of the answer. Once the person has heard themselves give two different answers in the same minute, the thinking-version is no longer the only voice in the room. The body's answer has been admitted into the conversation.

## Dialogue Examples

### The Shame Version

- **Joe:** "So tell me what's wrong with you."
- **Guest:** "Um, well, I guess the story right now is like—"
- **Joe:** "I don't care about the story. I want to know what the fuck is wrong with you."
- **Guest:** "What is wrong with you? I mean, when, when you ask, you know, when I feel into it, it's like, I, nothing's wrong with me, you know? I, yeah, nothing's wrong with me."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So when you feel into it, nothing's wrong with you."
- **Guest:** "When I like feel into the truth of it, nothing's wrong with me."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, but when you think about it —"
- **Guest:** "yeah, when I think about it, I mean, I could write a fucking book on it, right?"
- **Joe:** "Exactly."
- *(Source: I Have To Do What Others Want)*

### The Money Version

- **Joe:** "What's wrong with money? Well, don't think about it. You're thinking about it. Yeah, you're thinking when you feel, feel into money. When you feel into money, what's wrong with it?"
- **Guest:** "Well, the first thing that popped in my head was like that I haven't been able to make it."
- **Joe:** "You're totally able to make it. I, I can't buy that for a second."
- *(Source: I Have To Do What Others Want)*

In the second example, the person reports what "popped in my head" — and Joe immediately dismisses it. The head's answer is treated as not the answer being asked for. The body's answer is what is wanted, and Joe will not accept the head's substitute.

## Why It Works

The two faculties produce different answers because they are organized for different purposes.

The head's job is narrative coherence. It will produce a story that makes the felt state make sense. Given the felt state of "shame," the head will retrieve every reason there might be something wrong with the person — childhood, mistakes, fears, comparisons. The head can produce a book because it has been writing the book for years.

The body's job is present-moment truth. Asked what is actually wrong, the body checks. Most of the time, the body's report is "nothing right now." The body is not invested in the story. The body has access to a different layer of information.

The gap between the two is not a defect to be fixed. It is structural. The move makes the gap visible so the person can stop confusing the head's elaborate story with the body's actual report.

The move also surfaces an implicit choice: which answer do you want to live from? The thinking-answer or the feeling-answer? Once the gap is named, the choice exists. Before the move, the person was unknowingly defaulting to the thinking-answer.

## Tone

Direct, light. The instructions are short and clear. There is no preamble explaining what "feel into it" means — the instruction is given and the person figures it out. The trust that the person can do this is part of the move; treating "feel into it" as something that needs explanation makes it into another head-task.

When the person reports the body-answer, Joe's response is small: "yeah," "exactly." Not enthusiastic. Not therapeutic. The understatement matches the understated nature of the body's report. The body did not produce drama; the coach does not respond with drama.

## What Joe Will Not Do

- He will not let the head's answer pass when the body-answer was requested. "You're thinking about it" is the interrupt.
- He will not explain what feeling into something means. The instruction is the practice; explanation would turn it back into a head-task.
- He will not validate the head's story. The "I could write a book" is met with "exactly" — not in agreement, but in recognition of the gap.
- He will not stop at one round. The move is the toggle. One faculty alone does not produce the discovery; both faculties together do.
