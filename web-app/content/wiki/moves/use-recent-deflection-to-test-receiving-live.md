# Use Your Own Deflection to Test Whether They Can Stay Open

## The Move

In a session about compliment-receiving, the client gives the coach a genuine compliment. The coach, instead of accepting it, deflects it ("you're just saying that — you're just saying that"). The coach watches what happens in the client's body. If the client repeats the compliment with genuine conviction rather than collapsing or backing off, the coach names what just happened: the client just stayed in their open heart while their compliment was rejected.

The move's instruction the coach issues to the client *after* the deflection:

"So that moment I rejected your compliment and you stayed in your open heart. Well, how did you do that?" -- Joe Hudson, If You Can't Accept Compliments

The mechanism: the work the client is being asked to do (let a compliment land without deflection) has a structural counterpart that the client has already accidentally done. They gave the compliment, the coach pushed it away, and the client stayed open and re-asserted the truth of what they offered. That stance is the same one Joe is asking them to be able to receive from.

The move is a structural mirror. The client has just modeled the receiving-stance from the giver side. Now they can be asked to do the same thing from the receiver side.

## When Joe Uses It

The setup requires:

- The session is about compliment-receiving or related receiving-work
- The client has been deflecting compliments and is presenting the difficulty as a real problem
- Joe asks the client to give him a genuine compliment (often with the prompt: "you must have listened to podcasts — there must be something you can say that is some sort of compliment that's genuine")
- The client delivers a real compliment
- Joe then deflects, watching for whether the client stays open or collapses

The move requires the client to actually give a compliment that they deeply feel. A throwaway compliment will not work — there is nothing to defend when the coach deflects. The instruction asks for genuineness specifically so that the deflection creates an actual moment of being-rejected to navigate.

## What This Move Produces

When the move lands:

- The client stays in their open heart through the rejection and re-asserts the compliment
- Joe names what happened: "you stayed in your open heart through my rejection"
- The client recognizes that they just demonstrated the capacity they are trying to develop on the receiving side
- The teaching becomes embodied rather than intellectual — the client has just done it, in some direction

If the client collapses or backs off when Joe deflects ("oh, well, maybe not really"), the move produces different data: the client cannot stay open through rejection. The work then becomes the seeing of what happens to the body when someone deflects.

The client in the source session demonstrated the staying-open and Joe used it as the teaching moment. The client's own move ("no, I truly mean it. I really do appreciate you") became the model for the receiving-stance Joe was about to ask her to take on the other side.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client stayed open: Joe names it explicitly and asks how they did it. The client's answer usually surfaces the actual mechanism — sometimes "I knew I had to," sometimes "I felt it was true," sometimes a deeper somatic recognition. Joe affirms whatever they describe and uses it as the bridge to the receiving work.

If the client collapsed: Joe does not shame the collapse. Instead, he treats it as data. The collapse is the same move the client makes when receiving a compliment — they back off the truth in the face of pressure. The work is the same on both sides: stay with the truth, do not collapse.

If the client got angry or confused at being deflected: Joe usually softens and explains. The deflection was a demonstration, not a real rejection. The point was to make the dynamic visible. The acknowledgment of the gambit is part of the teaching.

If the client repeated the compliment with even more force, Joe affirms and gently notes that the more-force was the body adapting — the same way the giver eventually needs more force to penetrate the deflection of the receiver. The realization here is that the client has now experienced what their husband or other compliment-giver has been doing for years.

## Dialogue Example

After several compliment-receiving attempts where the client (a coach asking Joe a question) deflected:

- **Joe:** "I want you to give me a compliment. You must have listened to podcasts. There must be something you can say that is some sort of compliment that's genuine."
- **Client:** "I think that the work you're doing is some of the most impactful I've ever experienced, and I tell everyone about it. So I really appreciate what you do."
- **Joe:** "You're just saying that. You're just saying that."
- **Client:** "No, I truly mean it. I really do appreciate you."
- **Joe:** "So that moment I rejected your compliment and you stayed in your open heart. Well, how did you do that?"
- **Client:** "Well, I knew I had to because I'm here and you're gonna make me."
- **Joe:** "That's how I do it too. I like, oh, I know I have to because it's going to really fucking hurt if I don't."

*(Source: If You Can't Accept Compliments)*

The exchange shows the move's complete arc: invite a compliment, deflect it, watch the response, name what happened, ask about the mechanism, affirm with self-disclosure. The teaching is embodied. The client has already done the thing she is being asked to do.

## What This Move Reveals About the Receiving-Stance

The move makes visible something that is usually invisible: that receiving and asserting-truth-in-the-face-of-rejection are the same somatic capacity. The person who can stay open through a deflection is the same person who can let a compliment land. The person who collapses through a deflection is the same person who deflects when complimented.

The capacity is one thing. The configuration determines whether it is operating from the giver side or the receiver side. The move uses the giver side (which the client just demonstrated) as the on-ramp to the receiver side (which is the work).

This is structurally similar to [[transfer-the-receiving-from-them-to-yourself]] — both moves use a recently demonstrated capacity in one direction as the template for applying the same capacity in another direction. The advantage of this version: the demonstration was generated by the coach's deflection rather than relying on the client to have demonstrated the capacity earlier in the session.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not actual rejection.** The deflection is a gambit. The coach has to be able to hold this without the client experiencing it as a real betrayal of the coaching relationship.
- **It is not a test of toughness.** The move is not "let me see if you can take a hit." It is a demonstration of a capacity the client has, with the deflection as the prompt that reveals the capacity.
- **It is not for every client.** Clients who have significant trauma around being-deflected or being-rejected may experience the gambit as harmful rather than instructive. Read for whether the client can metabolize the move.
- **It is not the whole receiving work.** It is one move that creates a teaching moment. The actual receiving work (letting compliments land somatically) still needs to be done.

## Tone

Direct, slightly playful, completely clean. Joe's "you're just saying that" delivered in the source session is light, almost dismissive — not heavy or accusatory. The lightness is what allows the client to push back without it becoming a real fight. The lightness also signals that the deflection is a gambit rather than a position.

After the client re-asserts, Joe's tone shifts to admiring. "So that moment I rejected your compliment and you stayed in your open heart" is delivered as a recognition of what just happened. The client did a thing. The teaching is the noticing of the thing.
