# Look Me in the Eye and Say the Controlling Wish

## The Move

Have the person look the coach in the eye and state the actual controlling wish they have been holding without admitting — addressed to the coach as the literal recipient. Then ask what it feels like.

The form:
- "Look me right in the eye and say, 'I want to control what you think of me, Joe,' and see what it feels like."
- "Just see what it's like. Look at me and say it."

The point is not to humiliate the person. The point is that the controlling wish has been operating below the threshold of audibility — it has been the engine of the person's behavior, but it has never been said as a sentence to a real human face. The act of voicing it to the coach, with eye contact, makes the wish audible to the body in a way that thinking the wish cannot.

The body's response is the work. Most often it is laughter, embarrassment, the word "ridiculous" or "absurd." The position dissolves at the moment it becomes audible.

## When Joe Uses It

- Late in a session on approval-seeking, after the prohibition on wanting-to-be-heard has been collapsed and the underlying wish-to-control has been named in the abstract.
- When the person has intellectually agreed that controlling others' perceptions is impossible/unwise, but the body has not registered the wish itself as absurd.
- When the person is articulating their concern in a way that implicitly contains the controlling wish but does not state it directly ("I want to make sure I come across well," "I want to know they approve").
- As a closing move that lands the session in laughter and somatic recognition rather than insight.

The move requires:
- An established coaching connection — the person trusts the coach enough to speak the absurd sentence to them.
- Recent surfacing of the wish-to-control as a topic in the session ("what makes you want to control what other people think of you?").
- The person has not yet felt the absurdity. Insight has happened. Embodiment has not.

The move is not for early sessions or for people who are still defending the wish as legitimate. It is for the moment after the position has been seen but before it has dissolved.

## What It Produces

The most common sequence:

1. **A brief resistance.** The person hesitates, or laughs nervously, or asks the coach to repeat.
2. **The saying.** Halting, often quieter than their normal voice, with eye contact partial and unstable.
3. **The body's response.** Almost always laughter, often accompanied by "yeah, it feels ridiculous" or "totally absurd" or a shake of the head. The face does the work the words cannot.
4. **The session-closing recognition.** The person no longer needs the coach to talk them out of the position. The body has talked them out of it.

The source session example shows this almost completely:

- **Joe:** "Look me right in the eye and say, I want to control what you think of me, Joe, and see what it feels like."
- **Guest:** "Joe, I want to control what you think of me. Yeah, it feels ridiculous."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, totally absurd. I totally get it."
- *(Source: How Do I Stop Caring What Others Think?)*

The session essentially ended on this exchange. The work was complete. The body had registered the absurdity of a position the mind had been holding without realizing how it sounded out loud.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person laughs and says "it feels ridiculous":** the move has done its work. Joe does not extend or explore. The next move is closure ("what a pleasure. Thank you for coming up.").
- **If the person says it flatly with no felt response:** the person has performed the saying without doing it. The eye contact was not real or the wish has not yet been actually owned. Joe may ask "say it again, but actually look at me" or move to surface the unowned material directly.
- **If the person resists saying it:** the resistance is the data. The wish is being defended. Joe may name the resistance and pause, or move to ask what the resistance is protecting.
- **If the person says it and gets sad or sober instead of laughing:** the wish is touching grief about a longer pattern. The move has surfaced the cost of having held the wish for years. Joe moves into the grief rather than the laughter, and may pivot toward [[the-natural-integration-arc-after-breakthrough]].

## Why This Works

Three mechanisms run in parallel:

1. **The wish has been silent.** It has been operating as a structural orientation, not as a stated sentence. Stating it aloud is the first time the speaker has had to hear themselves want it. The auditory feedback exposes what silent thinking cannot.

2. **The addressee is a real person.** Controlling-what-someone-thinks-of-you is something you do TO a person, in your head. Saying "I want to control what YOU think of me" to that actual person, while looking at their face, instantly exposes the structural absurdity — the person you are talking to is right there, alive, sovereign, not actually controllable.

3. **The eye contact is the diagnostic.** The body cannot say this sentence to a face it is looking at without the absurdity registering. Eye contact breaks the abstract-other into a real-other. The wish to control collapses against the real-other.

## Tone

Light and slightly mischievous. Joe delivers the instruction with a small grin — he and the person both know what is about to happen. The lightness is what makes the move possible; said solemnly, the position would feel defensible. Said with a grin, the position is already half-acknowledged as the thing it is.

The "see what it feels like" framing is critical. The move is not "say this and admit you are wrong." It is an experiment the person is invited into. The discovery is theirs, not the coach's.

## Related Moves

Tell-me-to-compose has the client direct an internal command outward at the coach; look-me-in-the-eye has the client state an internal wish to the coach. The first surfaces what was done to them; the second surfaces what they have been wanting to do to others.

## What This Move Is Not

- Not interrogation. The person is not being challenged. They are being invited to discover something through their own voice and body.
- Not shaming. The wish-to-control is not the problem; the unconsciousness of the wish is. The move makes the wish conscious so it can be released. Shame would re-bury it.
- Not a confession. The point is not for the person to admit they were wrong. The point is for the body to register what the wish actually is when voiced.
- Not appropriate without rapport. The move requires the coaching relationship to be safe enough that the person can risk saying the absurd sentence without protective filtering.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not skip the eye contact instruction.** If the person says the sentence while looking down or at the camera frame, the move does not work. The eye contact IS the mechanism. Insist on it gently: "actually look at me when you say it."
- **Do not laugh first.** If the coach laughs before the person does, the lightness becomes the coach's, not the person's. Wait. The person's laugh is the data; the coach mirrors it after.
- **Do not extend the move past the laugh.** Once the absurdity has registered, the move is complete. Asking "what was that like?" or "say more about that" usually pulls the person back into the head. Let the body have the win.
- **Do not use this move when the wish is still being defended as legitimate.** If the person believes they should control what others think (because of safety, because of a job, because of social cost), the move will not produce the laugh — it will produce defense. Surface the defense first.
