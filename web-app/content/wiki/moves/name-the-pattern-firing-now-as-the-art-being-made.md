# Name the Pattern Firing Now As the Art Being Made

## The Move

After the special activity has been collapsed into the ordinary moment (see [[collapse-the-special-activity-into-the-ordinary-moment]]), the person often continues to demonstrate the very pattern that has been preventing them from doing the activity. They make the conversation hard. They reach for complexity. They try to figure it out. The next move is to name the live demonstration as the thing being created — the conversation itself is the artwork, and the pattern the person is firing right now is the piece they are currently making.

The move folds the resistance into the frame the collapse just established. If this moment is art, then whatever the person is doing in this moment is the art they are making. If they are making it hard, the art they are making is hardness. If they are trying to figure it out, the art they are making is figuring-out. The pattern stops being the obstacle to the art and becomes the visible content of the art being produced right now.

Verbal forms:

- "Well, that's an interesting piece of art you're making."
- "This is our art. We're having it. We're doing this art piece together, you and I. And you're making it hard."
- "Yeah, so that's the art you want to make?"
- "Like right now you're doing it. You're making a piece of art hard."

> "It is freedom. I don't know, I, I guess I'm trying to make it harder than it is."
>
> "Well, that's an interesting piece of art you're making."
>
> "Yeah, I got a lot of that around. I could probably sell it. It's hard. I make things really hard." -- (Source: 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled)

The play is real. The artist says she has "a lot of that around." The years of making-it-hard have indeed produced a body of work — just not the body of work she meant to produce. The move helps her see the work she has been making instead of the work she keeps not making.

## When Joe Uses It

- After the [[collapse-the-special-activity-into-the-ordinary-moment]] move has dissolved the separation between the activity and life, and the person continues to demonstrate the obstructing pattern in the conversation itself.
- When the person reports a pattern ("I make things really hard," "I always do this," "I'm in my head a lot") and is doing it in the moment of reporting it. The reporting is the firing of the pattern.
- When the person resists the simple frame and reaches for complexity. The reach itself is the pattern they have been doing on the creative work for years.
- When the person says they want one thing (ease, fun, beauty) and the body is producing the opposite right now (tension, complexity, hardness). See [[said-want-without-felt-want]].
- When [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]] is the read and the live trying needs to be made visible without shaming it.

The move requires that the person has identity around the special activity. The artist who makes a conversation hard is producing a small artifact of her artistic pattern. The non-artist making the same conversation hard would not get the same medicine from this naming. The move works because it uses the person's own identity-domain to make the pattern visible.

## What It Produces

A small disarming laugh. The person sees what they have been doing. The seeing is the move's work. Because the naming is light — "that's an interesting piece of art you're making" rather than "stop making it so hard" — the person can receive the observation without defensiveness.

After the laugh, often:

- **The pattern relaxes.** The person who has been making it hard sees themselves making it hard and the making-hard loses its momentum. The pattern was running on invisibility; the visibility interrupts it.
- **A new piece of self-knowledge lands.** "I make things really hard" goes from being a story the person tells about themselves to being a thing they can watch themselves do. The watching changes the relationship to the doing.
- **The person continues making it hard.** Not always does the naming dissolve the pattern. Sometimes the pattern keeps going. In that case, the move shifts: the next observation is that the person is *still* making it hard, and now they are also aware they are making it hard. The conversation becomes a slow demonstration of the pattern's grip.
- **The person tries to negotiate with the frame.** "I don't want that to be my art" — the negotiation is the same pattern firing on the meta-level. The move repeats: the negotiation is now the art being made.

## What Joe Does Next

- **If the pattern relaxes:** stay where you are. The relaxation is the opening. Continue without celebrating; the celebration would reload the importance.
- **If the person resists ("no, that's not the art I want to make"):** "Oh, then what makes you make it? Like right now you're doing it." The follow-up makes the discrepancy between the stated want and the produced output explicit. See [[said-want-without-felt-want]].
- **If the person tries to soften it ("I just want it to be fun"):** Joe redirects to the felt difference. "I hear that you say it, but I don't hear you actually wanting it, because if you wanted it, like, it would be easy and fun and beautiful right now, which it is for me anyways. For you, it's hard."
- **If the person collapses into shame ("I'm so bad at this"):** the move did not land. Back out and return to the lighter frame. The naming was supposed to be playful; if it landed as accusation, the room needs to be reset.
- **If the person sees it and asks how to stop:** the asking-how-to-stop is itself the pattern. See [[how-do-i-stop-is-the-fix-trap]]. Refuse the how-to-stop frame. The seeing is the work; the doing-something-about-it is the head reaching back into control.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is structural. The pattern that has been preventing the creative work has been operating outside the person's view. They have a story about making things hard, but the actual making-hard happens in moments the story does not cover. The move places the making-hard inside the very situation the person is paying attention to — the coaching conversation — and uses the artist's own identity-domain to make it impossible to miss.

The art frame matters because it dissolves the moral charge. "You're making this hard" is an accusation. "That's an interesting piece of art you're making" is an observation. The first generates defense; the second generates curiosity. The move requires the first move ([[collapse-the-special-activity-into-the-ordinary-moment]]) to have established that the conversation is art. Without that setup, the second move has no ground to stand on.

The move is also a small demonstration of [[apply-enjoy-to-whats-here]] applied to the resistance itself. The person had been treating the resistance as the obstacle to the work. The move treats the resistance as the work currently being done. The reframing relocates enjoyment to the place the person was trying to escape from. Once the resistance becomes interesting, the resistance often loses its grip.

## What This Move Is Not

- Not sarcasm. The "interesting piece of art" is delivered with genuine curiosity. If the artist is making complexity, complexity is what is being produced; that is interesting in its own right. The move does not mock the pattern; it makes the pattern visible.
- Not a trick to manipulate the person into stopping. The point is not to embarrass them out of the pattern. The point is to make the pattern visible so it can be related to differently. Whether the person stops the pattern is downstream of whether they see it.
- Not specific to art. The same move can be made with any identity-domain. "That's an interesting piece of coaching you're doing" to a coach who is making the conversation hard. "That's an interesting parenting move you just made" to a parent who is repeating the pattern they were complaining about. The art version is the most generative because art carries the connotation of intention and craft, which makes the observation land lighter.

## Tone

Curious, playful, slightly amused. Joe delivers this as if he has just noticed something interesting on the wall. The lightness is not optional. If the move is delivered heavily, the person hears accusation. If it is delivered with curiosity, the person hears observation.

The artist's response — "Yeah, I got a lot of that around. I could probably sell it." — is the marker that the tone landed. She received the observation with humor, and the humor produced the opening for the next move.

## Dialogue Example

- **Client:** "It is freedom. I don't know, I, I guess I'm trying to make it harder than it is."
- **Joe:** "Well, that's an interesting piece of art you're making."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I got a lot of that around. I could probably sell it. It's hard. I make things really hard."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so that's the art you want to make?"
- **Client:** "No."
- **Joe:** "Oh, then what makes you make it? Like right now you're doing it. You're making a piece of art hard. This is our art. We're having it. We're doing this art piece together, you and I. And you're making it hard. Still making it hard."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I don't want it to be hard. I want it to be fun and happy and beautiful."
- **Joe:** "I hear that you say it, but I don't hear you actually wanting it, because if you wanted it, like, it would be easy and fun and beautiful right now, which it is for me anyways. For you, it's hard."
- *(Source: 15 Years of Creative Block Unravelled)*

The exchange takes under a minute. The move sets up the next move ([[said-want-without-felt-want]]) by making the gap between stated and produced output visible. The artist cannot continue to claim she wants ease while producing hardness in the same conversation.
