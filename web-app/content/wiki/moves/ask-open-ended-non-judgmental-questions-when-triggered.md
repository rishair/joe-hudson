# Ask Open-Ended Non-Judgmental Questions When You're the Triggered One

## The Move

When you are triggered by something someone said to you, the move that interrupts the trigger most cleanly is to ask them an open-ended, non-judgmental question about it. Not a defensive question. Not a strategic question. A question that genuinely seeks more information.

This is one of the techniques Joe teaches in the Connection Course as the first response to triggers. It is paired with feeling into the body and accepting the triggered state. The body acceptance comes first; the question is what you do once the body is no longer fighting itself.

"Then the second piece is to ask questions that are open-ended and non-judgmental. And we've seen people do that in -- I've seen people when I used to do that course live, I saw people say, oh my gosh, like, we just resolved without a conversation. We just resolved multiple issues just by asking questions, just without even the response to the questions." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

The remarkable observation is that the resolution often happens before the question is answered. The asking itself is the move.

## The Navy SEAL Story

The clearest illustration. Joe was working in a company doing this exercise. A small, conflict-avoidant programmer was paired with an ex-CIA, ex-Navy SEAL whose job was to be triggered so he could practice the response. The programmer could not bring himself to deliver a trigger. Joe stepped in:

"I said to the Navy SEAL, I said, um, your hard exterior makes it so that, um, you're not going to ever get the love that you want."

The SEAL paused. He used a presence technique he had learned for combat to settle into his body. Then he looked at Joe and said:

"I really want to have deeper connections in my life. How can I -- how do you propose that I can get there?"

Joe's report: "It was just like this immediate thing, and like I got chills, and I looked over at the programmer who started weeping, and he started weeping, and it was like this amazing moment of just that one question immediately changed the trigger."

The question did three things at once. It told the person who delivered the trigger that they had been heard. It moved the asker from defense into wonder. And it converted the moment from a fight into a collaboration.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is structural, not just rhetorical.

**It is neurologically incompatible with the trigger response.** Joe's claim, framed carefully: "It's really neurologically impossible, as far as I can find, to hold wonder and anger or wonder and fear at the same time." The trigger lives in the fear/anger response. The genuine wonder of an open-ended question pulls the nervous system out of that state. See [[wonder]].

**The other person feels heard.** The person who delivered the trigger is usually braced for defense or attack. The question signals that they were received. The signal of being heard drops their own escalation. See [[being-heard]].

**It collapses the adversarial frame.** Triggers run on the implicit frame "you are doing this to me." The open question reframes the moment as "we are now both looking at this." The dynamic of two scared people each thinking they are being attacked dissolves. See [[power-dynamics-as-two-defenders]].

**It interrupts the rehearsed response.** When triggered, most people are running a pre-built defense. The question requires a new shape that the trigger response does not have. The neurology is forced to do something it has not done before.

## What Makes a Question Open and Non-Judgmental

Open and non-judgmental does not mean polite. It means genuinely seeking more. Examples:

- "How can I get there?" (the SEAL's question)
- "What would you like to see different?"
- "What is it that you actually want from me here?"
- "How do you see it?"
- "What's happening for you right now?"
- "What part of that is most important to you?"

What disqualifies a question:

- **Strategic questions.** "Why would you say something like that?" carries an accusation. The wonder is fake. See [[wonder-as-strategy]].
- **Closed questions.** "Did you mean that?" has a yes or no answer and does not open the field.
- **Rhetorical questions.** "Do you really think that's fair?" is a statement in question form.
- **Loaded questions.** "What's making you so angry?" is judgmental even if it sounds open.

The internal test: am I genuinely curious about the answer? If yes, the question is right. If no, find the question whose answer you actually want.

## What Joe Did Differently Than Most Coaches

Most coaches would have told the SEAL to "stay present" or "breathe." Joe instead delivered the trigger and let the SEAL produce the question himself. The SEAL had presence techniques; the question was the test of whether the techniques worked in the moment.

This points to a teaching pattern: Joe gives the move and then creates the conditions where the student has to deploy it. The exercise is the assessment.

## When To Use It

The move is for the moment you notice you are triggered, mid-conversation, with the other person still in the room. It is not the post-hoc move (that is the work of feeling the unfelt emotion). It is the live move.

The sequencing:
1. Feel the trigger in the body. Allow it. Do not try to make it not be there.
2. Notice that you are triggered. The recognition itself is part of the work.
3. Ask the open-ended, non-judgmental question.
4. Listen.

Step one is not optional. If you skip step one and go directly to the question, the question becomes a deflection. The body is still in the trigger; the question is performing wonder rather than embodying it.

"The first step of that is to feel into your trigger and accept this state, accept that state, you know, get into your body, allow yourself to feel that way and not try to tell yourself that you should be in a different way, but to be present with what actually is going on in your, in your physical body. And then the second piece is to ask questions that are open-ended and non-judgmental." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## What It Produces

When it works: the conversation immediately moves into how-do-we-actually-want-this-to-be territory. The triggering content gets metabolized in the asking and answering. Both people often end the exchange closer than they started.

When it does not work: the question was fake (a strategic move dressed as wonder), or the body step was skipped, or the trigger was too big to convert in real time. In the last case the move is to name the trigger as vulnerability and ask for space. See [[name-your-trigger-as-vulnerability]].

## Tone

The question is asked from genuine wonder, not from technique. The voice tends to slow down. There is often a quality of "I really want to know" that you can hear. If the tone is brittle or strategic, the move has not happened yet -- the body work has not completed.

## Anti-Patterns

- Using questions to avoid the feeling. The question is supposed to come after the body work, not instead of it.
- Using questions to keep score. "So you're saying I'm a bad partner?" is not a question. It is an indictment.
- Performing the move to look enlightened. The other person can feel the difference.

## The Specific Application to Receiving Criticism

A narrower but very common application of the move: responding to criticism, judgment, or disappointment directed at you with questions rather than defense. The criticism-case is treated separately in [[ask-the-criticizer-what-they-mean]] because it generalizes differently than the trigger-case for many people — criticism feels content-laden in a way that generic triggers do not, and learners often fail to apply the trigger-move to the criticism-receiving case unless it is explicitly authorized.

The specific operational questions for criticism: "What do you mean by that? What's going on? What am I missing? What would you have me do?" These are the criticism-specific instantiations of the open-ended-non-judgmental-question principle.

The underlying mechanism is identical: respond to the threat-stimulus with genuine wonder, the threat-dynamic dissolves, the substrate need (being-seen) gets met. The narrower scope of [[ask-the-criticizer-what-they-mean]] makes the application explicit for the case where the threat-stimulus is a content-laden criticism.
