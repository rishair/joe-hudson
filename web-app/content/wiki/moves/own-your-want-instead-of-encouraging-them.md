# Own Your Want Instead of Encouraging Them

## The Move

When the recognition arrives that the encouragement you have been delivering to a partner is perpetuating their self-criticism (see [[encouraging-them-agrees-with-their-self-attack]]), the replacement is not silence. The replacement is the direct naming of what you actually want and what the want is about for you. The encouragement was the want disguised as service. The move is to undisguise it.

The structure has four parts:

1. **Affirm them as whole.** "You can do whatever you do." Their wholeness is the starting position, not the destination.
2. **Name your own want directly.** "I would love you to put this book out in the world." The want is yours, not theirs.
3. **Name what the want is about for you.** "Because I don't want to be alone in this. I feel alone in this." The vulnerability under the want.
4. **Hand them back their agency.** "You can do what you got to do." The choice is theirs.

The complete form from the source session:

"I would love you to put this book out in the world because I don't want to be alone in this. And I feel alone in this. And you can do what you got to do. Like, I just have to own my want." -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

## When To Make This Move

- When the coachee has recognized that their encouragement of a partner has been perpetuating the partner's self-criticism.
- When the coachee has admitted (often only when pressed) that they have a specific want about what the partner does -- publish the book, start the business, change the job, lose the weight, do the thing the encouragement has been pushing toward.
- When the coachee is in the moment of seeing that they have been controlling the partner by managing the partner's emotional state instead of speaking their own.
- When the coachee asks "but what would I say then?" The replacement is the answer.

The move is sometimes done in the session: have the coachee say it out loud, to you, as if you were the partner. The saying is part of the recognition. The vulnerability of the wanting-them-not-to-be-alone often surfaces only when the words have been spoken.

## What Each Move Does

**Move 1 -- "You can do whatever you do."** This is the structural reversal of the project-stance ([[making-your-partner-a-project]]). The partner is restored to being a person making their own choices rather than a target of the speaker's management. The move installs the wholeness frame that the rest of the statement will rest on. Without this move, the want would land as a demand. With it, the want lands as information about the speaker.

**Move 2 -- "I would love you to put this book out in the world."** The want is stated. The verb matters: "would love" rather than "need" or "want you to." The framing keeps the want as the speaker's preference rather than the partner's obligation. The specific object of the want is named, so the partner can know exactly what is being wanted. The earlier encouragement-form ("you can do it") had been the want disguised as encouragement; this is the want undisguised.

**Move 3 -- "Because I don't want to be alone in this. I feel alone in this."** This is the vulnerability move (see [[vulnerability]]). The want has a reason that lives in the speaker, not in the partner. The reason is not a strategic framing designed to motivate the partner. The reason is the felt truth about why the want exists. The session source surfaces the loneliness of being on a public path while the partner is private. The loneliness is the actual driver. Naming it makes the want intelligible without making it the partner's responsibility to dissolve.

**Move 4 -- "You can do what you got to do."** The agency-return. The choice is restored to the partner. The speaker has spoken; the partner gets to respond. This move prevents the statement from becoming an emotional argument that pressures the partner toward the wanted action. The whole statement is the speaker's truth, fully owned, and the partner is free to respond from their own truth.

## What It Produces

The statement, delivered cleanly, often produces:

- A change in the partner's response. The partner had been receiving encouragement, which they often resented even when they could not name why. They are now receiving the speaker's vulnerability. The response is usually openness rather than the resistance that encouragement produced.
- Relief in the speaker. The work of running the encouragement has been continuous and exhausting. Speaking the want directly drops a chronic load.
- A different conversation. The conversation that follows is about the want and what each partner wants to do with it, rather than about the partner's deficits and how to fix them. The relationship has been temporarily restored to two adults talking about their wants.
- Possibility that the partner does the thing anyway -- but not for the reasons the encouragement was trying to install. The partner may choose to publish the book because they care about the speaker not being alone. That is a different motive than the encouragement's "you should do it because everyone will love it." The first is connection-driven; the second was deficit-driven.
- Possibility that the partner does not do the thing. The agency-return means the speaker has to be okay with this. If they are not okay with this, the work is on the loneliness, not on the partner. See [[treat-them-like-an-adult]].

## The Recognition That Accompanies the Move

The move often arrives in a session immediately after the recognition that the encouragement has been control. The coachee in the source session says, after Joe offers the alternative statement: "I just have to own my want."

The recognition has three parts:

- "I have a want." (The encouragement was hiding the want.)
- "The want is mine." (The encouragement was placing the want on the partner.)
- "I have to own it." (The encouragement was a way of not owning it.)

The owning is what the four-move statement operationalizes. Each move is the speaker holding the want as theirs rather than as something the partner is responsible for producing.

## Distinguished From Asking for the Thing

The move is not "ask your partner to publish the book." The asking-for-the-thing variant would still be locating the answer in the partner's behavior. The own-your-want move is locating the answer in the speaker's truth.

The asking-for-the-thing variant: "I want you to publish the book. Will you?" This puts the partner in the position of either complying or refusing. It still treats the partner's behavior as the variable to be moved.

The own-your-want variant: "You can do whatever you do, and I would love you to publish the book because I don't want to be alone in this." This does not ask. It informs. The partner is now in possession of the speaker's truth and can do whatever they decide is right for them.

The asking move is appropriate in some contexts (see [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]] for the specific form). The own-your-want move is the corrective for the encouragement pattern specifically. The encouragement pattern was a hidden ask; the corrective is to make the want visible without converting it back into an ask that puts the partner on the spot.

## When the Want Underneath Is Unclear

Sometimes the coachee runs the encouragement without being able to articulate what they actually want. The instruction "own your want" lands as a question rather than an instruction. The session work then becomes finding the want.

Useful inquiries:

- "If you knew your partner could not change because of anything you did, what would you want for yourself in this situation?"
- "When you imagine your partner doing the thing you have been encouraging, what would it give you?"
- "What are you avoiding feeling by managing this for them?"
- "Who would you be if you stopped managing this?"

Often the want underneath the encouragement is one of: not being alone in something, being seen by the partner in a specific way, the partner's success providing reflected status or security, the comfort of having the partner be the kind of person who succeeds at the thing. The want, named, often feels too vulnerable to speak directly -- which is why it has been hiding in the encouragement.

The four-move statement is the operational form of speaking it anyway.

## Tone

Direct, vulnerable, not pressured. The whole statement is short. It does not contain hedges. It does not contain encouragement. It does not contain blame. It contains the truth of what the speaker wants and the truth of what the want is about for them, framed inside an explicit affirmation of the partner's wholeness and agency.

The vulnerability is the key. The statement only works if the speaker is willing to be in the position of having the want and not getting it. If the speaker is using the statement as a sophisticated form of pressure, the partner will feel that and the same dynamic the encouragement was producing will continue.

## In Practice for the Coach

1. **Wait for the recognition.** The move is the answer to "what would I say then?" The coachee has to have asked it. Offering the move before the recognition arrives lands as homework rather than discovery.

2. **Have them say it out loud.** Saying the statement in the session installs it differently than discussing it. The body learns the new shape.

3. **Watch for the want underneath.** Often the coachee tries to say the statement and discovers, in the saying, what the want actually is. The structure of the four moves is enough scaffolding to let the truth emerge.

4. **Address the vulnerability.** The third move ("because I don't want to be alone in this") is often the hardest. If the coachee skips it or softens it, name the skip. The vulnerability is the active ingredient.

5. **Affirm the recognition that follows.** The coachee will often say something like "I just have to own my want." That recognition is worth naming back to them. The move is now intelligible as the operational form of the recognition.
