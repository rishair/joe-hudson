# Find What's Enjoyable in the Defense

## The Move

The person has just described a defense — a freeze, a blank, a numbness, a survival strategy. The standard coaching impulse would be to move toward releasing it. Joe does the opposite. He asks what is enjoyable about being in it.

"What's the thing about here that you enjoy? About that, that super blank place that is like — there's something about it that's like, it's like almost holy. There's some enjoyment there. What is that?" — Joe Hudson, I Can't Feel Anything

The question presupposes that there is enjoyment in the defense. Not just utility — enjoyment. The defense has been doing its job for years or decades; somewhere in there, the system has come to love being in the defense. That love is real. The move asks for the love to be named.

## When Joe Uses It

This move comes when:

- A defense or coping strategy has been named (freeze, blank, dissociation, numbness, going to a quiet place)
- The person describes the defense as something they retreat to "on purpose," not just something that happens to them
- The defense has positive valence in the person's description — it is not just absence, it is a presence the person values
- The work needs to honor the defense before attempting any movement (releasing it directly would activate the original guard)
- The person has been carrying shame about the defense (treating it as broken-ness) and needs the inversion

The move is especially powerful for [[the-blank-place-as-refuge]] — the inner blank that the dissociated system retreats to. The blank's positive quality is the reason it has been so durable; naming the positive quality directly disarms the shame layer that has been sitting on top of it.

## What It Produces

Several characteristic responses:

1. **The person names a specific quality.** "It's just quiet. Nothing else." "It feels safe." "Nothing can touch me here." The naming is itself the work — the person has rarely if ever articulated what they love about the defense. The articulation surfaces the structure.

2. **A small smile or softening.** The person's body relaxes when the defense is honored. The guard they have been keeping up against being told the defense is wrong gets to come down. The relaxation is visible — sometimes a smile, sometimes a deeper breath, sometimes a slight drop in the shoulders.

3. **Surprise at the question.** The person did not expect to be asked what they enjoy about it. The surprise is part of why the move works — the system that has been bracing against attack on the defense did not have a pre-prepared response for "tell me what's good about it."

4. **The defense's structure becomes legible.** Once the person has named what they enjoy, the structure of the defense is visible. The work can now proceed with the structure honored rather than threatened. The next moves — bringing the body in, allowing the feeling to come — happen on top of the honored defense rather than against it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Joe takes the named quality seriously. He repeats it back. He may add adjectives that reinforce the honoring — "lovely," "almost holy," "I know the place." The repetition is not technique; it is the coach showing he heard what was said and confirms its truth.

In the source session, after the person says "I don't know, it's just quiet. Nothing else," Joe responds: "It's just quiet. Yeah, yeah. So let's try this thing out. Go to that quiet place. That's — it is like I saw it, like there's, there's like a surrender to it almost. Yeah, like it's quite lovely."

Note the layered honoring: "It's just quiet" (repeat), "let's try this thing out" (the work proceeds, with the defense as the platform), "it is like I saw it" (confirmation that the place is real and visible), "there's like a surrender to it almost" (offering an additional positive quality the person may not have named), "it's quite lovely" (naming the affection Joe feels for what they built).

Once the defense has been honored, the move that often follows is to bring the body into it — keeping the defense intact, adding additional resources. See the source for the sequence: "Go to that quiet place... I just want you to feel 2 inches below your belly button. So stay in the quiet place, but just bring your body into it." The defense is not lost. The body is added.

## Dialogue Example

The full exchange from the source session:

- *(Person reports going blank: cold hands and feet, blank inside, nothing)*
- **Joe:** "Okay, so, uh I want you to become even more blank. So I'm going to ask you what the sound is, and you're going to go blank. But I don't want you to go blank like this blank. I want you to go blank, super blank. Okay, okay. So what's the sound that you want to make when you think about your entire life? Even more, just like all the way. Oh wow, you're really good at that. What's the thing about here that you enjoy? About that, that super blank place that is like — there's something about it that's like, it's like almost holy. There's some enjoyment there. What is that?"
- **Person:** "I don't know, it's just quiet. Nothing else."
- **Joe:** "It's just quiet. Yeah, yeah. So let's try this thing out. Go to that quiet place. That's — it is like I saw it, like there's, there's like a surrender to it almost. Yeah, like it's quite lovely."

*(Source: I Can't Feel Anything (Coaching Session With Master Coach))*

Note the sequence: Joe first amplified the defense (see [[amplify-the-feeling-until-it-breaks-open]]) — "go more blank, super blank" — which is what surfaced its quality as a positive place rather than a deficit. Then Joe asked for the enjoyment. The amplification and the enjoyment-question work together: the amplification surfaces the defense in its full form; the enjoyment-question honors what is found.

## Why This Move Works

The mechanism is the same as [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] applied at the felt-sense level. The pattern was built for a reason; the pattern continues to exist because it is still serving something the person values; the way to release the pattern is to acknowledge what it is serving, not to attack it for existing.

The specific addition of "enjoyment" rather than just "function" matters. A person can intellectually accept that their defense has function and still emotionally disown it. Asking for the enjoyment forces the emotional acknowledgment. The person has to feel what is good about the defense in order to answer the question. The feeling of the good is what shifts the relationship to the defense.

There is also a structural surprise built in. The defense has been guarded by a part of the person that expects coaching to try to remove it. That part is on alert for any move that would invalidate the defense. The enjoyment-question is the opposite of an invalidation move. It catches the guard off-balance. The guard cannot defend against being asked what is good. The guard relaxes because the threat it was scanning for is not there.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not condoning the defense as a permanent strategy. The honoring is not endorsement. The move is naming what is real about the defense — that it has value, that the person loves it — without saying that the defense is the optimal state to remain in forever.

It is also not flattery. Joe is not saying nice things about the defense to make the person feel better. He is articulating what he actually observes — that there is enjoyment in the blank, that it is almost holy, that there is surrender in it. These are reads, not compliments. The accuracy of the reads is what makes the move work.

It is also not a delay before the real work. The honoring IS the work. Without it, every subsequent move would be working against the system's existing investment. With it, every subsequent move works with the system's investment. The honoring shifts the entire field of the rest of the session.

It is also not appropriate when the defense is acutely dangerous to the person or others. A dissociation that prevents a person from noticing a current threat does not need to be enjoyed; it needs to be interrupted. The move is for chronic, settled, long-term defenses that have become inner real estate — not for acute defenses in active crisis.

## Tone

Curious, warm, slightly reverent. The "almost holy" descriptor sets the tone — the defense is being approached as something sacred, not as something pathological. The voice should slow. The pace should match the pace of the place being honored.

The curiosity is genuine. Joe is asking what the person actually enjoys, not setting up a rhetorical question whose answer is predetermined. The person's answer matters. The specific quality they name will shape how the next moves get delivered.
