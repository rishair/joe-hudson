# Talk to the Part That Knows, Not the Part Telling the Story

## The Move

In any coaching conversation, the client has multiple parts speaking at once. There is the part producing the verbal story ("I don't know," "I'm stuck," "I can't see it"). There is also a part that already knows — visible in the small smile, the slight shift of the eyes, the body's response to a question. The coach addresses the second part, not the first.

This is not a parts-work move in the IFS sense. It is more basic. It is a choice about whom to speak to inside a single person. The client is treated as already having the seeing-self present in the room, and the coach speaks to that self rather than to the storytelling-self.

"So that, like, my perspective whenever I'm working with somebody is that they have the answers, they know what's going on, and I'm following them. But I'm having a conversation with the part of them that knows, not the one that's telling the story." — Joe Hudson, How Do I Get Unstuck? (Coaching Commentary)

## When Joe Uses It

This move runs continuously throughout a session. It is not a single intervention; it is a stance the coach holds about who they are talking to. Specific moments where the move becomes most visible:

- The client smiles while saying "I don't know." See [[the-smile-of-recognition]]. The coach addresses what the smile is acknowledging, not what the words are disclaiming.
- The client produces a confused verbal answer that is at odds with what they have just demonstrated. The coach refers back to the demonstration, not the confused answer.
- The client says "I'm not sure I could identify the difference" but their body has already adjusted to the distinction Joe has named. The coach proceeds as if the distinction has landed, because in the body it has.
- The client offers a complex story explaining why they can't do something, while also reporting that they have, in fact, done it before. The coach treats the historical evidence as the report, not the current story.

The move is also visible in what the coach does NOT do. The coach does not argue with the storyteller. The coach does not try to convince the storyteller that the storyteller is wrong. The coach simply addresses a different part of the person, and lets the storyteller catch up at its own pace.

## What It Produces

The client experiences the conversation differently than they expected. They were prepared for a coach who would engage with their stated problem at face value. Instead, they encounter a coach who is somehow speaking to a version of themselves that they did not know was visible. Often the client's verbal stance softens, because there is no opponent for the disclaimer to argue against.

The seeing-self also gets activated by being addressed. A part of the person that has been operating in the background is now being treated as the primary participant. The seeing-self steps forward; the storytelling-self steps back. The conversation moves to a different layer without anyone explicitly negotiating the move.

The integration often happens in the next few exchanges. The client says something like "oh, yeah, I do see that," or laughs, or pauses with a shift in their face. The seeing-self has come fully online. The work can now proceed from that location.

## What Joe Does Next

Once the seeing-self is addressing the coach directly, Joe follows whatever it offers. The seeing-self knows what to do next; the coach's job is to keep the conditions available for it to keep operating.

If the seeing-self offers a question, Joe explores the question. If it offers a feeling, Joe goes into the feeling. If it offers a hesitation, Joe stays with the hesitation. The coach is not directing; the coach is accompanying. See [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] for the broader stance.

If the storytelling-self reasserts itself (the client slips back into a story about not knowing), Joe does not fight it. He returns to addressing the seeing-self. The reassertion is a small relapse, and the same move handles it: keep speaking to the part that knows, and the part that knows will keep showing up.

## Why It Works

The mechanism is operant. Whichever part of the person gets addressed gets reinforced. If the coach addresses the storytelling-self, the storytelling-self gets practice and becomes more elaborate. If the coach addresses the seeing-self, the seeing-self gets practice and becomes more available.

This is also why arguing with a client's stated story tends to backfire. The argument addresses the storyteller, treats the story as worthy of debate, and gives the story more ground rather than less. Skipping the argument entirely and addressing the part underneath is what allows the part underneath to take the lead.

There is also a deeper structural reason. The seeing-self is what produces change. The storytelling-self is what produces stability. A coach who wants to support change has to direct the conversation toward the agent of change. The seeing-self is that agent.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not dismissing the storyteller. The storytelling-self is a real part of the person. It is doing useful work (organizing experience, maintaining coherence, providing a public face). The move is not to attack it or eliminate it. The move is to recognize that it is not the part that produces change, and to direct the change-producing conversation to a different location.

It is also not pretending the storyteller's content is wrong. The storyteller's content is often partially true. "I am stuck" has elements of truth — the felt experience is real. The move is not to refute the content. The move is to recognize that even when the content is partly true, addressing it as the truth treats the part that says it as the primary participant, which slows the work.

It is also not the same as [[talk-to-your-parts-and-ask-for-trust]]. That move is a parts-work intervention performed by the client on themselves. This move is what the coach does in the moment-to-moment of speaking. The two can work together: the coach addresses the seeing-self, and the seeing-self may then engage in parts-work with other parts of the system.

## How to Tell Which Part to Address

The seeing-self is usually visible in:
- A smile that does not match the words. See [[the-smile-of-recognition]].
- A body adjustment (a softening, a settling, a shift of posture) that does not match the verbal disclaimer.
- A history of evidence the person has just provided that contradicts the current story.
- A precise question or observation the person has just made that demonstrates the awareness they are about to disclaim.
- A pause before a "no" or an "I don't know" that suggests checking has just happened.

The storytelling-self is usually visible in:
- A fluent, rehearsed-sounding presentation of the problem.
- Sentences that begin "I always" or "I never" or "I can't."
- A request for the coach to tell them what to do.
- A reframing of the conversation back to the abstract or general.

The diagnostic is not absolute. Both parts are present throughout. The coach is choosing which to address moment by moment, and the choice can shift mid-sentence.

## Dialogue Examples

The 2024 source session contains the canonical demonstration. The client has just disclaimed her own seeing:

- **Person:** "I'm not sure I could identify the difference."
- *(small smile)*
- **Joe:** "I see a smile. A little bit of a smile. She sees it."
- *(later, in the commentary, Joe explains the structural move:)*
- **Joe:** "So that, like, my perspective whenever I'm working with somebody is that they have the answers, they know what's going on, and I'm following them. But I'm having a conversation with the part of them that knows, not the one that's telling the story."

The naming of the smile is itself the move. By naming what he sees, Joe transfers his attention from the words to the body — and invites the client's attention to transfer with him. Once both attention-streams are on the seeing part, that part can take the conversational lead.

## Tone

Soft, attentive, slightly inquisitive. The coach is not announcing a tactical shift; the coach is simply paying attention to where the actual signal is coming from. The shift is internal to the coach, not declared.

When the seeing-self begins to take the lead, the coach matches its quality. If the seeing-self is tentative, the coach is tentative. If it is direct, the coach is direct. The accompaniment is by tone-matching as much as by content.

If the storytelling-self reasserts, the coach does not get firmer. The coach stays at the tone of the seeing-self and addresses the seeing-self again. The storytelling-self loses traction when it is not given an opponent.
