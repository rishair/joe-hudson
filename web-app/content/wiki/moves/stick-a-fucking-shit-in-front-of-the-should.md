# Stick a Fucking Shit in Front of the Should

## The Move

When a person notices a should arising — in their speech, in their thinking, in their internal voice — the move is to prefix it with a curse word. "Fuck the should." "Fucking should." The irreverent prefix interrupts the obligation reflex by introducing a tone that is incompatible with it.

The point is not the swearing. The point is the energetic mismatch. Shoulds carry a quiet, serious, parental weight. Cursing them carries a loose, playful, rebellious weight. The two cannot occupy the same internal space at the same moment.

"I give you a solution. The first thing you do is you're gonna stick a fucking shit in front of it." -- Joe Hudson, What Blocks Your Love?

## When Joe Uses It

After a person has identified that they are running shoulds — usually in real time during a session — and asks "so what do I do about this?" The move is the first-line answer. It is not a deep practice. It is a pattern interrupt. It is designed to be usable in the moment, every time, without preparation.

The move is particularly useful when:
- The person has just heard themselves say "should" and made a face
- The person has noticed that every sentence has a should in it
- The person is asking for a solution after seeing the pattern
- The person is sincere but a little serious about the work and the seriousness is part of the trap

It is not the move when:
- The person has not yet seen the should as the problem (they will hear the curse as edgy advice and miss the point)
- The person is in real distress and needs presence, not a pattern interrupt
- The person is in performance mode (the move will be performed too)

## What It Produces

The cursing accomplishes three things at once:

1. **Pattern interrupt.** The verbal habit of "I should" is broken by the prefix. The person can no longer say the should without first cursing it, which means they always notice it.

2. **Tone shift.** Shoulds are weighty. Cursing is light. The mismatch produces a moment of levity that the obligation cannot survive intact.

3. **Self-permission.** The act of cursing your own shoulds is a small declaration of independence from the inner voice that issues them. It says: I am not going to take you that seriously.

## The Larger Frame

This move sits inside the broader teaching that obligation cuts off love. See [[responsibility-stops-love]] and [[should-as-threat]]. Every should is a small obligation. Every small obligation cuts off a small amount of love. The cumulative effect over a life is the cessation of love for oneself.

The move is the cheapest available intervention: every time a should appears, curse it. The cursing does not by itself produce love. But it stops the obligation from establishing itself, which makes room for the love to return.

In the transcript, when Joe asks the client what's more important — love or obligation — and the client says "Love," Joe offers the move as the practical answer. Not analysis. Not a long practice. A prefix.

## Tone

Light, irreverent, almost playful. The move's effectiveness depends on the tone. If delivered solemnly, it becomes another should ("I should be cursing my shoulds"). The cursing has to come with a grin. The whole point is that the person stops taking the voice that seriously.

## What This Is Not

This move is not [[fixing-people]] or [[loving-to-transform]] applied to oneself. It is not "fix the shoulds." It is "interrupt them." The interruption creates space; the space allows the want to surface; the want is what actually moves things. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

This move is not a substitute for [[wants-over-shoulds]] as a developmental orientation. It is the in-the-moment hack that supports the orientation. The orientation is the deeper teaching; this move is the operational support.

## When the Should Comes Back

The shoulds will keep coming. The move is not "do this once and the shoulds stop." It is "do this every time." The person will catch themselves saying "I should have done better." Joe's response in the transcript is direct:

"Yeah, you couldn't do better. If you could do better, you would have done better. That's what you can do." -- Joe Hudson, What Blocks Your Love?

The move is paired with this reframe: every should about the past is impossible. You could not have done other than what you did. The should is the lie. Curse it.
