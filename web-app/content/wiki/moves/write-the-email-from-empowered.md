# Write the Email From the Fully Empowered Version of Yourself

## The Move

When a leader is about to write a critically important communication that they are loaded with shame, guilt, or fear about -- a layoff announcement, a public failure acknowledgment, a difficult board update, a partnership dissolution -- the coaching move is to redirect the question "what should I say?" into "how would the fully empowered version of you look at this and write it?"

The framing relocates the author. Instead of writing from the collapsed, shamed, guilty version of the self (the one who is most accessible in the moment), the leader writes from the version of themselves that has full ownership of the decision, full clarity about the necessity of it, and full care for the people on the receiving end.

Joe coached Johannes Langgraf to write his 30%-layoff email this way. The email was about to be sent in a state Johannes described as "a lot of shame and guilt." The coaching redirected the author position.

"You told me to write this email in a way of how an empowered, like fully empowered person would look at that. Because, you know, like 30% of the company is like a lot. People rely on you, they trust in you, you know, they trust you and you make that decision in a way, you know, yeah, like you feel, or I felt like a lot of shame and guilt as well as part of that. And going through that, though, and communicating through that has really, really shaped a lot of our culture." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

## Why This Works

The shame-loaded leader writes a different email than the empowered leader, even when they are the same person, on the same day, about the same decision. The shame-loaded version of the email leaks the shame into the recipients, who then have to manage the leader's shame on top of their own loss. The empowered version conveys the same hard news but with the steadiness that lets the recipients have their own response without also absorbing the leader's collapse.

The mechanism: see [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]]. The team reads the leader's nervous-system state through the communication, regardless of the content. A communication written from collapse propagates collapse. A communication written from empowerment propagates the ability to meet the hard news from a stable place.

The empowered version of the leader is not pretending the situation is not painful. The empowered version is the one who has fully accepted that the decision is theirs, that the consequences are real, that the people affected deserve clarity and care, and that none of those facts are softened by the leader being smaller in the email.

See [[empowered-vs-power]] for the underlying distinction. Empowered is not the same as forceful. The empowered email is often gentler than the shame-loaded email, because the empowered author is not defending against the shame -- they have already absorbed it and can focus on the recipient.

## What the Move Asks the Leader to Do

The leader is asked to do two things in sequence:

**Feel the shame and guilt.** Not bypass it. Not write the email before the emotions have been moved through. The empowered version cannot be accessed by skipping over the difficult emotional content. The empowered author exists on the other side of having felt the difficult content, not before it.

**Write from the post-feeling state.** Once the emotion has been moved through (not eliminated, but no longer running the show), the leader writes from the version of themselves that the emotion was protecting them from accessing. That version is the empowered author. The email they write is structurally different from the email they would have written before doing the emotional work.

This is the operationalization of [[embrace-intensity]] applied to a specific moment in leadership: the intensity is the shame and guilt about the decision; the embrace is feeling it through; the post-embrace version of the leader is the one who can write the email.

## What the Empowered Email Looks Like

The empowered email shares several features that distinguish it from the shame-loaded version:

- **Clear ownership of the decision.** Not "circumstances forced us to" but "I am making this decision because." The empowered author takes the decision as theirs.
- **Direct acknowledgment of the impact.** Not "we know this is hard for some of you" but "this is going to hurt, here is what we are doing about it, here is what you can expect from us."
- **Care without collapse.** The recipient can feel that the leader cares without having to manage the leader's grief. The leader's grief was processed before the email was written.
- **Honesty about what the leader does not know.** The empowered author can name uncertainty without it reading as panic.
- **Practical specifics that protect the recipient.** Severance terms, support structures, references, transitional resources -- the empowered author thinks about what the recipient needs operationally as well as emotionally.

The communication itself becomes a model of the principle. The team that watches the leader write a hard communication from the empowered place learns what the principle looks like in operation. Future hard communications across the organization tend to follow the pattern that the first one established. See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]].

## When Joe Reaches for This Move

- A leader is about to send a communication that they are blocked on because the emotional load is too high
- The leader is drafting and re-drafting and cannot finish because the draft keeps coming out wrong
- The communication is a defining moment for the company's culture and the leader knows it
- The leader is in shame or guilt about a decision they nevertheless need to communicate clearly
- A board update, layoff announcement, project shutdown, or partnership dissolution is pending
- The leader is asking "what should I say?" when the actual question is "from where should I say it?"

## What the Move Does Not Do

The move does not write the email for the leader. The leader still has to do the writing. The coaching move only relocates the author position; the content is the leader's.

The move does not eliminate the difficulty of the communication. The recipient is still going to receive hard news. The move only changes the texture of how the hard news arrives.

The move does not bypass the emotional work. If the leader writes the email from a performed-empowered place while privately still in collapse, the recipients will feel it. The empowered author is real, not performed. The performed version reads as cold or evasive, not as steady.

## The "Most Important Email You'll Ever Write" Frame

The move comes with a specific framing Joe gave Johannes about the layoff communication: this email will be the most important email Johannes ever writes to his team. The framing matters because it signals how seriously to take the author-position question. It is not a normal email being written from the empowered place as a refinement. It is an email whose author position will be visible to the team for years to come, and the version the leader chooses to write from will set a precedent for everything that follows.

"You said this will be the most important email that you will write to your team, which it also is." -- Johannes Langgraf, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The frame is operational. It tells the leader to invest more time in accessing the right author than they would in any other email. It also tells the team, by the texture of what arrives, what to expect from this leader going forward.

This is the same logic as [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]] applied to the inflection points where the culture gets shaped most: when something genuinely hard has to be communicated, the way it is communicated becomes the model for how the team handles hard things in the future. The leader who lets this moment pass without the empowered-author work has unconsciously installed a culture of shame-loaded hard conversations.

## What This Move Produces

The Johannes example illustrates the downstream effect. The layoff email that came out of this coaching became a defining cultural artifact for the company. The shared understanding that "we open the drawer here, we have hard conversations from the empowered place" was modeled in the most consequential communication the company had ever sent. Subsequent hard conversations across the organization inherited the pattern.

"Going through that, though, and communicating through that has really, really shaped a lot of our culture." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles

The cultural shaping is the downstream effect that justifies the coaching investment. The single email is the seed; the cultural pattern is what grows from it. Leaders who do not do this work on the hardest communications often build organizations where every hard communication is a fresh negotiation with collapse.

## Tone

Joe delivers this move quietly and as a structural redirect. The leader is not being told they are wrong to feel shame or guilt. They are being shown that those feelings are not the right author for the email. The redirect is gentle but firm: the email needs to be written from somewhere else, and the somewhere else is accessible after the emotion has been moved through.
