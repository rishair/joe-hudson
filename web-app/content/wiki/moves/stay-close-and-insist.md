# Stay Close and Insist (Boundary-Setting With Young Kids Without Distance or Force)

## The Move

When setting a boundary with a young child, do not deliver the boundary from a distance. Stay close, physically and emotionally, and frame the requirement as something you and the child are doing together. "We are brushing our teeth now." Not "You need to go brush your teeth."

"It's not like you need to go and do this. It's like, we're gonna — like, there's like a support and a gentleness in the boundaries." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The move has two parts that fit together:

**Close.** You are physically with the child as the requirement is being met. You are not yelling from another room. You are not sending the child off to do it alone. You are present for the difficult thing.

**Insist.** The requirement is not optional. The boundary is firm. The child does not have the option of negotiating their way out of teeth-brushing. The firmness is real.

Most parents fail by doing one without the other. The parent who insists without staying close becomes the demanding voice from across the house, which the child experiences as distance and pressure. The parent who stays close without insisting collapses the boundary into mush, which leaves the child without the structure they actually need to feel safe.

## Why The Boundary Is Necessary in the First Place

Children have two pulls that create internal friction.

"There's like a part of a kid that's like, I want to be independent, I want to do my own thing. And there's a part of the kid who's like, I want to feel safe. And I need boundaries to feel safe. And those two things create friction inside of the child. And if you don't give the boundaries, they feel unsafe. And so they start acting out more and more and more to try to get the boundaries." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

A child without boundaries does not get the freedom the parent imagines they are giving. The child gets escalating dysregulation until the parent finally provides the boundary or until the child finds another way to feel a limit (often by getting hurt, getting in trouble, or breaking something significant). The acting out is the child's request for the limit they need.

This is why "no boundaries" is not love. It is neglect dressed as permissiveness. See [[boundaries]] for the broader teaching and [[walled-no-vs-open-hearted-limit]] for the distinction between cold limits and warm ones.

## What "Stay Close" Does for the Child

Boundary-setting is hard for the child. There is a real disappointment when the thing they wanted is not available. There is real frustration when the thing they did not want has to be done. The parent who stays close to the child through this is communicating: "I am here with you while this is hard. You are not being abandoned in your difficulty. You are loved while this is happening."

The parent who delivers the boundary and walks away is communicating the opposite. The child experiences the disappointment alone, with no one to be in it with them. The disappointment becomes an experience of abandonment, which the child then resists with everything they have. The next time the parent reaches for the same boundary, the child resists earlier and harder.

This is why staying close lowers the resistance over time. The child learns that boundaries do not mean abandonment. The boundary itself becomes something the child can move through without bracing against.

## What "Insist" Does for the Child

A child who can negotiate their way out of brushing their teeth has learned that boundaries are unreliable. The reliability of the limit is what makes the limit useful. The child needs to know that some things are simply going to happen. This frees the child from carrying the responsibility for everything. The boundary holds the structure so the child can be a child.

A parent who waffles on the limit installs in the child the felt sense that nothing is firm, which the child often experiences as unsafety. The acting out increases.

The insistence does not have to be harsh. It is more like a kind certainty. "We are brushing our teeth now" delivered with warmth, presence, and the absence of any opening to negotiation, is a sentence that does both jobs: it tells the child you are here, and it tells the child this is happening.

## What "Insist" Looks Like When a Kid Is Yelling At You

The same move applies in the specific case of a child yelling at the parent. Most parents experience the kid's yelling as an attack and either yell back, withdraw, or pretend not to hear. The move is none of these.

"That's not how we talk to each other. But if you're angry, let's go get angry. I really want you to be able to be pissed, and then let's come back to -- or if you can talk to me with respect, let's do that." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The boundary holds the form of communication (we don't yell at each other) while affirming the legitimacy of the emotion (anger is welcome -- here is where to take it). The parent might even go get angry with the child: pound on a bed together, scream into a pillow. The emotion is honored; the form is held.

The structural reframe Joe offers is to imagine the inverse. Imagine being yelled at by a 1,000-pound, 10-foot man. What would that do to your nervous system? That is the experience the 2-year-old is having when you yell at them. The size differential is real. The respect the parent demands of the child has to be reciprocated -- the parent must also speak to the child in a way that does not flood the child's system.

"Imagine if there was some like 1,000-pound, 10-foot man or 20-foot man yelling at me at the top of their lungs. Like, what would that do to my fucking system? Like a grizzly bear just being like, whoa, respect, freaked out. And yet this is what this little 2-year-old's dealing with if I'm getting angry like that. I am huge. I am loud." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This connects to the same general principle as "treat them the way you want them to treat others." Joe and Tara learned this from the nibbling example: they would nibble the kids playfully, but then the kids started biting them. The kids were not being naughty. They were mimicking. The fix was for the parents to stop nibbling. The kids stopped biting.

"If you want your kids to treat you with respect, treat them with respect. They will mimic you way quicker than they will do what you tell them to do." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The principle scales beyond yelling. Whatever the parent wants the child to learn, the parent has to embody. The verbal instruction is the secondary channel. The modeled behavior is the primary one.

## The "We" Language

The grammatical move matters more than it sounds. "You need to brush your teeth" puts the requirement on the child alone, framed as a command. "We are brushing our teeth now" puts the requirement on a shared activity that you are doing together. The child is not isolated by the boundary; they are joined in it.

This is also the same general principle in [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] applied to parenting. The boundary is with the child rather than against the child. The "we" language is the surface marker that the underlying orientation is connection, not control.

The parent who is annoyed at the child may have trouble using the "we" language sincerely. That is information. If the "we" feels fake, the parent's actual state is "you" (against), and the child will read the actual state regardless of the words. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]] for why the words alone cannot save a state-mismatch. The fix is to address the parent's actual state first, then issue the boundary.

## What This Move Looks Like Across Ages

**Toddlers and young children.** "We are going inside now." "We are putting our shoes on." "We are saying goodbye." The we-language and physical proximity carry the boundary.

**Older children.** The we-language softens but the principle holds. "I need you to do this thing. I am here with you while we figure out how that is going to work." The physical proximity may not be needed; the emotional proximity still is.

**Teenagers.** The boundary becomes a "lane to swim in." Treat people with respect. Contribute. Take responsibility. The parent stays close in the sense of being available and in connection, not in the sense of micro-managing. See [[teach-teenagers-to-be-their-own-authority]] for the parallel principle at the teenage stage.

The age-appropriate form changes. The orientation — close and firm, with not against — does not.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Insisting from a distance.** "I told you to brush your teeth!" yelled from down the hall. The insistence is there. The closeness is missing. The child experiences pressure without contact.
- **Staying close but not insisting.** "Oh sweetie, please brush your teeth, would you do that for me?" delivered with warmth but no firmness. The child reads correctly that this is optional and continues whatever they were doing.
- **Using "we" insincerely.** Saying "we are brushing our teeth now" while the parent is internally angry and pushing the agenda. The child reads the actual state and either complies under pressure or rebels.
- **Treating the boundary as a battle to be won.** The boundary is not about winning. It is about providing the structure the child needs to feel safe.

## When This Move Is the Right One

- A young child who is escalating because the parent has not held a clear limit
- A parent who has been delivering boundaries from a distance and getting more resistance, not less
- A parent who has been collapsing every limit and is now watching the child spin
- A parent who is angry at the child for refusing the requirement
- A parent who wants the child to comply but does not want the relationship to become adversarial

## When This Move Does Not Apply

The move is for boundary-setting with young children whose primary mode is co-regulation through presence. It is not the move for situations where the child is legitimately asking for autonomy and the parent is treating routine independence as a battle. The 8-year-old who wants to dress themselves does not need "we are putting our pants on now." They need permission to do it on their own.

Read what the child is actually asking for. The move applies when the child needs structure and connection. It does not apply when the child needs space.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent in the throes of a power struggle with a 3-year-old over routines
- A parent watching their boundary-setting escalate the conflict rather than resolve it
- A parent who has been told they are too soft or too harsh and does not know how to find the middle
- A parent of a younger sibling who is acting out
- A parent who is trying to undo a pattern of either harshness or permissiveness inherited from their own childhood

## Tone

Warm and firm at the same time. This is hard to write down because most language presents these as opposites. The actual tone is something like a kind certainty. Not a harshness softened by sweetness — a fundamental presence that is also a fundamental clarity about what is happening. Joe describes it as gentle insistence. The gentleness and the insistence are equal partners.
