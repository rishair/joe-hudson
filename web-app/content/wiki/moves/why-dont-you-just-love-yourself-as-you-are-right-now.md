# Why Don't You Just Love Yourself As You Are Right Now?

## The Move

When the person has identified that the pushing/trying/performing was originally installed as the price of love ("I had to be something else to be loved"), cut to the chase. "So why don't we just cut to the chase? Why don't you just love yourself as you are right now? There is a feeling of love. Feel that love and put it towards you."

The move bypasses the strategy the person built. The whole pushing-to-be-different-to-get-love structure rests on the assumption that love has to be earned by being other-than-you-are. The cut-to-the-chase move declines that assumption and asks for the love now, in this body, in this moment, directed at this self.

It is the same somatic operation as [[love-as-deep-welcoming]] — locate the feeling of love that's already in the body, redirect it at the self. The novelty of this move is its timing: it arrives the moment the person has surfaced the conditional-love structure. Joe does not pause to explore the structure. He uses the surfacing as the entry point for the move.

## When Joe Uses It

This move is the typical follow-up to [[what-makes-you-push-yourself]] when the answer is some version of "I had to be something else to be loved" or "I needed to do X to be loved" or "they only loved me when I was Y."

The setup matters. The move does not land cold. It requires the person to have just surfaced the structural truth that their pushing was the strategy for getting love. Once that's surfaced, the strategy is briefly visible to the person as a strategy — a thing they're doing, not the way the world works. In that brief window, the move lands. The person can ask: why am I running the strategy when I could just give myself what the strategy was trying to earn?

The move is short. It does not lecture about self-love. It does not explain why the strategy doesn't work. It invites the operation directly.

## What This Move Produces

The response is usually some version of "I can't give it to myself" or "when I try to give it to myself, I want to run" or "it's like I've given that love to everyone else." This is data. The system has been giving the love outward; it has not been giving it inward. The redirect is structurally difficult.

The move opens the next layer of work: what does the running look like? What gets activated when the love is directed at the self? Often the answer reveals the next pattern — the running, the self-judgment, the body retreat. Each of these becomes its own work, but the work is now happening inside the territory of self-love rather than inside the territory of pushing.

The visible signature of partial landing: the person becomes briefly softer, often tearful, often confused. They report a felt complication ("it's like I want to run"). The complication is the work. Joe stays with whatever surfaces — often via [[feel-bad-as-the-running]] or [[its-okay-to-run-no-judgment]] — rather than redirecting back to "but you should be able to love yourself."

## What Not to Do

- **Do not lecture about self-love before the move.** The move depends on its directness. Adding context dilutes the leverage.
- **Do not soften the move into a question about whether they could try.** "Why don't you just love yourself?" is the move. "Do you think you could try to love yourself a little?" is not. The directness is the operative quality.
- **Do not abandon the move if the first response is "I can't."** The "I can't" is the next data. Stay with what's preventing the move from landing.
- **Do not deploy this move without the structural setup.** The person has to have just surfaced the conditional-love structure. Without that surfacing, the move sounds like generic self-help advice and lands as a should.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "So what makes you push yourself?"
- **Client:** "Like I had to be something else to be loved. The kid."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So why don't we just cut to the chase? Why don't you just love yourself as you are right now? There is a feeling of love. Feel that love and put it towards you."
- **Client:** "It's like I've given that love to everyone else."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- **Client:** "Feels like when I try to give it to myself, I want to run."
- **Joe:** "That's what you're doing. Okay, running, running, running. There you go. You let — you're letting some in. See what it's like to let, let a little more in. Running, running, running, running. Running, running. It's okay to run, by the way. I don't have any judgment."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Stop Trying So Hard)*

The arc: the move is issued; the response surfaces the next layer (the love has been outward not inward); the redirect produces the running; Joe stays with the running rather than fighting it; the running becomes its own work via [[its-okay-to-run-no-judgment]].

## What This Move Reveals About Joe's Coaching Stance

The move embodies a specific principle: when the strategy for getting X has been surfaced, ask for X directly. Don't work the strategy. Don't analyze why the strategy fails. Just request the thing the strategy was a workaround for.

This is structurally how Joe coaches across many concerns. The client wanting purpose: cut to the chase, what would you do if you knew you had it. The client wanting their partner to change: cut to the chase, what would you do if they wouldn't. The client wanting to feel loved: cut to the chase, love yourself now. The pattern is to dissolve the means-end loop by asking for the end directly.

The reason the move works is the same reason it's hard: the strategy has been the person's identity. Asking for the end directly threatens the strategy. The threat is what makes the move productive. The discomfort that arises is the strategy resisting its own dissolution. Joe stays with the discomfort.

## Tone

Plain, direct, slightly playful. The "why don't we just cut to the chase" carries some lightness — it's an invitation to skip the deliberation, not an accusation that the person has been wasting time. The instruction that follows ("feel that love and put it towards you") is matter-of-fact, like an instruction for a body move that's available now.

The voice is warm but the move is firm. Joe is not asking permission to ask the question. He is asking the question.

## When to Use This Move

- After [[what-makes-you-push-yourself]] when the answer surfaces conditional-love structure
- When the person has identified that they had to be something else to be loved as a child
- When the person has been working a long strategy to earn love that they could give themselves directly
- When the means-end loop around love has been surfaced and the directness of the move can puncture it
- When the work has gone too far into analysis of the structure and needs to return to the body

## The Turn-Toward-the-Self-Conscious-Part Variant

A specific operational variant of this move: when the love has been activated in the body for *someone else in the room* (not just for the self in the abstract), use that love as the medium to direct at a specific protective part that has just shown up.

The form: "I want you to turn that love towards [the self-consciousness / the part that's scared / the part that wants to be something]. Feel the love that you have for all these people, and then just turn it towards [the part]."

The variant is more precise than the general "love yourself now" because it identifies the exact protective part as the target and uses a present-tense activated love-feeling as the medium. The variant tends to be more effective when self-consciousness has just rushed in to manage an opening — the love is right there in the body, and the part that needs it has just announced itself.

See [[turn-the-love-toward-the-self-conscious-part]] for the full move article. The relationship: this article is the general cut-to-the-chase move; that article is the variant for the specific moment when self-consciousness contracts after an opening.

The two moves share the same somatic operation (locate love in the body, redirect at self) with different specifications:
- This move: love at the self in the abstract, after the conditional-love structure has been surfaced.
- The variant: love at a specific protective part, after the opening-then-contraction sequence has just happened.
