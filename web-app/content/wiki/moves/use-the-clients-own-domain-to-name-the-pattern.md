# Use the Client's Own Domain to Name the Pattern

## The Move

When a client has a domain of expertise (fitness training, music teaching, surgery, programming, coaching), use that domain to teach them the pattern operating in the area they came in about. Do not import a metaphor from outside their experience. Build the metaphor from the principle they already teach others.

The structural form: ask them what happens when one of their students/clients/patients does the thing they are themselves doing. Let them answer. Then point at the parallel. The teaching is delivered in vocabulary they have mastered.

The move is a sibling of [[use-domain-of-mastery-as-template-for-body-intelligence]], which uses the domain's flow state as the template for body intelligence. This move uses the domain's failure mode as the diagnostic for the client's current failure mode. Where the parent move asks "you do this when it works — do it here," this move asks "you know what happens when this doesn't work — that's what's happening to you now."

"What happens when somebody is training and they get in their head? They probably get hurt. How about that? Yeah. How is that any different than what just happened to you?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground

The recognition lands when the client says the principle aloud in their own words about their own field. The teaching has been delivered by the client to the client. The coach's role was to ask the question that produced the teaching.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the client has a coaching, training, or teaching practice in some domain (fitness, music, sport, surgery, programming) and the pattern operating in their personal life is structurally identical to a failure mode they coach their students through.
- After the in-room pattern has been surfaced and the client has acknowledged it but the bridge to their actual life problem has not yet been made. The domain analogy is what builds the bridge.
- When a direct teaching from the coach would land as instruction-from-above and the client needs to discover the principle from inside their own expertise.
- When the client's defensive system would block a generic teaching but cannot block their own teaching delivered back to them.
- When the pattern is most visible in a body-knowledge context (athletics, dance, surgery, music) and the client's life pattern is the same head-vs-body split.
- When the [[proving-instead-of-connecting]] read is operating and the client's domain is one in which they would never coach students to prove themselves (because the proving would interfere with the performance).

The move requires the client's domain to actually contain the principle. A management consultant coaching strategy may not have the somatic-vs-cognitive distinction available. A martial arts teacher does. The coach has to know enough about the domain to find the parallel principle. Better to ask the client what they teach than to project a generic version onto their work.

## What It Produces

**The client teaches themselves.** The diagnosis arrives in the client's own voice, in their own field's vocabulary, applied to themselves. The acceptance is immediate because the teaching is theirs. There is no resistance to overcome because no external authority is being asserted.

**The pattern becomes operational.** Generic teachings about "trying too hard" or "be present" are easy to nod to and not implement. The fitness coach who knows what happens when students get in their head has a precise, somatic, embodied understanding of the same principle. The translation from teaching to action is much shorter because the principle is already known operationally.

**The bridge from the room to the life gets built in the room.** The client does not have to construct the application later. The domain analogy is the application. They already do this in their work. The instruction is to do it in the rest of their life.

**The client's expertise is honored as expertise.** The move treats the client's mastery as real knowledge, not as decoration. This produces a quality of partnership — the coach is not coming in from above but is using what the client already knows. The client feels seen as someone with knowledge, not just as someone with problems.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Identify the client's domain of expertise.** Either they have already named it (often by saying what their business is) or ask briefly. The domain has to be one they coach or teach others in, where they have an articulate framework for what works and what doesn't.

2. **Find the parallel principle in their domain.** Often this is the body-vs-head distinction (athletics, music, surgery, performance), the connection-vs-pushing distinction (sales, leadership, parenting), or the listening-vs-performing distinction (any teaching practice). Ask yourself what failure mode in their domain matches the pattern you've just surfaced in their life.

3. **Ask them what happens to their students/clients/patients when the failure mode occurs.** "What happens when somebody is training and they get in their head?" Let them answer. Do not lead. They will name the consequence accurately because they have seen it many times.

4. **Ask the parallel question about their current situation.** "How is that any different than what just happened to you?" The juxtaposition does the teaching. The client almost always answers, "It's pretty much the same thing."

5. **Use the same domain's vocabulary for the solution.** "Where do you go when you're training that doesn't keep you hurt?" Their answer ("in your body") becomes the instruction for themselves. The fitness coach who tells students to stay in their body is told to stay in their body.

6. **Roleplay the absurdity if useful.** "So I'm training with you, I keep getting in my head. You're like, dude, get in your body or you're going to hurt yourself. And I'm like, yeah, but it's not that easy. What do you say to me?" The client hears their own counter-argument in the mouth of a struggling student and has to address it. The address is what loosens the same counter-argument in themselves.

7. **Operationalize the transfer.** "So how do you do a reach out to a potential client like this?" Now the client has to perform the bridged behavior in real time. The principle they teach is being applied to the work they have been failing at. See [[be-my-potential-client-show-me]] for the specific demonstration move.

## Why It Works

The move works because the client has spent years building a sophisticated, operational understanding of a particular principle as it applies to their domain. They have language for it. They have seen it succeed and fail many times. They know what to look for. They know what the remedy is. All of that knowledge is sitting in their head about their domain. None of it has been applied to themselves.

The defensive system is calibrated to defend the self-image. Generic teachings about the self trigger the defense. The same teaching, delivered as a question about their professional expertise, does not trigger the defense — because the client is being asked to perform expertise, not to receive correction. By the time the parallel is named, the principle has already been articulated. The defense has nothing to attack.

The somatic anchor also matters. A trainer who has felt — in their own body, watching students many times — what happens when someone trains in their head is anchored to that recognition somatically. The principle is not a concept. It is a felt-recognized pattern. When the same pattern is named in the client's own behavior, the recognition arrives somatically too. The body knows what the principle is even before the mind catches up.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence with a fitness coach who had 9-10 failed business attempts and was trying to push himself to the right answer in the coaching session:

- **Joe:** "What happens when somebody is training and they get in their head?"
- **Client:** "It's not very good."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. What happens specifically?"
- **Client:** "They probably get hurt."
- **Joe:** "How about that? Yeah. How is that any different than what just happened to you?"
- **Client:** "It's pretty much the same thing."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And where do you go when you're training that doesn't — keeps you not hurt?"
- **Client:** "In your body."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And when you're just being you, connecting to yourself, where are you?"
- **Client:** "In my body."
- **Joe:** "And when you're trying to get to the resolution, where are you?"
- **Client:** "In my head."
- *(Source: 2025-10-02_Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground)*

The dialogue is short. Each Joe question prompts a one-line answer. The client builds the entire diagnostic from his own domain knowledge. By the end, the principle has been stated four times by the client about himself.

Then Joe ran the absurdity roleplay:

- **Joe:** "So I'm training with you, I keep getting in my head. You're like, dude, get in your body or you're going to hurt yourself. And I'm like, yeah, but it's not that easy. What do you say to me? It can't be that easy. I keep on hurting myself. I mean, all I have to do is just be in my body."
- **Client:** "In a way, yes."
- **Joe:** "And what's the — what's in a way, no? What am I missing?"
- **Client:** "Well, I guess if they don't know, if they've never done that or they don't know how to do it. Yeah, it's like an instruction without how to — right — into action."
- *(Source: 2025-10-02_Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground)*

The client's answer to the absurd roleplay names the actual gap: the instruction is not enough; the body needs to be trained to do the thing. The client has now provided the curriculum for himself. The training is what comes next.

## Tone

Curious and matter-of-fact. The questions are real questions, not Socratic gotchas. Joe is asking the client to teach him something about training. The client is the expert. The tone honors that.

The pivot from "what happens to your students" to "how is that different than what just happened to you" is delivered without ceremony. No "ahh, see..." moment. Just the next question. The lack of drama lets the recognition land on its own.

The absurdity roleplay is delivered with a quality of friendly play. Joe is voicing the client's resistance back at them through the persona of a struggling student. The play makes the resistance visible without making the client defend it.

## Variations Across Domains

- **For a musician teaching students:** "What happens when one of your students is in their head about a passage they know? What do you tell them to do?" The performance state in music is the template for the life state.
- **For a martial arts instructor:** "What happens when a student is anticipating the move instead of being with the moment? What do you have them do?" The presence required in sparring is the template.
- **For a therapist or coach:** "When you have a client who is trying to push themselves to an insight rather than letting it arrive, what do you do?" The professional practice with their own clients is the template.
- **For a surgeon:** "When a resident is making the cut from their head rather than from the hands' knowledge, what happens? What do you do?" The surgical attention is the template.
- **For a parent (parenting is the expertise being borrowed):** "When your kid is trying to be perfect for you, what do you actually want from them?" The parent's wish for their child is the template for what they could give themselves.
- **For a chef:** "When a line cook is in their head about timing instead of in the kitchen rhythm, what happens to the dish?" The cooking flow state is the template.

The move requires the coach to know the domain well enough to name the parallel principle credibly. If the domain is unfamiliar, ask the client to teach you. "What do you do with a student who is in their head?" The asking is itself the move.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The client has a domain of mastery (often visible because it is their business or their work).
- A pattern has been surfaced in the coaching session — often [[proving-instead-of-connecting]], [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]], or the body-vs-head split.
- The client has acknowledged the pattern intellectually but the operational implication has not landed.
- A generic teaching from the coach would not land or would be resisted.
- The client's defensive system is alert to direct correction but open to being asked about their expertise.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not project a generic version of the principle onto a domain you do not understand.** The fitness coach knows what "in your head" means in fitness specifically. Generic gestures at "be present" will not land with the same force. Use their actual vocabulary or ask them to provide it.
- **Do not skip the client's answer.** The move works because the client articulates the principle. If the coach articulates the principle and asks the client to confirm, the bridge has been built by the coach, not the client. The recognition will be weaker.
- **Do not turn the move into a metaphor lecture.** The form is question, answer, question, answer. Each Joe question is short. The client's answer carries the teaching. Long coach speech defeats the structure.
- **Do not use the move when the client's domain does not actually contain the principle.** If the analogy is forced, the client will sense it and the defensive system will reject the diagnosis along with the strained metaphor.
- **Do not deploy this in place of feeling-work when the work is feeling-work.** This move is for surfacing patterns and bridging recognitions. If the client needs to feel something they have been avoiding, the move can be a useful entry but cannot replace the felt-experience work.
