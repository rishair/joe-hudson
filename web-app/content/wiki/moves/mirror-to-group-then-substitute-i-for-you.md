# Mirror to the Group, Then Substitute I for You

## The Move

A three-stage move used when working with self-shame and self-criticism in a group setting. The person is invited to say what they tell themselves out loud to the group, first as the critical voice, then as the loving voice without shoulds, then turning the loving voice inward.

Stage 1: Have them look at everyone in the room and say the harsh thing out loud as if to all of them. ("You should feel ashamed for cutting yourself off from your own self-love.")

Stage 2: Have them open their heart and say what they actually want to say to all of them — and then notice every "should" that creeps in and ask them to say it again without the shoulds. ("You all deserve love. You all deserve the ability to live without obligation.")

Stage 3: Have them say the same thing with "I" instead of "you." ("I deserve love. I deserve to be loved.")

## When Joe Uses It

When the person has identified self-shame or self-criticism but cannot feel the alternative directly. The internal voice that says "you should..." is too close, too automatic, too embedded to interrupt. Putting it outside, in the group, breaks the loop. The person can see the cruelty of the voice when it is aimed at other people. They can also feel the love they want to offer when it is aimed at other people — and then that love is right there to turn back inward.

The move is especially powerful when:
- The person can intellectually see the pattern but cannot feel it
- The person is performing the work rather than doing it
- The person can love others but not themselves (the [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]] mismatch)
- There is a live group available — eye contact with real people is the multiplier

## The Detailed Sequence

**Setup.** Have the person change the screen view (in a virtual setting) or look at the room so they can see every face. The visual is part of the move. The group must be present, not abstract.

**The pivot.** Establish a universal: "Every single person you're looking at has done this." (In this transcript: cut themselves off from love because of obligation.) This makes the shame the person feels not personal but shared. The room is a mirror.

**Stage 1: Project the should outward.** Have them say the harsh self-judgment out loud to the group. "You should feel ashamed for cutting yourself off from your own self-love." Then ask: "How does that feel to say to them?" The person almost always recognizes immediately: "Pretty unfair." The cruelty is visible because it is aimed outward at people they can see.

**Stage 2: Open the heart, but watch for shoulds.** Now ask what they actually want to say to all these people — but specifically: "Can you do that again without any shoulds? Just to see if it feels more or less in alignment." The shoulds will sneak in. Each "you should" that creeps in is itself the demonstration. The person starts noticing the shoulds in their own speech and often laughs or curses at how prevalent they are.

**Stage 3: Turn the I inward.** "Now just say the same thing with I instead of you." The person now has the loving sentence right there, ready, in the warm tone — and just has to substitute the pronoun. "I deserve love. I deserve to be loved. I deserve to live without fear."

## What It Produces

The move bypasses the inner critic's defenses by routing love through the group first. The person discovers:

- The voice's harshness is visible only when externalized
- The loving alternative is available to them — they can offer it to others freely
- The shoulds are everywhere — they are not just one harsh sentence but a structural feature of how they talk
- The love offered to the group can be turned back on the self with almost no friction once it is already spoken

Felt response is often visible: the body softens, the face changes, the voice slows. In the transcript: "Yeah, I, I can feel you feeling it. All the way."

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** Every single person you're looking at has done this... So I just want you to say out loud that to all of them, you should feel ashamed.
- **Client:** You should all feel ashamed for cutting yourself off from your own self-love.
- **Joe:** Yeah, how does that feel to say to them?
- **Client:** Pretty unfair.
- **Joe:** Pretty fucking unfair. What do you want to say to them if you open your heart...
- **Client:** You should be free to love yourself, that you're human...
- **Joe:** Can you do that again without any shoulds?
- **Client:** I'm trying to think... God, that word, I hate that word.
- **Joe:** Isn't that great? Check it out.
- **Client:** Fucking word.
- **Joe:** It's the obligation. Every time that shit hits, it's obligation.
- **Joe:** Now just say the same thing with I instead of you.
- **Client:** I deserve love. I deserve to be loved. I deserve to live without fear...
- **Joe:** Yeah, I can feel you feeling it. All the way.

## Tone

Warm and active. The first stage has an almost confrontational edge — Joe is inviting the person to say something out loud they would never say. The middle stage is curious and slightly playful as the shoulds get noticed. The final stage is quiet and present. The whole sequence has a momentum to it; it is not a one-question move.

## What Not To Do

- Do not let the person say the shoulds and move on without noticing them. The noticing is part of the move.
- Do not let the third stage become a recitation. The "I deserve love" has to land in the body, not be performed.
- Do not skip stage 1. The cruelty has to be aimed outward first or the person will absorb the "you should" back into the inner critic.
- Do not skip stage 2. If you go from stage 1 directly to stage 3 ("you should be ashamed → I deserve love"), the work is not done. The pivot from the shoulds to the want has to happen first in the safer outer-direction before it can happen inward.
