# Prove to Me You're Not Enough

## The Move

After a limiting belief has been named — usually "I'm not enough" or a close variant — the coach challenges the client to use their mind to prove the belief is true.

"You're not enough. So let's use that mind. Let's put the mind to work in a great way. Prove to me you're not enough." — Joe Hudson, Why Your Boss Never Sees You

The move asks the mind that has been silently asserting the belief to publicly defend it. The mind, asked to make the case in front of the coach, almost always fails. The failure is the deconstruction. The belief that survives only in the dark dissolves when asked to perform in the light.

## When Joe Uses This

The move requires several preconditions:

- The limiting belief has been clearly named, usually by the client themselves
- The belief is one the client has been operating under for a long time (often a lifetime)
- The client has a strong, articulate mind — the move depends on the mind being capable of attempting the proof
- The relationship to the coach is established enough that the client trusts the challenge is not an attack
- Asking the body has just produced the belief as the answer, so the move arrives right after the somatic surfacing

The move is the second half of a two-part sequence:

1. [[ask-the-body-the-question]] surfaces the belief somatically. The client says (or the body says through the client) "I'm not enough."
2. The coach challenges the client to prove it.

The first part bypasses the mind to surface what is actually running the system. The second part hands the surfaced belief back to the mind with a new job — prove it — that the mind cannot fulfill.

In the source session, Joe walked the move exactly this way. He asked the client to put attention in his feet and let the body answer. The body produced "you're not enough." Joe then immediately said: "Yeah, that's, that's the cause of the burnout. You're not enough. So let's use that mind. Let's put the mind to work in a great way. Prove to me you're not enough."

The client's response was immediate: "No, I don't have an answer for that."

The move had landed. The mind that had been silently insisting "you're not enough" could not produce evidence when asked.

## What It Produces

The standard response is some version of "I can't prove it." The client tries for a moment, finds nothing, and reports the absence. The absence is the diagnostic. The belief that has been organizing the client's life for decades has no evidence behind it that survives being asked for.

The somatic response is often a small lightening. The mind that has been carrying the belief as a fact discovers it is carrying it as an unsupported assertion. The weight that has been operating drops. Joe in the source session named the body's report: "Yeah, you felt that too, didn't you?" — and the client confirmed: "Yes, I did."

The whole exchange takes about ten seconds. The brevity is part of the power. The belief has been heavy for years. The deconstruction is light. The asymmetry between the weight of the belief and the speed of its dissolution is itself the medicine.

If the client does produce arguments for the belief, the move enters a different phase. The arguments are then examined one at a time. Each one usually contains the same structural error: it treats a specific failure as evidence of total inadequacy, treats one person's judgment as cosmic truth, or treats childhood conditions as adult permanence. Joe walks through them. The arguments fail the closer they are looked at. The deconstruction is more work but the same direction.

## Why This Works

The move works because limiting beliefs do their work in the dark. The mind has been asserting "you're not enough" silently, automatically, without ever being asked to defend the assertion. The assertion has functioned as background truth, not as a claim that has been examined.

When the mind is asked to perform the assertion in front of a coach who is paying attention, the mind discovers what it has been doing. It has been making a claim it cannot support. The claim has been operating as truth because nothing was asking it to be true. The instant the demand for evidence arrives, the claim's nature changes from "fact about reality" to "assertion without basis."

This is structurally the same mechanism as [[mind-deconstructs-itself]]. The mind is being deployed to dissolve its own outputs. The specific form here is the legal-trial form: make the mind the prosecutor, demand it produce the case, watch the case collapse. The collapse is the deconstruction.

The move also works because it is respectful of the mind. The instruction is "let's put the mind to work in a great way" — the mind is not being attacked or asked to shut up. It is being given a job that uses its strength. The client whose mind is dominant feels the move as a deployment of capacity rather than as a suppression. The mind cooperates because the job is one it can do — and the failure to fulfill the job is its own data, not an attack on the mind itself.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client cannot produce a proof: Joe names the body's confirmation of the absence. "You felt that too, didn't you?" The naming anchors the somatic shift to the moment of failed proof. The client now has a felt experience of the belief being undefendable, which is more useful than the intellectual recognition that it might not be defendable.

After the naming, Joe moves immediately to the take-home framing. "So use your mind to deconstruct your mind. That's the best use for your mind right now." The single instance becomes the general practice. See [[mind-deconstructs-itself]].

If the client produces a proof that they themselves don't actually believe: Joe lets them notice the disbelief. "Is that the proof? Does that feel like the proof?" The body usually shakes its head. The client offered the proof out of obligation to the question, not out of conviction. The proof's hollowness is data.

If the client produces a proof that they do believe: Joe examines it. Usually the proof has a structural error — it overgeneralizes, it treats one event as a universal pattern, it confuses someone else's framing for cosmic truth. Joe asks one or two questions that surface the error. The proof modifies or collapses. The work then continues with whatever survives, which is usually less than the original belief.

If the client gets defensive or angry: the move was probably mistimed. The client was not ready to have the belief challenged. Joe backs off the challenge and returns to the surfacing work. The challenge is for clients who have just had the belief surface and are still in the somatic field where the belief is felt as available for examination. A client whose defenses have re-engaged needs a different move.

## Dialogue Example

The compressed exchange from the source session:

- **Joe (asking the body):** "Body, put your attention in your feet. What does your body have to say?"
- **Client:** "You're not enough." [The body's answer.]
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's, that's the cause of the burnout. You're not enough. So let's use that mind. Let's put the mind to work in a great way. Prove to me you're not enough."
- **Client:** "No, I don't have an answer for that."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, you felt that too, didn't you?"
- **Client:** "Yes, I did."
- **Joe:** "So use your mind to deconstruct your mind. That's the best use for your mind right now."

*(Source: Why Your Boss Never Sees You)*

The exchange shows the full arc: body surfaces the belief, mind is challenged to prove it, mind fails, body confirms the failure, take-home framing closes the loop. The total time is well under thirty seconds. The structural simplicity is what allows the speed.

## What This Move Reveals

The move reveals something about the structural status of limiting beliefs. The belief has been operating as a fact. The instant it is asked to defend itself, it is exposed as something other than a fact. The exposure is not an argument against the belief — the coach has not produced counter-evidence. The exposure is the discovery that the belief has been operating without evidence the whole time.

This is the same discovery available through the longer practice of [[apply-how-is-that-not-true-to-every-thought]], where every thought is examined for the place it breaks down. The move compresses the practice into a single in-session demonstration applied to the specific belief that is most heavily running the client's life.

The client who has just experienced the failed proof of "I'm not enough" has a portable reference point for future encounters with the same belief. The belief will return — beliefs that have been operating for decades do not disappear in one session. But the next time it shows up, the client knows the question to ask it: prove it. The asking of the question, even silently, is enough to begin the dissolution again.

## Tone

Direct, slightly playful, completely confident. The instruction is delivered with the energy of a teacher inviting a student to attempt an interesting problem, not with the energy of a prosecutor demanding an answer. The confidence is structural — Joe knows the proof will fail because no such proof exists. The confidence transfers to the client. The client attempts the proof from a position of curiosity rather than panic, which is part of why the failure can be metabolized as light rather than as humiliation.

The "let's put the mind to work in a great way" frame is critical. The phrasing positions the move as a use of the mind, not a fight against it. The mind that has been dominant in the client's life is being honored — it is being given a chance to do something it can be proud of. The fact that the something turns out to be the dissolution of the mind's own output is the surprise the move delivers.

After the proof fails, the tone shifts to recognition. "You felt that too, didn't you?" is gentle, almost intimate. The shared noticing of the body's response is the connection moment that lets the deconstruction land somatically rather than just intellectually.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not [[what-do-you-lose-if-this-belief-goes-away]].** That question surfaces the structural payoff of the belief — what job it has been doing. This move asks for the belief's truth — what evidence supports it. They address different aspects of why the belief persists. They can be used together: this move first (the belief has no evidence), then that question (and yet it persists because it is protecting something).

- **It is not an argument with the client.** The move is not "you're wrong that you're not enough" delivered as a position. The move is "show me the case" delivered as an invitation. The coach does not have a position on whether the client is enough. The coach is asking the client's own mind to produce the case the mind has been silently asserting. The work is the mind's, not the coach's.

- **It is not a reframe.** The move does not replace "I'm not enough" with "I'm enough." It dissolves the belief without supplying a substitute. The space the belief was occupying is left open. The body can then report what is actually there, which is often something more specific and more workable than "I'm enough" or "I'm not enough."

- **It is not for every limiting belief.** Some beliefs have evidence — childhood trauma, identifiable failures, specific patterns. Those beliefs require different work. This move is for the universal-claim beliefs that have been operating as facts about being ("I'm not enough," "I'm fundamentally broken," "I'm unloveable") without any specific evidence behind the universal claim. The diagnostic: if the belief is "I'm not enough" the move applies. If the belief is "I failed at X" the move probably doesn't apply directly.
