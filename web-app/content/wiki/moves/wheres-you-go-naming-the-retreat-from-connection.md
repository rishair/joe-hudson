# Where'd You Go? Naming the Retreat From the New State

## The Move

When the client has been guided into a new felt state — connection, love, openness, vulnerability — and then visibly pulls back into their previous shape, name it immediately in the present tense and offer them the choice of returning. The form Joe uses:

"Wait, where'd you go? Like, you, you went back to yourself. Like, you, you were, you were finding this new place and you went back. What, what made you go back?"

Followed by:

"Do you want to be there, or do you want to be in that place where you saw [the new state]?"

The move has two parts. First: name what happened ("you went back") with the specificity of where it happened ("you were finding this new place"). Second: explicitly offer the choice ("do you want to be there, or...") so the client owns the next move rather than being pulled by Joe.

## When Joe Uses It

The move arrives in the moments after a client has begun to find a new felt state — usually love, connection, or vulnerability — and the body subtly retreats. The retreat is often visible only to the coach: a small shift in posture, a flick of the eyes away, a change in voice quality, a sense that the connection that was just present has thinned.

In the divorce session, the move arrives after the client has had a moment of feeling love for the strangers on the call and has said "I love you" out loud — and then has explained why he cannot say that to strangers in normal life. The explanation itself is the retreat: the client has moved from the felt state into the explanation of why the felt state is not normally available. Joe catches it.

The move is most useful when:
- The client has had a real shift but the shift is fragile
- The body language of the retreat is visible to the coach in real time
- The session has the time to dwell in the new state rather than rushing onward
- The client has the capacity to notice their own retreat (so the naming lands as data, not as criticism)

## What It Produces

The most important thing the move produces is the client's awareness that they pulled back. Without the naming, the retreat happens unconsciously; the client returns to the previous state without ever knowing they had a choice. With the naming, the retreat becomes visible to the client. They can see they had a state, they left it, and they have the option to return.

The choice question — "do you want to be there, or do you want to be in that place where..." — converts the retreat from involuntary to elective. The client now has agency over which state they occupy. In most cases, when offered the choice, the client says they want the new state. The previous retreat had not been a preference; it had been a habit.

Joe's follow-up after the choice is usually a brief naming of why the new state is hard — "the sensation of loving people is fucking intense" — followed by an instruction to stay and to breathe. The naming validates the difficulty of the new state. The breath instruction gives the body the somatic support to actually stay.

## Why the Specificity Matters

The move depends on naming exactly what happened. Generic naming ("you seem distant") does not work. The specific form — "you were finding this new place and you went back" — does work, because it tells the client what was real (the new place was real, the finding was real) and what just happened (you went back). The specificity gives the client something to identify with the language of.

The specificity also prevents the move from landing as criticism. A vague "what just happened?" can be heard as "you did something wrong." The specific form is descriptive: here is what was happening, here is what just happened. The descriptive form invites the client to look at the same data, not to defend.

The "what made you go back?" question is also specific. It does not ask "why did you go back?" (which would invite a story) or "did you want to go back?" (which would invite a defensive answer). It asks what made it happen, which positions the retreat as something that happened to the client rather than something the client did wrong. This protects against the shame about the retreat that would itself become another retreat.

## The Choice Question Is the Pivot

"Do you want to be there, or do you want to be in that place where you saw the love that you had for everybody?"

This question is the move's deepest piece. It does not assume the client should want the new state. It offers both possibilities as legitimate. The client gets to actually choose.

When the client chooses the new state ("I do want to be in that place"), they are claiming it as theirs. The state is no longer something Joe led them to; it is something they have asserted they want. The asserting produces a different relationship to the state — they are in it because they have chosen it, not because the coach has produced it. This is more durable than coaches-produced states because it carries the client's own agency.

When the client chooses the old state (which is rare but happens), the data is also useful. It usually means the new state was overshooting the client's actual capacity in this moment, and the work needs to step back. The choice question is not a trap; it is a real offering.

## Dialogue Example

- **Client:** I love you is the only thing that really comes up.
- **Joe:** Yeah.
- **Client:** And it's something that I often find myself wanting to say to people, yeah, but I know it's not okay to just go out and say that to strangers.
- **Joe:** I know. I think I created this entire organization so that I could say that to strangers. Is that important to me? What happened? Wait, where'd you go? Like, you, you went back to yourself. Like, you, you were, you were finding this new place and you went back. What, what made you go back?
- **Client:** I don't know. I just, I guess I just shifted my body.
- **Joe:** Do you want to be there, or do you want to be in that place where you saw the love that you had for everybody?
- **Client:** I know. Yeah, I do want to be in that place.
- **Joe:** Yeah. So the only thing that's stopping you is that the sensation of loving people is fucking intense.
- *(Source: I Can't Get Over My Ex)*

The retreat is visible to Joe even though the client cannot at first name it ("I don't know. I just, I guess I just shifted my body"). The naming gives the client language for what just happened. The choice question moves the client back into the new state, now claimed. The closing observation — "the sensation of loving people is fucking intense" — names the difficulty of the new state without pathologizing the retreat from it.

## What Distinguishes This From Just Saying "Come Back"

The move is not "come back to the connection." It is "name what happened, give you the choice." The distinction matters.

If Joe simply said "come back," the client would either:
- Comply, returning to a state the coach is producing (not durable)
- Resist, because being told what to feel produces resistance
- Pretend, performing the state without actually returning to it

By naming the retreat and offering the choice, the client returns through their own agency. The return is theirs. They will be able to find their way back to the state on their own in the future because they have already done it once on their own (with Joe's naming as the support, not as the puppet-string).

The same logic applies to why Joe does not interpret the retreat ("you're afraid of intimacy"). Interpretation would let the client work intellectually on the interpretation rather than experientially with the choice. The interpretation comes later, if at all, after the live work has been done.

## Tone

Surprised and curious, not corrective. The "Wait, where'd you go?" carries a quality of genuine noticing, as if Joe is the one being caught off guard by the retreat. This tone matters because it positions Joe as a witness to something that happened, not as a coach correcting something the client did wrong. The witness tone is what allows the client to look at their own retreat without shame.

The "What, what made you go back?" is curious without being investigative. Joe is not trying to diagnose the cause. He is asking what the client noticed, treating the client as the authority on their own experience.

The follow-up — "the sensation of loving people is fucking intense" — is matter-of-fact and warm. The swearing keeps it from sounding clinical. The naming of the difficulty validates the client without making the new state into something requiring willpower; it simply names the intensity that is causing the retreat.

## What Often Causes the Retreat

The body retreats from a new felt state for predictable reasons. Knowing them helps the coach name what is happening when the retreat occurs:

- **The new state is more intense than the body is used to.** Joe names this directly in the source session: loving people is intense. The body has a baseline of permitted intensity. The new state exceeds it. The body returns to the permitted baseline.
- **The new state contradicts an identity.** "I am someone who does not feel love easily" cannot tolerate "I am feeling love right now." The identity reasserts itself by returning the body to the familiar shape.
- **The new state is unfamiliar and feels unsafe.** Even when the state is positive, unfamiliarity reads as risk to the nervous system. The body returns to the familiar even when the familiar is worse.
- **The shame that produced the original wall returns.** The wall closed because shame is operating. The wall opening lets the shame in fully for a moment. The body then re-closes to escape the shame. See [[shame-blocks-connection-in-real-time]].
- **A thought intervenes.** The client thinks something ("I cannot keep this," "this is not real," "I do not deserve this") and the thought collapses the state.

The move does not require the coach to identify which cause is operating. The naming and the choice question work regardless of the cause. The cause may become visible later through the work; the move itself does not need it.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not deploy on every micro-shift.** The body naturally moves in and out of states all the time. Use this move only when a meaningful new state has been reached and the retreat is from that state.
- **Do not deploy as a routine question.** The move depends on Joe's actual present attention. Asking "where'd you go?" as a script question (when the coach has not actually noticed a retreat) produces confusion rather than recognition.
- **Do not interpret the retreat.** The interpretation belongs to the client, not the coach. Joe asks what made it happen; he does not tell the client what made it happen.
- **Do not press if the client cannot identify the retreat.** "I don't know" is a fine answer. The naming itself is the work; the diagnosis is optional.
