# Point at the Present Evidence (You're Already Doing the Thing You Fear You Won't Do)

## The Move

When a person fears they will not be able to do something in the future, point at the place where they are already doing it. The evidence is right in the room. Reflect it back so they have to see it.

Two specific forms:

1. **The just-taken action.** "How did you make sure you quit your job?" The person came in worried they won't take care of themselves later. They just took an enormous self-care action. The move reflects the action back as the answer to their own question.

2. **The live conversation.** "You're also on this call talking to me. That's another self-care." The fact that they showed up to coaching is another instance of the capacity they fear they lack. The move points at the present moment as additional evidence.

These two forms are usually used together. The just-taken action establishes the capacity. The live conversation extends the evidence into the present tense so it cannot be dismissed as a one-time event.

## When Joe Uses This

- When someone has just taken an action that demonstrates a capacity and is then expressing fear that they won't have the capacity in the future.
- When the person's question contains its own answer ("how do I make sure I take care of myself" — they're doing it right now by being in coaching).
- When [[im-afraid-ill-revert-to-my-old-pattern]] is the concern and the person is dismissing the action they just took as evidence.
- When [[identity-lags-behind-growth]] is operating and the present-moment data needs to be foregrounded before the identity can update.
- When the person treats a capacity they're currently exercising as something they have to develop in the future.

The move is the present-tense companion to [[ask-the-witnesses]]. Ask-the-witnesses summons third-party reports of long-standing capacity. Point-at-the-present-evidence makes the person their own witness, with the evidence being what they are doing in this very session.

## Why It Works

The fear of not having a capacity assumes the capacity is absent or unreliable. The present evidence makes the absence claim impossible to maintain. The person cannot simultaneously assert "I won't take care of myself" while sitting in front of a coach having just quit a job to take care of themselves.

The cognitive contradiction is the leverage. The body usually feels it before the mind articulates it. There is a small drop, sometimes a laugh, sometimes an embarrassed "oh." The fear-claim has just been falsified by the present moment, and the person can feel it.

It also works because the evidence is not abstract. It is what happened five minutes ago and what is happening right now. There is no way to argue against it. The person can dismiss third-party reports as not knowing the real them. They cannot dismiss what they have visibly just done.

The move is grounded in [[identity-lags-behind-growth]]: the capacity is real and operating; the identity hasn't caught up. Putting the operating capacity in the room gives the identity something to reorganize around.

## Dialogue Example

The person came in saying: "I quit without a plan. My fear is that I'll revert to my bad habits of overworking and not having good boundaries, and burn out again."

- **Joe:** "How did you make sure you quit your job?"
- **Person:** "I don't know if I—"
- **Joe:** "You basically are telling me, I just quit a job without a plan because I wanted to take care of myself, and I'm afraid that I'm not going to take care of myself in the future. Like, you just did something to take care of yourself, and then the first reaction is, I'm scared I won't do this again in the future. What, how did that happen?"
- **Person:** "I think it's because I didn't take vacation. And I'm afraid I'll do it again."
- **Joe:** "What I'm asking is, how is it that when you take one action to take care of yourself, that doesn't show any kind of proof or give you any kind of solace that you'll do that again? It's an interesting thing, right? Like, if you were playing golf and you hit a perfect swing, and then you were like, I'm scared, I'll never do that again."
- **Person:** "Yeah, I feel that. I feel that in my body now when you just said it."

Then, after the person partially recovers, Joe extends the evidence:

- **Joe:** "Well, you're also on this call talking to me. That's another self-care."

*(Source: Fear Of Overworking)*

The two reflections together create a cumulative effect. The first reflects the recent action. The second extends into the live moment. The person cannot keep saying they won't take care of themselves while sitting in front of evidence that they are doing it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person laughs or feels embarrassed:** good. The recursion is visible. Joe can be warm and let it land. Then he often moves to [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]] to anchor the felt shift, or to a deeper question about what's underneath the fear.

- **If the person discounts the evidence ("yeah but that was one time, I had to do it for four years to get to that point"):** Joe meets the discount as data. "That's lovely. I love that." Then he extends with another piece of evidence. "Well, you're also on this call." The accumulation matters. One data point can be argued. Two cannot. The discount itself is part of the pattern of refusing to let the capacity count.

- **If the person stays stuck in the future-worry:** the move can be combined with naming the recursion directly: "this pattern of worrying about your future — how much of that pattern is the thing that made you overwork?" This connects the move to [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]].

- **If the body produces a release (tears, laughter, a deep breath):** stay. Don't move on. The integration is happening. Apply [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]].

## Tone

Warm, slightly playful, slightly amused. Not "gotcha." The energy is closer to "isn't this funny — you're literally doing it right now" than "I caught you."

The golf-swing analogy ("if you hit a perfect swing and then said I'm scared I'll never do that again") is delivered with a smile. The absurdity is the teaching. The person laughs because the absurdity is recognizable as their own thinking.

Joe is direct with the reflection but does not push. If the person discounts, he meets the discount with curiosity and then extends with more evidence. No pressure. The accumulating evidence does the work.

## Variations for Different Contexts

- **For someone afraid they can't have a difficult conversation:** "You're having one right now."
- **For someone afraid they can't trust themselves:** "You're trusting yourself right now by being here."
- **For someone afraid they can't feel their emotions:** "You're feeling something right now — what is it?"
- **For someone afraid they can't set a boundary:** "You set one to be here for this hour. How did you do that?"
- **For someone afraid they can't ask for help:** "You're asking for help right now."
- **For someone afraid they can't slow down:** "You're slowing down to talk to me. How did you do that?"
- **For someone who thinks they need a substance to connect:** "How hard is your connection with me right now?" (The connection is happening without alcohol; the person's belief is being falsified in real time. See [[heart-in-a-box-want-to-drink]].)

The precision matters. The evidence should be exactly the capacity the person is afraid they lack, in its exact form, happening right now.

## The Connection-Is-Happening-Now Variant (For Substance-Use Patterns)

A specific form: when the person believes they need a substance (alcohol, cannabis) to access connection, warmth, or the openhearted version of themselves, point at the connection that is happening in the coaching room without the substance.

The form:
- "How hard is your connection with me right now?"
- (When the person reports it as hard:) "Right now it's happening."
- (When the person says they can't connect because of the rubbish in the way:) "Yeah, so that means you're connected to the rubbish and me."

The move usually has to be repeated across several turns. The first version often does not land because the person's habitual experience of trying to connect is that it is effortful. Joe re-asks "how hard is this?" at the moments the openhearted self is most present, and the body eventually reports what is actually happening: "super easy."

The variant operates as a live A/B demonstration of the boxed-quality-is-available-without-the-substance teaching. The body verifies. Once verified, the substance loses its monopoly on the access. See [[heart-in-a-box-want-to-drink]] for the underlying mechanism and [[do-i-stop-drinking-session]] for the full sequencing across a session.

## Connected Moves

- [[not-sometimes-right-now]]: the broader pattern of collapsing the distance between description and lived experience.
- [[how-is-that-not-here-right-now]]: the question form of a related move. That question asks how a desired future state isn't already happening. This move asserts the capacity is already happening.
