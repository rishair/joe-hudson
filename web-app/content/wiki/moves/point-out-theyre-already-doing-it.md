# Point Out They're Already Doing It (When They Say They Need to Go Away to Do It)

## The Move

When a person says they need to leave the room, end the conversation, or go be alone in order to do some inner thing — and they are visibly already doing it in the room — Joe points at the live evidence: "I don't see you not doing it right now. How is it happening now?"

The move is a one-beat dissolution of the imagined need for isolation. The person has been operating from a frame in which the inner work requires solitude. The frame is being contradicted by what is actually happening. Joe names the contradiction.

"What? I don't see you not being with yourself right now. What makes you need to go away to be with yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Am I Being Authentic Or Fake?

The structure of the move is two-part. First: a flat statement that the imagined required-condition is not actually a requirement (I don't see you not doing it). Second: a question that puts the person in contact with how the thing is happening now (how is it happening now / what makes you need to go away).

## What This Is Not

This is not [[how-do-you-not-trust-it]], though it operates with similar logic (invert the apparent absence into a question about its present existence). This move is specifically about an action the person says they need to leave to do, when they are doing it in the room.

It is also not a dismissal of solitude as a legitimate practice. Solitude is real. The move applies when the going-away is operating as an avoidance of the very thing that is happening, not when solitude is being chosen for its own value.

## When Joe Uses It

The move applies specifically when:

- The person says they need to leave to do an inner thing (be with themselves, feel a feeling, process something)
- The inner thing is visibly happening in the room
- The proposed going-away has the quality of avoidance of the live experience, not of considered choice
- The person is partly using the going-away to manage the discomfort of doing the inner thing with another person present

The move does NOT apply when:

- The person actually needs to leave for legitimate reasons (the room is unsafe, the time is up, the energy is exhausted)
- The going-away is part of an explicit practice (a meditation retreat, a planned solo period)
- The person has not yet started doing the inner thing and genuinely needs space to begin

## What It Produces

The most common response is the person realizing they are already doing the thing they thought required isolation. The next question — "how is it happening now?" — puts them in contact with the mechanism. They start to be able to articulate what is making it possible to be with themselves in company.

In the source transcript, the person answered: "Because I'm not trying to do any work to manage this or like keep this happening." The answer is the work. The person has discovered, in the moment, that the inner thing happens when they stop trying to manage it. The discovery would not have been available if Joe had let them leave.

## The Mechanism Underneath

The move works because the person has often been operating with a fused belief: "this inner thing requires conditions I do not currently have." The going-away is the proposed satisfaction of the conditions. The move reveals that the conditions are not what they thought, and the inner thing is happening without them.

There is also a relational dynamic. Being with another person while doing the inner thing is often what the person is most afraid of — because it makes the inner thing visible, exposed, witnessed. The proposed solitude is partly a way to do the inner thing without being seen doing it. The move declines the proposed escape and supports the person to stay with what is happening in the witnessed condition.

This is related to [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] but operates from the other direction. There the read is that the person has left themselves in order to manage the conversation; here the move is to prevent the person from leaving the conversation in order to be with themselves. Both moves point toward the same end-state: being with the self while in contact with the other.

## What Joe Does After

After the move lands and the person sees they are already doing the thing, Joe stays with them in the doing. The next move is often to name the mechanism that is making it possible — usually the absence of management or trying. See [[resistance]]: "the resistance to it is the thing that gives it energy." When the person stops trying to manage the inner state, the state becomes available. The going-away would have been a form of management; staying is what allows the state to continue.

## Tone

Direct, slightly surprised. The "What?" at the start of the move carries some of the work. Joe is not pretending not to understand; he is registering a genuine mismatch between what the person is reporting and what he is seeing. The mismatch is the leverage point. The person has been treating their experience as different from what it actually is, and Joe is pointing at the gap.

The follow-up question is curious, not challenging. "How is it happening now?" is asked the way you would ask someone to describe a phenomenon they are inside. The person is the expert on what is happening; the question helps them notice what they are doing.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Using the move when the person actually needs solitude.** The move presumes the going-away is avoidance. If it is not, the move is intrusion.
- **Following up the move with a teaching about why solitude is unnecessary.** The move's power is in the present-moment evidence. Adding a teaching dilutes it.
- **Repeating the move when the person insists they need to leave.** If the person continues to need to leave after the move has been offered once, accept that. The move offers a noticing; it does not impose a requirement.

## The Inverse Variant: Point Out They're Doing the Pattern Right Now

The move's structure also runs in the other direction. The person reports a pattern they have ("I dissociate," "I isolate myself," "I mask my emotions") and the pattern is happening live in the room. The move is structurally identical to the original — point at the live evidence — but the content is opposite. Instead of "you're already doing the good thing you fear you can't do," it is "you're already doing the bad thing you think is in the past."

Joe's compressed form: "You're isolated right now, bro. You're isolating the sadness that's happening right now, like in this moment."

The inverse variant applies when:

- The person describes a pattern in the past tense or as a historical feature of themselves ("I'm an isolated person," "I've been disassociating my whole life")
- The pattern is visibly happening in the room as they describe it
- The descriptive frame is preventing the person from working with the pattern as a live event
- The session is in danger of becoming an analysis of the pattern rather than an engagement with it

The mechanism is the same as the original. The standing-back from the pattern is dissolved by pointing at the in-the-room evidence. The person cannot keep the pattern at a descriptive distance when it is operating in their body as they describe it.

The follow-up after the inverse variant is different than after the original. The original opens to the question "how is it happening now?" — the person discovers the mechanism by which they are already doing the capacity. The inverse variant opens to "what just happened?" — the person attends to the felt moment of the pattern firing. From there the work usually moves to [[amplify-the-dissociation-then-opposite-with-no-effort]] or to a deeper inquiry into what the pattern is protecting.

See [[the-trying-is-the-pattern-firing-now]] for the specific read that triggers the inverse variant. The read identifies that the person's "I'm trying" or descriptive pivot is itself the pattern. The move names what the read identified.
