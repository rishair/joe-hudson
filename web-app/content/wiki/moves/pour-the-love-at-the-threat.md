# Pour the Love at the Threat

## The Move

The person has just had a felt shift — the grief has started moving, the joy is online, the heart has opened. Instead of letting the session end there, have them turn the opened heart toward the very thing they have been defending against. Have them pour their love, joy, pleasure, and grief at the feared person.

The instruction Joe gives:

"I want to do one more thing. I want to amp it up just a little bit more because I see your capacity. I want you to go back to the screen. I want you to look around for like the most narcissistic person that you can find. Okay, there's no way that they're gonna get you into their web. They don't even know you're looking at them right now, right? But I want you to just give them all the love and joy and pleasure that you feel, and grief, all of it. Just let them have the whole thing."

When the person hesitates or starts to pull back:

"Keep going, just keep going. They can't even interact with you. Oh, don't get distant. Do the whole thing. Don't protect yourself. The whole thing. The joy, the love, the pleasure. Yeah, all of it."

The move is structured as a one-way broadcast. The feared person cannot see, hear, or interact. The safety of the no-interaction is what makes the experiment possible. The person is asked to do the maximally undefended thing toward the maximally defended-against target.

## When To Use It

After a felt shift has already happened in the session. The grief has begun moving. The body has softened. The heart has opened to some degree. The previous moves — [[demonstrate-the-already-trained-detection]], [[whats-the-need-for-the-defense]], [[how-much-of-this-is-fear-of-feeling-the-hurt-you-already-have]] — have done their work. The person has felt the truth that grief gives joy rather than taking it.

Use the move when:
- The opened state is real and being verified in the body ("I feel my whole body sensation pleasure").
- There is a safe target available — someone the person fears but cannot interact with the person in this moment (a stranger on Zoom, an imagined person, the original narcissist held in mind but at a safe distance).
- There is enough rapport that the person will follow the instruction past the initial discomfort.
- The closing of the session would otherwise leave the work theoretical rather than embodied.

Do NOT use the move when:
- The person is still in the contracted state. The move requires the opened state to be present first.
- The feared person is actually in the room or actively interacting. The safety of the one-way broadcast is structural to the move.
- The person is dysregulated in a way that would make the broadcast performative rather than felt.
- There is no time to support the body's response after.

The move is a closer, not an opener. It works because the previous work has already opened the heart. The move tests and demonstrates what the open heart can do.

## What It Produces

The most common immediate report: a felt sense of capability, expansion, and — paradoxically — a loss of interest in the feared person.

From the source session:

- **Joe:** "I want you to just give them all the love and joy and pleasure that you feel, and grief, all of it. Just let them have the whole thing."
- **Person:** "Why do I do that?"
- **Joe:** "Keep going, just keep going. They can't even interact with you. Oh, don't get distant. Do the whole thing. Don't protect yourself."
- **Person:** "I feel that I'm pretty quickly losing interest."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, there you go. And they — yeah, they can't stop your joy. No. And your grief. Yeah."

The "I'm pretty quickly losing interest" is the report Joe is hoping for. The narcissist's hook lands in the defended, scanning, vigilant version of the person. The opened person has nothing for the hook to catch on. The pouring is what makes this experientially true rather than conceptually claimed.

Other reports:
- A felt sense of generosity and overflowing.
- A small smile or laugh of release.
- A body softening, especially in the chest.
- Sometimes a wave of grief or joy that intensifies before settling.
- The recognition that the opened state is the safety the vigilance was trying to provide.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **When the person reports losing interest:** Joe names it as the proof. "They can't stop your joy. They can't stop your grief." The naming anchors the felt experience as the principle: the opened person is not seducible by the narcissist's bait.

- **When the person hesitates mid-pour ("why do I do that?" "I don't want to"):** Joe keeps the instruction going gently. The hesitation is the defense trying to come back online. The instruction to keep pouring is what gives the defense permission to dissolve.

- **When the person tries to add a protective layer ("but they don't deserve it"):** Joe interrupts. "Don't protect yourself. The whole thing." The deserving frame is the defense in moral costume. The instruction is to pour regardless. The point is not whether the narcissist deserves the love — the point is what happens in the person's body when the heart is fully opened toward the feared target.

- **When the body produces a release (tears, laughter, deep breath):** stay. Apply [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]]. The release is the work landing.

- **When the person reports something different than expected (anger arises, grief deepens, joy turns to sadness):** trust the body. The pouring surfaces whatever is alive. The instruction was not "feel a specific thing." The instruction was "let the open heart move toward the feared target." Whatever moves is the right thing.

## Why It Works

The vigilance posture splits the person's heart from the feared target. The split is what the narcissist exploits — the split-off heart is the place where the narcissist's bait lands, because the heart, separated from awareness, becomes naïvely available to whoever appears to fill it.

The pouring move dissolves the split. The heart is no longer hidden from the feared target. The heart is being deliberately directed at the feared target. There is no longer a back-door where the narcissist's seduction can enter, because the front door is already open and pouring.

This is a structural inversion of the protective frame. The protective frame says "close the heart to stay safe." The pouring frame says "open the heart fully, and the openness is the safety, because the openness leaves no hidden corner for the bait to land in."

The move also works because the experience is short and reportable. The person can feel the loss of interest within seconds. The body verifies the principle in real time. After this verification, the conceptual framing ("the opened heart is the safety") is no longer theory. It is a felt fact.

The move is conceptually adjacent to [[take-contrary-action]] (do the opposite of what the imprint says) and to [[speak-the-welcoming-of-the-feared-state-aloud]] (welcome the feared state out loud). Both work by reversing the default protective behavior. This move is the relational version: reverse the default of closing toward the threat by opening toward the threat instead.

## The Importance of the Safety Frame

The instruction is repeated: "they don't even know you're looking at them right now," "they can't even interact with you." The safety frame is not a throwaway. It is what makes the experiment possible.

Without the safety frame, the move would activate the defense it is trying to dissolve. The body would tighten, the heart would close, and the experiment would produce defended performance rather than felt opening. The safety frame creates the conditions where the body can do the experiment.

This is one specific application of a broader principle in coaching practice: the experiment is only as useful as the safety of the container. A move that asks for vulnerability requires the vulnerability to be safe to perform. The pouring move asks for maximal vulnerability toward the feared target. The safety frame ("they cannot interact, they cannot see, they cannot get to you") is what permits the vulnerability.

## The Difference From Forgiveness Practice

The move is not a forgiveness practice. Forgiveness practices often ask the person to feel compassion for the harmful person, sometimes prematurely, sometimes as a moral instruction. This move is not about the feared person at all. It is about what the person's own body does when the heart is opened toward the feared person.

The narcissist is the target only because they are the body's most defended-against direction. The pouring is for the person, not for the narcissist. The reorganization happens in the person's nervous system, not in the relationship.

The instruction is not "forgive them." The instruction is "give them the whole thing — joy, love, pleasure, grief." The whole thing includes the grief. The grief is allowed. The move does not ask the person to feel only positive things toward the narcissist. It asks for the full opened-heart material to move in that direction.

This is why the move can be done with a narcissist the person rightly wants nothing to do with. The pouring does not require reconciliation, contact, or forgiveness. It requires only the redirection of the opened heart, for a few seconds, toward the feared target — and the witnessing of what the body does.

## Tone

Warm, slightly insistent, encouraging. Joe keeps the instruction going past the first hesitation. The energy is "I see your capacity, let's see what's here" rather than "you should be able to do this."

When the person hesitates or starts to pull back, the tone stays warm but the instruction stays steady. "Keep going. Just keep going. Don't protect yourself. The whole thing." The steadiness is the holding of the container while the person does the work.

The closing tone, when the person reports the loss of interest, is celebratory and confirming. "Yeah, yeah, there you go. They can't stop your joy. And your grief." The naming of what just happened locks it in as the felt experience the person can return to.

## Variations for Different Threats

- **For a feared narcissist:** as in the source session — pour the opened heart at the most narcissistic person available.
- **For a feared parent:** hold the parent in mind, pour everything (joy, love, pleasure, grief, anger if it comes) at them, with the safety frame of them not knowing.
- **For a feared boss or colleague:** the same structure. The safety frame: they cannot interact with this. You are doing this for yourself.
- **For an imagined feared future partner:** the abstract version. Pour the opened heart at the imagined feared future, watch the body's response.

The structure is the same: opened heart, safety frame, full broadcast at the feared target, named recognition of what the body reports.
