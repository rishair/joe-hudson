# Model the Emotional Movement (Let Them Witness You Doing It)

## The Move

When you want your kid to learn how to move an emotion, don't teach them. Model it. Move the emotion yourself, where they can see it, and let the witnessing be the teaching.

"There's two things you can do. The first one is show them what it's like, like just ask them to witness you as you're moving your anger. That one's a really very useful thing." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

The move is structurally different from teaching. The teaching approach is "let me explain to you what to do." The modeling approach is "let me do this, you watch." Kids learn the second way much faster than the first because their nervous systems are wired to read what the adult is actually doing far more reliably than they parse what the adult is saying. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]].

## What This Move Looks Like

Concrete forms:

- The parent who is angry about something does the anger work where the kid can witness — punching pillows, yelling into a designated container, moving the body — and then comes back to connection with the kid.
- The parent who is sad cries in front of the kid (without making the kid responsible for fixing it).
- The parent who is scared names the fear, lets the body do what it needs to do, and demonstrates that fear can be present without the system collapsing.
- After a dynamic where the parent has been angry at the kid (which happens), the parent goes and does the anger work in private, returns, and is available for repair.

The kid does not need a lecture afterward. The witnessing already did the work. Adding a lecture often undoes the modeling by converting it back into a teaching project. See [[dont-manage-your-kids-into-moving-emotion]].

## When This Move Applies

Specifically: when the parent wants the kid to develop emotional fluidity. The move is the alternative to the management approach.

The move also applies in moments when the kid is freshly resistant to their own emotion. Instead of trying to coax them into moving it, the parent moves their own (or a similar) emotion in the kid's presence. The kid sees that the practice is real, sees that it does not destroy the adult, and sees that connection survives the expression.

## The Witnessing Is Not a Performance

The move only works if the emotional movement is real. A performed version of the move — the parent pretending to move anger so the kid will see — produces the opposite of the intended effect. Kids read the difference between an actual emotion being moved and a demonstration being staged. The demonstration teaches them that emotion-work is something parents perform for kids' education, which is a kind of inauthenticity they will eventually reject.

The move works when the parent is actually doing the work for their own reasons and the kid happens to witness.

## What This Move Produces

- The kid develops an intuitive sense of what moving an emotion looks like.
- The kid develops the felt knowledge that emotions do not destroy the adult.
- The kid develops the felt knowledge that connection survives expression.
- The kid is less likely to need explicit coaching later because the template is already installed somatically.
- The kid is less likely to absorb the silent prohibition that "we don't do that in this family."

The downstream effect is that when the kid eventually has access to emotional inquiry as a teenager or adult, the practice does not feel foreign. It feels like something they already know how to do. They have been watching it for years.

## The Connection to Boundaries

This move pairs with [[draw-boundaries-with-kids-to-let-anger-arise]]. The boundaries produce the kid's anger naturally. The modeling shows the kid what to do with it. Together they form the structural alternative to the management approach:

1. Draw boundaries.
2. Welcome the anger that arises in response.
3. Model the movement of your own anger in parallel.

The kid learns from the field, not from being managed.

## Tone

Light, unforced. The move is not a parenting technique to be deployed. It is what naturally happens when a parent is doing their own emotional work and the kid happens to be in the house. The "asking the kid to witness" piece is optional — sometimes useful when the kid is curious, sometimes overkill. The default is just doing the work where it can be seen.
