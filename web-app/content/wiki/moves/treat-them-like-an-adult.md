# Treat Them Like an Adult

## The Move

The take-home instruction for a partner who has been caretaking the other: treat the partner like an adult. Stop managing their emotional state. Stop taking responsibility for their well-being. Stop being responsible for their happiness. Let them have their state without trying to fix it. Be present with them as one adult with another.

The move is often delivered as the second half of a paired instruction: "Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall."

"Yeah. So that's the job. Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall." -- Joe Hudson, Should I Stay With My Ex?

The two halves of the instruction are doing related but distinct work:

- **Unconditionally love yourself** is the internal work. See [[self-love-makes-the-decision]] and [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]].
- **Treat them like an adult** is the relational instruction. It is what exits the parent-child configuration that the caretaking has established. See [[nobody-wants-to-have-sex-with-a-kid]] for the configuration's structural consequence.

The "chips fall where the chips fall" is the orientation toward the outcome. The work is the configuration change; the result is not in the speaker's control.

## When Joe Uses It

The move is used:

- As the closing instruction in a session where the caretaking pattern has been surfaced
- When the person has just had the body-shift recognition (heart opens, joy returns) and needs an external instruction to anchor the work in their daily life
- When the stay-or-leave question has been redirected to self-love and the next step is a relational instruction that doesn't re-engage the deliberation
- When the person needs an answer to "okay so what do I actually do?" that doesn't put them back into management
- When the person is about to return to physical proximity with the partner and needs an orientation that doesn't recreate the parent-child configuration

The move is also used in non-romantic configurations: with adult children, with employees, with siblings whose emotional state has been the speaker's project. The instruction is the same: treat them as an adult who can handle their own state.

## What It Produces

The most common response: a felt sense of relief. The person has been carrying the weight of managing the other person for so long that the explicit permission to stop is a body-level relief. The chest opens. The shoulders drop.

A second response: anxiety. Some people, on hearing the instruction, immediately worry about what will happen if they stop managing. The depression will deepen, the anger will explode, the addiction will return. This is the caretaking voice trying to reassert. Joe usually does not engage with the catastrophic predictions. He returns to the instruction and the principle that the partner is an adult.

A third response: clarity about the relationship. Once the person imagines treating the partner as an adult, they sometimes realize the relationship would not survive that change — because the relationship has been organized around the parent-child configuration and would not function without it. This is data about the relationship, not a problem with the instruction. The relationship was already not viable; the configuration was the life support.

A fourth response: the person reports the move feels impossible. The years of caretaking have made the management feel involuntary. Joe will sometimes give a smaller-scale starting instruction: "Just for the next week, notice every time you take responsibility for his state. You don't have to stop. Just notice."

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person reports relief, Joe lets the relief stand. The session can end.

If the person reports anxiety about consequences, Joe returns to the principle that the partner is an adult. He may add: "He's his own man. Clearly." The reminder is doing structural work — it preserves the partner's agency in the speaker's mind, which is what makes the configuration change possible.

If the person reports clarity that the relationship won't survive the change, Joe stays with the clarity. He does not push toward staying or leaving. The recognition is enough.

If the person reports the move feels impossible, Joe scales it down. Notice, then choose. The first step is not stopping the caretaking. It is becoming aware of it in real time.

## Why "Chips Fall Where the Chips Fall"

The phrase is doing important work. It releases the speaker from being responsible for the outcome of the configuration change. The outcome — whether the marriage survives, whether the partner adapts, whether the relationship deepens — is not the work. The work is the configuration change itself.

This release is important because the speaker, accustomed to being responsible for outcomes (that is the caretaking pattern), will naturally try to take responsibility for the outcome of the configuration change. "Will he be okay if I stop managing him?" "What if he gets worse?" "What if we don't make it?" These questions are the caretaking trying to come back in under a new name.

"Chips fall where the chips fall" is the explicit refusal to engage with those questions. The chips are not the speaker's chips. The chips are what they are. The speaker's work is internal and relational; the outcome is the world's.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not "be cold to your partner." Treating someone as an adult is warm, not cold. The warmth is real because it is based on respect for the partner as a person rather than on management of the partner as a project.

It is not "stop caring." Care continues. What stops is the management. Care without management is the adult-adult configuration. Care with management is the parent-child configuration. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

It is not "leave the relationship." The instruction is internal to the speaker. It does not specify whether to stay or leave. The relationship's outcome is downstream of the configuration change.

It is not "tell your partner to grow up." The instruction is to the speaker, not about the partner. The partner does not need to do anything different. The configuration change happens in the speaker's stance, and the partner's adulthood is something the speaker grants by relating differently — not something the partner has to earn.

It is not "weaponize the instruction in an argument." The instruction is not "you're being a child and I'm done parenting you." That weaponization is itself the parent-child configuration reasserting in a different costume. The instruction works only as an internal stance.

## Tone

Direct, warm, casual. Joe delivers the instruction without solemnity. The "chips fall where the chips fall" framing keeps the instruction light. The lightness is purposeful — the move is producing relief, and the tone matches the relief.

The brevity matters. A longer take-home would re-engage the deliberation. "Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall." is short enough to be carried home and recalled in the moments when the caretaking impulse arises.

## Dialogue Example

From a trial-separation Q&A:

- **Joe:** "Yeah. So that's the job. Unconditionally love yourself and treat him like an adult. Chips fall where the chips fall."
- **Person:** "Thank you for, um, helping me feel that in my body."
- **Joe:** "You're welcome. What a pleasure."

*(Source: Should I Stay With My Ex?)*

The dialogue shows the move closing the session. The person's response — "thank you for helping me feel that in my body" — confirms the work landed somatically, not just intellectually. The instruction is now carryable.
