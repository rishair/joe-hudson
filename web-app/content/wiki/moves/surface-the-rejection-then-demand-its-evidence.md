# Surface the Rejection Then Demand Its Evidence

## The Move

When a person asks for something they have a hidden rejection of, surface the rejection by inviting it directly, then demand the evidence for it using what is present in the room. The move has two halves that work together.

**Half one: invite the full rejection.** Ask the person to list everything they hold against the thing. "Tell me all the bad things about money." "What are all the bad things about asking for help?" "Tell me everything wrong with being seen." The fluency of the list is the diagnostic. If the answer arrives fast and in detail, the rejection is operating in parallel with the asking, even though the person came in asking for the thing.

**Half two: demand evidence for each claim.** "Go ahead and prove to me that money is dirty." Use what is actually present in the room. People on the call have money. Their hands are not dirty. The person is forced to test the inherited claim against the actual world. The claim almost always fails the test, and the failure is felt rather than argued.

The point is not to win the debate. The point is to make the person notice that they have been operating from a claim that they themselves cannot defend with evidence.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the presenting concern is a how-to question ("how do I create wealth," "how do I find a partner," "how do I be more visible") and Joe suspects the asking contains a rejection. See [[how-do-i-create-wealth-and-financial-stability]].
- When a person describes wanting something while their body or behavior shows resistance. The verbal want is real; the verbal want is also being undermined by an unspoken rejection. The move surfaces what is being suppressed.
- When the projection has become invisible to the person who carries it. They have lived inside the claim long enough that they no longer notice it as a claim. They experience it as a fact.
- When the rejection is moral or contamination-flavored ("dirty," "evil," "selfish," "shallow," "pathetic"). Moral claims are the most defendable in the abstract and the most indefensible against present evidence. The move exposes this asymmetry.
- After [[say-the-forbidden-want-out-loud]] when the body has reported restriction and the source of the restriction needs to be made cognitively visible.

The move is most useful early in a session, before the surrogate work has begun. It is the surfacing step that makes the surrogate work possible.

## What It Produces

**A fluent list of rejections, surprisingly fast.** The person who asks "how do I create wealth?" answers "tell me all the bad things about money" with: "it's dirty, it's guilty, I don't deserve it." The speed of the answer demonstrates that the rejection was not absent; it was hiding.

**A visible contradiction.** Once the rejection is spoken, the structural absurdity of asking-for-what-you-have-just-labeled-as-bad becomes audible. The person can hear themselves. They do not need to be told there is a contradiction; they can see it directly.

**The collapse of the moral certainty under evidence.** "Prove to me that money is dirty" produces a claim ("it passes through people's hands and they get dirty"). Joe tests the claim ("I have money, let me see your hands. You have money, are your hands dirty?"). The claim fails. The certainty that operated for years collapses in a single exchange.

**A move toward the deeper origin.** Once the surface claim has been tested and found wanting, the person is often ready to ask why they have been operating from a claim that does not survive scrutiny. The next move ([[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]]) becomes available because the surface defense has been removed.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Invite the full list.** "Tell me all the bad things about [the thing]." Let the person speak the rejection in their own words. Do not interpret or argue.

2. **Name the contradiction in plain language.** "So what makes you want something that's dirty that you don't deserve, that is bad?" Make the structural absurdity audible. The point is not to embarrass; it is to make the structure visible.

3. **Demand the evidence.** "Prove to me that [the claim]." Push the person to defend the claim with specifics, not generalities. "Dirty how? Who is dirty? What does dirty mean here?"

4. **Use the room as the counter-evidence.** "I have money. Let me see your hands. You have money. Are your hands dirty? How much do you need to have for the hands to be dirty?" The room contains the evidence the claim cannot survive.

5. **Let the recognition land.** Do not over-explain. Once the person sees that the claim does not survive the test, allow them to notice. The recognition is its own event.

6. **Move to the origin.** Now that the surface claim has been weakened, ask where the relationship-with-the-thing originally came from. The trace usually goes to a primary relationship. See [[trace-emotion-to-its-first-felt]] (relational-structure variant).

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** "How can I create wealth and financial stability for my life?"
- **Joe:** "Okay, my question -- tell me all the bad things about money?"
- **Guest:** "Uh, it's dirty. It's guilty to have a lot of money. It's, um, it's not worthy of me to having money."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so what makes you want something that's dirty that you don't deserve, that is bad?"
- **Guest:** "I know it will give some stability to my life."
- **Joe:** "Stability of bad, dirty -- like, so you're basically, hey, I'm gonna throw all this shitty mud on you, but don't worry, it's going to make it harder for you to move, so you'll be more stable. You're asking me why it's not happening, and do you see my answer to the question?"
- **Guest:** "Yes."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so go ahead and prove to me that money is dirty."
- **Guest:** "It passes through all the people's hands, and people's hands are usually dirty and need to wash it."
- **Joe:** "I have money. Let me see your hands. You have money? They're not dirty. How much money do you have to have to be dirty?"
- **Guest:** "Million."
- **Joe:** "So there's dirty people on this call, plenty of them, I'm sure. Can you look and see any of them that are dirty?"
- **Guest:** "No."

The sequence took under a minute. The list arrived fast. The contradiction was named. The evidence test was demanded. The room produced the counter-evidence. The certainty collapsed. The session was then ready to move toward the origin and the embodied reframe.

## Why It Works

Three mechanisms run in parallel.

1. **Speaking the rejection breaks its invisibility.** As long as the rejection is unspoken, it operates as a fact the person lives inside. Spoken, it becomes a claim that can be examined. The act of speaking is itself the first crack in the projection's monopoly.

2. **The room is the harder test than the head.** The mind can defend a claim against the head indefinitely. The mind cannot defend a claim that contradicts what is sitting in front of the person right now. "Your hands are not dirty" is undeniable in a way that "rich people are not bad" is not.

3. **The person becomes their own witness.** Joe is not telling the person they are wrong. The person is testing their own claim against their own observation. The recognition lands as the person's own discovery, not as the coach's argument. This is more durable than persuasion.

The move is a specific form of [[falling-in-love-with-projections]] applied at the diagnostic stage. Before the projection can be loved, it has to be made visible as projection rather than fact. This move performs that surfacing.

## Tone

Slightly amused, direct, not contemptuous. Joe asks the question with the energy of someone curious about the structure rather than someone trying to win. The "go ahead and prove to me" carries no hostility; it is an invitation to see if the claim survives. The person feels invited into the test, not attacked for holding the claim.

The lightness matters. If the move is done with heaviness, the person experiences it as confrontation and defends. With lightness, the person experiences it as inquiry and looks. The looking is what produces the collapse.

## Variations

The move is portable to any projection that operates as if it were fact:

- **For "I'm bad":** "Tell me all the bad things about you. Now prove to me that you're bad. Show me the evidence in this room."
- **For "love is dangerous":** "Tell me all the bad things about love. Prove to me that love is dangerous. Show me the people in this room whose love has destroyed them."
- **For "being seen is unsafe":** "Tell me all the bad things about being seen. Prove to me that being seen is unsafe. Look around. Who here is being seen and is unsafe?"
- **For "asking is needy":** "Tell me all the bad things about asking for what you want. Prove to me that asking is needy. Show me someone in this room who asks and is therefore needy."

The structure is the same: surface the full rejection, name the contradiction, demand the evidence, use the room.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not skip the listing step.** Going straight to "prove it" without first having the person voice the full list of rejections leaves the rejection partially hidden. The full list is what makes the contradiction audible.
- **Do not argue back during the listing.** Joe does not contest individual items as they are spoken. He lets the list build. Contesting in the middle short-circuits the surfacing and the person clamps down.
- **Do not use the move with someone who came in openly rejecting.** The person who comes in saying "money is bad" already has the rejection visible. They do not need it surfaced; they need the underlying surrogate worked. See [[money-is-bad]] for the coaching path when the rejection is already conscious.
- **Do not use the move when there is no contradiction in play.** If the person is openly wanting and the body matches the wanting, the move has nothing to surface and becomes an empty drill.
- **Do not use the move to humiliate.** The point is not "see how stupid your belief is." The point is "look at the shape of what you have been holding." Lightness is required.
