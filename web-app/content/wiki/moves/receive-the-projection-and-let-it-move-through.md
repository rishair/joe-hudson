# Receive the Projection and Let It Move Through

## The Move

When you are facilitating, coaching, or teaching, the people in front of you will project onto you. They will project the asshole dad. The unloving mother. The all-knowing authority. The perfect parent. The savior. The fraud. The threat. The job is not to deflect the projections. The job is to receive them, let them move all the way through, and not take on either side -- neither agreeing with the projection nor defending against it.

The structural requirement is invisibility. Not invisibility in the sense of withdrawal or non-presence -- invisibility in the sense of not having a self there for the projection to attach to. The clearer the channel, the more freely the projection can pass through. The projection that passes through completely is the projection that gets seen by the person doing the projecting.

"When we teach, in MasterClass, the people who are in all the groups -- one of the most important things of training and facilitation and coaching is that you have to be invisible. People get to project whatever they want on you. They get to project that you're an asshole, that you're the fucked-up dad, that you're the mom who doesn't love them, or that you're the greatest person in the whole world, you're perfection. You have to allow all those projections for their healing, and not get caught in it. So it's allowing it, but allowing it to move right through you and not take on the projection. Like, 'yeah, I do have all the answers' -- because none of us do. Or 'yeah, I am that asshole dad, I am that authority figure.' You can't join them in the routine or the pattern that they're playing out. But your job is to receive that all the way, let that move all the way through you." -- Joe Hudson, Humility Q&A

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A student in a session treats you as the perfect authority. The impulse is to lean into the role -- "yes, I have what you need." Do not.

A student in a session treats you as the dismissive parent who never loved them. The impulse is to defend -- "I am not your father, I see you." Do not.

A student tells you, with energy, that you are full of shit. The impulse is to defend the teaching or to collapse into agreement. Do neither. Receive the message. Let it land. Do not block it. Do not agree with the structure underneath it. Let what is being projected move through. See [[agree-when-challenged-on-the-teaching]] for the related move.

The interior move is the same as the move with compliments (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]) but the substance being received is different. With compliments, the substance is recognition. With projections, the substance is the person's unresolved relational template being placed on you. Receiving without taking it on creates the conditions for the person to see the template as theirs.

## Why Not Take It On

If the coach takes on the projection, the projection becomes real in the relationship. The coach who agrees "yes, I am the authority who knows" actualizes the projection -- the student now has a real authority to either defer to or rebel against, and the projection has been confirmed rather than seen. The coach who defends "no, I am not your father" makes the projection a thing to argue about rather than a thing to notice. Either move loses the healing opportunity.

The healing opportunity is this: when the projection lands on a coach who neither takes it on nor defends against it, the projection has nowhere to anchor. It moves through. The person doing the projecting has the chance to feel the projection as a projection -- as something they are doing, not something the coach is.

This is the structural reason [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] happens. The teacher who takes on the projection of the halo becomes the halo (and then becomes either a fraud or someone who believes their own halo). The teacher who defends against attack-projections hardens into armor. Both are the same failure: the projection got taken on instead of allowed to move through.

## What Allows This

The move requires the structural condition of humility. There has to be no identity in the coach that needs to be either the authority or not-the-authority, the asshole-dad or not-the-asshole-dad. If those identities are present and being defended, the projections will hit them and the defenses will fire. The defenses are the failure of the move.

The practice that builds this capacity is the general work on identity. Each identity that relaxes is one fewer surface for projections to stick to. The coach who has done their own work has fewer hooks. See [[humility]] and [[every-identity-has-limits]].

A second condition: the coach has to be able to be uncomfortable. Receiving the asshole-dad projection without defending against it is genuinely unpleasant. The person is, in their experience, relating to you as the asshole-dad. The receiving costs something. The discomfort is part of the work, not a sign that something is going wrong.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not take on the projection.** "Yeah, I do have all the answers." "Yeah, I am that authority figure." These confirm the template.
- **Do not defend against the projection.** "I am not your father. I am not that authority. You are projecting on me." This makes the projection a thing to argue about and signals that you have an identity being threatened.
- **Do not join the pattern they are playing out.** If the student is enacting the rebellion-against-authority routine, do not become the authority who needs to be rebelled against. Do not give the routine a partner.
- **Do not perform receiving.** "I see you" or "I hold space for that" delivered as technique is itself a defense. The receiving has to be actual, in the body, not a phrase.
- **Do not interpret the projection back at them prematurely.** "You are projecting your father on me" is sometimes the right move (see [[mirror-the-judgment]]) and sometimes a way to deflect the substance. Receive first. Interpretation is downstream of receiving.

## In Practice (Coaching Sessions)

The classic transference moves: the student who keeps trying to get the teacher to approve of them. The student who keeps finding reasons the teacher is failing them. The student who idealizes the teacher and then crashes into disappointment. The student who needs the teacher to be wrong about everything in order to maintain their own position.

In each case the move is the same. Receive what is happening. Notice the felt sense of being made into the projected figure. Do not anchor either into being or not-being that figure. Let the projection move through. The student's actual relationship with the projection (not with you) starts to become visible to them.

The naming sometimes follows the receiving. "I notice that we keep coming back to whether I approve of you. What is happening in you when you are looking for that?" The naming works because the prior receiving has cleared the projection enough that it can be observed rather than enacted.

## The Cost (and Why It's Necessary)

Receiving projections continuously costs something. Joe describes his nervous system being shaken after sessions early in his work because he was saying things that were scary to say -- and the saying happened against the background of receiving whatever projections came back. The cost is real. The capacity to sustain the cost is what the practice of humility builds.

If the practice of humility is not present, the cost will be paid as armor. The coach will start to harden. The hardening will register as authority or as defense. Either way, the relational field will shift and the projections will land on the armor rather than passing through. This is the structural beginning of the downward spiral. See [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]].

The coach who can sustain receiving without armoring is the coach in whom the work can happen.

## When to Use This Move

- Whenever you are in a facilitation, coaching, or teaching role
- Whenever a student or client is treating you as a parental figure, authority figure, savior, or fraud
- Whenever you feel the pull to take on or to defend against the projected role
- Whenever a community is starting to elevate you in a way that wants you to be more than human
- Whenever a community is starting to take you down in a way that wants you to be less than human
- Whenever you notice yourself wanting the projection to be true (the seductive version) or wanting to argue it away (the defensive version)

## What This Produces

- The person doing the projecting eventually sees the projection as theirs
- The relationship between coach and person becomes less encumbered over time
- The coach's own identity gets sanded away as the receiving continues (see [[humility]])
- The teaching can land without being absorbed into the projected role
- The coach can stay in the role long-term without going berserk (see [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]])

## Tone

Joe describes this with the calm of someone who has practiced it for decades and still finds it costly. The tone is not heroic. It is matter-of-fact: this is what the job requires. The receiving is not a posture. It is the structural condition without which the work cannot happen.

When he describes the projections that have to be received, his tone is unsentimental about how unpleasant some of them are. He is not pretending it is easy. He is describing what has to be done.
