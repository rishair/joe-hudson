# Ask From Each of the Three Centers

## The Move

When someone is in a charged, overwhelming, or ambiguous situation — especially one where multiple feelings or positions are present at the same time — ask each of the three centers separately what it is holding. The mind. The heart. The gut. Not as one question delivered three times, but as three distinct questions that surface three distinct kinds of information.

The move is structurally simple. The complexity is in the willingness to follow each center's answer on its own terms before moving to the next.

Tara Howley's version, used live with Michael Nagel during a conversation about his cancer treatment:

- "It's a mindfuck. The mind is like, 'okay, this is a mindfuck.' And the heart -- how does the heart hold those two, if at all?"
- "Mind denial, heart grief and gratitude. And because you're dealing with colon cancer, how's your gut feel about holding this dichotomy?"

The structure: name what the mind is doing. Ask what the heart is doing. Ask what the gut is doing. Each becomes its own conversational turn, with its own pace, its own follow-up.

## When to Use It

- When the person is in a charged situation and reporting from only one center.
- When the person says "I don't know what to do" and the not-knowing is the collapsed surface of multiple centers running different programs.
- When a contradiction or dichotomy is in the room (grief and gratitude, denial and acceptance, fear and curiosity) and the person is treating it as a single overwhelming feeling.
- When the question is too big for any one answer ("how do I live with this diagnosis," "should I stay in this marriage," "what do I do with this opportunity").
- When the person has been speaking from the head for a while and the deeper centers have not been consulted.
- When the person has been speaking from the heart for a while and the gut's quieter signal is being missed.

The move does not require the person to know the head-heart-gut framework in advance. The asking installs the framework experientially. Most people can locate "what does my heart hold" or "what does my gut say" even if they have never thought in those terms.

## How the Move Works in Practice

The three centers carry different kinds of information about the same situation:

- **The head/mind** carries narrative, analysis, denial, framing, identity-protection, and rational evaluation. The head often runs first because it is the loudest and the most habitual location for speech.

- **The heart/chest** carries felt-emotion: grief, gratitude, love, fear, longing, tenderness. The heart often carries multiple emotions at once and can hold contradiction without resolution.

- **The gut** carries deeper knowing, raw curiosity, the felt-sense of what is true, and what Joe sometimes calls intuition. The gut is slower and quieter than the other two. It often gives the most direct answer when asked, and the most reliable, but it has to be asked.

The move surfaces what each center is holding so the person can see the full picture rather than collapsing it into a single state. Often the answer the person needs is in the center that has not been speaking.

Michael's response to Tara's full sequence demonstrates this: the mind was in denial; the heart was in grief and gratitude; the gut, asked specifically, was where the curiosity was living. The gut's answer ("under all of the turmoil, there is a 'so this is what's happening, okay'") was not available until the inquiry reached it. The deliberate sequencing made the gut's contribution visible.

## What the Move Produces

- **Differentiation.** The single overwhelming feeling becomes three distinct positions that can each be related to separately.
- **Permission for contradiction.** The heart can hold grief and gratitude. The mind can be in denial while the gut is in curiosity. The dichotomy stops being a problem to solve and becomes a configuration to live inside of.
- **Access to the deeper centers.** The person who would not have thought to consult their gut now has a direct experience of the gut having something to say.
- **A felt map.** The person leaves the inquiry with a more accurate sense of what is actually happening in them, which makes the next move (whatever it is) more grounded.

## How Tara Used It Live

The full sequence with Michael, compressed:

- **Tara:** "It's really an amazing dichotomy to be on an edge... to feel protected and perpetually held in that trust fall."
- **Michael:** "It's a mindfuck."
- **Tara:** "The mind is like, 'okay, this is a mindfuck.' And the heart -- how does the heart hold those two, if at all?"
- **Michael:** "Grief and gratitude. Those are the two big ones."
- **Tara:** "Mind denial, heart grief and gratitude. And because you're dealing with colon cancer, how's your gut feel about holding this dichotomy?"
- **Michael:** "I don't know if it's heart or gut, but let's go with gut. It's like under the denial and under the slamming back and forth from grief, gratitude... the gut is probably where the curiosity about this is living. The like, this is what's happening to me."

Three turns. Three centers. The naming of what each center is holding before moving to the next. The mind's contribution gets a name ("mindfuck"). The heart's contribution gets named ("grief and gratitude"). The gut's contribution surfaces when asked ("curiosity, this is what's happening").

The move took less than a minute of conversational time. The result was Michael moving from a single overwhelming experience to a three-part felt-map he could navigate.

## Tone

Curious, slow, even-paced. The move does not work delivered as a checklist. Each center gets its own attention. The coach is genuinely interested in what each center has to say, not running through the framework to get to a destination.

The phrasing matters. "What does the heart hold?" is different from "what does your heart feel?" "Hold" implies that the heart can be carrying more than one thing. "Feel" pushes toward a single answer. The looser, more spacious phrasing produces fuller responses.

A specific feature: when one center's answer is unexpected or complex, slow down. Let it land. Let the person hear themselves say it. The naming is part of what makes the inquiry useful, not just the answering.

## What Joe Does With the Response (or What Tara Did With Michael's)

After all three centers have spoken, the move is to let the configuration that emerged be the new ground. Not to fix it. Not to push the person toward any one center as the right answer. The full map is the medicine.

In Michael's case, Tara then continued the inquiry by following the gut's curiosity ("what does it mean, from the gut? Asking the gut, like, what does it mean?"). The gut's curiosity opened the spiritual question ("what if this isn't just a mistake?") that the mind's denial and the heart's grief had been blocking access to. The sequence honored each center first, then followed where the deepest center wanted to go.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't deliver this as a technique to be impressed by.** The framework is not the work. The work is the actual consulting of each center. A mechanical run-through misses the point.
- **Don't accept the first answer from each center without checking.** If the head answer comes too fast, it may just be the head's narrative running. Sometimes the deeper center underneath each answer is reachable with a follow-up.
- **Don't insist on three centers if two are enough.** If the heart and gut are both speaking clearly and the head is quiet, the head does not need to be forced to speak.
- **Don't use this to bypass a single center that is too painful to be in.** "Let's check the head, the heart is too much right now" is a different move (and sometimes a useful one). But naming it as a bypass when it is one is more honest than using the framework as cover.
- **Don't conclude with the head's answer.** The head is good at having an answer. The answer is more accurate when the other two centers have been consulted, even briefly.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person in a charged situation with a single overwhelming state
- A person reporting from one center (usually the head) without the others having spoken
- A person trying to resolve a contradiction by collapsing it
- A person in [[i-have-a-terminal-diagnosis]] or [[someone-i-love-is-dying]] where the situation is too big for one center
- A person in a decision deliberation where the head's analysis has reached its limit

## Downstream (Where the Move Leads)

- Toward a fuller felt-map of the situation
- Toward whichever center's answer most needs to be followed
- Toward [[ask-from-the-gut]] or similar follow-up if the gut's answer opens new territory (the gut often does)
- Toward the spiritual or meaning-question if the gut's curiosity opens it
- Toward staying with whatever the heart was holding if the heart's answer needs more time
