# Turn the Blame Into Wants

## The Move

When the impulse to blame arises -- about the partner, about anyone, about a situation -- catch it before it lands and convert it into an owned want. The blame and the want are the same energy in two different costumes. The blame externalizes the missing thing. The want owns the missing thing.

"Instead of blaming somebody, every time you feel that urge to blame, just own what you want. Say, 'Oh, I would want this. I want that.'" -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

The move is a real-time translation. The blame impulse is the signal. The translation is the practice.

## When To Use This Move

- When the partner has done something that triggers the blame impulse
- When the inner monologue is running a list of what the partner is doing wrong
- When the person is presenting a relationship problem framed entirely as the partner's failures
- When the urge to blame has become a habit and is producing the [[shame-hot-potato]] dynamic
- When the relationship has reached the [[walking-on-eggshells]] pattern and the suppressed material is leaking out as blame
- When the person is in [[i-feel-trapped]] and locating the trap in the partner

The move is especially useful early in the work because it is concrete and immediately actionable. The person does not have to do deep emotional excavation to begin. They just have to catch one blame impulse a day and translate it.

## What It Produces

The first effect is empowerment. Blame is the position of being acted upon; wanting is the position of having agency.

"Often when you're blaming, you feel disempowered. You feel like I can't have the life that I want. Which means asking for what you want is a way to feel empowered again." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

The second effect is clarity in the relationship. The partner now knows where the speaker stands rather than absorbing low-grade resentment.

"Sometimes people don't do it because it's a little scary to ask for what they want, but at least your partner knows where you are, knows where you stand, instead of you just being resentful in the background and going, 'Nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang, nyang.' Like, who the hell wants to be with that? Who wants to be married to that?" -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

The third effect is structural: blame produces shame, and shame is the lock that holds bad habits in place. The move breaks the lock by removing the blame that would have produced the shame.

"Placing blame onto another person or onto yourself, it doesn't actually help you get better. It just creates shame and shame stagnates the emotions that we're having and stagnates the reality we are having. There's this great saying that I love and it says, Shame is the locks that hold the chains of bad habits in place. And when you're blaming, you're putting shame on each other. And when you do that, it's just holding those bad habits in place." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Keys To Save Your Relationship

See [[shame-drives-behavior]] for the mechanism.
