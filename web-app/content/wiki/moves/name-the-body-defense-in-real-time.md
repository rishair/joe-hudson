# Name the Body Defense in Real Time

## The Move

When the client's body produces a defensive gesture mid-session — crossed arms, a stiffening, a turn away, a hardened jaw — name it out loud as it happens, get curious about what it is doing, identify what it is protecting against, then invite the client to stay in the unprotected state.

The sequence from the source session:

1. **Notice:** Joe sees the client cross his arms.
2. **Name:** "I notice all the defense in that."
3. **Inquire:** "What made you just cross your arms?"
4. **Get the body's report:** The client describes what the gesture does. ("It creates tension in my body... when I'm feeling awkward, that is something I do.")
5. **Name the protection:** "Ah, great. So it's a form of kind of protection of feeling awkward."
6. **Invite the unprotected state:** "Let's just be fucking awkward."
7. **Move on with the actual work.**

The whole move takes thirty seconds. It shifts the entire field of the conversation.

## Why It Works

The body produces defense before the mind does. The crossed arms appeared while the client was deciding which question to ask. The client's stated reason ("mulling over which question") was a story laid over the body's actual move (closing off from the awkwardness).

Naming the body move out loud:
- Surfaces the protection mechanism before it solidifies into a stance
- Gives the client conscious access to what was happening unconsciously
- Demonstrates that the coach is paying attention at the somatic level, which calibrates the client to the kind of work they are about to do
- Creates a tiny opportunity to choose differently, in real time

The client cannot un-cross their arms unconsciously after the gesture has been named. They have to either uncross them deliberately or notice themselves keeping them crossed deliberately. Either move is more conscious than what was happening before.

## What "Let's Just Be Fucking Awkward" Does

The invitation to remain in the awkward state is the operative move. The body crossed its arms to escape awkwardness. Joe's instruction is to stay in the awkwardness instead. This is the move from defense to welcoming, applied to the smallest possible felt experience.

The client cannot do the larger work of the session (loving the no, dropping hypervigilance, opening to disagreement) without being able to do the smaller version of the same work (staying in awkwardness rather than crossing arms to escape it). The small move is the prerequisite for the large move.

Joe's phrasing — direct, casual, with humor — also lowers the stakes. The instruction isn't "feel the awkwardness fully and notice what arises." The instruction is "let's just be fucking awkward." The same content, but easier to do because it's framed as a shared low-stakes choice. The "let's" includes the coach in the awkwardness. The "fucking" is permission to stop performing seriousness.

## What the Opening Gesture Tells the Coach About the Whole Person

Joe's commentary on the same crossed-arms moment reveals what a coach reads from a single opening gesture. The reading happens in seconds and informs the rest of the session:

"His typical way of like — if you have to think about him in the fear triangle, right, he's probably going to be more in the bully section of the fear triangle. Like, he doesn't have any problem presenting with anger. He does that immediately with that kind of crossed-arm defensive anger rather than say like, oh, I'm stuck, or I'm here to save somebody. But the more important thing that he announces already is that he actually is quite introspective. He knows how to feel into his body. He knows how to say what's going on with him underneath the surface level. Which means he's done a considerable amount of work." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free (Coaching Reaction Video)

Two things are being read at once:

1. **Which corner of the [[fear-triangle]] this person defaults to.** The crossed-arms posture under awkwardness signals bully position (fight) rather than victim (freeze) or savior (flight). The fear-response shape determines what coaching moves will land.

2. **How much capacity the person has to work with their own pattern.** When the client can answer "how does crossing your arms help you mull that over?" with "it actually doesn't help, it's when I'm feeling awkward, that is something I do" — the client is demonstrating somatic literacy and self-honesty. This is not a beginner. The coaching can move at a different pace.

The combination matters. A bully-position person who has done the work is rare and easy to misread. A coach who only sees the defensive anger will write them off as merely aggressive. A coach who only sees the introspection will miss the active defense pattern still operating. Reading both at once tells the coach: this person can go deep fast if you don't get scared off by the surface presentation.

"When somebody interacts with somebody like this, they'll be likely to write them off because of the propensity for like the urgh and the propensity for the defensiveness. And not be able to see that whole secondary thing that's happening, which is, he says he's actually quite introspective, quite thoughtful." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free (Coaching Reaction Video)

The "let's just be fucking awkward" invitation works specifically because the client has already shown they can do this. A coach offering the same invitation to someone without somatic literacy would get either deeper defense or compliance-without-feeling. With this client, Joe could see the freedom-behind-the-defensiveness already lived-in, so the invitation lands as a small homecoming rather than a scary instruction.

## Reading the Client's Smile at Their Own Pattern

A second moment in the same session shows another diagnostic layer. After Joe asks "what are you trying to control for?" the client laughs. Joe reads the laugh as evidence the client is seeing their own pattern from a non-shame position:

"The laugh that I see right there is that he sees something about himself. And not only does he see it, but he can also see that it's kind of funny, that there's like a — there's something in it for him. And so again, not shame, just like a lightness, which is really quite sweet." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free (Coaching Reaction Video)

The diagnostic value of the laugh: when a person sees their own pattern and laughs with lightness rather than collapsing into shame, the work can proceed quickly. The shame would have required its own intervention. The lightness means the recognition is already integrating in real time. See [[the-smile-of-recognition]] for the related read.

## When to Use This Move

Use it when:
- A defensive gesture appears at a pivot point in the session
- The client's body and words are giving different information
- The session is about defense / control / hypervigilance and a live example becomes available
- The client is about to launch into a long story that would route around the felt moment

Do not use it when:
- The gesture is normal body movement with no diagnostic content
- The client is already overwhelmed and naming more bodily signals would flood them
- The relationship is not yet established enough that the naming would feel like a gotcha

The judgment about which case applies is calibrated by the coach's read of the client's nervous system. The threshold is whether the client has the capacity to receive the observation without becoming more defended.

## What Not to Do

Do not lecture about the defense. Naming it is the move. Explaining what defense is, why it happens, what it means — all of that turns a thirty-second somatic move into a ten-minute intellectual detour. The client's body already knows what the gesture was doing. Trust that.

Do not require the client to drop the defense before continuing. The invitation is offered; if the client uncrosses their arms, fine; if they don't, fine. The naming itself was the move. The choice to drop or keep the defense is the client's, and either choice is now conscious.

Do not use the move to assert authority. "I notice all the defense in that" is information, not a verdict. The tone is observational, slightly curious, often with humor. If the client hears it as a judgment, the move has failed and the next minute should repair the misread.

## The Move Across Many Body Signals

The same move generalizes to other body signals:

- A hand going up to the face
- A leg crossing
- A sudden lean back
- A small smile that doesn't match the words
- An exhale that wasn't there before
- A jaw tightening
- A swallow before answering

For any of these: notice, name, inquire, identify the protection, invite the unprotected state. The protocol is the same. The specific signal changes.

The skill is in the noticing. The naming is easy once you've seen it. The hard part is having attention available for the body while also tracking the content of what the client is saying. This is a trained capacity. It develops with practice.

## Tone

Casual, light, slightly amused. The naming is observational, not accusing. The "let's just be fucking awkward" is delivered as an invitation between equals, not as an instruction from authority. The whole move is over in thirty seconds and then the session continues without ceremony.

If the tone gets heavy or instructional, the move stops working. The body re-defends in response to the heaviness. The lightness is part of the technique.

## Dialogue Example

From How Do I Love The "No"?:

- **Client:** [crosses arms while deciding which question to ask]
- **Joe:** "What made you just cross your arms?"
- **Client:** "Mulling over which question I want to ask because they're both important to me."
- **Joe:** "Okay, what — how does crossing your arms help you mull that over?"
- **Client:** "It creates tension in my body, and it actually doesn't help. It's actually, uh, when I'm feeling awkward, that is something I do."
- **Joe:** "Ah, great. So it's a form of kind of protection of feeling awkward, is what I'm trying to say."
- **Client:** "Yeah, absolutely."
- **Joe:** "Let's just — how about let's just be fucking awkward? Great. What's your question?"

Notice the second question — "how does crossing your arms help you mull that over?" — gently challenges the client's first explanation (the mind's cover story) and gets the body's actual report. The client comes to "it actually doesn't help" themselves. Joe does not tell them what the gesture means; he lets them discover it.
