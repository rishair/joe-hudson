# Name When You Can't Be in Loving Attention

## The Move

When the parent does not have loving attention available — they are depleted, dysregulated, having a hard day, at their limit — the move is to name this honestly rather than perform it. The performance is worse than the honesty. Three forms of the move, in order of preference:

1. **Tell the kid directly.** "I just can't be in loving attention with you right now."
2. **Hand off to the co-parent if one is available.** They take over the loving-attention role; the depleted parent steps back.
3. **Leave the situation if necessary.** "We just got to leave the store." Exit the environment so the parent can regulate.

"It's also okay to say to a kid, like, I just can't be in loving attention with you right now. We just got to leave the store. And that's totally fine. They'll understand that, which is interesting." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The move is the necessary companion to [[loving-attention-vs-consistency]]. The frame says loving attention is the actual variable. The move says: when loving attention is not available, name it and take an available exit, do not pretend to have what you don't have.

## When to Use This Move

The triggers for the move:

- The parent notices internal frustration, exhaustion, or shame rising
- The parent recognizes they are about to respond from a depleted state
- The parent has been white-knuckling presence for too long and the white-knuckling is about to break
- A specific situation (the grocery store, the dinner table, the bedtime routine) is overwhelming the parent's capacity
- The parent has been triggered by the kid's behavior in a way that has put them outside loving attention
- The parent's body signals (chest tightness, breath shortening, jaw clenching) that say capacity is gone

The signal is internal. The move is not "use this when convenient." The move is "use this when the alternative is performing loving attention from a state that does not actually have it."

## Why the Honesty Works Better Than the Performance

The kid is calibrating to the parent's actual state, not to the parent's performance. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]]. The parent who is performing loving attention from a depleted state is transmitting the depletion through the channel the kid is actually reading. The performed words land on top of a felt-state of "this parent is not okay right now," which the kid receives regardless of the words.

The honesty resolves the mismatch. The kid is no longer receiving the contradictory signal of "calm words / not-calm field." The kid is receiving a clean signal: "I'm not in a state to be present right now, and I'm naming it." The kid's nervous system can integrate this. The performance, the kid cannot integrate.

The honesty also installs a useful lesson the performance does not. The kid is learning that adults can recognize their own limits, name them, and take action to honor them. This is one of the most important things the kid will need to do as an adult. The parent who can never name a limit teaches the kid that limits cannot be named. The parent who can name limits teaches the kid that limits are nameable and that naming them is responsible behavior.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A few concrete versions:

**The direct naming.** "Hey buddy, I'm not in the right place to do this with you right now. I need a minute. Can we come back to this when I've taken a breath?"

**The handoff.** "I'm at my limit on this. Let me get [partner / other parent] to be with you on this one." Then the handoff actually happens. See [[pre-agree-on-the-handoff-signal-with-co-parent]] for the pre-established protocol that makes this clean.

**The environmental exit.** "We need to leave the store. I'm going to pick you up and we're going to go to the car." Sometimes the move is to remove the variables that are exceeding the parent's capacity. The exit is not a punishment. It is the parent honoring their actual state.

**The pause.** "I'm going to step into the other room for two minutes. I'll be right back." The parent leaves to do whatever they need to do to come back into loving attention. Often two minutes is enough. See [[model-emotional-movement-for-your-kids]] for the related move of letting the kid witness the parent doing their own regulation work.

The right form depends on the situation. The constant across all of them: the parent is honest about what they have available and acts from there.

## What This Move Doesn't Do

The move does not require the parent to explain in detail why they can't be in loving attention. The kid does not need the parent's reasoning. The kid needs the honest naming. A long explanation often becomes a request for the kid to validate the parent's overwhelm, which slips back into the [[kids-shouldnt-caretake-the-parent]] pattern.

The move does not require the parent to feel bad about not having loving attention. Beating themselves up for not having more capacity is its own form of depleting attention. The move is matter-of-fact: this is what is available right now, this is what I'm doing about it.

The move also does not produce abandonment. The parent is not disappearing. The parent is naming a limit and either taking the available exit or handing off. The relationship is not being severed; the present moment is being honored.

## The Kid's Capacity to Receive This

A predictable parental worry: "Won't the kid feel rejected if I say I can't be present right now?" The empirical answer Joe offers: "They'll understand that, which is interesting." Kids who have been treated as people whose understanding is real can receive this kind of naming. The naming, delivered with respect, is its own form of inclusion in the family's actual reality.

A kid who has been raised with constant performance — adults pretending to be okay when they are not — receives the honest naming as relief, often, because the dissonance they have been picking up is now being acknowledged.

This is one of the practical applications of [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]]: an adult you respect, you would tell. You would not perform a level of presence you do not have. You would say, "I'm at my limit, I need to step out." The same move applied to the kid is the respect frame in action.

## The Move Is Not a Permission to Disengage Generally

A risk: a parent uses the move as a cover to chronically disengage. The move is for the cases when loving attention is not available. It is not a strategy for parenting from disengagement. A parent who is naming inability-to-be-present multiple times a day, every day, has a different problem — usually depletion, marital strain, lack of support — that needs to be addressed at the structural level. See [[take-care-of-yourself-then-marriage-then-kids]].

The move is for the cases that arise even in a parent who has done the structural work. Even well-resourced parents have days when loving attention is not on tap. The move is the integrity of those moments.

## What This Move Is the Antidote To

The move is the antidote to a specific failure mode: the parent who has read the parenting books, knows that they "should" be in loving attention, cannot access it in the moment, and performs it anyway. The performance leaks. The kid receives both the performed words and the actual state. The mismatch is its own form of harm. The kid learns that adults lie about their state.

The move replaces this failure mode with clean honesty. The kid learns adults have limits and name them.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The move is delivered without drama or apology. The parent is not collapsing into how-bad-they-feel-about-not-being-present. The parent is naming a reality and taking an available action. The tone communicates that this is normal, recoverable, and not catastrophic. Hold the same tone in coaching: practical, non-apologetic, real.
