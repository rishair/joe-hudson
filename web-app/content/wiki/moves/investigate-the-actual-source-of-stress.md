# Investigate the Actual Source of Stress (What You Think It's About Is Usually Not What It's About)

## The Move

When a person presents stress and names what they think is causing it ("I'm stressed because of work," "I'm stressed because of the kids," "I'm stressed because of money"), do not immediately accept the named cause. Instead, treat the named cause as a hypothesis and investigate the actual source.

The investigation move opens space for the discovery that the named cause is often not the real one. The real cause is typically at a different layer than the one the person has named: lack of sleep, unfelt emotion, cultural pressure to perform stress, identity protection, a should about the situation, or an inner-critic loop running underneath.

The investigation has a specific form: "what could be all the possibilities that is the main culprit in creating that stress?" Not what is the one cause. What are all the possible causes. The plural-possibility framing prevents the conversation from settling on the named cause too early.

"When we get stressed, we tie it to something and say, this is the thing that we're stressed about, but actually we haven't investigated that fully. We haven't done the experiments to say, oh, how can I find what I'm actually, what is the underlying stress? And so one of the most important things that you can do when you feel that stress and you're like, oh, I wanna address this stress, is to start investigating what could be all the possibilities that is the main culprit in creating that stress." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

## When To Use It

- The person presents stress and immediately attributes it to a specific external cause.
- The person has tried to address the named cause and the stress has not resolved.
- The named cause is structural and unchangeable (e.g., "I have two kids," "I have a job") and the stress persists despite working with the structural reality.
- The person is in a loop of blaming a particular person or situation for their stress.
- The presenting concern is [[im-so-stressed-out]] and the work needs to move beyond the surface attribution.

Do not use this move when:
- The person is in acute crisis and needs containment rather than investigation.
- The named cause is a fresh, time-limited event that genuinely is the cause (e.g., a death just happened).
- The investigation would feel dismissive of the person's surface complaint and rupture the working alliance. Build trust first, then investigate.

## How to Run the Investigation

1. **Receive the named cause without immediately challenging it.** "Okay, you're stressed about work. Tell me more about that." The person needs to feel heard. The investigation cannot land if it feels like the named cause is being dismissed.

2. **Introduce the possibility frame.** "What if that's not actually what the stress is about? What if it's about something underneath?" Or: "I'm curious whether there's something else underneath." The framing is curiosity, not contradiction.

3. **Walk through the layers.** The [[four-causes-of-stress]] map provides the structure. For each layer, ask whether it might be loaded:
   - **Sleep / nervous system:** "When was the last time you really slept? When was the last time you had a stretch where the body actually rested?"
   - **Emotion:** "What are you feeling that you haven't let yourself feel? Anger, fear, sadness?"
   - **Thought:** "What's the story you're telling about this? What's the should running underneath?"
   - **Identity / culture:** "Is there a part of you that needs to look stressed to feel relevant or valuable? Is the culture you're in one where being stressed is the way you prove you're working?"

4. **Test the alternative hypotheses.** If a different cause surfaces as possible, suggest an experiment. "What if you slept 12 hours and saw what happens?" "What if you had a big cry and saw what shifts?" "What if you let yourself be angry about this and saw what's left?"

5. **Let the experiment teach.** The investigation is not concluded by debate. It is concluded by the body's response to the experiment. The actual cause is the one whose addressing produces relief.

## What This Move Produces

People often discover that the named cause is not the actual cause. Common discoveries:

- The "stress about work" is actually unprocessed anger that has been displaced onto the workload.
- The "stress about the relationship" is actually exhaustion from not sleeping for months.
- The "stress about money" is actually a cultural-identity issue about looking successful.
- The "stress about the kids" is actually the absence of any unstructured time for the self.
- The "stress about being stressed" is the layer on top — the rejection of the stress itself producing most of the load. See [[stress-as-rejection-of-stress]].

The discovery is usually surprising and often relieving. The person had been trying to fix the wrong thing. Once the actual cause is named, the path becomes available.

## The Sleep-Not-The-Marriage Example

The clearest example of the investigation working comes from a personal story Joe tells. He and his wife were in a period of constant fighting after the second baby was born. They had assumed the issue was the marriage: communication problems, dynamics, the relationship. They were exploring counseling. A friend offered to take the kids so each of them could go to a hot springs to sleep. Joe slept 18 hours the first night and 14 hours the second. When he came home, his wife went and slept the same amount. When they were both back, the marriage problems were gone.

"There wasn't any problems in the marriage anymore. And it blew me away because we had always thought about if there's any problems in the marriage, it's because something's going on emotionally or psychologically. It was just about sleep. There was no actual problem in the marriage." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

See [[sleep-not-the-marriage-story]] for the full example. The lesson Joe takes from it is the foundation of this move: "if I'm stressed, is it because of the thoughts that I'm having? Is it because I'm not having enough sleep? Is it because I am emotionally overwhelmed?"

## The Emotional Release Example

Joe describes a second pattern from his own life: when working really hard and feeling overwhelmed, if he had a big cry, got angry, or released an emotion, the overwhelm shifted. The next day he would be doing the same work and not be stressed.

"What I noticed is when I was working really hard and I would feel overwhelmed by that, fully stressed out by how much I had to do, if I had a big cry or if I got angry or I had frustration, I released some emotion. All of a sudden it wasn't — I found out it wasn't about having so much to do. The next day I'd be doing the same stuff and I wouldn't be stressed out." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

The work was not the cause of the stress. The unfelt emotion underneath the work was the cause. The work was the perceived cause; the body was holding feeling that needed to move. Once the feeling moved, the perceived cause stopped registering as stressful.

## The Cultural-Pressure Example

Joe describes a third discovery: sometimes the stress is not about the work or the emotion but about the cultural requirement to perform stress.

"I would find out, oh, it's not about my stress. It's about I am in a culture where I need to be stressed to seem relevant or to look like I'm doing my job." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

In this case, the stress is not a response to load. It is a performance of competence required by the social environment. The investigation has to extend to: "am I stressed because of what I am doing, or because I am expected to look stressed?"

See [[cultural-pressure-to-perform-stress]] for the underlying principle.

## What Joe Does With the Response

When a candidate alternative cause is identified, Joe often suggests a small experiment to test it. The experiment is the proof. Talking about possible causes does not produce certainty; doing the experiment does.

If the cause is sleep: "what if you really slept this weekend, and we saw what was left?"

If the cause is unfelt emotion: "let's see what happens if you let yourself feel angry about this right now." See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

If the cause is identity or cultural: "what would it be like to be the person in your work culture who isn't stressed? What would happen?" See [[every-identity-has-limits]].

The investigation is not concluded by the move; it is opened by the move. The person leaves with the inquiry alive. The actual cause becomes clear as the experiments produce data.

## Tone

Curious, exploratory, light. The investigation should not feel like the person's complaint is being dismissed. It should feel like a wider possibility space is being opened. The phrase "what if it's about something underneath?" delivered with genuine curiosity invites the person into the search, rather than telling them they are wrong about their stress.

The move requires the coach to hold the multiple possibilities without committing to one too early. The person watching the coach hold multiple causes without anxiety learns to do the same in their own investigation.
