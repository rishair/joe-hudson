# Press the Button Thought Experiment

## The Move

When a person has organized their life around avoiding something they think they want to avoid, Joe offers them a magic button. The button delivers the avoidance perfectly: never offend anyone, never feel anger, never get rejected. The diagnostic is the hesitation.

The form: "Let's say I could do it. Let's say there's a button and we could push that button and you would never [feared outcome]. Would you want me to push that button?"

The hesitation reveals what the person actually wants. Almost no one says yes immediately. The hesitation is the data. It says: there is something in the feared thing that the person values, or there is some impossibility in the avoidance the person already knows.

## When Joe Uses It

When a person's coaching frame is "help me avoid X." The frame is taken seriously by being offered. "Okay, let's say you could. Would you want it?" This forces the person to look at their wish rather than just live inside it.

Useful when:
- Someone wants to never offend people. [[im-afraid-of-offending-people]]
- Someone wants to never feel anger.
- Someone wants to never be rejected.
- Someone wants to be perfectly understood.
- Someone wants to never make a mistake.

The button can be pressed for any wish that the person is organizing their behavior around. The wish does not have to be plausible. The point of the experiment is the felt response, not the hypothetical.

## What It Produces

A few things, depending on the person:

1. **Hesitation.** The person pauses, says "yes," then quickly "no." The pause itself is the move. Joe lets it land: "Ah, yeah, right."

2. **Discovery of the impossibility.** When the person tries to imagine the world after the button is pressed, they see the contradiction. "Well, there's two versions. The one where I'm going out being an ass to everyone and they're all okay with it... but then they don't accept me, they ignore me." The wish collapses under its own weight.

3. **Discovery of the actual want.** The hesitation reveals that the person wants the very thing they thought they wanted to avoid: they want to be themselves, they want to be honest, they want to feel anger, they want to be seen as imperfect. The feared thing was protecting something the person values.

4. **A pivot.** With the wish exposed as either impossible or unwanted, the conversation moves to what is actually going on. The presenting concern collapses into the real coaching question.

## Joe's Follow-up Sequence

After the button question, Joe usually escalates:

1. **The first button.** "Would you want me to push that button?" Record the hesitation.

2. **Imagine the world after.** "What would it look like if you couldn't offend anybody?" The person tries to imagine and finds the contradiction.

3. **Test with the easy case.** "Say something that you are absolutely 100% won't offend me." The person tries. Joe finds the person who would still be offended ("a fashion person who insists it's periwinkle, not blue"). The wish is shown to be impossible.

4. **Calibrate the threshold.** Joe then moves to [[calibrate-the-threshold]]: "What percentage of the time do you want to not offend people?" The shift from absolute to percentage signals the real conversation.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "Let's say I could do it. Let's say there's a button and we could push that button and you would never offend anybody again. Would you want me to push that button?"
- **Guest:** "There's a hesitation."
- **Joe:** "Ah, yeah, right."
- **Guest:** "Yeah. First it's like, yes. And then quite quickly, no, but then I can't be myself or I can't say my meaning or like do my work."
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves)*

- **Joe:** "What would it look like if you couldn't offend anybody?"
- **Guest:** "Well, there's two versions. The one where I'm going out being an ass to everyone and they're all okay with it... but then they don't accept me, they ignore me."
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves. The person discovers the contradiction without Joe naming it.)*

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the person hesitates: name the hesitation as the data. "Notice you hesitated." Then ask what the hesitation knew.
- If the person says yes immediately: stay in the hypothetical and let them imagine the world. Reality usually does the work. "What would that actually look like?"
- If the person tries to qualify ("I'd want to be able to choose"): the choosing IS the issue. The wish for control over offense is the thing organizing their life. Move toward how that control already failed.
- If the person discovers the contradiction themselves: do not over-explain. Let them sit with the discovery. The body has already learned what it needed to learn.

## Tone

Light, playful, exploratory. The button is presented as a gift, not a trap. "Let's just see." Joe makes the hypothetical feel safe to enter so the person actually engages rather than dismissing it.

## The Past-Suffering Form: Would You Erase What You Went Through?

A third orientation of the button works in the past tense. When a person is afraid of an emotional state recurring (depression, anxiety, heartbreak, helplessness), the button can be offered to erase the original instance of the state from their history. "I have a button. I can push it and you were never depressed. Would you want me to push it?"

The diagnostic is the same: the hesitation, and specifically the answer that almost always arrives -- "no." The "no" reveals that the past suffering, however unwanted at the time, has retrospectively become something the person values. It taught them something. It produced who they now are. They would not trade it.

The "no" then becomes the entry to the recursion the session is heading toward. The person is simultaneously holding two positions: "I do not want to be depressed again" and "I would not erase the depression I had." The two positions are not compatible. The unspoken assumption holding them together is that the depression is bad and the gift is somehow separable from the depression. The session has just disproved that assumption.

From there, the move opens directly into [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]: "and here's the weird part -- the more that you don't want it to happen again, the more likely it is to happen again." The button has already done the work that makes this principle land. The person cannot retreat into "but I just don't want it again" because they have just declined to erase the prior instance. They have stated, with their own mouth, that the state they fear has been a gift.

The follow-up that consolidates the shift: [[speak-the-welcoming-of-the-feared-state-aloud]] -- "say it out loud, I can't wait to be depressed again." The button revealed the truth; speaking the welcoming aloud lands the truth in the body.

A dialogue example from the depression session:

- **Joe:** "I have a button right here. I can push it and then you were never depressed. You want me to push the button? Do you want me to take away the depression that you felt?"
- **Guest:** "No."
- **Joe:** "So your intuition was right, motherfucker. Right? Yeah. That depression was a gift for you. I can see it. What would make you reject the gift again? I think that's the gift. If that's the gift that wants to be given to you again, what would make you reject it in advance?"
- **Guest:** "Don't go through the same experiences and through the same pain."
- **Joe:** "But you don't want me to take it away from you?"
- **Guest:** "No."
- *(Source: I'm Afraid I'll Get Depressed Again)*

The second offer of the button matters. Joe repeats the question after the person has named their fear of the same experiences. The repetition forces the recognition: the person is afraid of the experiences AND would not erase them. The two co-existing positions are the structure that the rest of the session works on.

The past-suffering form pairs naturally with [[adversity-as-for-not-against]]. The principle says past adversity often turns out to have served the person. The button is the experiential test of that principle. The person who would push the button to erase past adversity has not yet seen the gift. The person who would not has seen it and can be brought into deeper relationship with it.

This form is also a relative of [[visualizing-feared-outcomes]], inverted in time. That move imagines a future feared event; this one imagines erasing a past one. Both expose the actual structure of the person's relationship to the feared state.

## The Inversion: Testing Value Through the Button

The button works in the opposite direction too. If someone has organized their life around moving away from something (anxiety, fear, anger), the button can test whether they actually believe that thing is bad — or whether they unconsciously recognize its value. The form: "If you could push a button that would [give this thing to someone else / install this thing in a new system], would you?"

The yes-without-hesitation reveals that the person sees value in what they personally moved beyond. The contrast between "I worked hard to escape this" and "I would absolutely give this to a new being" is the data.

Joe uses this with Sam Altman in the context of AGI and fear: "If you could push a button and the button was basically fear is something that AI gets to experience, would you push the button?" Sam answers "Of course" immediately. Joe then asks the follow-up that opens the actual teaching: "What makes that so? And I say that because obviously moving out of anxiety has been an incredibly important thing to you. So you see some wisdom in the fear. So what creates that?" -- Joe Hudson, Sam Altman

The answer Sam gives ("fear is an important emotion... it's a useful signal for learning and staying alive") is itself the discovery — the person who escaped anxiety still sees fear as valuable. The button surfaced what the person knows but had not explicitly named.

Useful when:
- Someone has worked hard to move past a state (anxiety, anger, neediness) and may have over-corrected into rejecting the underlying emotion entirely.
- Someone is teaching others to escape something they themselves are not sure they should escape.
- Designing systems (a team culture, a parenting approach, a product) and the question is whether the designer would actually install the absence of a difficult feeling if they could.

The yes-without-hesitation followed by "what makes that so?" surfaces the implicit wisdom the person carries about the rejected thing.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not argue with the person's first answer. The hesitation is the move, not the answer.
- Do not push the button rhetorically. The experiment is "would you?" not "you wouldn't, would you?"
- Do not let the conversation stay hypothetical. After the button, return to the felt sense in the room.
