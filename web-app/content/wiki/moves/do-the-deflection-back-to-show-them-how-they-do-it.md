# Do the Deflection Back to Show Them How They Do It

## The Move

When a client is deflecting compliments and cannot see the deflection from inside, the coach performs the deflection at the client. The coach offers a small acknowledgement and watches what the client does. Then the coach gives the same response back — the same "mm-hmm" the client just gave, with the same flat tone. The coach explicitly names that the imitation is a demonstration, not agreement.

The clarifying sentence:

"I'm saying I'm doing that, mm-hmm, not because I'm agreeing with you, because I'm showing you how you do it." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The move's mechanism: the client cannot see their own deflection because it is happening from inside their body and has been happening their whole life. Being on the receiving end of the same move, performed by someone the client trusts, makes the move visible from the outside. The client's "oh, I don't mean to do that" is the diagnostic that the seeing has happened.

## When Joe Uses It

The setup:

- The client is in a session about compliment-receiving or related receiving-work
- The client has been deflecting compliments verbally or somatically (often both)
- The client cannot name the deflection from inside — they say "yeah" and "mm-hmm" and continue, unaware that the deflection is operating
- Standard naming of the deflection ("you just deflected that") has not produced full recognition
- A more visceral demonstration is needed

The move is distinct from [[use-recent-deflection-to-test-receiving-live]] — that move is the coach deflecting the client's compliment to the coach in order to test the client's open-heart-through-rejection capacity. This move is the coach performing the client's own deflection back at them to make the client's deflection-pattern visible. The first move tests a capacity; this move installs an observation.

## What This Move Produces

When it lands:

- The client's body registers being on the receiving end of the move they have been performing
- The client often says some version of "oh, I don't mean to do that" — the recognition that the move has been happening without their consent
- The client's framing of themselves as "bad at compliments" deepens into a felt understanding of what the deflection actually looks like
- The coach is positioned to follow with the body-level observation ("your lips tighten and you go mm-hmm") and the cost-of-deflection teaching (see [[the-cake-metaphor-for-deflected-compliments]])

If the move does not land, the client may experience the imitation as confusing or insulting. In that case, the coach has to be quick to clarify the intent. Joe's clarifying sentence — "I'm doing that, not because I'm agreeing with you, because I'm showing you how you do it" — exists precisely for this risk. The move requires the coach to be ready to explain immediately if the demonstration is being read as agreement or rejection.

## The Critical Clarification

The move requires the named clarification to land. Without the clarification, the client may interpret the coach's deflection as:

- Real agreement that the client's self-deprecation is correct ("oh, I really am not pretty")
- Real rejection of the client's previous moment of softness
- A confusing emotional cross-current that derails the session

The clarification short-circuits all three readings: "I'm doing that, not because I'm agreeing with you, because I'm showing you how you do it." The sentence names the move (the imitation), names what it is not (agreement), and names what it is (a demonstration). The client can then re-read the previous 5 seconds with the demonstration-frame in place.

The order matters. The clarification should follow the demonstration, not precede it. If the coach announces "I'm about to imitate what you're doing," the demonstration loses its diagnostic power — the client braces and rationalizes rather than feeling the imitation land. Doing the demonstration first and clarifying after lets the body register the move before the head can intervene.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client says "oh, I don't mean to do that": Joe affirms the recognition and uses it as the bridge to the next layer. The client has now seen the deflection from outside. The work now is to see it from inside in real time. Joe may move directly to the body cue ("your lips tighten and you go mm-hmm") to install the somatic detection.

If the client gets defensive ("you're not really doing what I'm doing"): Joe acknowledges and re-demonstrates with more precision. Often the defensiveness is itself another form of deflection. The coach can name this too: "even this — pushing back against me showing you — is the same move."

If the client laughs: Joe sometimes joins the laughter and then returns to the work. The laughter is often the recognition arriving as embarrassment-relief. The work continues from a more open base.

If the client cries: Joe stays with the tears. The recognition that the deflection has been operating without consent often opens grief — the system that has been blocking love for years has just been seen blocking love.

## Dialogue Example

After the client said "I don't trust enough" and Joe offered a compliment that the client deflected with "mm-hmm":

- **Joe:** "Yeah. Like, if I was to show up and I'm like, here's this beautiful cake I baked you, and you went, mm-hmm, would I bring you — would I bring you another fucking cake?"
- **Client:** "Is that what I'm doing?"
- **Joe:** "Mm-hmm." [Joe does the same mm-hmm back at the client.]
- **Client:** "Oh, I don't mean to do that."
- **Joe:** "I'm saying I'm doing that, mm-hmm, not because I'm agreeing with you, because I'm showing you how you do it."
- **Client:** "Right."
- **Joe:** "Like, you're sitting here talking to me, and then I give you a compliment, your lips tighten, and you go, mm-hmm. It's like you're physically constricting to not let in my compliment."
- **Client:** "That's so true."

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The exchange shows the move's full arc: the imitation, the client's flash of confusion ("Oh, I don't mean to do that"), the clarification ("I'm showing you how you do it"), and the bridge to the body-level observation that follows. The move took about 15 seconds. The recognition that resulted ("that's so true") set up the rest of the session.

## The Mimicking-Resistance Variant

The move generalizes from compliment-deflection to the broader case of any pattern firing in the room that the client cannot see from inside. A specific variant: the client is in the [[wants-help-resists-help-in-real-time]] configuration, asking for help and resisting what the coach is offering. The coach mimics the resistance back at the client and names the imitation as the demonstration.

From the source session, after the client kept saying "I don't get the question" and "I'm trying to grasp" in response to clear offers from the coach:

"This thing that you're dealing with in this moment is the thing to be addressed. It's not, how do I move my life forward? It's like, what's creating this? I'm mimicking you right now, so you know what's creating that." -- Joe Hudson, She's Been Stuck for 10 Years

The clarifying sentence is structurally identical to the compliment-deflection version: "I'm mimicking you right now" (the move), "so you know what's creating that" (the explanation that this is demonstration, not agreement or accusation). The move converts the client's experience-from-inside (sincere effort to understand) into experience-from-outside (watching the same operation being performed at them) so the operation becomes visible.

The variant differs from the compliment-deflection variant in that the imitation is not of a specific verbal tic (the "mm-hmm") but of the broader configuration — the trying-to-grasp, the asking-question-form, the not-quite-landing. The coach performs the configuration at the client by responding to the client's questions with the same kind of confusion the client has been generating, or by re-asking the client's own questions back at them in the same trying-to-grasp register.

The variant is most useful when the client is articulate and self-presents as cooperative. The articulation and the cooperativeness mean direct naming of the resistance ("you're resisting me") will be denied — the client genuinely experiences themselves as cooperating. The behavioral mirror sidesteps the denial by making the configuration audible without naming it. The client's recognition arrives in their body before it arrives in their cognition.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not mockery.** The imitation has to be clean, observational, without ridicule. Mockery would shame the client into more deflection.
- **It is not a guilt trip.** "Look what you're doing" delivered with judgment would install another defensive layer. The tone has to stay observational.
- **It is not the same as [[use-recent-deflection-to-test-receiving-live]].** That move deflects the client's compliment to test the client's giver-side open-heart capacity. This move imitates the client's deflection of the coach's compliment to make the client's receiver-side deflection visible.
- **It is not always the right move.** A client who is highly sensitive to imitation, or who is already too dysregulated to metabolize the move, may experience it as harmful. The coach has to read for the capacity to receive the demonstration without collapse.
- **It is not a substitute for the receiving work.** The move makes the deflection visible. The actual work of letting compliments land somatically still has to happen. See [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]].

## The Cost of Not Doing the Move

When the client cannot see the deflection from inside, naming it from outside often fails to land. The client says "yeah, you're right" and continues deflecting because the recognition is intellectual. The body has not registered what the deflection actually feels like from the other side. Without the body-level recognition, the work stays in the head and the deflection continues.

The do-the-deflection-back move solves this by making the body feel what the deflection is. The coach's mm-hmm lands in the client's body as a small refusal of contact. The body now knows what mm-hmm-as-deflection feels like — it just felt it. The next time the client does mm-hmm, there is a body-memory available that the client can use to recognize the move from inside.

## Tone

Clean, observational, slightly playful, with the readiness to clarify quickly. Joe's mm-hmm in the source session is not heavy. It is a precise imitation, delivered with the same flatness the client had used moments before. The lightness is what allows the demonstration to be received as data rather than as attack.

The clarification that follows ("I'm doing that because I'm showing you how you do it") is delivered matter-of-factly, with no heat. The coach is not defending the move. The coach is naming what the move is for.

The body-level observation that often follows ("your lips tighten and you go mm-hmm") is delivered with the same evenness — describing what is observable, not interpreting what it means.
