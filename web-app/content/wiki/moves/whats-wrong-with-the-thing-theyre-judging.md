# What's Wrong With That?

## The Move

When a person presents a self-judgment as the problem ("I'm fake," "I'm corrupt," "I want things from love," "I'm cunning," "I'm needy," "I'm selfish"), the move is to refuse the moral collapse and ask: "What's wrong with that?"

The question is not rhetorical. It is not provocative. It is an actual request for the person to articulate why the supposedly-bad thing is bad. Most of the time, they cannot answer it cleanly. The supposed badness is inherited, not examined. When asked to defend it, the moral certainty wobbles.

The move comes early in a session, often at the first turn, and it works as a reframe of the whole encounter. Instead of accepting the client's frame ("here is the bad thing about me, fix me"), Joe introduces a different frame ("the thing you think is bad may not be — let's look").

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person presents a self-judgment as the topic of the session
- When the supposed flaw is something the person has clearly already moralized internally
- When the person is asking for confirmation that the trait is bad (so it can be removed)
- When the person is in a story of corruption, fakeness, manipulation, neediness, or any self-accusation
- Early in a session, before the work has gone deeper, to set the frame
- When the person's energy is collapsing into the self-judgment and they need a slight resistance to keep them present

## What It Produces

- The person often pauses. The question lands as surprising.
- The person often re-states the judgment in a more defended form, looking for confirmation. Joe asks again or rephrases.
- The person sometimes attempts to explain why the trait is bad, and the explanation reveals the underlying belief (often a childhood-installed equation: wanting = unlovable, cunning = corrupt, needy = burdensome).
- The person sometimes admits they don't know why it is bad, just that it is.
- The reframe begins to operate even without the person explicitly accepting it. The fact that Joe is not joining the collapse loosens the collapse.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the person answers with a logical reason, Joe explores the reason ("Where did you learn that?" or "What would happen if that were OK?").
- If the person cannot answer, Joe moves on. The unanswerable nature of the question is the point. He may proceed to a body-scan move or a calibration move.
- If the person redoubles the judgment ("No, it really is bad, you don't understand"), Joe continues to refuse the frame. He may agree on the literal fact but refuse the moral collapse: "Yeah, that's awesome. How fake?" The "awesome" is not sarcasm — it is the refusal to treat the supposedly-damning fact as damning.
- If the person tries to give a backstory that explains why the trait is bad, Joe may interrupt and return to the present question: "No, no, not what's wrong with [the backstory]. What's wrong with [the trait itself]?"

## Dialogue Examples

From the "Am I A Fake?" session, repeated four times in quick succession:

- **Guest:** "I don't really love, but I have some sort of hidden agenda that I'm spreading love, but I want something from it."
- **Joe:** "Cool. What's wrong with that?"
- **Guest:** "I, I think it's about I'm fake, something like that."
- **Joe:** "Ah, that's awesome. How fake?"

And:

- **Guest:** "I feel like it, it hardens me."
- **Joe:** "So no, no, not what's wrong with wanting something from love."
- **Guest:** "Well, I think originally it stems from one situation with my mom..."
- **Joe:** "What's wrong with wanting something from love? What's wrong with being corrupt around love?"
- *(Source: Am I A Fake?)*

The repeated refusal of the moral frame is what eventually surfaces the deeper belief: that wanting itself makes her unlovable. Joe does not get there by exploring her story. He gets there by refusing to accept her frame and asking the question again.

## The Two-Word Version (Closing a Session)

The move also has a compressed closing form. When a client collapses into self-judgment at the end of a session — usually as the recognition lands — the response is two words.

- **Client:** "Yeah, that's embarrassing."
- **Joe:** "It's human."
- *(Source: How Do I Make Money Doing What I Love?)*

The public speaking coach has just been shown that he teaches the marketing skills he won't apply to himself. The collapse arrives as embarrassment. Joe refuses the embarrassment frame with the smallest possible move. Two words. No elaboration. No reassurance.

The "it's human" works because it does what the longer form does — refuse the moral collapse — but in a register that does not invite further analysis. The session ends on the refusal. The client takes the refusal with them. Whether they integrate it or not is up to them.

This compressed form is for the close of a session, after the recognition has landed. It works because:
- The client is in a soft state and does not need a long intervention
- The recognition is already there; the embarrassment is the response to the recognition
- The simplicity matches the simplicity of the recognition
- "It's human" places the supposed flaw in the universal frame, which is the structural refusal of the moral collapse

The longer form ("What's wrong with that?") opens the inquiry. The closing form ("It's human") closes it. Both refuse the same frame.

## Tone

Light, curious, almost playful. Joe will often say "cool" or "awesome" or "great" before asking the question, which signals he is not horrified by the trait, not concerned, not validating the self-attack. The lightness is not dismissive — it is the felt counter to the heaviness of the self-judgment.

The "cool" / "awesome" / "great" function as quick moves of [[meet-resistance-with-okay]] applied to the supposed flaw. The supposed flaw is met with okay, not with concern.

## Anti-Pattern Risk

The risk is that this move becomes a glib dismissal: "What's wrong with that? Nothing! You're great!" That is reassurance, which is the opposite of the move. The move is actually inviting the person to look at why they think the trait is bad. Reassurance closes the inquiry. The move opens it.

Also watch for using this move on traits that are genuinely costly to the person or others. "I'm a chronic abuser, what's wrong with that?" is not the correct application. The move applies when the trait is a normal human trait that has been moralized into a flaw, not when the trait is actually causing real harm. The distinction is felt: if the trait is wired-with-shame from childhood imprinting, this move opens it. If the trait is currently producing harm, the move would be a bypass.
