# Apply the Childhood Pattern Directly to the Present Problem

## The Move

Once a childhood pattern has been surfaced — "my dad made shame more important than me," "my mom made her depression more important than me," "my parent made the affair more important than me" — apply it to the present problem in one move. Take the exact template from childhood and ask: "How much are you doing the same thing right now with [the current presenting issue]?"

The structure of the question:

- "How much are you making [the parent's avoided emotion / surrogate object] more important than [the thing you say you want]?"
- "How much are you making [shame] more important than [money]?"
- "How much are you making [avoidance] more important than [you / your work / your relationship]?"

The move collapses the childhood scene and the adult presenting problem into a single piece of work. Whatever the parent prioritized over the child is what the adult is now prioritizing over their own want. Naming this in the same sentence makes the wiring visible in real time.

## When Joe Uses It

- Immediately after [[what-was-really-more-important-than-you]] has landed the parent's avoided emotion. The childhood scene is fresh; the same emotion is usually running in the present problem; the parallel can be made in one move.
- When the client has named a current avoidance (avoiding marketing, avoiding the conversation, avoiding the decision) and there is a structural match to a childhood pattern.
- When the client is intellectually aware of the childhood pattern but has not connected it to the current behavior. The application makes the connection somatic, not theoretical.
- When the client is at risk of leaving the session with insight but no integration. Applying the pattern to the present moment turns the insight into a felt recognition.

## What It Produces

**Immediate recognition.** The client almost always answers "much more" or "yeah" without resistance. The pattern is obvious once stated. The work has been to make it stateable.

**A clean handoff to the current work.** Once the parallel is named, the work shifts from the childhood scene to the present pattern. The childhood was the diagnostic; the present is the work.

**A reduction in the moral charge on the current behavior.** "I keep avoiding marketing" lands as personal failure. "I'm making shame more important than money the way my dad made shame more important than me" lands as a wiring that can be undone. The client moves from self-criticism to recognition.

**A natural opening for the next move.** The client's body is now organized around the recognition. The next move (testing whether the shame is actually in the room, finding the want, naming the skill-transfer) can land cleanly because the work has been re-anchored in the present.

## Joe's Sequence

1. **Establish the childhood pattern.** Use [[what-was-really-more-important-than-you]] to drill to the parent's avoided emotion.
2. **Pivot to the present.** "And so what's your issue with [the current thing]?" or "And what are you doing right now?"
3. **Let the client name the surface problem.** They will give the market frame, the cash flow frame, the relationship frame, the surface story.
4. **Apply the template.** "How much are you making [the parent's avoided emotion] more important than [the current want]?" Spoken plainly. The match between the childhood emotion and the current avoidance is structural — name it as such.
5. **Stack the second application if relevant.** "And how much are you making [the avoidance pattern itself] more important than [you / the goal]?" Joe often stacks the two because the avoidance and the underlying emotion are both running.
6. **Move to the diagnostic.** "Either [the work is bad] or [you are avoiding]. I'm going to assume it's the second." See [[binary-that-forces-the-avoidance-into-view]] for the related move.

## Dialogue Example

- *(After establishing the dad's avoidance of shame and helplessness as more important than the kid)*
- **Joe:** "And so what's your issue with money?"
- **Guest:** "When I love what I'm doing, I don't make any money. And when I hate what I'm doing, I've made lots and lots of money."
- **Joe:** "Do you need money?"
- **Guest:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "And what are you doing right now?"
- **Guest:** "I'm a coach. Public speaking."
- **Joe:** "What is it that, like, what's hard about making money here in coaching public speaking?"
- **Guest:** "Just cash flow, clients. Getting enough clients."
- **Joe:** "How much are you — how much are you making shame more important than —"
- **Guest:** "Yeah, much more."
- **Joe:** "And how much are you making avoidance more important than you, slash money. Either you're really a crappy coach, or you are not marketing yourself. I'm going to assume it's the second. And so that would be avoidance due to shame."
- *(Source: How Do I Make Money Doing What I Love?)*

The childhood pattern (dad avoided shame/helplessness; that was more important than the kid) gets applied directly to the adult presenting problem (the client is avoiding marketing; the avoidance is more important than the work / the income / the self). The client agrees instantly. The work then opens to the marketing avoidance specifically.

## Why It Works

The childhood pattern and the adult pattern are not analogies — they are the same wiring expressing in two domains. The kid's nervous system installed the rule "shame is what gets prioritized over my needs." The adult's nervous system runs the same rule and now prioritizes shame-avoidance over the things they need. Naming the structural match makes the wiring visible.

The question form ("how much are you making X more important than Y?") also forces a quantitative answer. The client cannot deflect with "well, sometimes" or "it's complicated." The question demands a yes/much/some. The honest answer is usually "much more" or "yeah." Once the quantity is named, the avoidance cannot hide behind nuance.

The application also depersonalizes the current behavior. The client has been telling themselves a story about cash flow, the market, the field. The application reveals the pattern as inherited rather than chosen. The client did not invent the avoidance of shame. They received it. That recognition makes the avoidance available for release in a way that self-criticism never could.

## Tone

Direct, dry, slightly clinical. Joe asks the question as if asking how many cups of coffee the client drank. The matter-of-fact tone is part of the work — it conveys that this is a normal mechanism, not a special pathology.

The dryness is also what makes the binary that often follows land cleanly: "Either you're a crappy coach or you're not marketing yourself." Said warmly or apologetically, the binary would feel like an accusation. Said dryly, it lands as obvious.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not apply the pattern before the childhood emotion has been named.** Without the specific emotion, the application becomes generic ("you're avoiding things") instead of structural ("you're avoiding shame the way your dad did"). The specificity is what makes the wiring legible.
- **Do not soften the question.** "Do you think maybe you might possibly be making shame a little more important than money?" has none of the diagnostic power of "How much are you making shame more important than money?" The directness is the move.
- **Do not let the application become an explanation.** The point is not to explain why the client avoids. The point is to give them somatic recognition of the wiring in real time. If the conversation drifts into "well, my dad was like this because his dad was like that," redirect to the present.
