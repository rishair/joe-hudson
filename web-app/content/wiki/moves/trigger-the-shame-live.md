# Trigger the Shame Live (Role-Play the Offended Other)

## The Move

When a person describes their fear of shame abstractly, Joe stops the description and produces the shame in the room. He becomes the upset other party. He says the harsh thing, gets dismissive, accuses the person of wasting his time, threatens to leave. The person feels the actual shame in their body rather than continuing to think about it.

The move is in service of [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]: shame cannot be metabolized while it is being conceptualized. The body has to encounter it.

## When Joe Uses It

When the person:
- Has been talking about shame, conflict, or being criticized for several minutes without actually feeling anything.
- Is trying to think their way to a different relationship with the feared emotion.
- Says "I want to be okay with shame" but the body has not encountered any shame in the conversation.
- Reports the emotion they would feel in a hypothetical but their physiology is calm.
- Presents [[im-afraid-of-offending-people]] and the coaching has moved into intellectual analysis.

The trigger is timed for after the person has agreed to experiment. Joe asks "can we experiment with it?" and the consent is part of the move. The person must opt into the role-play so the experience is felt as material rather than as actual rupture.

## How Joe Does It

Three things make the move work:

1. **Use what is already in the room.** Joe finds something present — the microphone, the hat, the way they are speaking — and makes that the surface complaint. The proximate cause is plausible enough that the body cannot entirely dismiss it.

2. **Drop the warmth.** Joe's tone changes. He gets sharp, dismissive, blunt. "I just can't believe this. You've wasted my time. We should just end this thing right now."

3. **Add the abandonment threat.** Joe threatens to leave or end the call. The threat of abandonment is what activates the shame fastest. "Okay, fine. I gotta go. I'll see you."

The role-play is short. A few sentences are enough. Joe is not trying to traumatize. He is trying to produce a brief, felt sample of the thing the person was talking about.

## What It Produces

- Immediate body sensation: a wall, pressure, freeze, the impulse to apologize.
- The person's actual shame response, in real time, with Joe present to coach through it.
- Material for the next moves: "Stay with the shame and what wants to be said."
- A demonstration that the feared feeling is brief and survivable. "How long do you think the shame will last?" "Like minutes." The body learns something the mind has been refusing.

## What Joe Does After

- Stay with the body. "So be with the shame and what wants to be said."
- Invite the vulnerable response that wants to come out. The shame state produces an impulse. Joe asks what wants to be said and helps the person say it.
- Distinguish kinds of "sorry." If the person apologizes, Joe lets them try the apology two ways: one from shame ("I'm sorry, please don't be mad") and one from solidarity ("I'm sorry that your microphone broke, I'm sad about it too").
- Make it explicit: "It's like, oh, I'm sorry that you hurt yourself. It's not like I've done anything wrong."
- Vary the trigger. After the first round, Joe may say "let's try it a different way" and have the person say something offensive to him, then play the role of the offended person leaving.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe (in character):** "Your microphone is like blowing out and this podcast isn't going to be usable. And like, you're wasting my time. You're wasting my time. So don't, don't disconnect from yourself for a moment."
- **Guest:** "There's sadness. Yeah. First I smile and laugh, and then there is like this emptiness and sadness."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So what happens if you just say that to me?"

- **Joe (in character):** "I just can't believe this. You've wasted my time. We should just end this thing right now. Just end it."
- **Guest:** "There's like, so this wall. Coming bubble or like pressure from my body."
- **Joe (out of character):** "So the only thing that's happening right now that's different than normal is that you're actually going into the emotional state instead of normally you're trying to end it off."
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves. The role-play produces the felt experience the person has been avoiding.)*

- **Guest:** "Joe, your hat is a bit off and it looks weird."
- **Joe (in character):** "Okay, fine. I gotta go. I'll see you."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, there's this freeze."
- **Joe:** "So be with the shame and what wants to be said."
- **Guest:** "Don't go. I don't want you to go."
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves. The abandonment threat produces immediate shame, and the vulnerable impulse — "don't go" — comes through.)*

## Tone

In character: sharp, dismissive, blunt, occasionally cold. Joe is willing to be unkind for the duration of the trigger. Out of character: warm, light, immediate. The reversion is fast. The person needs to feel both that the trigger was real and that Joe is right there with them.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not extend the role-play past what is needed. A few sentences are enough. Holding the trigger longer is cruelty, not coaching.
- Do not use this without consent. Joe asks "can we experiment with it?" first. The opt-in changes what the body can metabolize.
- Do not skip the warm reentry. After the trigger, Joe returns to himself fully and explicitly. "The only thing that's happening right now that's different than normal..." The naming reanchors the person in the coaching frame.
- Do not use this to make a point. The move is to produce experience, not to win an argument.
- Do not use this when the person is already in trauma activation. The point is to give the body a brief sample, not to flood.
