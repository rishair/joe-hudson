# Ask "What's Wrong With You?" Into the Shame

## The Move

When a client says they have shame, that they feel something is wrong with them, that they are broken in some way — refuse the story. Ask them, directly and bluntly, to name what is wrong with them. Then keep asking until the shame either produces specific content or dissolves under the question.

The instruction is exactly the question the shame is claiming has an answer. Shame says: *there is something wrong with me*. The move responds: *fine, tell me what it is*. The shame is being asked to deliver on its own claim.

Two things happen, both useful:
1. The person tries to think and produces only a story (intellectual content the shame uses as cover).
2. The person feels into it and discovers that the body cannot find anything wrong.

The contradiction between (1) and (2) is the work. The shame survives by never being asked to specify. The move forces specification, and the body answers truthfully.

"So shame means that there's something wrong with you." — Joe Hudson, *I Have To Do What Others Want*
"So tell me. Tell me what's wrong with you." — Joe Hudson, *I Have To Do What Others Want*
"I don't care about the story. I want to know what the fuck is wrong with you." — Joe Hudson, *I Have To Do What Others Want*

## When Joe Uses It

- When a client has named shame as the problem ("I have a lot of shame around money," "I've been carrying this shame my whole life") but is starting to tell the story of the shame instead of feeling into it.
- When the person reaches for a narrative or biographical explanation ("Well, I guess the story right now is...") immediately after naming a felt state.
- When the shame is operating at the body level and needs to be invited to make its actual claim out loud.
- When the person has done a lot of work on shame already and the shame has gone into hiding behind sophisticated self-knowledge ("I've worked through so much shame," "I've felt it, released it..."). The question cuts past the meta-commentary.
- When the client's response to a question about the shame is to start *trying* — trying to work on it, trying to figure it out, trying to identify it. The trying is itself the shame.
- When the client presents [[i-get-anxious-in-relationships]] or social anxiety, and names hiding or wearing a mask as the operative strategy. The hiding is shame-driven; the question asks the shame what it is hiding from.
- When the [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]] move has put the live anxiety on the table and the next question is what specifically is being hidden.

## Variants of the Question

The question takes shape depending on what shame is operating. The core is the same — demand the content of the shame — but the surface form follows what the shame is claiming:

- **The shame of being defective:** "What's wrong with you?"
- **The shame of being seen:** "What is it that I'm gonna see that I'm gonna think is bad?" or "What is it that we're going to find out when we see you that's shameful?"
- **The shame of a specific trait (corruption, neediness, cunningness):** "What's wrong with being [trait]?" — see [[whats-wrong-with-the-thing-theyre-judging]] for the moral-collapse refusal cousin.
- **The shame of wanting:** "What's wrong with wanting [the thing]?"

The being-seen variant is the form for social anxiety. The person who reports they don't like being seen, who hides or wears a mask, is operating from the assumption that being seen would expose something shameful. The question asks the shame to specify what.

## The Fix-Premise Variant: "So What's to Fix?"

A specific application opens fix-everything sessions. When the person presents that they are trying to fix themselves, the move runs as a three-turn sequence: state-the-premise, ask-the-question, follow-with-the-implication.

- **State the premise:** "So what's wrong with you?"
- **Receive the answer:** The person almost always says "nothing's wrong with me."
- **Follow with the implication:** "So what's to fix?"

The third turn does the work. The person has just confirmed nothing is wrong; the fix-pattern is operating on a premise the person, when asked, does not actually endorse. The contradiction is suddenly visible. The session has begun with the broken-frame already wobbling.

From the source session:

- **Guest:** "I'm trying to fix myself. That's probably the biggest thing."
- **Joe:** "So what's wrong with you?"
- **Guest:** "Nothing's wrong with me."
- **Joe:** "So what's to fix?"
- *(Source: Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?)*

This variant runs the same structure as the canonical shame variant but operates on the fix-the-broken-self premise rather than on shame's general claim of defectiveness. The shame variant asks the shame to deliver content; the fix-premise variant asks the fix-attempt to identify its target. Both fail. The failure is the work in both cases.

When the person tries to escape into a substitute target ("when I caretake others in this way..."), Joe holds the frame. "Hold on, you've said the main thing I'm trying to do is fix myself. So what are you trying to fix? What's wrong with you?" The repetition is part of the move; the person will offer the substitute target several times before producing the actual material that the fix-pattern is operating on.

When the actual material is delivered, the descent continues from there. In the source session, the actual material was "my tendency to judge versus to be discerning." The judgment was the next layer, and the session moved into the [[im-judgmental-how-do-i-stop]] descent.

This variant is also the canonical opening move for the [[why-am-i-always-trying-to-fix]] presenting concern. The session needs to open by refusing the fix-frame; this move does that in three turns.

See [[dont-presume-the-person-is-broken]] for the broader anti-pattern this variant operates inside. The move is the live-coaching expression of the coach-stance that the person is not broken.

## The No-Answer-Required Instruction

There is a critical variant that pairs with the question: explicitly instruct the person that they do not need to produce an answer. Just keep going to the place the question points at.

"What's wrong with you? What is it that we're going to find out when we see you that's shameful? You don't need to find an answer, just keep going there for a minute." -- Joe Hudson, *Social Anxiety Breakthrough*

This variant is essential when the person's reflex is to surface and answer. The original "tell me what the fuck is wrong with you" demands the content because the person has been hiding from the content. The "you don't need to find an answer, just keep going there" releases the content-requirement because the person has been *trying* to produce content and the trying is what keeps them out of the felt place. Both variants are the same move at different points in the descent: one demands the answer to break the story; the other releases the answer-requirement to let the descent continue. See [[interrupting-your-own-descent]] for the read that triggers the no-answer-required variant.

The trick: the question stays the same. What changes is what the question is asking the person to do with it. The first variant is "answer this." The second variant is "stay with this." Joe reads which one is needed in the moment and delivers accordingly.

## The Three-Step Texture

The move has a recognizable rhythm in the [[comedian-to-coach-money-shame-session|comedian-to-coach session]]:

1. **State what shame is claiming.** "Shame means that there's something wrong with you."
2. **Demand the content.** "So tell me what's wrong with you."
3. **Refuse the story when it starts.** "I don't care about the story. I want to know what the fuck is wrong with you."

The third move is essential. The first attempt to answer almost always produces a story ("Well, the story right now is..."). The story is not the answer. The story is the cover. Refusing the story leaves the person with no choice but to feel into the question directly.

The profanity in the third instruction is calibrated. The bluntness signals that the question is real, not therapeutic, and that the coach genuinely wants the answer. It also removes the option of treating the question as a sophisticated coaching prompt to be answered cleverly.

## What It Produces

When the person finally feels into the question instead of thinking about it, the answer is almost always: nothing.

"I, nothing's wrong with me, you know? I, yeah, nothing's wrong with me." — Guest, *I Have To Do What Others Want*

The contrast immediately surfaces:

"Yeah, but when you think about it —"
"Yeah, when I think about it, I mean, I could write a fucking book on it, right?"
"Exactly."

The shame lives in the thinking, not in the body. The thinking can produce a book of content. The body produces nothing. The disparity is the point. The question makes the disparity visible.

This is also the route to the related principle: see [[shame-dissipates-in-the-question-itself]]. Asking shame to make its claim plain causes the claim to dissolve. Shame requires vagueness. Specificity kills it.

## Why It Works

Shame's structure is generalized self-condemnation. "Something is wrong with me" is an unfalsifiable, content-free claim. The person carrying the shame has never been asked to specify what the something is, because the conversation around shame is usually about the feeling of shame, not about its content.

The move treats the shame as if its claim were honest. If something is genuinely wrong, fine — name it. If nothing specific can be named, the claim collapses on inspection.

The move also separates the head from the body in real time. The person discovers they have two different answers to the same question depending on which faculty they use. The head says "I could write a book." The body says "nothing." Both reports come from the same person, in the same minute. The simultaneity reveals that the shame is operating in only one of them.

The work is not to argue with the shame. The work is to demand its content. The shame argues with itself when invited to speak.

## Tone

Direct, blunt, slightly provocative. The energy is not gentle. The bluntness is part of what makes the move work — gentleness would let the person stay in the soft middle ground where shame thrives. The profanity is calibrated, not performative.

There is a kind of warmth underneath the bluntness. The coach is taking the person's claim seriously enough to demand it be made specific. The bluntness comes from caring about the answer, not from impatience with the person.

The tempo is fast. Joe does not let the person settle into the story-version of the answer. He cuts the story off, immediately, and redirects to the question.

## Dialogue Example

- **Guest:** "I have a lot of shame around money."
- **Joe:** "And, um, and shame around having too much of it or not enough of it, or both."
- **Guest:** "Both. Both."
- **Joe:** *(after a body-feeling instruction)* "So shame means that there's something wrong with you."
- **Guest:** "Because there is. Yeah. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "So tell me. Tell me what's wrong with you."
- **Guest:** "Um, well, I guess the story right now is like—"
- **Joe:** "I don't care about the story. I want to know what the fuck is wrong with you."
- **Guest:** *(after pause)* "What is wrong with you? I mean, when, when you ask, you know, when I feel into it, it's like, I, nothing's wrong with me, you know? I, yeah, nothing's wrong with me."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So when you feel into it, nothing's wrong with you."
- **Guest:** "When I like feel into the truth of it, nothing's wrong with me."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, but when you think about it —"
- **Guest:** "Yeah, when I think about it, I mean, I could write a fucking book on it, right?"
- **Joe:** "Exactly."
- *(Source: I Have To Do What Others Want To Make Money)*

The discovery in three turns: shame lives in the thinking, not in the felt truth. The move produced the discovery by demanding the content rather than discussing the feeling.

## What Joe Does Next

After the shame fails to deliver content, Joe usually pivots to a related object using the same method. If the shame was about money, now ask the same question about money:

"What's wrong with money? Don't think about it. You're thinking about it. When you feel into money, what's wrong with it?"

The same gap re-appears: head says a lot, body says nothing or something different. The pattern is named across multiple objects, which strengthens the recognition.

Then Joe often connects the wired-in shame to a childhood pattern. The shame did not arise from nothing; it was installed by a relational dynamic where wanting or showing oneself produced love-withdrawal. See [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]] and [[apply-the-childhood-pattern-to-the-present-problem]].

## What Joe Will Not Do

- He will not discuss the shame. The move is the opposite of holding space for the shame to be expressed. The shame is invited to speak its content, not its feelings about itself.
- He will not let the person justify or explain. Justifications and explanations are the story; the story is what the move refuses.
- He will not soften the question once it has been asked. The bluntness is the operative element.
- He will not move on until the contrast between thinking and feeling has been registered by the client.
