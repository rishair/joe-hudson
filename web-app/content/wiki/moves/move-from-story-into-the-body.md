# Move From the Story Into the Body

## The Move

When the person is narrating something that the work requires them to *do* somatically — a quality of love, a way of attending, a felt state — interrupt the narration with a direct body instruction. Not "let's talk about doing it." The instruction itself, delivered to the body, now, with explicit physical directives.

The form:

1. The person is narrating the felt material. The narration is accurate and even insightful. They are explaining the quality of love they had, the way they were attended to, the way the dead person nurtured them, the shape of the relationship.
2. Interrupt the narration. Name what you are about to do: "I'm gonna ask you to move away from the story right now."
3. Deliver the body instruction. Use concrete physical directives. "Actually just do it in your body." Then specify exactly what to do: "Make the love that you have for him non-personal. Have it be for everything and everyone. See the world the way he saw you. Attune to the world the way he attuned to you."

The instruction has to be specific enough that the body knows what to do. "Feel the love" is too abstract. "See the world the way he saw you" is precise. The body can do it. The narration was preventing the body from doing it.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the person is narrating something the work requires them to feel.
- When the narration is accurate and even useful but has been going on long enough to substitute for the experience.
- When the felt material is right there and the words are keeping it slightly out of reach.
- When the person has already discovered the conceptual answer and is now in danger of leaving the session with the concept rather than the body change.
- When a previous body instruction has been given and the person has migrated back into narration about it.
- When the read is [[mind-narrative-bypasses-felt-experience]] or [[abstract-framing-as-defense]] in its more sympathetic form (the narration is not defensive but it is preventing the body from doing the work).

In the source session, this move arrives near the end. The widow has been narrating how her husband taught her to love — how he was nurturing, how she had been more guarded before him, how she could now hear the sweetness in her own voice when she spoke to her dog. The narration is true. It is also keeping the love at the level of memory rather than letting the love be active right now. Joe interrupts and gives the body the instruction directly.

## Why It Works

Narration about a felt state is structurally different from the felt state. The same word — "love" — points to a concept when spoken about and to a sensation when done in the body. The two are not the same.

The body can be in narration mode or in feeling mode. It cannot be in both modes at the same depth simultaneously. The narration uses the same channels — attention, working memory, motor planning around speech — that the felt state needs to be felt. As long as the narration is running, the felt state has to share resources with it. The narration usually wins because the narration is what the person has been practiced at.

The interrupt frees the channels. The body instruction tells the freed channels what to do. The combination produces the felt state in real time. The session moves from "we talked about this" to "this happened in the room."

The move also signals to the person that the work is not what they thought it was. They thought the work was understanding. The instruction reveals that the work is feeling. The signal recalibrates the rest of the session and often the person's relationship to coaching going forward.

## The Specific Body Directives

The body instructions Joe uses in this move are not generic. They are specific physical directives that the body can actually attempt. From the source session:

- "Make the love that you have for him non-personal." — Operationally: the same feeling-of-love that is currently aimed at one specific person, no longer aimed at one specific person.
- "Have it be for everything and everyone." — The directional instruction. Not "love it generally" but "have the love be for everything." The aiming is what the body does.
- "See the world the way he saw you." — Use a known felt-quality (the way the dead person saw you) as the template. Apply that template to a new target (the world).
- "Attune to the world the way he attuned to you." — Same structure. The attunement is a body capacity. The body knows what it felt like to be attuned to. Direct that quality outward.

The pattern: take a felt-quality the body already knows from the relationship being narrated, then apply it to a new target with a specific directional instruction. The body has the material. The instruction tells the body what to do with the material.

This is more useful than "feel love for everything," which gives the body a goal but no operational route. The Joe-version uses the known felt-quality (love-the-way-he-loved-you) as the route.

## The "Now Try Both" Refinement

A characteristic follow-on once the body has touched the new state: ask the body to hold the new state and the previous state simultaneously. Joe in the source session:

"I'm gonna get a little weird with you. I want you to now try both. I want you to feel that love that you have for him like the conduit, and then feel the love that you have for the non-personal love."

The move asks the body to do two things at once. The personal love for the specific person. The non-personal love that the personal love is now the conduit for. Both, at the same time.

The "now try both" is structurally distinct from the initial move. The initial move shifts the body from one state to a different state. The "now try both" asks the body to hold both states together. The simultaneity is the integration.

The widow's report after the "now try both": "Painfully and wonderful at the same time."

The pain-and-wonder-together signature is the operational confirmation that the simultaneity has landed. If the body could only hold one of the two, the report would be cleaner — either "painful" or "wonderful." The both-at-once report shows that the body is doing what was asked.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session, the move in real time:

- **Widow** (narrating): "I got a, I got a pet right away because we wanted a dog... So I've been able to just continue giving and that love unconditionally to, yeah, the pet."
- **Joe** (interrupting the narration with the body instruction): "And so I'm gonna ask you to move away from the story right now and actually just Do it in your body. Make the love that you have for him non-personal. Have it be for everything and everyone. Oh, see the world the way he saw you. Attune to the world the way he attuned to you."
- **Widow** (now from the body, not from narration): "The feeling is like he taught me how to love. He was so nurturing. Yeah, sweet. I had a guarded, macho, like, can't show that level. I was never a mother. So yeah, but I was a mother to my little dog in the way that he was tender. When I say to him, oh, you want a treat? It's sort of in the tone that he would, yeah, love me. I could hear it in my voice. And so yeah, that's the gift."
- **Joe**: "That's the invitation. Yeah."
- *(Source: She Came Asking to Let Go, She Left with a Love That Doesn't End)*

The arc is short. The narration was about giving love to the dog. Joe redirects to the body with three specific instructions. The next thing the widow says is a felt report from the body — "the feeling is like he taught me how to love" — rather than a continued narration about it. The report is past the surface story she had been telling and into the felt material the story was made of.

Then Joe runs the "try both" move (not shown in this dialogue excerpt but immediately following), and the widow lands the simultaneity: "Painfully and wonderful at the same time."

## What Joe Does NOT Do

He does not invalidate the narration. He does not say "you're in your story." He does not name the narration as a defense or as avoidance. The narration has been useful. It established what the love is. It built the context for the body instruction. The interrupt is not a correction. It is a transition.

He does not over-explain the body instruction. The instruction itself is specific and the body knows what to do. Explaining it more would extend the verbal layer the move is trying to step away from.

He does not check in immediately after the instruction. He gives the body time. If the body produces a report, he listens to the report. If the body produces silence, the silence is data.

He does not stop at one body instruction. The move usually includes 2-4 specific directives delivered in close sequence. The body needs multiple anchors. A single instruction is often not enough to fully shift modes. Three instructions in sequence is the more reliable form.

## Tone

The interrupt is gentle but clear. Joe names that he is about to do something different ("I'm gonna ask you to move away from the story right now") before doing it. The naming is care for the person — they know the shift is happening and are not surprised.

The body instructions are delivered with a slight slowing of pace. Not solemn. Just slower than the conversational pace. The slowing gives the body time to receive each instruction before the next one arrives.

When the body produces its report, the tone softens into receptivity. Joe does not jump in with the next move immediately. He lets the report be received. In the source session, his response to the widow's "that's the gift" report is the simple "That's the invitation. Yeah." Two short sentences. The first names what just happened. The second is a confirmation that asks for nothing more.

The "I'm gonna get a little weird with you" framing for the "try both" follow-on lowers the stakes. The phrasing tells the body it doesn't have to get it right. The lowered stakes are what allow the body to actually try the harder move.

## What Distinguishes This Move From Adjacent Moves

- This is not [[challenge-the-premise-in-the-framing]]. That move challenges a framing word in the opening of a session. This move shifts modes in the middle of a session, usually after significant material has been established.
- This is not [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]]. That move comes after a shift has happened and asks the body to anchor what just happened. This move is what produces the shift in the first place.
- This is not "let's do a somatic exercise." There is no exercise. The instruction is to do the felt thing directly, right now, in the conversation, while the conversation continues. The session is the practice ground.
- This is not the broader [[experiential-before-intellectual]] principle, though it is one operational form of it. The principle is the general orientation; this move is one specific way the orientation becomes a coaching action.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the person is narrating felt material with enough articulation that the narration is now substituting for the experience.
- When the teaching has been delivered conceptually and the work now needs to land in the body.
- When the conversation has reached the moment where the body can do the thing and the words are in the way.
- When the read is that the narration is genuine but is preventing the somatic shift.

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward [[let-your-body-remember-this-moment]] once the body has produced the felt state.
- Toward the "now try both" refinement when the work calls for simultaneity rather than a single state change.
- Toward silence — the body, having done the thing, often needs a few seconds to integrate. Joe does not fill the silence.
- Toward a short confirmation ("That's the invitation. Yeah.") that names what happened and stops.
