# Listen to the Objections Before Making the Decision

## The Move

Before making a significant decision about a team, a person, or an org that you are not directly part of, listen first to the people who will be affected. Hear their objections. Hear their knowledge of the ground. Then make the decision.

The move is not consensus-seeking. You can make the same decision you were already going to make. The point is not to be talked out of the decision. The point is that the decision lands differently when the people affected by it have been heard first, even if no input changed the outcome.

"If you just give a moment to hear the objections and make the same decision, people will understand the decision you make much better than if you don't listen at all." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

## When to Use It

The move applies whenever a decision will affect people who were not in the room when the decision was framed. The most common cases:

- Restructuring an org or team without first hearing from the people in it
- Making layoff decisions about a team without consulting the team's manager
- Setting policy that will land on people who haven't been asked
- Making technical or operational decisions where the people closest to the work weren't consulted
- Choosing a direction for a function that the function's own leaders haven't been part of

The principle: if your decision is going to affect them, your decision is improved (in its landing, even if not in its content) by having heard them.

## What People Get From Being Heard

Two distinct things people get from the listening that they cannot get from the decision itself:

**Information that the decision is not arbitrary.** When the leader has heard the objections, the decision is in dialogue with reality, not imposed from outside reality. Even if the decision is the same, the relationship the decision has to the actual situation is visibly different.

**Recognition that they are part of the organization.** Being asked is a structural form of recognition. The person who gets consulted is being treated as someone who matters; the person who gets handed a decision is being treated as someone who is acted upon. The recognition itself is what the listening produces, separately from the content.

This connects to [[all-disagreements-come-from-two-questions]] -- specifically, to the second of the two underlying questions ("how much do you love me?"). The listening directly answers that question in the affirmative. The non-listening directly answers it in the negative, regardless of the content of the decision.

## Why Leaders Often Skip This

The most common reason leaders skip the consultation is not arrogance. It is emotional avoidance. If you talk to the team first about a layoff, you will have hard conversations. You will hear objections you cannot fully refute. You may face anger, grief, or pleading. The path of "decide first, announce second" avoids the discomfort of being in the room with people's reaction to information that is still movable.

"I think in a lot of cases, people are afraid of actually doing that. Like if you're about to lay off somebody in your team and you talk to the team first to gather like advice and information, then you might have very challenging conversations and make even harder decisions. So sometimes it's even easier just to make a decision that you don't have the context for because getting the context would evoke emotions that are not comfortable." -- Brett Kistler, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

Joe's response: it's easier in the same way it's easier not to potty train a dog. The short-term ease produces long-term mess. See [[dont-skip-the-consultation-because-it-evokes-emotion]].

## The Common Anti-Pattern

The default in many large organizations: significant decisions about a team or division get made by people who have never spoken to the people in that team. The decision-makers can sit around a table and map out everything, believing they know what is best. The fact of not having listened is invisible from inside the decision-making room. The impact of not having listened is highly visible from inside the team being decided about.

"Most companies, they'll do this thing where they say, oh, we're gonna make significant decisions about you without talking to you about your org without talking to you because we can sit here around the table and map out everything and we know what's best. We're not gonna actually take a listen to the people on the ground and get their knowledge before we make the decision." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The team that has been decided about without consultation reacts predictably: "they didn't even ask me." The reaction is not primarily about the decision itself. It is about the structural fact of being decided about. This same reaction pattern then propagates down levels: regional managers feel about corporate the way the team felt about them.

## What the Move Does Not Require

**Not consensus.** You can hear the objections and still make the decision you were going to make. The move does not give the consulted party a veto.

**Not pretense.** Don't ask if you're not actually going to listen. Performative consultation is worse than no consultation -- it adds an experience of being unheard on top of the experience of being acted upon. If you cannot actually be moved by what you hear, do not ask.

**Not infinite listening.** The consultation has a scope. Hear the objections, understand the situation, then decide. The move is not "delay the decision indefinitely."

**Not lateral inclusion of everyone.** The people consulted are usually the people most directly affected -- the manager whose team is being restructured, the function leader whose function is being changed. Not "everyone in the company" for every decision.

## How This Differs From Asking for Help

The move is related to but distinct from [[ask-for-help-with-specifics]]. Asking for help is about getting input on a decision you don't yet know how to make. Listening to objections is about hearing reality before finalizing a decision you have already started to form. The first is open-ended; the second is bounded.

Both share the underlying assumption: the people on the ground have information the people at the table do not. Listening surfaces that information. Not listening loses it.

## Dialogue Example (Compressed)

Joe's description of a similar move in a coaching moment with a team:

- **Joe:** I can't trust this team to actually do the work that you all are saying you're gonna do. I would like to address that before we continue.
- **Joe (going around the room):** What made you do it late? What made you justify what was going on?
- **Each team member:** [describes what happened from their side]
- **Joe (in response to each):** How does that happen in the whole company? How do we structurally change that?
- *(Later in the same meeting)*
- **Team member:** Well, not all meetings can be 5-star, you know, like what if you have to really give people a talking to?
- **Joe:** Well, I just gave you a talking to an hour and a half ago. How was that?
- **Team:** *(laughter -- the calling out had been productive)*
- *(Source: The Secret to Thriving Company Culture)*

The listening produced the structural change. The structural change came from hearing each person before designing the response. The talking-to was not avoided; it was made productive by the listening that preceded it.

## What This Move Produces

When done well:

- The decision lands cleanly even when it is unwelcome
- The team feels respected even when they disagreed with the call
- The decision often gets better, because the listening surfaces information the decision-maker did not have
- Trust in the leader's decisions increases over time, because each decision has a known relationship to reality
- The team's own decision-making capacity develops, because they see what the consultation process looks like

When skipped:

- Even good decisions land badly
- People disengage from decisions they did not get to be part of
- Trust erodes over time as people accumulate the experience of being acted upon
- Politics expands to fill the gap, because the formal channel for input is closed

## Tone

Direct and matter-of-fact. Joe describes this not as a soft skill but as a structural observation about how decisions actually work in organizations. The leader who skips this is not being efficient; they are losing the leverage the consultation would have given them. The investment of time in listening is small relative to the cost of unconsulted decisions over time.
