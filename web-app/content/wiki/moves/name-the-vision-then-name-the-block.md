# Name the Vision, Name the Help, Name the Block

## The Move

When facing an angry person whose anger is in service of something they care about (a vision, a goal, an outcome), the move has four parts in sequence:

**1. Name what they are reaching for.** "I see that what you're pointing at is really important. I see your vision." This is the empathic move. You are receiving the underlying transmission. The yelling was the form; the underlying message was "this matters." You acknowledge the message.

**2. Name your wanting to help them achieve it.** "I see how important it is for us to listen to you. And for us to help you get that vision across." You are aligning with what they are trying to do. You are not telling them to fix themselves. You are not making the conversation about their behavior at the expense of their goal.

**3. Name the specific obstacle their behavior creates.** "However, when you yell at us, it makes it harder for us to help you." Not "stop yelling because it's wrong." Not "you're being abusive." A simple causal statement: this behavior interferes with the outcome you want.

**4. Make the ask in their terms.** "So if you could please not yell at us so that we can be more effective." The request is framed in service of their goal. The benefit is theirs.

This is the exact move the executive used with the famous Silicon Valley narcissist in [[the-silicon-valley-narcissist-who-stopped-yelling]]. He stopped yelling at her team. He yelled at the other teams. Within a year he agreed to join her board.

## What Each Part Does

**Naming the vision dissolves the wrongness loop.** The angry person is, underneath the anger, scared that what they care about will not be taken seriously. Naming the vision is the proof that it has been taken seriously. The yelling stops being necessary because the function it was serving has been performed by the receiver.

**Naming the wanting to help dissolves the antagonism frame.** Without this step, the receiver is positioned as resisting the angry person's vision. With this step, the receiver is positioned as on the same team. The conversation reframes from adversarial to collaborative. The angry person no longer has to fight to be heard.

**Naming the block makes the request structural rather than moral.** "Yelling is wrong" invites debate about whether it's wrong. "Yelling makes it harder for us to help you" is a description of cause and effect that doesn't need defending. The angry person can disagree with the causation but cannot easily attack the framing.

**Making the ask in their terms removes the implication of submission.** "Stop yelling at me" puts the receiver in the role of asking for protection. "So that we can be more effective" puts the receiver in the role of optimizing for the angry person's success. The angry person is being served, not opposed.

## What Makes the Move Work

The move works because the underlying state of the speaker is genuinely on the angry person's side. The receiver actually does see the vision; the receiver actually does want to help; the receiver actually does want the angry person to succeed. The move is not a technique. It is the operational form of an underlying state of partnership.

A speaker who delivers these words while internally bracing, scared, or counter-attacking will have their internal state read by the angry person. The words become a script that does not land. The angry person feels the disjunction and the dynamic escalates.

The prerequisite for this move is that the receiver has done enough of their own anger work that they can hear the angry person's underlying need without being thrown by the form. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the practice and [[anger-is-an-act-of-vulnerability]] for the reframe that makes the receiving possible.

## When to Use It

- When the angry person has a legitimate goal underneath the anger (a vision, a project, a deliverable) and the anger is in service of that goal.
- When you are in a position to actually help with what they care about. The move is dishonest if you have no intention of helping.
- When you have the capacity to deliver it from a non-scared place. Without the underlying state, the move fails.
- When you want to preserve and possibly deepen the working relationship.
- When repeated boundary-setting alone has not changed the pattern.
- When you can see, behind the yelling, what they are actually after.

## When Not to Use It

- When the angry person's "vision" is harming people and you do not actually want to help them achieve it.
- When the anger is physical abuse or escalating toward physical violence. Leave first.
- When the angry person is so far on the narcissistic spectrum that even named empathy gets exploited. See [[contain-the-narcissist]] — sometimes the boundary alone is the move.
- When you do not have the capacity to deliver it from a centered state. Use [[come-back-in-20-minutes]] instead.
- When the angry person is yelling for catharsis rather than communication. Welcoming the emotion may be more appropriate than naming the vision; see [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]].
- When you do not actually see the vision they are reaching for. Faking the empathy makes the move fail.

## What Distinguishes This from Flattery or Manipulation

The move can look from outside like managing the angry person. The difference is in what is being received and what is being asked.

**Flattery** receives nothing real. It says nice things about the angry person to soften them. The receiver does not have to mean any of it. The function is to make the angry person feel good so that they will be easier to handle.

**Manipulation** uses the appearance of empathy to extract a behavior. The receiver pretends to see the vision in order to get the angry person to stop yelling. The empathy is performative.

**This move** receives what is actually there (the underlying care) and asks for what is actually needed (the change in delivery). Both halves are real. The empathy is real because the underlying care actually exists. The request is real because the behavior actually is blocking effectiveness.

The diagnostic: would you say this if the angry person were behaving normally? If yes (you really do see their vision; you really do want to help), the move is clean. If no (you only mention the vision to soften them), the move is manipulation.

## What This Move Trains in the Angry Person

Used over time, the move trains the angry person that they get more of what they want by not yelling. The receiver-of-anger has reorganized the incentive structure. Yelling produces the leaving (via [[come-back-in-20-minutes]]); not-yelling produces real partnership on the goals they care about. The angry person's nervous system learns the new map.

This is also what produces the relationship deepening that often follows the move. The angry person, having found someone who can receive the underlying transmission, often gravitates toward that person. They have, for the first time, found someone who is not just managing them or running from them. The Silicon Valley narcissist agreeing to join her board after a year is the canonical example. The relationship moved from adversarial to partnership because the receiver could hold both the vision and the limit.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not a script. The words can be adjusted to fit the situation. The structural elements (name the vision, name the wanting to help, name the block, ask in their terms) are what matter.

This is not a tool for getting someone to do what you want. The move only works if you are actually on their side. A version aimed at controlling the angry person rather than aligning with them will fail.

This is not the only move available with an angry person. [[come-back-in-20-minutes]] is the basic boundary that works without this level of capacity. [[welcoming-the-others-emotion]] is the move for when the anger is more about emotional discharge than about a specific goal. This move is for the specific shape where there is a real goal underneath the anger that you can actually help with.

This is not [[view-as-technique]]. The empathy in this move is real; it is not deployed as a strategy to produce a result. The result follows from the realness of the empathy. If you make the empathy strategic, you have lost the state and the move fails.

## Dialogue Examples

A worked example from [[the-silicon-valley-narcissist-who-stopped-yelling]]:

- **Executive:** Hey, I see that what you're pointing at is really important. I see your vision, and I see how important it is for us to listen to you. And for us to help you get that vision across. However, when you yell at us, it makes it harder for us to help you. So if you could please not yell at us so that we can be more effective, I'd appreciate it.

The four parts are present in compressed form. The vision is named ("what you're pointing at is really important; I see your vision"). The wanting to help is named ("how important it is for us to listen; for us to help you get that vision across"). The block is named ("when you yell at us, it makes it harder for us to help you"). The ask is in his terms ("so that we can be more effective").

A workplace adaptation:

- **You:** I can see this project matters a lot to you and I really want to help us get it right. When the meetings get heated, I can't track what you actually need from me. If we can keep the energy lower in the room, I think I can give you more of what you're actually after.

A relationship adaptation:

- **You:** I see how much you want us to be close. I want that too. When you start yelling, my body shuts down and I can't be close to you the way we both want. If we can find a different way to do this, I think we can actually get there.

The variations preserve the four-part structure. The specific situations change the language.

## Tone

The delivery has to be matter-of-fact and warm. Not pleading. Not righteous. Not apologizing. The body has to be upright, soft, undefended. The voice has to be steady. If any of these are off, the angry person reads the bracing and the move fails.

The closing "I'd appreciate it" in the canonical example is part of the tone. The request is direct but not demanding. The relational positioning is "we are partners on this" not "I am asking you to stop being bad."
