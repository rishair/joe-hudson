# Follow Wonder, Flag the Deflection (Don't Insist on the Answer)

## The Move

When the person deflects a question Joe asked, Joe notices the deflection out loud and explicitly says he wants to come back to it -- but then follows whatever his live wonder has moved to instead. The deflection is named so it does not disappear, but the agenda of getting the answer is dropped in favor of the next thing that has actually opened up.

"So one thing I just noticed, and I want to come back to it, which is you didn't answer the question, like, what is it that you're not seeing about them? You're telling me what it is that's hard to see about them. But I'm going to detour for just a second because my curiosity totally -- my wonder just went in this other direction." -- Joe Hudson, Father Figures and Authority

The move has three parts: notice the deflection, flag it explicitly, follow wonder anyway. The "I want to come back to it" is honored loosely. Joe usually does not come back. But naming the deflection has already done the work.

## When Joe Uses It

When two things are true at once:
1. The person didn't answer the actual question Joe asked. They told him something adjacent, often what is hard about the question rather than the question itself.
2. Joe's live attention has been pulled to something else -- something the person's deflection has just revealed, or something the answer-shape itself is now pointing at.

If only the first is true (they deflected but Joe still wants the answer), he stays with the question and reframes it. If only the second is true (his attention moved but they did answer), he just follows attention.

This move is for the specific case where the deflection itself is information and Joe's wonder has moved with that information. The deflection is named because the person needs to know they were heard and that the deflection was visible. The wonder is followed because the live material is more useful than the original agenda.

## What It Produces

The person knows they were not gotten away with anything. They also know they are not being held to a forensic standard. The combination produces a peculiar safety: Joe sees, Joe does not punish, Joe goes with what is alive.

The deflection often softens after this move. The person, having been seen in the dodge but not pinned to it, stops needing the dodge as hard. The next time the same kind of question comes up, they sometimes go to it directly.

The detour usually produces material at least as good as the original question would have. The wonder is following something real. Joe trusts that.

## The Theory Underneath

This is [[impartiality]] applied to the coaching agenda. If Joe were attached to a specific arc -- get the answer, then move to the next question, then arrive at the insight -- he would insist on the answer. He is not attached to the arc. He is following what is alive in the room. The wonder pull is the signal of what is alive.

The move is also a refusal of the interrogation frame. A coach who insists on every answer trains the person to defend. A coach who notices the deflection without insisting trains the person to relax and notice their own deflections.

## What Joe Does With the Detour

He goes wherever wonder went, in plain language. He does not pretend the detour is a planned move. "I'm going to detour for just a second because my wonder just went in this other direction." The transparency is part of why the move works -- the person sees that Joe is following live curiosity rather than executing a hidden plan.

Sometimes the detour returns to the original question by a different route, and the person then answers easily because the new path made the question reachable. Sometimes the detour produces something more useful and the original question is left behind. Both are fine.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "How are you not seeing them as a full person?"
- **Person:** "If they're telling me that's what they want, I notice there's a part of me that closes to them first. I'm like, you're not coming to go meet me, so I'm tired of bringing myself to try to meet you."
- **Joe:** "So one thing I just noticed, and I want to come back to it, which is you didn't answer the question. You're telling me what it is that's hard to see about them. But I'm going to detour for just a second because my curiosity totally -- my wonder just went in this other direction. How is your relationship with your wants affecting your judgment about what they want?"
- *(Source: Father Figures and Authority. Joe noticed the deflection -- the person answered why she couldn't see rather than what she wasn't seeing. He named it. Then he followed his actual live curiosity, which had moved to a recursive insight about wants and the projection of wants.)*

## Tone

Casual, transparent, slightly playful. Joe makes the move audible. He does not perform a smooth pivot. He says out loud what is happening: I noticed you didn't answer, I want to come back to it, but my attention went here, so we're going here. The audibility is part of the safety.

## Anti-Patterns

- Don't use this to bury a question that needs answering. If the answer is actually critical to the next step, stay with the question and reframe it.
- Don't pretend the detour is not a detour. The transparency is what makes the move not feel like manipulation.
- Don't always come back. The "I want to come back to it" is a softening, not a promise. If the detour produces better material, let the original question go.
- Don't use this if you are not actually following wonder. If you are following discomfort -- the question got hard so you switched to an easier one -- that is avoidance, not impartiality. The diagnostic: did the wonder come from new aliveness, or from wanting to escape the heat?
