# Offer Yourself as the Bad Boss They Installed

## The Move

When someone is procrastinating on their own work — the side project, the app, the side hustle, anything they would do for themselves — and they have not yet seen that they are the worst boss they have ever worked for, externalize the inner-boss behavior by offering yourself as a hypothetical hire. Pose a job interview. The job is: be the coach (or the boss, or the trainer) who acts exactly the way the client acts toward themselves.

The client cannot defend the standards they have been imposing on themselves once they hear those standards offered to them as terms of employment.

> "Okay, check it out. You're gonna hire me as a coach, right? You're like, Joe, I'm thinking about hiring you as a coach. I'm like, cool. Here's the thing is I'm gonna always feel like I'm not spending enough time with you. I'm never gonna spend enough time with you. And if I don't make you absolutely perfect on the first attempt, I'm gonna think I'm a bad coach. Wanna hire me?" -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The client's immediate response in the canonical session: "All right, makes sense." The recognition lands before any further commentary.

## The Second Inversion

Joe often doubles the move by flipping the direction of the contract. First: would you hire me as a coach who treats you this way? Then: would you want to work for me as my employee while I treat you this way?

> "Also, do you want to work for me? Do I want to work for you? Like, you're like, if I was a programmer and you were like, hey, you, I want to hire you. Here's the thing is I'm always going to tell you, you're not doing enough. And if you don't get it absolutely perfect on the first attempt, I'm going to say that you failed." -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The double inversion makes the structural absurdity visible from both sides of the employment relationship. The person would never accept these terms as employer or as employee. Yet they have been running both sides of the contract on themselves continuously, often for years.

The client's response, again: "Yeah, that would be an awesome job, I guess." (Said dryly. The recognition is the dryness.)

The compression that lands: **"Exactly. So if you're not doing it, that's why. You're a shitty boss."**

## Why This Move Works

The internal critic is hard to see precisely because it is internal. It speaks in the first person, in the person's own voice, with all the credibility of a thought they thought themselves. The person experiences the critic's standards as their own ambition, their own high standards, their own discipline.

Once the same standards are presented as the terms of an interpersonal employment relationship, the disguise drops. Standards that sound like ambition when said in one's own head sound like abuse when offered as a job description. Nobody would hire a coach who said "I will always feel you are not doing enough; if you are not perfect on the first try I will conclude you failed." Nobody would work for that boss.

The move externalizes the inner boss into a relational form the client can evaluate as if it were happening to someone else. The evaluation happens automatically. The recognition arrives without the coach having to argue for it.

This is the relational form of [[imagine-doing-it-without-self-abuse]]. That move asks "if you couldn't be hard on yourself about this, how much would still be happening?" — a private thought experiment. This move asks the same question in the interpersonal register: "if I treated you the way you treat you, would you sign the contract?" The interpersonal frame is often easier to evaluate than the private one because the client has more practice judging external relationships than internal ones.

## When to Use This Move

- The person works for themselves (founder, freelancer, side-hustler, artist, anyone who would do the work they're not doing without an external boss).
- The person has been chronically procrastinating, paralyzed, or under-engaging with their own work.
- [[the-perfectionism-that-stops-you-from-starting]] has surfaced as the read, especially in the bounded form (works fine for others, can't work for self).
- The standards the person has installed are visible to the coach but invisible to the person.
- The person has named one or more of the standards explicitly ("I never spend enough time," "it has to be perfect on the first try," "if I fail once that proves I can't").
- Softer interventions have not produced the felt recognition that the standards are unworkable.

Use this move after the standards are on the table. The move needs material to work with. The client has to be able to recognize their own standards in the job description Joe offers. If the standards are still implicit, surface them first ([[prove-why-its-important]] works for this; so does the felt comparison between how the work feels for self vs. for others).

## How to Deliver

The delivery is structured as a small piece of role-play that the client does not have to perform. Joe does the performing. He sets up the scenario ("you're gonna hire me as a coach"), then voices the terms ("here's the thing — I'm gonna always feel like I'm not spending enough time"), then asks the question that closes the loop ("wanna hire me?").

Two stylistic notes:

1. **Voice the terms in Joe's own first person.** Not "imagine a coach who would say..." but "I'm gonna always feel like..." The first-person delivery is what makes the terms land as if they were really being offered. It also models the inner critic's first-person voice, which is what made the standards invisible in the first place.

2. **Use the client's own articulated standards.** Joe takes the standards the client has just named — "I never spend enough time," "it has to be perfect on the first attempt" — and offers those exact standards as the job description. The recognition is hard for the client to dodge because the standards are their own.

The question "wanna hire me?" is delivered casually, almost as a real question. The casualness is part of the move. A heavy delivery would tip the client off that they are supposed to say no. The casual delivery lets the absurdity land naturally.

## What This Move Produces

The most common response is a slightly stunned recognition. The client sees, for the first time, that the standards they have been operating under for years are standards no reasonable person would agree to in any other context.

The recognition is usually followed by a question Joe is ready for: "What kind of boss do you want to be?" The question is the bridge from the recognition to the redirect. See the next section.

## The Follow-Up Question: What Kind of Boss Do You Want to Be?

Once the bad-boss recognition has landed, the move opens onto a question that invites the new role:

> "What kind of boss do you want to be?" -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The question is not rhetorical. It invites the client to actually articulate the kind of boss they would want — for themselves. The articulation tends to land naturally in a softer register: "one that kind of lets things develop at the natural pace, but still kind of reaches goals and still is ambitious." The new boss has standards, but the standards are not abusive. The new boss is patient without being lazy. The articulation is itself the design of the new internal relationship.

The question also pivots from diagnosis to design. The move so far has made the bad boss visible. The follow-up question moves from "look what you've been doing" to "what would you actually want instead?" — which keeps the work generative rather than confessional.

## The Final Move: I'll Be Your Boss for a Minute

After the bad-boss recognition and the what-kind-of-boss-do-you-want question, Joe can offer himself as a brief good-boss to model the new contract. The frame is explicit: "I'm gonna be your boss for a minute. Here's what I'm gonna tell you to do."

The instruction in the canonical session:

> "I'm gonna tell you your job is to work on it 1 hour a day. I don't give a fuck if you accomplish anything for that 1 hour. The only thing I care about is that you enjoy yourself. That you work on the project and you enjoy yourself. That's the only thing I care about." -- Joe Hudson, My body won't let me build what my brain knows I can

The instruction is the explicit good-boss contract: bounded time (1 hour), no accomplishment requirement, enjoyment as the only success criterion. The casual profanity ("I don't give a fuck") signals that the relaxation of the standards is not a soft platitude — it is operationally serious. The boss really does not care about the accomplishment. The boss only cares about the enjoyment.

The follow-up question: "You wanna work for me?" The answer is almost always "Sounds awesome." Followed by: "How effective are you gonna be?" The answer is almost always "Probably pretty effective." The point lands without needing to be argued: the good boss produces more than the bad boss. The good boss produces more because the good boss does not extract energy through pressure. The good boss makes the work doable.

This sequence — bad-boss hire offer → what-kind-of-boss-do-you-want → I'll-be-your-good-boss-for-a-minute → would-you-work-for-me → how-effective-would-you-be — is the full arc of the move. Each step uses the relational frame to bypass the defenses the inner critic has built around its own legitimacy.

## What This Move Is Not

It is not a parent-figure role-play (see [[become-the-authority-figure]]). The authority figure being externalized is not a parent from childhood. It is the inner boss the client has installed in themselves, often by inheriting one parent's standards, often constructed from internalized cultural messages, often just the cost of overachievement.

It is not a reverse role-play (see [[coach-demonstrates-via-reversed-role-play]]). That move asks the client to play the trigger and the coach to play the new self. This move asks neither party to play anything — Joe simply offers himself, in his own voice, as the kind of coach the client has been being for themselves.

It is not satirical anti-advice (see [[satirical-anti-advice-as-teaching]]). That format is a one-to-many teaching delivered as instructions for the broken pattern. This move is a one-on-one interpersonal scenario that uses the client's specific articulated standards.

It is not an instruction. The move does not say "be a better boss to yourself." It surfaces the recognition that the client has been a bad boss; the redirect to enjoyment follows but is a separate move. See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] and [[be-a-good-boss-to-yourself]].

## Dialogue Example

- **Client:** "I'm never spending enough time."
- **Joe:** "And you're never spending enough time. So not only are you going to fail, you're currently failing. Like you're ensuring failure. Okay, check it out. You're gonna hire me as a coach, right? You're like, Joe, I'm thinking about hiring you as a coach. I'm like, cool. Here's the thing is I'm gonna always feel like I'm not spending enough time with you. I'm never gonna spend enough time with you. And if I don't make you absolutely perfect on the first attempt, I'm gonna think I'm a bad coach. Wanna hire me?"
- **Client:** "All right, makes sense."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, yeah. Also, do you want to work for me? Do I want to work for you? Like, you're like, if I was a programmer and you were like, hey, you, I want to hire you. Here's the thing is I'm always going to tell you, you're not doing enough. And if you don't get it absolutely perfect on the first attempt, I'm going to say that you failed."
- **Client:** "Yeah, that would be an awesome job, I guess."
- **Joe:** "Exactly. So if you're not doing it, that's why. You're a shitty boss."
- **Client:** "Yeah, I guess so."
- **Joe:** "What kind of boss do you want to be?"

The whole exchange runs about ninety seconds. The move is short because the recognition does not require length — it requires the precise externalization of the client's specific standards into the interpersonal frame.

## Tone

Casual, slightly playful, matter-of-fact. The move lives or dies on the casualness of the delivery. Joe is not pretending to be a stern figure giving the client a lesson; he is offering a hypothetical job interview as if it were a real consideration. The lightness lets the absurdity of the standards land as absurdity rather than as confrontation.

There is humor underneath ("Yeah, that would be an awesome job, I guess") — and the humor is part of the medicine. The recognition that the client has been signing an unconscionable contract with themselves is funny once it is named, and the humor releases the gravity the client has been carrying about why they cannot just push harder.

When the move opens to "what kind of boss do you want to be?" the tone shifts to genuinely curious — Joe is actually asking. The client is being invited to design the new contract.
