# Imagine Doing It Without Self-Abuse

## The Move

A short, operational thought experiment that surfaces the role self-abuse is playing in the person's relationship with procrastination. Ask them to imagine doing the same task — the one they have been telling themselves they are procrastinating on — without any internal self-attack about it. No "I should have done this." No "what's wrong with me." No "I have to be better at this." Just the task and the person.

The question:

> "If you couldn't be self-abusive, if you couldn't be hard on yourself, if you couldn't shame yourself, if you couldn't ride yourself like a shitty ass boss, how much procrastination would happen? Just generally, how much would you be procrastinating?" -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

When the question is asked seriously, most people pause. The pause is the move working. They are searching for what the experience would be without the layer they have always assumed was necessary. Often they discover that without the self-abuse the experience of "procrastinating" would not exist at all. What would remain would be either ordinary prioritization (the task happens later, no problem) or wisdom (the not-doing has something specific to tell them about timing, method, or emotion).

## Why This Move Works

The move works because it separates two things that have been fused in the person's mind: the not-doing and the suffering about the not-doing. The person has been treating these as one thing called "procrastination." The thought experiment forces the separation. With the suffering removed, what is the not-doing? Is it actually a problem? Is the work to change the behavior or to drop the suffering?

The answer is almost always informative. Either:

**The not-doing was prioritization all along.** The suffering was misidentified as evidence of a real problem. Without the suffering, there's no problem to solve. The work is to drop the self-attack, not to change the behavior. See [[prioritization-vs-procrastination]] and the principle at [[you-procrastinate-on-2-of-100-things]].

**The not-doing has wisdom.** Once the self-attack quiets, the part of them that won't do the thing becomes audible. The wisdom (wrong timing, wrong method, avoided emotion) becomes investigable. See [[procrastination-is-wisdom]] and [[ask-what-is-the-procrastination-telling-you]].

In both cases, the self-abuse was the engine of the felt procrastination, and removing it makes the actual underlying situation visible.

See [[procrastination-requires-self-abuse]] for the principle this move operationalizes.

## When to Use This Move

- When a coachee is presenting chronic procrastination as a self-evident problem
- When the conversation is heavy with self-attack about a task and the coach needs to break the frame
- When the bully voice is doing the dominant talking and the wisdom of the not-doing is inaudible
- When [[the-bully-passive-aggressive-loop]] is observable and the structural exit needs an entry point
- Early in a coaching conversation about procrastination, before any productivity-system or behavioral interventions
- When the person identifies as "a procrastinator" and the identity is the trap

## How to Deliver

The question is best asked simply and slowly. The pacing is important — the person needs time to actually try the thought experiment, not just to dismiss it as rhetorical.

Variations:

- "Let me ask a strange one: if you weren't allowed to beat yourself up about this task, how much of what you're calling 'procrastination' would still be happening?"
- "Imagine for a minute that you couldn't be hard on yourself about this. No 'I should have done this,' no 'what's wrong with me.' Just the task. What would your relationship with this task be?"
- "If you took out the part where you ride yourself like a shitty boss about this, what would be left?"
- "What would change if the self-attack just wasn't there?"

The tone is genuinely curious, not Socratic-with-a-known-answer. The coach is offering a thought experiment, not setting up a punchline. The honesty of the inquiry is what makes the answer land.

## What the Response Reveals

The response is diagnostic:

**If the person says "almost none of it":** the self-abuse was the engine. The work is on the relationship to the voice, not on the behavior. See [[procrastination-requires-self-abuse]] and [[menu-of-responses-to-the-self-critical-voice]].

**If the person says "I would still not be doing it but I would feel okay about it":** that is prioritization. The work is to drop the self-attack and let the behavior be what it is. The task is fine to delay; the suffering was misplaced. See [[prioritization-vs-procrastination]].

**If the person says "I would still be stuck but I'd want to figure out why":** that is the opening for procrastination-is-wisdom work. The self-attack was drowning out the listening. The next move is [[ask-what-is-the-procrastination-telling-you]].

**If the person resists the thought experiment ("but the self-attack is what motivates me"):** name the resistance directly. "I notice the self-attack feels necessary to you. What do you think would happen if it weren't there?" The resistance is its own diagnostic — the person is identified with the bully voice as the responsible part. See [[the-bully-passive-aggressive-loop]] and [[find-the-emotion-under-self-judgment]] for the deeper work.

**If the person performs an answer quickly and intellectually:** slow it down. "Sit with that for a second. Actually try to imagine the day without the self-attack about this. What does that feel like?" The bully voice often produces a quick tidy answer to dismiss the move. The embodied version takes a moment longer.

## What This Move Is Not

This is not "stop being hard on yourself" as a directive. That would just be another should. The move is a thought experiment that surfaces information, not an instruction to change.

This is also not "you don't really need to do that thing." The move does not eliminate the task. It separates the task from the suffering about the task, so that the task can be addressed cleanly (either by doing it, prioritizing it, dropping it, or investigating why it isn't happening).

This is not a one-time intervention. Once the move has landed, the structural lock surfaces, but the muscle of relating-without-self-abuse still needs development. The thought experiment is the diagnostic; the actual work on the voice unfolds over time. See [[menu-of-responses-to-the-self-critical-voice]] for the ongoing toolkit.

## The Move Beneath the Move

What this move actually does at a deeper level: it gives the person an experience of their own internal landscape without the self-attack layer. Even briefly imagining it produces a felt taste of what life would be like without the bully voice running. The taste is often the first time the person has noticed how much of their suffering was constructed by the voice rather than caused by external situations.

That taste becomes motivating. The person no longer believes the self-attack is just "how it is." They have felt — even in imagination — what is on the other side. The orientation toward dropping the self-attack becomes available because the alternative has become imaginable.

## Tone

Curious, slightly playful. Joe asks this question with a "huh, just consider this for a minute" energy. Not heavy, not therapeutic. The lightness is what makes the move land — a heavy delivery would re-invoke the seriousness that the move is trying to disrupt.
