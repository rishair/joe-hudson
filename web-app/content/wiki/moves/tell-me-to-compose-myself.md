# Tell Me to Compose Myself (Direct the Introject at the Coach to See It)

## The Move

Have the client speak the internalized command outward, directing it at the coach with eye contact, while the coach embodies the opposite of the commanded state.

The instruction is direct and slightly uncomfortable:
- "I want you to tell me to compose myself. Eyes open. Look at me when you do it."
- Pre-frame the contrast: "Tell me to compose myself because I don't know if you've noticed, but I'm like totally not that."

The client will resist. Their resistance is the point — having to direct the instruction outward, at someone visibly not in need of it, makes the internal application of the same instruction visible by contrast. The client is forced to feel what it is like to be the one issuing the command, which is a perspective they have never occupied consciously even though they have been issuing it to themselves constantly.

When the client says "I don't want to," the response is "I know, but just do it. Eyes open, look at yourself." The "look at yourself" is the move underneath the move — the client is not looking at the coach so much as looking at the part of themselves that has been doing this to them.

## When Joe Uses It

After the read of [[composing-in-the-session-itself]] has been established and the client has acknowledged that the composure is constant and painful. The move requires:

- The client has named that they were yelled at (or otherwise taught) to compose themselves. The historical source of the introject has been surfaced, even briefly.
- The coach has named that the voice doing the composing is now the client's own ("Yeah, well, it's you, so it is all people, meaning like you're saying it to yourself").
- The client is intellectually with it but has not felt the absurdity yet.

The move is the felt-sense delivery vehicle for the realization that has already been named verbally. Naming alone is not enough; the body has to enact it.

## What It Produces

The move usually produces:

1. **Resistance.** "I don't want to." This is the data. The client cannot easily say to another person what they say to themselves all day.
2. **A halting, awkward delivery.** When the client does say it, the words come out small, strained, with the body tightening as they say it.
3. **A sudden visibility of the absurdity.** The client, looking at a visibly un-composed coach being told to compose, often laughs or sighs. The thing they have been doing to themselves becomes seeable as a thing they would never do to another person.
4. **A spontaneous release.** Joe's report immediately after: "Just a little more room. I just saw you make a little more room for yourself. Do you feel that?"
5. **An opening that can be amplified.** Joe extends the release with "See what it's like to just play with 10. Like, make 10 times more room for yourself, just for a second. Room for your face, room for the expression that actually wants to be made."

The release is small at first. The amplification (10x more room) lets the client try the opposite end of the spectrum without being committed to it — it is framed as play, not as a new permanent posture.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client says "I don't want to," Joe insists. "I know, but just do it." The insistence is loving but firm — the resistance is exactly what needs to be moved through, so the coach does not collude with it by retreating.

If the client delivers the instruction and immediately recomposes, Joe catches the recompose ("you just did it again") and invites another round.

If the client manages to say it and the body softens, Joe immediately reflects what just happened. The reflection makes the change visible to the client, who otherwise might miss it. "I just saw you make a little more room for yourself. Do you feel that?"

If the opening is real, Joe invites amplification. The 10x instruction is calibrated — too small and the client cannot feel the difference; too much and the client gets overwhelmed and recomposes. "10 times more room" works for the same reason "for a second" works: it is large in magnitude but small in duration.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I don't want to."

- **Person:** (says it, halting)

## Tone

Insistent but warm. The "I know, but just do it" carries no irritation. The reflection of the change is delighted ("Oh my God, you're like a different person"). The amplification invitation is playful — "play with 10," "just for a second" — keeping the client's nervous system from treating the expansion as a commitment.

The coach's own un-composed state is part of the move. If the coach is rigid or formal while issuing this instruction, the contrast doesn't land. The move requires the coach to be visibly the thing the client cannot yet be.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not insist on the move before the client has acknowledged the pattern verbally. The move only works when the client is intellectually with the realization and needs to feel it.
- Do not let the client deliver the instruction without eye contact. The eye contact is the mechanism — it forces the client to see another being while issuing the command, which is what makes the absurdity visible.
- Do not use this move with someone whose history of being-yelled-at is acutely traumatic and unprocessed. The reversal can re-trigger rather than release. Use it when the historical material has enough distance that the client can witness it rather than be overwhelmed by it.
