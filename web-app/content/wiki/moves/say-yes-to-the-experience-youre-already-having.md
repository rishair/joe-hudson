# Say Yes to the Experience You're Already Having

## The Move

When a person is inside an experience they cannot escape — labor pain, chemo, a death they cannot prevent, a feeling they cannot make stop — the move is to say yes to it. Not yes to the desirability of it. Not yes as resignation. Yes as the body's active posture toward what is already happening. The same experience, met with "I want this, I choose this, I pick this," lands fundamentally differently than the same experience met with resistance.

The move is verbal at first and somatic in its effect. Saying the words "I choose this" while the painful thing is happening converts the body's relationship to the painful thing from forced-endurance to active participation. The pain may not lessen. The exhaustion may not lift. What changes is the body's posture toward what is happening, and the posture is most of what the experience actually is.

The move comes from Tara Howley's labor experience and gets applied live to Michael Nagel's chemo treatment.

## Where The Move Comes From

Tara's first daughter was in a 42-hour back labor — the baby was turned the wrong way (spine on spine), so the contractions hit Tara's back. She was attempting a home birth without medication. She was resisting the pain, trying to escape it, crawling up the walls.

Her midwife — from the Bronx, in Tara's telling — interrupted the resistance with a directive: 

"None of that. You say yes. I want this. I choose this. I pick this. You say, yes, baby, we got this." -- Tara Howley relating her midwife, I'm Not Dead Yet

Tara's initial response was internal refusal:

"My mind was like, 'I don't want this. I'm going to just say it because she's going to hit me.'" -- Tara Howley, I'm Not Dead Yet

She said the words anyway, with the felt refusal underneath. The midwife stayed over her like a drill sergeant. The required phrasing did not change. The repetition continued.

What Tara reports as the result:

"By the end, it was like I was turning right to it and it was like, yes, I want this. I choose this. Come on, baby. We got this. Thank you, body. And I was so grateful for the pain. And the more I said yes to it, actually, the easier it became." -- Tara Howley, I'm Not Dead Yet

The mouth saying yes preceded the body meaning it. The forced verbal yes eventually became a felt yes. The felt yes changed the experience without changing the situation.

## Tara Applying The Move to Michael

After Michael describes the chemo trade-off as "two things — yeah, I hate the three days for eleven days deal," Tara plays the midwife with him:

"If I were to play my midwife with you — no nos, no I hates. You say, fucking, I love this fucking chemo. Give it to me hard." -- Tara Howley, I'm Not Dead Yet

She is offering the same move that worked in labor to someone in a different unavoidable experience. The structure is the same: the person is inside something they cannot escape, the default posture is resistance, and the move is the active verbal yes that the body can eventually inhabit.

Michael's first response is honest:

"God, chemo is such an insane thing to say yes to. Why wouldn't I say yes to anything else in my life? Like, nothing is as extreme that I have found." -- Michael Nagel, I'm Not Dead Yet

The reframe is immediate. If he can say yes to chemo, the leverage extends. Nothing else is as hard as chemo to say yes to. The capacity built by saying yes to the hardest available thing makes saying yes to everything else more accessible. The move's value is not just in the chemo cycle; it is in what the capacity built there generalizes to.

He continues:

"There is a spark of life in there. There is a like, yeah, I want to see this out. It's so fun. Even the parts that I hate. About living. Yeah, it's a pleasure to get to struggle and get to resist and get to be confused. It's great, actually. It's fantastic. Not actually a problem." -- Michael Nagel, I'm Not Dead Yet

The yes opens onto the recognition that even the struggle is part of what he is here for. The struggle is not the obstacle to life; the struggle is part of the texture of being alive. Saying yes to the chemo is the door that opens onto saying yes to the whole shape of being alive in this body in this situation.

## How the Move Works

The mechanism has several layers:

- **The body responds to the verbal posture.** Saying "I want this" while in the experience produces a different neurochemical and muscular configuration than saying "I hate this." The same stimulus lands in a body that is either contracted-against or opened-toward. The contracted-against body amplifies the pain signal; the opened-toward body lets the pain pass through.

- **It removes the secondary suffering.** The first suffering is the actual painful thing (contractions, chemo nausea, the chemo cycle). The second suffering is the resistance to the first. Most of what people experience as unbearable is the second suffering, not the first. Saying yes ends the second suffering. The first suffering remains but is more bearable than it was when both were running.

- **It reconfigures the choice-structure.** The person who says yes to the experience is, in that saying, exercising agency. They are not just being subjected to the thing. They are choosing to be in it. The same body that is choosing to be in chemo has more of itself available for participation than the body that is being forced into chemo. See [[accepting-the-end-empowers-the-yes]] for the structural mechanism.

- **The verbal yes precedes the felt yes.** This is the counterintuitive feature. The move does not require the person to first feel yes and then say it. The saying is what trains the feeling. Tara's "I'm going to just say it because she's going to hit me" worked. The body caught up to the words.

## What This Is Not

This is not denial. The pain is real. The difficulty is real. Saying yes to chemo is not pretending chemo is something other than what it is. The person still hates the symptoms. The 3-days-for-11-days trade is still steep.

This is not [[pollyanna-vs-reframing-failure]] positive thinking. The move does not require believing the experience is good. The move is the active stance toward what is happening, regardless of whether the experience is desirable.

This is not bypass. The grief, the rage, the fear are still allowed. The move is not "say yes instead of feeling what you feel." The move is "say yes to having the experience you are having, including the grief and the rage and the fear."

This is not advice to spiritually accept your suffering. The move is operational and somatic. It is something the body does, not a belief the mind adopts.

## When to Deploy the Move

- When the person is inside an unavoidable difficult experience (labor, chemo, hospice, illness, intense grief)
- When the person is in resistance to the experience and the resistance is producing secondary suffering
- When the person has been told to "accept" the situation and the telling has not landed
- When the person describes the experience as something happening to them and the move opens the possibility of choosing it
- When the person is using denial or distraction to avoid the experience and the avoidance is costing them presence in the time they have
- After [[accepting-the-end-empowers-the-yes]] has been established and the question becomes how to inhabit the chosen-yes

## When Not to Deploy

- When the person has not yet been allowed to be in the resistance and the grief. The move can become a bypass if delivered too early.
- When the situation is not actually unavoidable. The move is for things that cannot be left. For things that can be left, the work is usually whether to leave.
- When the person is in acute trauma response and the verbal-yes would be a shoulding. The move requires enough capacity to make the choice; without that capacity, it lands as another should.
- When the coach is delivering it as a technique rather than as a real invitation. The midwife meant it. The drill-sergeant insistence worked because it was genuine.

## The Coaching Move

The move is delivered as a leading question or an invitation, not a prescription. Tara's actual phrasing makes the leading nature explicit:

"Can I ask you a totally leading question? And I'm coming from my experience with childbirth, which is, I want to acknowledge, totally different than chemo." -- Tara Howley, I'm Not Dead Yet

The acknowledgement of the difference (childbirth is not chemo) before the move lets the move land as offer rather than equation. The coach is not saying chemo is like labor. The coach is saying the move that worked in labor might be available in chemo, and inviting the person to test it.

The phrasing the coach uses can be theirs or the person's. Tara uses Michael's own register: "I love this fucking chemo. Give it to me hard." The profanity matches Michael's earlier language. The phrasing is custom to the person, not a generic script.

## What Produces the Shift

The move usually does not produce a felt shift on the first attempt. The first verbal yes feels false. The second feels slightly less false. The third begins to land somewhere in the body. By the fifth or twentieth or fiftieth repetition (Tara's took 42 hours of labor), the felt yes catches up with the verbal yes.

This is why the midwife stayed over Tara like a drill sergeant. Singleton attempts of the move do not produce the shift. The repetition is the practice. The verbal yes is the rehearsal; the felt yes is the eventual performance.

For a chronic situation like chemo, this means deploying the move at every cycle, every infusion, every recovery day. Each instance is a rehearsal. Over time, the body learns the posture.

## What Michael Catches About the Generalization

A specific corollary Michael identifies: if he can say yes to chemo (the most extreme thing he has found), the leverage extends to everything else.

"If I'm going to say yes to chemo, what would I be a no to then? Ever. Ever." -- Michael Nagel, I'm Not Dead Yet

The capacity built by saying yes to the most-extreme available thing makes saying yes to lesser things almost automatic. The hardest yes earns all the easier ones. This is the broader value of the move: the chemo yes is not just about chemo. It is the training ground for an entire posture of yes-to-life that extends past the cycles of treatment.

The implication for using the move with other unavoidable difficult experiences: the move is always partly a training of the broader capacity, not just a response to the specific situation.

## Tone

Direct, firm, even drill-sergeant-like. The move does not work delivered softly as an option. Tara's midwife was hitting her metaphorically. The directness is part of what produces the result.

This does not mean the coach is harsh. The coach is firm in the same way the midwife is firm — with full presence and conviction, in service of the person's capacity, not in service of imposing anything on them. The firmness is the felt-marker that the coach is confident in the move. The confidence is what lets the person try the move themselves.

Tara's own delivery to Michael includes profanity and intensity, matching Michael's register. The intensity is calibrated to the person and the situation, not standardized.
