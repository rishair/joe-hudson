# Interrupt the Abstract with a Specific

## The Move

The client opens the session with abstract pattern-language. They describe what they do in general rather than what is happening specifically. The move is to interrupt this within the first minute or two and demand the concrete particular.

The interruption has a specific form. Joe locates the failure in himself ("too abstract for me") rather than in the client. This keeps the interruption from landing as criticism. Then he asks the operationalizing question — usually some form of "how?" or "what specifically?" — that forces the abstract to become concrete.

The form from the source session:

"It's very abstract. It's too abstract for me. Tell me, what's the — what's — okay, you're getting in your own way. How?" -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The sentence has three parts:
1. Name the abstraction as abstraction ("it's very abstract").
2. Locate the demand for the concrete in your own need ("too abstract for me").
3. Repeat back the abstract frame the client used and demand the specific underneath it ("you're getting in your own way. How?").

## When Joe Uses It

- The opening minute of any session where the client begins with pattern-language rather than a situation
- When the client uses generalizations: "I always," "I keep," "I tend to," "I have this thing where"
- When the client uses self-help vocabulary fluently in the first sentences (self-sabotage, avoidance, distraction, pattern)
- When the client opens with a meta-frame ("I get in my own way," "I'm a perfectionist," "I have imposter syndrome") rather than a specific instance
- When [[abstract-framing-as-defense]] has been read in the opening
- When the client is performing self-awareness rather than bringing an inquiry
- After [[whats-the-question]] has produced an abstract question and the question needs to be grounded

The move arrives early because every additional minute at the abstract level reinforces the implicit contract that the session will work at that level. Once the contract has been established, breaking it requires more effort than preventing it in the first place. The earliest possible interruption is the best one.

## Why It Works

Coaching needs concrete material to work with. Patterns and generalizations are summaries of concrete material; they cannot be coached directly because they have no specific particulars to which intervention can apply. The move's purpose is to surface the particulars that the abstraction is summarizing so the coaching has something to engage with.

The "too abstract for me" framing is the move's structural innovation. By locating the demand for the concrete in the coach's need, the client does not have to defend their abstraction. They are not being told their abstraction is wrong; they are being told that the coach needs more in order to be useful. The frame preserves the client's autonomy while still extracting the concrete.

The "how?" question does specific work. It refuses the "why?" question the client may have been about to ask themselves. "Why do I get in my own way?" produces more abstraction (theories about why). "How do you get in your own way?" produces concrete behaviors (specific things you do). The how-question forces particulars; the why-question avoids them. See [[how-what-questions]] for the broader principle.

The repetition of the client's frame ("okay, you're getting in your own way") signals that the abstract frame has been heard. The client does not have to re-explain. The repetition followed by the how-question shows that the coach received the frame and now wants what is inside it.

## What It Produces

Usually: within one or two more questions, the client produces concrete content. In the source session, the move converted "how do I get out of my own way" into "I want to become my own business owner" within twenty seconds.

Sometimes: the client gives a still-abstract answer to the how-question. ("I go to what I know how to do instead of doing what I want to do.") The answer is more specific than the opening but still operates at the pattern level. Joe pushes one more level concrete: "And what is it that you want to do?" The pushing continues until concrete content surfaces.

Occasionally: the client cannot produce a specific. They have so thoroughly converted their experience into pattern-language that they have to think to find an actual instance. The pause and the searching is the diagnostic. The client has been living in summaries; the move asks them to drop out of the summaries and into the actual lived material.

Rarely: the client resists the interruption. They want to maintain the abstract level. The resistance is data — the abstract level is the territory of the defense, and the defense is not ready to let it go. The next move is to name the resistance directly: "I notice you keep returning to the pattern. What's at stake in letting the specific in?"

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client produces concrete content: Joe takes it and begins work. The concrete material is the workable material. Joe will often summarize it back simply ("you want to start a business, you do some things, you get distracted, you go to what's comfortable, is that generally what's going on?") to confirm the concrete framing.

If the client gives a still-abstract answer: Joe pushes one more level. "And what is it that you want to do?" or "What's the actual specific?" The pushing can fire multiple times in a session if the client keeps retreating to abstraction.

If the client produces a story rather than a specific instance: Joe handles the story differently. The story has specific content (it happened on a particular day, with particular people), but it tends to be in the past-tense and recounted at a distance. If the story works as the concrete material, Joe takes it. If the story is itself a defense (recounting events to avoid feeling), Joe shifts to [[in-their-story]] handling.

If the client resists the interruption: Joe names the resistance. The naming converts the resistance into the new material to work with. The client cannot continue maintaining the abstract level if the maintenance has been named as the work.

## Dialogue Example

The full opening from the source session:

- **Client:** "Okay, so basically what I'm trying to figure out is how I get myself out of my own way. I just keep going back to that and then it's like, okay, I recognize that's the problem. And then we resort to distraction to avoid actually getting our hands dirty in the fucking problem."
- **Joe:** "It's very abstract. It's too abstract for me. Tell me, what's the — what's — okay, you're getting in your own way. How?"
- **Client:** "I go to what I know how to do instead of doing what I want to do, because what I want to do feels like — yeah."
- **Joe:** "And what is it that you want to do?"
- **Client:** "I want to become my own business owner."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

The interruption produces movement within two exchanges. The client begins in pure abstraction. The first interruption produces a more specific but still-abstract answer ("I go to what I know how to do"). The second push ("what is it that you want to do?") produces the concrete content (becoming a business owner). The session has been converted from analysis-mode to concrete-particulars mode in twenty seconds.

The pause-and-restart in Joe's interruption ("Tell me, what's the — what's — okay, you're getting in your own way") is the in-real-time formation of the question. Joe is finding the right form of the demand in the moment of asking. The unfinished first attempt does not undermine the move; it shows the coach actively searching for the angle that will get inside the abstraction.

## Tone

Direct, slightly impatient, friendly. The impatience signals that the coach is taking the client seriously enough to redirect early. The "too abstract for me" can be delivered almost casually — as if mentioning a small practical limitation rather than making a major point.

The locating of the failure in the coach ("too abstract for me," not "too abstract for the work") is part of the friendliness. The client is not being told they are doing it wrong. They are being told the coach needs help. The frame preserves the client's standing while still extracting the concrete.

The "how?" at the end can be delivered with a small lift, almost as if curious. The curiosity is genuine — the coach actually wants to know the answer. The genuine want-to-know is part of what produces the answer.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not [[whats-the-question]]. That move converts narration into inquiry by asking the client to formulate what they want to know. This move converts abstract framing into concrete particulars by asking how the abstract pattern operates. The two moves can both fire early in a session but they target different defenses.

- It is not [[reject-all-the-stories]]. That move brackets the explanatory narratives the client is producing. This move demands the specific particulars underneath the abstract framing. The two moves often pair — the abstract framing produces stories, which need to be bracketed, which exposes the specifics, which the demand-for-the-specific then surfaces.

- It is not a criticism of the client's vocabulary. The abstract vocabulary is often accurate and useful. The move is just that the vocabulary cannot be coached directly. The specifics underneath the vocabulary can be.

- It is not the same as [[ask-the-body-the-question]]. That move redirects the question from the head to the body. This move redirects from the abstract to the concrete. The two are related but distinct — the body can answer concrete questions, but the move here is to get the concrete questions in the room first.

- It is not for every client. Clients who arrive with concrete material do not need this move. Clients who are emotionally activated and need to be met where they are also do not need this move — demanding the specific from a person in acute feeling would be a misread.
