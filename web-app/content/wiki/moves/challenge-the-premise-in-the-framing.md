# Challenge the Premise in the Framing

## The Move

When a person presents a concern using a framing word that contains an embedded premise (this is a "struggle," a "problem," "impossible," "broken"), interrupt the explanation and challenge the framing word itself. Do not ask about what they have tried, why it is hard, or how they have suffered. Ask what makes it the framing they are using.

The form:
1. The person begins their story using the framing word: "I've been struggling for years to..."
2. Interrupt: "Wait, wait, what makes it a struggle?"
3. The person tries to give the content of the struggle: "I try things and they don't work..."
4. Refuse the content: "No, no, no, I'm asking what makes it a struggle? I don't know what I'm going to say to you next, but it's not a struggle. What makes it a struggle?"

The move refuses to let the framing word stand. The whole conversation, if conducted inside the framing, will be a conversation about the inside of the framing. Challenging the framing word itself opens a different conversation.

## When Joe Uses This

- When the person opens with a framing word that contains an unexamined premise: struggle, problem, impossible, broken, stuck, can't, failure, suffering.
- When the framing has been in place for years and has become identity ("I've been struggling for many years").
- When accepting the framing would mean the entire conversation happens inside it — which would entrench the framing rather than dissolve it.
- When the person is moving quickly into content and would not pause on their own to examine the frame.
- When the framing is the work, even though the person thinks the content is the work.
- At the very beginning of a session, before the search frame has had time to establish the rules of the conversation.

The move is most effective in the opening seconds of a presenting concern. Once the content is established, challenging the framing becomes harder — the content has built momentum that the framing-challenge has to push against. Catching the framing on its way in is much easier than dislodging it once it has settled.

## Why It Works

Most concerns contain a framing word that the person has not noticed they are using. "I've been struggling" is heard by the speaker as a description of fact. It is actually a chosen interpretation. The struggle is not a thing in the world; it is a posture the person is taking toward a situation.

When the framing word is challenged directly, the person discovers they cannot easily defend it. Their first attempt is usually to provide more content ("well, it's a struggle because..."). The content does not address the question. The question was not "what is the struggle made of?" but "what makes you call it a struggle?"

The second attempt usually surfaces the actual structure — that the framing is a choice the person has been making automatically. Once the framing is visible as a choice, it becomes available to be made differently. The person who has been struggling for years is suddenly in a conversation where struggle is not assumed.

The move also signals to the person that this conversation is going to operate differently than they expected. The expected conversation was: I describe my struggle, the coach gives me strategies for the struggle. The actual conversation is: the coach is interested in why I am framing this as a struggle at all. The signal recalibrates the rest of the session.

"I don't know what I'm going to say to you next, but it's not a struggle." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Find My Purpose?

This sentence is precise. Joe is not saying the next move will be a particular reframe. He is saying he is not yet inside the struggle frame and will not be entering it. The conversation will happen somewhere else. The person is invited to come along.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I've been really struggling for many, many years with finding out what is it that I really want to do, and I have tried many different things and I don't seem to..."
- **Joe:** "Wait, wait, what makes it a struggle?"
- **Person:** "A struggle is — so the thing is, I try different things and then I don't find something that I can fully..."
- **Joe:** "No, no, no, I'm asking what, what, what makes it a struggle? I don't know what I'm going to say to you next, but it's not a struggle. What makes it a struggle?"
- **Person:** "I live the things and that causes me pain."
- *(Source: How Do I Find My Purpose?)*

Three rounds of the challenge. The first interrupts the opening sentence. The second refuses the content explanation. The third actually surfaces a structural answer — the pain of living the experiments. From there, the conversation has somewhere different to go.

The repetition matters. The first challenge alone usually does not land; the person's reflex is to explain the framing rather than examine it. The second challenge, especially with the "I don't know what I'm going to say next" framing, refuses the explanation and asks again for the structural answer. Most people produce the structural answer on the second or third pass.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person produces the structural answer ("it's a struggle because the trying causes pain"):** the framing has been surfaced. From here, the move is to demonstrate that a different framing is available. See [[feel-the-searching-then-feel-the-opposite]] for the most common next move when the framing is search-for-an-intangible.
- **If the person continues to give content explanations:** repeat the challenge. "No, I'm not asking what you've tried. I'm asking what makes you call it a struggle." Three or four repetitions usually shift the person from content-mode to structure-mode.
- **If the person resists by getting defensive ("it IS a struggle, you don't understand"):** the resistance is the framing protecting itself. Stay light. "I hear that you are calling it a struggle. I am asking what makes that the word." The persistent gentle pressure does not get into a fight with the framing; it keeps offering the question.
- **If the person says "I don't know":** treat this as data. "Right. So we don't actually know that it is a struggle. What if it is something else?" The "I don't know" is the opening to consider that the framing is not factual.

## The Pattern Generalizes

The same move applies to any framing word that contains an embedded premise. Examples:

- "I have a problem with..." — "What makes it a problem?"
- "I can't..." — "What makes it impossible?"
- "I'm broken." — "What makes that the word? What would broken even mean here?"
- "This is impossible." — "What makes it impossible?"
- "I'm a failure." — "What makes that the framing?"
- "I'm stuck." — "What makes you call it stuck?"
- "I've been suffering." — "What makes it suffering?"
- "How do I let go a little more?" — "What makes it need to be about you? / What if you just kept doing it the same way?"
- "My life has to become about me now." — "What makes it need to be about you?"
- "I have to figure out how to move on." — "What makes you need to move on?"

The move is the same in each case: refuse the framing word, ask what makes it the framing, do not accept content as the answer. Each framing word is a chosen interpretation; the move makes the choice visible.

## The Late-Grief Application ("What Makes It Need to Be About You?")

A specific application that surfaces in coaching with a bereaved person in the late-grief phase. The bereaved presents with a framing built around the necessity of "letting go" or transitioning from "us" thinking to "me" thinking. The framing word that contains the embedded premise is sometimes "let go" and sometimes "about me" — both contain the assumption that a change must happen for the next phase to be available.

The form in the source session, with a widow about a year and a half past her husband's death:

- **Widow:** "How do I let go a little more of this mindset of my life is about us and we?"
- **Joe** (rather than asking what she has tried to let go of): "If your story changed a little bit and you're still doing it for us, what happens?"
- **Widow** (confused by the unexpected angle): "Say that again."
- **Joe** (restating): "You said, like, I used to think about it as doing it for us, but now it's me. What if you just continued saying this is for us? What would you be doing?"
- **Widow** (now answering the unexpected question): "Oh, well, gosh, create stories, tell stories, find passion projects, do things that make me feel excited to give this love that I have."
- **Joe** (closing the loop): "Great. So what makes it need to be about you?"
- **Widow:** "Um, nothing. There's nothing that — like, I guess it doesn't matter, does it? No, no, I can still have that and continue."

The move has the same structure as the "what makes it a struggle?" version. The widow used "let go" as the framing word. Joe declined to engage with what she has tried to let go of or how let-go work might be done. He challenged the premise that letting go was necessary at all by asking what would happen if she did the opposite — kept doing it for us indefinitely. The widow's discovery that nothing required it to be about her is the structural answer to the question.

The variant phrasing for this application: "What makes it need to be about you?" The question presupposes that it does not need to be about her unless something specifically requires it. The presupposition does the work. The widow is now in the position of explaining what would require the transition, and the answer turns out to be nothing.

This variant is distinct from the "what makes it a struggle?" variant in one respect. The struggle version asks the person to examine why they are using a particular interpretive word. The "what makes it need to be about you?" version asks the person to examine why they think a particular change is required. Both are forms of premise-challenge. The first targets a framing word; the second targets an assumed-necessary action. Both refuse to engage the content the person has been organizing inside the premise.

The application is particularly useful when the person has done significant work already and is now stuck at a threshold organized by the let-go premise. See [[i-cant-get-over-this-loss]] (the ready-but-stuck widow variant) and [[the-dead-as-conduit-to-non-personal-love]] for the full coaching arc this move sets up.

## What This Move Is Not

This move is not "argue with the person about whether their experience is real." Their experience is real. The framing word is the interpretation of the experience, not the experience itself. Challenging the framing does not deny that something is happening; it questions the way the something is being interpreted.

It is not [[surface-the-assumed-dichotomy]], which addresses binary framings ("either A or B"). This move addresses single-word framings ("this is X").

It is not [[reframe-the-question-to-the-blocker-emotion]], which moves the question from behavior to underlying emotion. This move stays at the framing level and asks why that framing.

It is not the same as [[keep-asking-whats-the-problem-with-that]], which drills down through layers of consequences to reach the core fear. This move challenges the framing at the surface; the drilling move accepts the framing and goes deeper inside it.

It is also not a clever interrogation technique. The move only works if the coach actually does not yet know whether the framing is right. If the coach is using the move as a sneaky way to install a different framing, the person will feel the manipulation. Joe's phrasing — "I don't know what I'm going to say to you next, but it's not a struggle" — has integrity because he genuinely does not yet know what the conversation will become. The challenge opens a space, it does not pre-install an answer.

## Tone

Quick, direct, slightly insistent. The "wait, wait" is not aggressive but it is uncompromising. The framing word does not get to pass. Joe interrupts mid-sentence to make sure the framing does not establish itself before the challenge can be made.

The repetition pattern ("no, no, no, I'm asking what makes it a struggle") is not escalation. It is precision. The person produced an answer to a different question; Joe is restating the actual question.

The follow-up sentence "I don't know what I'm going to say to you next, but it's not a struggle" has a particular quality — open, exploratory, undefended. Joe is not selling a reframe. He is saying he is not yet inside the frame and will not be entering it. The person is invited to consider what conversation might happen outside the frame.

When the structural answer arrives ("I live the things and that causes me pain"), the tone softens slightly. The challenge has done its work; now the actual work can begin. The "wait wait" energy gives way to the slower energy of working with what surfaced.
