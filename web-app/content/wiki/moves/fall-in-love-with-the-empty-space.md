# Fall in Love With the Empty Space

## The Move

A person who has been chasing external things to fill an internal hole -- and who has recognized the chase is not working -- is offered a different orientation toward the hole itself. Instead of trying to fill it, get interested in it. Instead of avoiding it, enter it. Instead of treating it as the problem, treat it as the place where the answer is.

The move is delivered as a set of questions, not as an instruction:

"What is it like to go find out what that big empty space that I've been trying to fill, what is actually there? What's actually going on there? How do I actually fall in love with that? How do I enter into that? How do I stop avoiding that?" -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 100s of Top Performers

The questions are not rhetorical. They are operational. The person is being asked to actually do these things: go find out, get curious about, fall in love with, enter into, stop avoiding. Each question opens a possible engagement with the empty space.

## When Joe Uses This Move

The move belongs at the threshold after a particular kind of recognition. The conditions:

- The person has been chasing an external goal (or several) as the route to fulfillment.
- The chase has produced its target. The target did not produce the fulfillment.
- The person has noticed this. They can articulate the gap between what they got and what they hoped for.
- The next chase is forming, but has not yet fully installed.

This is the [[seen-through-the-disguise-of-success]] stage. The move is the high-leverage intervention at this stage.

It is not the move for the person still in the active chase. For them, the empty space is not yet acknowledged as the underlying problem. They will hear the move as "stop trying" and either reject it or convert it into a new chase ("I'll fall in love with the empty space and then I'll be free").

It is not the move for the person in active crisis or trauma response. For them, the empty space is too overwhelming to enter without other support. The move presumes a certain amount of stability in the system.

It is the move for the high-functioning client who has done the chasing, has noticed it didn't work, and is now at the threshold of either installing the next chase or doing something different. The window is short. The move is what makes the difference.

## What It Produces

When the move lands, several things tend to happen:

1. **A shift in attention.** The person stops scanning for the next external thing and starts noticing the felt-quality of the empty space itself. The empty space becomes an object of attention rather than the gap that attention is trying to skip over.

2. **Surprise at what is actually there.** The felt-emptiness, when actually attended to, is rarely the catastrophic void the avoidance has been treating it as. It is often quieter, more interesting, more textured. The catastrophic image dissolves on contact. See [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]].

3. **Discovery of what the chase has been preventing.** The person often discovers that what they have been chasing through external acquisition was actually available in the empty space the whole time. The freedom, the peace, the felt-okayness was not future-located. It was being held at bay by the chase. See [[nothing-is-required-for-freedom]].

4. **An identity shift.** The "person who has to keep chasing" identity loosens. A different ground becomes available. The person reports something like "I am not just the one running the engine; there is something underneath the engine that has been waiting."

5. **The release of energy from the avoidance.** The energy that was being spent on the avoidance is now available. The person often reports feeling more present, more spacious, more curious. The chase had been the consumer of an enormous amount of background energy.

When the move does not land (because the person is not at the threshold or the move is mistimed), the typical result is that the person hears the language, intellectually processes it, and continues the chase. The move requires the threshold to land. Without the threshold, it functions as another concept.

## How to Deliver the Move

The move is delivered through questions, not statements. The reason: a statement gives the system something to react to ("oh, so I should fall in love with the empty space"). A question gives the system something to do ("how do I actually fall in love with it?"). The question is operational. The statement is recreational.

The full sequence Joe uses:

1. **"What is it like to go find out what that big empty space that I've been trying to fill, what is actually there?"** -- the curiosity question. Open the inquiry without prescribing what should be found.

2. **"What's actually going on there?"** -- the inspection question. Get specific about the felt-content of the space.

3. **"How do I actually fall in love with that?"** -- the orientation question. The orientation toward the space is the move; the methodology is left to the person.

4. **"How do I enter into that?"** -- the entry question. The space is treated as something to enter rather than to fill.

5. **"How do I stop avoiding that?"** -- the avoidance-recognition question. The chase is named as avoidance, which it has been the whole time.

The five questions can be used together or in any order, depending on what the person needs to hear. They are not a script. They are a set of pointers that can be deployed as the conversation requires.

A specific feature: the questions use "I" rather than "you." Joe asks them as the client would ask them. This invites the client to take ownership of the inquiry rather than receiving it as instruction. The move is the client's, not the coach's. The coach is just naming the questions the client should be holding.

## The Tone

The tone is interested rather than urgent. The empty space is being treated as something worth getting to know, not as an emergency to address. There is a particular quietness to the delivery -- a slowing-down, a softening of voice, a leaving-room-for-the-question-to-sit.

There is also no judgment in the tone. The chase has not been bad. The avoidance has been intelligent. The shift is being offered, not prescribed. The person can take it or leave it. The coach is not invested in the outcome. The empty space is what it is. The person's relationship to it is the only variable.

The tone is also not "deep" in the heavy spiritual sense. The empty space is interesting, not sacred. The inquiry is curious, not reverent. This light touch is part of what makes the move land. Heavy reverence often re-triggers the achievement-engine ("now I have to do this spiritual thing correctly"). The light touch keeps the move available as a casual experiment.

## What This Is Not

- **Not a meditation technique.** The move is not "sit and observe the empty space." It is "go find out what is there." The verbs are active. The empty space is being engaged with, not just witnessed.
- **Not a renunciation of the chase.** The move does not require quitting the job, leaving the marriage, selling the company. The person can continue the activities that produced the kingdom. The shift is in where the meaning is supposed to come from, not in the external arrangement.
- **Not a one-time fix.** The empty space will need to be entered repeatedly. The chase will try to re-install. Each re-installation is the opportunity to do the move again. See [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]] on the 2-3 sessions of 2-3 hours dose.
- **Not a way to feel better quickly.** The first contact with the empty space is often uncomfortable. The reframe is "uncomfortable in a good way" rather than "comfortable." The discomfort is what the avoidance has been preventing for years. Some unwinding is required before the felt-experience becomes spacious rather than threatening.
- **Not a substitute for trauma-aware work where trauma is involved.** Some empty spaces have specific trauma content that requires specific support. The general move applies to the general case.

## What Follows the Move

When the move lands and the person actually enters the empty space, the work continues. The contact with the empty space is the doorway, not the destination. Several things become available next:

- The recognition of what the empty space actually contains, which is usually some form of love or longing-to-be-loved rather than the catastrophic void the avoidance had been imagining. See [[what-were-avoiding-is-usually-love]].
- The discovery that the freedom the chase was supposed to deliver has been available in the empty space the whole time. See [[nothing-is-required-for-freedom]] and [[freedom-is-recognized-not-produced]].
- The relocation of identity from "the chaser" to something more durable. See [[knowing-yourself-is-the-only-safety]].
- The reorientation of the activities that produced the kingdom toward the [[intrinsic-drive-to-build]] -- doing the work because the doing is alive, rather than because the achievement is supposed to produce the felt-state. See [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]] for the full distinction.

## In a Coaching Session

A typical use:

- **Person:** I got everything I thought I wanted and I'm still not okay. I keep thinking I just need the next thing.
- **Joe:** Right. So you've been trying to fill a hole with external stuff. And the external stuff doesn't reach the hole.
- **Person:** Yeah.
- **Joe:** What if you stopped trying to fill it? What if you got interested in the hole itself?
- **Person:** What does that mean?
- **Joe:** What is it like to go find out what that big empty space that you've been trying to fill -- what is actually there? What's actually going on in that space? How do you fall in love with it? How do you enter into it? How do you stop avoiding it?
- *(Then: silence. The questions need to sit. Do not answer them for the person.)*

The questions sit. The person's body usually responds before their words do. There may be a softening, a slowing, a tear, a long exhale. These are the signs that the questions are landing. Continue to leave space. Do not fill the silence with more questions or more explanation. The move's potency is in the inquiry, not in the instruction.

If the person responds with intellectual content ("I'm not sure what's there"), the move is to redirect to the felt-experience: "What do you notice in your body when you imagine getting interested in that empty space?" The body usually has more direct access to the felt-content of the space than the mind does.

If the person responds with resistance ("but if I stop chasing, what will I do?"), the move is not to argue with the resistance. The resistance is the chase trying to re-install. Name it gently: "That's the chase asking the next-thing question. Notice it. The question I asked was different. What is in the empty space?" Bring the inquiry back to the actual question.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- The person is in [[seen-through-the-disguise-of-success]] territory.
- The person has named that their chase isn't fulfilling them and is asking what to do instead.
- The person has just acquired a milestone and is in the brief gap before the next chase installs (the moment described in [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]]).
- The person has read or heard the principle that the avoided thing is the path to freedom (see [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]]) and is asking how to actually do it.
- The person is in [[whats-next-after-i-reached-my-big-goal]] and the question "what do you want?" has produced more potential chases rather than a redirect.
- The Scottie Scheffler quote or a similar public-figure example has been brought into the conversation.
