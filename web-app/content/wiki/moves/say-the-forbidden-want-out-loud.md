# Say the Forbidden Want Out Loud (Then Substitute the Surrogate)

## The Move

Have the person say the want they have been not allowed to want, out loud, in the room. Then ask what happens in the body when they say it. Then substitute the surrogate (what the thing actually gives them) and have them say that version too. Each substitution tracks the want closer to what the want is actually for.

The form has two halves. The first half is the saying. The second half is the substitution.

**Half one (the saying):**
- "Say out loud, 'I want a shit ton of money,' in front of all these people, and then tell me what happens when you say it."
- The point is not the sentence. The point is what arrives when the forbidden sentence is voiced and witnessed.

**Half two (the substitution):**
- "And what does money give you?" The person names it: freedom, safety, security, time.
- "So say, 'I want a shit ton of freedom.'" The person says it.
- "What else does money give you?" "The ability to feel safe."
- "So say, 'I want a shit ton of safety in my life.'"

Each new sentence tracks the surrogate one layer closer to the actual want. The body responds differently to each layer, and the body's response is the data.

## When Joe Uses It

- When a person has named a want that they have made shameful or forbidden, and the shame is what is holding the want stuck. See [[am-i-being-selfish]], [[money-is-bad]], [[asking-with-shame-invites-refusal]].
- When the want is a stand-in for something else, and the person has not separated the surrogate from the want. See [[money-as-surrogate]], [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].
- When there is a body restriction or constriction around the want that needs to be located rather than analyzed.
- When the person is in their head about why they should or should not want it. The move pulls the conversation out of the head and into the body.
- Especially in group settings, where saying it "in front of all these people" adds the witnessing pressure that surfaces what was hidden.

The move is most powerful early in a session, when the want is still loaded. After the surrogate is fully felt, the move loses its charge because the want has already been integrated.

## What It Produces

The first saying usually produces a somatic response the person did not expect: an "electric, ooh" in the chest, a wave of resistance, a smile that breaks through, tears, a viscerally restrictive "no." Whatever arrives is the data.

**A surfaced body response.** "I want a shit ton of money. It's like an electric, ooh, like right in my chest." The person discovers the want has been generating energy that was being held back.

**A relaxation as the substitution lands.** When the surrogate name is closer to the actual want, the body relaxes. "I want a shit ton of safety" lands differently than "I want a shit ton of money" because safety is closer to what was actually wanted.

**Recognition of the impossibility of the prohibition.** Once the want is rephrased as "I want a shit ton of freedom" or "I want a shit ton of security," and Joe asks the group "are you all selfish for wanting those?", the original prohibition (wanting money is selfish) collapses on its own.

**A pivot to what is actually constricting.** Often, once the surface want has been spoken, the actual barrier reveals itself: not the want, but a deeper fear (being seen, being burdened, being abandoned). The move clears the surface so the actual coaching territory becomes visible.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person reports a visceral body response (electric, restrictive, exciting):** Joe stays with it. The body is delivering the data. No need to interpret. Move to the substitution and watch what shifts.
- **If the person says it flatly with no felt response:** the person is performing the saying without doing it. Pause. The shame has likely jumped ahead of the saying. Often Joe will ask, "what stops you from really saying it?" or move toward what the prohibition is.
- **If the substitution lands clean ("I want a shit ton of freedom"):** Joe checks whether the prohibition has dissolved. "If we ask everyone here, do you want freedom and security? They all selfish?" The dissolution is usually instant.
- **If a deeper restriction surfaces under the substitution:** Joe follows it. The new restriction is the next coaching territory. Often it is shame about being seen ([[in-the-story-im-not-being-seen]]) or fear of safety being revoked ([[i-have-to-X-to-be-free]]).

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "So if you'd say out loud, 'I want a shit ton of money,' in front of all these people, and then tell me, tell me what happens when you say it."
- **Guest:** "I want a shit ton of money. It's like an electric, ooh, like right in my chest."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And what does money give you? Freedom. So say, 'I want a shit ton of freedom.'"
- **Guest:** "I want a shit ton of freedom."
- **Joe:** "What else does money give you?"
- **Guest:** "The ability to feel safe."
- **Joe:** "I want a shit ton of security, or I want a shit ton of an ability to feel safe, whatever the words are."
- **Guest:** "I want a shit ton of safety in my life."
- **Joe:** "So tell me, if, if we ask everybody in this group, do you want freedom and do you want security, I would assume most people would raise their hands. They all selfish?"
- **Guest:** "Not at all. Not at all."
- *(Source: What's Bad About Money? Coaching Session)*

The conversation moves from "money is selfish" to "what I actually want is safety" in under a minute, because the body had already been doing the work. The move surfaced what was already known.

## Why It Works

Three mechanisms run in parallel:

1. **Embodied speech surfaces the restriction.** The body cannot perform the want with its mouth without showing where the want has been held. The body's response is the diagnostic.

2. **Witnessing collapses the shame.** Saying it "in front of all these people" forces the want into the public field where the shame mechanism is exposed. The shame requires hiding to keep working. Speaking it kills the hiding.

3. **Substitution tracks the surrogate.** Each new layer ("freedom," "safety") gets closer to the want underneath the want. By the time the person says the most accurate version, the prohibition has dissolved because the prohibition was on the surrogate, not the actual want.

This is the live, conversational form of [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]]. The principle is the same: the unowned want is the sticky want. The move makes ownership audible and witnessed, which is more powerful than internal owning alone.

## Tone

Light, almost playful. Joe delivers "I want a shit ton of money" with a slight grin. The crassness of "shit ton" matters; it short-circuits the careful, hedged language the person has been using. The phrase is impossible to say carefully. That is the point.

The witnessing tone is steady, not interrogative. Joe is not testing the person. He is offering the saying as an experiment they can do, and his presence makes the experiment safe.

## Variations

- **For body shame:** "Say, 'I want a shit ton of pleasure in my body.'"
- **For attention:** "Say, 'I want a shit ton of attention.'"
- **For success:** "Say, 'I want a shit ton of recognition.'"
- **For love:** "Say, 'I want a shit ton of love.'"

The form is portable to any want that has been made shameful. The substitution sequence then works the same way: ask what the thing gives them, have them say the substitute, watch what shifts.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Do not skip the substitution.** Saying "I want a shit ton of money" alone, without the layered substitution, can leave the person stuck with the original guilt. The substitution is what dissolves the prohibition by revealing what was actually wanted.
- **Do not interpret the body response for them.** The "electric, ooh in my chest" is the person's own data. Joe lets it land before moving. Naming what it means rejects the embodied knowing in favor of the head.
- **Do not pile on substitutions until the want is intellectualized.** Two or three substitutions are usually enough. Going past that turns the move into a thinking exercise instead of a felt one.
- **Do not use this move when there is no shame to surface.** A person who is openly wanting does not need to be made to say it again. The move is for the want that has been hidden.
