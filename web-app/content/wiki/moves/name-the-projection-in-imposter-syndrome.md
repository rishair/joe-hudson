# Name the Projection in Imposter Syndrome

## The Move

When a client reports imposter syndrome in a role they were selected for, the coach names the hidden projection: to feel like an imposter, the client must implicitly assume that the people who chose them did not know what they were doing. The move makes the projection visible. The client then has to either revise their assessment of the selector (which most do not want to do) or revise their assessment of their imposter status (which the move makes available).

The move operationalizes [[imposter-syndrome-projects-incompetence-onto-the-people-who-hired-you]].

## When To Use It

- When a client reports imposter syndrome in a role they were hired into, awarded, or selected for
- When the client clearly respects the judgment of the person who selected them
- When the standard cognitive reframes (you're qualified, look at your credentials) have not landed
- When working with [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] and the projection layer is the next move

The move does not fit as cleanly for self-initiated roles (founder, independent artist) where there is no clear selector. For those cases, the projection often appears one layer over (onto customers, audience) and a modified version of the move can work.

## How To Do It

The move is a short conversational sequence:

1. **Ask who decided.** "Who decided you should be in this role?" The client names the person or system that selected them.

2. **Ask about the selector's judgment.** "And what do you think of [that person's / their] judgment, generally?" The client usually reports respect for the selector.

3. **Name the contradiction.** "So you respect their judgment. And they made the call to put you here. For you to be the imposter you say you are, they were not sharp at this moment. Which is true?"

4. **Stay with the pause.** The client usually pauses. They are now in the tension. Do not resolve it for them. Let them sit with it.

5. **Optionally extend the cascade.** "And who decided that they were the right person to make that call?" The projection cascades up. The full chain of incompetence the imposter feeling requires becomes visible.

The move is short. It is not an extended argument. The naming does the work; the coach does not need to convince the client of anything beyond what the move makes visible.

## What This Produces

- A small cognitive break. The client sees a contradiction they had not noticed.
- A loosening of the cognitive scaffolding that held the imposter feeling in place.
- Often a small laugh at the absurdity of the cascading version. The laugh is the projection loosening.
- A new question becomes available: "what if the people who selected me actually were competent at the selection?" This opens the door to the work in [[trying-to-be-someone-other-than-yourself-is-the-actual-imposter-act]] -- the imposter feeling is real but not about what they think.

The move does not always immediately cure the imposter feeling. It removes one of the structural supports. The full coaching path usually needs additional moves to address the other supports.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the client revises their assessment of the selector ("yeah, they didn't really know what they were doing"):** this is now the more interesting topic. The client has a problem with the selector's judgment, not with their own qualifications. The work moves there.
- **If the client revises their assessment of their imposter status ("yeah, maybe they did know what they were doing"):** the move has worked. The opening is now available to the other imposter-syndrome work.
- **If the client says "but they hired me by mistake / out of obligation / because they didn't have other options":** this is a defense against the move. The story of why the selector was wrong is being constructed in real time. Notice this gently. "It sounds like you have to make them wrong for the imposter feeling to make sense. Notice that."
- **If the client says "they did know but I was good at hiding it":** this is the [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]] pattern showing up. Move there next.

## What Not To Do

- Do not argue with the client about whether they are qualified. The move is structural; it does not require resolving the qualification question.
- Do not be heavy with the move. The lightness is what allows the client to see it without becoming defensive.
- Do not use the move as a "gotcha." The coach is pointing at something the client had not seen, not catching them in a contradiction.
- Do not skip the pause. The discovery has to land in the client; rushing past it to the next move skips the integration.

## A Sample Dialogue

- **Client:** "I just feel like I shouldn't be in this role. Like I got lucky and they're going to figure it out."
- **Joe:** "Who decided you should be here?"
- **Client:** "Well, [CEO] hired me."
- **Joe:** "And what do you think of [CEO]'s judgment, generally?"
- **Client:** "Oh, she's brilliant. I really respect her."
- **Joe:** "So she's brilliant, and she hired you. For you to be the imposter you say you are, she got that one wrong."
- **Client:** "...Yeah, but she didn't know that—"
- **Joe:** "What's it like to consider that she knew exactly what she was doing?"
- **Client:** *pause* "Uncomfortable."
- **Joe:** "Stay with that for a second."

The discomfort is the projection loosening. The work continues from there.

## Tone

Light, curious. The move should land as the coach genuinely wanting to know how the client makes the imposter syndrome cohere with their respect for the selector. Not interrogating. Not catching. Just noticing.

The slight comic edge of the cascading version helps. The full chain ("and who hired them, and who hired them") is funny when made explicit. The humor is part of how the projection loosens.
