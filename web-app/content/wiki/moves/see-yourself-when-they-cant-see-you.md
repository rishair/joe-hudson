# See Yourself When They Can't See You

## The Move

Mid-conflict, the practitioner recognizes that the other person is currently incapable of seeing them — too triggered, too defended, too in their own shame, too dysregulated. The practitioner stops waiting for the other person's seeing to deliver peace. The peace path now runs entirely through internal recognition: the practitioner sees themselves clearly, names their inherent goodness, names their own actual intentions, holds their own boundaries.

The move as Joe gives it:

"If somebody else can't see you and you recognize that, like, 'they're not capable of seeing me right now,' the only option for you to get to peace is to see yourself. 'Oh yeah, here I am. And I actually love this person. And I'm not trying to hurt anybody, and I can listen to them, and I want to be here with them, and I'm not going to take abuse. I'm going to draw my boundaries, whatever it is.' But you can just see that inherent goodness in you, in yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The move is for the case where the partner / family member / colleague is simply not in a state to do the seeing work. It is not abandonment of the relationship. It is the recognition that the seeing the practitioner needs cannot come from this person in this moment, and that there is another source — internal — that is always available.

## When Joe Uses It

The move is offered when:

- The other person is too dysregulated to do any seeing work
- The other person is in their own shame and cannot extend recognition outward
- The other person has a structural incapacity (narcissism, trauma response, addiction state) that makes seeing unavailable in this moment or this period
- The practitioner has tried [[i-dont-need-to-be-seen-right-now]] and the eventual hour-later seeing is not arriving
- The practitioner is in a one-sided dynamic (with a parent, an authority figure, a partner in a hard period) where waiting for the other's seeing has produced years of suffering

It is the next move when [[drop-the-hot-potato]] does not produce the response that the dropping was structurally supposed to invite.

## The Specific Internal Content

The move is not abstract self-affirmation. It has specific content drawn from the actual current situation. Joe models the content:

- **"Here I am."** — recognition that you exist, that you are present in this moment, that you have not disappeared.
- **"I actually love this person."** — recognition of the underlying care, separate from the fight.
- **"I'm not trying to hurt anybody."** — recognition of the actual intent, separate from any accusation that you are malicious.
- **"I can listen to them."** — recognition of the capacity that is intact and currently being used.
- **"I want to be here with them."** — recognition of the choice to stay, separate from any feeling of being trapped.
- **"I'm not going to take abuse. I'm going to draw my boundaries."** — recognition of the limits, separate from the loving stance.

The content is specific because vague self-affirmation does not produce peace. "I'm a good person" does little. "I actually love this person and I'm not trying to hurt anybody and I can listen and I will hold my boundaries" produces the recognition because it is true to the actual stance the practitioner is in.

## What It Produces

When the move lands:

- The practitioner stops needing the other person's recognition to validate their own goodness
- The dependence on the other's response collapses — they can stay loving even if the other never sees them in this moment
- The body settles. The braced waiting-to-be-seen releases.
- Boundaries become possible to hold without anger — they are held from a place of internal recognition rather than from a place of having to prove something
- The fight may continue, but the practitioner is no longer inside the loop that was sustaining it

Joe's claim: this is the only path to peace when the other party is incapable. Peace through the partner's recognition is not available. Peace through internal recognition is always available. The move makes the always-available path actually used.

## What Joe Does With the Response

When the coachee tries this:

- **If it produced peace**: the practitioner has learned that they can find peace without the partner's cooperation. This is structurally important — it ends the helplessness of "I can't be at peace until they see me." The skill, once practiced, transfers across all relationships.
- **If the practitioner could see themselves but still felt the loss**: that loss is real and worth feeling. The move provides peace from the fight, not the relief of being seen by the person you love. Those are different. The grief about the latter may need [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] work.
- **If the practitioner could not see themselves clearly**: this is the indicator that the practitioner is in shame about themselves and the work needs to be on [[inherent-goodness]] before this move can land. The move requires the felt-recognition of one's own goodness; if that recognition is not available even outside the fight, the work is upstream.
- **If the move was used to bypass the relationship**: the move can be misused as "I'll just see myself and not bother engaging with you." That is withdrawal, not the move. The move includes "I can listen to them, I want to be here with them" as part of the internal content. The seeing-yourself move and the loving-them stance are simultaneous. See the "what this is not" section.

## What This Move Is Not

- **It is not withdrawal.** The move includes continuing to listen, continuing to be present, continuing to love. It is internal seeing alongside external engagement, not internal seeing instead of external engagement.
- **It is not "I don't need you."** The wanting for the other to see you may still exist. The move is "the peace I need is not contingent on the other seeing me," not "I don't want them to see me."
- **It is not bypass.** The actual feelings about the partner's inability to see you — grief, hurt, anger — may need their own processing. The move provides peace from the fight, not bypass of the larger relational pain.
- **It is not arrogance.** "I am good and they can't see it" can become a story of being above them. The genuine move has no above. It includes the willingness to see how the other is right too, even when they cannot see you.
- **It is not a substitute for a long-term decision.** If "they can't see me right now" turns into "they can never see me," the move is not a long-term solution. It is the peace-path while the larger questions about whether to stay in the relationship are being worked. See [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]].

## The Felt Difference From Defense

Defense and this move can look similar from outside — both involve the practitioner asserting something about themselves. The difference is internal.

**Defense** says: "I am not what you are saying I am. You are wrong about me. See me correctly."

**This move** says: "I see myself. I do not need you to see me correctly for me to know who I am."

Defense is dependent on the other's response. This move is not. Defense fires the [[defense-feels-like-attack-feedback-loop]]. This move does not.

The body diagnostic, the same as for [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]]: defense feels braced and demanding. This move feels grounded and spacious. If there is tightness, the move has slid into defense.

## Connection to Inherent Goodness

The move is the in-the-fight application of [[inherent-goodness]]. The recognition that "I am inherently good" is what allows the internal seeing to be a stable ground rather than a wish. Without the underlying belief in inherent goodness, the internal seeing is just another defense ("see, I'm good, you should see that"). With the underlying belief, the internal seeing is just true ("oh, here I am, and I am okay regardless of what is being said").

This is why the move sometimes requires upstream work on [[inherent-goodness]] before it becomes accessible. The fight is not the place to learn that you are inherently good. The fight is the place to remember it. The remembering requires the prior learning.

## The Boundary-Holding Layer

A specific component of the move that distinguishes it from purely receptive non-defense: the willingness to hold a boundary if the other party crosses into abuse.

"I'm not going to take abuse. I'm going to draw my boundaries, whatever it is." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The boundary-holding is part of the seeing-yourself. A person who sees themselves clearly sees what they will and will not stay present for. The boundary is not the opposite of the loving stance; it is part of it. Without the boundary, the move slides into staying-no-matter-what, which is its own kind of self-betrayal.

See [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]] for the structure of how the boundary can be held from love rather than from defense.

## Tone

Grounded, present, calm. The move is not heroic. It is just the recognition of what is true: that you can see yourself even when they cannot, and that doing so is the path that is actually available. The tone is not "I'm taking the high road"; it is "this is what is here."
