# Make the Session Itself the Demonstration

## The Move

When the conversation has reached a moment where the person *understands* what is being pointed at but cannot find it experientially, stop talking about it and have the moment in the room. Drop the conceptual conversation. Ask the person to be with you — eye contact, no fixing each other — and do the thing right now that the person says they cannot do anywhere.

The move converts the coaching from teaching to embodiment. The thing the person came to learn about is no longer a topic. It is happening between the coach and the client in real time. The session itself becomes the example, the experiment, and the evidence.

"Out of the head for a minute. Just be with me. You can do this. Be with me. Just like give me eye contact, I'll give you eye contact, and let's not fix each other. What's to do? What do we do if we're not fixing each other?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The instruction is concrete. Eye contact. Mutual presence. No fixing. The setup makes the alternative immediately practicable. The person who has been telling Joe about her fix-everything pattern is now being asked to do the opposite of fixing, with the person in front of her, for the next sixty seconds.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person has been describing a pattern with intellectual clarity but has not yet been in the felt experience of the alternative.
- When the conversation is starting to recycle — the same point being made in different words without movement.
- When the person says "in theory" or asks the question conceptually ("how do I do this?") rather than experientially.
- When the coach can offer the alternative in the relationship that already exists between the two of them. (The two of them are already in a relationship; the relationship is available as a laboratory.)
- When the person's fix-everything or caretaking pattern can be unmade by removing the very target of the pattern (the coach refuses to be fixed; the client is invited to refuse the role of fixer).
- When the person needs to discover that the thing they have been chasing is available right now, in this room, with this person.
- When the conceptual frame is the obstacle to the felt experience.

This is a more specific application of [[bring-the-presenting-problem-into-the-room-now]]. That move uses the live firing of the problem as the data. This move uses the live availability of the solution as the data — the coach offers the alternative posture in the room, and the client is invited to enter it.

## What It Produces

A pause. The person was talking; now they are being asked to stop. The pause is the transition.

Often the person becomes briefly anxious. The unfamiliar territory has been opened. The fix-everything strategy has been temporarily disallowed. What rises in its place is the sensation that the strategy was protecting against.

In the source session, the woman immediately reported the anxiety: "I want it— I want it to be right, and I don't know how to get there, and so I feel like I'm failing at it over and over again." The naming of the failure-feeling was the work. The pattern of failure-driven fixing was suddenly visible because the fixing was unavailable. See [[everything-i-touch-fails-drives-fixing]].

Once the person can stay in the live moment, the felt sense of the alternative arrives. Aliveness. Sensation moving through the body. The feeling of being met. The very thing they have been fixing toward is here, in the room, available without fixing. The recognition is the gift the move was designed to deliver.

## What Joe Does With What Arrives

- **If the person stays in the head:** Joe names it lightly ("we can keep on talking or we can just be together again") and re-extends the invitation. He does not lecture about the staying-in-the-head; he just offers the alternative again.
- **If the person reports anxiety:** Joe stays with the anxiety as the live material. The anxiety is the threshold. He does not engineer it away. He lets it be the data of what the fixing has been holding off.
- **If the person reports a felt sense of aliveness or sensation:** Joe receives it and names it. "Feel how alive this is. Just to be with each other like this. It's a lot of energy, right?" The naming lets the person register what is happening as the thing they were seeking.
- **If the person tries to fix the moment itself:** Joe names that move ("your mind just went to trying to fix it") and re-extends the invitation. The same pattern keeps firing; each firing gets named, and the room becomes the laboratory for noticing the firings as they happen.
- **If the person asks "how do I keep this?":** Joe declines the keeping-frame. "Exactly not that way, because that puts you back into the fixing, which takes you out of this moment." See [[undoing-not-doing]] for why the keeping-question is the wrong question.

## What This Move Requires of the Coach

The coach has to be in the moment, not narrating the moment. If the coach is teaching about being-with while the client is supposed to be being-with, the coach is in the same trap the client is in. The move requires the coach to actually be with the person — actual eye contact, actual presence, actual willingness to not fix.

In the source session, Joe explicitly reports his own experience of the shift: "And now I feel you with me more now than I have the whole time." This is not a coaching technique. It is the coach's lived experience offered as data. The client can register the shift in the coach's body because it is real.

The move also requires the coach to be unattached to whether the move works. If the coach needs the move to succeed, the move becomes another version of fixing — the coach fixing the client into presence. The move only works when the coach can offer the invitation cleanly and let the client take it or not.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Guest:** "It's very sad because, I mean, where do you go? Like, why are we here? It goes into that realm. I know you do a lot of like, what's my purpose? Who am I? Like the ego identification."
- **Joe:** "Out of the, out of the head for a minute. Just be with me. You can do this. Be with me. Just like give me eye contact, I'll give you eye contact, and let's not fix each other. What's to do? What do we do if we're not fixing each other? There's an anxiety there that's got you a little frozen. Feel into that. And yeah, and just see what it's like to— what's the purpose of us being together if it's not to fix each other?"
- **Guest:** "My, my first thought is to go to, like, to be here to support each other, which I don't know how that's much different..."
- **Joe:** "What I'm noticing is everything you're doing is trying to avoid just actually being with me. Like, you go into your head to stop being with me, you go to fix me, you try to go to fix yourself. And then when you're with me, like right here, there's like some anxiety that occurs."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, I want it— I want it to be right, and I don't know how to get there, and so I feel like I'm failing at it over and over again."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, right. And that, that's actually the driver of all the fixing the feeling of that everything I touch fails. ... How about you just be in that feeling with me of everything that I touch fails, and I'll just be with you in that, and we'll hang out? And now I feel you with me more now than I have the whole time."
- *(Source: Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?)*

The sequence shows the move working in real time. The first invitation is declined (the woman goes into the support-each-other reformulation). Joe names what just happened. The naming surfaces the failure-feeling that drives the fixing. The failure-feeling is met by Joe sitting with her in it. The being-with then happens — and Joe reports it: "I feel you with me more now than I have the whole time."

The entire arc takes about three minutes. By the end, the thing the woman came to learn how to do is something she is doing.

## Tone

Warm, slow, slightly playful. The instruction "give me eye contact, I'll give you eye contact" has a small smile in it. The mutuality is the medicine; the playfulness signals the mutuality.

The "you can do this" is delivered without urgency. It is encouragement, not pressure. The pacing widens. The voice gets quieter. The coach is modeling the state the client is being invited into.

When the person arrives in the moment, the coach's tone shifts from inviting to receiving. "Feel how alive this is" is said quietly, as a shared observation of what is already true rather than as a prompt.

## Why It Works

The move works because conceptual understanding does not produce the felt shift. The person who knows intellectually that they should be present cannot become present by knowing more about presence. They become present by being present, which requires actually doing it, which requires an occasion to do it, which is exactly what the move provides.

The move also works because the coach is the perfect partner for the demonstration. The coach is already in the relationship with the client. The relationship has structure (a coaching session) and time-boundedness (the session has a duration). The relationship is also low-stakes in the sense that the coach is unlikely to be hurt by the client's failure to be present, and high-stakes in the sense that the client genuinely wants the recognition. This combination makes the relationship the ideal laboratory.

The move also works because it bypasses the explanation entirely. There is no theory of presence. There is no instruction in how to be present. There is just the invitation: be with me. The simplicity is part of the medicine. The complexity of the topic is collapsed into a single action — make eye contact, do not try to fix me.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not a meditation guide. The coach is not walking the person through a guided experience of presence. The coach is just offering presence and waiting to see what the person does with it.
- This is not role-play. The relationship between the coach and the client is real, not performed. The being-with is actual being-with.
- This is not a power move. The coach is not demonstrating their own superior presence. The coach is offering mutuality; both people are doing this together.
- This is not a closing flourish. The move can come at any point in the session when the conceptual conversation has run its course and the felt experience is needed.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Doing it too early.** If the conceptual frame has not yet been established, the move lands as strange rather than as the natural next step. The conversation has to have reached the point where the topic is clear and the felt shift is what is missing.
- **Talking through it.** Once the move has been made, the coach has to actually be present rather than narrating the presence. If the coach keeps explaining what is happening, the move is converted back into a teaching moment, which is exactly what the move was designed to leave behind.
- **Needing it to work.** If the coach needs the move to produce a shift, the need becomes the obstacle. The move only works when offered cleanly and released.
- **Performing presence.** The coach has to be actually present, not enacting a presence-script. The client can read the difference.
