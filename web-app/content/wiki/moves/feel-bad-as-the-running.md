# Feel Bad Is How You Run

## The Move

When the person has been making contact with something (love, fear, an emotion, a moment of presence) and then runs from it — and then says "I feel bad for running" — name the feeling-bad itself as the running. "That's how you run. One of the ways you run is to feel bad."

The move identifies a structural feature of the avoidance pattern: the self-judgment that arrives after the pattern is not a sign that the person has stopped the pattern. The self-judgment IS the pattern continuing in a new form. The system has discovered that "feel bad about X" is a way of moving away from X while appearing to take X seriously.

This is the meta-loop: the contact arrives, the running happens, the self-judgment about the running happens, and the self-judgment is itself the second instance of the running. Without the naming, the person interprets the self-judgment as evidence of growth ("at least I notice I'm running") when it is actually evidence of pattern-continuation ("the running is just wearing a new costume").

## Why This Works

The avoidance pattern is structural. It can run via many surface behaviors: dissociating, distracting, intellectualizing, smiling, deflecting, leaving the room. The system uses whatever is available. Self-judgment is one of the available forms. When the more obvious forms have been called out, the system retreats to self-judgment as the next-best avoidance.

The self-judgment has a specific advantage as an avoidance form: it looks like the opposite of avoidance. The person seems to be confronting themselves, taking responsibility, doing the work. The appearance is the cover. The self-judgment maintains the felt distance from the actual material while presenting as honest self-examination.

Naming the self-judgment as the running interrupts this. Once the person sees that "feel bad for running" is the next run, they can no longer use self-judgment as the relief valve. The pattern loses a degree of freedom. The system either finds a new avoidance form (which can also be named) or starts to actually contact what it has been running from.

## What This Move Produces

The recognition is usually quiet. The person often goes still for a moment. They have just discovered that the move they thought was self-awareness was actually avoidance. The discovery is structural, not emotional.

What often follows: the person tries to find a way out of the loop and discovers the loop has another iteration. "I get into this loop a lot. I get into you and then I get into this." Joe's response is usually some version of "yeah, you do, that's okay" — meta-acceptance of the loop itself, without trying to break it. The acceptance is the loop-breaker.

The shift is visible when the person stops trying to escape the loop. The body becomes available. The contact that the running was avoiding becomes possible. Often the person reports a body sensation — tingling, goosebumps, warmth — that signals the contact has actually happened.

## What Joe Does Around This Move

This move usually sits inside a larger sequence:

1. Person makes contact with the avoided material (often love directed at self)
2. Person runs (often via "I want to run" or by leaving the body)
3. Joe names the running: "okay, running, running, running. There you go."
4. Joe adds the meta-acceptance: "It's okay to run, by the way. I don't have any judgment." See [[its-okay-to-run-no-judgment]].
5. Person says "I feel bad for running" — the next iteration
6. Joe names the feeling-bad as the running. "That's how you run. One of the ways you run is to feel bad."
7. Person notices the loop. Often: "I get into this loop a lot."
8. Joe accepts the loop: "Yeah, you do. That's okay."
9. The person's body shifts — often tingling, goosebumps, warmth
10. Joe confirms: "Yeah, it's like you're letting some of it in."

The sequence shows that this move is part of a meta-acceptance arc, not a standalone instruction. The work is to let all the iterations of the running be present without trying to stop them. The stopping-the-running is itself another form of running.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not make the person feel bad for feeling bad.** That installs a third iteration. The naming is observational, not punitive. The point is to surface the structure, not to add another layer.
- **Do not try to argue the person out of feeling bad.** "You shouldn't feel bad for running" is a directive that the system will convert into the next avoidance (feeling bad for not being able to stop feeling bad). The instruction-format is what's structurally available to the running.
- **Do not abandon the naming if the person doesn't immediately get it.** The recognition often takes a beat. Stay with the moment. The body usually registers the truth before the head catches up.
- **Do not deploy this move without the meta-acceptance pairing.** Naming the running without accepting it ("it's okay to run") produces another iteration. The acceptance is what allows the running to land as data rather than as another mistake to fix.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "That's what you're doing. Okay, running, running, running. There you go. You let — you're letting some in. See what it's like to let, let a little more in. Running, running, running, running. Running, running. It's okay to run, by the way. I don't have any judgment."
- **Client:** "I feel bad for running."
- **Joe:** "It's natural. That's how you run. One of the ways you run is to feel bad."
- **Client:** "Feel like I get into this loop a lot. I get into you and then I get into this."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, you do. That's okay."
- **Client:** "Feeling tingliness, oh, through my body and shoulders. It's like, it's like different but goosebumps."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, it's like you're, you're letting some of it in."
- *(Source: Why You Can't Stop Trying So Hard)*

The arc shows the move working as part of the meta-acceptance sequence. The naming of feeling-bad-as-running is followed by the client recognizing the loop, Joe accepting the loop, and then the body registering the contact (tingling, goosebumps). The somatic confirmation is the mark that the running has lost its grip.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly warm, no charge. The "it's natural" is essential — it removes the moral framing from feeling bad. The naming "that's how you run" is observational, not accusatory.

Joe's tone here is light. The whole sequence is delivered with a quality of "look, here's what's happening, isn't that interesting." The lightness is what prevents the move from installing a new iteration of self-judgment. Heavy tone here would defeat the move.

## When to Use This Move

- After [[why-dont-you-just-love-yourself-as-you-are-right-now]] when the person reports wanting to run and then feels bad about running
- When the person has been running from something and the running has been named, and the next move is self-judgment about the running
- When the person reports being stuck in a loop and the loop is composed of running plus feeling-bad-about-running
- When the person interprets their self-judgment as growth and the self-judgment is actually pattern-continuation
- When the meta-acceptance of the pattern is the work and the self-judgment is the obstacle to the meta-acceptance
