# Diagnose the Meeting Live (Ask Each Member What's Happening)

## The Move

In a team meeting, name what you are observing about the dynamics in the room right now, then go around and ask each person individually what is producing the dynamic from their side. The move surfaces the dysfunction in the meeting that the meeting was structured to avoid.

Joe's prototype: He walks into a company. The CEO talks for seven minutes to introduce him. The team is not listening. Joe takes the floor and opens with the observation, then runs the diagnostic.

"So what I just noticed was that he spoke for 7 minutes and none of you were listening. And I asked each person what made them not listen to the CEO. And then I asked the CEO what made him speak for 7 minutes when he wasn't being listened to." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Structure

The move has three parts:

1. **Name the observation** of the dynamic. Specific, descriptive, not interpretive. "He spoke for seven minutes and none of you were listening." Not "this meeting is dysfunctional."

2. **Ask each member**, one at a time, what was producing the dynamic from their side. Not the group as a whole; individuals, by name. The personalization is what makes the question land.

3. **Ask the central party** (in this case, the CEO) what was producing their side of the dynamic. The leader does not get to be exempt. The leader's contribution to the pattern is part of the diagnosis.

The structure produces a complete map of the dysfunction in roughly an hour. What the standard facilitator would explore through individual interviews over a week is surfaced in the room while the dynamic is still live.

## The Three Phases of Response

Joe's account of what happened in the meeting:

"There was avoidance of the truth of the reality. And then the second thing was discomfort. And then the third thing was they were talking about the real stuff. Then they were talking about what's really going on in the company. And it got really good really quickly." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

**Phase 1: Avoidance.** "I was listening to them." The team members defend the position that nothing was wrong. Joe pushes back specifically: "I don't consider you typing on your phone listening. There was no eye contact. You were not involved."

**Phase 2: Discomfort.** Once the defensive position has been dismantled, the actual feeling of being seen in dysfunction surfaces. The room gets uncomfortable. Joe stays.

**Phase 3: The real material.** What emerges is what the meeting was structured to avoid. In this case: the team felt the CEO needed to be right rather than to get it right. The team had lost trust in the CEO. The team was divided into fiefdoms. All of it surfaced in roughly an hour.

The phases are predictable. The first two are required to get to the third. A move that tried to skip to the third would fail; the avoidance and discomfort are how the truth gets unburied.

## What Makes This Different From Public Truth-Bomb

[[public-truth-bomb]] is the move of naming the hidden thing directly to a leader. This move is broader: name the dynamic to the room and let each member surface what they are contributing.

The public truth-bomb is more concentrated and more dangerous. It addresses one person. It requires high accuracy. It depends on the leader's consent and capacity.

This move distributes the diagnostic across the team. It is less concentrated and more participatory. It does not require any one person to be the recipient. The leader is included but is not the sole target. The team itself does the surfacing once the structure is provided.

Both moves work on the same underlying logic: name what is happening, in the moment, in front of the people involved. Both rely on the same observation that the unspoken thing in a room has a particular kind of weight that is released when named.

## When the Move Works

The move requires:

- **A container.** Joe was brought in specifically to help the team. The team had agreed to the meeting and to his presence. The container created consent in the broad form: "you can do what facilitators do."
- **Accuracy.** The observation has to be specifically correct. "None of you were listening" was true and observable. If it had been a guess or an interpretation, the avoidance phase would have produced a defense the team could win.
- **Specificity by member.** The question is asked to each person, not to the group. Group questions invite group denial. Individual questions invite individual reflection.
- **Standing with discomfort.** The discomfort phase is real and unpleasant. The facilitator who flinches and pulls back during the discomfort phase loses the move. The truth that would have emerged in phase three goes back underground.
- **The leader's inclusion.** The CEO has to be asked the parallel question. If the leader is exempted, the team learns the diagnostic is about them and not about the system. The move's truth-quality collapses.

## When the Move Does Not Work

- When the facilitator is brought in to validate the existing leadership rather than to surface what is actually happening. The implicit consent is to confirm, not to diagnose.
- When the observation is wrong or fuzzy. The avoidance phase wins. The team learns the facilitator does not actually see what is happening.
- When the leader cannot tolerate being included in the diagnostic. The team will read the leader's distress and self-censor. The third phase never arrives.
- When the move is done as performance rather than service. The team can feel the difference. A facilitator doing the move to demonstrate their ability produces different results from a facilitator doing the move to help the team.

## The Reframe for Resistant Leaders

Joe offers the reframe directly: a CEO listening to this story might think "if I had a way for you to figure out the ten reasons your product does not work inside of an hour, would you hire me to do it?"

"Yes." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The reframe collapses the leader's resistance. The same leader who would resist this diagnostic is desperate for the equivalent diagnostic on the product. The team's dysfunction is being treated as personal in a way the product's dysfunction is not. The reframe makes them parallel: both are diagnosable, both are addressable, both deserve the speed.

"It's that, again, that emotional thing that people don't want to feel, which prevents them from finding the truth about their company quickly." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## What Self-Application Looks Like

The move is a facilitator move. When the individual leader applies the underlying logic to their own team without an outside facilitator, it takes a different form: simpler, less concentrated, owned by the leader.

"When you're doing it for yourself, you just say what you want. Oh, I notice I wanna hear you more, Lisa. I wanna hear you more, Ben. I wanna hear what you have to think about it. Or, oh, I noticed that I want more trust in this group and it seems like there's politics that get in the way." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The leader version uses [[state-the-want-not-the-action]] applied to relational dynamics. The leader names what they want from the meeting and from the people in it, rather than naming what is wrong. The structure is parallel but the form is owned and personal.

## Tone

Joe is matter-of-fact during the recounting. The move is delivered with the quality of "here is what was true." Not dramatic. Not gentle. The drama is in the content, not the delivery.

The directness with the avoidance phase requires firmness. "I don't consider you typing on your phone listening" is not softened. The firmness is what allows the avoidance to collapse rather than to win.
