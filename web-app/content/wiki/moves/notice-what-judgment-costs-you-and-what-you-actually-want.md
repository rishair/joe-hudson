# Notice What the Judgment Is Costing You and What You Actually Want (Instead of Should-ing About It)

## The Move

When a person is running judgment and wants to stop, the lever is not "you shouldn't judge." It is "notice what the judgment is producing in your life, and notice what you actually want." The shift is from a moral frame ("judgment is bad, you should stop") to a results frame ("judgment is producing this life, here is the life you actually want").

The move is structurally compatible with any addiction. The judger, like any addict, already knows the behavior is costing them. Argument about the morality of the behavior reinforces the cycle. Felt clarity about the results the behavior is producing — and felt clarity about the results they actually want — is what shifts the system.

"Just notice how judgment makes you feel and notice what kind of life you want. Don't make it into a should because that's just another judgment. Don't tell yourself you shouldn't judge. That's another judgment. Then you're judging yourself. All you have to do is just notice the pain, just notice what you want, and allow yourself to go in that direction. It's as easy as that." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Judgmental

## What Reads Trigger This Move

- The person has named the judgment as a problem and is asking how to stop.
- The person is in the "I keep judging and I shouldn't" loop, where the trying-to-stop is producing its own judgment.
- The person has intellectual agreement that judgment is bad but the pattern continues.
- The person is asking for a technique to stop judging, which is itself often a defense against feeling the cost.
- The person is using moral framings ("I'm such a bad person for thinking this way") and the moral frame is keeping them away from the felt-cost frame.

## Why This Works

The morality conversation puts the person's defense in the room. "You should stop judging" produces a defended "but they really are that way" or a defended "I know, I'm trying." Either response keeps the pattern intact because the conversation is about whether the judgment is justified, not about what the judgment is doing.

The results conversation routes around the defense. "Notice what kind of life you want, and notice what judgment is producing" is unanswerable in defensive terms. The person has to actually check the results, and the results are usually visible: people withdrawing, intimacy thinning, joy absent, body tense. The data the person needs is already in their life; the move just points at the data.

The wants conversation is the second half. Pure noticing of the cost can become its own should ("I shouldn't accept this cost"). Pairing the noticing of the cost with the naming of the wants gives the system a direction to move in. The wants are not abstract goals; they are the felt sense of what kind of life is being foreclosed by the pattern.

## The Demonstration Joe Uses

"Imagine I'm talking directly to you and I say, 'I'm judging you. What do you want to say right now?' Well, what do you want to say? I bet it wasn't like, 'Hey, let's hang out.' I bet it was more like, 'Go screw yourself, Joe.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Judgmental

This demonstration installs the principle in seconds. The person's immediate visceral response to being judged is to want distance. The demonstration shows them their own response, which is data they cannot argue with. From their own response they can infer, without being told, what other people experience when the person runs judgment at them. The data is now present without the coach having to make a claim about other people.

The demonstration also pre-empts the "I just think it, I don't say it" defense. The next claim is operational: "People know when you're judging them. Don't pretend that if you're silent about your judgment, they don't know it. Of course they know it. The facial expression, the body language tells somebody if you're judging them." The read goes through whether or not the judgment is voiced. So the cost is paid whether or not the judgment is spoken.

## What the Results Frame Sounds Like in a Session

Joe stacks the consequences as a series of conditional clauses that the person can check against their actual wants:

- "If you want people around you who are kind of eh, then great, be judgmental."
- "If you want people to basically reject you, then great, be judgmental."
- "I'm gonna make it so you don't wanna be around me. I'm gonna make it so you don't wanna buy from me. I'm gonna make it so that you don't wanna be a part of my company. I'm gonna make it so that you don't wanna marry me. I'm gonna make it so that you don't wanna date me. That's what you're saying."

The conditional form ("if that is what you want, then great") is doing specific work. It declines the moral conversation. It hands the responsibility back to the person to check whether the consequences match what they actually want. The implicit answer is almost never yes — but the answer arrives from the person's own seeing, not from the coach's verdict.

## How to Make the Move

1. **Decline the moral frame.** If the person opens with "I know I shouldn't judge," do not agree. Do not say "right, you shouldn't." Reroute to function and results. "What is the judgment producing in your life?"

2. **Use the immediate-response demonstration.** "If I told you right now that I was judging you, what would you want to say to me?" Wait for the answer. Let them feel their own response.

3. **Translate the answer.** "Notice that response. That is what people are responding to when you run judgment at them, whether or not you voice it. People know when they are being judged."

4. **Run the consequence cascade.** Step through the relationships and outcomes the person cares about. Partners. Customers. Colleagues. Children. Friends. Ask what kind of contact each of these wants. Match it against what the judgment is producing.

5. **Name the wants.** "What kind of life do you actually want? What do you want from your relationships? What do you want from your work?" The wants are not a list of goals; they are the felt sense of what is being foreclosed.

6. **Refuse the new should.** When the person says "okay, I have to stop judging," do not let the new should land. "Not 'I have to stop.' Just notice the pain when it happens, notice what you actually want, and let yourself move toward it." See [[dont-make-stop-judging-into-a-should]].

## The Pivot from "I Have to Stop" to "I Notice the Pain"

A specific structural pivot the move accomplishes: the person was operating in the should frame ("I have to stop judging people"). The move converts the question to the awareness frame ("when I judge, I notice the pain, and the pain points me toward what I want").

The first frame is unsustainable because it produces a loop where every judgment generates a fresh round of self-judgment. The second frame is sustainable because it uses the pain of the pattern as information rather than as ammunition against the self. The information is: this is not the life I want. The information points the system in the direction of change without requiring an act of will.

This is structurally identical to the move with any compulsive behavior. The "I have to stop" version reinforces the pattern by adding self-judgment to it. The "I notice the cost and what I want" version dissolves the pattern by making it incompatible with the wants the system is also aware of.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not a guilt trip. The consequence cascade is not delivered as "look at how bad you are making things for everyone." It is delivered as "look at what is being produced, check against what you actually want."
- It is not behavior modification through fear. The fear of consequences is itself a should and produces its own loop. The work is in the noticing, not in the avoiding-of-bad-outcomes.
- It is not a substitute for the deeper work on the emotion underneath the judgment. See [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]] and [[find-the-emotion-under-self-judgment]]. The results frame opens the door; the emotion work walks through it.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and slightly playful. The "if that is what you want, then great, keep judging" framing is not sarcastic — it is a real offer of agency. The lightness signals that the coach is not invested in the person stopping; the coach is just pointing at the data. The person's actual wants do the work. The coach's job is to make the data and the wants both visible at the same time.

The contrast is the moralizing tone, which signals "I want you to change." That tone produces resistance. The matter-of-fact tone signals "you can keep judging if that's what you want, here is what it is producing." That tone produces honest self-observation.
