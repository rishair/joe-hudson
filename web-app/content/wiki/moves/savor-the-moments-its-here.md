# Savor the Moments It's Here

## The Move

When someone reports they have tasted the recognition but cannot hold it — "I know what you're talking about, but it's not persistent. What do I do?" — the response is not to give them a technique for making it persist. The response is: savor the moments it is there. Be grateful for them. The rest will happen naturally.

"For the people who are in it, in some version of it, there's a lot of folks who get into this place where they're saying, 'I know what you're talking about, but it's not persistent. What do I do?' And my answer there is to be really grateful for the moments that it's there. Savor the moments that it's there, and the rest will happen naturally." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The move has two parts: the savoring direction (what to do when it is here) and the natural-unfolding direction (what to trust about the rest).

## When To Use This Move

The move is for the specific population of people who:
- Have had a real taste of the recognition (so they know what is being pointed at)
- Are currently in a phase where the taste is not persistent
- Are framing the lack of persistence as a problem to be solved
- Are pulling toward effort or technique to make the recognition persist

This is a common population in any community that does this work. People get a taste, they want more, they pull toward effort. The move redirects them away from the effort response and toward the savoring response.

It is not the move for someone who has never tasted the recognition — they need a different door first. It is not the move for someone in zen sickness — they need the heart channel to open, not more emphasis on the spaciousness. It is not the move for someone deep in the chase — they need the structural reason the chase doesn't work (see [[state-seeking]] and [[awakening-is-received-not-achieved]]) before the savoring move will land.

## What the Move Produces

When the move lands, the orientation shifts from grasping at the recognition to receiving the recognition. The grasping was the very activity preventing the recognition from arriving more often. The receiving makes it more available.

The mechanism: gratitude and savoring are operational forms of the receiving register. The receiving register is the register in which the recognition is most available. By cultivating the savoring response to the moments of recognition that arrive, the person is also training the receiving register that lets the recognition arrive more often.

This is the same mechanism that operates in the pleasure path of the gut awakening (see [[inherent-safety-as-gut-awakening]]). The savoring is structurally the same operation — the body in receiving mode rather than grasping mode. The savoring of the recognition specifically is just the application of the general pleasure-and-receiving capacity to the moments where the recognition is what is being received.

## The "Rest Will Happen Naturally" Half

The second half of the move is as important as the first. Without it, the savoring becomes its own should: "I should be savoring this so it persists." The savoring becomes a technique aimed at persistence, which is back inside the effort framework.

With the "rest will happen naturally" clause, the savoring is freed from the persistence question. The savoring is just the response to what is here when it is here. What happens with the rest is not the savoring's job.

This is the trust dimension. The person is being asked to trust that the unfolding will continue without their effort to make it continue. This trust is structurally appropriate because the recognition is received, not achieved — see [[awakening-is-received-not-achieved]]. The natural unfolding is what was producing the moments in the first place. Continuing to trust that produces more of them. Trying to take over the production prevents them.

## The Companion Move

Brett's addition in the same exchange completes the picture: "And maybe even the moments that you think it's not there, question that. Just really look, see what you find." -- Brett Kistler

The savor-when-it's-here move covers the moments the person identifies as containing the recognition. The question-when-it-seems-absent move covers the moments the person identifies as not containing it. Together they form a complete instruction: receive what is here, and check whether what seems absent is actually absent.

The mechanism of the second move: the recognition often is there and is not being noticed because the person's picture of what the recognition looks like doesn't match the actual phenomenology. The looking sometimes reveals that the recognition was operating and the person was holding it to a standard that excluded the form it was actually taking.

Both moves serve the same underlying orientation: stop trying to produce the recognition; receive what is already happening more accurately.

## Why This Move Works When Effort Doesn't

The structural reason: the recognition is the seeing through of the bounded self that would be the one to make it persist. Any effort to make it persist is the bounded self trying to operate on the recognition. The bounded self cannot do this. The recognition is what is loosening the bounded self, not what the bounded self can hold.

Savoring is not bounded-self activity. It is the receiving register. The savoring does not try to do anything with the recognition. It just receives what is here when it is here. Because savoring is not bounded-self activity, it does not strengthen the bounded self in the way effort does. The bounded self continues to soften, and the recognition continues to become available.

This is also why "savor and gratitude" is the move and not "more meditation" or "more inquiry." The other practices have their place, but in this specific situation (someone who has tasted the recognition and is trying to force it to persist), the practices can become the effort framework in a new disguise. The savoring move sidesteps that risk by being structurally non-effortful.

## Connection to Pleasure Path

The savoring move is closely related to the pleasure-as-gut-path teaching. Both are training the receiving register. Both work through the body's capacity to enjoy what is here rather than reach for what isn't.

The difference: the pleasure path is general (savor anything — the dishes, the breath, the conversation). The savoring move is specific (savor the moments the recognition is present). The general practice trains the capacity. The specific application uses the trained capacity on the moments where the recognition is what is being received.

Someone who has done pleasure work will find the savoring move easy because the capacity is already developed. Someone who has not done pleasure work may find the savoring move difficult because the receiving register is underdeveloped. For the second person, the pleasure path may need to be cultivated first as the foundation that the specific application rests on.

## What Not to Say

The move is also a guard against several common but counterproductive responses to the same situation:

**"You need to meditate more."** This installs more effort. Effort is the wrong direction.

**"You need to do specific awakening practice."** Installs a target and a technique aimed at producing the persistence. The targeting prevents the recognition.

**"Don't worry, you'll get there."** Future-oriented. Reinforces the picture that the recognition is somewhere ahead. The recognition is here in the moments it is here.

**"You probably haven't really had it yet."** Invalidates the taste the person did have. The person did have it. The persistence question is separate.

**"It will become persistent if you do X."** Promises a result from a technique. The recognition is not produced this way. See [[awakening-is-received-not-achieved]].

The savoring move avoids all of these. It receives what the person has tasted, validates it, and gives them an orientation toward what is here rather than what isn't.

## Tone

Joe delivers the move gently. There is no urgency in it. The person asking the question is often anxious about the impermanence — wanting the answer that will make the recognition stay. The gentle delivery is part of the medicine. The anxiety is itself produced by the bounded self that the recognition is loosening. Meeting the anxiety with calm reception is part of the modeling.

The "and the rest will happen naturally" line is delivered with quiet confidence. Not as a promise, but as a structural report. The natural unfolding is what produces the recognition. Trusting it is appropriate.
