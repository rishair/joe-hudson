# Wait Before Speaking in a Group (Someone Else Has the Same Butler)

## The Move

When facilitating a group conversation, do not say the thing you are about to say. Wait. Often five minutes. Often more. Someone else in the room will say it -- or say something close enough that what you were about to say no longer needs to be said.

This is the operational form of the recognition that the wisdom is not personal (see [[the-butler-metaphor]] and [[dont-claim-the-wisdom-as-yours]]). If the butler is for everybody, then the insight that arrived in your awareness has also arrived in someone else's, or is about to. Your job in the room is not to be the source of the insights. It is to make room for the insights that are already arriving in multiple places.

"Now when I'm in a room, often unless I'm asked a direct question, if I'm facilitating like a group conversation, I won't say it until it's like 5 minutes has passed because I'm sure somebody else will say it." -- Joe Hudson, Speaking Authentically With Tristan de Montebello

## When To Use It

The move applies in any setting where Joe is facilitating a group conversation rather than delivering content. It does not apply in monologue, in one-on-one coaching, or when Joe is asked a direct question. The condition is that the group is the source of the wisdom and Joe is hosting the surface.

Specifically:

- Group coaching sessions where multiple participants are working together.
- Workshops where students are processing material in real time.
- Team meetings where the leader is facilitating rather than directing.
- Sangha-style gatherings where collective wisdom is the goal.
- Q&A formats where audience members are engaged with each other, not just with the host.

The move does not apply in:

- One-on-one coaching where there is no second person to surface the insight.
- Direct questions to the facilitator from the group.
- Time-bounded contexts where waiting would compress the rest of the session.
- Crisis moments where the facilitator needs to intervene.

## What This Produces

Several effects follow from this move that are not obvious in advance:

**The insight is more landable when it comes from a peer.** When a student in the room says the thing the facilitator was about to say, the rest of the group receives it differently than they would have received it from the facilitator. There is no authority overlay. The student receiving it does not have to handle the projection of being told something by an expert. It just lands.

**The student who said it is empowered by saying it.** The act of being the one who produced the insight is developmental for the student. If the facilitator says it, the student does not get that development.

**The facilitator gets to keep observing.** Time spent not-speaking is time spent reading the room more deeply. The facilitator who waits five minutes has five more minutes of data about how the conversation is moving, who is engaged, what is being avoided.

**The group develops its own intelligence.** Over time, a group that has been taught (implicitly) that the facilitator will not just deliver the answer learns to surface insights on its own. The group's capacity grows. The facilitator becomes increasingly unnecessary, which is the goal. See [[become-unnecessary]].

**The unspoken insight often gets better.** Tristan describes this directly in [[the-butler-metaphor]]: each unspoken insight allows the next, deeper insight to arrive. The facilitator who waits often finds that the original thing they were going to say has been replaced by a more precise observation that emerges only because of the waiting.

## What This Asks of the Facilitator

The move is harder than it sounds. The facilitator who is used to delivering content has built a self-image around being the source of insight. Sitting on an insight that wants to come out is uncomfortable. The discomfort is partly social (will the group think I don't know?) and partly identity-level (if I am not the one delivering the insight, who am I in this room?).

The waiting also requires real trust in the group. The facilitator who does not trust that someone will say the thing will start to manage the situation -- coaxing, hinting, leading questions designed to surface the predetermined insight. All of this is the opposite of the move. The move requires actual willingness to find out whether the group will surface it. Sometimes they will not. That is also data.

When the group does not surface the insight after sufficient waiting, the facilitator has options: ask a more direct question, share the insight while explicitly noting it as one perspective, or let the group go without that particular insight if the rest of the conversation has moved past it. Each option is fine. The move is not "never share insights." The move is "do not be the first source if you can help it."

## The Counterintuitive Confidence This Requires

The move requires a kind of confidence that does not look like confidence from outside. The conventional confident facilitator is the one who has answers and delivers them. This move requires the confidence to not deliver, to sit with the silence, to trust the room.

This is the same confidence that lets a coach not jump in to fix the client's problem. It is the same confidence that lets a leader run a meeting where she does not give the answer. The shape of it is the same across contexts. The facilitator who can do this trusts that the wisdom is in the room, including in themselves, but not exclusively in themselves.

## Related Specific Variants

A related leadership move that follows the same logic: in decision-making meetings, the most senior person speaks last and the most junior person speaks first. The reason is the same. If the senior person speaks first, they suppress the intelligence they should be mining. See [[the-last-to-speak-move]] for the leadership-specific version.

The two moves are sometimes the same move and sometimes distinct. The senior-speaks-last move is structurally enforced for decision-making contexts. The wait-five-minutes move is a more general orientation for any group conversation. Both are organized around the same recognition: the wisdom is distributed, and the person with positional authority can either elicit it or suppress it.

## Why This Connects to the Channeling Frame

The move is downstream of the recognition that the wisdom is being channeled. If a facilitator thinks they are the source of the wisdom, they have no reason to wait. The insight is theirs; they should deliver it; faster is better. The waiting only makes sense if the facilitator has located the wisdom somewhere other than in themselves -- in the group, in the lineage, in the situation.

This is also why facilitators who slip into the cascade described in [[dont-claim-the-wisdom-as-yours]] stop being able to do this move. Once the wisdom is theirs, the obligation to deliver it kicks in. The waiting feels like withholding. The room feels like an audience that needs to be served. The move requires the channeling orientation to be intact.

## Tone

Quiet. The move is not delivered as a technique. It is described as something Joe does because he has noticed that someone else usually says it. There is no urgency in the recommendation. The move is offered as something a facilitator might do if they have arrived at the same observation. The implicit invitation is to test it -- run a group conversation while waiting five minutes before sharing any insight, and see what happens.
