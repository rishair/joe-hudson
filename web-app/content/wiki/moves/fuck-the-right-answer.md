# Fuck the Right Answer

## The Move

When the client produces an answer that sounds like the right answer rather than the felt one, refuse it. Out loud. Without apology.

"No, it sounded like the thing to feel is boo. That's what I saw here."
"Yeah, fuck the answer. Fuck what I have to say. And just see what you have to feel if you go underneath the guilt." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Love My Mom

The move has two parts:

1. **Reject the answer.** Not as wrong-in-content but as wrong-in-source. The answer came from the head, from a guess about what the coach wants, from a search for the spiritual-sounding endpoint. That source produces a word, not a felt thing. The word is rejected.

2. **Redirect to the felt source.** "Fuck what I have to say." The coach's authority is explicitly de-centered. The client is told to stop checking against an external rubric and instead check against the body.

The move is fast, blunt, and warm. It is not punitive. It is correcting the location of the inquiry without making the client wrong for having gone to the wrong location.

## When Joe Uses This Move

- When the client produces a word that sounds like a destination (love, compassion, forgiveness, gratitude, peace) in response to a question that was actually asking what the body has next.
- When the client looks at the coach for confirmation while answering, signaling they are checking the answer against the coach rather than producing it from themselves.
- When the answer is suspiciously clean. The felt answer is usually less neat. A perfectly composed emotional word is often a clue.
- When the client has been around enough coaching or spiritual material to know what the "right" things to feel are. Sophisticated clients are at higher risk for this; they know the script and can run it.
- When the descent through emotion layers (see [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]]) hits a word that is the expected endpoint rather than the actual next layer.

## What Reads Trigger This Move

- The client says the spiritual word ("love") with no body shift. The word arrives like a recitation rather than a discovery.
- The client says "yes, but" — agreeing intellectually with what they think the coach wants, then immediately reverting to the actual material.
- The client laughs nervously after producing the answer, often because they themselves can sense it was performed.
- The client offers the answer and then waits for the coach to confirm. The wait is the tell.
- The client's answer matches the question's framing too neatly. Joe asked "if you couldn't feel the guilt, what's underneath?" The client says "love." The answer was constructed from the question rather than felt.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The client usually has one of three responses to the rejection:

- **Embarrassment + the real answer.** "Yes, but that's because I thought it was the answer, love." The performance is acknowledged. The body produces the next material. Continue working with that.
- **Repeat performance.** The client tries another "right" answer. Reject again, more firmly. Sometimes the second rejection lands harder because the first one didn't fully take.
- **Drop into the body.** The client goes quiet, drops in, and produces the actual felt material. This is the goal.

The move is not over until the felt material arrives. Often it takes one rejection. Sometimes two. The coach holds the position throughout.

## Why This Works (Where the Move Comes From)

The relationship between client and coach contains an inherent risk: the client will produce what they think the coach wants. The risk increases with the client's investment in being "good" at the work. A sophisticated client who has done a lot of self-development knows the destinations of common coaching moves. They can run the script. The script produces words. The words are not the same as the felt material.

The move dissolves the dynamic that produces the performance. By rejecting both the answer and the implicit authority structure ("fuck what I have to say"), the coach removes the audience for the performance. With no audience, the performance has no purpose. The client is left with their actual experience, which is what the work needs.

This is the inverse failure mode of the [[coach-as-mirror-not-customer-service]] principle: the customer-service coach lets the client succeed at the performance and reinforces it. The mirror coach refuses to be the audience.

## Variations

The exact words "fuck the answer" are not load-bearing. Joe uses the bluntness here because the client needed it to land fast. Softer forms of the same move:

- "That sounded like an answer, not a feeling. What does the body have?"
- "I'm not interested in the right answer. What's actually there?"
- "Don't perform it for me. Just see what's there."
- "Forget what you think the answer should be. What's the next thing the body has?"

The choice of register depends on the client. Some clients need the F-word to break the politeness loop. Some clients hear the F-word as aggression and shut down. Joe's calibration is implicit: he uses the blunt form with clients who can take it and who need the speed.

## What This Move Is Not

- It is not a rejection of the client. The move rejects the answer, not the person. The warmth has to stay in the room. If the rejection lands as personal attack, the client will retreat and the inquiry will stall.
- It is not anti-spirituality. The word "love" is not a wrong word. Saying "love" because love is the destination, before the felt material has been moved, is the problem. Once the felt sequence has been completed, the same word may arrive cleanly from the body and that arrival is welcomed.
- It is not a license to be a contrarian. The move applies specifically to performed-right-answer cases. It is not a general practice of refusing the client's words.
- It is not the same as [[meet-resistance-with-okay]]. That move accepts the client's "no" as real. This move refuses the client's "yes" when the yes is not real.

## Tone

Direct, fast, warm. The "fuck" carries energy, not anger. The energy says: come on, I know you can drop in. The warmth says: I'm with you in the drop-in.

The move should not feel cold. It should feel like a friend interrupting another friend who is being too polite about something that matters.

## Related Moves and Anti-Patterns

This move shares structure with [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]] applied to any virtuous emotion. The anti-pattern there is the client (or coach) jumping to compassion before the felt sequence has completed. This move is the in-session intervention when that jump happens live.

This move also relates to [[trying-to-be-right-is-the-voice]]: trying to give the right answer is the voice doing the work the client should be doing from the body. The move refuses the voice's offering and asks the body to speak.

For the broader principle, see [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]]. The client's wisdom is what is being protected here, by refusing to let the client substitute the coach's apparent wisdom for their own felt knowing.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When the descent through emotion layers produces an answer that is too clean.
- When the client is sophisticated and known to perform.
- When the session is in a felt-inquiry phase and the client is reverting to the head.
- When a [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]] question gets a script-answer rather than a felt-answer.
- When a "right" word arrives without a corresponding body shift.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Back into the felt inquiry. The work resumes from the actual underneath-material rather than the performed one.
- Often toward an embarrassed acknowledgment ("yes, that's because I thought it was the answer"), which is itself useful — it makes the performance visible to the client.
- Toward [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] as the underlying principle.
- Toward [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] once the actual material is available.
