# Love the Doing, Not the Reward

## The Principle

Success at any sustained activity comes from loving the doing of the activity, not from loving the reward at the end. The reward, when it arrives, feels good for about two minutes and then is gone. The doing is what fills the rest of the time. If the doing is not loved, no amount of reward at the end will make the life feel worth it.

"The problem with winning is it feels good for about 2 minutes and then it's gone." -- the world champion golfer Joe references, recounted by Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The reframe is precise. The two-minute window of post-reward satisfaction is what most people are organizing their entire lives around. They spend years of unloved doing in service of two minutes of liked having. The math does not work.

## The World Champion Golfer

Joe references a video he made about a world-champion golfer. The golfer wants to win, likes winning, has won many things. When asked what made him successful, he did not say the winning. He said the practice.

"He kept on saying, though he didn't say that this was the key to his success, he kept on saying, 'I just love to practice. I just love practicing. I just love practicing.' And that's what makes him successful, is that he loves the doing of the thing. Not the getting the reward." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The golfer was reporting a structural feature of his own life. The winning was the visible part. The thing producing the winning was the love of practicing. The practice was the substrate; the wins emerged from the substrate. Reverse the relationship and the practice becomes effortful, the wins become harder, and the love drains out of the activity.

The pattern generalizes. The person who loves the doing of the thing produces the wins as a byproduct. The person who is doing the thing for the wins eventually has a hard time producing wins because the engine driving the doing is too weak to sustain the practice required.

## The Universal Pattern

The principle applies across domains:

- **Business.** "If you love running a company, you'll be successful. If you are running a company to get rich, you may or may not be successful. You definitely won't be as successful." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You
- **Art.** Loving art for the sake of art is what sustains the art. The artist who loved making things and then gets the opportunity to monetize the art often finds the love drains out of the art. The activity has been re-aimed at the reward, and the reward-aiming kills the doing-love that produced the art in the first place.
- **Practice in any craft.** The musician who loves practicing. The writer who loves the sentence. The athlete who loves training. The teacher who loves teaching. In every case, the love of the doing is what produces the sustained excellence; the reward is the byproduct.
- **Parenting.** The parent who loves the parenting (the daily noticing, the small games, the listening) raises a different kind of child than the parent who loves the idea of having raised a child. The doing is the parenting; the idea is the reward; only the first one is operational.

## The Artist Who Loses the Art When They Monetize

Joe names a specific failure mode: artists who love making art and then get the chance to make money on art and find the love disappears.

"So many people, they love art, they love doing art, and then they have the opportunity to make money on art, and their love for the art goes completely away because they're going for that end state." -- Joe Hudson, There's A War Inside You

The mechanism: the activity has not changed; the engine driving the activity has. Before the monetization, the engine was the love of the doing. After the monetization, the engine becomes the pursuit of the end state. The activity looks identical from the outside. From the inside it is now drudgery, because the love of the doing has been replaced with the wanting of the reward, and the wanting of the reward cannot fuel the doing the way the love of the doing did.

This is not a principle against making money on art. The principle is about which engine is driving. Money can arrive without becoming the engine. The work is to notice when the engine has shifted, and to return to the love of the doing as the primary fuel.

See [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] for the related principle about importance-loading killing fun. The reward-loading is the importance-loading at the level of outcome.

## Why the Reward Engine Is Insufficient

The reward engine fails for two reasons:

1. **The reward is brief.** The two-minute window is real. Most accomplishments produce a small window of satisfaction that closes quickly as a new want spins up. See [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]] for the structural explanation. If the doing was not loved, the brief window is paying for the entire stretch of unloved doing. The math is bad.

2. **The reward engine has lower thrust.** The love of the doing is renewable in each moment of the doing. The pursuit of the reward is renewable only in moments of anticipation. The first one fuels continuous engagement; the second one fuels intermittent pushing. Over years, the first one produces vastly more action than the second.

The combination of the two failures means that reward-organized lives produce less of the reward than doing-loving lives, and the holders of the reward feel worse about having it. Both axes are worse.

## The Connection to Craving

This principle is the action-level version of [[wanting-vs-craving]]. The love of the doing is the clean want — present, felt, allowed, expansive. The pursuit of the reward is craving — gripped, attached, fixated, contracted. The same activity can be approached from either form. The form determines the experience and the result.

The traditions that point at "do the thing for its own sake" are pointing at the doing-love form. The traditions that point at "let go of attachment to outcome" are pointing at dropping the craving form. They are the same teaching from two angles. The thing they both point at is the engagement that comes from inside the activity rather than from the projected reward.

## Why the Same Activity Becomes Different Activities

A specific feature of this principle: the same activity changes its character depending on which engine is driving it. The golf swing of the player who loves practicing is not the same swing as the golf swing of the player who is grinding for the win. The notes are the same; the experience and the result are different.

This is observable at the body level. The doing-lover's body is relaxed, attentive, present in the activity. The reward-chaser's body is tense, anxious, ahead of the activity (already projecting to the result). The body knows which engine is running before the conscious mind does.

The diagnostic in oneself: notice the body while doing the activity. If the body is in the activity, the doing-love is operational. If the body is ahead of the activity, projected to the reward, the craving is operational. The shift between the two engines is sometimes felt as a moment-by-moment thing rather than a permanent state. Both engines are accessible. The work is to notice which one is running and to come back to the doing when the body has run ahead.

## The Implication for Coaching

When someone reports that they have lost motivation or feel depleted by their work, the move is not to find a bigger reward. The move is to ask whether they loved the doing of the thing and when they stopped. Usually they did love it once. Usually the love drained out when the activity became about something else — money, status, recognition, fear of failure.

The recovery is not about restoring the love of the doing through willpower. The love of the doing returns when the reward-pressure is dropped. The question is what has been making the doing serve the reward, and what would it take to do the activity for its own sake again. See [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] for the move that often unlocks the return.

## What This Is Not

- Not a claim that rewards are bad. Rewards can be welcome. They just cannot be the engine.
- Not a claim that doing-love guarantees worldly success. The principle says doing-loving lives produce more sustained excellence than reward-chasing lives, on average. It does not guarantee any specific outcome.
- Not a license to do unloved work and call it doing-love. The principle requires the love to be actual. A performed love of the doing is the craving engine wearing the doing-love costume; it produces craving's results.
- Not anti-ambition. The doing-loving life can be highly ambitious. The ambition just runs on a different fuel — the wanting to build more (see [[intrinsic-drive-to-build]]) rather than the wanting to have the next thing.

## Tone

Direct. The two-minute window claim is delivered as a structural observation, not as an exhortation. The golfer example does the persuasion. The teaching is "look at how this actually works for people who are unambiguously successful," not "you should love the doing more."
