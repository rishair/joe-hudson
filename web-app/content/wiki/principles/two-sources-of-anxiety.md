# Two Sources of Anxiety: The Action Not Taken and the Bigger Room Not Filled

## The Principle

Anxiety comes from two places, and only two. The first is the thing you know needs doing that you are not doing. The second is the moment you are doing the thing you are meant to do but not feeling it all the way -- the bigger room you are stepping into that you are not letting yourself fill.

"Most of the anxiety in our life comes from not doing the thing that we know that we want to be doing, but it also comes from that moment when we are doing the thing that we know we're meant to do, stepping into that bigger room, and we're not feeling it all the way." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety Explained In A Way That Finally Makes Sense

These are the two arenas. Every chronic anxiety can be sorted into one or the other (or both at once). The sorting is the first move because the response differs.

## Why This Compression Matters

The cultural picture of anxiety is that it comes from external load: too much to do, too much going on, too much pressure from outside. The principle inverts the picture. The load is not the cause. The load amplifies. The cause is one of these two specific internal states.

If the cause is the unmoved action, the response is to find and take the action.

If the cause is the unfelt expansion, the response is to feel the expansion -- to let yourself be in the bigger room at the size the room is.

Either way, the anxiety is information about something specific that the system is not doing. Knowing which kind tells you what to do.

## Source 1: The Action You Know To Take But Are Not Taking

This is the most common form. The system has been registering for some time that a specific thing needs to happen. The conversation that needs to be had. The job that needs to be quit. The boundary that needs to be set. The truth that needs to be told. The body knows. The anxiety is the body's continuous notification that the known action has not yet happened.

The action can be external or internal:

- **An external action.** The conversation, the decision, the move, the project, the request, the boundary.
- **An internal action -- feeling a held emotion.** The anger, the grief, the fear that has been suppressed and is the cap under the anxiety.
- **An internal action -- authentic expression.** Showing up as who you actually are rather than the performance.
- **An internal action -- letting in a positive state.** Allowing the calm, peace, or bliss that you have been blocking.

See [[anxiety-three-definitions]] for the longer treatment of the four-form action step. The principle here is the compressed form: any of the four counts as "the thing you know needs doing." Find which one. Do it.

The hint is always available. Even when the action is not fully conscious, the body has some access to what it is. Asking "what is the specific thing I am telling myself I should do that I am not doing?" usually surfaces the answer within a sentence.

## Source 2: The Bigger Room You Are In But Not Filling

This is the form most people misread. The person has actually started doing the thing -- the new job, the new relationship, the new way of being, the new set of friends, the new level of responsibility. They have stepped into a bigger room. And then they do not let themselves fill it.

The body is producing the activation that matches the size of the room. The person is reading the activation as anxiety. The activation is actually excitement. The bigger room calls for a bigger version of the person. The person is staying small and labeling the call to expand as a problem.

The compressed form of this teaching is: anxiety in the bigger room is excitement that has not been allowed. See [[fear-vs-excitement]] for the somatic mechanism. The same physiological energy is read as fear when the breath is constricted and as excitement when it is allowed.

The body-level intervention is [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] or the somatic move [[fill-the-room]]. The cognitive intervention is the recognition that the room is bigger than the person has been letting themselves be in it, and the room is asking the person to expand to match it.

The reason the principle highlights this second source separately from the first is that the response is different. The first source asks the person to act on something they have been avoiding. The second source asks the person to feel something they have been holding back from feeling -- specifically, the expanded aliveness of the bigger room they are already in.

## Both Sources at Once

Many cases of chronic anxiety run both sources at the same time. The person has the dream life (bigger room) and they are not filling it (source 2), and there are specific conversations they have been avoiding within that life (source 1). The two reinforce each other. The unfilled room produces a baseline of held activation; the unmade asks produce specific pressure points within the baseline.

The coaching path for the doubled case: sort which is currently more accessible. If a specific action surfaces immediately when asked, start there. If no specific action is named but the person can identify the bigger room, start with the fill-the-room work. Either entry tends to make the other more visible.

See [[i-have-everything-im-waiting-for-the-shoe-to-drop]] for the concern that most often presents the doubled form -- the good life that the person cannot enjoy.

## What This Principle Predicts

If the principle is right, the following should be true:

- Anxiety should resolve when the specific action is taken, not when the load is reduced. Removing items from the calendar should not consistently reduce anxiety. Doing the one thing the body has been signaling should.
- Anxiety should resolve when the bigger room is felt, not when the room is made smaller. People should not need to shrink their lives to be less anxious. They should need to feel the largeness of the life they already have.
- "Successful" people who are wildly anxious should consistently report one of two things on inquiry: a specific avoided action or a specific room they are in but not feeling. Both should be addressable.

These predictions match what is observed. People who try to manage anxiety by reducing load find the anxiety returning at the reduced load. People who feel into the room they are in and let themselves expand to match it report the anxiety dropping with no change in load.

## What This Principle Does Not Address

The principle is the psychological-and-emotional layer. There is also a nervous-system layer below it. If the body is dysregulated, neither the action work nor the fill-the-room work will land cleanly. The system has to be regulated enough to do the work. See [[regulate-the-body-before-doing-anxiety-work]] for the prerequisite.

The principle also does not address acute panic in the moment, which is its own discharge problem. See [[stage-fright-as-fuel-cell]] and [[nervous-system-prep-before-high-stakes]] for the in-the-moment work.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly inversion-flavored. The teaching is doing structural work by inverting the cultural picture. The delivery says: the thing you have been told about your anxiety is wrong. It is not the load. It is one of these two specific things. Find which one. Address it.
