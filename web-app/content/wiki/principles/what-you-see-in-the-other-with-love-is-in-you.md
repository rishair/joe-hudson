# What You See in the Other With Love Is in You

## The Principle

When a person sees something loving, generous, or beautiful in another person, the seeing is itself the evidence that the seen quality exists in the seer. The perceptual mechanism for recognizing love, generosity, or beauty in someone else requires the perceiver to have that quality available within themselves. You cannot see what you do not have.

The principle is the positive-form counterpart to [[what-you-cant-love-in-partner-is-what-you-cant-love-in-self]]. The negative form: what you cannot love in another is what you cannot love in yourself. The positive form: what you see and love in another is what is loving in you.

Joe's compression at the close of a coaching session, when the client expressed gratitude for being seen:

"The thing that's seen me right now, I see it in you. The thing you're saying about me right now, I see it in you. That's what I mean to say. Yeah, you couldn't see that about me if it wasn't true in you."

The move is a redirect of the gratitude back at the person. The person experiences having been seen; the principle says the capacity for that seeing is theirs, not the coach's alone. The being-seen is also a having-the-seeing.

## In Practice

In the source session, the client closes by thanking Joe for seeing him:

- **Person:** "Yeah, I can feel love for you. Yeah, thanks. A lot of it. I, I listen to your podcast so much every time I drive. And thank you, thank you, thank you, thank you for seeing me."
- **Joe:** "Like, I — The, the, the thing that's seen me right now, I, I see it in you."
- **Person:** "Yeah, the thing that you're—"
- **Joe:** "the thing you're saying about me right now, I see it in you. That's what I mean to say. Yeah, you couldn't see that about me if it wasn't true in you."

The move accomplishes three things at once:

1. **It receives the gratitude.** Joe does not deflect with "oh, thank you, that's kind." He acknowledges what was said by responding to it directly.
2. **It returns the recognition.** What the person said about Joe (that Joe sees him) is now said about the person (that the person sees Joe). The recognition is bidirectional.
3. **It teaches the principle.** The person now has a frame for why they were able to feel what they felt: because the quality was in them. The session that started with the person not believing he could be his own self ends with him being told that his capacity for love is operating right now in the room.

## Why This Is Not Deflection

The standard mistake when someone offers gratitude is to deflect: "oh, you did all the work." Deflection is its own form of [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]] — it tells the giver that what they perceived was wrong.

The mirror-return is different. The coach does not contradict what was said. The coach affirms what was said by extending its scope. "You saw love in me. Yes. And the seeing means the love is also in you." The giver's perception is treated as accurate and is then expanded.

This is why the move is particularly powerful as a session-close. The work of the session has been to surface contact with what the person essentially is. The final move is to use the person's spontaneous expression of love-toward-the-coach as evidence of the very thing the session was trying to establish.

## The Perceptual Mechanism

The principle relies on a specific claim about perception: recognition requires the perceiver to have the recognized quality available. This is not a metaphor. The claim is structural.

To recognize generosity, you have to know what generosity feels like from the inside. The pattern-matching that lets you identify "this person is being generous" is a pattern-matching against your own experience of generosity. The pattern is in you. If it were not, you would not have a referent to match against.

The same applies to love, kindness, warmth, attention, presence, care. Each of these recognitions is a self-recognition extended outward. The other person is the occasion; the recognition is internal.

This is the same machinery as [[projections]] in the negative direction. The negative form: you see hostility in others because hostility is in you. The positive form: you see warmth in others because warmth is in you. Both rely on the same perceptual fact. Neither is a moral claim. Both are descriptions of how seeing works.

## What This Move Is Not

- This is not the negative-projection move ([[how-is-that-not-true-about-you]]). That move is used when the person has delivered a hostile judgment of someone else; the question turns the judgment back. This move is used when the person has expressed positive recognition; the response amplifies the recognition rather than questioning it.
- This is not [[see-yourself-and-acknowledge-others]] as a practice. That practice is a daily bidirectional seeing the person does on their own. This is a real-time move in a coaching conversation when the person has spontaneously offered recognition.
- This is not a closing technique applied for effect. The move only works when it is actually true — when the coach has, in fact, been able to see the person, and the person has, in fact, been able to see the coach. If either of those is not true, the move is hollow and lands as hollow.

## When Joe Uses It

The move fires when:

- A client expresses gratitude or recognition toward the coach at a moment of opening
- The expression is spontaneous and embodied rather than performed
- The session has done enough work that the person's recognition is itself a recovered capacity, not a habitual social move
- The closing of the session is an appropriate place to reinforce what the session opened

The move is more useful at the end of a session than in the middle. In the middle, redirecting the gratitude back at the person can interrupt the work. At the end, it consolidates the work — the person leaves with the recognition that the love they just felt is theirs to keep.

## Tone

Warm, slightly stumbling. Joe restates the move ("That's what I mean to say") because the first attempt was imprecise. The slight stumble is part of why the move lands — the coach is reaching for the right words to give the recognition back, not delivering a polished line.

The "you couldn't see that about me if it wasn't true in you" sentence is the load-bearing one. It is delivered plainly, almost as an observation rather than as a teaching. The plainness is what makes it receivable.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A coaching session in which the work has surfaced contact with the person's essential self
- A spontaneous expression of love, gratitude, or recognition from the client toward the coach
- The moment of closing a session where the recognition the person has built needs to be located inside them rather than in the coach

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward closure of the session
- Toward [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]] if the person deflects the return-of-recognition
- Toward the person's own continuing contact with what they essentially are, which the in-session recognition was evidence of
