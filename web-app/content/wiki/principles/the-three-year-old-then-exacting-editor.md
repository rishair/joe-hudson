# Be a 3-Year-Old on the First Pass, an Exacting Editor After

## The Principle

Creative production accelerates dramatically when the first pass and the editing pass are temporally separated. On the first pass, drop all judgment. Be a 3-year-old playing. Let whatever is going to come, come. On every subsequent pass, become an exacting editor. The same person doing both — but with the right mode in the right pass.

> "You do your first pass of something like you're a 3-year-old playing. And then you become an, like, an exacting editor of every pass after, right? You learn how to just get out of your head, get out of judgment, just let whatever's gonna come, come, and then take as many passes as you need until you think, wow, that thing is amazing." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The principle is Joe's own naming of how he learned to be creatively productive at high speed and consistency. It is the operational alternative to the perfectionism-in-the-first-pass mode that produces either no output or slow output of brittle quality.

## Why the Sequencing Matters

The two modes — playful first pass and exacting editor — are both necessary, but they cannot coexist in the same pass. Trying to be both at once produces neither.

**The exacting editor on the first pass produces nothing.** Every sentence, every brushstroke, every line of code is evaluated before it is finished. The evaluation interrupts the creation. The piece either never gets started or comes out brittle and mechanical because the editor was strangling the play.

**The 3-year-old on every pass produces messy work that never gets refined.** The play is sustained but the editing never happens. The work stays in its first-pass form. The quality plateau is low.

The right pattern is play first, edit later. The play produces the raw material. The editing shapes it. Each pass alternates between letting more come and refining what is there. The combined sequence produces work that is both alive (from the play) and polished (from the editing).

This is structurally why iteration produces better work than perfectionism: iteration is the operational form of "play first, edit later, repeat." The first pass is allowed to be wrong because it will be iterated. The editor passes are where the quality emerges. The mode switching is what makes the iteration generative.

## The Podcast Origin Story

Joe and Brett ground the principle in the early history of *The Art of Accomplishment* podcast. They recorded a lot of episodes early on and threw many of them out. The play-and-edit sequence happened across whole episodes rather than within a single episode. The good episodes that survived were the result of having recorded many that did not.

> "Just the same way when we started the podcast, we did — tons of them until we're like, oh. ...Yeah, threw a handful of them out. Yeah, exactly. Here, now, this feels great." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The episodes that were thrown out were not failed episodes. They were the 3-year-old first-pass work that produced the conditions for the good episodes to be made. Without the first-pass volume, the editor would have had nothing to evaluate. The iteration that produced quality required the willingness to make work that would not survive editing.

This is also why podcasters who try to make every episode perfect from the start often never publish. They cannot get the 3-year-old first pass to happen because the exacting editor is online from the beginning. The work that would have produced the calibration never gets made.

## The Connection to Procrastination

The principle is one of the operational answers to procrastination on creative work. When the procrastination is reason two (wrong method — see [[three-reasons-for-procrastination]]), the wrong method is often: trying to do the first pass perfectly. The body knows that the first pass cannot be perfect and refuses to start under the perfectionist conditions. The procrastination is the body's structural protection.

The intervention is to drop the editor from the first pass:

> "How do I have to enjoy the process that I'm, I'm supposedly procrastinating? So that was one of the big aha moments. But the second aha moment was to do that most of the time, I require — it requires an iterative mindset, which is the job isn't to get it done on a timeline, the job is to iterate on it." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

Once the first pass is allowed to be 3-year-old work, the body releases the protection. The work starts. The editor can come back online on the next pass and shape what now exists. The work that exists can be iterated. The work that doesn't exist cannot be.

See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] for the related move that often surfaces this principle in coaching. The enjoyment-redirect frequently lands as "be a 3-year-old playing for the first pass" — the enjoyment frame and the 3-year-old frame are the same orientation named two different ways.

## What the Principle Replaces

The standard creative-work narrative is some version of "get it right." This narrative carries an implicit assumption: there is a right way the work should come out, and the work of creation is to discover it and execute it. Under this narrative:

- The first pass has to be careful (because you might miss the right way)
- Mistakes are costly (because they take you away from the right way)
- The editor is always on (because slipping into 3-year-old play would compromise the right way)

The principle replaces this with a different narrative: there is no pre-existing right way. The work emerges through iteration. The first pass exists to produce raw material the editor can work with. Mistakes are part of the process; they get edited later. The editor and the 3-year-old are both needed but they cannot be on at the same time.

This connects to the broader [[iterative-mindset]] principle. The 3-year-old / exacting editor sequence is the operational form of iteration applied to creative work specifically. The iteration mindset says everything is an iteration; this principle adds: each iteration has two modes, and the modes should not collapse into each other.

## The Performance-vs-Play Connection

The principle is also a specific application of the [[performance-vs-play]] distinction within a single creative session. The first pass is play. The editing pass is closer to performance — there is a standard being held, work is being evaluated, the editor is being exacting. Both are needed. Performance without play produces sterile work. Play without performance produces unrefined work.

The mistake people make is treating the entire creative process as performance. They never enter the play state. The work they produce is what you get when you only ever perform — slow, brittle, often unfinished, rarely surprising. The principle restores the play state to its proper place at the beginning of each creative cycle.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is about the inner mode the creator brings to each pass, not about the visible polish of the work at any given stage. A first-pass piece of writing might be presentable; a first-pass piece of music might be performable. The principle is about whether the editor was active during the creation, not about whether the work meets external standards.

The principle also does not say "first passes should always be raw." Sometimes a first pass turns out to be the finished work because the play happened to produce something complete. The principle is about the orientation, not about a mandatory edit count.

The principle is for first passes and editor passes within a single piece of work. It is not a developmental theory about creators (3-year-olds shouldn't be told to grow up and become editors). The principle applies to adult creators learning to access both modes in service of a single creative process.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They cannot get themselves to start creative work because they are pre-editing
- Their first drafts are over-polished and lifeless
- Their finished work is brittle because the play never made it in
- They have been told to "just write" and the instruction didn't land because they couldn't access the play state
- They are stuck in a pattern of starting and stopping the same piece of work because the editor keeps interrupting the play
- They are productive in low-stakes domains but freeze when the stakes are higher (the editor comes online and the play exits)
