# Seeing Through Projections Expands the Field of Possibility

## The Principle

When you see through a projection, the field of options in front of you gets larger. Not because you are smarter. Because fewer filters are deciding in advance what is and is not possible.

The people who get called brilliant, innovative, or uniquely creative are often not running better processing. They are running fewer projections. The same situation that looks like a single forced move to a person buried in projections looks like a fan of options to a person whose projections have loosened.

"People think somebody who really sees through projections is really smart because they come up with really cool, unique, innovative ideas, or they act in a way that's seemingly not normal but yet it works. And it's not so much really that they're smart or not smart, it's that they don't see the same level of limitation on everything that somebody who fully buys into their projections sees." -- Joe Hudson, Your World Is A Projection (2022)

## The Brick Metaphor

Brett offers the operational version in the same conversation. A psychological test: take an object, give yourself ten minutes to write down how many uses you can think of for it. A brick is for masonry, for building a wall. That is the projection at full strength. The brick is the brick is for the wall.

The more you see the brick as just an object rather than as "a brick," the more uses arise. Counterweight for an elevator. Broken into sand for Play-Doh. Doorstop. Heat sink. Improvised hammer. A million other uses.

"If your projection would be that this is a brick, it is used for masonry and you could build a wall with it. But the more you start to see the brick just as an object, as just something that's not a brick, then you could start to see other purposes for it." -- Brett Kistler, Your World Is A Projection (2022)

The same applies to every situation a person encounters. The first read is the projection. The first read says: this is what this is, this is what you do with it. Loosening the projection reveals the second, third, tenth read. The options were there all along. The projection was hiding them.

## The Mechanism

A projection is a perceptual lens that has decided in advance what category a thing belongs to and what actions are appropriate to that category. The lens speeds up perception. The lens also closes off possibility. The closing is invisible to the person running the lens; they experience the constrained option set as just what is true about the situation.

When the lens loosens, the option set expands. Not by adding new information. By no longer filtering out information that was always available.

This is the perceptual face of [[choosing-the-projection-that-serves]] and the structural complement to [[projections]]. The reason choosing a more useful projection works is that the person has, in the moment of recognizing they are projecting, briefly stepped out from under the absolute claim of any single projection. In that moment, choice exists. In that moment, options exist.

## Why It Looks Like Intelligence

The visible behavior of a person whose projections have loosened is creativity, innovation, lateral thinking, unusual moves that work. From the outside, this looks like superior cognitive horsepower. From the inside, it is closer to: I am not as committed to the obvious reading as you are, so I see what you do not see.

This reframes intelligence. The intellectual machinery may be identical. The difference is in the filtering layer that sits in front of it. Two people with the same IQ can have radically different option sets in the same situation because one has more projections actively narrowing the field.

This connects to [[limiting-belief]] structurally. A limiting belief is a projection at the level of self-concept. "I am not good at this" forecloses options before the person ever tries. Loosening the limiting belief does not make the person better at the thing. It returns the options that were always available.

It also connects to [[untruth-in-every-thought]]: every projection contains the structural lie of "this is the whole story." Spotting the untruth opens the alternatives.

## The Connection to VIEW

The 2022 conversation makes this explicit. The qualities that loosen projections are the qualities of [[view]] applied inward and outward.

- [[impartiality]] is the willingness to not be committed to any particular reading of what is happening.
- [[wonder]] is the active expansion of the option set through asking rather than knowing.
- [[vulnerability]] is the willingness to be wrong about your read so that more information can land.
- [[empathy]] is the willingness to actually receive the other person's experience rather than substituting your projection of it.

"This is really kind of a core of how view can change our lives. Because particularly impartiality and wonder, but also vulnerability for other people to have this experience with you, with getting more information and empathy, being curious about other people's experience and being with them in it. These characteristics or these traits lead us to have a more granular awareness of reality beyond the initial assumptions that we might have had." -- Brett Kistler, Your World Is A Projection (2022)

Joe affirms this framing. VIEW is not just a communication framework. It is the practice that loosens projections in real time. The granularity of awareness it produces is what shows up later as "creative" or "innovative" or "wise."

## Why This Is Not a License To Drop All Frames

The principle does not mean stop using projections. That is not on offer. Action still requires some kind of working read on what is in front of you. The principle is closer to: hold every projection lightly enough that the option set stays larger than any single projection allows.

"You start choosing the projection that serves you best, the projection that allows for more freedom, that allows for more love, that allows for more joy, and you start choosing it. But you can't stop seeing through it." -- Joe Hudson, Your World Is A Projection (2022)

The choosing and the seeing-through happen together. The choosing lets the person act. The seeing-through keeps the option set open in case a better move appears.

This is the operational form of [[choosing-the-projection-that-serves]]: pick the most useful frame for the situation, and stay loose enough on it to switch when the situation changes.

## The Diagnostic for the Coach

When a person presents a situation as having only one or two possible responses, both bad, the situation is almost always more open than they are seeing. The constraint is in the projection, not in the situation.

Useful coaching moves at this point:
- Ask what other readings of the situation are possible (do not require the person to commit to them; just have them generate them).
- Use the brick metaphor explicitly: what if this situation were just an object you had never seen before, what are ten things you could do with it?
- Apply [[view]] to the situation: what would impartiality, vulnerability, empathy, and wonder reveal that the current projection is hiding?
- Name the projection. Once it is named, the option set opens. See [[mirror-the-judgment]] for the move when the projection is psychological.

The coach is not adding intelligence to the person. The coach is loosening the projection that is constraining the intelligence the person already has.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The same problem keeps appearing because the same two options keep being available.
- A creative person describes feeling stuck and listless when the actual block is a projection narrowing what they can see.
- An obviously intelligent person produces obviously poor results because their projection field is collapsed.
- A person describes a difficult situation in a way that contains no possibility, and the coach can hear immediately that the option set has been pre-narrowed.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone presents a situation as having only one or two possible responses, both bad.
- When [[projections]] has been introduced and the person is wondering what the practical benefit is.
- When the person is in a creative or innovation context and stuck on a narrow option set.
- When [[view]] has been taught and the person is asking what concretely changes when they use it.

- Toward [[choosing-the-projection-that-serves]]: now that more options are visible, which one serves.
- Toward [[wonder]] as the active operation that expands the field further.
- Toward [[view]] as the disciplined practice that loosens projections in real time.
- Toward [[falling-in-love-with-projections]] when the person starts hunting projections like enemies again.

## Tone

Joe presents this with a particular kind of admiration for the move and a slight deflation of the mystique around intelligence. The framing is freeing rather than diminishing. The brilliant move is available to anyone willing to loosen their projection field. It is not a fixed trait of a special class of people.

The tone shifts toward play when the brick metaphor enters. Both Joe and Brett note multiple times in the episode "I've never thought about it that way" — which is itself a small live demonstration of what the principle describes.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is not "more options is always better." Some situations call for decisive action and the rapid narrowing of options. The principle is about not being narrowed by projection when the situation does not actually require it. Discernment about when to hold the field open and when to commit is itself part of the work.

The principle is also not a route around emotional work. Loosening projections cognitively without addressing the unfelt emotion underneath produces [[zen-sickness]]: the intellectual freedom is real, but the emotional patterns continue running underneath, sometimes with reduced access. The cognitive expansion of possibility lasts only as long as the underlying emotional currents do not collapse it again.
