# The Inherent Longing for Parental Love Survives Any Abuse

## The Principle

The longing for a parent's love is structural. It is wired into the system before the system has any history. It is not extinguishable by what the parent does or fails to do. No matter how violent the father, no matter how absent the mother, no matter how thoroughly the parent failed at the job — the child's longing for that parent's love persists. It cannot be willed away. It cannot be reasoned away. It cannot be replaced with hatred, even when the hatred is justified.

This matters because the longing is one of the unfelt feelings that has to be felt for trauma to fully resolve. The configurations that get installed in childhood abuse — the endurer, the victim-recreator, the rage — are partly defenses against the longing. The longing has to be allowed before the configuration can release.

"You can't get rid of that longing. It like, it's always going to be there no matter how much of an asshole your father is. There's something inherent in us that wants a relationship with our dad or our parent. And if you can't feel that entirely, you're never going to be completely over the traumatic experience, meaning you're never going to have fully processed it." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

## What the Longing Feels Like and Why It's Hard

The longing has a specific texture. It is the wanting that lives underneath the easier emotions of anger, contempt, dismissal, and forgetting. It is the part of the child that still wants the parent to be the parent they needed, even after decades of evidence that the parent is not and will not be that.

The longing is hard for two reasons.

**It is unbearable.** Feeling the longing means feeling that what was wanted was not given, that the absence was real, that the loss is permanent (in the sense that the parent the child wanted is not coming, regardless of what the actual parent does now). This is a primary grief. The system organizes elaborate defenses against it precisely because feeling it is so much.

**It seems to contradict the protection of the configuration.** If the configuration the person has built is "fuck him, I don't care, he can't touch me" — the endurer configuration — feeling longing for that father seems to dismantle the whole protective structure. The configuration registers the longing as a threat. Feeling the longing seems like undoing the work of survival.

Both of these are why the system speed-bumps the longing. See [[speed-bumping]]. The speaker says "yeah, fuck my dad, of course you can call him an asshole" and moves on. The dismissal is not unfelt; the longing underneath the dismissal is unfelt. The speed-bumping protects the configuration by skipping past the place where the longing would arise.

## The Specific Speed-Bumping Joe Tracks in the Sean Clip

In the Sean clip, the fighter delivers a moment where he is asked, essentially, whether his father was a bad person. He says "yeah, fuck my dad," and the conversation moves on. Joe identifies this as the precise moment the longing is being speed-bumped.

"There's this moment in him where he's just like, yeah, fuck my dad. It's okay. Like, of course you can call him an asshole, like this whole thing, but he's speed bumping the emotion. You can just watch him. And when I mean speed bumping, it means like there's an emotion there that he's not allowing himself to feel, so he's going right over, which is like that longing for your dad's love, that longing for connection with your dad. You can't get rid of that longing. ... If you can't feel that entirely, you're never going to be completely over the traumatic experience." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The structural pattern: dismiss the parent → move on. The dismissal is real. The configuration is also real. The longing is underneath both, unfelt, holding the configuration in place. The work to resolve the trauma includes letting the longing be felt without the dismissal stopping it.

The signal that this speed-bumping is happening: the speaker easily and quickly dismisses the parent, often with profanity or contempt, and the conversation moves on without the body responding. The ease and the speed of the dismissal are the signal. Real anger at a parent who hurt you tends to move the body. Easy dismissal that moves on quickly often functions as the speed-bump.

## Why the Longing Doesn't Mean the Parent Was Good

A common confusion the principle addresses: feeling the longing for the abusive parent does not mean the abuse was acceptable, does not mean the parent should be reconciled with, does not mean the configuration the abuse installed was wrong to develop. The longing is structural, not evaluative. It exists because the system was wired for parental love, not because the actual parent earned it.

This matters because many people who have been through abuse refuse to let themselves feel the longing on principle. They have decided that the parent does not deserve to be longed for. They are correct that the parent does not deserve it. They are incorrect that this means the longing is not there or that feeling it would betray their adult judgment. The longing is older than the judgment. The longing is what the infant brought into the world. Feeling it is honoring what the infant needed, not what the parent earned.

The work, then, is to feel the longing fully while keeping the adult judgment intact. The longing is mine; the adult judgment about the parent is also mine; both can be true at once. See [[holding-contrary-beliefs-as-development]] for the cognitive form of this both-and capacity.

## Why the Longing Has to Be Felt for the Trauma to Resolve

The trauma configurations — the endurer's hardness, the victim-recreator's recreating, the rage, the shame, the chronic anxiety — are partly built to prevent the longing from being felt. The longing is the most unbearable piece because it is the original wound: I needed love and did not get it.

Feeling the central event (the violence, the abuse) is necessary but often insufficient. The events can be processed, the configuration can soften somewhat, and the underlying longing can remain unfelt. As long as the longing is unfelt, the configuration that was built to prevent the longing continues to operate. The adult is still organizing their life around not feeling the wanting.

When the longing is finally felt — usually in stages, usually with grief and weeping that takes its time — the configuration's purpose dissolves. There is no longer a feeling that needs to be prevented. The capacity that the configuration built can be redirected. The adult is no longer running the structure that was built to defend against a feeling that is now allowed.

This is why "I've done the trauma work and I still feel alone, I still can't ask for help, I still can't trust people" is so common. The events have been processed; the longing under the events has not. The aloneness configuration that the longing produced is still running because the longing itself has not been allowed.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The work of feeling the longing is often slow and indirect. It is not as simple as "now feel the longing for your dad." Direct instruction often triggers the configuration's defense.

What tends to work:

**Anger first.** The anger at the parent is often more accessible than the longing. Letting the anger move (in safe ways — pillows, exercise, writing, the car) opens the layer underneath. See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] and [[get-angry-in-the-car]].

**Sadness for the child.** Easier than longing for the parent is sadness for the child who needed the parent. The displacement to the child is sometimes a doorway to the original longing.

**The body's free association.** The system surfaces the longing when it is ready. The coach's job is to be present with whatever surfaces and not interrupt the longing when it arrives. See [[what-cracks-you-open-shows-whats-processable-now]].

**Welcoming what arrives without categorization.** When the longing surfaces, the speaker may not know it as longing. They may experience it as confusion, as unexpected sadness, as the sudden tenderness toward the parent that feels like a betrayal of their adult judgment. The coach's job is to welcome whatever arrives without labeling it.

**The self-love substitution.** Sometimes the longing for the parent's love is met by the person finally giving themselves the love the parent did not give. See [[love-the-worst-feeling-part-like-your-parents-should-have]] and [[welcome-the-hurt-the-way-you-wanted-as-a-kid]]. The substitution is not denial; it is the person finally meeting the original need with a present-day capacity.

## The Coaching Move When the Speed-Bumping Is Visible

When a coach sees a speaker speed-bumping the longing — quickly dismissing the parent, moving on, body intact — the move is not to force the longing. The move is to gently notice and stay available.

Possible moves:
- Stay silent for a beat after the dismissal, letting the room hold the question of what is under it.
- "I notice you moved through that pretty quickly." (Observation, not interpretation.)
- "What's underneath the 'fuck him'?" (Direct invitation, usable only when the relational ground is solid.)
- "It makes sense to be angry. And there's also something else there. I'll be here if it wants to come up." (Permission without pressure.)

None of these force the longing. They make the room safe for the longing to surface if the system is ready. If the system is not ready, the speed-bumping continues and the coach stays available for next time.

## Boundary: Not Every Speaker Will Get to the Longing

Some speakers will never feel the longing for an abusive parent. Some configurations are too tight, some defenses are too necessary, some life conditions do not provide the safety required. This is not a coaching failure. The configuration is doing what the original conditions required.

The principle is offered as the structural truth that the longing exists, not as a goal that every speaker must reach. A speaker who lives a meaningful life with the longing held back has done a lot of work. The full resolution requires the longing being felt, but partial resolution is real and valuable.

The principle is also not an argument for reconciliation with the abusive parent. Feeling the longing internally is different from contacting the parent externally. The internal work can be done without any external contact, and external contact is sometimes harmful when the parent is still abusive. See [[i-feel-guilty-for-cutting-off-family]] and [[i-want-to-reconnect-with-estranged-parents-but-im-scared]] for the relational complexity.
