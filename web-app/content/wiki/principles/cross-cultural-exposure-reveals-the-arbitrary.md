# Cross-Cultural Exposure Reveals What Is Culture and What Is Humanity

## The Principle

A culture observed without contrast looks like reality. Two cultures in contrast reveal that what looked like reality was, in part, the specific texture of one organization of human life. Three or more cultures in contrast make the relative-ness operational at the level of perception itself. The exposure does the work that argument cannot. Once a person has lived inside enough cultures to see them sitting next to each other, the distinction between "this is culture" and "this is humanity" becomes part of how the world is seen, not a conclusion that has to be reasoned to.

This is one of the structural mechanisms by which someone develops an interest in what is actually human, distinct from the cultural costumes humans wear.

## Joe's Naming

Joe articulates the principle directly when describing the effect of his international childhood (Iran, Saudi Arabia, Wales, England, Switzerland):

"You go to another culture and you see how you learn so much more about your own culture by seeing it in contrast to another one." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

And the consequence:

"And you get to see, oh, all of this stuff that's all choice. That's all culture. All of this stuff, that's humanity." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The two sentences together compress the principle. The contrast reveals that things which felt necessary turn out to be choices. The same contrast reveals what is not a choice — what remains stable across the cultural variation — which is humanity.

## Why The Contrast Is Necessary

Inside a single culture, the texture of the culture is invisible because it is the only thing the perceiver has to compare against. The way people greet each other, the way bodies move in public space, the assumed timing of meals, the relationship between adult children and parents, the way money is talked about, the way conflict is handled — all of these feel like "the way things are" because there is nothing else available for comparison.

Stepping into another culture is the first time these textures become visible. The greeting is different. The timing of meals is different. The relationship between adult children and parents is different. The perceiver suddenly has access to the original culture as a culture, rather than as reality.

Joe's specific situation amplified this. As a child, he was not visiting other cultures briefly. He was living in them. The cultures were not exotic; they were just the conditions of his life at various ages. The relative-ness of any one of them was built into his perception by the simple fact that he had been inside others.

## What This Produces

The cognitive consequence of operational cross-cultural exposure:

- A reduction in the felt necessity of the perceiver's home-culture norms
- An increased fascination with what humans have in common under the variation
- A predisposition to ask "what is human?" as a real question rather than a theoretical one
- A reduction in the moral charge around culture-specific practices in any one direction
- An increase in the ability to be in many different rooms because the assumed rules of any one room are recognized as one specific organization, not as the rules
- The early installation of the [[wants-over-shoulds]] orientation, because what is "should" varies across cultures and what does not vary turns out to be the underlying wants

The combined effect: a person whose orientation toward humans is structured around what is invariant rather than what is variable.

## The Principle Generalizes Beyond Geographic Cultures

The same dynamic applies to cross-class exposure, cross-subculture exposure, cross-generation exposure, cross-profession exposure. A person who has been only inside one socioeconomic class assumes that class's norms are reality. A person who has been inside several can see each as a specific organization. The contrast does the work.

This is part of what made Joe's twenties productive (see [[joes-experience-gathering-orientation]]). He was not only exposing himself to international cultures; he was exposing himself to American subcultures that were as different from each other as separate countries — cowboys in trailers, homeless folks riding rails, billionaires, fishery workers, the Bay Area artist community. Each subculture was a new comparison point. Each comparison point made the previously-invisible textures of the others visible.

The principle: contrast is the perceptual mechanism by which culture becomes visible as culture. Without contrast, culture is mistaken for reality.

## Implications For Coaching

This principle is relevant in several coaching situations.

**A client who is treating their cultural conditioning as if it were reality.** The first move is often to introduce contrast. Sometimes the contrast is geographic; sometimes it is class; sometimes it is subcultural. The client needs to see that what they have been treating as "the way things are" is actually one organization of things that other organizations differ from.

**A client who is in conflict with someone from a different culture, subculture, or generation.** The principle suggests that the conflict is often partly the collision of two different "this is the way things are" frames, neither of which is visible as a frame to the person inside it. Making both visible as cultures often de-charges the conflict.

**A client who is fascinated by humans across contexts.** This often signals a person who has had cross-cultural exposure of some kind in their formation. The fascination is not arbitrary; it is the natural orientation of a perception that has been calibrated by contrast.

**A young person considering whether to expose themselves to multiple cultures.** The long-arc value is real. The cognitive consequence of operational cross-cultural exposure is not replaceable by reading about other cultures. The exposure has to be lived for the perception to be calibrated.

**A parent considering whether to move children internationally.** The disruption is real; the consequence is also real. The principle does not say to move or not to move; it says that the cognitive effects of the move are durable in the kind of perception the child develops.
