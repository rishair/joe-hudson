# People Want You In It With Them, Not Giving Advice

## The Principle

When someone is in something hard, what they want is for you to be in it with them. Not above it. Not adjacent to it. Not handing them advice across a distance. In it. The advice-giving impulse, however well-intentioned, communicates the opposite of what the person needs. It communicates: I am over here on dry land where I know things, and you are over there in the water. You should listen to me about how to swim.

The person in the water does not need swimming instructions. The person in the water needs you to get in the water with them.

"People want to be -- like, people want to be -- know that people are with them in it. They don't want to be like given advice or told what to do or judged or anything like that. People want to, oh, like you're here in this with me." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

This is a basic principle that scales across every relationship. Kids want it. Teenagers want it. Grieving people want it. Coaching clients want it. Romantic partners want it. The impulse to advise is almost always the failure to be present, dressed up in helpfulness.

## Why Advice Lands as Distance

Advice is a different relational stance from presence. To give advice you have to step out of the experience the other person is in, take a position above it, and report from there. The structure of advice-giving creates distance even when the content of the advice is correct.

The person in the difficulty registers the structural distance more clearly than they register the content. They feel: you are not with me. You are watching me. You are evaluating me. You are using your knowledge to organize my experience for me. The advice-giver imagines they are being helpful. The receiver experiences being alone in the difficulty with someone narrating it from outside.

This is why the same correct information lands completely differently depending on whether it arrives as advice or as shared experience. "Have you tried..." spoken across the table lands as a tax. The same observation offered while sitting in the difficulty alongside the person lands as connection.

## The Parenting Application: Why Teenagers Shut Down

The most legible domain for this principle is parenting older kids. Teenagers are constantly being given advice by their parents. The parents are usually right. The teenagers shut down anyway.

"I see that a lot with teenagers. Like I see a lot of teenagers shut down to their parents because the parent isn't in it with them. The parent is telling them what to do. The parent is giving them advice. The parent is worried about their choices. The parent is trying to control what they're doing through influence or whatever, instead of just like, oh yeah, fuck, that sucks that like all your friends have just like gone to a party without you." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The contrast in that quote is exact. "Have you considered why your friends are excluding you" is advice. "Fuck, that sucks" is being in it with them. The teenager who hears the first goes silent. The teenager who hears the second stays in the conversation.

Joe describes watching this on a hike: two college students were talking, and the adults nearby started talking to them like "I know something." The "I know something" tone is the failure mode. The students did not want what the adults were offering. They wanted to be heard.

"When you were a teenager, you didn't want a whole bunch of advice from your parents unless you asked." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The principle works in reverse, too: a teenager who asks for advice is asking. The asking changes the structure. Advice given in response to being asked is not coming from above; it is coming from the same level, in response to a real request. The damage is in the unsolicited advice, particularly the kind that comes with "I told you so" or "have you done that yet?"

## Why This Is So Hard for Parents

Parents have information their kids do not have. They have lived longer, made the mistakes, watched the consequences. The impulse to share that information so the kid does not have to repeat the mistake is strong, and the love behind it is real.

The problem is not the information. The problem is the position from which it gets delivered. The parent who has not done the work to be with the kid in the difficulty defaults to delivering the information from outside the difficulty. The kid receives a lecture rather than company. The information does not land because the kid is busy bracing against the position the parent is in.

The fix is not to withhold the information. The fix is to be in it with the kid first. To feel what the kid is feeling, to be present to the actual difficulty as the kid is experiencing it. From inside that, if information is useful, it can be offered. From outside it, even useful information becomes a problem.

## The Application Beyond Kids

The principle is not parent-specific. It is the same principle that shows up in grief ceremonies that work versus grief ceremonies that fail (see [[dont-comfort-the-mourner-from-your-own-discomfort]] and [[host-a-grief-ceremony-as-community]]). The mourners do not want to be told it will be okay. They want to be in it with people who are also in it.

It is also the structural answer to why most leadership advice fails. The CEO who lectures the team from above does not move the team. The CEO who is in the difficulty alongside the team does. The lecture and the presence transmit different things regardless of what is said.

It is the same principle behind [[empathy]]. Joe defines empathy as "being with somebody's experience without losing yourself in it." Not watching the experience. Not trying to change it. Being with them in it. The "being with them in it" is the move. Advice is what people do when they cannot be with them in it.

## The Diagnostic

The diagnostic is whether you can name what the person is actually feeling right now without trying to change it. If you can sit with them and say "yeah, that sucks" or "that's hard" or "I can see you're in it," and let the conversation stay there, you are in it with them. If you find yourself immediately reaching for what they should do, what they could try, how they could think about it, you have stepped out of the experience and into the advisor position.

The reach for advice is rarely about the person's good. It is usually about the advisor's discomfort with being in the difficulty. Sitting with someone in their hardship is hard. Giving advice is easier because it lets the advisor feel useful without having to feel the hardship. The advice is the exit door from the experience the person needed company in.

## The Reverse Diagnostic for the Receiver

The same principle, read from the receiver's side, gives a diagnostic for whether you are being met. If the people in your life respond to your difficulty with advice and instruction, you are not being met. You are being managed. Many adults who report loneliness despite having friends and family are reporting this exact thing. They have people who will advise them. They do not have people who will be in it with them.

The desire for the latter is often misread as a desire for the former. "I need help" gets answered with help when what was being asked for was company. The miss compounds over time into a felt sense that no one is really there.

## What This Looks Like When It Works

The kid asks for advice. Joe sits with them in the difficulty first. He notices what they are actually feeling. He does not move to solve the problem until they are felt as a person, not as a problem-to-be-fixed. When advice arrives, it arrives from inside the experience, not from above it. The kid takes the advice or does not; either way, they have been with their parent in the difficulty, and the relationship is intact.

Joe says: "I'm in view with my kids a lot." See [[view]]. The four-part orientation -- vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder -- is the operational definition of being with someone in their experience. The parent who is in VIEW with the kid is, by definition, not advising from above. The parent who is in advising mode has stepped out of VIEW.

## The Mistake of "Helpful Advice"

The category of "helpful advice" can be a kind of cover. Almost everyone giving unsolicited advice believes their advice is helpful. The receiver almost never experiences it that way. The gap between the giver's intention and the receiver's experience is the principle in action. The intention is help. The structure is distance. The structure wins.

A small set of conditions makes advice land well:

1. The person asked for it
2. The advice-giver has been with them in the experience first
3. The advice is offered, not delivered -- the receiver can take it or leave it without consequence
4. The advice-giver's tone is from-the-same-level, not from-above
5. The advice is responsive to what the person actually said, not to what the advice-giver thinks they should have said

Most "helpful advice" fails at least three of these. The principle says: when in doubt, do not advise. Be in it with them. If they want advice, they will ask. The asking changes the structure.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A teenager who has stopped talking to their parents and the parent does not understand why
- A grieving person who is exhausted by people telling them they're sorry for their loss
- A coaching client whose previous coach was always offering interventions and never sat with them in the difficulty
- A coachee who finds themselves wishing their partner would stop solving the problem and just sit with them
- A leader whose team has stopped bringing them the real issues because every issue gets advice attached to it
- A coachee who realizes their own advice-giving habit is part of why they are lonely

## Tone

Direct and conversational. Joe says "fuck, that sucks that like all your friends have just like gone to a party without you" as an example of what being in it with a teenager sounds like. The principle is not delivered with reverence. It is delivered with the casualness of someone who has watched the failure mode happen a thousand times. Use the same tone: matter-of-fact, with the trust that the listener will recognize the pattern in their own life. Do not preach this. Describe it.
