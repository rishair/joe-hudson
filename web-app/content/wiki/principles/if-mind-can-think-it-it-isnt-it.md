# If Your Mind Can Think of It, It Isn't It

## The Principle

The mind constructs a picture of what awakening will be. That picture is always wrong. Not slightly wrong — categorically wrong. Anything the mind can imagine awakening to be is not awakening, because awakening is precisely the recognition that the mind that does the imagining is not the I it has been organizing experience around.

"It isn't what you think it is. If your mind can think of it, it isn't it." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The principle is not a poetic flourish. It is structural. The mind operates within the very identification that awakening sees through. The mind's pictures of awakening are made out of the material that awakening dissolves. Any imagined version is therefore part of what is to be seen through, not a description of what is being seen.

## Why the Mind Always Gets It Wrong

The mind constructs pictures by reference to past experience. It imagines awakening as a more-of-something or a less-of-something — more peace, less suffering, more bliss, less fear, more wisdom, less confusion. The picture is always a variation on the mind's existing categories.

The recognition that awakening is doesn't fit those categories. It isn't more of something. It isn't a state at all — see [[awakening-is-stateless-state]]. The categories the mind uses to imagine it are the very categories the recognition dissolves.

The simultaneous-opposites nature also breaks the imagining. The actual recognition is "way bigger than you could ever imagine and way smaller and more insignificant than you could ever imagine simultaneously." The mind cannot hold both at once. The recognition holds both as the same thing.

"It is without a doubt way bigger than you could ever imagine and way smaller and more insignificant than you could ever imagine simultaneously. And I remember listening to teachers talk about that and being like, 'Oh, shut up. Give me something concrete.' But it is without a doubt something that is really hard to describe." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The mind that hears "way bigger and way smaller simultaneously" cannot accept this as a useful description because the mind operates in categories that have to be one or the other. The recognition operates in a register where the categorical disjunction is not a constraint.

## Why Teachers Frustrate Their Students With This

Most teachers in non-dual traditions are familiar with the frustrated student who wants a concrete description of the destination. Joe was that student. The teacher says something like "it is without a doubt way bigger than you could ever imagine and way smaller and more insignificant than you could ever imagine," and the student wants to throw something.

The teachers are not being cute. They are being accurate. Giving a concrete description would install a picture in the student's mind that would then function as a target. The target would not be the recognition. It would be a mental construct shaped like the recognition. Aiming at the construct would actively prevent the recognition from landing, because the aiming is the activity of the mind that is to be seen through.

"I remember listening to teachers talk about that and being like, 'Oh, shut up. Give me something concrete.' But it is without a doubt something that is really hard to describe." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The non-description is the teaching. It refuses to give the mind a target so that the recognition has a chance to arrive when the targeting has quieted.

## What the Words Are Good For

Words pointing at awakening are not useless. They are pointers, not contents. The phrase "I am infinitely you, I am everything and nothing in the silent vastness that everything arises in" (see [[self-realization]]) does not contain the recognition. It points at the territory where the recognition is found. The same is true of all the descriptions: "stateless state," "non-duality," "self as awareness," "Christ consciousness," "everything is one."

Each phrase points. The student who tries to inhabit the phrase as the destination misses the recognition. The student who uses the phrase as a finger pointing at the moon and looks at the moon instead of the finger can sometimes find the recognition.

## Why the Recognition Is Often Unrecognized When It Lands

A direct consequence of this principle: when the recognition lands, the person often does not know they have had it. The picture they were holding does not match the actual phenomenology. The recognition arrives in an ordinary moment, sometimes without dramatic markers, and the person dismisses it because it does not match what they thought they were waiting for.

Joe's own landing of the recognition came in a bathroom while peeing, with the thought "I'm that." He did not register that the awakening had happened until months later, in a conversation with someone else describing the same experience. See [[joes-bathroom-realization]]. The chase-self is poorly equipped to identify the very thing it has been chasing because the chase-self's picture of the recognition is not what the recognition is.

This is also why the recognition often arrives only after the chasing has quieted. The chase actively installs the picture that prevents the recognition from being seen. When the chase quiets, the picture relaxes, and the recognition (which may have been there) becomes visible.

## The Implication for Practice

The practice cannot be organized around achieving the recognition. Any organization toward the recognition installs a target made of the wrong material. The practice has to be organized around something else — welcoming, presence, enjoyment, vulnerability, impartiality — that has a different relationship to the mind's targeting.

This is why Joe's work does not teach awakening directly. The teaching of the practice is in different terms (welcoming, embodiment, emotional fluidity), and the recognition often arrives as a side effect of the practice working on what the practice is actually working on. See [[awakening-as-natural-development]].

## Coaching Use

Three places this principle becomes operationally useful:

**With someone who is chasing the state.** When the picture they are chasing comes up, name that the picture is not the thing. Anything they can imagine is not it. This does not stop the chase, but it can introduce a wedge of doubt about whether the picture they are holding is worth the chase.

**With someone who has had an opening and is dismissing it.** "But it wasn't dramatic enough" or "I didn't feel what I was supposed to feel" — both are versions of the picture being held alongside the recognition and the recognition being measured against the picture. Name that the picture is not the standard.

**With someone trying to understand awakening intellectually.** The intellectual project is structurally limited. Not because the person is not smart enough, but because the recognition is not made of the material the intellect is made of. Acknowledge the limit, allow the intellectual exploration to continue, and add the pointer that the recognition does not land through understanding.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with a touch of self-deprecation. He remembers being the frustrated student and the teachers refusing to give him something concrete. He is now in the position of having to refuse to give his own listeners something concrete. The tone acknowledges the frustration without yielding to it. The non-description is the only honest description available.
