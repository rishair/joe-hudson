# Wrestle With Your Principles (The Wrestling Matters More Than the Principles)

## The Principle

A set of principles is not a deliverable. It is an ongoing inquiry. The power of having principles is less about which principles you have and more about the continuous process of testing, refining, and re-evaluating them.

"The power, like I said, the power of having principles is less about the principles you have and more about the process. And I think that this is the thing that gets people all the time is that they, whether they're CEOs or they're creating their own personal principles, it's like, I've got them and that's it. And then what happens is like, you know, 3 years later, they've completely forgotten their principles and they're not living them." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The principle is named for what Joe attributes to Steve Jobs: one of Jobs' principles was "wrestle with your principles." The job is to continually wrestle with them.

## Why the Wrestling Matters More Than the Conclusion

A principle is a thesis. A thesis is held provisionally. Without the wrestling, the principle hardens into dogma or fades from memory. Either way, it stops doing work.

"It is a process of learning. It is not a process of knowing." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

Two failure modes:

**The CEO who locks in.** The principles are decided once, in a retreat, and never revisited. Three years later, the team cannot recite them. The principles still exist on a slide deck but have no operational presence. They are no longer principles; they are artifacts.

**The CEO who never decides.** The principles are constantly under review, never settled enough to act on. The wrestling becomes a substitute for the operating. Nothing gets used long enough to test it.

The right rhythm: decide on a small set, live by them, run experiments, return to them at regular intervals to refine. The wrestling is paced -- it is neither one-time nor constant -- and the cadence is what keeps the principles alive.

## The Iteration Applies to the Principles Themselves

This principle is recursive. "Everything is an iteration" is one of Joe's own current five principles, and it applies to the set of principles too.

"Yeah. So I'm constantly -- the power, like I said, the power of having principles is less about the principles you have and more about the process... So for me, it's the same thing. It's everything is an iteration that each of these things, I'm -- it's a process of learning. It is not a process of knowing." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The recursive quality matters: a person who treats their principles as final has violated one of the principles by treating it as final. The framework eats itself.

## Joe's Own Current Five Principles (As of 2023)

Joe lives by five principles that have been mostly stable for about five years. They are an example of what wrestled-with principles look like over time, not a prescription.

1. **Connection, connection, connection.** Start in connection, stay in connection, end in connection. See [[connection-as-life-bottom-line]].

2. **Embrace intensity.** Move toward the avoided thing -- the difficult conversation, the unfelt emotion, the trigger, the gut punch. See [[embrace-intensity]].

3. **Everything is an iteration.** No finish line. No shame about getting it wrong. Constant learning. The principle that allows all the others to keep evolving.

4. **Wonder!** Earlier versions were "What are you curious about right now?" and "What's the question?" The current form is just "wonder" with an exclamation point. The evolution is significant: wonder does not require an answer, only openness and awe. See [[wonder]] and [[wonder-vs-curiosity]].

5. **Loving accountability.** Hold accountability without shame. See [[five-principles-of-the-path]].

Only the wonder principle has shifted in the last five years. Even with that level of stability, Joe describes himself as "constantly thinking about them." The stability is what stability looks like after years of wrestling, not absence of wrestling.

## A Second Worked Example: Ona's "We, Not Me" Iteration

Johannes Langgraf's company iterated a principle in real time, providing a worked example of what wrestling produces at the company level. The original principle was "We, Not Me," which pointed at collective orientation and explicitly framed ego as not particularly helpful. The team noticed a side effect: people were not showing up fully empowered. The principle had over-corrected the individual orientation that empowerment requires.

The iteration retained the underlying intent (connection with each other) and added two directions the original had missed (connection with self, connection with customers). The new principle: "Connect, Connect, Connect."

"What it also did was that people did not show up fully empowered. Because yes, it's also about yourself. It's not only about others. It's like about yourself. It's about having end-to-end ownership of things, right? Really, really, you know, driving those results and thinking also what you want as a person. So we changed that and iterated that to Connect, Connect, Connect, which keeps the, you know, connection part and both with our customers and similarly connecting with each other, but also, and we spoke about that previously, connecting with yourself." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles

The example illustrates several features of wrestling-with-principles at scale: side effects are the diagnostic; the replacement principle can be more compressive than the original; verb-form principles travel further than descriptive ones. See [[we-not-me-iterated-to-connect-connect-connect]] for the full treatment of this example and [[open-the-drawer-as-shared-verb]] for the related verb-form upgrade in the same company.

The Ona iteration also illustrates the boomerang effect: a phrase that works at the company-of-the-coachee level can return into the coach's own teaching. Joe's company adopted "Open the Drawer" from watching Ona's team use the phrase in meetings; the original Joe-taught principle had been "Embrace Intensity." The principle iteration moves in both directions when the wrestling is happening at both ends.

## The Wonder Evolution as an Example of Wrestling

The trajectory of the wonder principle illustrates what wrestling produces.

- **First iteration:** "What am I curious about right now?" Pointed at looking for the unknown, keeping a learner's mindset.
- **Second iteration:** "What's the question?" Pointed at staying in the inquiry rather than rushing to answers.
- **Current iteration:** "Wonder!" Open awe without needing an answer.

Each iteration was a refinement based on what Joe noticed about how the previous one operated in his life. The current iteration is stronger because curiosity, in his experience, still required an answer at the end. Wonder does not.

"I find that wonder is a stronger force. It doesn't require me looking for an answer. It's the openness and it's the awe without the needing of an answer, which seems to drive much better decision-making, problem-solving than trying to get to the answer. That try and disconnects me." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The wrestling is what produced this. A person who had locked in "what am I curious about" twenty years ago would still be operating from a principle that was right for them in their twenties.

## The Daily Cadence of Wrestling

Wrestling is not an annual retreat exercise. It is daily experimentation.

"Every day you do an experiment. Every day, maybe 2 or 3 times a day you do an experiment. How did that work? What worked for me? Does it work overall? Because there's no principle that you're going to have that's going to feel great all the time." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The experimental orientation produces a relationship to principles that does not exist when they are treated as fixed. The principle becomes something you are in conversation with rather than something you are obeying.

## What Wrestling Looks Like in an Organization

In a company, wrestling is collective. Joe describes companies of 1,200 people where everyone runs experiments with the principles on a shared channel, where the executive team revisits them quarterly, where the CEO holds the final say on refinements but everyone contributes evidence.

The wrestling at scale requires infrastructure: shared channels for sharing experiments, regular cadences for re-evaluation, a CEO who treats the principles as their own ongoing inquiry rather than as a finished mandate. See [[rollout-principles-in-organization]] for the operational details.

The failure mode at scale is the same as at the individual level: principles get announced, the experimentation phase is skipped, the principles become wall decorations within a quarter. See [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]] for what fills the vacuum.

## What Happens When Wrestling Stops

When the wrestling stops, the person or organization reverts to operating on unconscious principles -- which were running everything before the conscious principles were articulated.

"What I notice is what they do is they just don't live by the principles and then they're back into the old patterns. And so, they don't make decisions quickly, gracefully, or I would say with a better result." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The old patterns were principles too. They were just unconscious ones. See [[unconscious-principles-run-your-life]]. The work of articulating conscious principles is undone when the wrestling stops, because the unconscious principles never went away. They just stopped being challenged.

## Boundary Conditions

Wrestling is not constant doubt. A person who is wrestling with their principle of connection by considering disconnection every morning has misunderstood the wrestling. The principle holds. The wrestling is at the edges: where the principle is unclear, where its application is uncertain, where its current form is producing surprising results.

The wrestling is also not promiscuous principle-collecting. Adding a sixth, seventh, or tenth principle dilutes the set. Joe is explicit: keep it to about five. The wrestling refines the existing five, occasionally retires one, occasionally adds one, but does not turn the principles into a long list of preferences.
