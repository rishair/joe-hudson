# Not Forgiving Doesn't Actually Protect Anyone

## The Principle

The most common reason people hold onto blame, resentment, or the refusal to forgive is that they believe the held charge is protecting themselves or others from future harm. The principle: it is not. The other person continues to exist and to act as they will. Not forgiving them does not change their behavior. The protection is structural (boundaries, action, communication) or it is illusion.

"The truth of the matter is that guy's going to go into another company whether we like it or not. Just because we're not forgiving him, that doesn't change his behavior. He hasn't — we haven't — unless we put him behind bars, but he can actually go and he's free to work where — and go off into other communities, even if we exile him from our own. So we haven't truly protected anybody. And we're just holding this false idea of not forgiving so that we can think we're protecting something we're not actually protecting." -- Tara Howley, I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

## What the Held Charge Is Doing

The held charge is doing something — just not the thing the holder thinks it is doing. It is:

- **Doing the substitute-boundary job.** See [[resentment-takes-the-place-of-boundaries]]. The body is holding the charge because no actual boundary has been drawn.
- **Holding the relationship alive in absence.** The charged frame keeps the person present in the holder's daily experience.
- **Confirming an identity.** "I am the kind of person who would never let this happen." The identity needs the held grievance to keep existing.
- **Avoiding an emotion.** Often grief or fear underneath. The charge functions as a wall between the holder and what would otherwise be felt.

What the held charge is not doing: changing the other person's behavior, preventing the other person from harming someone else, providing actual protection.

## What Actually Protects

Protection comes from one of three places, none of which require held resentment:

1. **A drawn boundary.** "I will not be alone in a room with this person." "I will not work with this person." "I will not be in this situation again." The boundary is the structural protection. See [[boundaries]].

2. **Communicated information.** Telling people who need to know what happened so they can make their own decisions. The communication is the protection. The other party may use it or not — that is their responsibility.

3. **Action against the harm.** Legal action, advocacy, organizing, removal of the person from a position of power. The action is the protection.

"What do I have to feel good about at the end of the day to keep my heart open to myself and others?" -- Tara Howley

Each of these is compatible with forgiveness in the [[forgiveness-as-self-care]] sense. None of them require the holder to maintain the bad-person frame in their own body.

## The Sales Manager Example

Tara recounts a situation Joe and AOA have observed: a person at a company hired a sales guy who had previously caused harm at another company. They warned the CEO, the head of sales, and others. Leadership did not act. The sales guy then "cooked the books" and damaged the company's IPO.

"That person had warned leadership and they hadn't taken action. So he felt totally clear and like, I spoke to them, I told them what I saw and what I'd seen... What they choose to do with it is theirs. And so the boundary just is me sharing, not their action or reaction." -- Tara Howley

The example illustrates the structural truth: the actual protection move was communicating the information. After that, the other parties had the data and could act or not. The person who warned had done what was theirs to do. Held resentment toward the sales guy would not have changed anything.

The principle in the example: the boundary is what you communicate or what you do, not the charge you sustain in your body about the other person.

## Why This Releases the Refusal to Forgive

The held refusal to forgive often persists because the holder believes it is doing work. Once the principle lands — the held charge is not actually protecting anyone — the work-justification for the refusal collapses. There is nothing keeping the charge in place except the body's inertia.

This is often the moment forgiveness becomes possible. Not because the holder has been argued into virtue, but because the holder sees the refusal was never protective in the way they had been telling themselves it was. The body releases what it had been holding under false pretense.

The release is structural, not moral. It is not "I should forgive." It is "this thing I have been doing was not doing what I thought it was doing."

## The Substitution Move

When the principle lands, the question becomes: what would actually protect? The substitution:

- Was holding the charge → Drawing the boundary
- Was wanting to make them bad → Communicating what happened to those who need to know
- Was waiting for them to be punished → Taking action where action is available
- Was sustaining the rage → Trusting that the structural protection (boundary, communication, action) is doing the job

Each substitution moves protection from a costly internal posture to a less costly external structure. The body is relieved of holding what the structure can hold instead.

## When Behind Bars Is Appropriate

The exception case: physical danger that requires containment. In rare cases, the protection actually requires removing the person from being able to act — which means legal action, criminal proceedings, prison.

"Sometimes that's actually 100% appropriate. But even then, we can forgive, right? That's the boundary behind bars. If they've done something that's literally breaking a law, injuring, physically injuring other people. We can — that might be the boundary." -- Tara Howley

Even in this case, the principle holds. The structural protection (incarceration) is doing the protection job. The internal forgiveness work in the holder is separable from the external action. The grandmother forging a relationship with her grandchild's murderer (a story Tara references) — that is the forgiveness work happening while the structural protection (prison) is also in place.

The two are not in tension. The external action is the protection; the internal forgiveness is the heart-work for the holder.

## Implications for the Coach

When a person says "I can't forgive them because they might hurt someone else," the question is: "what actually protects from them hurting someone else?" The answer is rarely "my refusal to forgive." The answer is usually some combination of: communicate to those who need to know, take available action, draw your own boundary, and let go of the parts that are not yours to control.

Once the actual protection moves are identified and (where possible) acted on, the refusal to forgive often releases. The body discovers that the protection job is being done elsewhere and no longer needs to be carried in the form of held charge.

If the person says "but what if they do hurt someone else and I haven't kept holding the wrongness?" the answer is structural: whether they hurt someone else has nothing to do with whether you maintain a frame about them. The frame is in your body. Their behavior is in their body. The two are not connected.

## The Larger Principle

This is a specific case of a more general truth: internal states do not have causal power over the external world in the ways people often assume. Worrying does not prevent. Held resentment does not protect. Wishing does not produce. The world responds to action, communication, and structural arrangement — not to the emotional posture the wisher holds.

The release of false-causal beliefs about internal states is part of the work of any contemplative tradition. This principle is its application to the specific case of forgiveness. The held charge feels like it is doing something. It is not — at least not the thing the holder thinks it is.

## Tone

Plain. The principle is offered as a structural observation, not a moral instruction. It does not say "you should forgive." It says "the reason you are not forgiving is not actually true."
