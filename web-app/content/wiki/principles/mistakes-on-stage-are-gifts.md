# Mistakes On Stage Are Gifts (Pray For The Fuck-Up)

## The Principle

In any live performance -- a stage show, a board pitch, a fundraising talk, a team meeting, a podcast, a class -- a mistake is going to happen. Forgetting a line, mixing up a word, going blank on the next slide, the set falling over, the laptop dying mid-deck. The mistake is not the failure of the performance. The mistake is the doorway to the realest moment of the performance.

The principle: prepare for the mistake. Welcome it when it arrives. Treat it as a gift. Pray for it as bonus points.

"On stage when something happens or an actor forgets their lines, it's a gift. And I think that happens too when speakers are speaking and they forget where they were. It's a gift they're giving the audience of like hanging in the unknown, like what's gonna happen here? Ah! They made it. Okay. Oh, they didn't make it. What's gonna happen next? And we all get to sit in the unknown together." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

The principle is operational. It changes how the performer prepares, how they respond when the mistake arrives, and what they take away after.

## Why It Works

The audience is not there for the perfection. The audience is there for the human. Perfection -- when it actually happens -- is impressive but cool. It does not let people in. The slight imperfection is the doorway. The dropped line, the laugh at the slip, the visible response to something going wrong -- this is where the audience sees a person rather than a performance. The person is what they came for.

"People who might invest in your company or your team, we want to connect with each other's humanity, not some bigger-than-life perfect image. We want to connect with reality and humanity. And so when I blubber, I always mix up my words and I go, there I go mixing up my words again. I just call it and name it and it de-shames it for me. And it's like, oh, look, I can laugh at myself. We don't call this company the Art of Perfection. It's the Art of Accomplishment. Like, you're going to be highly imperfect." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

Joe's reflection on this in the same conversation: when a co-presenter forgets things or misspeaks, "it felt like a permission to just be human with you. Of like, 'Oh, that doesn't need to even be perceived as a failure. It's just another piece of what's in the room.'"

The structural mechanism: the mistake forces presence. It forces the performer to actually be here, in this room, dealing with what just happened, rather than executing a memorized chain. The presence the performer was trying to manufacture through preparation arrives for free in the moment of the mistake. The mistake is not the obstacle to presence. The mistake is the route to it.

This is the live-performance form of [[failure-is-an-idea]] and [[iterative-mindset]] -- in the live arena, the iteration happens in real time, and the audience is part of the iteration.

## The Three Stages of Working With Mistakes

**Before the performance: expect the mistake.** Prepare for it the way you prepare for the rest. Hold the content as a tree, not a chain -- see [[chain-vs-tree-thinking-for-content]] -- so that any one branch falling off does not collapse the whole. Internally permission: "this could all go to shit, and that's okay." The permission is what releases the grip that produces the freeze.

"This could be a dumpster fire. We actually really invite the chaos. To permission ourselves, like, this could go all to hell, and that's okay." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

Note the careful distinction Joe draws: this is not inviting the dumpster fire, not wanting it to happen. It is signing up for whatever happens, trusting that when the blip arrives there can be magic in it. The permission is at the meta-level, not the wish-level.

**During the performance: name it and breathe.** When the mistake arrives, the instinct is to hold the breath and beat yourself up internally. That instinct deepens the hole. The body locks; the next thought does not come; the moment compounds. The alternative: breathe, feel the body, name what just happened, often with humor.

"What often people do is they'll forget a line or forget where they were in a speech or what they wanted to say next, and then they hold their breath and start beating themself up. When you're stuck in the mud, the tires start spinning. The more you hold your breath, the more you're just digging yourself into the hole." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

The naming move can be as simple as "there I go mixing up my words again" or "let me start that one over." It can be silent: "okay, that just happened, I'm just going to breathe." Either way, the move is to acknowledge the mistake rather than pretend it did not happen. The acknowledgment is what de-shames it. The audience is already with the performer; the only question is whether the performer joins them or leaves the room internally.

**After the performance: celebrate the mistakes alongside the wins.** The post-stage reflection is where the next performance is built. If the only thing celebrated is what went right, the performer learns to fear what went wrong, which makes the next stage fright worse. If the mistakes are also welcomed and named, the performer accumulates evidence that mistakes are not catastrophes. The next stage is met with more capacity. See [[celebrate-failures-publicly]] for the team-level version of this practice.

## The Canonical Example

Tara's King-and-I-on-Mount-Tam story is the compressed form of this principle:

"I saw a performance of The King and I on Mount Tam, and it was windy, and the set started falling over. And the actor playing the king, he's supposed to be an asshole, and he just started yelling at everyone to, 'Get the steps! Get those back up!' And this was 20 years ago. I've never forgotten that moment. It was like one of the greatest moments in theater ever. Because something real happened, and he had to respond in real time. And it was like the whole audience was like, 'Oh, shit, what's gonna happen?' And then he pulled that out. He's like, 'I'm the king!' He's like barking at everybody. It was genius. And that's available to us when we're facilitating a retreat, when we're leading a meeting, when we're making a presentation. I used to almost say, pray for an accident, like pray for a fuck-up. It's a gift." -- Tara Howley, What To Do About Stage Fright

The story holds the whole principle. The mistake (the set falling over) became the most-remembered moment of the entire performance. The actor did not pretend the set was not falling. He used the role he was already in to respond to the real thing happening in real time. The character became more present because the situation became more real.

The take-away the audience carries 20 years later is not the rehearsed scenes. It is the moment the play met the wind.

## What This Principle Is Not

- This is not an invitation to be sloppy. The performer still prepares. The mistake is not an excuse for skipping preparation; it is what makes preparation worth doing. A well-prepared performer can respond to a mistake; an unprepared one cannot. See [[nervous-system-prep-before-high-stakes]] for the prep that this principle assumes.
- This is not the wish for the dumpster fire. The performer does not want the set to fall over. The performer signs up for what happens. The wish-for-disaster reading is the misreading.
- This is not "every mistake is a gift." Some mistakes are real costs that need fixing. The principle applies to the in-the-moment mistakes that are inevitable in live performance -- the dropped line, the technical hiccup, the missed cue. Not to the avoidable mistakes that come from negligence.
- This is not a license to perform without practice. The performer who has only "pray for the mistake" without the underlying craft will deliver the mistake without the magic. The magic comes from the meeting of preparation and presence.

## The Forgetting-The-Word Live Example

Tara demonstrates the principle live in the same conversation, mid-sentence:

"It can actually lead to the beautiful moments, the precious moments. I saw a perfect -- I saw a -- Oh, there I go doing it again."

Joe responds: "Nice."

The moment is the move. She blanks on the word, names the blanking, and the room receives it without anyone losing the thread. The same teaching is happening at the meta-level: the speaker who is teaching about mistakes-as-gifts is making a mistake while teaching it and demonstrating the move in the same breath.

The pattern that lets this work: the speaker has so internalized that the mistake is not a problem that the mistake does not produce the freeze. The body does not lock. The breath continues. The naming happens. The room laughs and the teaching continues. This is what the principle looks like when it is operating, not just stated.
