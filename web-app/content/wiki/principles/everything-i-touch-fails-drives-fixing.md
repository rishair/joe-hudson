# Everything I Touch Fails Is the Driver of All the Fixing

## The Principle

The compulsive fixing pattern — fixing the self, fixing others, fixing the world — runs on a chronic underneath-feeling that everything the person touches fails. The fixing is not the source of the trouble. The failure-feeling is. The fixing is what the system generates to outrun the failure-feeling.

This is why the fixing never produces relief. Each act of fixing temporarily quiets the feeling and then immediately recharges it, because the fixing presupposes there is something broken. The presupposition is the feeling expressing itself. Stopping the fixing does not stop the feeling. Locating the feeling and being with it is what does.

"That's actually the driver of all the fixing — the feeling of that everything I touch fails. Yeah, but you being in that feeling, it's like, how about that? How about you just be in that feeling with me of everything that I touch fails, and I'll just be with you in that, and we'll hang out?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

## The Mechanism

The failure-feeling is a chronic low-grade signal. It does not arrive as a discrete emotion that can be felt and released. It is a sustained background hum that says "this is going to fail / this is failing / I am failing." The person rarely names it because it has been there too long; it has the texture of identity rather than the texture of emotion.

The fixing is the response. The body, registering the failure-signal, generates activity. Activity feels like progress. Progress feels like the opposite of failure. The system mistakes the activity for the relief.

But the activity does not reach the signal. The signal is not in the world that is being fixed; the signal is in the body of the person fixing. So the activity has to be sustained continuously to keep the signal at bay. The moment the activity stops, the signal returns. The person experiences the return as urgency to fix the next thing.

The result: a life of compulsive fixing, where each act of fixing produces brief quiet followed by the return of the failure-feeling, which produces the next act. The person feels exhausted and ineffective even when they have accomplished a great deal, because the accomplishments were never going to address what the system was actually trying to fix.

## Why "Everything I Touch Fails" Survives Contradictory Evidence

A person operating under this feeling will continue to feel like everything they touch fails even when, by any external measure, they have succeeded at many things. The feeling does not respond to evidence. It is not a belief about the past; it is a sensation in the present.

When someone presents with this driver, attempting to argue with the feeling ("but you've done all these things successfully") will not move it. The feeling is structural, not propositional. The person can know intellectually that they have succeeded and still feel that everything they touch fails. The two reports come from different faculties.

This is the same structural feature as [[self-judgment-protects-from-emotion]]: the judgment-of-self does work regardless of the truth-content of the judgment. The work the feeling is doing is to generate the fix-activity. As long as the system needs that activity, the feeling will be maintained.

## What the Failure-Feeling Is Often Protecting

The failure-feeling is doing a structural job. It is the engine of the activity that prevents the person from arriving at what is underneath the activity. Removing the activity (or even imagining removing it) reveals the territory the activity has been holding off.

Often the territory is one of:
- **Silence and emptiness.** The activity has been protecting against having nothing to do. The nothingness is intolerable because the person has not built capacity to be in it. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]].
- **The grief of not being able to fix what cannot be fixed.** The activity has been protecting against the heartbreak of recognizing that some things genuinely cannot be made right by effort.
- **The shame of being a person who needs to be doing something to feel valuable.** The activity has been protecting against the recognition that the value-from-doing was always a substitute for the value-from-being.
- **The aliveness of present-moment experience.** The activity has been protecting against the vulnerability of being here, of being met, of being seen without producing anything.

The discrimination matters because the work is not to remove the failure-feeling. The work is to find what the failure-feeling has been protecting against and be with that. The failure-feeling releases on its own when the territory underneath it is no longer being avoided.

## The Coaching Move

When the failure-feeling surfaces, do not engage the content of the failure ("what specifically have you failed at?"). The content is the cover. The work is to sit with the feeling itself.

"How about you just be in that feeling with me of everything that I touch fails, and I'll just be with you in that, and we'll hang out?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The instruction is: stop the activity, stay with the feeling, do it together. The "with me" is the load-bearing piece. The person has been alone with this feeling for years; the fixing has been the substitute for not having to be with it alone. Being with it together with the coach is what the activity has been substituting for.

The being-with does not "process" the feeling in the sense of working through it to a resolution. It just acknowledges the feeling. The feeling has been alone; now it is not. The feeling does not need to be fixed (the fixing-of-the-feeling would be the same pattern reapplied). It needs to be met.

## Why the Feeling Is Often Surprised by Itself

When the failure-feeling is finally named, the person often experiences a small recognition-shock: "yes, that's the feeling, I've been carrying that forever." The shock is real. The feeling has been operating underneath awareness, doing its work without being seen. Being seen is itself a shift in its situation.

In the source session, the woman immediately confirmed the naming: "Yeah, I want it— I want it to be right, and I don't know how to get there, and so I feel like I'm failing at it over and over again." The fluency of the confirmation is the evidence that the feeling was waiting to be named. She had not been hiding it; she just had not been asked.

Once the feeling is named and the coach is sitting with it, the next thing that happens is often a quiet that the person has not had access to before. The energy that was going into the fix-activity now has nowhere to go. The first experience of having no fix-activity to do is one of the bottlenecks the person has been avoiding their entire life. Being here for it, with another person, is the medicine.

## Connection to Other Drivers of the Same Pattern

The failure-feeling is one of several drivers of the fix-everything pattern. Others include:

- **The judgment-driver:** The person fixes because they are judging what is — see [[im-judgmental-how-do-i-stop]]. The fixing is the action-form of the judgment.
- **The shame-driver:** The person fixes because they cannot tolerate what they would be if they were not fixing — see [[ask-whats-wrong-with-you-into-the-shame]].
- **The caretaking-driver:** The person fixes others because they cannot tolerate their own emotional state when others are in distress — see [[i-caretake-people]].
- **The control-driver:** The person fixes because letting go would mean surrendering control of outcomes — see [[what-do-you-lose-if-this-belief-goes-away]].

The failure-feeling is often present alongside one or more of these. The coaching move is not to identify the single driver but to find which one is presently the most accessible, work it, and let the others surface in their turn.

## Why This Principle Is Counterintuitive

The standard advice for chronic fixers is to "stop fixing" or to "let go." The advice does not work because it asks the person to remove the activity without addressing what the activity is doing. Without the activity, the feeling has no buffer. The person experiences the removal of the activity as exposure to the bare feeling, which is more painful than continuing to fix. So the person resumes the fixing.

The principle inverts the move. The work is not to stop the fixing. The work is to find the feeling underneath and be with it. The fixing then drops on its own, because the activity is no longer needed.

This is the same structural logic as [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]] and [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]]. The activity is held in place by what it is protecting against. Removing the activity does not address the protection. Addressing what is being protected against releases the activity automatically.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When a person presents the fix-everything pattern (see [[why-am-i-always-trying-to-fix]]) and the fixing has been ongoing for years.
- When the person reports exhaustion alongside the fixing — they are working hard and getting nowhere.
- When the standard interventions for the fixing ("stop trying to fix, let it be") have been tried and have not worked.
- When the person, in the room, is visibly trying to fix the session itself — fix the conversation, fix the coach, fix the answer.
- When the conversation has reached a point where the fixing is being interrupted in real time and the person can feel that something is underneath but cannot name it.

## When This Principle Lands

The principle lands when the person confirms the naming with a fluency that surprises them. "Yes, that's exactly the feeling." The fluency is the body recognizing what it has been carrying.

After the naming lands, the person often quiets. The energy that was going into fix-activity has been redirected into being-with-the-feeling, which is much quieter. The quiet itself can be alarming because the person is not used to it. The coach's job is to stay with the person through the quiet without filling it with more content.

## Tone

Soft, ordinary, warm. The naming is delivered as a statement of fact, not as a revelation. "That's actually the driver of all the fixing — the feeling that everything I touch fails." The matter-of-factness is part of what makes the principle land. If delivered with weight, the principle becomes another thing to fix; delivered ordinarily, it is just true.

The offer of companionship ("we'll hang out") is even more casual. The casualness signals that being with this feeling is not a coaching feat. It is just sitting together. The person can do this.
