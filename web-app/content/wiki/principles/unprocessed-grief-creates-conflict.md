# Most Human Suffering Comes From Unprocessed Grief

## The Principle

If grief is what happens when an identity dismantles (see [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]]), then a person whose identity is being challenged without the grief being felt has to do something else with the energy. What they do, in most cases, is fight to keep the identity intact. They project the dismantling outward and turn it into a struggle with the people they perceive as threatening their identity. Most conflict, individual and societal, is unprocessed grief leaking out as defense of identity.

"Most of humans' pain and suffering comes from unprocessed grief." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The claim is structural: the grief energy does not disappear when refused. It comes out as conflict.

## How It Works at the Individual Level

A boyfriend-girlfriend fight, on this frame, is usually two people defending identities that are being challenged by the relationship. "I'm going to be abandoned" is an identity. "I need to fight for myself" is an identity. "I have to be right" is an identity. Each is something that has not been allowed to die. So instead of grieving the identity, the person fights the partner who is threatening it.

"Most of our fighting comes because our identity hasn't been dismantled in a way that creates peace in our system, and therefore we project it out into the world." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The fight is not really about the surface content. It is about preserving the identity that is being threatened. If the grief were allowed -- if the identity were allowed to die rather than defended -- the fight would stop having the same charge.

This connects to [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]]: an emotion that is not felt has to go somewhere, and where it usually goes is at someone.

## How It Works at the Societal Level

Identity politics is the macro version of the same mechanism. People fight over their identities -- religious, racial, national, political -- because those identities have not been allowed to be questioned, much less dismantled. The fighting is the grief avoidance.

"They even call it identity politics. We're Christian, you're Muslim. We're Black, you're white. That's all identity. My way of thinking is right, your way of thinking is wrong. Most of our conflict is wrapped up in an identity." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

This is not a claim that the identities are wrong or that people should drop them. It is a claim about what happens when identities are held without ever being allowed to be reconfigured. The unfelt grief over their fragility becomes hostility toward whoever seems to threaten them.

## The Roe v. Wade Example

In the episode, Joe applies this to the political moment of the recording (the overturning of Roe v. Wade). He observes that there is real grief on both sides of the issue. People who grieve for unborn children on one side, people who grieve the loss of bodily autonomy on the other. Both sides have real grief and real identities being challenged.

"There's real grief on both sides. I think that our ability to see that and feel that and be with each other in that is the solution to the problem, not one thing or another way of handling it. It's not in a law. I don't think that there's a law that's going to make this issue go away either way." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The claim is not that the right policy will emerge from grief. The claim is that without the grief being felt, no policy will resolve the conflict. The fight continues because the underlying grief has nowhere to go.

## The Evidence: Societies That Grieve Together

The argument is supported by what tends to happen in societies that have built collective grief practices into how they function. Joe references cultures in East Africa with group grief rituals (he names this as outside his deep experience but as something he has read about), and notes that such societies tend to have less interpersonal conflict.

The most concrete case Joe points to is the Truth and Reconciliation Commission in South Africa after apartheid -- a national grieving process that allowed the transition to be substantially less volatile than comparable transitions in nearby countries that did not undertake one.

"If you look at South Africa, when the apartheid shifted, they did this thing, Truth and Reconciliation. It was like a national grieving process. And it allowed the transition to be very different than it did, say, in the other countries nearby that switched, a lot less volatile." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

See [[truth-and-reconciliation-south-africa]] for the example.

The same dynamic operates in families. Families that can grieve together produce less compartmentalized emotion, less passive aggression, less leaked anger.

## The Practical Corollary

If conflict is unprocessed grief, then the path through conflict is not better arguments or better laws or better persuasion. It is making space for the grief that the identities are protecting against.

"Have your feelings, yes, and now we are fighting for this. Or maybe just have your feelings and then see what action comes out when that has fully moved through you." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

This does not mean no action. Action that comes after the grief tends to be more effective and less power-driven. Action that comes from grief avoidance tends to recreate the cycle, even if it appears principled or strategic.

"I have noticed in my own world that when I fully grieve something, my actions become far more effective and far less power dynamic, far less like it's either I'm controlling you or you're controlling me." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

See [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] for the developmental version of this in personal life.

## The Common Objection

A predictable response: if I really grieve, I will lose my fight. I will become passive. I will stop caring about what matters.

The claim is the opposite. Grief is not the alternative to fighting for what matters. Grief is the alternative to fighting from a place where the identity is preserving itself.

"Maybe you won't fight. But it doesn't mean that you're not going to take action, that helps move the ball forward, that you're not going to be part of the process of finding a something that works for everybody." -- Joe Hudson, The Beauty of Grief

The grief reorganizes the action. It does not eliminate it. What it eliminates is the power-dynamic shape of the action, the controlling-or-being-controlled frame.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- When someone is in chronic conflict with a person and the conflict does not resolve no matter what they say.
- When someone is upset about a political or societal situation and the energy is stuck on the opponents.
- When a person is in a couple where the fights keep recurring with the same shape.
- When someone is defending an identity that is being threatened and cannot see why the threat lands so hard.
- When the political or relational fight is the surface and the underlying grief about a changing identity is the real material.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When grief has been refused or compartmentalized at scale.
- When [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] has been established and the person is asking what happens if you do not grieve.
- When a person is in a chronic conflict that the grief frame might unlock.
