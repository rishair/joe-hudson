# The Nervous System Codes Fight Threats As Real

## The Principle

When a fight escalates past a certain line — threats of physical violence, threats about the future of the relationship, insults — the partner's body records the threat as real, regardless of whether the mind interprets it as "just" what was said in anger. The conscious correction does not reach the nervous system. The body keeps the record.

"Our nervous system is like, oh, that's real. And we're going to be like this around them every time they get angry. And then their anger can't be okay, and then we can't heal through that anger." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

The mechanism is one-way: the body codes the threat at the moment it happens. The mind, hours later, can say "they didn't really mean it, that was the fight talking." The mind's correction has no effect on the body's coding. The body keeps the data.

## What This Implies for Fights

The principle has a sharp implication for how fights work. Once a threat lands in the body, the body braces around the angry partner thereafter. Every time the partner's anger arises, the brace fires. The anger that arises is no longer just anger — it is anger plus the body's whole record of the prior threats.

This is why anger work in a relationship that has had threats becomes structurally harder: the partner's anger can't be okay (the body is bracing), and so the anger can't be moved through together. The medium that should be available for trauma-healing through loving witness gets foreclosed by the brace.

"And then their anger can't be okay, and then we can't heal through that anger." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

This is the operational consequence: the principle is not a moral teaching about threats being bad. It is a structural account of why threats foreclose the medium in which the trauma underneath the fight could otherwise heal. See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] for what the medium can produce when it is intact.

## What Counts as a Threat the Body Codes

The class of things the body codes as real when delivered in a fight:

- **Threats of physical violence.** Even if no contact happens. The threat of the contact is what the body codes.
- **Actual physical violence.** Obviously.
- **Yelling.** The body registers the volume as the threat.
- **Insults.** Not "what you did was wrong" but "you are X" — the targeting of the person rather than the behavior.
- **Future threats.** "I'm going to divorce you." "I'm going to cheat on you." "I'm going to leave." Any if-this-continues-then-this-future statement that names the end of the relationship or a major breach.

The unifying feature: each one is a delivery of "the worst thing you fear about this relationship is possible" into the partner's body. The body's job is to track such information. The body's job is not to evaluate whether the speaker meant it.

## Why the Mind's Discount Does Not Reach the Body

The mind's discount sounds like: "they didn't really mean it, they were just upset, that's not who they really are, I know they would never actually do that."

The mind's discount is true. The speaker probably did not mean it. The relationship probably is not over. The threatened action probably will not happen.

The discount has no effect on the body because the body is not running the same evaluation. The body's evaluation is: was a threat to my survival or my primary attachment delivered? If yes, log it and prepare to brace. The body does not need the threat to be sincere to code it. The body only needs the threat to have been delivered.

This is the same mechanism by which any threat works on the body. The body of a person who has been mugged braces around that street regardless of how unlikely a second mugging is. The body of a child who was yelled at braces around the voice of authority regardless of how reasonable the current authority figure is. The fight-context delivery of a relationship-ending threat is in the same category for the body of the partner.

## Why This Is the Basis for the Verbal-Abuse Line

The principle is what makes the verbal-abuse boundary load-bearing. See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the definition. The reason that definition matters is not that the moves involved are uncivil. It is that those moves cross into territory where the body will code the threat, and the coding produces erosion that no later apology removes.

"That erodes trust over a long period of time." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal (on insults specifically)

The trust erosion is not a moral consequence. It is the structural consequence of the body having coded the threat and braced thereafter. The brace is the trust erosion in its felt form.

## Why This Takes a Long Time to Heal

The healing is slow because the body's coding has to be slowly disconfirmed by accumulated evidence of safety. The mind can decide in a day that the relationship is repaired. The body decides on its own timeline, as the partner's anger arises again and again without the threatened consequence following, over months and years.

This is why Joe specifies that any of these crossings — physical threats, yelling, insults, future threats — will take a long time to heal. Not because the harm is huge in some abstract sense. Because the body's coding is durable and is updated slowly by the lived experience of the threat not actually arriving.

## The Implication for the Fight-Agreement Boundary

The principle is the reason the fight-agreement boundary cannot be soft. Once you know the body codes the threat regardless of the speaker's later regret, the move "don't go past that line in a fight" becomes mandatory rather than aspirational. Going past the line installs a cost that no in-fight apology and no post-fight repair can fully remove.

This is why the boundary articulated in this episode is so firm:

"Holding that boundary of saying like, we're not doing that in a fight, or if you're doing that, I'm going to leave. I'll be back in 20 minutes, or I'll be back when you call me and tell me you're ready to do this without threatening me, without insulting me. Holding that boundary is incredibly important, or you're going to start going in the wrong direction." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Your Relationship Fights to Heal

The boundary is the only available way to keep the body from being given more material to code. The leaving is not punishment; it is prevention of the next threat-coding event. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for the form the leaving takes.

## The Asymmetry Between Damage and Repair

A related implication. The damage of a threat in a fight is delivered in the moment of the threat. The repair has to be earned over many subsequent interactions. The asymmetry is built into the principle: a single delivery codes, but the disconfirmation requires repetition.

This is the structural reason why the fight-agreement practice is preventative rather than reparative. See [[fight-agreements-made-outside-the-fight]]. The agreements are upstream of the threat-delivery; they keep the threat from being delivered in the first place. Once the threat is delivered, the agreements cannot un-code it. They can only keep the next one from being added on top.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Finding that you brace whenever your partner's anger arises, even when you know the anger is not aimed at you
- Noticing that something said years ago in a fight still lives in your body around that partner
- Realizing that you cannot make yourself feel safe by reminding yourself that "they didn't mean it"
- Finding that even small expressions of irritation from your partner produce a disproportionate reaction in your body
- Feeling like you are "walking on eggshells" around someone whose anger has previously threatened you (see [[walking-on-eggshells]])
- Discovering that the apology was sincere and the relationship moved on, but the body still has not received the message

## Tone

Joe states the principle matter-of-factly. There is no moralizing. The point is structural: this is how bodies work, this is what it implies for what you can and cannot do in a fight. The implication is firm — these lines should not be crossed — but the firmness comes from the structural account, not from a teaching about virtue.
