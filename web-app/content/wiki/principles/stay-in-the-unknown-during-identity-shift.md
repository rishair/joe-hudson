# Stay in the Unknown During an Identity Shift

## The Principle

When something in your identity shifts — through insight, through loss, through a change in life conditions — there is a period in which you do not know who you are, what you want, what you will do, or how you will be. The right move during that period is not to figure it out. The right move is to stay in the unknown for as long as you can and watch what unravels naturally.

The wrong move, and the one most people make automatically, is to shove the new identity into a box as quickly as possible. The boxing-up feels like progress. It is what relieves the discomfort of not-knowing. But the box you reach for is built by the old identity. The old mental model is what is available to do the boxing. So the new identity gets stuffed into a container shaped by the very self that just dissolved. The shift gets undone in the act of trying to land it.

"The best thing to do is to just be present with what's going on, feel everything that's coming up to the surface and not try to reconstruct your life. So what's happened if you've had an identity shift is that all of a sudden you don't know exactly what you're gonna do or who you're gonna do or how you're gonna do or what you're gonna be or how you're gonna be because your identity is different. But if you try to shove it into a box as soon as possible, what you're doing is you're using the old mental model to shove it into the box. So instead of that, just be in the unknown for as long as you possibly can be and watch what unravels naturally. And it will, it will." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

## Why The Unknown Has To Last

The reason the unknown period has to be honored is that the new identity has to find itself the same way any new capacity finds itself — by feeling its way around. The hands of a baby do not know they are hands. The baby does not know they can be controlled. The baby scratches its face. Then it discovers: oh, that's my hand. Then it discovers: oh, I can control it. Each stage requires time in the not-knowing before the next stage becomes available.

The same is true for adult identity shifts. You do not yet know what you are now. You have to feel your way around it. The supermarket aisle that used to be familiar feels different. The friends that used to fit feel different. The food you used to like, the music you used to play, the work you used to do — all of it is up for re-discovery. The re-discovery is the work. It cannot be skipped by deciding in advance what you are now.

"What I notice is that in each stage of development, what occurs is that you don't quite know anything, and you have to feel your way around it. And this happens as an adult, but it also happens as a kid. So when you're a little one, and you're learning how to move your hands, the first thing is you don't even know it's your hand. And then the second thing is, 'Oh, I know this is my hand, but I don't know how to control it.' So I scratch my face. And then I will go, 'Oh, that's my hand and I can control it.' And that's the important thing is those shifts have to be allowed to be in the unknown for an extended period of time so that you can learn and go through all the stages of, 'Oh, this is my hand and it's mine and I can control it.'" -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

## What Happens When the Process Is Forced

The cost of forcing the process to resolve faster than it wants to:

- **The epiphany stops before it has fully bloomed.** The shift was offering something the old self could not produce. The forcing closes the offering before it lands.
- **The growth stagnates.** What was about to become a real change reverts to a version of what was already there.
- **The work has to be done again.** The forced resolution does not stick. The next iteration of the shift starts from the same place because the previous iteration was prematurely closed.

"If you try to force that process, it often stagnates the growth or it stops the epiphany before it has a full chance to blossom." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

"If you try to make it happen too quickly, it gets all fuckada and you have to do the work often, again and again, until you can just allow yourself to be in the unknown because you yourself are somewhat unknown in that moment." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The strong language ("all fuckada") is in the original. The forcing produces a mess. The mess has to be cleaned up before the original shift can be returned to. The clean-up is the cost of having tried to rush the landing.

## The Felt Cost of Trying to Control the Change

A more structural cost: trying to control the change is itself the way you lose the power of the change.

"If you're not being with what is and you're trying to make it something that you can control, then you're losing the power of changing who you are." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The new identity's power is precisely that it is not what the old identity would have produced. The old identity reaching for control to manage the new identity is the new identity being reverted to the old. The power of the change lives in not-knowing. The grasping for known-ness is what dissipates the power.

This is why the instruction is not "stay in the unknown briefly" but "stay in the unknown for as long as you possibly can be." The longer the not-knowing is sustained, the more of the new identity gets to actually emerge. The shorter the not-knowing, the more of the new identity gets reverted to the old.

## What "Staying In The Unknown" Actually Looks Like

The instruction is not passive. It is active presence with what is, without making anything of it yet.

- **Feel everything that is coming up.** Identity shifts produce emotion. Grief for the old self. Fear of the unknown. Excitement at the openness. Confusion. Sadness. Joy. Let it move. None of it needs to be sorted into "this is what the new identity is."
- **Don't decide what to do next.** The decisions that get made from inside the unknown are usually the old identity's decisions in disguise. The decisions that get made when the new identity has actually arrived will be different and clearer.
- **Don't tell people what you are now.** The telling forces a verbal commitment to a structure you have not yet inhabited. The commitment locks in the old-model version.
- **Don't make major life rearrangements yet.** Quitting the job, ending the relationship, moving cities — all of these will get done if they need to be done. They will get done from inside the new identity rather than from the unknown.
- **Watch what unravels.** The new identity reveals itself in small moments. A food you used to love no longer appeals. A friend who used to fit no longer does. A piece of work that used to drain you suddenly interests you. Notice these moments. They are the new identity announcing itself.

The eventual arrival: "All of a sudden you'll be in the supermarket, you'll know what to order, know what to eat, know who to be with, know what friends work, know what feels good." The knowing arrives. It does not have to be manufactured. The unknown is the field the knowing emerges from.

## When This Principle Becomes Urgent

The principle is most urgent in the moments people are most tempted to violate it:

- **Right after a breakup or divorce.** The pull to immediately identify what kind of relationship to look for next, what kind of person you are now, what your dating profile should say. The new identity has not arrived. The forcing produces a profile shaped by the old relationship's wounds.
- **Right after losing a job, quitting, or leaving a long-term career.** The pull to decide immediately what the next career is. The next career is downstream of the new identity, which has not arrived.
- **Right after a major insight or therapeutic shift.** The pull to immediately restructure life around the insight. The restructuring is the old mental model trying to absorb the insight into its own architecture.
- **Right after the death of a loved one.** The pull to immediately redefine what life is for now. The new relationship to life is going to emerge through the grief, not through decisions made in the early shock.
- **Right after a relationship begins or deepens.** The pull to decide what this is, where it is going, what role each person plays. The relationship is still becoming itself.
- **Right after a child is born.** The pull to identify what kind of parent you are now. The parent you are now is going to emerge through the daily encounter with the child.
- **Right after a major financial or status change.** The pull to figure out who you are with the new resources or position. The new identity has to encounter the changed conditions slowly to find its actual shape.

In every case, the felt impulse is to relieve the discomfort of not-knowing by making the new thing known. In every case, the impulse is to be resisted in service of letting the new thing become what it actually is.

## The Connection to Anxious-With-Relaxation

The flip side of this principle is the anxiety that often comes with the not-knowing itself. The body has been organized around the old identity and its known structure. When the structure dissolves, the body registers the dissolution as a threat. The anxiety with relaxation, the unease with not-knowing, the discomfort with the unknown — all are the nervous system asking for the old structure back.

The same instruction applies: be with the anxiety. Don't act on it. The anxiety is part of what is in the field during the shift. It is not the signal to reconstruct. It is one of the things to feel in the staying-with.

The anxiety also tends to diminish as the new identity emerges. Not because the anxiety is being managed, but because the new identity does not have the same need for the structure the anxiety was demanding.

## What This Is Not

- Not a license to drift indefinitely. The unknown is honored, but the new identity does emerge. The instruction is to wait for it to emerge, not to refuse all action forever.
- Not a meditation practice. The unknown is lived in real life, with real conditions to manage. The instruction is to manage the conditions without using the management to prematurely close the new identity.
- Not an excuse to avoid responsibility. The responsibilities that need to be met are met. The instruction is about the meta-level construction of who you are now, not about whether to show up to work or care for your kids.
- Not nihilism. The new identity does emerge. The principle is about the conditions for the emergence, not about whether the emergence will happen.

## The Coaching Move

When a coachee is in an identity shift and is reaching for premature closure, the move is to name what they are doing and offer the alternative.

The naming: "You just had a shift, and now you are trying to decide what to do about it. Notice that the deciding is happening from before the shift. The new you is not the one trying to figure this out."

The offer: "What if you didn't have to know yet? What if you stayed in the not-knowing for a while and let the new you show up on its own?"

The protection: the coachee may resist the staying-with because the not-knowing is uncomfortable. The coach's job is not to relieve the discomfort with answers. The coach's job is to keep the field open enough that the new identity can arrive on its own schedule.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They feel like they have changed but don't know who they are now
- They keep trying to figure out what to do next and the figuring-out doesn't produce clarity
- They keep reaching for the old framework and it doesn't fit anymore
- They feel anxious about not having a clear identity right now
- They are tempted to make a big decision to "settle this" — quit the job, leave the relationship, move cities — and the temptation has urgency that feels suspicious
- They keep narrating to themselves and others what they are now, and the narrations keep changing
- They want to be done with the transition more than they want the transition to complete itself

## Tone

Gentle but firm. The principle is delivered with the calm of someone who has watched this play out many times. The discomfort of the unknown is real, and the temptation to relieve it is understandable. The firmness is in not letting the discomfort drive the premature resolution. The gentleness is in honoring how hard the staying-with is.
