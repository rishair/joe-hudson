# The "I'm Bad For Feeling It" Loop Confirms the Original Abuse Narrative

## The Principle

When someone has trauma responses they cannot control — the urge to hurt, the chronic rage, the cravings, the fantasies they hate having — they often conclude that having these responses means they are a bad person. The conclusion is a secondary layer on top of the trauma itself. It does two things at once: it makes the response harder to feel and process (because feeling it now also means confirming the badness), and it loops back to confirm the original abuse narrative ("something must have been wrong with me for this to have happened").

The loop runs:

1. Abuse happens to the person.
2. The unprocessed material produces unwanted responses (rage, urge to hurt, recreating patterns, addictions, intrusive thoughts).
3. The unwanted response is registered by the person as evidence of their own badness.
4. The badness conclusion confirms the implicit story the original abuse installed: "there must have been something wrong with me for this to have happened to me."
5. The badness story makes the unwanted response harder to feel and process, because feeling it now means also confirming the verdict on the self.
6. The unprocessed material continues to produce more unwanted responses, more badness conclusions, more confirmation of the original story.

The loop is self-sustaining. The trauma produces the response; the response produces the badness conclusion; the badness conclusion prevents the response from being processed; the unprocessed response keeps producing more responses. The cycle does not break by trying harder to suppress the response or by trying harder to be a better person. It breaks by interrupting the conclusion that the response means badness.

"In a human's mind, if you want to go and kill somebody, then you must be bad. Right? That's the — then the thing is, oh, I'm bad. So not only are you contending with this horrific feeling like that you want to hurt somebody, because that doesn't feel good in anybody's system, so which it is a response to the trauma, it's a thing that he's just like, I don't want to feel that, but you're also then having a very good reason to tell yourself that you're a bad person, that you — that there's something wrong with you, there's something broken with you, and then that story perpetuates the whole thing. It makes the whole thing harder to process. And like, you're bad for feeling angry, you're bad for wanting revenge, you're bad for wanting to crave somebody's skull in, you're bad for being weak, you're bad for — everything is just another way in which you're bad, which agrees with why you got the abuse in the first place, because there was something wrong with you." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

## The Specific Sequence Joe Names

The loop has a specific structure that Joe spells out in the Sean clip analysis. Each layer compounds the one before.

**Layer 1: The trauma response is unwanted.** The urge to hurt, the rage, the recreation, the addiction — none of these feel good in the body. They are not desired by the person having them. The person already does not want to have them.

**Layer 2: The unwanted response is interpreted as moral failing.** Wanting to hurt someone, in human moral grammar, means you must be a bad person. Wanting revenge means you are vindictive. Wanting to give in to a craving means you are weak. The interpretation is automatic and culturally reinforced.

**Layer 3: The moral failing confirms the implicit abuse narrative.** The original abuse installed a story (sometimes explicit, often implicit) that something was wrong with the person. "You deserved it." "You made me do this." Even when the words were not said, the act of being abused as a child installs a sense that the child was responsible. The bad-person conclusion in the present confirms this old story.

**Layer 4: The confirmation makes the response harder to process.** Feeling the response now means confirming the verdict. The person who tries to feel the urge to hurt as raw material (which is what processing requires) cannot, because the act of letting the urge be felt is taken as proof that the urge is who they are.

**Layer 5: The unprocessed material continues to produce responses.** The cycle continues. More urges, more responses, more moral failures, more confirmation, more inability to process.

The loop is robust because each layer reinforces the others. Trying to interrupt any single layer in isolation often fails. The interruption has to come from outside the loop — from a perspective that does not accept the equation between the response and the self.

## The Bad-Person Conclusion Has the Same Voice as the Original Abuser

A specific structural feature of the bad-person loop: the voice that delivers the badness verdict is often the same voice that delivered the original abuse. The internal critic that says "you are a bad person for having this urge" speaks in the language of the abusive parent, the violent partner, the cruel coach. The voice was installed by the abuse and now narrates the trauma response.

This means the bad-person conclusion is not the person's own native judgment. It is the abuser's voice, internalized, operating without the original abuser present. The person is doing the work of the abuser by continuing to deliver the verdict.

The recognition of this — that the bad-person voice is the inherited voice of the original perpetrator — is sometimes itself the move that begins to dissolve the loop. The voice loses some of its authority when it is recognized as borrowed. The person can begin to separate from the voice and from the conclusion.

## How the Loop Manifests in Specific Configurations

**With the endurer:** The endurer concludes that being the one who endures means they should not have weakness, should not need help, should not have hurt. When the hurt arrives, the endurer registers it as failure of the configuration. The hurt now means the endurer is breakable, which the configuration was built to prevent. The bad-person conclusion takes the form "I am weak for feeling this." See [[endurer-pattern]].

**With the victim-recreator:** The victim concludes that being on the receiving end again means they are pathetic, complicit, or stupid. The recreation now means they are deficient. The bad-person conclusion takes the form "I keep doing this because there is something wrong with me." See [[trauma-mapping]].

**With the urge-to-hurt-back configuration:** The person with the abuser-direction urge concludes that having the urge means they are dangerous, evil, or fundamentally violent. The urge now means they are the kind of person who would do this. The bad-person conclusion takes the form "I am a danger and cannot trust myself." See [[victim-or-abuser-cycle]].

**With addiction:** The person with cravings concludes that having the cravings means they are weak-willed, broken, or hopeless. The cravings now mean they are the kind of person who cannot be trusted with their own choices. The bad-person conclusion takes the form "I am an addict, I cannot trust myself."

In each case, the response (which is data about the unprocessed trauma) gets converted into a verdict (which is the bad-person conclusion), and the verdict prevents the response from being felt as data.

## How the Loop Is Interrupted

The loop is interrupted by separating the response from the verdict. The response is allowed to be what it is — a response to the trauma, a piece of data about what happened, a feeling that exists — without taking it as evidence of the person's intrinsic badness.

The specific moves:

**Treat the response as information about what happened, not about who the person is.** The urge to hurt is information about the original wound. The recreation is information about the imprint. The craving is information about what the body is trying to manage. The information is meaningful; the verdict on the self based on the information is the loop.

**Recognize the bad-person voice as the abuser's voice.** The internal narrator that says "this means you are bad" is often the inherited voice of the original perpetrator. Naming this can begin to loosen the voice's authority.

**Let the response be felt without producing the conclusion.** This is the actual work and it is slow. Each time the response arises, the person can practice meeting it as feeling rather than as verdict. Each time, the feeling moves a little more and the verdict has a little less power. Over time, the response can be felt fully and the conclusion does not follow.

**Receive the response from someone who does not deliver the verdict.** A coach, therapist, or partner who can hear "I have the urge to hurt someone" without flinching or moralizing or pathologizing is providing the relational container in which the response can be felt without the verdict. The other person's lack of moral judgment models the lack of judgment the person needs to develop internally. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] and [[how-to-receive-someones-trauma-sharing]].

**Process the underlying unfelt material.** As the original trauma is processed — the rage felt, the helplessness felt, the longing felt — the responses that the unprocessed material was producing diminish. The cycle starts to dissolve from the bottom rather than just at the top.

## What This Means for the "I Can't Trust Myself" Outcome

The bad-person loop produces, downstream, the self-distrust pattern. The person concludes from the unwanted response that they cannot trust themselves. They restrict their lives — avoid relationships, isolate, decline opportunities — to prevent the response from surfacing or acting.

The self-restriction is not based on accurate assessment of the person. It is based on the badness conclusion that the loop produced. The person who has done the work to interrupt the loop can often discover that their actual judgment is sound; the self-distrust was the bad-person conclusion's voice all along. See [[how-do-i-trust-myself]] for the developed concern article on this presentation.

The self-trust returns not through trying harder to trust, but through dissolving the bad-person conclusion that the self-distrust was based on. The trust was always available; the conclusion was blocking access to it.

## Implications for the AI Coach

When someone names having a trauma response — an urge, a craving, a recurring pattern, an intrusive thought — the AI coach is in a high-leverage position for either reinforcing the bad-person loop or interrupting it.

**Reinforcing moves (avoid these):**
- Reacting with alarm or judgment to the response.
- Treating the response as evidence of who the person is.
- Pathologizing the person ("you should see a professional about this").
- Implying that the response means something is wrong with them.
- Echoing the bad-person voice by validating it ("yes, that does sound concerning").
- Moralizing about the response.

**Interrupting moves (use these):**
- Acknowledging the response as data about what happened, not data about who the person is.
- Naming the loop explicitly: "the urge is the trauma's response, not who you are."
- Pointing at the bad-person voice as a separate thing from the person's actual self.
- Offering presence with the response that does not include verdict.
- Pointing toward the underlying work that dissolves the unprocessed material producing the responses.

The interruption does not require the AI coach to have a complete picture of the person's trauma. It requires the AI coach to be a place where the response can be named without producing the verdict. The lack of verdict from the coach models the lack of verdict the person can begin to give themselves.

## Boundary: The Person Is Still Responsible for Actions

The principle that the trauma response is not evidence of badness does not relieve the person of responsibility for actions taken on the response. Having the urge to hurt is not the person's fault. Acting on the urge to hurt is still the person's responsibility. The interruption of the bad-person loop is for the urge, not for the action.

This boundary matters because the principle could be misread as "since the response is just data, the action is also just data." It is not. The response is the unprocessed material expressing. The action is a choice made in response to the material. Processing the material reduces the pressure of the response and makes the action less likely. But the action remains a choice for which the person is accountable.

The principle is also not an argument against the person seeking professional help when the responses are intense or when the risk of acting is real. Pointing at the underlying work does not mean the AI coach (or any non-professional) is the place where the work should fully happen. It means that the framing of the response — as data versus as verdict — is itself a piece of the work that can be done anywhere.
