# Building a Company Is the Fastest Way to Become the Best Version of Yourself

## The Principle

The CEO and founder role, taken seriously as a developmental container, confronts a person with more high-stakes problems at a higher frequency than any other job. The bank that disappears overnight. The 30%-layoff conversation. The product pivot that requires reorienting everyone the founder has trusted with their professional lives. Each confrontation surfaces patterns the founder did not know they had. Each requires development the founder did not know they needed. The role, run honestly, produces self-development at a rate that no other role matches.

"I think at the core of it is that I fall in love with the CEO and founder job when I really understood that this is the fastest way to grow me and myself as a person. I don't think there is another job that confronts you with that frequency with problems such as your bank, doesn't exist from one day to the other. You have to make the call to ultimately lay off 30% of people that, you know, really put a lot of trust in yourself. How do you communicate that? I think those things bring up so much about also who you are as a person that I'm very grateful for the role and the job. Because I believe it's the fastest way for myself to ultimately become a better version of myself." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

The principle reframes the question of why someone would take on the founder role. The standard frame is wealth or status or impact. This principle adds a structural frame: the role is a developmental accelerator. The founder who treats it as a developmental container gets compound returns on their own growth that other roles cannot produce.

## Why the Role Is Structurally Different

Several features of the CEO role combine to make it a uniquely demanding developmental container.

**Confrontation frequency is high.** Most jobs produce 1-3 high-stakes self-confrontations per year. The founder role produces several per week. Each crisis is an opportunity (and a demand) to face a pattern that would normally stay invisible. The frequency is what compounds the development.

**The stakes are real.** Self-development practices done in low-stakes containers (meditation retreats, coaching sessions, journals) operate at a different intensity than self-development done while a company depends on the developer. The body cannot easily pretend at high stakes. The patterns that survive low-stakes practice get exposed by high-stakes work.

**The feedback loop is short.** A founder's bad decision-making affects the team, the customers, the runway, all within weeks. The feedback on the development work is fast. Other roles allow patterns to operate for years before consequences become visible. The founder cannot defer the consequences as long.

**The decisions are non-delegable.** Many things in the company can be delegated; the consequential decisions cannot. The founder is repeatedly required to do the thing they cannot pass off. The pattern that wants to avoid the hard decision (see [[approach-the-pink-elephant]]) gets exposed by the structural impossibility of avoidance.

**The relational demands are continuous.** The team that depends on the founder. The board that funds them. The customers that trust them. The cofounders and partners. Each is a relationship that requires the founder to do their relational work in real time, repeatedly. The relational patterns get exercised at a rate other jobs cannot match.

The combination makes the role a developmental forcing function. The founder either grows at the rate the role demands or fails at the rate the role demands. There is rarely a stable middle.

## What "Best Version of Yourself" Means Here

The phrase is not aspirational fluff. It points at something specific: the version of the founder whose patterns have been faced rather than worked around, whose internal political theater has been quieted, whose decision-making is less driven by shame or fear or status-seeking, whose relationships are built on actual presence rather than performance.

The best version is not the most successful, the wealthiest, or the most admired. It is the most integrated. The version where what the founder thinks, feels, and does are aligned. See [[three-brain-alignment-required-for-action]] for the integration model.

The CEO role accelerates the development of this integration because the role surfaces the disintegration. The founder who is split between what they want and what they should do gets exposed by the decisions they cannot make. The founder who is split between what they think and what they feel gets exposed by the team that no longer trusts them. The disintegration becomes visible faster in this role than in others.

## Why This Is Joe's Reframe of the CEO Role

The cultural narrative about being a CEO emphasizes the cost: the burnout, the loneliness, the impossible hours, the personal sacrifice. Joe's reframe (and Johannes's echo of it) adds another dimension: the role is also the most demanding self-development practice available outside of formal contemplative traditions. The cost the standard narrative names is real. The compound developmental benefit, when the role is taken on with that orientation, is also real.

The asymmetry is significant. The founder who treats the role as a job-with-self-development-on-the-side gets the cost without the compound benefit. The founder who treats the role as a self-development practice that happens to also produce a company gets both. Same role, different relationship to it, different developmental trajectory.

This is the extension of [[business-as-spiritual-practice]] applied specifically to the founder role. The business-as-spiritual-practice principle applies to anyone in business. This principle is its specific form for the founder: the role itself, by its structural features, is the practice.

## The Failure Mode

This principle does not say "become a CEO if you want to develop yourself." There is a specific failure mode it warns against: taking the role for the developmental reasons without the integrity to do the actual work.

A founder who takes the role as a "growth opportunity" but does not do the work the role demands produces a worse outcome than not taking the role at all. The team suffers from the founder's avoidance. The company underperforms because the founder cannot make the decisions the role requires. The founder themselves develops less, not more, because they have set themselves up in a container they cannot meet.

The principle requires the willingness to actually meet the role. Without that willingness, the developmental claim is empty. With it, the claim is structurally true.

This is also the connection to [[dont-make-self-discovery-entertainment]]: the founder role provides the "flaming field" of real pressure that makes self-development work rather than become recreation. The pressure is what makes the development unavoidable. Without the pressure (the founder who takes the title without taking the work), the development does not happen.

## What This Principle Implies for Selecting Founders

A specific application: when evaluating someone considering taking on a founder role, the predictive question is not "are they smart enough?" or "have they got the technical chops?" The predictive question is closer to "are they willing to be confronted by themselves at the frequency this role will confront them?"

Founders who fail are rarely the ones who could not do the work. They are usually the ones who could not face the version of themselves the role exposed. The technical or strategic gaps are real but rarely the final cause of failure. The integration gap is what catches most failed founders.

The implication for coaching: the foundational work for a new founder is not strategy training or operational training. It is developing the capacity to meet what the role will surface. The founder who has done this work is positioned to do the strategic and operational work as it arrives. The founder who has not is positioned to be undone by the first sustained confrontation.

## Joe's Affirmation

Joe affirms Johannes's articulation of this principle directly, naming it as a convergent insight across people who have done the developmental work:

"It's funny how that works because my experience is anybody who is really interested in a -- I'm going to truly understand myself, better version of themselves. Anybody who goes down that eventually lands in the same place, which is part of me becoming the version of myself that is deeply aligned, that is who I want to be, is contributing to other people also becoming better versions of themselves." -- Joe Hudson, 6 Operating Principles

The convergence Joe names: people who pursue self-development at any depth end up wanting to contribute to others' self-development too. The CEO role is structurally aligned with this. The company is a vehicle through which the founder's development becomes available to others, through both the culture they build and the impact the company has. The "best version of yourself" becomes a "best version of yourself that also helps others become themselves."

The structural alignment is part of why the role accelerates development. The founder's growth is not an isolated project; it is bound up with the team's growth, the customers' growth, the partners' growth. The growth has nowhere to hide and many places to land.

## The Company-Builder as Parent (The Founder-Child Analogy)

A complementary framing Johannes offers: building a company is structurally similar to growing a child. The founder is in deep relationship with something living that is becoming itself. The fun of it is bound up with the alignment between the founder's principles and the company's principles. The same alignment that produces a thriving child-parent relationship produces a thriving founder-company relationship.

"In a way, building a company is, you know, in a way, like growing a child that is growing up. And if you are in deep, authentic alignment with your own principles and the company's principles, I think it's a lot of fun. It's very invigorating and it really helps you a lot to grow." -- Johannes Langgraf, A Company Culture That Moves At The Pace of AI

The analogy explains why the developmental claim is mutual rather than extractive. The child does not just receive from the parent; the parent develops by being asked to meet the child. The company does not just receive from the founder; the founder develops by being asked to meet the company. The growth runs both ways. The principle holds because the relationship is structurally one of mutual evolution, not one-directional control.

This is the same logic as [[parenting-as-real-world-test-of-inner-work]]: the relational container exposes what other containers can hide. The CEO role and the parenting role share this feature, which is why they are the two domains Joe treats as the most reliable load-bearing tests of inner work.

## What This Principle Does Not Say

It does not say the CEO role is the only path to development. Other paths exist: parenting, partnership, sustained contemplative practice, art, service. Each has its own structural features. The principle says the CEO role has a specific combination of features that make it especially intensive.

It does not say everyone should become a CEO. Most people would be miserable in the role. The principle is for the person who is already inclined to take it and is asking whether to take it seriously as a developmental container, not for the general population deciding what to do with their life.

It does not say that doing the role developmentally guarantees the company's success. The development is the inner result. The outer result depends on many other variables (market, timing, capital, luck). The principle is about the inner accelerator the role provides, not about the outer outcome it produces.

## Tone

Joe and Johannes both deliver this principle with gratitude rather than grandiosity. The role is not glorified. The cost is acknowledged. The principle is offered as a reframe that makes the cost worth bearing, not as a sales pitch for being a CEO. The tone is the tone of someone who has lived inside the principle and is reporting back, not the tone of someone advocating for an idea.
