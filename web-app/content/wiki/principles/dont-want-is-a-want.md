# The Don't-Want Is a Want

## The Principle

Every "I don't want" is a "I want" in evasion. The rejecting frame is what people reach for when feeling the actual wanting is too vulnerable. The form looks like aversion. The function is hiding the want.

"Don't wanting is in a want. Like, I don't want that as a way to not actually feel the feeling of wanting." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

## Why the Don't-Want Is the Common Form

For people trained out of their wants in childhood, naming what they want is more vulnerable than naming what they reject. The don't-want frame gives them a target without requiring them to expose the wanting underneath. They can be against something without admitting they are for anything.

The structure: "I don't want money to rule my life" is felt safer to say than "I want freedom" or "I want my father's love" or whatever the actual want is. The don't-want feels like a stance. The want feels like an exposure.

But the don't-want is structurally a want: a want for the opposite, a want for distance from the thing, a want for the thing the don't-want is protecting. The wanting is happening. It has just been routed through aversion to keep it hidden from the person who is having it.

## The Diagnostic

When someone speaks fluently about what they do not want, attention shifts to what is underneath. The list of don't-wants is data — about the wants that are not allowed to be said directly.

A person who can list ten things they reject and zero things they want is not a person without wants. They are a person whose wants have been driven entirely underground into the don't-want form. The work is to surface the wants the don't-wants are covering.

"Don't wanting is in a want, right? Like, I don't want that as a way to not actually feel the feeling of wanting." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

## The Practice

A direct exercise that operationalizes the principle:

1. Write down all the things you know you don't want.
2. Then write down all the things you do want.
3. Allow yourself to feel the second list.

"That's a great practice generally is if you can write down on a list of, like a list, here's all the things I know I don't want, then write down all the things that you do want and allow yourself to feel those. It's tremendously transformative." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The two-list exercise reveals the structure. The don't-want list is usually long and well-articulated, because the person has practiced naming what they reject. The want list is usually short, halting, and uncertain — because the wanting has been suppressed. The exercise itself trains the want-naming muscle that the don't-want frame has been substituting for.

## Playing Not to Lose vs. Playing to Win

The don't-want frame produces a life of avoidance. The want frame produces a life of pursuit. The two produce very different outcomes, even if they look superficially similar from the outside.

"It's like playing to win rather than playing not to lose. And so much more success is driven in business or in art or in life by being able to live into the thing you want rather than to live to the thing that you don't want." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The person playing not to lose is organized around the don't-wants. They make decisions to avoid the bad outcome. The energy is constrained and defensive. The person playing to win is organized around the wants. They make decisions to move toward the wanted outcome. The energy is expansive and generative. The same situation produces opposite trajectories depending on which orientation is running.

See [[play-to-win-not-to-not-lose]] for the dedicated treatment of this distinction.

## Why Sublimating Through Don't-Want Costs You

Joe's own life is the example. He rejected money for years because his father pursued it and Joe felt rejected by the pursuit. The don't-want frame ("I don't want money to rule my life") looked principled. The want underneath was "I want my father's love." The don't-want frame did not get him the want underneath. It just structured his life around avoidance of the surrogate (money) while never addressing the actual want.

See [[joes-expensive-way-to-be-poor]] for the full example. The pattern generalizes: any life structured around don't-wants is a life that has not addressed the wants the don't-wants are covering. The work is to surface those wants and let them be the organizing energy instead.

## What This Is Not

- It is not "all aversions are wrong." Some don't-wants are clean and accurate — "I don't want to eat that mushroom because it's poisonous." The principle applies to the don't-wants that carry charge and that are structuring large parts of a life.
- It is not a license to convert everyone's don't-wants into wants on their behalf. The recognition has to come from inside. Telling someone "your don't-want is hiding a want" without their own discovery of it produces defensiveness.
- It is not the same as saying "you secretly want what you say you don't want." Sometimes the underlying want is for the opposite of what is being rejected; sometimes it is for something the don't-want has been used as a proxy for. The work is to find the actual want, not to invert the don't-want.

## The Connection to Craving and Aversion

The principle has a Buddhist parallel: craving and aversion are both the same operation, the active rejection of present experience. The don't-want, in this frame, is the aversion side of the same coin as craving. Both are the system pushing away the actual want by either grasping for the wrong thing (craving) or rejecting the want's vehicle (aversion).

See [[wanting-vs-craving]] for the full distinction. The clean want is the form that has neither the craving nor the aversion contamination — it is just the wanting, felt as wanting, with no defensive structure around it.
