# Not Letting the Leaving Be Part of the Anger

## The Principle

When you leave a difficult moment to discharge anger somewhere else, the act of leaving itself must not be a way of expressing the anger. The leaving and the discharge have to be structurally separated. The leaving is the means by which you get to the container where the anger will move. The leaving is not the container.

When the leaving carries the anger — a slam of the door, a parting shot, charged body language, a dismissive tone on the way out — the leaving becomes the discharge. The partner being left receives the leaving as the message. Whatever happens privately afterward is downstream of the message that has already landed.

The principle was named precisely by Tara in conversation with Joe about what they had learned:

"And not letting the leaving be part of the anger, right?" -- Tara Hudson, The Path To Healthy Fighting

Joe immediately affirms: "Not letting the leaving — that's a great way. Yeah. Yeah, that's a great way to put it. Not letting the leaving be part of the anger."

## Why the Leaving Wants to Carry the Anger

The body that is in anger wants to discharge. When the discharge has been forbidden in the relationship (no yelling, no aiming at the partner — see [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]]), the system looks for an available outlet. The act of leaving is available. The leaving can become the discharge by default if no other channel is open.

This is why the principle has to be named explicitly. Without naming, the system will use any available channel. The leaving is the most readily available channel that does not directly involve speaking anger at the partner — but it carries the anger anyway, through tone, body, and the felt sense of slamming.

## The Structural Separation

The fix is to have a separate, private container that the leaving is a means of reaching. The car. The bedroom with the wall to press on. The woods. The pillow. The shower. Once there is a container, the leaving has somewhere to go that is not "at the partner." The leaving stops being the discharge because the discharge has a real place to land.

The practice that produces this is [[get-angry-in-the-car]]. The practice presupposes that the body will be discharged in a specific place; the leaving is just the transit to that place. The discharge happens at the destination, not en route.

When the destination is real and committed-to, the leaving relaxes. The body does not need to discharge through the act of leaving because it knows that real discharge is moments away.

## The Difference From a Distance

A partner who has been in the relationship for years often cannot articulate why "you said you were going to your study to take 20 minutes" landed as a wound. The words were technically correct. The timeline was given. The withdrawal was framed.

The reason is usually that the leaving carried the anger. The body that received the leaving felt the discharge in the leaving itself. The 20 minutes the leaver spent in the study was real, but the partner had already received the brunt. By the time the leaver came back regulated, the wound had already happened.

This is one of the subtle ways that timeline-given leavings can still feel like abandonment. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for the broader case and [[open-hearted-vs-closed-hearted-leaving]] for the closely related distinction about the interior state of the leaver.

## What the Principle Asks of the Leaver

Three things, in order of priority:

1. **Have a real container.** Without a place to actually discharge the anger, the leaving will carry it. Build the container (the car practice, the room with the wall to press on, the woods) before the next fight.

2. **Make the leaving itself clean.** The transit from the room you are in to the container is not where the anger lives. It is the bridge. Walk the bridge with the anger held in your body, not aimed through your body at the partner you are leaving.

3. **Name what is happening if needed.** "I am going to go discharge this in [the container]. I will be back in [timeline]." The explicit naming converts the leaving from an act that could be read as aggression into an act that is read as care. See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for the timeline mechanics.

## The Body Test

There is a body test for whether the leaving is carrying the anger:
- Does your jaw soften as you walk out of the room? Or does it tighten?
- Are your shoulders dropping or rising as you cross the threshold?
- Is your gait fluid or is it driving?
- Does the door close with the weight of the door or with extra weight added?
- Is your last look back at the partner soft or sharp?

If the body markers all indicate added charge, the leaving is carrying the anger. The fix is not to soften the body performatively. The fix is to recognize that the body has more to discharge than can fit in the bridge and to either pause to release some of it before walking, or to accept that this leaving is not yet clean and to come back and do this conversation later, after a fuller discharge.

## What This Principle Is Not

This is not the requirement that the leaver be regulated before leaving. The whole point of leaving is to go somewhere to regulate. Demanding that the leaver be calm before leaving defeats the purpose. The principle is finer: the leaving can be charged inside (the anger is still in the body) without being charged outward (the anger is not being aimed at the partner through the act of leaving).

This is also not the requirement that the leaving be silent or affectless. The leaving can include a clear statement: "I am furious right now. I am going to go move this in the other room. I will be back in twenty minutes." The statement names the state. The naming does not aim the state.

## The Pair With Open-Hearted Leaving

This principle pairs with [[open-hearted-vs-closed-hearted-leaving]]. The two distinctions describe the same shift from different angles:
- "Open-hearted vs. closed-hearted" names the interior posture of the leaver.
- "Not letting the leaving be part of the anger" names the structural separation of the leaving from the discharge.

When both are operating, the leaving lands as care: the heart is open and the act is not carrying the anger. When one is missing, the leaving lands as wound — even when the timeline is given.

## Where This Sits in the Anger-Work Maturation

This principle is the bridge between Stage 1 and Stage 2 of [[stages-of-couple-anger-work-maturation]]. Stage 1 is "go discharge in another room while leaking on the way out." Stage 2 is "make a clean announcement plus timeline, then discharge."

The principle is the precise naming of what gets fixed in the transition from Stage 1 to Stage 2. The leaking on the way out is the leaving carrying the anger. The clean announcement plus timeline is the leaving structurally separated from the discharge.

A couple in Stage 1 who hears this principle named can often skip directly to Stage 2 because the naming makes visible what was previously diffuse.

## Why This Principle Is Hard

The principle is hard because the body in anger reaches for any available channel. The act of leaving is the most available channel that does not explicitly violate the relationship's no-yelling-at-each-other rule. The body uses it.

The fix is to give the body a channel it can actually use. The car. The room. The pillow. The body will gladly route the discharge to a real container instead of the leaving — but only if the real container is reliably available. The principle and the practice (see [[get-angry-in-the-car]]) are not separable. The principle without the practice is just suppression of the leaking, which produces other leaks.

## In Coaching

When a person describes leaving a fight and being told later that the leaving itself was the wound, ask:
- Was there an audible parting (door, sigh, last word)?
- What did your body feel like as you walked out?
- Did you have a real plan for what you were going to do?

The answers usually disambiguate. The clean leavings (real plan, soft body, no parting) tend to land well. The charged leavings (no plan, tight body, parting shot) tend to wound regardless of the timeline.

## Tone

The principle is delivered in the matter-of-fact tone of an observation that was hard-won through specific incidents. Joe's affirmation — "that's a great way to put it" — registers the precision. The principle is the kind of distinction that lands as obvious once named but is rarely articulated because the components are usually fused.
