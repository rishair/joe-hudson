# One-to-One Ratio of Heavy Emotion and Yummy Self-Care

## The Principle

Heavy emotional work has to be balanced with equal restoration. The rule of thumb: for every ten minutes spent with grief, hurt heart, or any intense emotion, give yourself ten minutes of yummy self-care. Not as a treat at the end. As an integrated part of the practice. The ratio is one to one.

The principle exists because emotional work without restoration burns out the nervous system, dysregulates the practitioner, and eventually causes the practitioner to abandon the work or develop a strained relationship to it. The work and the restoration are not separate; they are two halves of one rhythm.

"What I would also encourage is for every 10 minutes of being with grief or a hurt heart or a broken heart, give yourself 10 minutes of yummy self-care. So you're not just diving down in grief, like the — it's balanced one-to-one. With self-care." -- Tara Hawley

## Why the Ratio Matters

The body has a finite capacity for sustained intensity. Grief, in particular, is metabolically expensive. The nervous system runs at high activation during real grief work. The muscles work. The breath patterns shift. The face and chest hold for long periods. The body needs recovery time the same way it needs recovery time after physical exertion.

Practitioners who skip the recovery do not skip the cost. The cost shows up as exhaustion, dissociation, irritability, sleeplessness, or a sudden inability to access the emotion at all because the system has clamped down to protect itself. The clamping is not a personal failure; it is the body's correct response to being asked to do too much without rest.

The one-to-one ratio is the calibration that lets the practitioner do as much grief work as they need to do over weeks and months without breaking the instrument. It is the difference between a sustainable practice and a sprint that ends in burnout.

## "Yummy" Is the Operative Word

The self-care has to be enjoyable to count. A check-off list of self-care activities the practitioner does not actually enjoy will not regulate the nervous system. The body knows whether a thing is a treat or a chore. Only the treat regulates.

The vocabulary "yummy" carries the test: does this feel yummy to you right now? If yes, do it. If no, find what does. The list is personal and shifts moment to moment. What is yummy after one grief session may be different from what is yummy after another. The practitioner's only job is to track what their actual body is asking for.

Examples of yummy self-care (the list is illustrative; the practitioner builds their own):

- A hot bath.
- A walk in a place that feels good to the body.
- A meal that is satisfying to eat.
- A nap.
- Time with a pet.
- A massage.
- A warm drink in a comfortable chair.
- Music that the body wants to move to or be still with.
- Time in nature.
- Time with a person who is easy to be with.

The common feature: the body wants it. Not the head's idea of what should be restorative. The body's actual yes.

For more on this vocabulary as design principle, see [[make-the-practice-juicy]]. The same word — yummy, juicy — applies here: the restoration must be enjoyed to function.

## Why This Differs From "Reward After Work"

The principle is not "do the hard thing, then give yourself a reward." A reward frame keeps the work and the restoration as separate units, with the restoration earned by the suffering. The body does not respond well to that frame; it sets up the work as suffering and the restoration as compensation, and over time the practitioner avoids the work because the framing has made it punitive.

The one-to-one frame is different. The restoration is part of the practice. The body is engaged in a sustained rhythm of intensity and care. Both halves are the practice. The grief is not the hard part that earns the reward; the grief is the part of the rhythm in which depth is reached, and the care is the part of the rhythm in which the depth is integrated.

This is a small distinction in language that produces a large distinction in how the body holds the practice. The reward frame produces strain. The rhythm frame produces flow.

## What Happens Without the Balance

The standard failure mode for committed practitioners is to skip the self-care half. The reasoning is usually some version of: "the work is what matters; the self-care is indulgent; I should be able to handle more intensity." All three premises are wrong.

The work matters AND the self-care is part of what makes the work possible. The self-care is not indulgent; it is necessary infrastructure. "Should be able to handle more" is the should-configuration showing up in the practice itself — see [[should-as-threat]] and [[make-the-practice-juicy]].

The visible signs that the balance has failed:

- The practitioner reports feeling depleted between sessions.
- Sleep is disrupted.
- Irritability or short fuse with people around them.
- The emotion that was being worked stops showing up — the body has clamped down to recover.
- A pattern of starting a deep practice, going hard for two weeks, and then abandoning it.
- A sense of emotional work as a heavy obligation rather than as a meaningful practice.

When any of these show up, the diagnostic is the missing self-care. The correction is to add restoration time at a one-to-one ratio and watch what changes.

## How to Use the Ratio in Practice

The ratio is a guideline, not a stopwatch. The practitioner does not need to measure precisely. The instruction is more directional: roughly equal time in restoration as in heavy emotion. If a person spent twenty minutes crying, plan twenty minutes of something yummy after. If a person spent an hour in deep grief work, plan an hour of recovery (or several shorter restoration moments spread through the day adding up to roughly an hour).

The pacing can be:

- **Immediately after.** Cry for ten minutes; then take a hot bath for ten minutes. Tight cycle.
- **Same day.** Heavy session in the morning; restoration practices through the afternoon and evening.
- **Same week.** Concentrated emotional work for an hour; offset by extra restoration distributed across the following few days.

Any of the timing patterns work. The key is that the restoration happens, that it is enjoyed by the body, and that it is roughly proportional to the depth of the emotional work.

## What This Looks Like as Coaching

- When a client describes a heavy emotional practice they are doing without naming any restoration, ask what they do for restoration. The blank look is the diagnostic.
- When a client reports getting more dysregulated as they do more emotional work, suggest the ratio. Often the issue is that the ratio is currently zero to one.
- When prescribing emotional work, prescribe the restoration as part of the prescription. Do not leave the restoration as the client's optional follow-up.
- When a client reports the work has stopped producing results, look at whether the ratio has slipped. The body may have shut the access down to protect itself from over-work.

## Tone

Tara delivers this gently and practically. The word "yummy" is not accidental — it carries warmth, sensuality, permission. The instruction is not delivered as a serious clinical protocol. It is delivered as care for a person who is doing hard work. The tone itself models what self-care sounds like.
