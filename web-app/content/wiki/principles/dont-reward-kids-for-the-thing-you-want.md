# Don't Reward Kids for the Thing You Want Them to Do

## The Principle

The standard parenting model includes both correction (don't do that) and reward (great job, that was amazing). The first installs the failure-orientation. The second installs the external-reference orientation. Both train the child away from listening to themselves. Both teach the child that the authority on what they should be doing lives outside them — in the parent's approval or disapproval — rather than inside them.

> "We wouldn't shame our kids because that's teaching them not to listen to themselves. We didn't, um, say, great job, that was amazing. That we wouldn't reward our kids for stuff that we wanted them to do, because that was also helping them not listen to themselves." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The principle is the symmetric companion to [[attempt-over-success-in-parenting]]. That principle addresses what not to do on the correction side (don't correct outcomes; honor attempts). This principle addresses what not to do on the reward side (don't reward the behavior you want; don't install your approval as the operative motivator).

Both correction and reward share the same underlying mechanism: they make the parent's evaluation the determining input. The child develops the internal orientation "what does the parent want?" instead of "what do I want?" The internal compass is replaced by an external one. The child grows into an adult who cannot find their own wants because their wants were systematically overwritten by the wants of others.

## Why Rewards Look Helpful But Aren't

Rewards feel positive in the moment. The child smiles. The parent feels they have encouraged something. The behavior the parent wanted is reinforced. From outside this looks like a clean win — the child is encouraged, the behavior happens more.

The cost is invisible in the moment but compounds over years. The child learns that the way to know whether they did the right thing is to check the parent's face. They develop the habit of monitoring external response as the primary feedback channel. Their own felt sense of what was good or right or aligned gets weaker because it is no longer needed — the external evaluation is doing that work.

By the time the child is an adult, they are running on external-reference. They do work because someone told them it was good. They make decisions by polling people they trust. They cannot tell whether they want something or whether they are supposed to want something. The self-listening apparatus that would have answered these questions never developed because every developmental moment that would have built it was short-circuited by parental approval.

This is the upstream of much of the adult work Joe later does with clients. The "I don't know what I want" presenting concern, the inability to find one's own purpose, the chronic seeking of validation, the dependence on external approval — all of these have, in many cases, the same family-system origin: a childhood where the parents rewarded the right things at every step, and the child learned to track the rewards instead of tracking themselves.

## What the Principle Replaces

The standard parenting model says: reward the behavior you want, ignore or correct the behavior you don't. This is operant conditioning applied to children. It produces predictable short-term behavior change. It also produces the long-term cost named above.

The alternative the principle points at: stay out of the evaluation business. Let the child notice their own experience. Let them figure out for themselves whether they liked what happened, whether they want to do it again, whether it served them. The parent's role is presence and support, not validation.

This is harder than it sounds because the parental impulse to validate is strong. Watching a child do something well produces a natural desire to mark the moment with praise. The principle asks the parent to feel that desire and not act on it — or at least to act on it in ways that don't install external-reference (sharing in the moment, marveling alongside the child, asking what the child noticed).

What the parent can do instead of "great job":

- **Notice with the child.** "You did that." "I see you tried that." Description without evaluation.
- **Ask what the child noticed.** "What was that like for you?" "How does that feel?" The question turns the attention inward instead of toward the parent.
- **Be present without commenting.** Sometimes the right move is to just be there, available, not evaluating. The child does the noticing themselves.
- **Share genuine response if it arises naturally** — "wow" or laughter or surprise — but not strategic praise designed to reinforce.

The line is between authentic shared response (which the child reads as the parent being with them) and strategic praise (which the child reads as evaluation). The first builds connection. The second installs the external compass.

## The Connection to Procrastination

The principle has a direct line to the procrastination work Joe does with adult clients. The adult who grew up under heavy reward-conditioning often presents with:

- **Chronic procrastination on their own work.** They cannot motivate themselves to do things they care about because the motivational structure was always external (parents, teachers, employers). Without an external evaluator, the wanting doesn't operate. See [[i-procrastinate-only-on-the-thing-that-matters-most]] for the specific case.
- **High productivity on other people's work.** The same person who cannot do their own project is reliable, productive, and excellent at work assigned by someone else. The external structure is what makes the motivation work.
- **Difficulty knowing what they want.** The wanting apparatus was overwritten by tracking what others wanted. As adults, they ask "what should I want?" rather than "what do I want?"
- **The self-abuse that produces procrastination.** See [[procrastination-requires-self-abuse]]. The internal bully voice is often a residue of the external evaluator that was once the parent. The child internalized the evaluation function, and as an adult it runs as the inner critic.

The adult procrastination work, in these cases, is partly the work of installing the self-listening apparatus that was never built in childhood. See [[listen-to-yourself-one-minute-a-day]] for the operational practice. The work is slow because it is building something that should have been built earlier, but it is buildable.

## The Symmetric Structure: Both Praise and Shame Install the Same Thing

The structural insight that organizes the principle: praise and shame are not opposites. They are the same move with opposite signs. Both make the parent the evaluator. Both teach the child to track external response. Both install self-abandonment as the operating mode.

> "That's the core of self-abuse, and therefore that's the core of procrastination, right?" -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The self-abuse engine of procrastination is the residue of the evaluation function being external. As children, the evaluation came from parents. As adults, the evaluation runs internally — but it is still the parents' voice, still external in its origin, still treating the self as object-to-be-evaluated rather than as subject-doing-the-evaluating.

The parenting move that prevents this is to never put the parent in the evaluator seat in the first place. The child grows up doing things because they want to or noticing for themselves whether they liked what they did. The evaluation function develops as their own, not as an internalized other.

## What Joe Did Instead

Joe describes the orientation he and his partner held with their kids:

> "For us, it was like an incredibly important thing to, to do that." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The work of withholding both correction and reward is not passive. It requires the parents to actively resist the impulse to evaluate. The default cultural script for parenting includes constant evaluation, and most parents are running that script unreflectively. Joe and his partner had to consciously not run it.

What this looks like operationally — the kid does something interesting, draws something, builds something, attempts something difficult. The parental impulse is to respond with "wow, great job." The disciplined alternative is to be present with what the child is doing, possibly comment factually ("I see you drew the dog"), possibly ask a question ("what's the dog doing?"), and stay out of the evaluation. The child gets to develop their own relationship with what they made.

Over years, this produces a child who has access to their own response to their own work. Their first reference is what they think of what they did. They can hear other people's reactions and weigh them, but the other people's reactions are inputs, not the source. The internal compass developed because nothing was installed in its place.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not mean parents should be emotionally absent or withhold connection. Connection is essential. The principle is specifically about evaluation — the moves where the parent's response is encoding "you did the right thing" or "you did the wrong thing." Connection without evaluation is the goal. Presence without rewards. Being with the child without grading the child.

The principle also does not mean the parent never has preferences or never expresses them. The parent is a person with values. Sharing those values is fine. The line is between "I notice I felt sad when you said that to your sister" (sharing the parent's experience) and "good kids don't say that to their sister" (installing the parent as evaluator).

The principle does not eliminate teaching. Teaching happens through doing-with, modeling, offering, asking. It does not have to happen through evaluating outcomes. The teaching that requires evaluation (the elite cellist case, the safety-critical case) operates in specific domains; the home does not have to be one of those domains. See [[attempt-over-success-in-parenting]] for the parallel discussion of where evaluation might appropriately operate.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with the warmth of a parent who has lived it. The naming "we wouldn't shame our kids... we wouldn't reward our kids for stuff that we wanted them to do" is matter-of-fact, not preachy. The principle is offered as obvious to anyone who has thought about what praise actually trains — but the implication that the standard cultural script needs to be inverted is delivered without apology.
