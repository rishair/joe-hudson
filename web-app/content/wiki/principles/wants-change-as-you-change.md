# Your Wants Change As You Change

## The Principle

As a person does the work and starts to see the world and themselves differently, what they want changes. Often it changes substantially. Wants that felt central in one phase of life become irrelevant in the next. Wants that did not register before become foundational.

This evolution often gets misread, by the person experiencing it, as a loss of desire or as desire becoming "diffuse." The person remembers the urgency of what they used to want and notices that the same urgency is not present now, and concludes that desire itself has weakened. The more accurate read: desire has not weakened. It has changed targets. The old wants no longer pull because they were tied to who the person used to be, and the person is no longer that.

"As we start to see the world differently and interact with the world differently, our desires change." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

## Joe's Tara Example

Joe gives a personal example that makes the principle concrete. When he and Tara first dated, she was young and conventionally beautiful. He noticed nitpicky things in his mind -- the curve of one part of her body or another, small features that did not quite match what he thought he wanted.

Now they are both middle-aged. They do not look the way they did at twenty. And he cannot find any fault in her when he looks at her.

"What I thought I wanted then is not at all what I want now. So my wants have changed over time, and it's more a reflection of the work that I do. And so if I used to be motivated by finding the perfect body to be with, I'm no longer motivated by that. And what I want has completely changed." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The wants did not get suppressed. They did not get moralized away. They were not replaced by a more virtuous list of preferences. They simply became uninteresting as the person who held them became someone else. The new wants that took their place came from the new person, not from an effort to want better things.

## The Diagnostic Question

When someone reports that their wants are becoming diffuse or that they cannot feel motivated, the diagnostic Joe offers is to ask back:

"How do you know that your desires are becoming more diffuse? Or are your desires changing and you're finding your more authentic desires? That would be the question." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The reframe is critical. "Diffuse" implies dispersal, weakening, fade. "Changing" or "becoming more authentic" implies replacement and refinement.

A test for which one is actually happening: notice that from the moment you wake up until two hours later, you have made roughly 100 choices with 100 preferences. The cup, the breakfast, the route, the order of tasks. Each of those is a preference. The system is producing preferences constantly. If diffuseness were really happening, the morning would not contain those preferences. It does. So what looks like diffuseness is more likely a difference in the kind of want being noticed or a phase of integration.

"There's clarity of preference, otherwise you'd just be in bed, and even that may be a preference, right? So the question is, are they becoming more diffuse, or are you learning to listen to yourself differently and therefore don't understand the really big movements?" -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

## Three Possible Explanations When Wants Feel Off

When a person reports that their wants are not what they used to be and is uncertain what is happening, the explanation is usually one of these (and sometimes more than one):

**1. The wants have actually changed.** The old wants belonged to an old version of the self. The new self has different wants and is still learning to listen to them. The work is to attend to the new wants as they make themselves visible.

**2. The person is in an integration phase, not an action phase.** Doing the work alternates between periods of going out and acting (which surface what one wants by testing) and periods of integration (which assimilate what was learned and reshape the underlying wants). The integration phase looks like reduced wanting from the outside, but it is part of how the next round of clear wanting becomes possible.

"There's this moment of going out and acting and doing the experiments to see what I want. There's a moment of integration of what I've learned and seeing how my motivation, my wants change. So I would experiment and play with that, see how true that is for you." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

**3. The nervous system needs rest.** The person was operating on adrenaline for a long time, pushing through, "I have to, I have to." As that subsides, the body needs to recover before it has the bandwidth to want big new things. This is not the wants changing; it is the wants being temporarily masked by exhaustion.

"You were really operating on adrenaline for a long time. You were really operating on pushing yourself and, 'Oh my God, I have to,' and da, da, da. And as that subsides, you just need to rest. You're just going to need to let the nervous system recover before you're ready to go and dig your next big well or start your next company or whatever it is." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

See [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]] for the full treatment of explanation 3.

## Why This Principle Matters

Without this principle, a person doing the work can panic at the natural evolution of their wants. They notice that the things they used to chase have stopped pulling and conclude that the work has broken something in them. They reach back for the old wants out of fear of having no wants at all. They effortfully try to recover the urgency that was tied to the old self.

This recovery effort is itself the friction. The old wants do not come back because the person they belonged to does not exist anymore. The new wants are present but quieter and require attention to notice. The recovery effort distracts from the attention required to hear the new wants.

The corrective frame: wants change is normal. The change is evidence the work is working, not evidence it has gone wrong. Trust the evolution. Notice what is actually wanting now, rather than reaching for what used to want.

## The Connection to Craving and Connection

Brett's articulation in the same Q&A connects this principle to two adjacent distinctions:

"I think a lot of us have been used to for much of our lives associating wanting with craving. And when the craving falls away, we wonder where the wanting is, but there's still wants there. And I think this also relates back to Dominique's previous question. I think a lot of times we conflate connection with clinging. And when we find ourselves no longer clinging and craving something from somebody, then we might feel more alone but not lonely." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A January 2023

Joe affirms this directly:

"All of our emotional states, as we learn to love them, as we're not resisting them, as we're not putting tension in the line and letting them move through, they all become less intense. And sometimes we're like, that's a relief. And sometimes we're like, wait, where'd that go?" -- Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

The mechanism: what felt like wanting was often [[wanting-vs-craving|craving]] -- the gripping, sticky, painful version. What felt like connection was often clinging. As the gripping releases through the work, the underlying clean form of the same thing becomes available. But the clean form has a different texture, often less intense, sometimes initially unrecognizable as the same thing.

The trap: mistaking the loss of the gripping form for the loss of the underlying thing. The clean form is still there. It is just quieter and requires more refined attention to notice.

## Intensity Migrates

A related principle that Brett surfaces and Joe affirms: intensity does not disappear from life. It moves. The intensity that was on the craving migrates to something else as the craving dissolves.

"That doesn't mean that life is less intense, it just might mean the intensity moves to something else that we're not used to experiencing intensely, like joy. Or intensely enjoying feeling sadness." -- Brett Kistler / Joe Hudson, Q&A January 2023

Joe extends: "Or all of a sudden the thing that was like I could live with because I didn't feel it as intensely, now I can't live with. So there's the intensity of being more sensitive."

This means the experience of "I do not feel intensely anymore" is rarely accurate at the system level. The intensity is somewhere. It has moved from the things it used to inhabit. It now inhabits things that may not have registered as intense before. Joy. Sadness. Sensitivity to small misalignments that used to be invisible.

The work is to find where the intensity has gone, not to mourn that it has left.

## In Coaching

When someone reports that their wants are becoming diffuse:

1. Reframe with Joe's question: "How do you know your desires are becoming more diffuse? Or are your desires changing and you're finding your more authentic desires?"

2. Check the 100-preferences test. Have them notice that they have already made many small preferences today. The system is still producing wanting.

3. Run the three diagnostics: are the wants actually changing, is the person in integration, or is the nervous system recovering?

4. If the wants are actually changing: invite attention to the new wants as they make themselves visible. Do not push them to recover the old wants.

5. If in integration: name the phase. Stop expecting clear action. Let the integration complete.

6. If nervous system recovery: see [[nervous-system-recovery-after-stress]]. Let the body sit down.

## Tone

Reassuring and slightly amused. The structural confusion -- "where did all my wants go?" -- is a recognizable place in the work. Joe's example with Tara is delivered with warmth. The principle is offered as relief from a worry that does not need to be the worry it has become.
