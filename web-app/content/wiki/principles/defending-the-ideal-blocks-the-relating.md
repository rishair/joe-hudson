# Defending the Ideal of the Relationship Blocks the Actual Relating

## The Principle

A relationship has an actual shape, which is whatever is happening between the people right now. It also has an idea, which is the picture of what the relationship is supposed to be -- what was agreed, what was hoped for, what the early version was, what a good version would look like. When the idea is defended as if it were reality, the actual relating cannot proceed. The energy that would have gone into meeting what is here goes into preserving what is supposed to be here.

The principle generalizes the death-acknowledgement work of [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] to all relating: the moment-to-moment dying of each version of the relationship is the structure that makes the next version possible. Defending an old version prevents the next.

The frame Brett offers in conversation with Joe:

"If you have the ideal of a relationship and you're defending that that ideal is reality rather than discovering what reality is throughout the process and allowing your ideas of it to die, then more and more in your life has to be some form of swimming back upstream against the tide." -- Brett Kistler, How Death Brings Life Closer

The image is precise. The river is flowing one direction (toward what is actually present in the relationship). The defender of the ideal is paddling the other direction (toward what the relationship is supposed to be). The longer the defending continues, the more effortful it becomes, because the river keeps flowing.

## What the Ideal Looks Like in Practice

The ideal is rarely conscious. It usually shows up as the unspoken standard against which the actual relating gets measured and found wanting.

- The ideal of the early infatuation, preserved as the standard for what the relationship should still feel like
- The ideal of the partner who never gets triggered, preserved as the standard against which actual triggering gets framed as failure
- The ideal of the sibling relationship as it was in childhood, preserved as the standard against which adult sibling relating gets framed as drift
- The ideal of the marriage agreement made at 26, preserved as the standard against which the agreement at 50 gets framed as betrayal
- The ideal of the friendship as it was when both people lived in the same city, preserved as the standard against which long-distance friendship gets framed as deficient
- The ideal of the parent-child relationship as it was when the child was 12, preserved as the standard against which the adult child relating gets framed as estrangement

In each case, the ideal is doing the same work: providing a measuring stick that the actual relating cannot meet, because the actual relating is the next version of the relationship, not the preserved version of an earlier one.

## What Defending the Ideal Costs

The cost is the relating itself. The energy that would have gone into meeting the partner who is actually here goes into mourning, complaining about, or fighting against the absence of the partner who is supposed to be here.

The cost shows up as:
- Chronic resentment that the relationship is no longer the relationship it once was
- A persistent sense of having been betrayed by the other person changing
- A persistent sense of having been betrayed by the relationship itself
- Withdrawal of presence from the actual person, in service of preserving the felt-sense of the ideal
- A felt-sense of being alone in a relationship that, by external measures, is still ongoing
- A felt-sense that nothing the partner does now counts, because it is not what the ideal called for

This is the dynamic underneath much of what shows up as [[we-have-built-up-resentment]] in long marriages. The resentment is not primarily about what the partner did or did not do. It is about the gap between what the partner did and what the ideal said the partner should have done. Without the ideal, the same behavior would be met as data about who is actually here. With the ideal in place, the same behavior is evidence of the partner's failure to be the partner of the ideal.

## What Allowing the Ideas to Die Looks Like

The alternative is not to have no ideas. It is to hold the ideas lightly enough that they can be revised by what is actually happening.

The structure is the same as the structure of [[every-moment-is-an-exit-point]]. Each moment in the relationship is an exit point for the previous moment's version. The version of the relationship that existed five minutes ago is gone. What is here now is what is here now. The work is to meet what is here now, not to defend what was here five minutes ago.

Applied to the relationship-ideal: the version of the relationship that existed five years ago is gone. The version that existed when you got married is gone. The version that existed before the kids is gone. What is here now is what is here now. The work is to discover what this new version is rather than to defend the rights of the old version against it.

This is the basis of [[agreements-renegotiate-as-you-change]]: the only commitment is to the relationship itself, and the relationship is whatever it is right now, not whatever it was supposed to be when the agreements were made.

It is also the basis of [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]]: the marriage dies many times across its life, and the willingness to grieve each death is what allows the next version to come alive.

## Why This Is Connected to Acknowledging Death

The principle is structurally an application of the death-acknowledgement work to the relational domain. Each moment that the partner is alive is a moment in which the previous version of the partner has died. Each moment that the relationship continues is a moment in which the previous version of the relationship has died. The willingness to acknowledge these small ongoing deaths is the willingness to be in the actual relationship rather than in a defended idea of it.

Brett's framing in the December 2024 conversation makes this connection explicit. He has been talking about literal death (his brother's diagnosis) and pivots to relational death: "Even a short enough timeline, you know, like, and this can happen in a relationship too. If you have the ideal of a relationship and you're defending..." The "this can happen in a relationship too" marks the move. The same structural recognition that the literal-death conversation has been making available is now being applied to the ongoing relationship.

The visceral acknowledgement of mortality compresses life. The visceral acknowledgement of relational impermanence compresses the relationship. Both produce the same result: the actual moment in front of the person becomes available, no longer subordinated to a projected continuity that was always partly imagined.

## What This Looks Like With Someone Who Is Dying

The principle has a particularly clear application when someone in the relationship is actually dying. The ideal of "we will have many more years" is no longer plausibly defendable. The actual situation -- a finite, unknown amount of time, a person who is who they are now, a relationship that is whatever it is in this moment -- becomes hard to look away from.

This is the structural reason that terminal diagnoses often produce deepening in relationships that had previously felt stagnant. The structural defense of the ideal collapses, because the ideal becomes obviously unsustainable. What is left is the actual relating, which the participants now have to meet directly because they cannot postpone it to the imagined future.

See [[bretts-brother-scott]] for Brett's account of how the diagnosis of his brother dissolved a long-running ideal about who-left-whom and freed up the actual relating that had been buried under it.

The corollary: the same deepening is available without the diagnosis. What the diagnosis forces, the principle can offer voluntarily. The willingness to let the ideas of the relationship die in each moment produces the same access to the actual relating that the diagnosis produces by force.

## Boundary: Discovering Reality Is Not Lowering Standards

The principle is not "expect less of your relationships." It is "discover what is actually here rather than defending what is supposed to be here."

A relationship that is actually contemptuous, neglectful, or abusive is not improved by abandoning the ideal of mutual respect. The principle does not collapse into resigned acceptance. The discovery of what is actually here can produce a decision that what is actually here is not workable, in which case the work becomes [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] or the conclusion that the relationship needs to end. See [[apathy-vs-disdain-as-end-of-marriage]] for the marker that the relating has actually died.

The discrimination: the discovery of reality is the basis on which any decision about the relationship can be made. The defense of the ideal blocks the discovery and therefore blocks any clear decision.

## When This Comes Up in Coaching

- When a person describes a relationship as not being what it once was
- When a person is exhausted from managing the gap between what the partner is doing and what they should be doing
- When a person has been carrying chronic resentment in a long relationship and the question is what to do with the resentment
- When a person is processing a terminal diagnosis of someone they love and a long-running story about that person is starting to dissolve
- When the question is whether to stay in a relationship and the assessment is being made against the ideal rather than the actual
- When [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] is being introduced and the precondition is that the partner be met as the partner who is here, not as the failed version of the partner who should have been here
- When a parent-child relationship is being inquired into and the parent is being measured against the parent-from-childhood rather than discovered as the parent-who-is-here
