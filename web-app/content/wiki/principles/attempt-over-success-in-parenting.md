# Attempt Over Success in Parenting (Did You Try? Great.)

## The Principle

For a child to develop the iterative mindset, the operating frame in their home has to be: the attempt is what matters, not whether the attempt produced the right result. The parent who praises attempts and stays out of correcting outcomes raises a child who can iterate. The parent who corrects outcomes and ignores attempts raises a child who installs the failure-orientation and inherits the critical voice.

"For us, it was just always: Did you try? Great." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

The principle is simple to state and difficult to live. Most parental impulses run the other way. The kid does the dishes wrong; the parent corrects. The kid sets the table wrong; the parent fixes. The kid spells the word wrong; the parent points it out. Each correction installs the same lesson: the right outcome is what matters and the parent is the judge of right.

Over years, the child stops trying things they cannot do perfectly. The critical voice that the child grew up under becomes the critical voice they carry as an adult. See [[my-inner-critic-wont-stop]].

## The 2.5-Year-Old Setting the Table

Joe's example: when his kids were about 2.5 to 3 years old, they were setting the table. The table was higher than them. Their hands at that age do not go fully over their heads -- their wrists go to about ear level when they try. So they were holding the dish above their head and sliding it onto the table.

"And to us, that was really important. It was really important for them to know that they were capable. And yeah, we broke some dishes. We broke some dishes, you know, like, but to us, that was the framing. The framing was the attempt was more important than the success." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

The dish-sliding-from-above-the-head was the attempt. Some dishes broke. The dish breaking was not the metric. The kid setting the table at all -- using a developmentally-impossible technique because that was the technique their bodies were capable of -- was the metric. The kid learned: I can do this. The kid learned: my body is capable. The kid learned: my parents see me trying.

The lost dishes were the cost. The cost was small relative to what was being installed. The parent who refuses to pay the cost (who steps in to set the table to protect the dishes) is choosing the dishes over the child's developing sense of capability.

## What Parents Often Do Instead

Joe describes Esme talking about a family they observed correcting their kid doing the dishes:

"That kid, they're learning that they're a failure. They're learning that they can't even wash dishes without being corrected. To me, that's a deeply disempowering move that people make." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

The parent who corrects the kid doing the dishes is not malicious. They want clean dishes. They want the kid to learn the right way. They believe correction is teaching. But what is actually being taught is the larger lesson the correction sits inside: you cannot do this without me overseeing it. The dishes get cleaner. The kid's sense of capability gets smaller.

Multiply across the thousands of small corrections that happen in a childhood and the adult emerges with the operating belief that whatever they do is not quite right. The voice in the head saying "you screwed up" is the parent who corrected the dishes, internalized.

## Joe's Operational Move

When Joe sees his kids mess up on something:

"Sometimes I'm like, I just don't even say anything. Maybe I'll go back and fix it, but I won't let them know about it. Or maybe I'll be like, hey, you know, here's this other component. Do you want to think about doing that? How do you want to do that? But I'm not gonna criticize. I'm not gonna step in and do it." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

Three operational variants, each calibrated to the situation:

**Say nothing, fix it later silently.** The kid did their best version. The parent has higher standards. The parent meets their own higher standards quietly without making the kid carry the gap. The kid learns: I am capable. The parent gets the result they wanted. Nothing was installed.

**Offer the next component, not the correction.** "Hey, here's this other component. Do you want to think about doing that?" The frame is not "you missed this." The frame is "there's more available." The kid can take it or leave it. The agency stays with them.

**Ask, do not assert.** "How do you want to do that?" The parent does not assert the right method. The parent asks the kid to articulate their approach. The articulation itself is part of the learning. The kid's method becomes more conscious; the parent stays out of imposing.

What Joe does not do: criticize, step in, take over. The criticism and the takeover are the moves that install the failure-orientation. The parent who cannot help themselves from intervening is the parent who is raising the next generation's critical voice.

## The Exception: Becoming the World's Best Cellist

Joe is careful to bound the principle. The principle covers normal life. The principle does not necessarily cover high-elite performance development.

"It just doesn't help them have the self-esteem, doesn't help them be empowered, doesn't help them learn. None of that works, at least for normal life. And maybe when it comes to like being the best cellist in the world, there's some criticism needed. I don't know." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

Brett offers a refinement: "critique" might be the right word for what high-elite development needs, which is different from "criticism." Critique can be done without installing the failure-orientation; criticism cannot. Joe takes the refinement.

The bound: the principle is about the parenting of normal life. The cellist in training may need a different framing in their cellist-training arena (which is closer to a [[performance-vs-play]] performance arena than to ordinary family life). The home is still the place where the attempt-over-success principle operates, even for the elite-track kid.

The cellist case is also why [[performance-vs-play]] matters: the elite cellist needs a play arena (the practice room, where iteration without performance-stakes happens) and a performance arena (the recital, where the trained skill performs). The home, in the attempt-over-success treatment, is the play arena. The recital is the performance arena. The critique that helps the cellist is in the performance arena; it would harm the kid if it dominated the home.

## Why the Principle Produces Better Outcomes

The kid who grows up under attempt-over-success develops:

- **A larger sense of personal capability.** They tried things and were not corrected. They formed the belief that they can do things.
- **An iterative orientation to learning.** When a thing did not work, the response in the family was not "you failed" but "what's the next attempt." This becomes the inner orientation in adulthood.
- **A quieter critical voice.** The voice is built from the parental voice the child grew up with. The parent who praised attempts produces a child whose internal voice praises attempts.
- **More willingness to try the next hard thing.** Each attempt was met without backlash. The kid does not fear the attempt because the attempt has never produced backlash.
- **A bottom that holds when things go wrong.** The family relationship survived the broken dishes. The kid learns: the relationship is bigger than the outcome. They carry that into adult relationships.

The kid who grows up under outcome-correction develops the inverse. The list reads as a description of the adult clients Joe sees: small sense of capability, achievement-oriented frame, loud critical voice, fear of trying the next thing, brittle bottom that collapses when outcomes go wrong.

The parenting principle is the upstream intervention on most of the adult work the client will later need to do. The parent who applies the principle is doing decades of preemptive coaching for their kid.

## Connection to the Larger Framework

This principle is the family-system version of [[reward-failure-in-the-organization]]. The same structural insight, scaled down to the family. The parent is the leader. The kid is the team member. The dishes are the iteration. The breakage is the cost of iteration. The frame the parent uses determines what kind of person the kid develops into.

The principle is also the family-system upstream of [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]]. The kid who grew up under attempt-over-success is less likely to organize their adult life around the assumption that they need to be fixed. The kid who grew up under correction is more likely to enter adulthood already running the self-improvement project, because the parent installed the orientation.

The principle connects to [[people-want-you-in-it-with-them-not-advice]]: the kid wants the parent in it with them, not advising them from above. Praising the attempt is being in it with them. Correcting the outcome is doing it to them.

## What This Principle Is Not

The principle is not "never teach your kid anything." Teaching happens. The teaching happens in the form of being in the activity with the kid, modeling, sometimes offering the next component to consider. The teaching does not happen in the form of "you did this wrong."

The principle is not "ignore the broken dishes." The broken dishes are real. The parent can notice the broken dishes, the parent can teach the kid how to hold the dish more securely, the parent can change the dishes to less-breakable ones if the cost is too high. What the parent does not do is take the broken dish as evidence that the kid should not be setting the table.

The principle is not "praise every attempt indiscriminately." The praise is not the technique. The technique is letting the attempt count, which often means saying nothing at all about the outcome and just letting the kid feel their own capability. Manufactured praise ("great job!") is its own problem and can install a different kind of dependence.

The principle does not eliminate consequences. Natural consequences (the broken dish has to be cleaned up; the kid participates in the cleanup) are different from imposed criticism. The natural consequence teaches without installing the failure-orientation.

## Tone

Joe delivers this principle with the warmth of a parent who has lived it. The voice is direct, sometimes amused. The broken-dishes story is told with a "yeah, we broke some dishes" that signals: this was the cost, we paid it gladly, this is what it looks like in practice. The principle is offered as obvious to anyone who has watched the alternative play out.
