# The Only Thing You Were Ever Stuck With Was the Thoughts

## The Principle

When a chronically stuck person fully works through their pattern, the recognition that arrives is that the actual stuckness was never the situation. It was always the thoughts about the situation. Strip the thoughts away and the trap disappears, even when the external constraints remain.

"What I see when people really solve this puzzle about feeling stuck all the time in their life, and one of the things that I notice that is really clear in them is that the only thing they were ever stuck with was the thoughts. It was a set of thoughts." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

This is the principle that the unsticking work converges on. After the nervous-system release, the emotional movement, and the intellectual recognition, what becomes plainly visible is that the cage was made of thoughts. The bars had no other substance.

## The Gravity Example

The structural setup for the principle is given through a homely example. We are stuck with many things. Gravity. The sun. The Earth. But we don't feel stuck by them. The stuckness is not a feature of the constraint; it is a feature of how we relate to the constraint.

"For instance, we're stuck with gravity. We're stuck with the sun. We're stuck on Earth. But we don't feel stuck in these ways. Typically, most of us don't. Maybe a couple, couple people do." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

"So it's a feeling. It's not a reality, meaning that we're stuck in tons of ways and we don't feel stuck. And oftentimes we feel stuck in ways that we actually aren't." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The gravity example does specific work. It separates two questions that are usually conflated: "Am I constrained?" and "Do I feel stuck?" The answer to the first is almost always yes; humans are constrained in countless ways. The answer to the second can be either yes or no, and is independent of the first. The same constraints can be experienced as either trap or simply the conditions of being.

## The Test Case: If You're Not Scared of Death

The cleanest stress-test of the principle:

"It's -- if you're not scared of death, just to be extreme about it, person with a gun can't oppress you. You can't be stuck. You just have some choices to make." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

A person with a gun is, by most measures, the maximal external constraint. They can end your life. They can take what you have. They control the immediate situation. And yet, if you are not afraid of death, the person with the gun cannot oppress you. You have choices. You can comply or not. You can speak or not. You can fight or not. The fear of death is what makes the gun-bearer's threat into oppression. Remove the fear, and the situation is just choices.

The example is extreme on purpose. It demonstrates the principle at the limit. If even the gun-with-the-death-threat is not stuck-making when the fear is gone, then nothing in ordinary life is stuck-making without the corresponding fear.

The corollary: stuckness in the office, in the marriage, in the depression, in the body of emotions -- all of these are made of fears that, if dropped, would convert the stuckness into choices.

## The Prison Cell Examples

Two specific examples that test the principle in extreme confinement:

**Example one: prisoners who were not stuck.** "There are many people who have been in prison cells for a very long time and they were not stuck. The moment they got out, they were moving. As though they had been cooking that entire time. Their consciousness constantly transforming into whatever it was that made them." -- Joe Hudson and Brett Kistler, How To Get Unstuck

The most famous case is Mandela's 27 years (see [[mandela-empowerment]]). Mandela was in a physical cell. He was constrained in ways most people will never experience. He was not stuck. His consciousness continued to transform; he came out moving, with developed political and ethical capacity that the cell could not contain.

**Example two: the monks in tiny cells.** "3 years in prison for one person is completely stuck. 3 years in, what is it like, I think some monks that they're in like for 3 years or 2 years or something, they're in a 2.5 by 2.5 foot room and they can't even lay down. It's like far harsher. And they call that, like awakening. Somebody else calls it stuck in a prison." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

Two people in roughly comparable confinement -- one in prison, one in a monastic retreat -- experience radically different things. One is stuck; one is awakening. The constraint is roughly the same. The relationship to the constraint is opposite. The relationship is what determines the experience.

**Example three: the long darkness retreat.** "I've heard of somebody who did like a 6-year meditation, silent darkness retreat in a house, and afterwards they were like, well, that was a waste of time." -- Brett Kistler, How To Get Unstuck

This example does additional work. Even the same person's retrospective on the same experience can be either profound freedom ("that was a waste of time" said with the freedom of someone who tried it and is done with it) or shame-cycle stuckness ("that was a waste of time" said with the bitter regret of someone who feels they wasted years). Same words. Two completely different felt-states.

"It's a very specific nuance on how you want to frame that internally." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The principle is consistent across all three examples: the external situation does not determine the felt-state. The internal framing does.

## The Brain's Two-Choice Collapse

The mechanism by which thoughts produce stuckness:

"What's actually happening there is that they feel like, I don't want this consequence and I don't want this consequence, and my brain can't show me anything besides these two consequences. And so I'm going to go into one of those places and I'm stuck." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The brain collapses the option-space to two bad consequences. Both are unacceptable. The person freezes between them. The freezing is the stuckness. The two-choice collapse is the thought-structure that produces the freezing.

The option-space is almost never actually two-choice. There are usually many options. The collapse is a feature of how the brain is processing the situation, not a feature of the situation itself. The work is to see that the option-space is not what the brain is reporting it to be.

This is structurally similar to [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]]. Binary thinking is a fear-state. When the brain is reporting "only two options and both bad," the system is in fear-mode and other options are not being scanned for.

## The Three Cognitive Distortions Underneath the Stuckness-Thought

A finer-grained articulation of what the "thoughts" the principle names actually are. Stuckness is not produced by a single thought; it is produced by three cognitive distortions stacked together. Each is a specific way fear shapes the mind's processing of a situation. Together they construct the felt experience of having nowhere to go.

**The option-space collapse.** The mind narrows the visible options to a small set. The person sees "ask or don't ask" rather than the many actual ways to ask. See [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]].

**The false end.** The mind freezes time at the worst predicted moment of each option and treats that frozen moment as the permanent state. The boss's Tuesday reaction is treated as the boss's permanent verdict. The fact that people change their minds about each other constantly is excluded from the calculation. See [[change-your-mind-frequently-about-others]].

**The safety-vs-risk inversion.** The mind labels not-acting as the safe choice when the math is the other way around. Staying in the position that has produced the stuckness is usually the higher-risk position over any meaningful timeline; the iteration that feels risky is usually the safer one. See [[safety-is-in-evolution-not-stagnation]].

All three distortions are thoughts. All three are the "thoughts" the principle names as the only thing the person was ever stuck with. The synthesis is: strip the three distortions away, and the cage disappears even when the external situation is unchanged.

"There are many issues with being scared of the consequences. One of the issues with being scared of the consequences is that you're thinking that there's only one way to do it... I'm just thinking in a binary way, yes or no... The other thing that's happening because I am in fear is that I see this false end... So we're sitting here thinking that we're stuck because we're scared of the consequences, and the fear of the consequences is making it so that we can't see all the optionality that we have." -- Joe Hudson, The More Stuck You Feel, The Closer You Are To Freedom

The cognitive-layer treatment of stuckness is the explicit unwinding of these three distortions. The felt-amplification treatment is the body-first complement that releases the fear underneath the cognitive distortions. Both paths arrive at the same place: the cage was made of thoughts; once the thoughts are recognized as thoughts, the cage is no longer operative. See [[fear-of-consequences-creates-stuck]] for the concept that organizes the cognitive-layer treatment.

## The Convergence with the Earlier Move

This principle is what comes into focus after the full sequence:

1. Question the fact-frame: are you stuck or do you feel stuck? See [[are-you-stuck-or-do-you-feel-stuck]].
2. Work the nervous-system layer: double the freeze. See [[double-the-freeze]].
3. Work the emotional layers: anger first, then fear. See [[anger-first-then-fear-out-of-stuck]].
4. Recognize the self-oppression: you are doing what you're avoiding. See [[feeling-stuck-is-self-oppression]].
5. Be yourself despite consequences. See [[be-yourself-despite-consequences-undoes-stuckness]].

After all of that, the principle that becomes clearly visible is: the stuckness was always just thoughts. The cage had no other substance. Once the work has been done, the principle is not theory; it is direct observation.

The principle isn't meant to be deployed prematurely. Telling a stuck person "it's just thoughts" before they have done the felt work usually produces more shame. The principle becomes operative when the work has produced enough clearing that the person can actually see it.

## The Universal Decay Reframe

A small extension of the principle:

"It is not an actuality. We're all evolving until we die and then we're all decomposing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The truth that no one is ever actually stuck. Everyone is changing every moment, whether they are aware of it or not. Cells are dying and being replaced. Thoughts are arising and dissolving. The body is moving toward its end. The world is changing around the person. The only thing that is genuinely static is the felt-experience of stuckness, which is itself a process the body is generating moment by moment.

The reframe is humble and grounding. The constant motion of being alive contradicts the felt-fact of stuckness. The motion is happening regardless. The recognition that everything is in motion is part of what loosens the felt-grip of stuckness.

## When This Principle Is Operative

This principle lands best when:

- The unsticking work has been done and the person needs the synthesis statement
- The person is ready for the more universal frame (early in the work, the frame can produce shame; late in the work, it produces freedom)
- The person has seen the option-space open and can hold the recognition that the closure was thought-made
- The person has examples of others (Mandela, the monks, etc.) that they can connect to their own experience
- The work is shifting from the specific stuckness to the larger relationship to constraint

It is not the right principle when:

- The person is in the early stages and hasn't done the felt work yet (premature deployment produces shame)
- The person is in acute crisis where the constraint is genuinely immediate and dangerous
- The person needs the validation of their felt-experience before the framing can land
- The work is on a specific situation that does require external action, not just reframing

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not "your problems aren't real." The constraints are real. The principle is about the difference between the constraints and the stuck-feeling. The constraints can stay; the stuck-feeling can lift. The two are separable.

It is not bypass. The principle does not skip the felt work. It is what becomes available after the felt work. Trying to deploy the principle without the felt work just produces a thinking-loop about how the person should be free of their thoughts, which is itself thoughts producing stuckness.

It is not the claim that all situations are reframable. Some situations call for action -- leaving the marriage, quitting the job, getting out of the dangerous environment. The principle does not say "stay and reframe." It says "the stuckness about whether to leave is made of thoughts." Once the stuckness lifts, the action becomes clear, and the action may well be to leave.

It is not "just change your thoughts." Thoughts change when the body changes. The principle is what becomes visible when the body has cleared enough that the thoughts can be seen as thoughts rather than as facts. The work is body-first, recognition-second.

## Tone

Grounding, slightly reverent. The principle has weight because it is what unsticks the long-stuck. Joe holds it with the recognition of how much work goes into being able to see it. The tone is not "this is obvious" but "this is what becomes clear when the work has been done."

The Mandela and monk examples are delivered with quiet respect. These are humans who lived the principle at extreme limits. The principle is not invented; it is observed in the people who have lived it.
