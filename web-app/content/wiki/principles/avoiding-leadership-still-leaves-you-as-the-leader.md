# Avoiding Leadership Still Leaves You as the Leader

## The Principle

The person who refuses leadership to avoid the exposure of failing in it has not exited the position. They are still in it. The withholding from leadership is itself a leadership move — and the consequences of failing in the position still land on them. The only thing the refusal accomplishes is making the leadership unconscious and the failure inevitable.

"If I were to take a leadership role and fail, if I were to make myself responsible for everything that would happen — and as a result, I don't step into that role — then I actually already am making myself responsible for everything that's happening. So either way, I'm in the leadership authority position, but I'm just frozen in it rather than fluidly moving into it and owning it." -- Brett Kistler synthesizing Joe's teaching, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The reasoning runs like this: the person was already in the position. The position carries the responsibility regardless of whether they step into it or not. By refusing to step into it, they trade the active form of leadership (where mistakes are made but movement is possible) for the frozen form (where no mistakes are made because nothing is attempted, but the consequences accrue anyway). The freeze does not remove the leadership. It just incapacitates it.

## The Cost Pattern

The frozen-leader pattern produces a predictable cost pattern:

1. **The same outcomes the person feared.** The team underperforms. The relationship deteriorates. The project drifts. The exact failures the freeze was meant to avoid happen anyway — because they were going to happen unless someone provided leadership, and the person who refused was the someone.

2. **The added cost of having frozen.** On top of the failure, the person now has the residue of having watched the failure happen while doing nothing. The shame of inaction compounds the shame of the outcome.

3. **The narrative that "this proves I was right to refuse."** The person watches the failure and concludes that leadership is dangerous and they were wise to stay out of it. The diagnostic the freeze installed is exactly wrong: the failure was the cost of the freeze, not a vindication of it.

## Why The Freeze Feels Like Safety

The freeze feels protective because it removes the visible decision-point. If the leader does not step into the role, no one can blame them for what they did in it. The disowning of the role is read internally as the disowning of the consequences.

But the disowning only operates internally. Externally, the responsibility still rests on them. The team still looks to them. The position still has its leverage. The consequences still land in their lap. The internal disowning produces a gap between what they are responsible for (the same as before) and what they feel responsible for (less than before, until the consequences arrive). The gap is what makes the frozen leader's experience so disorienting.

See [[fear-triangle]] for the broader pattern. The frozen leader is often in the victim role of the triangle, experiencing themselves as done-to by circumstances they are in fact shaping.

## The Move Out Of The Freeze

The exit from the frozen position is not a leap into confident leadership. It is the recognition that the freeze is not actually an out. Once that recognition lands, the question shifts from "should I step into leadership or not" (false binary — they are already in it) to "what kind of leadership do I want to bring to the position I am already in." The latter question is workable. The former is not.

The recognition does not happen by argument. It happens by surfacing the gap between the felt sense ("I am not in it") and the structural reality ("I am in it"). The coaching move is to name the gap and let the person feel it. Where in your life are you frozen in a leadership position you are pretending you are not in?

This connects to [[leadership-is-for-everyone]]: the recognition that you are already leading is the prerequisite for choosing how to lead. The frozen leader is the person who has not made that recognition consciously yet.

## The Related Failure: The Blame Engine

A specific symptom of the frozen leadership pattern: the frozen person typically blames others. They are blaming the team for not stepping up, blaming the manager for not giving them authority, blaming the partner for not making the relationship work, blaming circumstances for not handing them a clearer mandate. The blame is the engine of the freeze. As long as the blame holds, the leadership can stay refused.

See [[blame-is-the-abandonment-of-leadership]] for the full mechanism. The short version: blame is acknowledging the leadership of somebody else and giving yours away.

"In a marriage. The person who's blaming is really saying, you take leadership, you are the leader, you're taking leadership. Blame is acknowledging the leadership of somebody else and giving yours away." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The frozen leader who stops blaming usually finds the leadership move become available. The blame and the freeze are the same posture from two angles.

## The Three Forms Of Avoiding Leadership Without Exiting It

The same principle expresses through three common forms:

1. **The not-stepping-in form.** The classic case. The person who refuses the role and discovers they were in it anyway.
2. **The over-responsibility form.** The person who takes responsibility FOR the people they are leading instead of leading them — feeling guilty for everyone's experience, trying to manage everyone's emotions — which disempowers the people being led and prevents the actual leadership. See [[responsibility-stops-love]] for the love-side cost, and the cleaner formulation: feeling responsible for others is itself a way of disowning the leadership move, because it inflates your role beyond what is actually yours and prevents the other person from leading themselves.
3. **The all-or-nothing form.** The person who refuses leadership in some domains while claiming it in others, treating the refused domains as somehow separate from their leadership. The "but I am not the leader of THAT" exemption keeps the freeze in place while looking like clean delineation.

All three are versions of the same move: trying to exit a position the person is in. None of them work.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the inaction is itself an action
- The disorientation of seeing that the consequences of the failure have been theirs the whole time
- The relief of dropping the pretense of being uninvolved
- The fear of what the active form of leadership will require
- The grief about the time spent frozen

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person describes a situation where they have been waiting for the right moment to step into leadership
- A person complains about a team or relationship that is failing and locates the cause externally
- A person says "it's not my place" about something they have significant influence over
- A leader in a formal role who is "not getting involved" in a dysfunction they are presiding over
- A partner who is "not getting involved" in a relationship pattern they are participating in
- The frozen-by-the-stakes person who has been deciding-by-not-deciding

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a quiet inescapability. The teaching is not delivered as accusation. It is delivered as observation — the structure was always there, the recognition makes it visible. The texture is not "you should be doing more." It is "you are doing this, look."
