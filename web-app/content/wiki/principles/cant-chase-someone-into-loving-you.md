# You Can't Chase Somebody Into Loving You

## The Principle

If the other person has decided not to work on the relationship, no amount of effort on your part will change that. You cannot chase them into wanting to be in it with you. The mechanism that produces love is not a mechanism that responds to pressure. Pressure produces the opposite of what it reaches for.

"You can't chase somebody into loving you. It doesn't work that way." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The principle applies most cleanly in the situation where one partner has signaled they are done and the other is still trying. The trying becomes a chase. The chase activates the partner's avoidance further. The space the chasing partner needs to give for the other to even potentially return is the space the chasing prevents.

## Why It Doesn't Work

The structural reason: love is a state that arises in availability and presence, not in pressure and demand. The person being chased is being given a message that registers, at the body level, as "you owe me something." Love does not arise in response to debt. It arises in response to safety, freedom, and the absence of strategic intent.

The chasing partner usually believes they are doing the loving thing. From their inside, they are demonstrating care, persistence, willingness, devotion. From the chased partner's inside, the same actions register as accumulating pressure that confirms the decision to leave. The chasing produces the leaving.

See [[searching-for-love-cuts-off-love]] for the broader frame: any strategy to get love presupposes lack of love and produces more of the lack.

## The Specific Application: When the Partner Has Said They Are Done

The principle applies most sharply when the partner has explicitly said they do not want to work on the relationship. The work for the chasing partner is to recognize: this is their business now. The doing-the-work response that would be functional in a mutual context becomes an intrusion when one side has withdrawn consent.

"You can respect them enough to just let them have that moment." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The respect is not a strategy. It is not "if I give them space they will come back." It is a clean recognition that the other person's withdrawal is theirs, that you cannot will them out of it, and that you can only do the work on yourself.

The chasing partner usually has a long list of internal arguments against this recognition: "but I love them," "but we have history," "but they don't mean it," "but if I just," "but they always come back." Each of these is the chasing logic looking for a foothold. The principle does not engage with the logic. It names the structural fact: chasing does not work, regardless of how convincing the chase feels.

## Who Asks the Question

Joe names the audience for this principle precisely. The person asking how to bring a relationship back from disdain or apathy is usually the chaser in the dynamic. That is part of why they are asking.

"Usually the person who would ask that question, the reason I say it, is usually the person who would ask that question is the person who chases the love." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The recognition matters because the chaser often does not see themselves as the chaser. They see themselves as the committed one, the trying one, the one who cares. Being named as the chaser is sometimes the first time the dynamic gets surfaced for them.

This is not pejorative. The chaser is usually doing what they learned love looks like in childhood -- often where love was scarce and required pursuing. The pattern is competent in context; it is just not effective for producing love in the present situation.

## The Joe Example: The Co-Founder Conversation

Joe describes a recent phone call with a person running a business with two co-founders. She was describing ways she could fight, ways she could leave, all the moves she could make. Joe asked one question that bypassed all of it.

"I just said to her, 'Do your partners want to work on it?' And she was, 'Nope.' I'm like, 'Okay, so what does that tell you?' And she was like, 'Yeah, it means that we have to not work together.' Like just that quickly." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The question collapses the entire planning apparatus. If the other party does not want to work on it, the elaborate strategic options are not strategies; they are forms of chasing. The answer becomes structurally clear.

The example is from a business partnership, not a romantic one. The principle applies identically. Any relationship -- romantic, business, family, friendship -- where one party has decided to disengage cannot be saved by the other party's effort.

## The Grief That Follows

The functional response to the recognition is grief. The relationship that you were trying to save is, in fact, ending or has ended. Grieving it is the work that remains.

"If the person you're with doesn't want to work on the relationship, then grieve. Then you're not -- nothing's going to happen." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The grief is not optional. The grief is what allows the chasing energy to convert into something that can produce a future relationship that works. Without the grief, the chasing energy stays mobilized, looking for a new target, and the next relationship is likely to recreate the same chase. See [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]] for the longer-term mechanism.

The grief includes mourning the version of the relationship that could have been, the time invested, the future you were planning, and the part of you that was sure this would work. The completeness of the grief is proportional to the cleanness of what comes next.

See [[mourn-the-breakup-fully]] for the practice.

## What This Is Not

This is not a claim that you should not try to repair a relationship. The principle applies after the other party has explicitly signaled they are done. Before that, the work of repair is exactly the work. See [[save-your-relationship-three-moves]] for what to do when there is still mutual willingness.

This is not a claim that no relationship ever recovers from one party deciding it is over. People sometimes return. The principle is operational, not absolute. The structural fact is: you cannot make them return through chasing. If they return, it will be because something shifted on their side, not because you applied enough effort.

This is not permission to give up easily. The recognition that chasing does not work is for the situation where chasing has been the actual move, often for a long time. A person who walks away after one difficult conversation is not in this principle's territory; they are in a different territory of avoiding the work.

## The Anxious-Avoidant Application

The chasing dynamic is the romantic version of the [[anxious-avoidant-dynamic]]. The chaser is the anxious one; the chased is the avoidant one. The principle holds across both forms: chasing intensifies the avoidance. The work for the anxious partner is to stop chasing, which is also the work that produces a more secure attachment.

The application to the long marriage that has reached the brink is the same dynamic in long-form. The chase has often been running for years. The recognition that the chase itself has been part of what produced the partner's withdrawal is sometimes the most painful piece of the recognition.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A long pattern of trying harder when the partner pulls away
- The conviction that "if I just love them enough" will produce a change
- A history of relationships where you ended up doing all the work
- A partner who has said clearly they do not want to engage and the felt-pull to engage them anyway
- The realization that your chasing has been producing the very distance you were trying to close

## Tone

Joe says this plainly, without softening. The flatness of "it doesn't work that way" is the medicine. The principle does not engage with the chaser's hope because engagement with the hope would feed the chase. The plainness is the kindness; it is the only way the recognition lands.

The follow-up "you can respect them enough to just let them have that moment" carries the warmth. The respect is the form of love that is still available after the chase has stopped. It is not love that will bring them back. It is love that the chaser can give themselves and the other simultaneously by stopping the move that does not work.
