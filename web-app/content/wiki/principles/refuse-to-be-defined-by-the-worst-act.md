# Refuse to Be Defined by the Worst Thing You Have Done

## The Principle

How a person defines themselves is how they will act. The definition is operational, not descriptive. A person who internalizes the identity of "murderer," "addict," "abuser," "failure," "broken" will act from that identity. The accountability for the act is not in question. The identity built from the act is what determines whether the same act becomes more likely or less likely going forward.

Refusing to be defined by the worst act is not denial of the act. It is the necessary condition for not repeating it.

"How we define ourselves is how we end up acting. If we think that we are -- that our value is that we are smart, then we will go around acting like we're smart, maybe less likely to listen to people, more likely to be arrogant. Or if we go around thinking, oh, we are, more important than other people, then that's how we're going to act. Or if we go around thinking we're less than other people, that's how we're going to act. And so to take away the self-definition of murderer, to me, is an incredibly important thing because it prevents it from happening again." -- Joe Hudson, Life After Murder

## Accountability Is Separable From Identity

The most common objection: "If you refuse the identity, you are dodging accountability." The principle answers this directly. Accountability is for the act and its consequences. Identity is about who you are in the world going forward. These are different.

Emile DeWeaver, who killed a man at 18 and served 21 years in prison: "This isn't a conversation about, like, I'm not accountable for killing a man. I am accountable for killing a man. I will always be accountable for killing a man, whatever the consequences of that are... So, like, that's like, you know, you got to live with that."

And: "I don't believe in identifying a person based on the worst act they've ever committed. I did murder somebody. I am not a murderer."

The grammatical move is precise. The act is named in the past tense as a verb the person committed. The identity is refused as a noun the person is. The accountability is in the verb. The identity-as-noun is the trap.

This connects to [[fault-vs-responsibility]] -- responsibility for what happened does not require identifying yourself with what happened. The two have been conflated by most cultural and institutional pressures. Separating them is itself the work.

## The Both/And Clarification: Not Bypassing the Act, Refusing It as the Whole

A common worry about this principle: does refusing the worst-act identity slide into denial of the act itself? "Oh, that wasn't really me, that was some other dissociated thing." The principle does not endorse that move. The work is both/and, not either/or.

Brett Kistler's framing of the distinction lands cleanly:

"It's not that you're like bypassing the identity of a murderer, that's something that you've done. In some sense, teaching kids not to murder involves being, hey, look, I am somebody who has murdered. I am in that regard a murderer, and that's not the only thing I am. That doesn't entirely define me. There's still freedom to be had in who I am and how I show up in the world, regardless of what I've done in my past. It's not this 'oh, I'm not a murderer, that was just some other thing that I'm dissociated from, and don't hold me accountable for those actions.' It's just, yeah, I am all of me, I'm all of my actions, and I am everything beyond that as well." -- Brett Kistler, Life After Murder

The grammatical refinement: the act remains owned. The identity refuses the act as the totality. "I am all of my actions" -- accountability is unconditional. "And I am everything beyond that as well" -- the act is part of the person, not the whole of the person.

Émile's response confirms the distinction: "This isn't a conversation about, like, I'm not accountable for killing a man. I am accountable for killing a man. I will always be accountable for killing a man."

The work is to hold both at once. The act is yours. The act is not the totality of you. Both are true. Collapsing into either pole alone produces a failure -- the first into the worst-act identity that prevents change, the second into the bypass that prevents accountability.

## Why the Worst-Act Identity Prevents Change

The identity of "I am the kind of person who does this" is a recipe for continuing to do this. Behavior follows identity. A person who has internalized "I am a murderer" carries a worldview that makes the next violent moment more available, not less. A person who has internalized "I am broken" carries a worldview that confirms the brokenness in every interaction.

This is the operational version of [[shame-drives-behavior]]. The worst-act identity is a stable shame structure. The shame produces the conditions for the behavior to repeat. The person who hates themselves for the murder is more likely to do the next violent thing, not less. The hating is the engine.

The path out is the same as the path out of shame: love, accountability without identity-collapse, and the construction of a different identity strong enough to provide motivation that overrides the patterns of the old one.

## The Vision That Replaces the Identity

The replacement identity has to be operationally useful. Not a denial of the past act. A direction for the going-forward life.

Emile, on his replacement identities: "I became a writer and I became a father. And those 2 things became my identity to the point where, like, I would literally have conversations with myself and others, especially when it came to prison rules and prison politics. Like, you know, you got to do this thing, 'cause those are the rules of prison. And that identity as a father and that identity as a writer gave me the actual strength to say, like, nah, fuck that shit. No. I know those are prison rules, and I'm not gonna live like I'm in prison. I refuse to live like I'm in prison."

The new identities had operational force. They made certain actions impossible and others necessary. They produced refusal of the local rules (prison politics) because the local rules were inconsistent with who he had decided to be.

Joe's reflection: "If you want to be the person who helps little kids not kill other little kids, defining yourself as a murderer makes that a lot less likely than if you have actually learned to love yourself, learned to forgive yourself, and overcome that limiting identity."

The aspirational identity is not aspirational in the soft sense. It is the operating system that makes the aspirational action possible.

## The Time Required

The principle is not "decide tomorrow that you are not the worst-act version of yourself." The shift takes years. Premature self-forgiveness skips the accountability and produces the spiritual bypass that justifies the next harmful act.

Emile, on the timeline: "It took me a long time to come to a place where I could, one, forgive myself, and two, recognize that hating myself was not the answer to doing a heinous act... I'm 42, 43 years old. And it took me a long time."

Twenty-one years in prison preceded the working version of this. The shift came after sufficient accountability had been lived through. The forgiveness was not a slogan; it was the conclusion of a long process.

## Why the Standard Pressure Goes the Other Way

Most cultural and institutional pressure works in the opposite direction. The criminal justice system asks people to identify themselves by their worst act. Recovery programs sometimes require it as the opening of every meeting. Therapeutic frames sometimes insist on it as proof of remorse. The pressure is structural and well-intended.

Emile, on the prison practice: "There's this practice in prison, in the name of accountability, of like always leading with like, this is the crime I committed and I'm sorry. Right? And I can see why in a world where people are struggling to even come to terms with, like, 'Hey, I did a horrible thing,' I can see why someone could be like, we need this extreme practice in order to assure ourselves that this person is actually feeling remorseful for this thing they did."

The acknowledgment of why the practice exists matters. The practice was built to prevent the easier failure: people who never accept what they did. But the practice's mechanism (worst-act-as-introduction) produces the harder failure: people who can never become more than the worst act. Both failures need to be solved. The first requires acknowledgment of the act. The second requires refusal of the act as identity.

Emile: "Who in this world do you know who introduces themselves with the worst thing they've ever done? You don't. There's only a certain, I'll say, class of people that we expect to do that."

## The Boardroom and Office Parallel

The same principle scales down to lower-stakes versions in ordinary professional and personal life. The mistake at the previous company. The relationship that ended badly. The thing you said that you regret. The pattern you used to be in.

If you carry these as identities, you act from them. The mistake at the previous company becomes the script for the current job: you are the person who makes this kind of mistake, so you avoid the situations where it could come up, and avoid the growth those situations would have offered. The bad relationship becomes the lens for the next relationship: you are the person who does that, so you cannot fully show up, so you reproduce some version of the dynamic.

The work is the same as Emile's at much higher resolution: hold accountability for what happened, refuse identity-formation around it, build operating identity from the direction you are going.

## When the Pattern Has Become the Diagnosis

The principle also applies to the way mental-health and personal-growth language can be wielded. "I am an anxious person." "I am codependent." "I am a trauma survivor." "I am an addict." These can be useful as descriptions of patterns to be worked with. They become the trap when they harden into identity. The identity makes the patterns part of the person rather than part of the person's history.

The diagnostic question: does the label allow the person to act differently, or does it predict the person will continue acting the same way? If the label is operational for change, keep it. If it has become the explanation for staying the same, refuse it.

See [[dont-make-family-dynamics-the-story]] for the related principle in the family-of-origin domain. The dynamic "I always date my father" can be the start of useful work or the cement that makes the pattern permanent. The difference is whether the description is being held lightly as data or held tightly as identity.

## The Connection to Limiting Beliefs

This principle is one of the largest cases of the [[limiting-belief]] structure. The belief "I am [worst act / pattern / diagnosis]" is a limiting belief at the identity level. Working through it is the same arc as working through any other limiting belief: see through the belief, feel what is underneath, find that the operational self is much larger than the belief allowed.

The difference is the moral weight. Other limiting beliefs ("I am not smart enough," "I am not lovable") tend to be challenged by the person and the people around them. The worst-act identity is reinforced by the surrounding culture. The release requires not just internal work but a stance against the external pressure.

## Tone

Joe holds this principle with both firmness and warmth. The firmness is about the principle: the identity must be refused for change to be possible. The warmth is about the difficulty: the refusal is hard, takes time, requires support, and exists in a culture that mostly pushes the other way.

Emile holds it with the specific firmness of someone who has done the time: "I feel really solid about my own integrity." The position is not defensive. It has been earned.

## When Someone Experiences This

- "I am a [terrible label they apply to themselves]."
- Hearing yourself open every difficult conversation with the worst thing about you
- A pattern from the past that has become the explanation for the present
- A diagnosis that started as a description and became the ceiling
- A relationship failure or business failure that has become the reason you cannot try the next thing
- The felt sense that the identity you carry is preventing you from acting in the way you want to act
