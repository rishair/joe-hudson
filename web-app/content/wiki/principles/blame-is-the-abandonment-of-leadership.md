# Blame Is the Abandonment of Leadership

## The Principle

When you blame, you are not just complaining. You are handing your leadership to the person you are blaming. The blame frame is the explicit transfer: I am acknowledging that you are the one who has the agency in this situation, that you are the one running it, that you are the one whose decisions matter — and I am positioning myself as the one being acted upon. The blame is the ceremony of giving your leadership away.

"The person who's blaming is really saying, you take leadership, you are the leader, you're taking leadership. Blame is acknowledging the leadership of somebody else and giving yours away." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The implication runs both ways. The blamer loses the leadership they had — the situation becomes "what they did to me" rather than "what I am shaping." The blamed person receives the leadership the blamer disowned — they are now positioned as the actor in the situation, the one whose moves the blamer is reacting to. The transfer happens regardless of whether the blamed person wants the transfer.

## Why This Matters Operationally

The principle clarifies why blame does not produce the change the blamer wants. The blamer wants the other person to change. But the act of blaming has handed the other person the leadership of the situation — and a leader does what they want, not what they are told. The blamer has just installed the structural condition that prevents the change they are demanding.

If you want the other person to change, the move is to take your leadership back. Take responsibility for what you are doing in the situation, name what you want, take action that flows from your conviction. The other person will respond to leadership. They will not respond to blame.

This is also why the organizational principle Joe describes works: in his company, blame is structurally not rewarded. Leadership is followed; blame is not.

"In our organization, that generally just does not get rewarded at all. Because we follow leadership. We do not follow blame. So if that person was like, that's wrong and I'm going to go fix it, fantastic. Go, go fix. We'll all follow you." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The framing converts every blame moment into a leadership opportunity. If the thing the person is blaming is real, they have just identified something worth leading on. The next move is to step in. If they cannot or will not step in, the blame is exposed as not really about fixing the problem — it is about the avoidance.

## The Common Workplace Form

A specific recurring pattern Joe identifies:

"Somebody will say, there's something wrong here, nobody's listening to me, why isn't everybody listening? And now you're upset at me for saying that there's something wrong here. One thing is they're not taking the leadership to actually go fix the problem." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The pattern has a recursive quality: the person blames others for not listening, then blames the leader for being upset about being blamed. Every move stays in the blame frame because the leadership move (going and fixing the problem) is the move the person is avoiding. The blame produces a self-perpetuating dynamic where each new piece of blame is offered as evidence that the original blame was justified.

The exit is the same as anywhere: stop blaming, identify what you want, take the leadership move that gets you there. The structural condition for the exit is recognizing that the blame is the avoidance, not the diagnosis.

## The Connection To The Three Stages Of Blame

The principle adds a layer to [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]]. The three-stages teaching identifies that the path of blame moves from blaming-others to blaming-self to no-blame. The leadership lens reveals what each stage costs:

- **Stage one (blame others):** leadership is handed to the other person
- **Stage two (blame self):** leadership is handed to a punitive internal authority (see [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]] for the related dynamic)
- **Stage three (no blame):** leadership is reclaimed and the situation becomes workable

The three-stages path can also be read as the developmental arc of recovering the leadership that blame keeps externalizing. At stage three, no blame means no transfer — the person stays in their own leadership throughout the situation.

## The Marriage Variant

The most common everyday domain for this dynamic is the marriage or long-term partnership. The blamer-partner is constantly assigning the leadership of the relationship to the blamed-partner. The blamed-partner then experiences themselves as the one running the relationship — which they did not consent to, and which produces resentment, withdrawal, or counter-blame.

The dynamic is structurally identical to the workplace version. The blame frame transfers the leadership. The receiving partner ends up holding the leadership they did not ask for. The blaming partner ends up frustrated that the receiving partner is not leading in the direction they want — the same trap as the workplace blamer who wants the leadership-recipient to do what the blame says.

The exit, in marriages as in workplaces, is the reclaim. The blaming partner takes the leadership back: what do I want here, what am I doing, what am I going to do about it. The relationship reorganizes around two people leading their own halves rather than one person blaming the other for both halves.

## The Subtle Self-Blame Variant

Self-blame transfers leadership too — to the internal critical voice rather than to an external person. The self-blamer is handing their leadership to the punishing voice in the head, which now gets to operate as the authority that the self-blamer keeps trying to satisfy.

This is the bridge to [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]]. The voice does not actually own the leadership it has been handed — it does the blame thing, not the leadership thing. So the self-blamer ends up serving an authority that is not actually leading anywhere. The transfer was made and the recipient is incapable of executing on the role.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the chronic complaint to their partner is a daily ceremony of leadership transfer
- The discomfort of seeing that the blamed person has been "in charge" of their experience for years
- The surprise that the way out is not making the other person fix the thing but reclaiming the leadership
- The relief of catching a blame impulse and converting it to a leadership move
- The grief about all the leadership that was given away

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person presents a complaint about a partner, boss, team, or institution that they have been repeating for some time
- A person is in stage one of [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]] and the leadership angle has not been surfaced
- A person who is angry that "nobody listens" without taking the move to fix the thing themselves
- A person describing a marital pattern of one partner blaming and the other withdrawing
- A leader noticing that team members are blaming peers instead of taking the leadership move to resolve the issue
- A frozen leader (see [[avoiding-leadership-still-leaves-you-as-the-leader]]) whose blame is the maintenance mechanism for the freeze

## Tone

Joe delivers this with a clarifying directness. The framing is not moralizing about blame — it is just identifying what blame is structurally doing. The texture is matter-of-fact: this is the mechanism, this is what it costs, this is the exit. The teaching tends to land hard for people who have not seen blame this way before — because the recognition reframes a lot of their daily life in one move.
