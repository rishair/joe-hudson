# The Leader's Job Is Not to Take Care of Everybody

## The Principle

The role structure inside a team has three layers, and confusing them is one of the most common ways leaders sabotage their own organizations.

- **The team member's job is to take care of the team.**
- **The leader's job is to help the team members take care of the team.**
- **The leader's job is not to take care of everybody.**

The leader can be supportive. The leader can be a coach. The leader can root for the team. The leader can have someone's back. But the leader does not save people, does not make excuses for them, and does not absorb their work into the leader's own responsibility.

"The job of a team member is to take care of the team. The job of the leader is to help the team members take care of the team. The job of the leader is not to take care of everybody. You can be helpful, but it is not to take care of them. You can be supportive, you can be a coach, best you root for them. You're rooting for them, like, oh, I know that person's got my back. That's fantastic. But they're not saving me. They're not making excuses for me." -- Joe Hudson

The principle gives the leader a precise role boundary. The role includes a lot: rooting, supporting, coaching, having someone's back. It excludes one specific thing: saving them or making their excuses.

## Why This Distinction Matters

The leader who slips into taking-care-of-everybody mode produces several specific dysfunctions:

**Uneven care.** A leader cannot take care of everybody perfectly or evenly. If the leader's role is to take care of the team, the leader will inevitably take care of some people at the expense of others. The team experiences this as favoritism. The team members who got dropped feel abandoned.

"There's also a really funny thing there where if you are, if you think it's your responsibility to take care of the team, you're never gonna do that perfectly or evenly. So there's always gonna be somebody who you're taking care of at the expense of someone else who's completely getting dropped." -- the dialogue

The distortion is structural. The role itself is the problem. No amount of skill can make the leader-takes-care-of-everybody role work cleanly, because the role does not scale.

**Disempowerment of the team.** When the leader takes responsibility for the team's emotional state, success, and well-being, the team learns they are not the source of those things. They become recipients of the leader's caretaking rather than agents of their own work. The team becomes dependent on the leader's care, which the leader then has to keep providing. The dependency is structural, not personal.

See [[feeling-responsible-for-them-disempowers-them]] for the deeper version of this dynamic.

**The leader becomes the bottleneck on team emotion.** A team where the leader is in charge of everyone's well-being is a team where every emotional issue has to route through the leader. The leader becomes the single point of failure for the team's relational health. When the leader is unavailable, busy, or struggling themselves, the entire team's emotional life stops.

**The team stops taking care of the team.** Most critically: when the leader takes care of everybody, the team members stop taking care of each other. The role of caring-for-the-team has been usurped by the leader. The team becomes a collection of individuals each in a one-to-one relationship with the leader, rather than a team that holds itself.

## What the Leader Actually Does

The leader's role is not absent of care. It is structured care:

- **Set the vision and the goals.** What is the team trying to do? What are the criteria for success? The leader holds this clarity.
- **Help each team member take care of the team.** The leader's individual conversations with team members are oriented around how that person can contribute to the team, not around how the leader can take care of that person.
- **Root for them.** The leader genuinely wants each team member to succeed and communicates this.
- **Be supportive.** The leader removes obstacles, provides resources, makes hard conversations possible.
- **Have their back.** The team member can count on the leader to advocate for them in situations they cannot advocate for themselves.

What the leader does not do:

- Save them from consequences they earned
- Make their excuses for them
- Take responsibility for their happiness
- Absorb their failures into the leader's own performance
- Carry the relational work of the team alone

## The Team-Wins Frame

The principle pairs with a specific team frame. The team wins together; the team loses together. Individual success is in service of the team's success, not the other way around.

"If you were going to bet on 3 teams, one of the teams was everybody's in it for themselves, the other team is that everybody's in it to help the other people, and the other one is everybody's is in it for the team to win? Like, which one are you betting on? It's pretty obvious. You're gonna bet on the one where everybody's focused on the team, helping the team, making the team win." -- Joe Hudson

The three possible orientations:
1. Everyone in it for themselves
2. Everyone in it to help other individuals
3. Everyone in it for the team to win

The third produces the strongest team. The leader's job is to point at the team's win as the organizing principle, not at individual care as the priority. Individual care is included in the team's win, but it is not the organizing principle.

"Yeah, but it's the priority is important. Like, oh, am I doing this to help the team win or am I doing this so that I personally can get ahead?" -- Joe Hudson

The leader who slips into individual-care mode has reordered the priorities. The team's win has become secondary to the individual's care. The leader's care now distorts the team's purpose.

## Why "It's Not Personal, It's Just Business" Has the Truth Backwards

The principle illuminates the common phrase "it's not personal, it's just business." The phrase usually means: don't take this personally because it's just business. The principle inverts this. If you are tolerating something that's not working in the business — paying someone $200k who isn't delivering, keeping a relationship that's broken, allowing a pattern that's hurting the team — the only reason you would do that is something personal.

"There's that saying, it's not business, it's, it's not personal, it's just business. ... Which is pretty hilarious because the only thing that could make it that you would put up with something that is not, uh, not working in the organization. The only reason you would allow, you know, paying somebody $200K who's not holding up their role is because something personal is going on." -- Joe Hudson

The personal thing might be the leader's caretaking pattern. It might be the leader's belief that they don't deserve an A-player. It might be the leader feeling responsible for the person's life. Whatever it is, it is the personal dynamic that is producing the unproductive business decision. The phrase "it's just business" usually covers the personal pattern that is doing the actual work.

## What the Principle Frees the Leader To Do

When the leader releases the take-care-of-everybody role, several specific freedoms become available.

**They can have hard conversations cleanly.** The leader who is not responsible for the other person's well-being can deliver hard feedback as information rather than as an emotional event. See [[block-calendar-time-for-hard-conversations]].

**They can let people fail.** The leader who is not absorbing the team's failures can let team members experience their own failures, which is what teaches them. See [[allow-mistakes-as-the-cost-of-real-delegation]].

**They can let people leave.** The leader who is not responsible for everyone's happiness can let people who aren't a match move on, without taking the departure as a personal failure of care. See [[it-just-isnt-a-match]].

**They can be present without absorbing.** The leader can be in the room with someone's pain or struggle without taking it on. Care, not caretaking. See [[caring-vs-caretaking]].

## The Connection to Caring vs. Caretaking

This principle is the team-scale version of [[caring-vs-caretaking]]. Caring is being present, attentive, supportive. Caretaking is taking on the other person's responsibility, managing their emotional state, making their decisions for them. The leader-doesn't-take-care-of-everybody principle is the operational expression of this distinction at the team level.

The leader who is in care mode supports the team while leaving the team's work to the team. The leader who is in caretaking mode absorbs the team's work into their own and disempowers everyone. The shift between the two is the same shift in any other context — but at the team level it has organizational consequences, not just relational ones.

## What This Is Not

**Not coldness.** The leader who has internalized this principle is still warm, attentive, and supportive. The principle is not "be distant." It is "be in your actual role, which is large but specific."

**Not a license for callousness.** The leader who uses the principle to justify ignoring real distress or refusing reasonable support has misread the principle. The role includes being supportive, rooting for them, having their back. The role excludes saving them and making their excuses.

**Not a rejection of personal relationships.** The leader can have warm personal relationships with team members. The principle is about the role-based responsibilities, not about the texture of the relationships. The two layers can coexist.

**Not abandonment of the team.** The leader is not pulling away. The leader is being in the role that the team actually needs them to be in. The team is not abandoned by the leader being in their actual role; they are served by it.

## When the Principle Fires

- A leader is exhausted from absorbing the team's emotional life
- A leader is tolerating an underperforming team member and the diagnostic surfaces that something personal is driving the tolerance
- A leader is favoring one team member at the expense of others and the work is to surface the role confusion
- A leader is being asked to be more available emotionally and the question is what kind of availability is actually their job
- A leader is in [[i-want-to-delegate-better]] work and the caretaking pattern surfaces as the underlying block

## Tone

Direct and clarifying. Joe delivers this as a role description, not as a moral imperative. The leader who absorbs the principle is not being told to be a different kind of person; they are being told what their actual role is. The clarification is the gift.
