# How You Approach the Work Matters as Much as How Much Work You Do

## The Principle

Mastery is not produced by hours alone. The approach to the hours matters as much as the count of them. Two people can put in the same hours and produce wildly different results because one is relating to the practice as a thing they love and the other as a thing they have to push through.

The mechanism is observable: a person who beats themselves up for not practicing finds practicing harder and harder to do. A person who has found a way to enjoy the practice finds it easier and easier to do. Same activity, opposite trajectories. The relationship to the activity is the variable.

"It's not just how many hours you put in, it's how you approach it that makes you successful." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

## The Jazz Musician Example

The clearest case Joe uses: a top jazz player who became one of the best in the world. The story is not that he put in more hours than everyone else. The story is that he changed his relationship to practice.

"There's this great musician who talks about how he became one of the world's best jazz players. And it wasn't just how many hours he put in, though he did put in hours, but what he did was that he learned how to fall in love with practice. And so his practice wasn't about getting better. His practice was like brushing teeth. It was something that was supposed to feel good both in the moment and that would make you feel better in the long term." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

Two operational features in the framing:

- **"Like brushing teeth."** Not optional but not effortful. A daily activity that does not require willpower to do, because there is no internal resistance to it. The activity is woven into the rhythm of the day in a way that bypasses the decision to do it.
- **"Supposed to feel good both in the moment and over time."** The practice is not endured for some future payoff. It is enjoyable in the moment AND it produces long-term benefit. The two timeframes are not in tension. The in-the-moment enjoyment is what makes the long-term benefit possible, because the in-the-moment enjoyment is what makes the practice sustainable.

The implicit comparison: a person who practices the same instrument while hating it produces a different trajectory. They put in fewer hours because each hour is paid for in willpower. They lose the willpower fight eventually. The hours stop accumulating. The mastery does not happen.

## The Self-Flagellation Tax

The mechanism that makes the un-loved practice unsustainable:

"Beating yourself up for not practicing and it's harder and harder to practice. It doesn't work as compared to somebody who is not beating themselves up." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

Each beating-up makes the next session harder. The person sits down to practice and the body already remembers being yelled at for the last session. The body's anticipation of the inner critic during practice becomes part of the practice itself. The practice is now an activity-plus-self-attack. The compound activity is much harder to sustain than the simple activity.

The person who is not beating themselves up gets only the activity. The activity is the same difficulty for both people. The compound activity is much harder than the simple activity. Over months and years, the difference is the difference between a person who practices daily and a person who practices in fits and starts.

See [[punishing-the-remembering]] for the closely related principle: beating yourself up for forgetting to practice punishes the moment of remembering, which is itself the practice. The same mechanism.

## How This Applies Beyond Music

The jazz musician is the illustrative case. The principle generalizes to any sustained activity where mastery accumulates over time:

- **Writing.** The writer who has found a way to enjoy the daily writing produces more writing than the writer who white-knuckles it. The white-knuckler gives up before the body of work accumulates.
- **Exercise.** The person who has found a form of movement they enjoy continues doing it for decades. The person who hates their workout abandons it within months.
- **A business or company.** The founder who enjoys the work outlasts the founder who is grinding through it, by a wide margin, even if the grinder is technically working more hours.
- **A relationship.** The partner who enjoys their partner produces a different relationship than the partner who is performing relationship obligations.
- **A meditation practice, language learning, any skill acquisition.** Same shape.

The unifying claim: the variable that scales mastery is not hours; it is sustainable hours. Sustainability is produced by the relationship to the activity, not by the activity itself.

## Why This Is Different From "Just Enjoy It"

The principle is not "you should enjoy your practice" delivered as another should. That instruction is itself the kind of thing the jazz musician transcended. He did not enjoy his practice because someone told him to. He found a way to relate to his practice that made it enjoyable.

The work is to find the relationship, not to manufacture the feeling. See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] for the operational move: ask "how do I enjoy this?" instead of "how do I do this right?" The question opens a different search. The answer to that search is what makes the practice loveable.

See also [[lack-of-enjoyment-as-diagnostic]] — when the practice cannot be enjoyed, that is information about how the practice has been structured. The lack-of-enjoyment is the diagnostic; the structural fix is what makes the enjoyment available. The enjoyment is not separately willed.

## The Inverse: The Same Hours With Self-Attack Produce Less

Joe's full claim contains both halves:

"All that hard work that they put in day after day hasn't paid off in the same way." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

The "all that hard work" person and the "two or three good decisions in four weeks" billionaire are not on different IQ tiers. They are on different relationships to their work. One is doing the same activity through a fog of self-criticism, urgency, and obligation. The other is doing the activity from a relationship that allows clarity and freshness.

This is the same observation [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] makes from the productivity side. The human system runs efficiently when it is enjoying what it is doing. It runs inefficiently when it is fighting itself. The hours are the same. The output per hour is not.

## When Someone Experiences This

- When they have been practicing something for a long time without reaching the mastery they expected.
- When they observe someone else doing the same activity with much better results and assume the difference must be talent.
- When they sit down to practice and have to negotiate with themselves to get started.
- When the practice has come to feel like work in the bad sense — required, dreaded, paid for in willpower.
- When they have been told "you just have to put in the hours" and the hours are not producing the result.

## The Coaching Move

The diagnostic question: "How do you relate to your practice?" Not "how much do you practice." The relationship is what scales the practice.

If the relationship is hostile (self-criticism for missing sessions, dread before sitting down, relief when finished), the work is not to add more hours. The work is to address the relationship. Once the relationship softens, the hours will increase naturally because there is no longer an internal cost to each hour. See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] for the practical entry point.

If the relationship is already healthy and the person still wants more, then the hours-question becomes useful. But the hours-question is downstream of the relationship-question, not upstream of it.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. The jazz musician is named as a known fact: he became one of the best, and this is how. The principle is not delivered as inspirational; it is delivered as mechanical. The hours-equation people have been running is wrong. Here is the better equation. The better equation is also easier to live by. The reframe is good news.
