# Plan How, Not Just What

## The Principle

A to-do list specifies what is going to be done. It almost never specifies how. The missing how is most of the reason the list does not work. The how determines whether the task actually gets done, whether the person enjoys doing it, and whether the doing produces energy or drains it. Planning what to do without planning how to do it is planning half the work and leaving the rest to chance.

"Doesn't make any sense at all to think about what you're gonna do without how you're gonna do it. And yet our to-do lists never really talk about how we're gonna do it." -- Joe Hudson, Your To-Do List is Just Your Inner Critic on Paper

The standard list looks like this:
- Respond to client emails
- Write the proposal
- Schedule the meeting with the team

The how-augmented version looks like this:
- Respond to client emails while sitting outside in the sun
- Write the proposal as a conversation with the client, in plain language
- Schedule the team meeting as a walk together, not as a Zoom

Same tasks. Different how. The second list will produce different behavior, different energy, and different output than the first. The same person is more likely to do the second list, more likely to enjoy doing it, and more likely to do it well.

## The Toothbrushing Example

The principle is easiest to see with a 3-year-old. The what is "brush your teeth." The standard execution is to issue the what as an instruction: "Go brush your teeth." The 3-year-old is not going to do it.

The how-shifted execution is to add a how to the what: "How about we brush our teeth together?" The 3-year-old is now much more likely to brush their teeth.

"If you say to a 3-year-old, 'Hey, how about we brush our teeth together?' They're far more likely to brush their teeth than if you just tell them to go and do it. So how you do something, how you approach it is gonna make a very big difference in whether you get the thing done." -- Joe Hudson, Your To-Do List is Just Your Inner Critic on Paper

The 3-year-old example matters because adults do not think they need the same scaffolding. They do. The adult issuing themselves a what without a how is producing the same response in the adult-self that the parent issuing a what produces in the 3-year-old: resistance, avoidance, half-compliance. The 3-year-old is honest about it. The adult covers it with guilt, procrastination, and the "I should have done that" loop.

See [[stay-close-and-insist]] for the broader parenting move the toothbrushing example is the simplest case of. The how-shift in the toothbrushing example is the same operation: adding closeness and togetherness to the task changes whether the task happens.

## Why the Missing How Is the Critic's Specialty

The how is missing from the standard list because the critic's job is not to plan how. The critic's job is to issue shoulds. The shoulds are statements of what should be done. The how is none of the critic's business -- the critic does not have to do the work; the critic just has to tell you that you have not done it yet.

This is why the to-do list-as-inner-critic-on-paper (see [[to-do-list-as-inner-critic-on-paper]]) reliably omits the how. The list was authored by the part of the system that does not do the work. When the part that does the work shows up at the list, the only information available is the what. The how has to be improvised from scratch every time. The improvisation usually defaults to whatever-is-easiest, whatever-the-body-can-tolerate, whatever-will-get-this-checked-off-fastest.

The default how is rarely enjoyable. The default how is rarely well-designed. The default how usually drains energy because it was not chosen to be the right how; it was the residue left after no how was planned.

The work of the principle is to insist that the how is part of the plan, not an afterthought. If the how is in the plan, the part of the system that does the work has the information it needs. If the how is missing, the doing-part is being asked to invent the how under pressure, which is exactly the condition that makes any invention bad.

## The How Is What Makes It Enjoyable

The principle's strongest payoff is on enjoyment. The same task can be done in a way that produces energy or a way that depletes it. The how is the variable. If the how is chosen for enjoyment, the doing is enjoyable. If the how is left to default, the doing is whatever it is -- usually neither enjoyable nor unenjoyable, just done.

Enjoyment is also the efficiency metric (see [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]]). A task done enjoyably costs less fuel than the same task done grimly. So planning the how for enjoyment is also planning the how for efficiency. The two are the same variable. Choosing the enjoyable how is also choosing the most-efficient how.

"It's really important to figure out how to make it enjoyable. And why is that the case? It's the case because if it's enjoyable, you're more likely to do it. If it's enjoyable, it's more efficient." -- Joe Hudson, Your To-Do List is Just Your Inner Critic on Paper

The three consequences of choosing the enjoyable how:
1. **You are more likely to do it.** The how that produces enjoyment is the how that the system will move toward; the how that produces dread is the how the system will avoid.
2. **The doing is more efficient.** Less fuel is consumed. Energy remains at the end of the day. The work can compound rather than depleting.
3. **The product is better.** A product made by someone who is energized and enjoying is structurally different from a product made by someone who is suffering through. See [[consciousness-is-the-product]].

## Two Test Questions for the How

When adding the how to a task, two questions surface what the right how is for you:

**1. How would I do this in a way I would enjoy?**

This is the positive form. It pulls the imagination toward configurations of the task that produce energy. The answer often surprises the person -- the task they thought they hated has an enjoyable version they had never considered. The hating was attached to the default-how, not to the task itself.

**2. What would stop me from enjoying this?**

This is the inverse form. It surfaces the specific mechanism by which the default-how has been blocking enjoyment. See [[whats-stopping-you-from-enjoying-x]] for the move. The not-enjoying is a known practice; the person can describe what they have been doing to prevent enjoyment long before they can describe how to enjoy. Naming it makes it optional.

Both questions are present-tense and operational. They convert the abstract instruction "plan the how" into the practical instruction "find a how that makes the doing of this not-miserable."

See [[enjoyment-as-path]] for the broader principle these questions operate inside. The how-of-task is one specific application of the broader teaching.

## What This Looks Like in a Worked Example

A YouTube content creator describes hating YouTube meetings. The standard list-level analysis: "I have a YouTube meeting on Tuesday" -- the what is set, the how is whatever-happens-when-it-happens. The default how (which the creator has been running for years) is to dread the meeting, walk in resistant, leave drained.

The how-augmented version starts by listing what makes the meeting unenjoyable, then asks what would make it enjoyable. The answers might be: shorter, with fewer people, more focused on the part of the work the creator finds interesting, with a clear next step at the end, with a different physical configuration (walking, not sitting), with humor included rather than excluded, with the creator's actual perspective included rather than suppressed. Each of these is a how-change. The what stays the same -- there is still a YouTube meeting on Tuesday. The how changes everything about whether the meeting happens, whether the work that follows the meeting happens, and whether the creator is depleted or energized after.

The empirical result for the specific creator in this story: the YouTube channel's numbers jumped substantially over the year following the how-shift. The shift was not about doing more meetings, working harder, or grinding through the resistance. The shift was about finding a how for YouTube work that the creator could actually enjoy. The enjoyment produced the doing. The doing produced the result. See [[joes-friend-tiago-youtube-meetings]] for the full story.

## Why People Default to What-Only Lists

The default to what-only lists is not random. There are several reasons it is the default:

**1. The critic only knows the what.** As described above, the part of the system that authors the list does not know how to do the work. It only knows what should be done. Lists authored by the critic are structurally what-only.

**2. The what is easier to write down.** "Respond to client emails" fits on one line. "Respond to client emails while sitting outside, starting with the easiest one, taking breaks every three emails to look up at the sky" takes more thought to write. The list reflects what is easy to put on the list, not what would actually work to do.

**3. The how feels frivolous.** A list with "while sitting outside" looks unserious. A list with bare tasks looks responsible. The cultural frame says serious work looks effortful and stripped-down. The frame is wrong (see [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] for the structural argument) but it is the frame most people are operating inside.

**4. The how takes upfront time.** Adding the how to each item takes a few minutes of thinking per item. The standard list-authoring move is to dump tasks as fast as possible and move on. The minutes saved up front are paid back many times over in the worse execution that follows, but the up-front cost is what most people optimize against.

None of these are good reasons. They are just the default reasons. The principle is the invitation to violate the defaults and discover that a how-augmented list is the actual productivity tool the what-only list was pretending to be.

## When to Use This Principle

- When someone presents [[i-keep-getting-stuff-done-but-nothing-changes]] and the list is a central artifact.
- When someone describes hating tasks they have to do anyway, and is reaching for discipline rather than design.
- When [[to-do-list-as-inner-critic-on-paper]] has been named and the next move is "how do I make a list that is not the critic's list?"
- When someone is procrastinating on a specific item and the procrastination is about the how, not the what.
- When someone reports that their day is exhausting even though they got through their list.
- When a parent is fighting with a kid about a task that could be reconfigured.

## Tone

Plainly practical, almost obvious-once-stated. The principle is delivered as if it should not need stating -- "doesn't make any sense at all to think about what you're gonna do without how you're gonna do it." The light surprise that nobody is already doing this is part of how it lands. The toothbrushing example is what makes the principle stick; the example is what people remember, and the example does the work of making the abstract principle concrete.
