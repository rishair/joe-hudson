# Subjugating Yourself to a Group Prevents Healing

## The Principle

When people in a group sacrifice their authenticity, their wants, or their truth for the cohesion of the group, the result is trauma, not healing. Self-sacrifice in service of the group does not produce the healing the group was supposed to deliver. It produces the same dynamics that created the wound in the first place: the suppression of self in the name of belonging.

The pattern is structurally identical at every scale: a couple where one person walks on eggshells to keep the other happy, a workplace where dissent is dangerous, a transformative community where people subjugate themselves to the group's wisdom or the leader's authority. In each case, the surface effect is cohesion. The deeper effect is the perpetuation of the dynamic that produced the original injury.

## In Joe's Words

"I see that when groups subjugate themselves for the group, there is no healing that happens. Like as people in a group subjugate their needs for the, it's like, it just creates trauma. It doesn't actually create healing." -- Joe Hudson, Group Cohesion vs. Cult Dynamics

The framing is direct. Healing requires the person to remain authentic. The group cohesion that comes from people suppressing themselves is the cohesion of trauma, not the cohesion of healing.

## The Same Pattern at Couple Scale

The principle scales down to two people. Walking on eggshells with a partner -- saying the right thing, doing what keeps the other person from getting upset, managing the other's emotional state at the cost of your own truth -- is the couple-scale version of group subjugation. It produces the same effect.

"There's a tendency for two people get into a relationship and both of them start making sacrifices to their authenticity to make sure the other person is happy or stays happy. Walking on eggshells or saying the exact right thing or doing whatever they need to do so that the other person doesn't get upset with them or angry at them or get sad or whatever it is. And I think it's the same thing that happens. And I think it's really unhealthy in a relationship. And I think it's really unhealthy in a group." -- Joe Hudson, Group Cohesion vs. Cult Dynamics

This connects to [[caring-vs-caretaking]]. The walking-on-eggshells stance is caretaking applied to a relationship. Caretaking at scale -- across a community of people all caretaking each other or all caretaking a leader -- is group subjugation.

## Why It Fails

The mechanism is the same as the mechanism that produced the original wound. Most people learned, early, that being themselves was unsafe and that suppressing themselves was the price of love. The childhood version of this is what gets re-enacted when an adult joins a group that requires the same suppression.

A group that asks for the suppression of self is offering the same deal the wound came from. The body recognizes the deal because it knows the deal. The body knows how to make the trade. The trade gets made. The familiar suffering of self-suppression is re-experienced as the price of group belonging.

This is not healing. This is the original dynamic in a new costume.

Healing requires the opposite: the person being more themselves in the presence of others, not less. The discovery that the truth of who they are is welcome, not the discovery that suppression buys belonging.

## The Pull Toward Group Subjugation

The pull is real. Brett names it: "There's like a desire within the people of the group to start to surrender to the group. Be like, please just solve my problems, meet all my needs, make my decisions, make life easy for me and heal me, change me."

The pull is structural. Groups that promise healing -- therapeutic groups, spiritual groups, transformative communities -- attract people who are tired of carrying themselves and want to be carried. The fantasy is that surrender to the group will be the medicine. The reality is that surrender to the group is the disease being recapitulated.

The pull exists because the unmet need underneath it is real. The person wants to be held. The person wants the burden of self-direction to be lifted. These are legitimate human desires. The group that promises to meet them through subjugation is offering a counterfeit.

What can actually meet the need is being held in a way that does not require self-suppression. See [[container-of-love]] for the alternative shape. The container of love makes space for more of the person, not less. The cult dynamic makes space for less.

## The Joe Disgust Response

Joe describes a strong personal aversion to this dynamic.

"There is a deep dislike for that kind of, like, dislike as in, I don't like the taste of that. Like, I just like, I don't, I don't like cooked fish. I want to eat cooked fish. I'll eat raw fish. It's like that." -- Joe Hudson, Group Cohesion vs. Cult Dynamics

The aversion is not moral judgment. It is taste, body-level recognition that the dynamic is not the thing the work is for. The work he is interested in is the opposite: people becoming more themselves, not less. People accessing their own wisdom, not subjugating to group wisdom.

This is why the cult question is sensitive for him. The dynamics he is most disgusted by are the dynamics that the work he is doing could most easily devolve into if not held carefully.

## The Design Implication

The principle has a structural consequence for how transformative work is designed. The work has to be structured so that subjugation to the group is not what is happening, because if it is, the work is producing trauma rather than healing.

The design moves include:
- The teacher pointing people back to their own wisdom rather than offering their own wisdom (see [[community-over-teacher]])
- Encouraging dissent and difference rather than uniformity
- Making it easy and natural to leave
- Avoiding hierarchy of access (no inner circles, no special status)
- Naming the dynamics openly when they start to operate (see [[keeping-cult-dynamics-conscious]])
- Building the work around individual empowerment rather than group identification

The 25 markers of high-control groups are useful in the negative form: the absence of those markers is what makes a group of transformative work different from a cult, even when the surface practices look similar.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Sacrificing what you actually think to keep the peace in a group
- Modifying your behavior to match the group's norms in ways that feel inauthentic
- The exhaustion of performing the group's expected version of yourself
- The resentment that builds when subjugation has gone on too long
- The relief of leaving a group and the confusion about why it feels good to leave something that was supposed to be healing
- The walking-on-eggshells experience in a couple or family

## Tone

Joe holds this with conviction and a small edge. The conviction comes from having watched the dynamic operate and seen the damage. The edge comes from being part of work that could most easily slide into this if not held carefully. The tone is also non-judgmental: the pattern is human, the pull is understandable, the disgust is taste rather than morality.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle is not "never adapt to a group" or "always assert yourself in collective settings." Healthy groups require some compromise, some accommodation, some willingness to hold the collective alongside the self. The line is between compromise that retains the self and subjugation that suppresses the self. The diagnostic is internal: does the participation in the group leave you more yourself or less yourself over time? The principle is about subjugation, not about cooperation.
