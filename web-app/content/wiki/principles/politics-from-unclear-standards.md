# Politics Arise From Unclear Performance Standards

## The Principle

When the standards for staying or going, succeeding or failing, are unclear in a team or organization, politics fill the gap. People will direct their anxiety somewhere; if they cannot direct it at clear performance criteria, they direct it at relationships, alliances, and impression management. The presence of political behavior in a team is a direct diagnostic that performance standards are not clear.

"If you create a team where it's not clear and cut and dry what will make them stay and what will make them leave the team, what will make them successful and what will make them fail in that team, then politics arise. So if you're sitting in a company right now and you have, and it's a political company, it means that the success is based on relationship, not on performance." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Mechanism

People need to know how to succeed. The need is not optional; it is what allows them to direct their effort. When clear standards exist, the effort goes into meeting the standards. When the standards are vague, the effort goes into reading the room, predicting what the leader will value tomorrow, and aligning with the people who seem to be ascendant.

"Politics is just misdirected anxiety." -- Brett Kistler, Building a Functional Team

Joe agrees, with a correction: "Well, perhaps correctly directed, but just the system isn't properly defined."

The anxiety is doing its job. The body is asking "how do I stay here, how do I succeed here?" In a clear system, the answer is visible. In an unclear system, the answer is "manage your relationships, position yourself, do not get on the wrong side." The behavior people call "political" is the rational response to the lack of clarity.

## The Sports Comparison

Professional sports is among the least secure professions: you can be cut at any time. Yet professional athletes often experience the kind of clarity that office workers do not. The standard is visible: perform at this level, you stay; do not, you go.

"In professional sports, there's a way that people feel safer 'cause they just know if I perform at this level, I stay, and if I don't, I go. And so the hard part is that people in organizations, they don't know often when they're gonna get fired. And if they know that, it's far more secure feeling for them, and therefore they can perform better." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The lesson: clarity, even harsh clarity, produces better functioning than warm vagueness. The team that does not know what gets them fired will perform worse than the team that does, because the anxiety has nowhere productive to land.

## The Soccer Team Thought Experiment

Joe's reductio: imagine a soccer team where you stay or go based on how much the owner's wife likes you.

"Could you imagine how effective a soccer team would be if you weren't kept because of performance on the field or because of the relation, how well you played with others, but instead it was how much the owner's wife liked you? They would not win very much." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The thought experiment makes the dysfunction obvious. The same dynamic, with different specifics, is happening in most companies. The criteria for survival are not performance and team contribution; they are some mix of relationship management, political alignment, and impression formation. The companies just produce less than they could because of it.

## The Inverse: Clear Standards Free the Anxiety

When standards are clear, anxiety directs itself at the work. The same nervous system that was scanning for political risk now scans for performance risk. The first produces drama. The second produces output.

"Their anxiety can be directed in the right direction. The direction of their attention -- it directs their attention to the thing that is actually most valuable for them to perform." -- Joe Hudson and Brett Kistler, Building a Functional Team

This is not the elimination of anxiety. Friction is required for life; see [[friction-is-required-for-life]]. The principle is that anxiety, like any current, will flow somewhere. The structure determines where.

## What This Looks Like as Diagnostic

If a leader walks into their company and sees:

- Alliances forming around individuals rather than around the work
- Decisions being made for relational reasons rather than substantive ones
- Substantial energy going into managing how things appear to leadership
- Gossip about who is in and who is out
- People positioning themselves rather than producing

The diagnosis is not "we have toxic people." The diagnosis is "our performance standards are unclear, and people's anxiety is going where it can." Fixing the toxic people while leaving the standards unclear produces a new set of toxic people. Fixing the standards usually resolves what looked like personality problems.

This is the parallel diagnostic to [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]]. There, politics arises from individual wants that have not been named. Here, politics arises from organizational standards that have not been made clear. Both are forms of necessary information being denied a legitimate channel and routing through illegitimate ones.

See also [[structure-and-empowerment]]: politics is the marker of disempowerment, and structure (including clear standards) is the design intervention that addresses it.

## The Compounding Effect

Unclear standards compound. The team that has been operating under vague criteria develops habits of political behavior. Even when standards are eventually clarified, the political habits persist. The team has been trained by the environment to look for the relational signal rather than the performance signal.

This is one reason late-stage clarifications often fail. The leader announces clear standards, and the team performs the new standards while continuing the old political behavior. The training has to be unlearned through sustained consistency, which can take longer than the original training took.
