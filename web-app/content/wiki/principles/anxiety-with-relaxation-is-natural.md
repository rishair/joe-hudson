# Anxiety With Relaxation Is Natural

## The Principle

When you start to relax — when love starts coming in, when you stop having to earn it, when the body finally lets down — anxiety often shows up. This is not a sign that the relaxation is wrong or that the love is unsafe. It is the natural response of a body that is being asked to inhabit a new and unfamiliar position. The position is uncomfortable not because it is bad but because it is new.

The mechanism is the same as the body's response to any unfamiliar physical position. Fold your hands one way: that feels normal. Fold them the other way (other thumb on top, other fingers interlaced): that feels weird, off, uncomfortable. The folded-hands feel "wrong." Nothing is wrong. The body has been holding one position for so long that the other position registers as wrong by comparison.

The same is true emotionally. A body that has been chasing love, earning love, performing for love, has been holding one position for years. When that position relaxes and the love is allowed to land on its own, the new position feels weird. The body registers the weirdness as anxiety. The anxiety is not the signal to go back to chasing. It is the signal that something new is happening.

"It's completely natural for you to be anxious with the relaxation. I know that that sounds weird, but it's a completely natural thing." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

## The Hand-Fold Demonstration

The image Joe uses to make the principle concrete. Try it in 10 seconds.

1. Fold your hands together, interlacing the fingers, with one thumb on top (whichever way feels natural). Notice: this feels normal.
2. Now unfold and re-fold with the other thumb on top, all fingers interlaced the opposite way. Notice: this feels strange. The strangeness is immediate. It is not because the new fold is bad. It is because the body has the other fold as its default.

"It's as easy as this. Like, you put your two hands together like this, you fold your fingers down, you go, okay, that feels a certain way. And then you put your hands like this and you do it the other way. You're like, oh, that feels uncomfortable, that feels weird." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The demonstration is operational. The person can do it in any moment and feel the principle in their own body. The strangeness of the second fold is the same kind of strangeness as the anxiety with relaxation. The interpretation is what matters: not "this is wrong" but "this is unfamiliar."

## The Specific Case: Anxiety When Love Arrives

The most common deployment of the principle in coaching: someone who has lived a long time chasing love, performing for love, or earning love begins to actually receive love. The body's response is anxiety.

The anxiety is structurally accurate as information about position-change. The body has been doing the chasing-and-performing for years. Stopping that activity and just letting the love land is a new posture. The new posture is uncomfortable. The discomfort registers as "I'm scared I'm going to lose this." Underneath the fear of loss is the simpler discomfort of being in an unfamiliar position.

"So yeah, it feels weird to start relaxing when you felt like you had to earn all the love that you needed or wanted. And so you can find out, oh, I don't have to do anything but then what's occurring is the love is actually coming in. That relaxation is actually allowing the love to come in." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The key insight: the relaxation is not just incidentally accompanied by the love arriving. The relaxation is what allows the love to arrive. The chasing-and-performing was actually blocking the love from landing. Stopping the chasing is what makes the receiving possible. The anxiety that comes with the stopping is the body's response to the new condition, not a verdict against the stopping.

## The "I'll Lose This" Variant

A specific shape the anxiety often takes verbally: "I'm scared I'm going to lose this." The body that is finally receiving love starts to brace against the loss of the love the moment the love is felt.

"Having that anxiety with the love is a completely natural thing because having that anxiety with love is basically saying, 'Oh, I'm scared I'm going to lose this.' And it's a very natural piece, especially because most of our lives we have been chasing the love instead of actually allowing it to land in us all on its own." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The "I'll lose this" framing is the cognitive translation of the structural anxiety. The body is uncomfortable in the new position; the mind interprets the discomfort as "this means I'm going to lose the new thing." The interpretation is what then drives the impulse to either go back to chasing (familiar position) or to grip the love tightly (which is just chasing in a new costume).

The corrective is to recognize the interpretation as the cognitive layer on top of the structural discomfort. The discomfort is real and natural. The "I'll lose this" story is a story. Holding the discomfort without the story lets the body acclimate to the new position. Acclimation is what happens if the body is allowed to stay in the position long enough.

See [[fear-of-joy]] for the related pattern, where the joy itself becomes the trigger for the anxiety. The two principles overlap in the joy-after-relaxation case: the joy comes when the relaxation arrives, the body brakes at both the joy and the relaxation, and the resulting anxiety is the brake operating on both fronts simultaneously.

## Happiness As What Is Here Minus the Expectations

A related insight Joe articulates in the same passage. When the relaxation lands and the earning-of-love stops, what shows up is not the absence of love but the presence of what was already here. The love was always available. The earning was what was preventing it from being received.

"If you think about what happiness is, what joy is, one way to define it is that it is what's going on right now minus the expectations. And so if you don't have the expectation that you have to do something to earn it, all of a sudden it's just there and it's available to you." -- Joe Hudson, Walk with a Master Coach

The operational definition: happiness = present-moment reality - expectations. The earning-of-love is the expectation that something has to be done to receive love. Drop the expectation, the love is just here. The relaxation is the dropping of the expectation. The anxiety with the relaxation is the discomfort of being in a position where the expectation is no longer organizing experience.

This connects to [[joy-as-natural-state]]: joy is the water under the boats, not a boat that arrives. The relaxation is the discovery that joy was always here. The anxiety is the body's response to no longer having a job to do to produce what was producing itself.

## What This Implies for the Work

When a coachee reports anxiety as they start to relax, the move is not to address the anxiety as a problem to solve. The move is to name the anxiety as the natural response to the position-change and to invite the coachee to stay in the new position long enough for it to become familiar.

The instruction follows directly from the principle:
- **Don't go back to the old position to relieve the anxiety.** Returning to chasing or earning makes the anxiety stop, but at the cost of giving up the relaxation and the love-landing that was happening with it.
- **Stay in the new position.** The body acclimates. The unfamiliar becomes familiar over time. The anxiety diminishes as the position becomes the new default.
- **Don't grip the love.** The "I'll lose this" interpretation produces gripping, which is chasing in a different form. The love that is landing requires open hands, not gripping ones.
- **Notice that the anxiety is signaling the change, not blocking it.** The anxiety is evidence that something genuinely new is happening. A body that was used to relaxing would not generate anxiety from relaxing. The anxiety is the proof that the position is actually different.

## The Connection to Love-Surfaces-the-Unloved

The anxiety with relaxation is also one of the unloved things that the arriving love brings to the surface. See [[love-surfaces-the-unloved]] for the full mechanism. The body's anxiety about the new position is one of the parts that wants to be loved. The instruction "be with the anxiety" can be deepened to "love the anxiety" — the anxiety is part of what is here, and the same loving that produced the relaxation is the right response to the anxiety that surfaced from the relaxation.

The integration: the relaxation lets love land. The love surfaces the unloved (including the anxiety about the relaxation). The unloved is loved. The next layer comes up. The cycle continues until what was anxious about the relaxation is itself relaxed.

## What This Is Not

- Not a license to ignore actual signals of danger. If the relaxation is happening in a context that is genuinely unsafe, the anxiety may be accurate information. The principle applies when the conditions are safe and the anxiety is structural.
- Not an instruction to override the body. The body's discomfort is real and is to be honored. The instruction is to not let the discomfort drive a return to the old position prematurely.
- Not a promise that the anxiety will disappear immediately. The acclimation takes time. The principle is about the right interpretation, not about a guaranteed quick relief.
- Not a contradiction of the practice of welcoming difficult emotions. The anxiety here is welcomed too. The welcoming is what allows the acclimation.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They start meditating and feel anxious when the meditation actually works
- They start receiving compliments without deflecting and feel a wave of unease
- They stop earning approval from a partner and feel scared the partner will withdraw
- They take a real day off and feel restless instead of restored
- They let love land from a child or pet and feel the tightening of "I'm going to lose this"
- They drop the productivity push and feel anxiety about not being productive
- They allow themselves to be cared for and feel uncomfortable being the one receiving
- They take a vacation and feel anxious the whole time about the work waiting at home

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and warming. The teaching is delivered with the implication: "you are not broken, this is just what happens." The naming of the principle is itself part of the relief. The person who has been interpreting their anxiety with relaxation as evidence that the relaxation is wrong gets to hear that the anxiety is evidence the relaxation is real. The reframe lands and the principle starts to do its work.
