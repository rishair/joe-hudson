# Own Your Narcissism (Or Recognize There's Nothing Special About You)

## The Principle

Anyone who teaches or facilitates has to arrive at a specific posture: holding the role of authority and influence without putting themselves above the people they work with. Joe describes two equivalent paths to this posture. Either you genuinely recognize there is nothing special about you, or you own your narcissism -- the wanting to be special, the wanting to be heard, the wanting people to thank you. Both paths lead to the same place: the capacity to hold the role honestly, without the distortion of pretending you have transcended ordinary ego.

The path that does not work is pretending the narcissism is not there. The teacher who claims to have no need for recognition, no want for being special, no enjoyment of being thanked, is not free of those wants -- they have just hidden them, which is the structural setup for [[spiritual-narcissism]] and [[the-arrogantly-humble-paradox]].

"The final ingredient is -- it's a hard thing to describe, but it's either to understand that there's nothing special about you or it is to own your narcissism. It's two different ways to explain the same thing." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## Why This Is the Fourth Foundational Ingredient

For coaching one-on-one, the narcissism problem is contained. For facilitation, especially facilitation that scales, the narcissism problem becomes structurally amplified. People look up to the facilitator. People come to learn. People say thank you. If the facilitator is metabolizing these inputs through unowned narcissism, the inputs feed the unowned want, the want grows, and the facilitator drifts into the teacher-becomes-guru pattern documented in [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]].

The structural protection is owned narcissism. The teacher knows they like the recognition. The teacher knows they wanted to be special. The teacher knows the role gratifies something in them. Because these are owned, they are available for evaluation, and they do not silently direct the work.

"It's so necessary for you not to put yourself above anybody. It's so necessary. And it's so necessary to realize that you want to be special and that you want to be heard and you want people to say thank you. And that is a natural, beautiful part of the process." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## The Both-And of the Posture

The posture is built from two simultaneous truths, both of which have to be held:

1. **I understand the power I hold here.** The role has authority. People are looking to me. What I say has weight. The reality of the influence is acknowledged. Joe's specific version: "I understand the power that I hold here. I can be in that and I can still recognize that I am not special, that essentially I'm not better than anybody."

2. **I am not better than anybody.** Essentially, structurally, I am not above the people I work with. I still have my own lessons to be learning. I am not a finished person looking down on works-in-progress. The peer-level reality is held alongside the role-level reality.

Either side without the other distorts. Holding the power without holding the equality produces the guru posture. Holding the equality without holding the power produces false humility -- the teacher who pretends they have no authority and so cannot use the authority responsibly. See [[humility-vs-disempowerment]] for the related distinction.

## "I Don't Know Why People Come to Me" Is Not the Right Move

A common move that looks like humility but is actually distortion: the teacher who claims not to understand why people come to them. "I don't know why people come to me to be coached." On the surface this looks modest. Underneath, it is a refusal to own the reality of the situation.

"It's not to be in your head saying, I don't understand why all these people come to me to get coaching. Like, I don't know why that is. It's like, yeah, I know why that is. I understand the power that I hold here." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

Owning that you understand why people come is part of the posture. Pretending not to understand is part of the dressed-up version that quietly amplifies the wanting.

## The Two Paths Are Equivalent

The "nothing special about you" path: the recognition that, at the level of being human, there is no significant difference between the teacher and the student. Both are working out the same kinds of things. Both have the same kind of consciousness. The role-difference is not a being-difference.

The "own your narcissism" path: acknowledge the wanting-to-be-special directly. Notice it. Let it be a normal feature of having a self. The owning does not make it disappear; it makes it transparent. The transparency is what prevents the wanting from secretly directing the work.

Both paths arrive at the same operational posture: hold the role, hold the authority, and remain in peer-level equality with the people in the room. Neither path is more advanced. They suit different temperaments. The teacher who can land in the "nothing special" recognition has one entry point. The teacher who is more naturally aware of their wanting to be special has the other. Both work.

## What This Asks Continuously

This is not a one-time achievement. The pull to be special, to be the one who knows, to be looked up to, is a continuous structural pressure on anyone in a teaching role. The owning is therefore continuous. The recognition that one is not special is continuous. The structural correction is the daily practice of returning to the posture, not the one-time arrival at it.

Joe's structural corrections include: having people around him who make fun of him ([[surround-yourself-with-people-who-make-fun-of-you]]); the [[the-fu-joe-handle]] practice; teenage daughters who readily call out anything; an office team that names what he is missing; long-term colleagues in the work who can say "this feels wonky." These are not separate practices from the principle -- they are the structural form of the principle in daily life.

## The "Gentle Narcissist" Form (Real Care Paired With Real Wanting)

A useful refinement of the principle for the helping professions specifically: the narcissism that arrives in coaches, teachers, and facilitators is often not the loud, obvious kind. It is what Joe's daughter calls "gentle narcissism" -- the configuration where the practitioner genuinely cares about the people they serve and is also using the serving to be the one who knows, to be needed, to be valid, to be looked at as the answer.

"What I notice is that there's a lot of things in that call often. One of them is a bit of a narcissism. My daughter has a phrase, she calls people gentle narcissists. And so there's like a gentle narcissism in that call. Typically, and sometimes it's an important part of the call because it's, you're doing it to have that part of you eroded away." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The gentle narcissist is structurally harder to detect than the loud version because the affect is warm. The wanting is wrapped in genuine care. The need to be needed is wrapped in the actual desire to help. This is exactly the configuration the principle is for: the wanting cannot be dressed up as if it were not there, even if (especially if) the helping is real.

Joe specifies two sub-configurations within the gentle narcissism:

- **The person who needs to be the one who knows.** "It allows them to stay in this idea of the person who knows instead of the person who's ignorant, the person who's looked at for an answer, the person who's needed, the person who's still valid or validated or some version of that." This connects to [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]] as the failure mode.
- **The person who needs to be the helper.** "I don't think you go through being a coach without having some of that." The wanting to help is so common in coaches that Joe treats it as nearly universal. The work is not to eliminate the wanting; the work is to own it. See [[coaching-as-savior-role]].

The key claim: the gentle narcissism can be the curriculum if it is owned. The role amplifies it; the owning lets the amplification become integration. The unowned gentle narcissism becomes amplification without integration -- the configuration becomes more entrenched rather than eroded.

The diagnostic for which version is operating: notice which form of recognition feels essential. If being looked at as the wise one feels essential, that wanting is operating. If being needed feels essential, that wanting is operating. If neither can be acknowledged without defense, the owning has not yet happened, and the unowned wanting is silently directing the work.

## The Failure Mode This Prevents

The failure mode if this is unowned: the teacher slowly believes their own press. The "I am the one who knows" identity calcifies. Feedback is read through the lens of "I am the authority." Criticism is read as misunderstanding rather than as data. The teacher's own development stops because no information is allowed to reach them in the unmediated form that would update them.

This is the same structural failure as the one [[spiritual-narcissism]] documents. The corrective is the same: name the ordinary want directly, do not dress it up, do not pretend it is not there. The naming makes it available for inquiry. The dressing-up makes it invisible and uncorrectable.

## Boundary Condition

This is not a license to perform large narcissism as if owning makes it acceptable. "I own that I want to be special, so I'm going to act extra special" is not the posture. Owning the want is the first move. The owned want is then held alongside the recognition that acting on the want would betray the work. The owning makes the work possible; it does not exempt the teacher from the work itself.

The posture is closer to: I notice I am enjoying being thanked. The enjoyment is fine. I am not going to organize my coaching around producing more thank-yous. The enjoyment is available; the organizing principle is the client's freedom, not my enjoyment. Both are present. One directs the work.

## Tone

Wry, plain, sometimes self-deprecating. Joe describes this with a mix of seriousness and lightness because the principle requires both. The seriousness: this is a real failure mode that causes real harm. The lightness: owning the narcissism is not tragic; it is human, and it is workable once acknowledged.
