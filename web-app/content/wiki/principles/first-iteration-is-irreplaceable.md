# The First Iteration Is Irreplaceable (The Teacher Is in Their Own Work)

## The Principle

The first time a teacher runs a retreat, a course, a curriculum, or a new piece of work, the version the participants receive carries a quality the later, more refined versions cannot replicate. The reason: in the first iteration, the teacher is wrestling with the material themselves, in real time, as they teach it. The teaching is alive in the teacher's own system because the teacher does not yet have the comfort of repetition. Later versions improve in many ways. The aliveness of the teacher being in their own first encounter with the material is not one of those ways.

The principle Joe names mid-conversation with Brett:

"What I've noticed is when I do a retreat for the first time, there's a way in which it's like, the people are guinea pigs and they're getting the first version of it, which is not going to be in some ways as good as the future versions of it, but in some ways it's going to be better than any version, you know, because there's something that can happen on the first one that can't happen anywhere else." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The framing names the trade explicitly. First iterations are worse along some dimensions and better along others. The dimensions that matter are not the same dimensions for all participants. For some, the better-polished later version is what they need. For others, the more-alive first version is what they need. Neither version is unambiguously superior.

## The Mechanism

Why first-iteration teaching has this quality:

A teacher with twenty repetitions of a curriculum has internalized the moves. They know what works, where the dips come, when to pivot. Their facilitation is smoother. The participants experience the smoothness as competence. The room runs cleanly.

The teacher's own nervous system, however, is not activated by the material the way it was the first time. The teacher has metabolized the activation. They are running the room from a settled internal state. The settled state is a feature for many purposes, and a loss for one specific purpose: the transmission that happens when participants can feel the teacher's own activation.

Brett describes the personal version of this from his own retreat:

"For myself, I really wanted to experience facilitating this work in a context that is really deeply -- that I'm really deeply steeped in... another aspect of it was facilitating, you know, being, being the sole facilitator in a group in a place with no cell service where there are real risks was something that just re-enlivened something." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The "re-enlivened something" is the felt experience of the teacher being in their own work while teaching the work. The participants register this even when they cannot name it. The teacher's nervous system is in the same activation the participants' nervous systems are in. The shared activation is the transmission.

Joe names the parallel directly:

"You were on your own high line as you're doing the fear thing. You're actually doing the being with the dance, enjoying, welcoming your own fear in the facilitation, which I find really deeply helps facilitation. I notice that a lot of times when people are facilitating something, particularly for the first time, they are wrestling with it themselves in their own way as they're teaching it because it makes it very alive in their system." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The teacher on their own high line is the teacher whose system is in the same state the room is in. The teaching transmits because the teacher is doing what they are teaching, in real time, in front of the room.

## What the First Group Gets

Specific things only available in the first iteration:

- **The teacher's own activation as live curriculum.** The room watches the teacher meet the same difficulty they are being asked to meet. No subsequent iteration can manufacture this.
- **The structural intimacy of "we are all figuring this out together."** The teacher cannot pretend to have it figured out; everyone in the room knows it is the first time. The pretense layer is gone.
- **The teacher's full attention.** A teacher running their twentieth retreat is partly running the muscle memory. A teacher running their first is fully present because they have no muscle memory to fall back on.
- **The teacher's risk in real time.** The first iteration carries actual risk for the teacher's reputation, business, and self-concept. The risk is felt by the room. The risk being felt is part of what makes the room serious.
- **Discoveries made in the room rather than rehearsed.** Some of the best material in any curriculum is discovered live in the first iteration. After that, the discovery becomes the curriculum, and the teacher's part in the discovery becomes a story they tell.

Brett names the related dimension that depended on his being personally activated:

"And so, like, I feel like energetically I was really there in the retreat, um, and I -- that -- not that that can't be true in the future, But there was something, something about that, that it was like, everybody knew that this was my first time running the retreat. In the future, they'll know that I've done it before. There's a different thing going on there." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

## What the Later Iterations Get

The complementary list:

- **Smoother flow.** The dips and breakdowns of the first iteration become smoothable in later ones.
- **Better questions.** The teacher has heard the same response patterns enough times to develop more incisive follow-ups.
- **More accurate timing.** The teacher knows how long each piece actually takes, rather than guessing.
- **Better integration.** The teacher has had time between iterations to think about what would have made the first iteration better.
- **Less anxiety.** The teacher's own nervous system is calmer, which is good for some participants and a loss for others.

Brett names what he plans to do differently in the second iteration:

"One thing I'd love to do is add another day of integration and deep work between the two days of highlining and jumping that we did. Uh, so future iterations will be a little bit longer. And for that, there might be some more depth. There might be some more, um, kind of more spaciousness." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

The added day is a real improvement. It is also impossible in the first iteration, by definition. The first iteration is constrained by what the teacher can hold; the later iterations add what the teacher learned they could hold.

## What This Implies for the Teacher

A teacher contemplating running something for the first time often has the question "should I wait until I am more ready." The principle inverts the question: the not-yet-ready state is part of what makes the first iteration valuable. Waiting until ready loses the first-iteration quality without replacing it with anything the later iterations would have had anyway. The "not ready" anxiety is itself the activation that makes the teaching alive.

This does not mean teaching unprepared. The teacher should be as prepared as they can be for the first iteration. It means recognizing that "I'm not as prepared as I will be in three years" is true and not a reason to wait three years. The first iteration is structurally a different teaching than the third one. Both have value. The first is unrepeatable.

This connects to [[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]]: the difficulty of running something for the first time is the development the teacher will not get from running the more polished later versions. The first iteration is the teacher's own curriculum, not just the participants'.

It also connects to [[welcome-the-dumpster-fire-in-facilitation]]: the things that go wrong in the first iteration are the curriculum, both for the participants who watch the teacher navigate them and for the teacher who learns by navigating them.

## What This Implies for Participants

A participant choosing between a first iteration and a polished later iteration of the same offering should know what they are choosing between. The polished version is generally easier to recommend, less likely to have a chaotic moment, more likely to deliver the announced outcome. The first iteration is more likely to deliver something the participant did not know to want -- the felt experience of the teacher's aliveness, the structural intimacy of shared not-knowing, the discovery that emerged in the room.

Participants who specifically value the live, in-the-moment quality should seek first iterations. Participants who specifically value reliable delivery should seek later iterations. Both choices are legitimate. The diagnostic is what the participant is looking for.

## In Coaching

When a coachee is hesitant to teach, lead, or run something for the first time because they "are not ready yet," the coaching move is to name this principle. The not-yet-ready feeling is information about what is alive in them about the material. The aliveness is what they would bring to the room. Waiting to be ready will lose the aliveness; it will not replace it with anything the room cannot get from the later, more polished version of the teaching.

When a coachee is running something for the first time and is anxious about the participants getting a less-good version than they will get in the future, the coaching move is to refrain that ledger. The participants are getting a different version, not a less-good version. The first-iteration version has its own form of value. The teacher's job is to be in their own work while teaching, which the participants will receive as the teaching.

When a coachee has been teaching the same thing for many years and is asking why the teaching feels stale, the coaching move can be to introduce a new piece that the teacher is in their own work on. The new piece restores the first-iteration quality. The whole curriculum benefits from the teacher being alive in part of it.

## What This Does Not Mean

The principle does not romanticize incompetence. The first iteration should be the best version the teacher can produce with the preparation they have done. The principle is not "throw something together and trust the first-iteration energy to carry it." The principle is: among the well-prepared first iterations and well-prepared later iterations, neither dominates, and each has its own value.

The principle also does not mean teachers should run only first iterations. The polish and refinement of later iterations is real value for many participants. The work of building a sustainable practice involves running the same things many times. The first-iteration principle is a check on the assumption that newer is always less good. It is not an instruction to prefer first iterations as a category.

## The Continuing Practice of First-Iteration Quality

A practical extension: experienced teachers can maintain something of the first-iteration quality by introducing live material into established curricula. A question they have not asked before. A reflection they are working out in real time. A piece of new content they have not delivered before. Each of these gives the teacher's nervous system a piece of fresh activation in the middle of the well-rehearsed material. The room feels the live piece even when the rest is muscle memory.

The discipline is to continue putting the teacher into their own work, not to coast on the established curriculum. The teacher who has stopped doing their own work cannot deliver the first-iteration quality, even in their first iteration of a new thing -- because they have stopped being a person who can be in their own work. The capacity has atrophied.

This connects to [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]]: the teacher's own ongoing self-awareness work is the source of the aliveness they bring to teaching. Without the ongoing work, the teaching becomes the performance of teaching, regardless of whether the curriculum is first-iteration or fortieth.

Joe offers this with appreciation rather than instruction. The tone honors what is, rather than positioning it as on the way to what will be.
