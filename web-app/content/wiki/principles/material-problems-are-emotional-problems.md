# Material Problems Are Emotional Problems

## The Principle

What people call "material" problems -- the company is failing, the marriage is ending, the money is running out, the kids are struggling -- are not actually material problems. They are emotional problems wearing material costumes. The "problem" is not the event. The "problem" is the anticipated emotional experience the event will produce.

The test: if you knew with certainty that the material outcome would feel great -- that the company imploding would produce freedom and success and joy, that the divorce would produce peace and aliveness -- there would be no problem. The event is identical in either case. The "problem" lives in the relationship to the anticipated feeling.

"What we call material problems out in the world, what they really are are emotional problems. What they really are is that we don't want to feel a certain way." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

## The Two Thought Experiments

Joe uses two thought experiments to make the principle concrete.

**The company implosion:** "Let's just say, for instance, your company, you're facing your company's implosion. And, and in that implosion, you knew you were going to feel great, and you were going to feel free, and you were going to feel successful. What's the problem with your company imploding?" -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

**The divorce:** "Let's say I was going to get a divorce. And I knew that I was going to feel wonderful about the outcome, that I could look around and I would go, 'Wow, this is exactly what needed to happen and I can see the benefits of it,' and there's just a full feeling of freedom and success and joy in the result, then there's no problem." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

The shape of both is identical. Take the material event the person calls catastrophic. Hold the event constant. Change the anticipated emotional outcome from devastation to ease. Notice the problem evaporate. The work shows that the "problem" was never the event; the problem was the relationship to the projected feeling.

## Why This Matters Operationally

This principle is the reframe that makes [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] -- the golden algorithm -- operable. Without it, the algorithm gets applied to the wrong layer.

The wrong layer: "How do I prevent the material outcome?" This generates strategies designed to avoid the event. The strategies are usually shaped by the underlying emotional avoidance -- they are sophisticated forms of the avoidance behavior that produces the very outcome they are trying to prevent. The harder the person works at the material level, the more the avoidance pattern intensifies, the more reliably the feared event is generated.

The right layer: "What feeling am I avoiding here, and how is the avoidance generating the conditions for the feared event?" This question can only be asked once the person has accepted that the problem is emotional, not material. As long as they believe the problem is the event, they cannot reach the avoidance behavior that is producing it.

## The Universal-Religion Frame

Joe positions this principle as something every major religion has pointed at, each in its own language:

"You're never gonna get the happiness you want from the external world. And I think that's something that every major religion points to in its own way." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

The Buddhist frame: attachment to external conditions produces suffering. The Christian frame: the kingdom of heaven is within. The Sufi frame: you are looking for the beloved outside who lives inside. Each tradition encodes the same observation: the strategy of trying to produce internal happiness through external conditions does not work. The strategy can succeed at producing the conditions, and the happiness still does not arrive in the form expected.

The principle is not the religious claim that material conditions do not matter. The principle is the more precise claim that the route from conditions to happiness does not run through more conditions. The material strategy is a surrogate for the emotional one, and surrogates always fail in the end because they are not what was wanted.

## Money as the Cleanest Example

Money is the cleanest illustration of the principle because the surrogate function is so visible. The person believes more money will produce security, freedom, ease, love. They organize a life around producing more money. The money arrives. The internal state does not match the anticipation. The same anxieties show up around the larger sum. The same scarcity story runs.

See [[money-as-surrogate]] for the dedicated treatment and [[same-scarcity-story-regardless-of-wealth]] for the empirical observation that the scarcity loop runs independent of actual wealth. See [[if-i-had-more-money]] for the concern this principle most directly addresses. The money case is the principle in concentrated form.

But the principle generalizes far beyond money. Career success, the right relationship, the perfect home, the kids being okay, the body being right -- every material project that is being pursued for an anticipated emotional outcome is operating under the same principle. The project can succeed at the material level and still fail at the emotional level, because the route from one to the other does not run the way people imagine.

## The Boundary Condition: Events Still Happen

A careful clarification: the principle does not mean that material events do not happen, or that the only thing that matters is internal state. A tree can fall on the car. The economy can tank. The doctor can deliver bad news. These events occur in the material world and have material consequences.

The principle says two things about such events. First, for the recurring problems in a life -- the patterns that keep showing up -- the events are almost always being generated by the same emotional avoidance that the person is calling the problem. The recurrence is the evidence of the emotional layer underneath. Second, even for genuinely random events, the duration and intensity of the "problem" they produce is determined by which emotions the person will and will not feel. The same accident becomes a sustained crisis for the person who avoids the underlying grief and a temporary disruption for the person who lets it move.

"To some degree, it's a problem because the emotions that you're feeling are being resisted and that makes it a bigger problem instead of, 'Oh, this is this fluid thing that's moving through me.' We have all had something stolen from us and there's a time when it's a problem and there's a time when it's not a problem... how we deeply feel into those emotions and allow those emotions has a huge influence on whether it's currently a problem or not a problem in our life." -- Joe Hudson, The Golden Algorithm

The distinction Joe draws is between "something happening" and "a problem." Something happens. The thing happening becomes a problem when it consumes the person, when it guides them, when they rebel against and resist it. See [[problem-vs-something-happening]] for the dedicated distinction.

## Where This Leads in Coaching

When a person presents a material problem, the first coaching move is to test whether the principle applies. The diagnostic question: "If you knew you were going to feel great about the outcome, would this still be a problem?" The answer is almost always no. The follow-up: "So what is the feeling you're not wanting to have?" This question moves the work from the material layer to the emotional layer, where the actual work can be done.

The corrective is not to ignore the material situation. The corrective is to address the material situation from a state that is not driven by emotional avoidance. Action taken from non-avoidance is responsive to what is actually needed. Action taken from avoidance is responsive to what would prevent the feeling, which is almost always the wrong action.

See [[decisions-are-emotional]] for the related principle that decisions are emotional all the way down -- the intellectual surface of decision-making is serving the emotional question of how the person will feel. See [[feel-over-figure]] for the orientation that allows the emotional layer to be addressed before the material layer is acted on.

## Tone

Plain and structural. The principle is offered as an observation, not as an exhortation. The thought experiments do the persuasive work. Once the company-implosion experiment is run, the principle becomes hard to argue with from inside the person's own experience.
