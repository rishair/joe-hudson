# Next-Generation Institutions Create Healing Moments

## The Principle

The previous generation of institutional design optimized for fairness -- equal opportunity, equal access, equal rules. This was a real and important achievement. It was also incomplete. The institutions that emerged out of the fairness orientation often did not produce healing, growth, or the deeper kind of development that people actually need from the structures they live inside.

The next-generation institution makes healing and growth a designed feature. Every wedding can be a healing wedding. Every funeral can be a healing funeral. Every business can be a healing business. Every boardroom can be a healing boardroom. The healing is not an accidental side-effect of getting the other thing right. It is the structural intent of the design.

"My hope would be that the next generation of institutions is: how do we, every institution we have, create healing moments and growth? Every once in a while you get to a wedding that feels healing. Oh, wow, that's healing. Get really lucky. Sometimes you go to a funeral and it's a healing funeral. It's not just people getting drunk and avoiding their emotions. And I think there's an opportunity for that to be the case in business, and it makes business more productive, and in boardrooms, and it makes boardrooms more effective, and in every one of our institutions. So my hope would be that if there's enough people who are holding that reality field of everything that we do can be a path of development, and then I think that we have a really great society." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## What This Replaces

The default institutional design treats the human-emotional dimension as orthogonal to the institution's purpose. Weddings are about celebrating a union. Funerals are about marking a death. Businesses are about producing economic value. Boardrooms are about governance. The emotional content of the people involved is treated as something to manage on the side rather than as something the institution is structurally serving.

This default produces predictable failures:

- Weddings that are about the performance of a wedding rather than the actual integration of two lives. The pictures are good. The integration does not happen. The couple does not metabolize the transition.
- Funerals that are about the social management of grief rather than the actual grieving. See [[dont-comfort-the-mourner-from-your-own-discomfort]]. People get drunk. People avoid their emotions. The mourner is asked to perform okay-ness. The grief is not held; it is deferred or fragmented.
- Businesses that produce economic value while producing chronic emotional damage in the people who work there. The damage is treated as the cost of doing business, or as the responsibility of the individual employees to manage on their own time.
- Boardrooms that exchange information about the state of the company while leaving the emotional intelligence of the room offline. Decisions are made from defended positions. The actual data the room could be producing through its emotional intelligence is unavailable.

In each case, the institution is doing one thing well and another thing badly. The next-generation framing asks: can the institution do both? Can the wedding celebrate a union and also produce the healing that supports the union? Can the funeral mark a death and also produce the grief-completion that allows the survivors to continue? Can the business produce economic value and also produce the development of the people doing the work? Can the boardroom govern the company and also be the structural condition for the emotional intelligence of its members?

The principle says: yes, this is possible, and a society in which enough institutions are designed this way is a better society.

## The Healing Wedding

Joe's example: "Every once in a while you get to a wedding that feels healing. Oh, wow, that's healing. Get really lucky."

The healing wedding is the wedding where something integrates that needed integrating. The family members who had not spoken meet and meet honestly. The couple's commitment is felt by everyone present. The community recognition of the union is real. The transition into marriage is metabolized, not just performed.

Most weddings are not this. The principle is that the healing wedding is possible by design, not just by luck. The design includes the choice of who is present, what is acknowledged, what rituals are run, what space is held for the family-of-origin material that the wedding surfaces. A wedding designed for healing produces healing. A wedding designed for performance produces performance, and the healing that was structurally possible does not happen.

See the related teaching about grief ceremonies in [[community-grief-ceremony]] for the parallel principle in the death-of-a-loved-one case. The format is different; the underlying design choice is the same.

## The Healing Funeral

Joe's example: "Sometimes you go to a funeral and it's a healing funeral. It's not just people getting drunk and avoiding their emotions."

The healing funeral is the funeral where the grief actually moves. The community holds the container. The mourners are not asked to perform okay-ness. The dead person is honored in a way that allows the relationships to complete. The survivors leave more whole than they arrived, not less.

The conventional funeral often produces the opposite. The mourner is treated as the audience for everyone else's grief-management. The community present at the funeral is uncomfortable with grief and discharges that discomfort onto the mourner. See [[dont-comfort-the-mourner-from-your-own-discomfort]] for the structural failure mode. The dead person is eulogized in a way that smooths over the real relationships rather than completing them. People get drunk afterward because the actual grief never had a container.

The healing funeral is built. It is not accidental. The principle says it is worth building.

See [[host-a-grief-ceremony-as-community]] for an operational format. See [[grieving-in-groups-beats-grieving-solo]] for the structural reason the design works.

## The Healing Business

The hope extends to business. "There's an opportunity for that to be the case in business, and it makes business more productive."

This is not a hope that business should be incidentally nice. It is the structural claim that business that produces healing is more productive than business that does not. The development of the people doing the work is not orthogonal to the work. It is upstream of the work. A business that designs for human development produces:

- Better decisions, because the people making them have more available emotional intelligence
- Less internal friction, because the inner work that would otherwise be deferred is being done in the structure
- Lower turnover, because the place is one where people grow rather than one where people deplete
- Better products, because the people building them are not running on unprocessed material that distorts the work
- Higher loyalty, because the business is actually serving the people in it, not just extracting value from them

See [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]] and [[vulnerability-in-the-workplace]] for specific designs that move a business in this direction. See [[applied-self-awareness]] for the broader frame that this all rests on -- self-awareness is not a personal-development hobby; it is operational for everything.

## The Healing Boardroom

The same principle applied to governance. "Boardrooms and it makes boardrooms more effective."

The boardroom is where the most consequential decisions in many institutions get made. It is also typically the room where the emotional intelligence is most actively suppressed. People are performing. People are positioning. People are defending their constituencies. The actual data that the room could produce -- the felt intelligence of the people in it, the underlying concerns that no one is willing to name, the relational truths that would change the decision -- is offline.

A boardroom designed for healing-and-effectiveness builds in the structural conditions for those data to come online. People can name fear. People can name disagreement. People can name the underneath of the position they are about to take. The decisions improve because the data improves. The board members develop because the room is one in which their full intelligence is operational.

This is rare. The cultural script for boardrooms is the opposite. The next-generation framing says the rare can become the norm, and that the institutions that do this are the institutions that will outperform the ones that do not.

## The Reality Field

The principle's mechanism for adoption: "If there's enough people who are holding that reality field of everything that we do can be a path of development, and then I think that we have a really great society."

The reality field is the shared assumption among enough people that the design choice is real and worth making. No single person can install healing-institutions by themselves. A critical mass of people who believe this is possible and act accordingly can produce the field in which the institutions can emerge.

This is why the conversation in which this principle is being articulated -- three coaches talking on a podcast -- matters operationally. The reality field is being built one conversation at a time. Each person who hears the framing and starts holding it is part of the field. The institutions emerge when the field is dense enough to support them.

## The Predecessor Achievement This Builds On

The fairness orientation of the previous generation of institutions is honored, not dismissed. "The institutions of the last generation of folks was very much about fairness, maybe some equality, but there was very much like a, how do we give everybody an equal opportunity? Not an equal reality, but maybe an equal opportunity."

The next-generation institutions inherit the fairness work and build on it. The healing-and-development orientation is in addition to fairness, not in place of it. An institution that is fair but does not produce healing is incomplete. An institution that produces healing but is not fair is also incomplete. The work is to hold both.

## Tone

Aspirational without being naive. Joe is not claiming that next-generation institutions are easy to build. He is claiming that they are worth building, that they outperform the previous generation when they exist, and that the hope is reasonable because there are working examples in every category (the wedding that healed, the funeral that healed, the business that healed). The tone is the patient optimism of someone who has seen the principle work in enough specific cases to believe it can scale, while being honest that the scaling depends on enough people holding the field.
