# You Can't Be Argued With About Your Want

## The Principle

Your wants are not propositions about external reality. They cannot be contested with evidence. They are not accusations about who is wrong. They are simply yours. There is no defensive move someone else can make against your statement of what you want.

Joe credits this teaching to Kasey DeBrawn:

"It was something that Kasey DeBrawn used to teach all the time, which is somebody can argue with the fact that they're a drunk or not a drunk. Somebody can argue with the fact that you look like shit or you don't look like shit, or that they look like shit or they don't look like shit, but they can't really argue with the fact that you want to be in a family where people love each other because it's what you want. They can't argue with what you want. And so there's less of a place for them to be defensive." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

## Why This Is Structurally True

An argument requires two propositions that can be tested against each other. "You are a drunk" is a proposition: it claims something about external reality, it can be measured against the evidence, it can be contested, and the contest can be won or lost.

"I want to be in a family where people love each other" is not a proposition. It is a report of inner experience. There is no evidence that contradicts it. The other person cannot say "no, you don't want that," because they are not in your head. They cannot say "you shouldn't want that," because the wanting is already happening — the "should" cannot reach back into a present-tense fact.

The structural property: wants are facts about you, not claims about reality. They cannot be argued because they cannot be falsified. The other person's only options are to receive the want or refuse to receive it. They cannot defeat it in argument.

## What This Enables in Hard Conversations

The principle makes a specific move available: when you need to name something hard in a relationship, lead with the want rather than the accusation.

The accusation invites defense. The want does not. The want creates a conversation where the other person has nothing to push against. Whatever they do next is their own choice, not a forced response to an attack.

Joe's example, applied to the alcoholic-father situation he grew up in:

- **Accusation form (invites defense):** "Dad, you're an alcoholic."
- **Want form (no defense available):** "What I really want is a family where we can love each other and not fight at the table every day."

The accusation produces decades of fight. The want produces an opening, if the other person can meet it. Even if they refuse to meet it, the conversation has not become a fight about whether the accusation was true. It has become a statement of what is wanted, which sits there, undefendable.

See [[speak-your-own-elephant-to-surface-theirs]] for the move that applies this principle.

## The Same Logic for Your Own Role

The principle extends to statements about your own role in a dynamic. "I keep playing the role of X, and I do not want to anymore" is not contestable in the same way. The other person was not in your head when you played the role. They cannot tell you what role you were playing. You can name your own role with no defensive move available to them.

This is why naming your own elephant works in places where direct naming of the other person's elephant fails. Both moves rely on the same underlying principle: when you speak from your own truth — your wants, your role, your experience — there is no rhetorical attack surface for the other person to find.

## Why This Is Not Just Strategic

The principle is descriptive, not strategic. Joe is not teaching "use the want form to disarm the other person." That framing would collapse the practice into a manipulation tactic, and the manipulation would be readable — the listener would feel the strategy and respond to it as another attack.

The principle works because wants are *actually* undefendable. The honesty of the statement is what gives it its undefendability. If the want is genuine, the principle holds. If the want is performed as a tactic, the listener reads the performance and the principle collapses.

This connects to [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]]: the act of genuinely owning a want changes the want, often refining it into a deeper one. The want you arrive at after the dissolution is the one that has the most undefendable quality, because it is the most authentic.

## The Permission This Gives You

A common block to speaking hard truths: "I don't have evidence yet" or "I might be wrong." The principle reveals that evidence and rightness are categories that apply to propositions, not wants. You do not need evidence to want something. You do not need to be right about anything to state your want.

The block usually disguises a different fear — the fear of stating a want and not getting it. The undefendability cuts both ways: the listener cannot argue you out of the want, but they also cannot be forced to give it to you. They can simply refuse. The undefendability is not a guarantee of getting what you want. It is a guarantee that the want itself cannot be diminished by argument.

This is the freedom the principle offers: you can state what you want without having to defend why you want it. Whether the listener gives it to you is a separate question.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Using "I want" as a sneaky form of accusation.** "I want you to stop being an alcoholic" is not a want; it is an instruction. The genuine want underneath might be "I want our family to be peaceful" or "I want to feel safe at home." The disguised accusation gets read as an accusation.
- **Stating wants you do not actually have.** Performance wants do not have the undefendable property; the performance is readable.
- **Stating the want and then defending why you want it.** The defense undoes the principle. You do not need to defend the want; defending it implies it requires justification, which puts it back in the propositional category.
