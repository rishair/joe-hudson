# Leadership Is Fragile

## The Principle

The power a leader appears to have is not actually theirs. It is given to them by the people who treat them as a leader, and it can be taken back. The leader does not own the power. They are using it on loan. The moment the people stop conferring it, the power disappears — even if the title, position, and trappings remain. This makes leadership-power structurally fragile in a way that gets obscured by its appearance of solidity.

"Leadership is super fragile. You don't really have power. You have power because people think you have power, but that can be taken away because people stop thinking you have power. So leadership is, it can come and go really quickly. So to even confuse it with power is kind of like a silliness." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The principle reframes leadership from a possession (power one has) to a permission (power one is currently being granted). The grammar of "the leader has power" gets replaced with "the people are currently giving the leader power." The shift is small in language and large in implication.

## What Makes The Power Real While It Lasts

The fragility does not mean the power is illusory. It is real while it is operating. Decisions get made. Resources move. People respond. The teeth of the power are present. But the source of the teeth is not the leader's position — it is the ongoing decision by the group to treat the position as carrying authority. The group can revoke that decision. When it does, the position remains and the power leaves.

The historical record is full of examples: leaders who held formal positions but had no actual leverage because the group had stopped following. Leaders who lost formal positions but kept all their leverage because the group continued to defer. The position is one input. The group's ongoing conferral is the other. Both are needed for the power to operate.

## Why The Principle Matters For The Person Considering Leadership

The principle directly addresses a fear pattern: the person who refuses leadership because they imagine that having power would corrupt them or that they would become someone they do not want to be. The fear is operating on a false picture. They are imagining the power as something they would acquire and possess — a substance that would change them. The reality is that the power is something the group would lend them on revocable terms. The picture of being permanently changed by acquiring power is structurally wrong.

This connects to [[leadership-requires-closed-heart-inheritance]]. The binary the person is fighting (be loving OR be the powerful asshole) assumes the power is a stable thing the leader holds. Once the power is recognized as a permission, the binary loses its bite. The leader is not "becoming powerful" in a transformative sense. They are being temporarily entrusted with leverage that operates relationally.

## Why The Principle Matters For The Person With Formal Authority

For the person who currently holds a formal position of authority, the principle is a check against the most common drift: treating the title as the leadership. The title produces some default conferral (people defer to titles as a baseline), but the actual leadership requires ongoing relational maintenance. The moment the group stops conferring, the title becomes ceremonial.

This is why the [[save-leadership-framework]] components matter operationally. Each component is a thing the leader does that earns ongoing conferral. Self-development for the team. Autonomy honored. Vision shared. Esteem present. These are the maintenance practices that keep the borrowed power flowing. A leader who stops doing them does not lose the title — they lose the function. The fragility of the power means the maintenance is not optional.

The corollary: the leader who is consciously aware that the power is borrowed tends to lead more wisely than the leader who imagines the power as theirs. The borrower handles the loan more carefully than the owner handles their own thing. The conscious awareness of the fragility is itself a stabilizer.

## The Distinction From Ownership

A specific operational implication: the leader should own the influence they have without overestimating the durability of the power.

"I think there's an ownership of the power, recognizing like, oh, what I say has influence. But that should be at any level of leadership. That kind of ownership needs to be there." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The ownership of the influence is honest acknowledgment of what is currently operating. The overestimation of the durability would be the failure mode the principle warns against. Both can be present at once: I have influence right now, AND that influence depends on the ongoing conferral of the people who are treating it as influence.

The person who refuses to own the influence they currently have is doing a different version of the avoidance pattern (see [[avoiding-leadership-still-leaves-you-as-the-leader]]). The person who overestimates the durability is doing the failure pattern the fragility-principle is correcting. The both-and is the accurate stance.

## The Connection To Empowerment Over Power

The principle deepens the [[empowered-vs-power]] distinction. Power-over depends on the conferral of the people being controlled. The control is fragile because the conferral is revocable. Empowerment is the capacity to act from one's own truth — which is not dependent on the conferral of others. The fragile thing is the power-over move; the durable thing is the empowered stance.

The leader who builds their leadership on power-over has built on something fragile by structure. The leader who builds on empowerment has built on something the group cannot revoke. The shift from the first to the second is the developmental arc of leadership. The fragility-principle is the diagnostic that surfaces the difference.

## The Implication For The Negotiation Of Leadership

When the power is recognized as conferred, the negotiation of leadership becomes more honest. The leader is not entitled to the power they are using — they are renting it from the group on the basis of the group's continued willingness. The leader's job is not to consolidate the rented power but to operate the leadership well enough that the conferral continues for legitimate reasons rather than from inertia.

This is why the question for the leader is not "how do I keep my power" but "what would make me worth continuing to follow." The first question is unanswerable in any stable way because the power was never the leader's. The second question has actual answers — and the answers tend to be the [[save-leadership-framework]] components, [[view]], the leadership-by-conviction (see [[leadership-is-following-your-conviction]]).

## What This Is Not

**Not a counsel of weakness.** The principle does not say "act humbly because your power could be taken away." It says "act accurately — the power is borrowed, lead from that truth." The lead-from-conviction move is unchanged. The relationship to the power that move uses is what changes.

**Not "everyone is your equal."** The principle does not flatten the structural differences in authority. The leader still holds the formal position. The team still operates within the container. The principle is about the source of the leverage, not about the existence of the structure.

**Not a license to refuse responsibility.** The leader is still accountable for the use of the conferred power. The fragility does not reduce the accountability — it sharpens it, because the leverage is borrowed and the consequences of mishandling it land on the leader and on those they led.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The recognition that the formal position they hold is not actually the source of their leverage
- The fear-relief of seeing that the power they were afraid of acquiring is not the kind of thing one acquires
- The reorientation of the question from "how do I get/keep power" to "what makes me worth following"
- The humility of seeing that the leadership operating in them is dependent on the people who are following
- The strange freedom of leading from a stance that does not require the leadership to be permanent

Joe delivers this matter-of-factly. The principle is offered as a clarification, not a warning.
