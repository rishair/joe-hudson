# Compartmentalizing Can Be Adaptive

## The Principle

It is not the case that the well-developed person feels everything as it arrives. The well-developed person matches the feeling to the available container. Sometimes the container is "right now in the corner alone with my partner holding me for an hour." Sometimes the container is "ten minutes between meetings to let the steam out of the kettle." Sometimes the container is "a few seconds of tears that surface in conversation and pass." Sometimes the container has to wait until after the high-stakes work is done.

Compartmentalizing -- holding a feeling for later rather than discharging it now -- can be a legitimate developmental adaptation. The demand to feel everything immediately is itself often a should, a self-blame loop, a way of converting the emotional work into another performance the person fails at.

## The Worked Example: The Brother's Diagnosis During the Retreat

Brett found out about his brother's stage 4 glioblastoma diagnosis at the beginning of a week-long retreat he was facilitating. He was running a retreat about how to fluidly feel emotions. He had just received news that would obviously trigger massive feeling.

He compartmentalized through the retreat. He let small moments of crying happen as they surfaced. He facilitated. He stayed functional for the participants. When the retreat ended, he asked his partner Alexa to hold him and cried for an hour.

"And so the compartmentalizing was adaptive." -- Brett Kistler, Making The Most of Uncertainty

"Yeah, you had like little moments of crying. You were like letting the steam out of the kettle. Like, if I recall correctly, we had that grief process and there's some other places, but you were compartmentalizing enough and then boom, you totally [released]." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

The compartmentalizing was not avoidance. It was titration. The kettle had a slow steam release during the retreat to prevent buildup. The full release came when the container was actually available. The work was the matching of the feeling to the appropriate container, not the demand that the feeling discharge fully on first arrival.

## The Trap This Principle Names

The trap: people listen to teaching about feeling emotions and convert it into a new should. "I need to feel my emotions all the time, and if I am not, that means I am failing." The should produces self-blame. The self-blame produces another problem to solve. The new problem absorbs the energy that the actual feeling would have used.

"Sometimes people listen to our podcast and they're like, I need to feel my emotions all the time, and if I'm not, then that means I'm fucking up. And then here comes the self-blame." -- Brett Kistler, Making The Most of Uncertainty

"Yeah, exactly. The next thing I have to do to be good enough." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

The teaching about feeling emotions becomes the source of a new self-improvement project. The project requires constant monitoring of internal state. The monitoring creates a meta-layer that interferes with the feeling itself. The person ends up further from contact with their actual emotional life than they were before they learned the teaching.

See [[tools-to-change-not-love]] for the larger pattern of using the work to fix yourself rather than to love yourself.

## Why This Matters in Uncertainty Work

In sustained uncertainty, compartmentalizing is often the only way to function. The full grief about the situation cannot be felt continuously over months or years. The system needs windows of compartment to hold the load.

The work is not to abolish compartmentalization. It is to ensure the compartments open periodically, that the feeling gets its container at least sometimes, and that the eventual release is allowed when the conditions for it are present.

This is part of [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty|the three-move practice]]. The second move (feel the resisted emotion) does not mean feel everything all at once. It means find the feeling that has been resisted and let it have a container. The container can be small. The full release can be later. The first contact with the actual feeling is what changes the body's relationship to the situation.

## When Compartmentalization Becomes a Problem

Compartmentalizing becomes a problem when:

- The compartments never open. The feeling is held permanently.
- The release containers are never created. The kettle has no steam-release.
- The compartmentalization extends to numbing rather than holding.
- The compartmentalization is being used to avoid the work, rather than to titrate it.
- The compartmentalization is rigid -- the same response to every feeling -- rather than calibrated to the situation.

In these cases, the principle does not change but its application does. The work shifts toward creating the containers that have been missing, opening the compartments that have been sealed, and developing the capacity to be in the feeling at all.

## When Compartmentalization Is the Right Move

- High-stakes external work where the feeling cannot have its container yet (running the retreat, performing surgery, finishing the negotiation, parenting the children through bedtime)
- The middle of a crisis where the system needs the executive function the feeling would temporarily disrupt
- Periods where the supportive container (partner, friend, therapist, time alone) is not currently available
- Acute danger or instability where the feeling would compromise safety

In each case, compartmentalizing is not avoidance. It is matching the feeling to the appropriate container. The matching is the skill. The discharge happens when the container becomes available.

## The Patience Component

Underneath the principle is a quality of patience with oneself. The work is not a deadline. The feeling does not have to resolve by next Tuesday. The titration that the system actually needs may extend across weeks or months. The patience to let it move at the pace it needs to move is part of the work.

"You have patience with yourself in that process. It wasn't like, I have to feel it all right fucking now. You know, it doesn't work like that." -- Joe Hudson, Making The Most of Uncertainty

The impatient demand to feel-it-all-now is often the residue of a culture that wants quick results and converts emotional work into another quick-results domain. The patient titration is closer to how the system actually metabolizes large feelings.

## Tone

The principle is delivered with tenderness toward the person who has been demanding too much of themselves. There is a quality of recognition: I have seen many people convert this teaching into another self-blame loop, and here is the off-ramp.

The tone communicates: you are not failing if you are not feeling everything all the time. You are not failing if you compartmentalize through a high-stakes period. The work is the matching, not the volume.
