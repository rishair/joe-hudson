# What We Don't Want to Look At Is Where the Healing Is

## The Principle

The thing a person does not want to look at is the thing the person most needs to attend to. The not-wanting is not noise. It is the signal. The shame that produces the turning-away is also the marker for where the healing lives.

The mechanism is direct. Humans turn away from things because of shame. The shame attaches to the thing that the person believes makes them bad, wrong, or unlovable. The thing the shame is attached to is the same thing that is preventing the person from being whole. So the shame's turning-away function is also a compass: follow the not-wanting-to-look, and you will arrive at the work.

"If we don't want to look at something as humans, it means that there's a shame that we have in our system. And every time we think about that thing, that shame arises and we don't want to think about it. That's exactly where our healing is. That's exactly the thing to pay attention to." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of People

The principle inverts the usual relationship between avoidance and progress. The typical assumption is that the work is to find new things to look at, to add insight, to expand awareness. The principle says: the work is already pointed at. Look at the thing you have been not looking at. The map is the aversion.

## Why the Shame-Avoidance Connection Is Reliable

People do not avoid neutral material. Things that are emotionally inert do not produce active not-wanting. They produce indifference, which is a different texture. The active turning-away — the body shift, the conversation pivot, the "let's not talk about that" — happens specifically when the thing being avoided is loaded with shame.

The shame is loaded because at some point in the person's history, the thing got coupled with a felt sense of being bad. The coupling may have been installed by a parent's reaction, a cultural message, a religious teaching, an experience of being seen at a moment of vulnerability. Whatever the install mechanism, the shame got attached, and now the thing cannot be approached without the shame arising. The shame is uncomfortable, so the person turns away. The turning-away preserves the shame in its undisturbed state.

This is why the avoidance pattern is durable. Each turning-away reinforces the shame-thing pairing without ever giving the person a chance to feel the shame fully, see what it is attached to, and discover that the thing-without-the-shame is something they can hold. The avoidance is the maintenance procedure for the shame.

The diagnostic value is that the maintenance procedure is loud. The person is doing visible work — every time they avoid — to preserve the shame. The work points at where the shame is.

## How the Principle Operates in a Coaching Session

The diagnostic moves in two directions.

**From topic to shame.** When the client steers around a topic, the steering is the signal. The coach does not need to demand the client go there. The coach can name that the steering is happening: "I notice we keep approaching this and moving away. What is making it hard to look at?" The naming honors the avoidance and asks the client to describe the shame.

**From shame to topic.** When the client reports a chronic shame without knowing what it is attached to, the coach can ask the inverse: "What is the thing you don't want to look at? What is the thing you don't want to feel? What is the thing you don't want me to know?" The shame's attachment-object will surface in the answer. The avoidance is the cover; the cover is the location of the thing.

In the Good Will Hunting scene, the therapist offers the file. Will responds "Why?" — the question is the avoidance. The character does not want to look at what's in the file. The "why" is the shame's voice. The therapist does not push. He has the diagnostic ("there's shame here, this is where the work is") and the patience to let the trust be built before the looking happens. See [[good-will-hunting-its-not-your-fault]] for the full scene and [[trust-building-before-exposure]] for the patience principle.

## What the Principle Replaces

The principle replaces three common but ineffective approaches:

1. **"Let's focus on what's working."** Sometimes the right move, but often the move that lets the shame remain undisturbed. If the working stuff is the topic, the not-working stuff (which is where the shame is) is being avoided by both client and coach together.

2. **"Let's not go there, we don't need to retraumatize you."** Sometimes the right move when the trust isn't there or the nervous system is overwhelmed. Often a way for the coach to collude with the avoidance because the coach is also uncomfortable with the material. The principle does not say push past the client's no; it says recognize that the no is where the work is, so the work plans to go there eventually with the right preparation.

3. **"Let's find a positive frame for this."** The reframe move is useful for some patterns. For shame, the reframe often does the same thing as the avoidance: it preserves the felt sense by replacing it with a thought-package that the client can hold without feeling. The shame stays in place. The principle says the felt sense is the material, not the frame.

The principle redirects all three: from the comfortable to the avoided, from the bypassed to the felt, from the reframed to the experienced.

## What Makes a Topic the Right Topic

The principle is not a license to chase every flinch. Some flinches are protective in ways that need to be honored — a recent trauma may need stabilization before approach, a survival situation may not be safe to touch. The principle is operational rather than mechanical.

The shape of a topic that is the right topic to go toward:

- The client returns to it repeatedly even while avoiding it. The topic has gravity.
- The client describes shame about it directly, or shows shame markers (downward gaze, voice drop, body collapse) when it comes up.
- The avoidance is producing visible cost in the person's life — the marriage, the work, the body, the sense of self.
- The person is asking, in some form, "why can't I get past this?" — which is the conscious version of the not-looking that the principle names.

The shape of a topic that may not be the right topic right now:

- The person's nervous system is in active crisis and would not survive the approach.
- The trust between coach and client has not been built ([[trust-building-before-exposure]]).
- The topic is one the client has been pressured to address by others (partner, parent, therapist's agenda) and the not-looking is partly a healthy refusal to be coerced.

The discrimination is part of the work. The principle gives the direction; the discrimination tells you whether to walk toward it now or to build the preconditions first.

## The Principle's Inverse Failure

The principle does not say "any avoidance is shame avoidance, therefore push through any avoidance." Some avoidance is wisdom. Some things are not yours to look at. Some material is properly someone else's. The principle's reliability rests on a specific signal: the avoidance is producing suffering, the person is showing shame around the topic, the topic keeps re-surfacing. When those conditions hold, the avoidance is the marker. When they don't, the avoidance may be wisdom and the work is to honor it.

The pathological inverse is the coach who hears "avoidance is the signal" and starts treating every reluctance as something to push past. This produces violation rather than healing. The principle is paired with [[trust-building-before-exposure]] for a reason. The diagnostic value of the avoidance does not give the coach permission to bulldoze it.

## Why This Principle Matters For The AI Coach

The AI coach is especially prone to two failure modes that this principle addresses:

1. **Following the client's stated agenda.** The client says "I want to work on X." The AI works on X. The avoidance is whatever the client did not bring up. The AI never gets there. The work circles around the not-presented thing forever.

2. **Treating reluctance as a "no" to be respected at face value.** When the client flinches, the AI backs off. The flinching is the signal that this is where the work is, but the AI reads it as the signal to look elsewhere. The work then keeps moving away from itself.

The principle gives the AI coach a different orientation. The agenda is information; the avoidance is also information. The reluctance is a sign of the location of the work, not a sign that the work is somewhere else. The AI's job is to honor the reluctance (not force) while also tracking what it points at (and bringing it back gently, in service of the trust the relationship has built).

## Joe's Words

"If we don't want to look at something as humans, it means that there's a shame that we have in our system. And every time we think about that thing, that shame arises and we don't want to think about it. That's exactly where our healing is. That's exactly the thing to pay attention to." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of People

The phrasing matters. "That's exactly where our healing is" — not "that's where the problem is." The thing the shame is attached to is not the problem. The shame is the problem, and the shame is in a particular location, and feeling-through the shame in that location is the healing. The principle reframes the avoided thing from "the thing that is wrong with me" to "the place where my wholeness lives."

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not say all healing is in shame work. Plenty of healing happens elsewhere — through joy, through love, through service, through embodied practice. The principle says: when there is something you don't want to look at, that is one location of healing. It is not the only location. It is a reliable one.

The principle also does not say healing requires looking at painful material directly forever. The looking is not the work; the felt-experience of the underneath is the work. Looking is the entrance. Once the entrance is taken, the work is to feel what the looking reveals (often anger, often the sadness underneath the anger, often the love that the shame was guarding). ## Tone

Joe delivers this principle matter-of-factly. The principle is not a discovery to be celebrated; it is a description of how shame and avoidance interlock. The delivery is more like naming a structural fact than offering a profound insight. The matter-of-factness is part of why the principle is useful — the client cannot disagree with a description of the mechanism the way they could disagree with a more interpretive frame.

The principle is also delivered with warmth toward the avoidance. The avoidance is not bad behavior. It is the system protecting itself from material it does not yet have the resources to feel. The principle is not "stop avoiding." The principle is "the avoidance is showing us where to go, and we will go there when the conditions are right."
