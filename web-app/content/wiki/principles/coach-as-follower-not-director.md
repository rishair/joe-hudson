# The Coach Follows the Person, Doesn't Do It To Them

## The Principle

What looks like masterful coaching from the outside is, from the inside, almost always following. The coach is not doing things to the client. The coach is being moved by what the client produces. The skill is in the receiving and the responding, not in the directing.

"It's not -- just to be clear, it's not me doing it to you, but yeah, it's, it's me following you. My experience of it is far more me following you than --" -- Joe Hudson, Ali Abdaal session

This is harder to do than to direct. Directing has a script. Following has only attention. The coach who follows is constantly listening for what just emerged and adjusting in real time. The coach who directs is executing a plan.

## Why This Matters

Three reasons it matters that coaching is following, not directing:

**1. The client knows where the work is. The coach does not.** The body of the person being coached has already organized around what needs to come up. The kernel is in their first sentence (see [[the-first-thing-the-client-says-is-the-kernel]]). The reactions, the somatic signals, the moments of pause -- all of these are pointers. The coach who follows reads the pointers. The coach who directs ignores them in favor of their own agenda.

**2. Directing produces resistance. Following produces movement.** The client who feels something is being done to them gets defensive. The defense slows the work. The client who feels followed gets curious. The curiosity accelerates the work. Same client, different posture, different result -- because of what the coach was doing.

**3. The transmission is the relationship, not the technique.** The client absorbs what the coach is being more than what the coach is doing. A coach who is following is in a particular state: present, responsive, undefended, willing to be surprised. The client borrows the state. A coach who is directing is in a different state: planned, ahead, slightly above. The client borrows that state too. The first state is the work. The second is performance.

## What This Principle Is Not

- It is not passivity. The coach is highly active in following. The activity is in the attention and the responsiveness, not in the agenda-setting.
- It is not absence of skill. Following is the skill. It is harder than directing, not easier. It requires the coach to know enough about the territory to follow accurately while not so much that they pre-decide where the conversation will go.
- It is not giving the client whatever they want. The coach who is following may take the conversation into uncomfortable territory because that is where the client's signal is pointing. Following the signal is not the same as following the client's surface preferences.
- It is not non-intervention. The coach intervenes constantly. Naming what is happening, reflecting back, asking questions, sitting in silence -- all interventions. The interventions are in service of what the client is producing, not in service of where the coach thinks the client should go.

## The Asymmetry With Directing

The two stances feel similar from the inside if you have not done both. A director can tell themselves they are "responding to the client" when they are actually executing a sequence they knew about before the session started. A follower can tell themselves they are "having no plan" when they are actually trained to listen for specific signals.

The diagnostic that distinguishes them: how often does the conversation go somewhere the coach did not predict? In directing, almost never. In following, almost always. The follower is surprised by what the session becomes. The director is confirmed by what the session becomes.

Another diagnostic: what does the coach do when the client says something the coach did not expect? The director routes the conversation back to the plan. The follower goes with what was said. The willingness to go with what was said is the follower's signature. It cannot be faked because the body shows whether the response is "yes, let's go there" or "wait, let me figure out how to use that."

## How to Train This

Following is not learned by trying to follow. It is learned by doing one's own work to the point where the need to direct dissolves. The director directs because something in the coach needs the session to go a particular way -- usually because the coach is trying to be helpful, look competent, produce a result, get a good outcome for their reputation, or avoid the discomfort of not knowing what comes next.

When the coach's own work has dissolved the need for any of those, the directing impulse weakens. Following becomes available because nothing is pulling against it. See [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]].

The intermediate move, before the own-work is complete: notice the moments where the directing impulse arises. Notice what feeling the directing is trying to manage. Often it is the coach's own discomfort with the client's discomfort. The coach wants to fix because the coach cannot sit with what is in the room. The fixing is the directing. The capacity to sit with what is in the room is the followership. See [[being-with-anothers-emotion-is-being-with-your-own-pain]].

A practical exercise: in any conversation, notice the moment you decide where the conversation should go next. Notice the impulse to steer. Then do not steer. Wait. See what arrives. The conversation will produce something on its own that you can follow. The training is in the not-steering.

## The Connection to Wonder

The follower stance is closely tied to [[wonder]] -- one of the four faces of [[view]]. Wonder is the active receptivity that makes following possible. Without wonder, the coach is in their head, producing options, planning. With wonder, the coach is open to what the client will reveal.

"My experience of it is far more me following you than --" The unspoken half of this sentence ends in some version of "doing something to you." The follower stance is not just an internal posture. It is the posture that lets coaching happen as a real-time dance rather than as a delivered service.

The coach in wonder is not pretending not to know things. The coach in wonder genuinely does not know what will come up next in this specific conversation with this specific person, because what comes up next is generated by what is happening between them in this moment. The conversation has not happened yet. The coach cannot know where it goes because where it goes is being made in real time.

## How This Looks in a Session

The session starts. The client says something. The coach receives it. Something in what the client said pulls the coach's attention. The coach asks about that. The client responds. Something in the response pulls the coach's attention. And so on. The chain is the session.

The coach is not building the chain. The coach is following each link as it appears. The chain looks intentional from the outside because each link follows naturally from the previous one. From the inside, the coach is simply doing what was made available by the previous link.

Sometimes the chain produces a result the coach did not predict. The result is the right result, because it was generated by the client's actual material, not by the coach's plan. The coach gets credit for skill, but the skill was in the following.

Sometimes the chain produces no clear result. The session ends without a clean conclusion. This is also fine. The follower does not insist on conclusions. The conclusion may arrive later, in the client's life, after the session has settled.

## What the Client Experiences

The client experiences something they often have not experienced before: being completely listened to. Not just heard. Followed. The coach's attention is moving in response to what the client is doing, with no parallel agenda, no countercurrent.

This is rare in modern life. Most people are not followed in conversation. They are countered, redirected, half-listened to while the other person prepares their reply. The follower coach gives them an experience the rest of their life does not give them.

The experience itself is part of the work. The client who has been fully followed learns what it feels like to be fully met. They become more able to give that experience to others. They also become more discriminating about who actually follows them and who does not. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] for the underlying mechanism.

## Tone

Quiet, gracious, slightly self-effacing. When Joe names this principle out loud, he is usually being asked how he does what he does. The honest answer is "I don't really do it -- I follow." The honest answer can sound humble or modest, but it is also accurate. The skill is genuinely in the receptivity.

The principle should not be delivered as a teaching about how to be humble. It is a teaching about how the work actually happens. The humility is a side effect, not the point.
