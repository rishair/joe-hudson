# Triggers Fire When External Words Rhyme With Your Internal Negative Self-Talk

## The Principle

A trigger does not fire because of what was said. It fires because what was said rhymes with what the voice in your head has been saying to you about yourself. The external speech is just the matchstick. The fuel is already inside, in the form of the self-talk you have been running for years. Without the inner fuel, the matchstick lights nothing.

"We get triggered when somebody says something to us that rhymes with our negative self-talk." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

This is the firing mechanism. It is the precondition without which a trigger cannot land.

## The Sky-Is-Purple Test

The cleanest demonstration of the principle is by negative example. If someone says something to you that you genuinely do not hold an internal version of, the words land flat. No charge. No defense. No reaction.

"If I said to you right now, you know what, sky is purple, and you don't agree with that, you're probably not going to get triggered by it. But if you think to yourself, wow, the sky is purple, but everybody else's is blue, so I shouldn't think it's purple. And then I say, you think the sky is purple, you're going to get triggered by it." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The structure: it is not the absurdity of the claim that prevents the trigger. It is the absence of an internal rhyme. If you secretly thought the sky was purple and felt bad about thinking that, the same words would suddenly carry a charge. The trigger is in the internal alignment, not in the external content.

## The Pills Example

The same mechanism shown in everyday form. The same comment lands differently in two different people based on what each person already says to themselves.

"If you say to somebody, boy, you're taking a lot of pills. And they have no problem with that, they're going to say to you, 'Oh yeah, yeah, I'm sick and etc., and I need to take these pills for X, Y, and Z.' But if you say to somebody, 'Boy, you take a lot of pills,' and the voice in their head is telling them like, 'What's wrong with you? You have to take all these pills,' man, they're going to get triggered. They're going to get defended. They're going to be upset with you." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The diagnostic implication is exact: the presence of the trigger is positive evidence that the person already runs the criticism on themselves. The absence of the trigger is positive evidence that they do not.

## The Three Forms the Trigger Can Take

Once the rhyme-condition is met, the trigger can take any of three primary forms. The form depends on the person and the moment, but the underlying firing mechanism is the same.

"When you get triggered, you could get triggered by getting angry. No, the sky isn't purple. Or you can get triggered by shame. Oh man, I keep on thinking that sky is purple. I got to stop doing that. Or you can get triggered by fear. You can go, oh my gosh, he knows that the sky is purple and I think the sky is purple. What is he going to think of me?" -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

- **Anger form:** push the external speaker away. Disagree, defend, attack.
- **Shame form:** collapse inward. Yes, that is true about me, and it is bad.
- **Fear form:** worry about what the external speaker now knows about you. Manage the exposure.

The form is informational. Same firing mechanism, different downstream defense. See [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]] for the parallel sorting between defense and shame-collapse.

## The Implication: Triggers Are Information About Your Inner Voice

Because triggers fire on the rhyme-condition, every trigger is a readout of what your inner voice has been saying to you. The trigger reverse-engineers the negative self-talk that produced it. The voice in your head may be hard to hear directly, especially if it has been background for years. But the trigger is unmissable. When the trigger fires, the voice that produced the fuel is, by inference, already established.

This makes the trigger a uniquely efficient instrument for mapping the inner voice. The voice can hide. The trigger cannot. Working backward from the trigger to the voice is one of the most reliable methods for finding the [[negative-self-talk-as-authority-without-leadership]] running underneath.

For each trigger:
- What was said?
- What would the inner voice have to be already saying for that comment to land?
- That voice is what is running.

## The Reverse Direction: Judging Others Is the Same Mechanism

The principle works symmetrically. The things you get triggered by in *other people* are the things you have already disowned in yourself. The mechanism is the same — you are noticing a part the inner voice has been criticizing in you — but the direction of the attack flips outward.

"It's always the case that the thing that triggers you about somebody else is something in yourself that you don't like, right? If you're really happy to be able to share all the cool things you've done, and then somebody else shares the good things they've done, there's no problem. You're like, 'Oh, cool, thanks for sharing that. I love to hear those stories.' But if you're one of those people who thinks that's bragging and that you're not allowed to brag and then they do it, then you're gonna say, 'Oh, that person's a bragger.' You're gonna judge them, they're gonna be bad." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The recursion shows up in its cleanest form when the trait you judge is itself judgmentalness:

"Let's say I'm triggered by you because you're so judgmental. Now, first of all, obviously I'm judgmental. I just got triggered by you for being judgmental. So I'm judging your judgment. So clearly I'm judgmental." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The recursion is the diagnostic. There is no version of "triggered by their judgmentalness" that is not itself an enactment of judgmentalness in the triggered person. The very response demonstrates the trait. See [[pointers-to-unseen-parts]] for the diagnostic use of this pattern in identifying disowned parts.

## The Universality Move

A crucial extension Joe makes after establishing the mechanism: the parts of yourself that you are judging are parts that *everyone* has. You are not the only person who is sometimes a bragger, sometimes a know-it-all, sometimes judgmental, sometimes self-promoting. The judgment is operating on a trait that is genuinely human and genuinely universal.

"The crazy thing is whatever that thing is, whether it's wanting to be seen or wanting to share all the cool things you've done, whether it's being judgmental, whether it's being a braggart, whether it's being arrogant, whether it's being falsely humble, we all do it. Every single one of us does it. And so you're actually judging yourself for something that other people and most likely everybody has experienced." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The universalization is what makes the trait re-includable. See [[loving-all-parts]] for the structural form of this move. The trigger is showing you a part. The universalization is showing you that the part is a part of being human, not a personal defect. Both readings are needed for the trigger to convert from suffering into freedom.

## Relationship to the Defense Diagnostic

This principle is the firing-mechanism complement to [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]]. The two principles describe the same phenomenon from different angles:

- **Triggers reveal your own beliefs (the defense diagnostic):** if you defend against an accusation, the defense itself shows you hold the story. This reads the trigger from the *response* side.
- **Triggers fire when words rhyme with negative self-talk (this principle):** the trigger only fires in the first place if the inner voice has been running the same content. This reads the trigger from the *firing-condition* side.

The two angles compose. The firing-condition explains why the trigger arose. The defense diagnostic explains what to look for in your own response to read what belief is held. Together they form the full readout: the inner voice produces the fuel, the external comment lights it, the defense (or shame, or fear) is the burn, and the work is to map all four pieces back to the self-talk that started the cycle.

## Why This Reframes the Trigger as Useful

Without the principle, a trigger is suffering with no purpose. With the principle, every trigger is a precision pointer at one specific piece of the negative self-talk that is running you. The piece can be unhooked once seen. The self-talk can be welcomed, examined, softened. The same comment, after the work, will not trigger.

This is the trigger-as-doorway frame in its mechanistic form. The doorway is real precisely because the firing-condition (the inner self-talk) is something you can work on. If the trigger were caused by the external content, there would be no work available except managing the external. Because the trigger is caused by the rhyme, the work is on the rhyme — and the rhyme is in you.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Hearing a comment from a stranger that lands with surprising force
- Recognizing that the same comment from a different person on a different day did nothing
- Catching yourself defending against an observation that you secretly believe
- Noticing that your judgment of someone else's trait is a near-mirror of how you talk to yourself
- The realization, after a fight, that the partner's words were the matchstick but the fuel was already there
