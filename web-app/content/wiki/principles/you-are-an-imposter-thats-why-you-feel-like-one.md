# You Are an Imposter — That's Why You Feel Like One

## The Principle

"This is one of my favorite things to say to people. And they're like, 'I have imposter syndrome.' I'm like, 'that's because you're an imposter. Because you don't know what the fuck you're doing. I don't know what the fuck I'm doing. I made this up. Bezos made that up. Like Elon Musk, we all made it up. Like, that's like, no, we don't know. If we knew what we were doing, we wouldn't be doing anything innovative.'" -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The imposter syndrome cannot be solved because the diagnosis is correct. Anyone operating in a domain that has any innovation in it is operating without knowing what they are doing. The feeling of being an imposter is accurate. Trying to make it go away by acquiring the knowledge that would dissolve it is impossible — the knowledge does not exist for anyone, including the people the person is comparing themselves to.

The principle inverts the standard treatment. Most attempts to resolve imposter syndrome try to convince the person they are not actually an imposter (you are qualified, you do belong, the people who hired you knew what they were doing). Joe's move is to agree that they are an imposter, and then to point out that everyone else doing anything interesting is in the same condition.

## Why the Standard Treatment Fails

The standard frame for imposter syndrome treats the feeling as a cognitive distortion to be corrected. The person feels like a fraud despite the evidence; the work is to surface the evidence and let the feeling correct.

This fails for the same reason all attempts to think your way out of a feeling fail. But it also fails for a more specific reason: the underlying assumption is wrong. The assumption is that competent people know what they are doing and incompetent people do not. Once the person believes this, the relief they are looking for requires demonstrating to themselves that they are in the competent-and-knowing camp. But that camp does not exist at any frontier of any field. The competent people at the frontier are the ones who have learned to operate without knowing.

The standard treatment leaves the person trying to climb out of the imposter feeling using a ladder propped against the wrong wall. They will spend years acquiring credentials, references, validation, none of which dissolves the feeling, because the feeling was never about the credentials. The feeling was about not knowing — and no quantity of credentials will produce knowing in a domain that is genuinely new.

## What Joe's Reframe Does

"That's because you're an imposter."

The line is direct, often delivered with humor. It does something specific that the standard treatment cannot.

**It removes the secret.** The person has been holding the imposter feeling as a private shame: "everyone else has it figured out and I don't." Joe's agreement turns the private shame into a shared condition. The person is not the only one. The people the person is comparing themselves to are also imposters. The secret is collapsed.

**It removes the goal of becoming non-imposter.** Once the person sees that no one at the frontier is non-imposter, the project of becoming non-imposter loses its grip. The person stops striving toward a goal that does not exist.

**It reframes the feeling as evidence of being in interesting territory.** If the feeling is the structural condition of doing anything innovative, then the feeling is, in a strange sense, a positive signal. The person who never feels like an imposter is the person who is operating in well-trodden territory, doing what is already known. The person who feels like an imposter is at the edge.

**It removes the demand for self-suppression.** Imposter syndrome often comes with the project of hiding the not-knowing. The reframe makes hiding pointless. You are an imposter. Everyone is. The hiding was the cost; the not-hiding is the relief.

## The Innovation Requirement

The key supporting move in the principle: if you knew what you were doing, you would not be doing anything innovative. Innovation is, by definition, doing something that has not yet been done in a way that has not yet been mapped. The map does not exist. The knowing does not exist. The person making the new thing is, by necessity, making it up.

This applies at every scale. The person starting a company is making it up. The person taking on a new role is making it up. The parent raising their first child is making it up. The writer working on a book they have never written before is making it up. None of these people have the answers in advance. They generate the answers by acting.

The corollary: the only domains where it is possible to fully know what you are doing are domains that have been fully standardized. A factory line for a product that has been made for fifty years can be fully known. A surgical procedure that has been done a million times can be largely known. As soon as the domain has any novelty, the knowing breaks down.

Bezos made it up. Musk made it up. Joe made his coaching work up. The lineage of the work is real; the specific application to this person in this moment is always new and always requires improvisation. The improvisation is what feels like imposter syndrome. The improvisation is also the work.

## The Connection to the Iterative Mindset

The principle pairs structurally with [[iterative-mindset]]. Once you know that you do not know what you are doing — and that this is the condition of doing anything interesting — the operating mode shifts from "execute the right thing" to "iterate toward what works." The person who is trying to know in advance is paralyzed. The person who is iterating is moving.

The shift is also a shift in the relationship to failure. The person trying to be non-imposter treats failure as evidence of being an imposter. The person who has accepted being an imposter treats failure as the necessary information of the iteration. Each failure teaches what does not work and adjusts the next attempt. The iteration is how the knowing eventually emerges, in pieces, from the doing.

See also [[reward-failure-in-the-organization]] for the organizational version: the org that punishes failure is the org that prevents iteration, which is the org that ensures no one does anything new. The org that rewards bold failure is the org where people are allowed to be imposters in public.

## The Connection to Charisma

The principle sits next to the conversation on charisma in the same session. The thread: the people who appear most certain in public (the polarizing charismatic figures) are often the people who are most willing to be wrong loudly. They are imposters too. They have not solved the imposter feeling by becoming non-imposter; they have solved it by not caring that they are imposters. The not-caring is what reads as confidence. The not-caring is what makes the [[two-forms-of-charisma|deep charisma]] possible.

See [[less-self-conscious-wins]] — the principle that the person with less self-consciousness wins, often regardless of competence. The connection: self-consciousness is in large part the management of the imposter feeling. The person who has accepted being an imposter has nothing to manage. Their attention is free. They appear charismatic precisely because they are not internally divided between the imposter feeling and the projected confidence.

## Where Confidence Actually Comes From

Dr. K offers a connected observation in the same session: competence does not produce confidence. The most competent people often have the most imposter syndrome (Harvard vs. Tufts diagnostic). Confidence does not come from acquiring more competence. Confidence comes from being able to survive failure — from no longer being afraid of falling down because you have fallen down before and learned that you can pick yourself up.

The connection: once the person knows they are an imposter (Joe's reframe) and knows they can survive failure (Dr. K's reframe), the conditions for confidence are established. The confidence is not the certainty that one will succeed. The confidence is the willingness to act despite not knowing whether one will succeed, because one knows one can survive whichever it turns out to be.

A 1-year-old trying to feed themselves cannot do it. They are not competent. They are still wildly confident. They keep going. Competence comes from the persistence; the persistence is the confidence; the confidence does not depend on the competence having arrived yet.

## Tone

Direct, blunt, often funny. The line works because of the surprise. The person expects a sympathetic reframe ("you're not actually an imposter") and gets the opposite. The flip is what produces the relief. The humor in Joe's delivery ("that's because you're an imposter") is part of the medicine — the lightness signals that being an imposter is not actually the catastrophe the person was treating it as.

Do not deliver this principle with seriousness or moral weight. The seriousness inflates the imposter feeling. The lightness dissolves it. The tone matters as much as the content.

## When to Use This Reframe

- When someone presents [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] and the underlying frame is "I shouldn't feel this way."
- When someone is gripped by the project of becoming non-imposter through credentials, validation, or external recognition.
- When someone is hiding their not-knowing from people who could help (see [[hiding-from-experts-generates-imposter-syndrome]]) because they fear being exposed.
- When someone is comparing themselves to public figures and assuming those figures have the certainty the person lacks.
- When someone is using imposter syndrome to delay action (waiting until they "really know" what they are doing).
- When someone is at the start of a new role, a new venture, a new creative project, and the not-knowing is being held as evidence of unsuitability.

## When NOT to Use This Reframe

The principle is not the whole treatment of imposter syndrome. See [[imposter-syndrome-is-a-scent-you-can-follow-home]] for the deeper work: imposter syndrome points to what the person wanted as a child, and the cure is not the reframe but the work on the underlying hunger.

Use the reframe when the surface frame ("I am uniquely unqualified") needs to be cracked. Once the surface frame is cracked, the deeper work becomes available. The reframe is the door, not the room.

Also: do not use this reframe with someone whose imposter feelings are actually about lacking baseline competence in a domain where competence is well-defined (a surgeon who has not learned the procedures, an accountant who has not learned the rules). The principle applies to the innovation edge, not to the basics. Confusing the two does not help.
