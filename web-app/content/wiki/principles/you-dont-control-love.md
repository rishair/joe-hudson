# You Don't Get to Control Love

## The Principle

The person who is approaching love with the question "how do I let it in without losing control?" is asking the wrong question. The control is over the contact, not over the love. The amount of contact a person makes with love is theirs to titrate. What love does once it is being contacted is not within the contract.

"You don't get to control love. That's not how it works. You just get to be heartbroken from time to time, increasing your capacity to love." — Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

The principle is direct and not a comfort. Love is a force the person enters into a relationship with, not a quality they manage from outside. The person retains agency over their participation. The person does not have agency over the consequences of participation.

This is the structural fact that makes love different from most felt experiences a person works with. Other emotions have a quality of moving through. Love has a quality of changing the system it moves through. The system cannot prepare for this in advance. The system can only choose to be willing for it.

## What Love Does That Can't Be Controlled

The teachings that bracket this principle in the source session name several specific things love does that the person cannot prevent once they are in contact with it:

- **Love rips away identity.** "It will. That's what love does. It rips away our identity." The defended self softens. The structure that has been managing the person's life loses its rigidity. This is not gentle. It is what is happening.

- **Love is really big.** "Yeah, love is really fucking big." When the person stops managing the size of love, the actual size becomes felt. Often it is much larger than the managed version suggested. The bigness is not exaggeration; it is description.

- **Love produces heartbreak.** The heart that opens to love is the heart that can break when love meets what cannot receive it, when love is lost, when love finds what is already broken in the beloved. The heartbreak is not optional. It is what the open heart does in contact with reality. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]].

- **The heartbreak expands capacity.** Each heartbreak increases the capacity to love. The system that has been broken by love is more capable of love. This is not a consolation; it is a mechanism. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]].

The person who wants to be in love but not have any of these things happen is asking for something that is not love. They are asking for a managed simulacrum of love that does not do what love does. The simulacrum is what most defended relationships consist of. It does not produce the felt experience the person was actually looking for.

## What Can Be Controlled

The principle is not "you have no agency." It is precise about what the agency is over.

The contact is in the person's control. They can spend a little time with the longing today and more tomorrow. They can pull back when the contact becomes too much. They can taper. They can be in intimate relationship with the love without giving in. See [[be-intimate-with-the-feeling]] and [[intimate-with-vs-giving-in-to-feeling]].

"You can do that if you want. You can just like spend a little time with the longing. Tomorrow you can spend a little more time with the longing. The next day you can taper if you'd like." — Joe Hudson, I Am Scared Of The Unknown

The person decides how much contact to make. The person decides when to make it. The person decides what to do with the contact (sit with it, let it move them to action, allow the heartbreak, return to it later).

What the person does not decide is whether the contact produces dissolution, heartbreak, identity-loosening, expansion. Those are downstream of the contact. Once the contact is made, the downstream happens.

## Why This Is the Trade

A natural objection: if love cannot be controlled, why would anyone choose to be in contact with it?

The principle's second half answers this: "You just get to be heartbroken from time to time, increasing your capacity to love." The reward for the un-controllability is increased capacity for the very thing being contacted. The system that has been through enough cycles of heartbreak is more capable of love, which is more capable of expansion, which is more capable of all the qualities the person was originally seeking when they reached for love.

The control is what was preventing the access. The system that is trying to control love is the system that doesn't have love. The system that gives up the control gets the love. The expansion of capacity is what was wanted; the willingness to be uncontrollable is the entry fee.

This is why people who have been through many cycles of allowing love describe themselves as more, not less, equipped to be in love. They have lost the desire to control it. The cost of losing the desire was getting the thing the desire was trying to produce. The trade is not symmetrical in the way the defended self assumes. The defended self imagines that giving up control means losing love. The actual mechanism is that holding control means losing love. Releasing control is what makes love available.

## The Identity Stakes Restated

The deeper layer beneath "you don't control love" is the identity question. The person who controls love does so by means of the managing self. The managing self is what does the controlling. When love is allowed to do what love does, the managing self loses its job. The person is left without the structure that has been running the show.

This is why the un-controllability of love is felt as a threat to existence. It is not just that love will do what it will do. It is that the person who has been doing the controlling will no longer be needed in the same way. The dissolution of the controller is what the controller resists. It is structurally impossible for the controller to accept the un-controllability of love, because accepting it would dissolve the controller.

The work, then, is not to talk the controller into accepting the un-controllability. The work is to allow contact with love directly, which dissolves the controller through its own action rather than through the controller's consent. The controller will continue to protest. The contact happens anyway. After enough contact, the protest stops mattering because what survives the dissolution is something the person can trust.

See [[losing-self-in-love-two-forms]] for the distinction between the dissolution that love produces (Form A, healthy) and the self-abandonment a person can mistake for it (Form B, unhealthy). The principle here addresses Form A specifically — love's identity-dissolving function as it actually operates rather than as the person fears it.

## The Coaching Application

When a person is in close contact with love (longing, intimate connection, contact with a beloved, contact with self-as-loved) and asks how to control how much they feel, the move is to acknowledge the calibration that is possible and then name what is not within it.

The acknowledgment matters. The person is not wrong to want some agency. The calibration over the contact is a legitimate request. Refusing the calibration would be asking them to give in (see [[intimate-with-vs-giving-in-to-feeling]]) rather than to be intimate.

The naming also matters. The person needs to know, in advance of the work, what is and is not within their control. The naming converts surprise into expectation. The heartbreak that comes later was named in the beginning. The expansion that follows the heartbreak was named in the beginning. The identity-loosening was named in the beginning. The person consented to the actual relationship, not to a managed version of it.

The principle stated cleanly in the coaching moment: you have agency over the contact. You don't have agency over what love does inside the contact. Love is going to do what love does. The trade is that what love does is the thing you wanted love for.

## What This Principle Is Not

This principle is not the romantic claim that love makes people helpless. It is structural: love produces effects that cannot be prevented while one is in contact with love. The agency lies elsewhere (in whether and how much contact is made), not in the effects.

It is also not a claim that all love-experiences require the same depth of dissolution. The contact can be calibrated. A small contact produces a small dissolution. A larger contact produces a larger one. The proportionality is preserved. The person who wants a smaller experience of love can have one. They cannot have a managed experience of love that does not dissolve anything.

It is also not the claim that love is the only force that operates this way. Grief operates similarly. Awe operates similarly. The principle is named in the love-domain because love is the most common place a person tries to apply control and discovers the application doesn't work.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person beginning to allow love after a long avoidance and discovering that the control they assumed they would retain has been revoked
- A person in a deepening relationship who realizes the relationship is changing them in ways they did not sign up for
- A person whose heart breaks and who is shocked that they cannot prevent the next heartbreak
- A person who is doing emotional work and finds that the work is loosening identity structures they wanted to keep
- A person who asks for the "how do I let love in without losing myself" technique and is told there isn't one in the form they're asking for
- A person who has been managing love through proxies (relationships organized around safety, partnership organized around prediction) and is beginning to feel the cost

## Tone

Direct and warm. The principle is delivered without softening. "You don't get to control love. That's not how it works." The flatness of the statement is the point. The person needs to hear that the control they were hoping for is not on offer.

The follow-up — "you just get to be heartbroken from time to time, increasing your capacity to love" — is delivered with the same flatness. There is no consolation in the framing. The compensation (capacity expansion) is named factually, not promotionally.

The "fucking" in "love is really fucking big" is intentional. It signals that the speaker is not talking about love-as-concept. The expletive locates the speaker in the felt reality of love's size. The person being coached hears that the speaker has been there and is reporting from inside.
