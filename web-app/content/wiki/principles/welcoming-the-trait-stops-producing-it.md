# Welcoming the Trait You're Triggered By Stops Producing It

## The Principle

The trait you cannot admit to in yourself is precisely the trait that keeps showing up in your behavior. The fighting-against-the-trait is what holds the trait in place. The instant you can welcome the trait — admit you have it, accept it without shame, see it without recoiling — the behavior that expresses the trait quietly recedes. Not because you tried to stop it. Because you stopped needing to defend against it.

This is the operational payoff that makes trigger work worth doing. The trigger surfaces a trait you have been disowning. Owning it dissolves the behavior the disowning was producing.

"What was weird was I noticed that I wasn't doing the things that made me be a dick as much." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

This is the corollary of [[shame-drives-behavior]] applied to the trigger-work specifically. Shame holds behavior in place; admission releases it. The release happens automatically — no separate behavior-change project is required.

## The Bodega Bay Origin

The principle came clean in Joe's own life from the friend-who-called-him-a-dick exchange. The friend named the trait. Joe initially defended. Eventually Joe admitted he could see how he was a dick in specific ways. Then the surprising thing: the behaviors associated with being a dick started to fall away.

"And so I owned it. I was like, 'Yeah, okay, I can totally see it.' And I guarantee you that all of us can see some way in which we are a dick, right? We're not nice. And so I looked at those places. I was like, 'Yeah, you know what? That's true.' I can totally see that. I can see how you would say that. Absolutely. And then what was weird was I noticed that I wasn't doing the things that made me be a dick as much." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The sequence is exact: defend, then admit, then notice the behavior reducing. Joe did not try to be less of a dick. He admitted that he was one in specific ways. The behavior reduced as a side effect. See [[friend-who-calls-you-a-dick]] for the full story and the receiver-side dynamics of the openhearted naming that made the admission available.

## The Mom Example: Why Fighting It Produces It

The contrast case is sharper. Joe's mother had spent years getting defensive every time he, as a kid, said she was a bad mom. The defense was the form her trigger took. Underneath the defense was the self-talk she would not let herself admit: she thought she was a bad mom.

The mechanism then ran exactly as the principle predicts. The more she could not admit it to herself, the more energy went into the defense, the more the friction in her system showed up as the behaviors she felt ashamed of, the more it confirmed the self-talk. The loop sustained itself.

"And I just sat there, I looked at her, and it just broke my heart because I realized for the first time, that whole thing, what it was about was that she was thinking she was a bad mom. All those triggers, all that stuff was that she was criticizing herself. And the more she thought it, the more likely she was to be a bad mom. The more she could actually just own the fact that she wasn't a perfect mother and that nobody's a perfect mother and that she wanted to be a good mother. Like, so many of the behaviors that she felt ashamed about would have just gone away." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The same mechanism in reverse: admission would have released the friction, and the behaviors held in place by the friction would have fallen away. The defense was the engine of the very behavior the defense was trying to deny.

## The Friction Mechanism

The bridge concept is friction. The trait is there in some form (most traits are universal — see [[loving-all-parts]] on universalization). The disowning sets up a constant inner friction between "this is in me" and "this cannot be in me." That friction is itself a source of behavior. The system reacts to its own internal pressure. The reaction shows up as more of the behavior the disowning was trying to suppress.

"You're actually judging yourself for something that other people and most likely everybody has experienced, and you're not allowing yourself to be that. And by not allowing yourself to be it, you're creating this friction in your system that often creates more of it." -- Joe Hudson, What To Do When You Get Triggered

The friction is doing the work. The disowning is not preventing the behavior; it is producing it. When the friction releases — when the trait is welcomed as a normal-human-trait you have along with everyone else — the behavior loses its inner driver. It does not need to keep showing up to make itself heard.

This connects to [[voice-creates-what-it-fears]] and [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] as instances of the same broader algorithm: pushing-against a content tends to produce it. The trigger-version is just one specific case.

## Why "Just Stop Doing It" Fails

The standard advice for any unwanted behavior is to try harder to stop it. The principle explains why that consistently fails. The trying-harder is more friction. The trying-harder reinforces the disowning. The disowning is the producer of the behavior. So the trying-harder produces more of the behavior. The harder you try, the worse the pattern gets.

The person who cannot stand being seen as judgmental tries harder not to judge. The trying-harder requires constant monitoring of their own judgments. The monitoring is itself a form of judgmentalness. They become more judgmental in the trying-not-to-be way than they were before.

The person who cannot stand being seen as needy tries harder not to be needy. The suppression of need produces the eventual eruption of disowned need. The need shows up in deformed ways — passive aggression, manipulation, sudden collapses — because it cannot come through cleanly.

The release of the friction is what the trying-harder is structurally preventing. The principle reverses the order. Welcome first. The behavior changes as a side effect of the welcoming. See [[love-is-the-best-change-agent]] for the broader mechanism.

## What Welcoming Looks Like in Practice

Welcoming is not the same as celebrating or performing acceptance. It is the simple internal admission: "Yes, I do that sometimes. I see how that is true about me. I am a person who has that in them, like other people do."

Concrete operationalizations:

- **Naming the specific instances.** Not "I am a dick in general." But "Last Tuesday I said that thing to my partner that was actually pretty mean. I can see how that was a dick move." The specificity is what makes the admission a real admission rather than an abstract concession.
- **Universalizing the trait.** Not "I am uniquely a dick." But "Everyone is a dick sometimes. This is one of the human traits, and I have it." See [[loving-all-parts]] for the universalization move.
- **Refusing both the defense and the collapse.** Not "no I'm not" (defense), not "oh my god I'm so broken" (shame collapse), but "yeah, I do that." The neutral admission is the middle path.
- **Letting the affect be light.** Joe's tone in admitting it is amused, not heavy. The lightness is the marker that the welcoming is actually happening. Heavy admission is usually still shame in another costume.

## What This Is Not

This is not permission to be cruel and call it ownership. "Yes, I'm a dick, deal with it" is not welcoming — it is the trait now defended under the cover of admission. The diagnostic: did the behavior reduce, or is it being maintained while the verbal label is now claimed? Real welcoming reduces the behavior. Defensive welcoming maintains the behavior with new linguistic packaging.

This is not a one-shot conversion. The principle works dimension by dimension. Welcoming one trait that was disowned reduces the behavior associated with that trait. Other disowned traits continue to produce their behaviors until they too are welcomed. The work is iterative.

This is not loving-to-transform. The welcoming is not done in order to change the behavior — that would be another form of fighting the trait, just with a friendlier mask. The welcoming is done because the trait is human and the friction of disowning is harmful. The behavior change is the bonus, not the goal. See [[loving-to-transform]] for the failure mode where this distinction collapses.

## The Connection to the Trigger Work

This principle is what makes [[trigger]] work productive at the behavior-change level rather than only at the awareness level. Without this principle, trigger work surfaces self-knowledge but does not necessarily change behavior. With this principle, the surfaced self-knowledge converts to behavior change automatically — provided the surfacing is followed by genuine welcoming rather than continued defense.

The full chain:

1. The trigger fires because external words rhyme with internal self-talk. See [[triggers-fire-when-words-rhyme-with-negative-self-talk]].
2. The defense or the shame is the diagnostic — you also hold the story. See [[triggers-reveal-your-own-beliefs]].
3. The disowning of the trait the story names is creating friction in your system.
4. The friction is producing more of the trait's behaviors.
5. Welcoming the trait releases the friction.
6. The behaviors recede on their own.

Each step is necessary. Skipping the welcoming step (going directly from awareness to behavior-management) re-enacts the friction-loop and produces the very thing the awareness was supposed to be ending.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the affect of having noticed something useful. The "what was weird" framing in the Bodega Bay story carries the discovery quality. The mom example shifts to "it just broke my heart" — the heartbreak is for the loop she was caught in, not at her. Both deliveries position the principle as good news rather than as moral instruction. The trait you are fighting can stop running you. The route is admission, not effort.
