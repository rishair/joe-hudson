# Dehumanization Begins With "They Are Wrong"

## The Principle

The structural prerequisite for hating, oppressing, or harming another human being is the belief that they are wrong. Atrocity does not begin with violence. It begins with a thought.

"It's like they made the others, the people that they're oppressing — they made them into others. They're not humans. They're not people. The beginning of that is they're wrong. The first step of that is they're wrong. You have to think that they're wrong to hate them." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The principle is structural, not psychological. It applies to large-scale atrocity (Hitler, Pol Pot) and to small-scale daily contempt (political tribalism, family estrangement, in-group sorting). The structure is the same. The scale differs.

## The Sequence

The progression from "they are wrong" to dehumanization typically runs:

1. **They are wrong** (about something specific).
2. **They are wrong** (in general; the wrongness becomes characteristic).
3. **They are bad** (the wrongness fuses with their identity).
4. **They are not really people** (the bad ones are not full human beings).
5. **It is acceptable to harm them** (because the harm is not happening to a full person).

Steps 4 and 5 are where the violence becomes possible. Steps 1-3 are where it is enabled. Step 1 is where it starts.

This is why the principle matters as practice rather than just as analysis. The work happens at step 1. The dehumanization that follows can be unwound at the source by examining the rightness/wrongness frame itself.

## Why This Matters for Self-Inquiry

The principle is one of the reasons [[questioning-assumptions]] is recommended at the level of right and wrong specifically. The thought-layer right-and-wrong is the source code of in-group/out-group sorting. As long as the practitioner is operating from a thought-layer morality ("I am right, they are wrong"), they retain the structural capacity for dehumanization, no matter how well-intentioned they are.

When the right-and-wrong frame transparentizes (see [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]]), the source code stops compiling. There is no longer a structural way to make the other side fully wrong, and therefore no structural way to dehumanize them.

This is what Joe means when he says the practice produces a person who, structurally, cannot do the things that lead to atrocity. Not because they decided not to. Because the prerequisite thought is no longer believable.

## The Political Application

The principle is named explicitly in the context of contemporary political polarization.

"You can see this in our political divide in our country right now. Right. Everyone, you know, it's the first step of dehumanization." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

The political case is instructive precisely because it is everyday and recognizable. The same person who would be horrified to be told they have anything in common with Hitler will, on a daily basis, do step 1 about a politician, a voter on the other side, a family member with different views. The structure is the same.

This is not "both sides" rhetoric. The principle does not require holding that the harm being objected to is okay. The whole point of the principle is that you can object to the harm without entering the wrongness-frame that legitimates more harm. See the "What This Does Not Mean" section below.

## What This Does Not Mean

**Not "anything goes."** The principle does not say that what is happening is okay. Joe is explicit: "Just to be really clear, that doesn't mean what is happening is totally not okay. And I'm even to the point of, I'm going to fight to stop that. Great. Not saying not to do that."

**Not pacifism.** The principle is compatible with action against harm, including fighting. What it constrains is the inner posture from which the fighting is done. You can act against an action while holding the actor as human.

"I'm just saying you can do it with an open heart and you can do it without making the other an animal or taking steps to make them less than you." -- Joe Hudson, Question the Assumption

**Not moral relativism.** The principle is not "no one is ever wrong about anything." Plenty of actions cause harm. The principle is about the structural cost of using "wrong" as a frame for the *person* who committed the action. The action can be opposed. The person cannot be made into not-a-person without producing the structural conditions for atrocity.

**Not naive.** The principle is not a recommendation to forgive everything immediately. Forgiveness is its own slow process (see [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]]). The principle is about the long-term structural orientation, not about the immediate emotional response.

## The Felt Test

The principle has a body-level test. Holding someone as "wrong" produces a specific felt state: a closing, a hardening, a small armor. The wrongness-frame and the closed-heart-state are the same event in two registers.

When the practitioner can feel this — feel that judging another as wrong produces the felt closure of their own heart — the principle stops being abstract. The cost of dehumanization becomes immediate. You are not asked to abstain from it for moral reasons. You are unable to do it because the cost is felt at the moment of doing it.

This is the same mechanism described in [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]]. The morality moves to the body. The body cannot maintain the wrongness-frame because the body's own openness is the cost.

## How This Is Worked With

The recognition that "they are wrong" is the first step of dehumanization is not enough to dissolve the pattern. The pattern is held in the body and in the nervous system, often inherited or installed by family of origin. The work is gradual.

Some moves:

- Notice the constriction the moment "they are wrong" lands as a thought. Use the constriction as the doorway, not the thought.
- Ask: what truth is in their perspective that I am refusing to see? Not "are they right?" (which the system rejects), but "what truth is in their perspective?" (which the system can hold).
- Use [[questioning-assumptions]] on the specific wrongness-claim. What are the assumptions inside "they are wrong"? What contexts make the claim false?
- Use [[letting-judgment-in]] when the wrongness is being directed at the self by another. The wrongness frame is hardest to dissolve when one is on the receiving end of it; the practice is the same.
- Practice the felt distinction between objecting to an action and dehumanizing the actor. Hold the action as wrong in the body-sense; do not let the wrongness contaminate the person.

## What Makes This Principle Hard

The principle is structurally simple but practically hard, because the wrongness-frame is one of the most reinforced and rewarded patterns in social life. Communities form around shared wrongness-judgments. In-group bonding is heavily mediated by who-we-think-is-wrong. Releasing the frame can be felt as betraying the in-group.

This is why the principle is often resisted even by people who would intellectually agree with it. The thought "they are wrong" delivers identity, belonging, and the small pleasure of moral superiority. Giving it up requires giving up those goods.

The practice does not ask the practitioner to forgo all in-group belonging. It asks for the slow dissolution of the wrongness-mediated form of it. What replaces it, eventually, is belonging based on shared care rather than shared contempt — different in texture, less addictive, more sustainable.

## Connection to Comparative Mind

The wrongness-frame is one specific form of [[comparative-mind]]. "I am right and they are wrong" is the better-than position. It produces the same misery as all comparative-mind positions, and it has the same structural function. Releasing the wrongness-frame is one of the specific applications of dissolving comparative mind.

## Tone

Joe is grave when he names this principle. The examples (Hitler, Pol Pot) are not chosen for shock. They are chosen because the principle holds at all scales and the largest scales make it undeniable. The tone is sober, not preachy. He is careful, repeatedly, to clarify what the principle does and does not mean — because the misreadings are easy and politically dangerous.
