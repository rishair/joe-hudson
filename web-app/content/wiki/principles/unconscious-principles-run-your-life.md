# You Are Already Living By Principles -- The Question Is Whether They Are Conscious

## The Principle

You are already operating from principles. Every consistent pattern in how you make decisions IS a principle, whether you have named it or not. The work is not to invent principles. The work is to make the unconscious principles conscious so they can be examined and chosen.

"In a way, most of us are operating on a set of principles anyways, and might be to avoid fear. That might be the principle, or the principle might be to make as much money as possible in the shortest period of time. It might be to just win at any cost. And it might be to put somebody's discomfort ahead of profit... And so whatever that is, that principle is how you're making decisions." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The key insight: if you consistently make decisions based on something, that something IS a principle, even if you have never said it out loud. It is operating effectively as a principle whether you chose it or not.

## How Unconscious Principles Form

The unconscious principles are usually inherited or installed by fear. Joe gives an autobiographical example of his own principles in his twenties before any therapy:

"If I look at the principles I was living by, those are the principles I was living by. And, and I think there was also taking care of others even if it wasn't... I know that's true -- taking care of others even if it wasn't good for me. Those are the principles, out of a sense of obligation, that obligation made me good and moral. And so those were what I was living by, but I wasn't conscious of it at all." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

His unconscious principles at that age:
- Be scared of love
- Try to get love but be scared of it
- Find any way outside of the pain from self-abuse
- Take care of others even when it is not good for me
- Obligation makes me good and moral

He was running on these. They were producing decisions. They had operational consequences. He just had not articulated them.

## The Diagnostic: What Do Your Decisions Reveal?

You cannot ask yourself "what are my principles?" and get an accurate answer. The conscious mind will report aspirational principles. The actual operating principles can only be inferred from the pattern of decisions.

Brett offered this version of the diagnostic:

"If you consistently make decisions based on something that you're not aware of, it is effectively a principle. So you might be making decisions as a business that profit is more important than our people. And you may never have explicitly said that, but if those are the decisions you tend to make because there's the fear of not making enough profit, then you're going to go wherever those principles would lead you, but you're not aware of it, so you can't iterate on the principle." -- Brett Kistler, Wrestle With Your Principles

Joe's response: "That is right."

The investigative question becomes: when forced to choose between A and B in your life, which do you consistently pick? That pattern is the principle you are actually living by. The aspirational principle is the one you wish you were living by. The gap between them is the work.

## Why Unconscious Principles Are Worse Than No Principles

Unconscious principles cannot be iterated on. They cannot be questioned. They produce their consequences without ever being subject to the wrestling that would refine them.

A person who is consciously living by "win at any cost" has a chance to notice when the principle is producing damage and revise it. A person who is unconsciously living by "win at any cost" only notices the damage and looks for external explanations -- the team is wrong, the market is unfair, the family does not understand.

The unconscious principle protects itself from revision by staying invisible.

## The Process of Making Principles Conscious

Articulating principles is itself the intervention.

"One of the things about a principles exercise in the way that you live is that it brings those things to light and it allows you to iterate and modify them." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The exercise has three movements:

1. **Notice what is already operating.** What are the consistent patterns of choice in your life? What gets sacrificed when you are under pressure? What gets protected?
2. **Name it without judgment.** "I am operating from a principle of fear avoidance" is more useful than "I should not be operating from fear." Naming makes it available for revision; judgment makes it hide again.
3. **Compare to the principles you would consciously choose.** The gap between the unconscious operating principle and the consciously-chosen principle is the territory where wrestling happens.

The wrestling is not just refinement of stated principles. It is also continuous excavation of the principles that are still operating below awareness.

## The Common Default Principles (What Most People Are Actually Running)

A specific catalogue of the unconscious principles Joe encounters most often when people first do the excavation work. These are the "default principles" — the ones installed by the culture, the family, the developmental environment, that operate without ever being articulated:

- Stay out of trouble.
- Make sure everybody's happy.
- Please people.
- Be codependent.
- Get angry when you don't get what you want.

"We are actually already living by a set of principles. Unfortunately, a lot of those principles aren't great principles. They're like, stay out of trouble, make sure everybody's happy, please people, be codependent, get angry when you don't get what you want. Like, those are the principles that you might be living by." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The list is useful as a diagnostic for an AI coach. When a client is reporting decisions that don't make sense to them, asking which of these principles is doing the deciding often produces immediate recognition. "Stay out of trouble" is doing the deciding when someone keeps not raising the issue. "Make everybody happy" is doing the deciding when someone keeps over-committing. "Be codependent" is doing the deciding when someone keeps making their partner's emotions their problem. "Get angry when you don't get what you want" is doing the deciding when someone keeps escalating after a no.

None of these would be chosen consciously. All of them are operating. The recognition that "yes, that is in fact what is deciding my actions" is itself the first move of the work. See [[shame-drives-behavior]] for the related mechanism of how some of these default principles get installed and held in place.

The list also matters because it makes the unconscious-principles point experientially available rather than abstract. A person who is told "you have unconscious principles" may not be able to locate them. A person who is asked "is 'make sure everybody's happy' something that decides your actions?" can usually answer immediately. The named-from-the-outside principle gets recognized faster than the unnamed one gets excavated from the inside.

## The Awareness Itself Begins the Change

A specific feature of the consciousness move that matters operationally: just bringing the unconscious principle into awareness starts to change it. The articulation is itself the intervention. The person does not have to add a behavior-change project on top of the recognition. The recognition is doing work on its own.

"It's such a profound experience to just bring into light what you're actually already doing. Just that awareness in itself starts to change things." -- Joe Hudson, Every Decision You Struggle With Has One Thing in Common

The mechanism: an unconscious principle can only continue operating unconsciously. The moment it has been named, it is no longer unconscious. The next time it tries to operate, the person sees it operating. The seeing is what introduces choice into a process that had been automatic. The choice does not have to be exercised perfectly to begin to shift the principle. Just the seeing — without judgment, without management — begins the change.

This is consistent with [[awareness-as-change-agent]] generally. The awareness is not a precondition for change; the awareness is itself the change. Adding effort on top of the awareness usually re-installs the same unconscious principle in a new costume (now it is "I should be aware of my unconscious principles" as the new should that does the same work the old one did).

The coaching implication: when someone names an unconscious principle for the first time, do not immediately propose a remediation plan. Let the naming do its work. Often the next session reveals that something has already shifted just from the naming, with no further intervention. See [[awareness-of-pattern-is-halfway-through]] for the related principle about pattern-recognition being half the work.

## Joe's Example: The Transition

Joe's transition from unconscious to conscious principles started with one principle becoming conscious: embrace intensity. He did not invent it. He noticed he was already doing it in his marriage with Tara, and that it worked. The act of noticing made it a principle he could then deliberately apply elsewhere.

"For me, that was the first one. And then, I was like, oh, this is this thing in my life that once I recognize it, I do it all the time, even when I don't want to, even when it's not the convenient thing, even because I see that it works just so consistently and so well." -- Joe Hudson, Wrestle With Your Principles

The pattern: a person who is doing something consistently and not naming it brings the doing into awareness. Once named, it becomes a principle they can use deliberately. Other principles get articulated the same way: by noticing what is already happening and what is working.

The reverse is also true. The principles he was running on before therapy were also being applied "all the time, even when I don't want to." They had the operational signature of principles. They just had not been named.

## In Organizations

This applies at every scale. A company has unconscious principles operating before anyone articulates them.

"The company is run by trying to make the CEO happy. Which is wholly ineffective." -- Joe Hudson, quoting what he hears from many executives, Building a Functional Team

Nobody wrote that on the wall. It is operating anyway. It produces every meeting, every product decision, every promotion. Until it is named, it cannot be revised. See [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]] for the organizational anti-pattern.

The same diagnostic applies: what are the consistent patterns of choice in this company? What gets sacrificed when pressure is applied? What gets protected? Those patterns are the principles. They are operating whether the leadership chose them or not.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A growing recognition that your life is the sum of decisions you have made, even decisions you do not remember making
- The discovery that you have been operating from a principle you would not consciously endorse
- Relief, after the initial discomfort, when an unconscious principle is finally named
- The realization that "I do not have principles" is itself a principle (often: "I do not commit to anything I might have to live up to")
- The recognition that asking what you should value misses the point -- the question is what you already do value, as evidenced by your choices
