# Most of the Generosity You've Received and Given Goes Unrecognized

## The Principle

There is a vast field of generosity that has already happened in your life that you have not registered. Both directions: the generosity you have received from others without naming it as such, and the generosity you have offered without naming it as such. The unrecognized field is much larger than the registered field. Most of the work on generosity is not about producing more generosity. It is about letting in the generosity that is already there.

"There's a million opportunities to be generous every day. But there's also a million ways that you've been generous that you haven't even seen, a million acts of generosity that you haven't even recognized, as well as the opportunities." -- Brett Kistler, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being (relaying his dying brother Scott)

The teaching has the weight of being delivered from inside a terminal diagnosis. Scott, looking back at his life from where he is, sees both the opportunities he still has and the field of generosity already given and received that went unnamed. From that vantage, the field is large.

## Two Directions of Unrecognition

**The unrecognized generosity you've received.**

The teacher who taught your daughter. The friend who showed up. The colleague who covered for you. The stranger who held the door. The person who introduced you to the work that became your career. The parent who, for all their failures, put food in front of you for years. The mentor who took a meeting. The patient who let you fumble through your first attempts. The supplier who extended credit. The doctor who took the extra minute.

Most of this is invisible to the person living the life. The bar for "I received generosity" is implicitly set very high -- usually requiring some kind of dramatic gesture or substantial gift. Most generosity does not meet that bar. Most generosity is small, ongoing, and absorbed without notice.

Joe's example: his daughter's teachers. To become a teacher for a living is itself an act of generosity. The pay is modest; the work is hard; the impact is mostly invisible to the teacher. Esme, looking back, recognized her teachers as good people but did not register what she did with strangers on the passport trip as generosity. The strangers' generosity was registered because conditions forced the recognition. The teachers' generosity was not, because conditions did not.

"My daughter went to school. There's all these teachers who taught her. There's generosity in each one of them. To become a teacher for a living, there's a generosity in that. Maybe they didn't even recognize it." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

**The unrecognized generosity you've given.**

The hour you spent listening when you did not have time. The check you wrote without making it a story. The hire you took a risk on. The kindness you offered a stranger and forgot about. The decade you put into your children's lives. The relentless small attentions in a long marriage. The mentorship you provided that you would have done anyway. The professional generosity built into the way you do your work.

Joe notices this in his own case during the conversation. People get on calls with him and thank him for being so generous. It does not feel like generosity to him. It feels like living on purpose. The work has integrated past the point where he registers it as offering. The generosity is real -- people receive it as such -- but the giver no longer experiences it as giving.

"It never feels like generosity to me. ... I'm wondering now how much of that is me not letting it in and how much of it is that I can't think of another way to do it." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The implication: the field of unrecognized generosity in your own giving is likely larger than what you register, in the same way the field of unrecognized generosity in your receiving is larger than what you register. Both can be let in.

## Why the Unrecognition Happens

Several structural reasons for both directions of unrecognition:

**The bar is set too high.** Cultural narratives about generosity feature dramatic acts -- the Nobel-prize-level philanthropist, the rescuer of strangers, the saint. Daily small generosity does not feel like generosity by comparison and so does not get filed under the category.

**Receiving requires capacity that has not been developed.** Most people have an underdeveloped capacity to receive (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]). The receiving channel is the same whether what is being received is a compliment, a gift, or the recognition that someone was generous toward you. When the channel is small, the received generosity slips by without registering.

**Self-narration filters out the giver-evidence.** People often have a self-image organized around being someone-who-falls-short-of-generous or someone-who-has-to-give-more. That self-image filters out evidence that contradicts it. The generosity that has been given is invisible because the self-image needs it to be invisible. See [[challenge-the-not-enough-identity-with-the-compliment]] for the parallel pattern.

**Integration removes the felt-sense of giving.** As Joe describes for himself, work that has integrated past a certain point no longer feels like offering. It feels like living. This is the maturity of the work, but it has the side effect of disconnecting the giver from the felt-sense of being generous. See the same phenomenon in [[generosity]] (the choice-becomes-automatic inversion).

**Crisis is usually required to break the unrecognition.** The Esme-passport-stolen example (see [[esmes-stolen-passport-in-spain]]) shows the typical structure: a crisis forces the receiving channel open and makes the field of generosity visible all at once. Most lives do not provide enough crisis to do this. The field stays invisible.

## The Practice

The practice that follows from this principle is letting in the unrecognized generosity in both directions:

**For the receiving side:** look back over your life and list specific people whose generosity toward you you did not fully register at the time. Do not make a list of dramatic gifts; make a list of teachers, friends, colleagues, family members, strangers whose ordinary generosity sustained you. Then let in -- somatically, not just intellectually -- the recognition that this happened.

"If I really let that in for a second, our business wouldn't be here if it wasn't for the generosity of a lot of people." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The felt experience of letting it in is described by Joe as "a bit overwhelming" -- the body is registering a field much larger than the cognitive frame had been holding.

**For the giving side:** look at what you do regularly and ask which of those things, from another vantage, would be received as generosity. Do not inflate. Notice what was actually offered. Let yourself receive the fact of having given it.

The practice is structurally identical to the gratitude practice (see [[gratitude-as-full-body-sport]]) -- it is a felt experience of receiving the recognition, not a cognitive listing. The list is the scaffold for the felt experience; the felt experience is the practice.

## The Connection to Gratitude

This principle is the structural foundation of gratitude. Gratitude is the felt reception of generosity. If most generosity goes unrecognized, then most gratitude is unavailable -- not because the underlying gifts are not there, but because the recognition has not happened.

This is why [[always-something-spectacular-happening]] points at the same field. The "spectacular" that is always happening is the field of generosity that is already in motion around you. Searching for it pushes it away; receiving it lets it land. The recognition that the field is already there does not require finding something new. It requires letting in what is.

The practice is also why gratitude tends to expand the more it is practiced. Each round of let-in recognition raises the floor of what can be registered. The thirty acts of generosity Esme received on the passport trip would have been invisible to her ten years earlier. The crisis forced the registration. After the registration, the capacity remained.

## The Anti-Inflation Caveat

The principle is not "everything everyone does is generous" or "you should feel grateful for everything." Real generosity has the signature described in [[generosity]] -- the ego-cracking, the empowerment of both parties, the desire-base. Acts that lack these qualities are not generous regardless of how the receiver wants to frame them.

The principle is more specific: among acts that do have the signature of real generosity, most go unrecognized. The work is to let in the ones that are real, not to inflate ordinary transactions into generosity to feel better about them.

The diagnostic: does the recognition produce a felt expansion in the body, or does it produce the strained quality of forced positivity? The first is letting in real generosity that was already there. The second is manufacturing a frame to feel better. Trust the body to distinguish them.

## When This Principle Is Active in a Coaching Conversation

- A person reports feeling alone or unsupported despite having people in their life
- A person says they have no one to be grateful for
- A person describes themselves as not being a generous person despite obviously generous work
- A person is in the middle of a hard event and recognizing for the first time how much support is showing up
- A person has lost someone and is looking back at what that person gave that they did not register
- A person is doing gratitude practice and getting no results
- A person reports they "don't feel generous" the way they used to and is wondering if something is wrong
- The conversation arrives at the question of what to actually let in versus what to keep at arm's length

## Tone

The principle should be delivered slowly. The point is to invite the listener to actually let it in, which requires more time than the cognitive content takes to convey. Fast delivery would treat the principle as information; slow delivery treats it as something to receive. The teaching is in the receiving.

When the listener begins to let the recognition land -- often signaled by a deepening of breath, a softening of the face, a small wetness in the eyes -- stay with them. Do not rush to the next point. The receiving is the work.
