# If You Are Falling in Love, You Are Signing Up to Be Hurt

## The Principle

There is no version of real love that does not include being hurt. Falling in love is, structurally, agreeing to be hurt. The hurt is not an unfortunate side effect of love that better partnership skills could eliminate. The hurt is part of what love is. The willingness to be hurt is the threshold the body must cross to actually be in a relationship.

"There's this like inherent thing of willing to be hurt if you're falling in love. If you are falling in love, you're saying, I sign up to be hurt, and there's no fucking way around it. There's no way you are going to get married and live happily ever after or be in a, you know, loving 6-way relationship and live happily ever after, however you do it, and you're not going to get hurt. You're going to get hurt. You're going to get hurt potentially daily, right? And potentially really, really, really badly. Like, that's what you're signing up for. Hey, I'm willing to be hurt." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The principle is stark and is meant to be. The common framing of love -- find the right person, do the work, do not get hurt -- is a misunderstanding of what love actually is. Love is the willingness to be hurt. The relationship is the form that willingness takes.

## Why This Is True

Two people in love are constitutively vulnerable to each other in ways that strangers are not. The opening that love requires is the same opening through which hurt arrives. There is no version of opening that opens only to the good. The capacity that lets the love in lets the hurt in.

The hurt comes from multiple sources:
- The partner's mistakes, mis-attunements, failures to show up
- The partner's growth and the necessary realignment that produces
- The partner's pain that lands as the lover's pain because of the closeness
- The eventual ending of the relationship, whether by breakup or by death (see [[finite-love-is-precious-love]])
- The daily friction of two interior worlds attempting to meet

None of these are eliminable. The lover who attempts to eliminate them eliminates the love. The protection that would prevent the hurt is the same protection that would prevent the connection.

This is the same mechanism as [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]]: the strategy to avoid the cost of love is what produces the cost of love. The willing-to-be-hurt stance is what allows love to actually function. The unwilling-to-be-hurt stance produces the configurations described in [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]] -- jealousy, neediness, hyper-independence, transactionality, the checklist -- which keep love away precisely because they are unwilling to sign the contract.

## Love Is Not a Secure Feeling

Love produces, at moments, a deep felt sense of security. The principle does not deny this. The principle says that the security is not what love is. Love is not the secure feeling. Love includes the secure feeling and includes the loss of the secure feeling and includes the moment-to-moment risk of the secure feeling being gone.

"I think love is not a secure feeling. It's a kind of a weird thing to say, meaning that on one level it's very secure. You feel love and you feel this deep sense of security. On another level, it goes away. And so, and the loss of love is one of the most painful things that humans go through." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The "weird thing to say" is the move the principle requires. Most people's conscious description of love centers on the secure feeling. The fuller description includes the constant possibility of the loss. The relationship is the willingness to live in both at once.

## Why This Reframe Matters in Coaching

For the person who has been waiting for a relationship in which they would not be at risk: there is no such relationship. The wait is for a thing that does not exist. The fear of being hurt has been preventing the very thing the fear was protecting against in the wrong way -- by preventing love from arriving at all.

For the person who has been hurt and is determining whether to love again: yes, you will be hurt again. The choice is not between love-and-hurt and love-without-hurt. The choice is between love (which includes hurt) and the absence of love (which is its own form of hurt). The principle does not promise that loving again is better. It clarifies what is actually on the table.

For the person who is in a relationship and trying to avoid the hurt of it: the avoidance is the source of the slow death of the relationship. The "I will love you but I will not let you hurt me" stance is the closure that, over years, produces the resentment and the eggshells and the dead sex. See [[walking-on-eggshells]] and [[we-have-built-up-resentment]].

For the person who is about to make a deeper commitment: the commitment is the explicit signing of the contract that says yes, I am willing to be hurt by you, potentially daily, potentially badly. The clarity about what is being committed to is part of what makes the commitment real.

## The Connection to Fear of Losing Yourself

The principle is one of the layers of [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]]. The fear of being hurt is the fear of being taken down for months by the hurt. The fear that, if I let myself love this person, the hurt will dissolve me.

"If I get hurt like that, I'm gonna be gone for months, maybe, you know, I'm gonna be totally lost in it." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The fear is partly correct. The hurt of significant love loss is real and large. The work is not to deny the size. The work is to develop the capacity to be hurt without being annihilated. See [[knowing-yourself-is-the-only-safety]] for the internal capacity that makes this possible. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] for the structural finding that each heartbreak, fully felt, increases capacity rather than depleting it.

## The Hurt Is the Tool

The principle has a second-order edge that is part of the teaching. The hurt is not a tax on love. The hurt is the tool that develops the capacity to love more deeply.

"If we can get to the place of, oh, every time I allow my heart to break, every time it, it increases my capacity to love. Every time that I draw a boundary, it increases my capacity to love. Every time that I'm vulnerable, and I -- it increases my capacity to love myself. Like, all of these things that are being avoided are the tools to learn how to love more deeply." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

This is the move that lifts the principle from a grim acceptance of the cost to an active relationship with the cost. The hurt expands the heart. The boundary expands the heart. The vulnerability expands the heart. The same experiences that the avoidance strategies were avoiding are the experiences that grow the capacity for love.

The person who has avoided being hurt for years has not just avoided the hurt. They have avoided the capacity-growth that the hurt would have produced. They have less heart capacity than the person who has been hurt and stayed in. See [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] for the developed treatment of this mechanism.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not a license for harm. The principle says that hurt is part of love. It does not say that any hurt in a relationship is therefore love. Abuse, gaslighting, coercion -- these are not the hurt the principle is naming. The principle is naming the structural hurt that comes from two open hearts meeting in the world: the mis-attunements, the inevitable losses, the friction of two interior lives. Abuse is a different category. See [[love-vs-nice]] for the distinction between hard love and bad love.

It is not a justification for unwillingness to do the repair. The hurt that the principle names is the hurt that arises and is met. The repair work is part of what relationships do with the hurt. The principle does not say "you signed up for this so do not address it." It says "the hurt is part of what you signed up for; do the work the hurt requires."

It is not a romantic principle. The directness about the cost is not a romantic flourish. It is structural. The principle is stated with the same matter-of-factness as any other observation about how the mechanism works.

## The Commitment Inside Existing Relationships

The principle does not stop at the entry to a relationship. The willingness-to-be-hurt has to be renewed throughout. The committed long-term relationship requires the same willingness on a continuing basis. The drift over years toward not being willing to be hurt by the partner is one of the central ways long relationships go cold. See [[grieve-the-marriage-to-save-it]] for the periodic renewal practice.

The marker of a living long-term relationship: both partners are still willing to be hurt by each other. The marker of a dying long-term relationship: both partners have closed the doors through which the hurt would arrive, and the love is being conserved by being kept too small to wound.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The moment of asking whether to make a deeper commitment
- The moment after a partner has hurt them and they are considering whether to stay open
- The moment of considering whether to date again after a significant loss
- The recognition that "I want a relationship where I won't get hurt" is a description of no relationship that exists
- The relief, sometimes, of being told directly that the hurt is part of the deal -- the unwillingness to admit this had been making the choice harder than it needed to be

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person describing fear of being hurt as the reason they are not in a relationship
- A person describing the avoidance of hurt as their primary relational competence
- A person who has just been hurt and is closing the doors
- A person at the precipice of commitment
- A person in a long relationship that has gone cold because both have stopped being willing to be hurt

## Tone

Direct, almost stark. The principle does not lend itself to soft delivery. The "no fucking way around it" register is part of the teaching -- the directness clears the fog of partial framings. The teaching depends on the listener feeling the truth of the claim, not on being protected from it. The matter-of-fact register is the protection: this is just how it is, you are not being singled out for an unusually hard fate.
