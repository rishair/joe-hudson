# I'm None of My Own Goddamn Business

## The Principle

A specific inner posture that produces freedom: stop taking yourself personally. Stop treating yourself as the project you have to solve. Show up to life, observe what happens, let it unravel, and find out what life has to offer rather than making sure your life conforms to who you think you should be.

Joe encountered the formulation through his wife Sarah, who heard it from someone else:

"It was this woman and she said something to the effect of, uh, I'm just none of my own goddamn business." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

"It was that same thing. It was like, I'm not going to take myself personally. It's that same kind of open-hearted, I'm not going to take this personally. This isn't personal. This is just what is happening right now. I'm not going to buy into the shame. I'm not going to, you know, and it's not my job to figure out where I'm going to be next. I'm going to just show up and let the whole thing unravel and observe it. I think is the rest of what this woman was saying that I can find out what life has to offer. I don't have to be personally responsible for it." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The phrase captures a posture that is hard to name in conventional spiritual language without sounding bypassy. "None of my own business" is the exact right level of irreverence. The speaker is not transcending the self. They are simply releasing themselves from being their own life-project.

## Why This Connects to Speaking the Elephant

Joe brings this principle up in the context of how to name the elephant in the room from open heart rather than from fear. The connection: you cannot name the elephant openheartedly while you are silently managing what the naming will mean about you, who you are, whether you are doing it right, whether you will be judged.

The "none of my own business" posture clears the field. You are not in the act for what it says about you. You are in the act because the act is the truth that needs to happen now. The self-monitoring that produces managed delivery is the same self-monitoring that this principle dissolves.

A speaker who is "none of their own business" speaks because the truth needs to be spoken. They do not speak to prove anything about themselves. The speaking is clean. The listener reads the cleanness. The delivery lands.

## What This Is Not

The principle is easily confused with three things it is not:

**Not self-abandonment.** Self-abandonment is leaving yourself to manage someone else. The "none of my own business" posture is not leaving yourself; it is releasing yourself from being a project. You are still here. You are still feeling what you feel. You are just not making it personal. See [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]] for the contrast.

**Not spiritual bypass.** Bypass is using transcendent language to skip the feeling. This posture does not skip the feeling — it removes the self-importance about the feeling. You can be heartbroken and also be none of your own business. The heartbreak is happening. You are not making it mean something about who you are.

**Not nihilism.** Nihilism says nothing matters. This posture says the self matters less than the showing-up. The act of being present is what matters. The story you tell yourself about who you are is what gets dropped.

The distinction with [[humility-vs-disempowerment]] is sharp here. Disempowerment makes yourself small. The "none of my own business" posture does not make you small; it makes you not-the-point. There is a difference between "I am insignificant" (disempowerment) and "I am not the topic" (this principle). The first shrinks you. The second frees you.

## What Gets Dropped When You Are None of Your Own Business

Several specific burdens fall away:

- **The constant self-evaluation.** Was that the right thing to say? Did I handle that well? What does this mean about who I am? These questions stop being interesting.
- **The shame-buying.** When someone tries to install shame, you do not have anywhere to install it because you are not running a self-assessment project that the shame can populate. See [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]].
- **The strategic self-presentation.** You stop having to figure out how to come across, because you are not the thing being presented.
- **The future-self management.** You stop needing to engineer your life into a particular shape, because what shape your life takes is none of your business — it is something to find out, not something to make.
- **The personal stakes in your own opinions.** Your opinions get to be wrong without you having to be wrong.

## What Stays

The posture does not erase you. You still:

- Feel what you feel
- Want what you want
- Speak what is true for you
- Show up where you are
- Care about the people you love
- Take action when action is called for

The difference is that all of this is happening to a person who is not their own subject of investigation. The feelings are present without becoming "what does this feeling say about me." The wants are present without becoming "am I right to want this." The actions happen without becoming "did I do this well."

This is closely related to [[humility]] as Joe defines it: not making yourself small, but having no self to defend. The posture is the felt version of that humility. The empty self is none of its own business.

## The Connection to Wonder

The "find out what life has to offer" part is the wonder component of [[view]]. You are not going through life to confirm what you already think life is. You are going through life to discover. The discovery requires that you not be over-invested in the outcome being a particular thing.

If you are heavily invested in being a successful person, you cannot wonder what life has to offer — you have already decided. If you are heavily invested in not being a fraud, you cannot wonder what your work has to offer — you have already determined the framework. The wonder requires the dropping of these investments.

The posture is what makes the wonder operationally possible. None of your own business is the prerequisite for actually getting to find out.

## What This Looks Like Day-to-Day

A person operating from this posture:

- Tries things without rehearsing what trying them will mean about them
- Speaks the elephant without managing the listener
- Receives criticism without needing to defend or fix
- Watches their own habits with curiosity rather than self-judgment
- Lets their life take shapes they did not plan
- Apologizes when they have caused harm without making the apology about whether they are a good person
- Does the work in front of them without it being a referendum on their identity

The posture is recognizable in people who are genuinely free. It is rarely named in conventional psychology because conventional psychology is largely the business of helping people become better versions of themselves — which presupposes that you are your own business.

## When It Applies

The principle is useful in specific moments:

- About to deliver hard news and noticing self-monitoring is taking over
- Receiving criticism and feeling the impulse to defend
- Stuck in self-evaluation about a decision
- Caught in a cycle of trying to figure out who you are
- Preparing to name an elephant in the room and feeling the management start
- Approaching a creative act with self-consciousness

In each case, the move is the same: drop yourself as the subject. You are not the topic. The topic is what is actually happening. Return your attention there.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You spend a long time obsessing about whether something you said was the right thing to say, and at some point realize the obsessing is not getting you anywhere
- You watch someone live who clearly does not take themselves personally, and notice how light their presence is
- You drop the self-question for a moment and the field of attention immediately becomes wider
- You realize that most of your suffering is about what things mean about you, not about the things themselves
- You catch the irony of being your own most-fascinating subject and the laugh breaks the spell

## Anti-Patterns

- **Performing "none of my own business" as a personality.** Then you are very much your own business; you are just hiding it behind a posture.
- **Using the principle to avoid taking responsibility for harm caused.** "I'm none of my own business" is not a license to ignore the effects of your actions on others. You can take responsibility without making the responsibility a referendum on your identity.
- **Using the principle to bypass real emotion.** The feelings still happen; the principle is about how you relate to yourself having them, not about whether they happen.

## Tone

The principle is delivered with humor and a certain amount of irreverence. The phrase itself — "I'm none of my own goddamn business" — has the right level of casual profanity for what it is naming. The posture cannot be taught too seriously without becoming another form of self-importance. The lightness is part of the teaching.
