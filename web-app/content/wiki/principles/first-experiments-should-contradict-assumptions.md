# First Experiments in a Domain Should Contradict Your Assumption

## The Principle

When starting to experiment in a new area, the first experiments should be designed to test something you think could not possibly be true. They should contradict your assumption about the domain. They should not be designed to reinforce what you already believe about yourself in this area, especially not the things you believe that are shame-based.

If the first experiment is "I am going to try to stop smoking and see if I can do it," the experiment is structurally pre-loaded to either confirm you can (which you already half-believed) or confirm you cannot (which is the shame-based belief looking for validation). Either outcome adds little, and the second outcome actively reinforces self-attack.

If the first experiment is instead "what is there to learn about smoking that I have not considered?" or "what does smoking feel like if I approach it with curiosity instead of self-judgment?" -- the experiment is now opening territory rather than confirming pre-existing beliefs.

"I've noticed that when people start running experiments, they'll run an experiment that proves that they can't do it, or they'll run an experiment that proves that they're broken in some way. It'll reinforce some sort of shame. So typically I like running the first couple experiments in any area as something that's like completely contradictory to something that I would believe. I don't think that's true at all. I don't -- that couldn't be right. And I'll run that experiment." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## Why It Works

The first experiment in any domain has outsized effects on what the rest of the experimentation in that domain will look like. The first experiment shapes the frame.

If the first experiment confirms a shame-based belief, the frame is now "I am broken in this way," and every subsequent experiment runs inside that frame. The experimenter is no longer running experiments; they are running confirmations.

If the first experiment opens the frame -- if it shows something the experimenter did not believe was possible -- the frame is now "I do not know what is possible here," and every subsequent experiment runs inside that more open frame. The wonder is preserved. The actual experimentation can happen.

The principle is structural protection. The first experiment is chosen specifically to defeat the gravitational pull of the existing belief structure. Once the belief structure has been disturbed, the more conventional experiments can happen without reinforcing shame.

## The Marketing Example

Joe gives a worked example from his own business. He hates most marketing. The Stage 1 version of running experiments in this area would have been "let me try to do marketing in a way I would feel okay about and see if I can." That experiment is pre-loaded to confirm the existing belief (most marketing is gross).

The actual first experiment Joe ran: "how do we do it exactly the way most people market?" Pop-up screens, timers, the full state-of-the-art marketing playbook. The experiment was specifically designed to do the thing he most disliked, exactly the way the people he most disagreed with did it.

"The first experiment we ran in marketing was how do we do it exactly the way most people market? Like take that, this state-of-the-art marketing and like complete with timers and pop-up screens. And we did exactly that to see what works about it, what we could learn. There's a lot of intelligence there that has been put into that. There's a lot of people put a lot of time and energy into that. What could we learn? And then how do we make it ours in a way that feels good and to us." -- Joe Hudson

What this experiment produced: an honest understanding of why state-of-the-art marketing exists, what it does well, what intelligence has gone into it. From that understanding, a version that was actually theirs could emerge -- not in opposition to marketing, not in submission to it, but with awareness of what the marketing playbook actually solves for.

The shame-based first experiment would have produced: a confirmation that marketing was bad, a continued aversion to it, no learning, and probably worse business outcomes. The contrarian first experiment produced: real information, a more sophisticated relationship to the domain, and a basis for designing what actually fits.

## The Exercise Example

Joe gives a second example. He had grown up not exercising. He hated it. He believed exercise was painful. The shame-based first experiment would have been "let me try to exercise more and see if I can stick with it." That experiment confirms the belief that exercise is hard.

The actual first experiment: "how can I see if exercise is fun?" He tried 50 different things -- yoga, dance, contact improv, every class he could find. Cha-cha on Sunday nights. The experiment was designed to break the assumption "exercise is painful" by going wide on what counted as exercise.

The discovery: pickleball. Joe currently exercises by playing pickleball. The exercise belief was not "I cannot exercise" or "exercise is painful"; it was "I have not yet found the version of exercise that is fun for me." The contrarian first experiment surfaced the real situation, which the shame-based first experiment would never have reached.

## What This Replaces

The default first experiment is the one that confirms what the person already believes. The default is the experiment most easily designed because it operates inside the existing frame. The default produces no learning, only confirmation.

The contrarian first experiment requires more design effort. The person has to construct an experiment that genuinely tests the opposite of what they assume. This is uncomfortable. The discomfort is part of why the design effort gets skipped. The principle says: do not skip it. The first experiment is the highest-leverage one in the domain. Use the design effort.

## The Anti-Pattern This Prevents

Without this principle, experimentation often becomes a sophisticated form of self-attack. The person believes they are stuck, runs an experiment that proves they are stuck, and now has experimental evidence of being stuck. The experimentation has reinforced the shame.

This is the version of experimentation that does not produce growth. The person reports back, "I tried it and I am the way I thought I was." The conclusion is unwarranted (the experiment was pre-loaded) and the self-image has hardened (now it is empirical).

The contrarian first experiment is the structural protection. By definition, an experiment designed to contradict the assumption cannot confirm the assumption easily. If the contrarian experiment produces an unexpected result, the assumption has to be reconsidered. If the contrarian experiment produces the expected result, that result has more weight than a shame-confirming first experiment would have, because it survived a serious test.

## What "Contrary to What You Believe" Looks Like

Joe's framing of the contrary-to-belief stance: "I don't think that's true at all. I don't -- that couldn't be right. And I'll run that experiment."

The marker is the felt resistance. The contrarian first experiment is the one the person finds it hardest to design because it disturbs the assumption. The disturbance is the indicator. If the experiment is comfortable to design, it is probably reinforcing rather than testing the existing belief.

The questions for designing a contrarian first experiment:
- What do I believe is impossible in this domain?
- What do I believe is bad or wrong in this domain?
- What is the conventional approach I have dismissed?
- What would a person who is the opposite of me do here?
- What would I never try?

The answers to these questions are the candidate first experiments. The best first experiment is usually one that combines several of these.

## The Open-Aperture Effect

The contrarian first experiment opens the aperture of what counts as possible in the domain. Once the aperture is open, the subsequent experiments can be more conventional and still produce real learning, because the existing belief structure has already been disturbed enough that the conventional experiments are no longer just confirmations.

This is why Joe says "the first couple experiments." The principle is most strongly applied to the very first experiment in a domain. The second and third experiments inherit the opened frame. By the fourth or fifth experiment, the person can design experiments more directly aligned with their actual question, because the question itself has been reshaped by the opening.

The implication: invest disproportionately in the design of the first experiment in any domain. The leverage is highest there. The rest of the experiments will follow whatever frame the first one established.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the energy of a structural insight earned through pattern recognition. He has watched people run experiments and noticed the same failure mode repeatedly: the experiment that confirms what they already half-believed. The corrective is practical and specific: design the first experiment to disturb the belief, not to test it from inside the belief.
