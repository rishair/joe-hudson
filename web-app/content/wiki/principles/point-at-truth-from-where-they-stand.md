# Point at the Truth Based on Where the Person Is Standing

## The Principle

The coach is not delivering a doctrine. The coach is pointing at freedom. The specific words used to point depend on where the listener is standing. The same coach in two consecutive sessions can point at exactly opposite content — and both pointings are correct because the freedom they reveal is the same.

"I'm always pointing to the thing that I think will show someone the possibility of freedom. And the truth that I'm pointing to is the freedom, not the intellectual idea that's coming out of my mouth." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free

The principle becomes explicit in the coaching reaction video. Joe had just coached a person in a previous session through the belief that money is bad, helping that person see through it ("money is the sun"). In the next session — being analyzed in the same recording — a different person uses Joe's earlier teaching as a defense: "Joe, you just said capital and money is the sun. I think anti-capitalism is fucking stupid." For that person's freedom, Joe immediately pivots and says capitalism is wrong: "I can give you fucking 30 reasons why it's wrong. It does not create equal opportunity is just one of them."

The contradiction is not a mistake. It is the principle. The intellectual content is the arm pointing at the tree. The arm moves depending on where the viewer is standing. The tree does not move.

## The Tree Metaphor

"If I'm going to point to a tree I'm going to point to it differently depending on where you're standing. Like, my arm's going to move. And so where he's standing, pointing to the truth is going to be different. It's gonna sound different, it's gonna look different. And his freedom isn't in seeing the goodness of money, though there's probably some freedom in that for him. There's still some judgment that he has around it, or he wouldn't buy into any of this stuff. But the bigger freedom is for him right now is to like, hey, what are you defending? Like, why? What makes you have to defend this?" -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free

The metaphor names the structure cleanly:
- The tree is freedom itself — non-defended, non-identified being.
- The arm is the specific teaching used to point at the tree.
- The listener's position determines which direction the arm needs to point.

Two people standing on opposite sides of the same tree both need to look at the tree. The arms pointing them there will be aimed in opposite directions. If the coach is identified with the arm rather than the tree, both directions cannot coexist. If the coach is identified with the tree, both directions are obviously the same instruction.

## What This Requires of the Coach

The coach has to be non-identified with the specific content of any teaching. The teaching is an instrument. The freedom is the destination. If the coach starts believing the teaching itself is the truth, the coach can no longer point at the freedom — only at the teaching. The arm becomes the object instead of the indicator.

See [[identification-with-the-teaching]] for the distinction between certainty of delivery and identification with content. The coach in this principle can speak with full certainty in any given moment ("capitalism is wrong, just simply") while holding the meta-position that the certainty is calibrated to the listener, not to objective truth.

See [[hold-a-perspective-without-thinking-youre-right]] for the felt-sense version of the same stance applied to one's own views. This principle is the coaching application: the coach can deliver any perspective passionately if the perspective serves the listener's freedom in this moment.

## Why It Matters for the Listener

A coach who points at the same content regardless of listener position is teaching a doctrine, not freeing anyone. The doctrine becomes a new prison: now the listener has to agree with the doctrine to be considered correct, which is the exact identification structure that locks freedom away.

A coach who points at freedom from where the listener stands can free the listener through any door. The door is not the freedom. The door is the way into the room where the freedom lives.

The cost of mistaking the door for the freedom: a generation of students who become defenders of the teaching rather than people who have actually arrived at what the teaching was pointing at. See [[dont-cement-insight-as-identity]] and [[limiting-belief-meta-trap]] for the failure modes this produces.

## The Both-And of Holding Opposing Viewpoints

The principle in self-application: a person living this way can hold two opposing ideas simultaneously and see the truth in both of them. This is not relativism. It is the recognition that any single perspective is partial, and the freedom is in not being captured by any one of them.

"As you're approaching life, being able to hold two opposing ideas at the same time and see the truth in both of them, there's a tremendous amount of freedom in that." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free

The coach pointing at truth from where the listener stands is doing this move in real time. The capitalist position has truth. The anti-capitalist position has truth. Both are partial. The coach speaks from whichever one will free the specific person in front of them right now.

This is the same structural move as [[mind-wide-as-sky-action-fine-as-barley]]: the mind is wide enough to hold all perspectives as partial; the action is precise and chosen for this moment.

## Why People Find This Hard

Three reasons, named explicitly in the same teaching:

1. **You usually have to feel something you don't want to feel.** Holding both sides means feeling the fear or loss inside each side. The defended position protects you from the felt material of the opposing position.

2. **The way you create distinction is the way you define your personality.** To hold both Biden and Trump as partially right, you have to let go of identity-as-distinction. The identity defends itself by refusing the both-and.

3. **You don't get to be right anymore.** You can't believe the voice in your head. You can't trust your own thoughts as universal truth. The arrival of "yes and also" dissolves the secure ground of being correct.

"You usually have to feel something you don't want to feel. Two is, you know, the way that we create distinction is the way that we define our personality. So to be able to see the wisdom of the Biden side and the Trump side. Whoa, man, you're gonna have to let go of some of your personality. You're gonna have to feel certain things... The other reason it's scary is because like you don't get to be right anymore." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free

The same three forces operate when a coach tries to hold this principle: feeling the discomfort of contradicting yourself, losing the identity of being a doctrinal teacher, giving up the certainty of being objectively correct. The principle costs the coach exactly what it costs the student.

## Distinct From Just Telling People What They Want to Hear

A misreading of this principle: "the coach just tells the listener what they want to hear." That would be a different practice — pandering, not pointing at freedom.

The test: does the specific teaching, delivered to this specific person, open the door to freedom from a defended position? If yes, it is pointing at the truth. If it confirms the defended position and makes it more secure, it is the opposite of this principle.

In the source session, the anti-capitalist person wanted Joe to confirm "capitalism is wrong" so the person could remain comfortably anti-capitalist. Joe did say capitalism is wrong — and then immediately used that agreement to expose the heart-hardening the person was doing in defending the position: "Hold on a second, hold on, because something big is about to happen, but I want to talk about what just happened... I notice all the defense in that. Like, your heart had to harden to be able to say that."

The teaching that pointed at "capitalism is wrong" was deployed in service of the deeper teaching: don't defend any position with a hardened heart. The content (capitalism is wrong) was the door. The freedom (undefended being) was the room.

## The Coach's Movement Through Teaching

The implication for the coach's own development: the more skillfully you can point at freedom from where each listener stands, the less attached you are to any single teaching. Over time, the teaching repertoire becomes large, but the coach's identification with any one teaching shrinks toward zero.

A young coach often has one or two teachings they deliver to everyone. The teachings are their identity. A mature coach has many teachings and uses whichever fits the moment. The teachings are tools. The coach's identity is in the freedom being pointed at, not in any tool.

See [[coach-as-follower-not-director]] for the related stance: the coach follows the person, doesn't impose a curriculum.

## When This Principle Comes Into Play

The principle is alive any time a coach reaches for what to say next. Specifically:

- When a previous client received teaching X, and this client would be freed by teaching not-X
- When the listener is using a teaching as a defense ("you said money is the sun, so I'm right that anti-capitalism is wrong")
- When the coach feels the pull to be consistent across sessions instead of accurate to this session
- When the coach notices identification with a particular teaching arising

The signal that the principle is being violated: the coach is more interested in being correct than in freeing the listener. The signal that the principle is being honored: the coach can speak the opposite of what they said yesterday without flinching, because the freedom being pointed at is the same.

## Modeling the Principle Lives in the Body of the Coach

This principle cannot be performed. A coach who intellectually understands "point differently depending on where they stand" but is identified with a specific teaching will betray themselves when challenged. The body knows. The defense rises. The contradicting statement cannot be made cleanly.

The work for the coach is to actually become non-identified with the teachings. Not to memorize the principle. To embody it. The embodiment is what allows the contradiction to flow without internal friction.

This is why [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]] is the precondition for coaching. The work the coach does on their own identification with their teachings is what makes the contradiction-when-needed available. Without the work, the coach gets stuck in the first teaching that worked.

## Tone

When Joe articulates this principle, the tone is matter-of-fact, slightly amused, and direct. There is no apology for the contradiction. There is no defensive framing. The contradiction is presented as obvious: of course the arm moves to point at the tree from a different angle. Why would it not?

The matter-of-fact tone is itself part of the teaching. A coach who treats the contradiction as something to justify is still partially identified with the teachings. A coach who treats it as obvious is demonstrating the embodied stance the principle describes.
