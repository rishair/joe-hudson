# If You Do X to Your Kid, You're Telling Them It's Okay to Do X to You

## The Principle

Whatever the parent does to the kid, the parent is implicitly telling the kid it is okay to do back. The principle works in both directions and across registers — physical, emotional, relational. The kid registers the relational behavior as the template for what is permitted in the relationship, regardless of any verbal rules the parent has set.

The principle's most concrete origin: Joe and Tara with their infant daughter. Joe was nibbling on Esme as play. Tara stopped him.

"You're biting her. And I was like nibbling on you. I'd be like, and it was like playful, but she was like, you're telling her it's okay to do that." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The frame extends to anything: "If you yell at your kid, you're telling them it's okay to yell back at you. If you don't like your kid, you're telling them it's okay not to like you."

## Why This Works

The kid does not learn the rules of the relationship from what the parent says. The kid learns the rules from what the parent does. Every behavior the parent enacts is being absorbed as a model for how relationship is conducted.

This is the parenting application of [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]]. The verbal rule ("we don't yell in this house") does not override the modeled behavior (parents yelling). The behavior is the actual instruction. The verbal rule is noise around the signal.

The principle is also why [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]] works the way it does. The parent cannot hide their actual behavior from the kid, and the kid is calibrating to it constantly. Whatever shape the parent runs the relationship in is the shape the kid will run their part of the relationship in.

## The Examples

The principle plays out across many specific behaviors:

- **Yelling.** A parent who yells at their kid teaches the kid that yelling is the appropriate response to frustration in this relationship. Later, when the kid yells back, the parent is surprised. The parent should not be surprised. The kid is enacting the rule the parent installed by example.
- **Disliking.** A parent who treats the kid with low-grade disapproval — constant correction, evaluation, adjustment — teaches the kid that disapproval is the affective baseline of the relationship. The kid learns it is okay to not particularly like the parent either. See [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]] for the related distinction.
- **Biting (literally).** Joe's example. The infant is learning what kinds of contact are sanctioned. Nibbling from the parent installs nibbling as sanctioned contact, and the parent will later be bitten.
- **Lying.** A parent who tells the kid white lies, or lies about their own state ("I'm fine"), is teaching the kid that lying is acceptable in this relationship. The kid learns to lie back.
- **Stonewalling.** A parent who goes silent when upset is teaching the kid that going silent is the conflict-handling protocol. The kid will go silent.
- **Boundary-crossing.** A parent who walks into the kid's room without knocking, reads their messages, or otherwise crosses boundaries is teaching the kid that boundary-crossing is fine in this relationship.
- **Showing up.** This works in the positive direction too. A parent who consistently shows up is teaching the kid that showing up is the relationship's standard. The kid will show up.
- **Apologizing cleanly.** A parent who apologizes when they are wrong is teaching the kid that clean apology is part of how this relationship operates. The kid will apologize back.

## The Inverse Application: The Parent's Conscious Choice

The principle gives the parent a clear question: how do I want my kid to treat me when they have the autonomy to choose? Whatever the answer is, that is how the parent should treat the kid now. The parent is installing the kid's future relational style with them through the parent's current relational style with the kid.

Joe makes this explicit as a guiding question:

"I want to treat you like the way I would treat somebody like an adult that I respect." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The respect treatment is not just the right thing in itself. It is also the form of relationship Joe wants Esme to extend back to him over the course of their lifetime relationship. He is installing the long-term shape of the relationship by enacting it now.

See [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]] for the full treatment of this stance.

## The Misunderstanding This Corrects

A common parenting belief: "I can be hard on my kid because I am the parent; that does not give them permission to be hard on me, because they have to respect me as the parent."

The principle says no. The kid is not making the parent/child distinction the way the parent thinks. The kid is learning the rules of relationship and applying them. The asymmetry the parent wants (parent can yell, kid cannot) is a verbal rule. The behavioral instruction is symmetric: yelling is what happens here.

The kid may behaviorally comply with the asymmetry while young, because the parent has more power. But the kid is also absorbing yelling as a relational tool. They will use it. They will use it on the parent eventually (in the teen years or as adults). They will use it on partners, on their own kids, on colleagues. The behavior gets transmitted regardless of the verbal exemption the parent tried to install.

## What This Asks of the Parent

The principle asks the parent to treat themselves as a model whether they intended to be or not. The behavior the parent is willing to enact is the behavior the parent is implicitly endorsing. There is no way to enact a behavior and exempt the kid from learning it as available.

This is hard because parents often want to act from frustration, exhaustion, or unprocessed emotion. The principle is not "never lose it" — humans lose it. The principle is that when the parent does lose it, the parent should not be surprised when the kid later does the same, and should not pretend that the rule was different from the behavior.

The repair side: when the parent does enact something they do not want to model, naming it as such is part of the repair. "I yelled. I do not want to yell. I do not want you to yell either. I am going to work on that. I am sorry." The naming makes the behavior legible as something the parent is not endorsing, which gives the kid the chance to also treat it as not the rule.

## The Specific Case of Liking and Disliking

The principle has a particular force around the like/dislike axis, which Joe frames in the conversation:

"If you don't like your kid, you're telling them it's okay not to like you." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

Most parents will dispute that they dislike their kid. They love their kid. But "liking" in the felt-transmission sense is what the kid registers. See [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]]. The parent who transmits low-grade disapproval is being read by the kid as not liking them, regardless of how much love is present underneath. And the kid is learning that not-liking is permitted in this relationship.

The teen years are when this lands. The teen who has been transmitted "I don't like you" for years has been given implicit permission to not like the parent. When they have the autonomy to act on it, they do. This is one mechanism for the cold teen-parent relationship that older parents warn about. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]] for the full treatment.

## The Specific Case of Emotional Modeling

The principle is the foundation of [[model-emotional-movement-for-your-kids]]. The parent who wants the kid to be able to move anger has to let the kid see the parent move anger. The parent who wants the kid to be able to cry has to let the kid see the parent cry. The kid learns the relational tool by watching the parent use it.

The inverse: the parent who hides their emotions from the kid is teaching the kid that emotions are to be hidden. The parent who manages their anger through repression is teaching the kid to manage anger through repression. The verbal rule "you should feel your feelings" does not override the modeled "I do not feel mine in front of you."

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent surprised that their teen yells at them
- A parent surprised that their teen does not seem to like them
- A parent realizing they cannot ask their kid to do something the parent does not do
- A parent in conflict with their adult child where the patterns are mirror images
- A coachee recognizing their own relational style was installed by what their parents did, not what their parents said
- A parent trying to repair a relationship and recognizing they have to change the behavior, not the rules

## Tone

Joe's tone is matter-of-fact, with a moment of recognition when telling the biting story. The phrase "you're telling them it's okay to do that" came from Tara originally, and Joe credits it. The tone is not "you are a bad parent if you do this." The tone is "here is the structural fact about what kids learn." Hold that observational stance.
