# The Urgency to Be Important Is Itself the Selfishness

## The Principle

The approval-seeker often justifies the self-monitoring with a moral claim: "I don't want to be selfish, so I have to make sure my contribution is actually valuable before I make it." The structure presents the gating of one's voice as humility — modesty about whether one deserves the floor.

The principle inverts this: the requirement to be important is itself the selfishness the person is trying to avoid. The position "I should only speak if I have something valuable to say" places the speaker at the center of every interaction as the bearer of significance, the agent whose contributions must reach a threshold of worth before they are permitted. The position assumes the speaker matters enough that their inadequate contributions would damage the field. That assumption is the self-importance.

"If you think you have to be important, that's a selfishness of itself. Like anything you do, you can call selfish." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Caring What Others Think?

The inversion is exact. The position the person believes is humility (gating their voice to avoid being self-important) is the position that requires self-importance to operate (the assumption that their voice matters enough to need gating).

## What the Position Was Actually Doing

The "I shouldn't speak unless I have something valuable to say" position appears to be other-centered. It is framed as concern for the listener — not wanting to waste their time, not wanting to add noise. The framing hides what is actually happening.

What is actually happening: the speaker has constructed a tribunal that evaluates their worthiness to participate in the field. The tribunal exists in their head. The speaker is the defendant, the prosecutor, the judge, the audience. Every potential contribution is run through the tribunal before release. The tribunal is the entire field of attention.

A speaker who is not running this tribunal is paying attention to the actual field — what is happening in the room, what is being discussed, what their actual perception is. They are not the topic. The conversation is.

The principle names what the tribunal is: a self-importance machine wearing the costume of humility. The person who is exhausted from gating their voice is exhausted because they have made themselves the constant subject of an internal court. The court is the selfishness, not the speaking.

## The "Right Now You Are Being Valuable to Somebody" Frame

A specific consequence of the principle, used as the dissolving move:

"Right now you're being valuable to somebody. Right now somebody on this call is having an epiphany because you and I are having this conversation. Right now two people are fucking bored. What the fuck? Why did she even spend the time? This is how life is." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Caring What Others Think?

The frame does several things at once:

- **It dissolves the gating question.** The question "am I being valuable" has already been answered (yes, to someone). The question is no longer meaningful because the value has been produced without the gating.
- **It names reception as inherently mixed.** See [[simultaneous-reception-is-uncontrollable]]. The same act lands as epiphany in one listener and as boredom in another, at the same moment. There is no version of speaking that escapes this.
- **It strips the value-judgment of its operational meaning.** If value-versus-boredom is happening simultaneously, the question "is my contribution valuable" is not a useful filter for whether to speak. The filter is doing no work except to gate the speaking.

## The Connection to Mortality

The principle often arrives accompanied by the mortality frame:

"None of us are gonna be remembered in 100 years. Fucking none of us. And maybe one person on this call remotely might be, but 1,000 years? Nah, fuck it. Like, we're dust in the wind. What makes it that you have to be so fucking important?" -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Caring What Others Think?

The mortality frame is targeted deployment of [[acknowledging-death-compresses-life]] specifically against the self-importance machinery. The argument: the project of being important enough to deserve speaking is being run against a timeline (centuries of being remembered, of having mattered, of leaving a worthy mark) that does not exist. The system you are trying to satisfy is fictional. The dust-in-the-wind recognition does not produce despair; it produces freedom from a task that was never possible to complete.

This is not nihilism. The principle is not "nothing matters so do whatever." The principle is "the importance-machinery you are operating is producing the exhaustion you are reporting; the machinery is run against a target that does not exist; therefore the machinery can be released."

## The Speaker Who Is Not Important Is Free to Speak

The downstream consequence of accepting the principle: the speaker who has dropped the importance requirement is now free to speak. Not free in the sense of "speak constantly" — see [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] for the distinction. Free in the sense that the gating mechanism is no longer running. What the person says in any given moment is now a function of what is actually true for them to say, not a function of whether the saying has been pre-validated as worth saying.

The paradoxical consequence: the speaker who has dropped the importance-tribunal often becomes more useful to listeners, not less. The tribunaled speaker is producing managed speech filtered through the wrong question. The unmanaged speaker is producing actual speech — which is what listeners want. The dropping of the worthiness-filter is what makes the contribution worth receiving.

See [[do-it-for-yourself-to-find-purpose]] for the related principle: doing-it-for-yourself, not doing-it-for-the-audience, is what produces work that lands. The same structure operates with speech.

## Why This Is Hard to Accept

The principle is unintuitive because it inverts a teaching the person has carefully installed. "Don't be self-important" is a real teaching. The person has spent years applying it. The application has produced the gating mechanism that is now exhausting them.

The principle does not say "be self-important." It says: the gating mechanism you installed in the name of avoiding self-importance is itself a sophisticated form of self-importance. The form is harder to see because it looks like its opposite. The recognition usually arrives with a laugh — the body registers the trick before the mind articulates it.

The recognition often pairs with [[its-a-human-thing-redirect]]: the gating wasn't a moral failing. It was a strategy that solved one problem (being shown to be inadequate) by creating another (being the constant subject of internal evaluation). The recognition releases the strategy without requiring shame about having held it.

## When This Principle Is Useful

- When the presenting concern is some variant of "do I have something valuable to say"
- When the approval-seeker is justifying the self-monitoring with concern about being self-important
- When the person has been working on "not being self-centered" and the working has made them more self-centered
- When the moral frame of humility is being used to defend the gating mechanism
- When the speaker has internalized "wait until you have something to say" and is now permanently waiting
- When the question "is this worth saying" has become the gate that no contribution can pass through

## The Coaching Move

The principle is usually delivered as a question, not a statement:

- "What makes it that you have to be so fucking important?"
- "Who are you to judge if your input is valuable?"
- "What's the problem with showing up just to be heard?"

The question form is necessary because the principle is structurally hard to install as a claim. Claims about humility get evaluated against the listener's existing humility-teaching and rejected as suspicious. Questions force the listener to look at the actual machinery they are running, and the machinery does not survive being looked at directly.

After the question lands, the move is often [[look-me-in-the-eye-and-name-the-controlling-wish]] — the somatic landing form. The intellectual recognition is not enough. The body has to register that the position of "I must be valuable before I speak" is absurd at the level of felt-experience. The eye-contact move does that.

## What This Is Not

- Not permission to be careless with others' time. The principle is about the gating mechanism, not about being deliberately wasteful.
- Not "what you say doesn't matter." It often does matter, both more and less than the speaker can predict. The principle is about removing the unanswerable pre-filter, not about treating speech as inconsequential.
- Not a denial of skill or craft. A practiced speaker may produce more landing-contributions than an unpracticed one. The principle is about the gating machinery, not about the development of skill.
- Not anti-modesty. Modesty as a felt-orientation is fine. Modesty as a gating mechanism that requires constant self-evaluation is the structure being addressed.

## Tone

The principle is delivered with energy and a certain irreverence. The "what makes it that you have to be so fucking important" is not an accusation; it is the friendly puncturing of a position that the speaker has been treating with unwarranted seriousness. The expletive is part of the delivery — it dissolves the moral seriousness that the position has been wearing.

Delivered solemnly, the principle would land as the very self-importance it is critiquing. Delivered with the lightness of "we're all dust in the wind," it lands as freedom rather than as critique.
