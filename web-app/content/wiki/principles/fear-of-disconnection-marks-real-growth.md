# Fear of Not Being Able to Relate to Others Is a Marker of Real Growth

## The Principle

When a person is about to step into a meaningfully different way of being, one of the most common fears that surfaces is that they will not be able to relate to other people from the new place. This fear is not a sign that the shift is dangerous or wrong. It is a sign that the shift is large enough to matter.

"One of the marks of big movements in ourselves is that we get scared that we won't be able to relate to other people. In my journey now, every time that thought comes up, I'm like, ooh, it's gonna get good." -- Joe Hudson, Am I Being Authentic Or Fake?

The reframe Joe offers is to use the appearance of this fear as a positive signal. The fear shows up specifically when a shift is real. Real shifts threaten the existing social configuration: relationships were set up around the old self, and the new self does not fit them in the same way. The body knows this and registers it as fear of disconnection. The fear is accurate. The conclusion the fear delivers — "and therefore I should not change" — is what needs to be reframed.

## Why the Fear Appears

Identity is constructed in relationship. The current configuration of self has been negotiated with the people around you over years. When the configuration changes, the negotiated arrangement is disrupted. The relationships that depended on the old configuration will either renegotiate, transform, or end. The body knows this in advance and registers it as fear.

The fear also tracks the fact that the new self is, for a period of time, less fluent than the old self. The person learning to operate from a new place is slower, less articulate, less able to perform the old social moves. See [[slowness-after-the-drop]]. The person can tell they are less socially capable in the immediate term. The fear is partly accurate about that.

What the fear gets wrong is the long-run conclusion. The temporary social awkwardness of operating from a new place is not the end-state. The end-state is being able to operate from the new place with people who are also there — and the person finding those people, or transforming the existing relationships into ones that can hold the new configuration.

## The Reframe

Joe's reframe inverts the signal: every time the fear of not relating comes up, treat it as the announcement that something good is about to happen. The fear is the marker that a real shift is in progress.

"Every time that thought comes up, I'm like, ooh, it's gonna get good." -- Joe Hudson

The reframe is operationally usable in the moment. When the fear appears, instead of asking "should I really do this?" the person asks "what is the shift this fear is announcing?" The fear becomes intelligence about the size of the move, not a vote against the move.

## What to Do When the Fear Appears

1. **Notice the fear specifically as fear of disconnection.** Not generic fear. Not fear of failure. The specific fear of not being able to relate to people you love after the shift.

2. **Treat it as a marker, not a warning.** The fear is reporting accurately that the shift is real. The conclusion "therefore don't change" is the part to question.

3. **Acknowledge what the fear is right about.** The relationships will change. Some of them will not survive the change in the same form. The temporary social awkwardness is real. The fear is not delusional.

4. **Reject what the fear is wrong about.** Connection from the new place is more available, not less, once the temporary period passes. The connections that survive the shift are deeper. The new connections that become possible from the new place were not available from the old place.

5. **Use the appearance of the fear as a forward signal.** "Ooh, it's gonna get good" is not a denial of the fear. It is the choice to read the fear as an indicator of the size of the shift in progress.

## What Happens If the Fear Is Believed

If the fear is treated as a warning rather than a marker, the person aborts the shift. They return to the old configuration. The relationships are preserved in their original form. The voice that was pointing at the work that wanted to happen continues to point at it, because the work has not happened.

Over time, the cost of aborted shifts accumulates. The person is increasingly aware they are not living from their fullest self. The relationships that were preserved are preserved in a configuration that does not include the parts of the person the work would have allowed to come forward. The "preserving the relationships" turns out to have been preserving a specific shape of the relationships at the cost of the person.

The fear of disconnection that aborted the shift produced exactly the disconnection it was trying to prevent: the person is now disconnected from themselves in order to remain connected to others in a specific way that requires the self-disconnection.

This is structurally the same as [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]: avoiding the disconnection produces the disconnection in a different form.

## The Connection to Aloneness

The reframe pairs with the work on [[the-more-i-grow-the-more-alone-i-feel]]. The aloneness that appears as a result of the shift is a real perception. The fear of that aloneness is what holds people back from the shift. Reframing the fear as a marker that something good is about to happen is one of the moves that allows the shift to complete and the aloneness to be met rather than avoided.

The two are sequenced: the fear of disconnection is what appears before the shift; the experience of aloneness is what appears after the shift completes. Both are part of the same process. Treating either as a problem to be eliminated keeps the work from happening. Treating both as accurate perceptions of real territory allows the work.

## When Joe Uses This

In the moment when a person is about to do something that would constitute a real change in how they show up, and the person verbalizes some version of "but then I won't be able to connect with anyone / will I still have anyone / what if no one understands me." Joe names the fear as a growth marker and offers the inversion: this is the signal it's about to get good.

The principle also operates as a teaching offered before the fear arrives, in coaching sessions where the work is approaching a meaningful shift. Forewarning the person about the fear's appearance lets them recognize it as a marker when it shows up rather than mistaking it for a verdict.

## Tone

Light, almost playful, with conviction. The reframe is offered the way a guide who has crossed this terrain many times might mention a landmark. There is no urgency about whether the person believes the reframe in the moment. The lightness is what makes the reframe land — if it were delivered as a doctrine, the person would brace against it.

The phrase "ooh, it's gonna get good" carries the tone in compressed form. It is not solemn. It is not anxious. It is the response of someone who has learned to read the appearance of this specific fear as good news.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When a person is in the middle of dropping a long-held mask and worries no one will recognize them
- When the work has touched a real shift and the person preemptively defends against it with social-loss fear
- When a person says they should go away and be alone to integrate (and the going-away is partly an avoidance of the social-fear)
- When [[theres-a-voice-that-says-im-fake-around-everyone]] is being worked and the person worries that operating without the mask means losing connection
- When a person is at the threshold of a transformation and asks "but what happens to my relationships?"

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- Toward staying with the shift rather than aborting it
- Toward [[the-more-i-grow-the-more-alone-i-feel]] for the aloneness that appears after the shift completes
- Toward [[slowness-after-the-drop]] for the temporary disorientation that accompanies operating from a new place
- Toward [[fuck-them-if-they-hold-it-against-you]] for the move that allows the person to keep going even when specific relationships do not survive the shift
- Toward the discovery that the relationships that survive the shift are deeper, and new relationships become available that were not available from the old configuration
