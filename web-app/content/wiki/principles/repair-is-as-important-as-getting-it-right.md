# Repair Is as Important as Getting It Right

## The Principle

The parenting frame most people enter with: do not mess up your kid. Be the perfect parent. Do not make mistakes that will scar them. The frame quietly produces enormous parental anxiety, because mistakes are inevitable, and the frame treats them as failure.

Joe's reframe: you will undoubtedly mess up your kids. The repair you make afterward is as important to the child's development as the getting-it-right. Maybe more important. The perfect parent who never makes repairs is not actually giving the kid more than the imperfect parent who repairs cleanly. They may be giving them less.

"That repair is just as important as getting it right. If you get it perfect and you never make repair, I don't think that's as good for a kid as messing up and making repair, showing them how that's done, showing them that we're all human and that we get to love our mistakes and that we get to own them. And then that lets us grow. I think that's just as important as being a perfect parent. And that opportunity only comes because we will undoubtedly fuck up our kids in some way." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The reframe inverts the anxiety. The mistakes are not the threat to the child. The mistakes are the conditions for one of the most important things the child needs to learn: how humans actually live with their imperfection.

## What the Kid Learns From the Repair

A child raised by a parent who never visibly messes up does not learn how to recover from messing up themselves. They learn that mistakes are unspeakable, that the right response to imperfection is to pretend it didn't happen, and that adult life involves an unbroken performance of competence.

A child raised by a parent who visibly messes up and visibly repairs learns:

- **Mistakes are survivable.** The world does not end when a person screws up. The parent demonstrates this by surviving theirs.
- **Ownership is possible.** The parent owns the mistake. The owning is itself a skill the child learns by watching.
- **Repair is a real thing.** The relationship can be ruptured and rebuilt. This installs the felt sense that relational damage does not have to be permanent.
- **Self-love includes loving the parts that mess up.** "We get to love our mistakes." The parent who loves their own mistakes teaches the child to love theirs. The parent who hates their own mistakes teaches the child to hate theirs.
- **Growth requires acknowledgment.** "Owning them lets us grow." The repair is upstream of the growth, not the cleanup after it.

The unmistaking parent is not delivering on a higher standard. They are withholding the lessons that the messing-up-and-repairing parent is teaching by living.

## The Depth That Becomes Possible

A specific kind of relational depth becomes available through repair that does not become available through perfection. The vulnerability required for the parent to clearly see and name what they did, and the child receiving the naming without having to take care of the parent's guilt, produces a connection that perfect-parenting does not.

Joe's example from his teenage daughter Esme: she came to him for advice about an attachment dynamic with her boyfriend. He recognized in the moment that the dynamic was his own imprint from when she was three. He stopped, looked at her, and said: "That's not his fault. That's not your fault. That's me. I taught you that attachment thing. That was what I did when you were a kid. And I'm so sorry." See [[joe-apologizes-to-esme-for-the-3-year-old-imprint]] for the full story.

The outcome surprised him. The pattern in her dissolved. The relationship deepened. And then, the line that the rest of the principle hangs on:

"I remember thinking, I kind of wish I would have fucked her up more. I want more of those moments." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The line is not a wish for harm. It is the recognition that the depth produced by clean repair is not available through the path of having-never-made-mistakes. The parent who can hold this frame stops fighting against their own imperfection and starts noticing what becomes possible when the imperfection is met with clean repair.

## What "Clean Repair" Requires

The repair that produces the development is specific. It is not generic guilt, generic apology, or self-flagellation that requires the kid to reassure the parent. The requirements:

1. **Specificity.** "When you were 3 years old, I did this specific thing, and that installed this specific pattern, and I see it running now." Not "I'm sorry I was a bad parent." The specificity is what makes the repair land. See [[joe-apologizes-to-esme-for-the-3-year-old-imprint]].
2. **No extraction of forgiveness.** The parent does not ask the child to make the parent feel better about the mistake. The parent does not require the child to say "it's okay." The apology is the parent's, not transactional. See [[upright-apology]].
3. **No shame in the parent's delivery.** A parent who delivers the apology while collapsing into shame re-installs the lesson that mistakes are unsurvivable. The clean delivery comes from upright posture — the parent can see what they did, regret it, and still love themselves through it.
4. **Loving the mistake.** "We get to love our mistakes." This is the modeling piece. The child watches the parent's relationship to the parent's own imperfection. If the parent treats it with love, the child learns to treat their own with love. If the parent hates it, the child learns to hate theirs.

## The Frequency Question

The principle does not say to make mistakes intentionally. Mistakes will happen on their own. The principle says: when they do, the repair is the practice. The frequency of mistakes is not the variable to manage; the quality of repair is.

In practice, the parents who internalize this stop white-knuckling perfect parenting and end up making fewer of the small chronic mistakes that come from over-management. The looser, more honest stance produces fewer eruptions because the parent is not always at the edge of their capacity. The mistakes that do happen get repaired. The total damage is lower and the depth is higher than the white-knuckle version.

This is the parallel to [[loving-attention-vs-consistency]]: the parent grading themselves on never-messing-up is grading the wrong variable. The parent grading themselves on repair-when-it-happens is grading the actual one.

## Repair as the Model for the Kid's Self-Relationship

The repair is also where the kid learns how to be with their own mistakes. The kid watches the parent fail, watches the parent name the failure cleanly, watches the parent return to themselves, and learns the choreography. Decades later, when the kid has failed at something as an adult, the inner template for what to do is the parent's template.

A kid raised by a parent who repairs cleanly arrives at adult mistakes with the felt sense that they can name what they did, own it, repair what can be repaired, and continue to love themselves through it. A kid raised by a parent who either hid mistakes or collapsed into them arrives at adult mistakes with the felt sense that mistakes are catastrophic and that the right response is either denial or self-attack.

The repair is not just about the specific relational rupture being healed. The repair is the template installation for how the child will treat themselves for the rest of their life.

## The Buddhist Resonance

Nathan, in the conversation, surfaces a connection to the Buddhist principle of not resisting things. The principle here lives in the same space. The parent who resists the inevitability of messing up their kid spends enormous energy on a fight they will lose. The parent who allows the messing-up while committing to clean repair is in a different relationship with the work — one that includes their own humanity rather than fighting it.

"The opportunity that creates a lot of beauty and also is super important because no one in this world is perfect. And so to model what to do with our imperfections is important." -- Nathan Baschez, How to Be a Parent

The principle is not specifically Buddhist, but the underlying structure — accept what is, work with it skillfully — is shared.

- A parent of a teen or adult child carrying guilt about earlier parenting they did not repair
- A coachee describing a parent who never apologized and the long-term cost of the unrepaired pattern
- A new parent overwhelmed by the standard of perfection

## Tone

A little wistful, slightly funny. The principle is delivered with the lightness of a parent who has done it long enough to find the humor in the fact that the mistakes ended up being some of the best material. Joe's line about wishing he would have messed up more is funny because it inverts the standard anxiety so completely. Hold both: the seriousness of the work and the lightness of the relationship to one's own imperfection.
