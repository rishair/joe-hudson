# If Trust Cannot Arrive, the Grief Isn't Complete

## The Principle

When someone has experienced a loss and reports that they cannot now trust -- cannot trust a new partner after a breakup, cannot trust people generally after a betrayal, cannot trust a market after a crash -- the cause is structurally upstream of the trust itself. The grief is not complete. Trust cannot arrive while the grief is still active. Try to install trust on top of incomplete grief and the trust will not hold.

The diagnostic move is therefore not to work on trust. The diagnostic move is to recognize that more grief is needed.

"The thing that struck me in the question was that if somebody's grieved and said, 'now I have to find trust,' I'd be like, 'you need to grieve some more.' Because what I notice is that when the grief moves all the way through, there's nothing to be scared of. So when I see people having a hard time trusting after a breakup or a marriage or a bad business partnership or something like that, it typically means that they haven't grieved the whole thing." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## Why Incomplete Grief Blocks Trust

Trust requires a felt sense that the present moment is not being run by the past wound. When grief is still active, the body is still partially in the original event. The present that is being offered for trust is not the actual present; it is the past being projected forward as the next instance of the same wound. The mind reports this as "I can't trust." The body is reporting "I am still in the event that has not finished."

The block on trust is therefore not a character feature, a maturity failure, or a wisdom deficit. It is the structural consequence of grief that has not completed. The person trying to install trust on top of incomplete grief is asking the system to do something that is mechanically not yet possible.

When the grief actually completes, the system is no longer projecting the past forward. The present becomes available as actually present. Trust does not need to be installed. It arises naturally, because there is nothing being substituted in its place.

"When the grief moves all the way through, there's nothing to be scared of." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The "nothing to be scared of" is structural. The scariness was the past wound still active. With the wound metabolized, the scariness has nowhere to live. Trust does not have to be manufactured. It is what is left when the wound is no longer running.

## The Diagnostic in Live Coaching

When someone arrives saying "I can't trust again after [the loss]," the question to hold internally is not "how do we build trust?" The question is "is the grief actually complete?"

The signs that the grief is incomplete:

- The person still has strong emotional charge when describing the loss
- The person is hyperscanning the new partner / person / situation for signs of the old wound
- The person describes themselves as having "moved on" but the body shows the original event is still active
- The person reports thinking about the loss constantly or never thinking about it (both can be signs of incomplete grief)
- The person has been telling the same story about the loss for years and the story does not shift
- The person reports that trust feels like a decision they cannot make (decisions about feeling are usually signs of unfelt emotion)
- The person feels protective scanning is necessary even in situations where the original threat is not present

If any of these are running, the work is not on trust. The work is on the grief that has not completed.

## The Coaching Response

The response is to redirect from the trust question to the grief work. Not as a refusal of the trust question, but as the structural answer to it.

The redirection sounds like: "What I notice is that you're asking how to trust again, and underneath that is some grief that probably hasn't moved all the way through. Let's look at the grief first. When the grief is actually complete, the trust question will likely answer itself."

The person may resist this. The cultural framing of post-loss recovery says: grieve for a while, then move on, then trust again. The redirection inverts this: grief is not the thing that happens before trust; grief is what produces the conditions for trust. The trust does not have to be worked on directly. The grief has to be allowed to finish.

See [[grief-as-identity-dismantling]] for what the grief is actually doing structurally, and [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]] for why most grief is not finished even when people think it is. The grief that contains the anger, the helplessness, the denial, and the multiple identity-deaths inside the original event usually has not had time and space to move through all of those. The sadness gets the public acknowledgment. The anger and the helplessness often do not. Until they do, the grief is structurally incomplete, and the trust is structurally blocked.

## The Specific Cases

The principle applies across loss-types:

- **Breakup or divorce.** The person who cannot trust the next partner has incomplete grief about the prior one. The grief often includes anger that was never expressed, helplessness about how the relationship ended, denial about what the partner actually was. See [[i-cant-get-over-this-loss]].
- **Bad business partnership.** The person who cannot trust the next collaborator has incomplete grief about the betrayal or failure of the prior partnership. The grief includes the loss of the imagined version of the partnership and the recognition that the time, money, or trust invested cannot be recovered.
- **Narcissistic abuse.** The person who cannot trust after a narcissistic relationship has incomplete grief about the relationship that was projected onto the actual relationship that was happening. See [[how-do-i-stay-open-after-narcissistic-abuse]]. The hypervigilance ([[defensive-scanning-for-the-feared-threat]]) is a symptom of the unfinished grief, not a security feature.
- **Major loss of a loved one.** The person who cannot trust life or the world after a death has incomplete grief about the death. The trust will not return until the grief moves through all of its components.
- **Market crash, layoff, or institutional betrayal.** The person who cannot trust the market, the company, or the institution has incomplete grief about the prior loss. The "I'll never invest again" / "I'll never work for a startup again" / "I'll never trust an institution again" is the unfinished grief speaking.

In each case, the structural answer is the same: the trust will not return through work on trust. The trust will return through completing the grief.

## Why People Resist This Answer

The grief work is harder than the trust work. The trust work can be done at a conceptual level: think about trust, decide to trust, take small trust-actions, etc. The grief work has to be felt, and the unfelt part of the grief is unfelt for a reason. The person has been avoiding it because feeling it is intense.

The conventional framing also resists this answer. The cultural script says: grieve, then move on. The implicit message is that grief should be finishable on a normal timeline, and that grief that goes too long is itself a pathology. This script makes the grief-completion answer hard to receive because the person believes their grief should already be over.

The reality is that grief takes the time it takes. The completion is felt-completion, not calendar-completion. A grief that has had two years to move through partially is still incomplete if the parts that have not moved are still active. The body knows when the grief is finished. The trust returns when the body's report is "this is done."

See [[engineering-heartbreak-to-avoid-heartbreak]] for the related anti-pattern: the person who, in trying to avoid being heartbroken again, creates the conditions for the same heartbreak by treating the new situation as if it were the old one.

## Connection to Order of Operations

This principle is a specific instance of the broader pattern in [[recognition-feeling-balance-then-call-emerges]]. Trust is one of the things that becomes obvious once the emotional component has been fully felt and the system has returned to balance. Trying to install trust before the emotion has moved is the same structural error as trying to find the call before the pattern has been felt.

The general form: most of what the conscious mind tries to install -- trust, clarity, decisiveness, joy -- is downstream of emotional completion. The installation attempts fail because the substrate is not yet ready. The substrate becomes ready by feeling what has been unfelt. Once it is ready, the installation is not needed; the thing arises on its own.

## Tone

Direct and certain. Joe delivers this principle with no hedging. The question "how do I trust again?" has a specific answer: grieve more. The certainty is not because the work is easy. The certainty is because the structure is reliable. The person who completes the grief will find the trust available. The person who tries to install trust on incomplete grief will not.

There is also a quiet protectiveness in the framing. The person who is asking how to trust is often suffering from the failed installation attempts. Joe is not adding shame to the failure. He is reframing the failure as evidence of where the actual work is. The redirect is the relief.
