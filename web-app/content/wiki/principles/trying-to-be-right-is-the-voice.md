# Trying to Be Right Is the Voice

## Definition

The most subtle form of the [[voice-in-the-head]] does not sound like criticism. It sounds like integrity. "What would the right thing to say be?" "What would the highest version of me do here?" "What's the correct response?" This sounds noble. It is the same voice in a costume.

"That's how the voice gets more and more subtle. Right? Because it sounds, it sounds like a great thing. Like, what would my highest integrity self say? Sounds like a, like a great thing, but it's still trying to get it right." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The fear underneath is the same as crude self-criticism: fear of being wrong. The disguise is what makes it persistent. The crude critic gets noticed and resisted. The integrity-sounding critic gets followed.

## How to Spot It

The signature is the orientation toward a "right" answer. Whenever the internal question is "what's the right thing to do?" — even when the question seems pious — the voice is operating. The fear of being wrong is dressed up as caring about being good.

A related signature: the question "what would [admired person] do here?" When the question is genuine inquiry, it produces movement. When it produces paralysis (trying to perform the answer the admired person would give), it is the voice. The admired person is being used as the new should.

Joe's example in the transcript: Brett says "I think, what would Joe say?" and finds himself unable to come up with anything — meanwhile his own truth is right there waiting to be spoken. The "what would Joe say" question is the voice in a flattering costume.

## The Alternative: What Do I Want?

The replacement for "what's the right thing to say?" is "what do I want?" The first question routes through a fictional standard. The second routes through actual present experience.

"I find the much neater trick is just to say, what do I want? And then speak into that want, which is far more vulnerable than trying to be right." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The shift produces different content. Where the right-thing question produces careful, hedged, performative speech, the want question produces direct, vulnerable, contactful speech. The want question also lands differently for the other person because it is honest.

See [[the-want]] and [[wants-over-shoulds]] for the broader treatment.

## Why "Being Right" Is the Voice's Endgame

The voice has always been about safety. In the crude form, safety means avoiding criticism: "don't say anything that will make them mad." In the subtle form, safety means avoiding the criticism of being wrong: "say the right thing so no one can criticize this." Same goal. Different surface.

The deepest version of this trap: the search for moral correctness becomes an avoidance of the vulnerability of just being a person with wants. The seeker of the right answer never has to put their actual want into the room. Their actions are always defensible. They are also always at one remove from contact.

See [[being-right-college-story]] for Joe's history with this pattern.

## The Freedom in Already Being Wrong

The release point Joe describes: "life is really great once you realize you're already wrong."

"That's why I said to you, life is great when you know you're wrong, because then you don't have to try to be right, and then you're just operating from that place naturally, that place of integrity naturally." -- Joe Hudson, Who is the Voice in Your Head?

The mechanics: as long as being right is the goal, every interaction has high stakes. Any mistake is a failure of the project. If you have already accepted that you are wrong about plenty of things — that being wrong is a baseline condition of being a person — the stakes drop. Wrongness becomes information rather than catastrophe. Action becomes possible.

Ironically, integrity also becomes easier. The person trying to act with integrity to avoid being wrong rarely makes the most integrous moves (those are usually the most vulnerable, with the highest risk of being judged wrong). The person who has accepted being wrong can make the integrous move because they no longer need it to be defensible.

## Being Right Is an Impossible Game

The voice's standard is consensus: the right thing to say is the thing both your inner critic and others would agree was right. This is impossible. Different people have different standards. Different parts of you have different standards. There is no statement that would satisfy all judges.

"Being right is trying to say or do the thing that is perceived by both the voice in your head and others as everybody agrees that it's right, which is an impossible task." -- Brett Kistler (responding to Joe), Who is the Voice in Your Head?

Recognizing the impossibility takes the goal off the table. What replaces it: speaking what is true and wanted, and finding out what happens.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They ask themselves "what's the right thing to do?" in many situations without realizing this is a form of self-criticism.
- They notice paralysis around important conversations: they cannot speak because they have not yet found the "right" thing to say.
- They are running a "what would [admired figure] do?" question that produces silence rather than clarity.
- They are mistaking carefulness for integrity.
- They notice that their most authentic moments come when they have given up on getting it right.
