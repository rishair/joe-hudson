# Focus on 3-5 Leverage Principles, Not Everything

## The Principle

Non-perfectionists who do high-quality work do not try to optimize every variable. They identify three to five load-bearing principles for the work and focus all their attention there. Everything else they let fall out automatically as a downstream effect of the principles being right. The perfectionist trying to make everything perfect is structurally unable to find the leverage point because their attention is spread across all the variables. The non-perfectionist's narrower focus is paradoxically what produces the better result.

"If I'm going to do something in marketing, I'm gonna go to like 3 or 4 principles. If I'm gonna run a company, I'm gonna go 3, 4, or 5 principles. And what those principles are are things that I know that if I get them right, everything else is gonna work out in the wash. Everything else is gonna fall out of that." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism Creates the Chaos it's trying to Prevent

The principle is operational. It tells the listener what to actually do in place of perfectionism: not "stop caring about quality," not "lower your standards," but "find the three to five things that are load-bearing and focus there." The leverage-principles approach produces better work than the perfectionism approach does because it concentrates attention on what actually matters.

## Why This Works Structurally

Two mechanisms operate at the same time:

**Attention is finite.** A person trying to optimize twenty variables gives roughly 5% of their attention to each. A person focused on three variables gives roughly 33% to each. The same person doing the same work produces visibly higher quality on the three than on the twenty because the attention-per-variable is higher. The perfectionist's belief that they can optimize everything ignores the actual cost of distributing attention. The non-perfectionist's choice of three is the recognition that attention has to be concentrated to be effective.

**The right three propagate.** If the three or five chosen are actually load-bearing, getting them right produces everything else as a downstream effect. The marketing function that gets connection-with-customer right does not need to micromanage the email cadence; the right cadence emerges from the connection. The company that gets the right principles right does not need to specify every employee's behavior; the right behavior emerges from operating in the principles. The leverage-principles approach uses the actual structure of the work -- some variables matter more, some matter less, and the high-leverage ones propagate to the low-leverage ones automatically.

The perfectionist approach inverts both mechanisms. It distributes attention thinly across many variables, and it does not exploit the propagation effect because it treats every variable as independent. The result is a lot of effort spread across all the variables with little of the propagation that would have made the effort efficient.

## Joe's Three Worked Examples

Joe gives three concrete examples in the perfectionism conversation:

**Marketing: connection with the customer.** Rather than trying to perfect every marketing artifact, the leverage principle is connection with the customer. If you feel deeply connected with the customer, the product gets better, the customer-service gets better, the customers feel cared about, the messaging finds its tone. Everything downstream of "I am in connection with the customer" gets pulled in the right direction by the upstream variable being right.

The perfectionist marketing function loses track of the connection because attention is spent on getting the email script right, getting the response time right, getting the voice quality of customer service right, getting the timing right. Each variable is being optimized in isolation. The actual leverage point (connection with the customer) has been lost in the optimization. "If you're trying to get it perfect, perfect customer service, you've lost track of the fact that connection is actually the main thing."

**Running a company: three to five principles.** Joe does not specify which three to five in the perfectionism conversation, but the structure is the same. The company runs on a small set of load-bearing principles. The principles are the high-leverage variables. Everything else falls out of those being held.

See [[two-things-ceo-must-get-right]] for one specific version of this -- two things a CEO must get right: build great relationships, make great decisions. Two leverage variables, and the rest follows. The "rest follows" is the propagation effect. See also [[principles-goals-metrics]] for the broader framework that organizes how principles relate to goals and metrics.

**Coaching videos: heart open, follow them, no agenda.** The three variables Joe organizes his own coaching videos around: is my heart open, am I following them, am I making sure I'm not trying to get them anywhere. Three connection-related variables. Everything else (video quality, coaching effectiveness, audience response) is downstream. See the section on Joe's coaching-video example in [[connection-over-perfection]] for the canonical articulation.

**Marriage: love.** The contrasting case Joe uses: trying to have the perfect marriage. The perfectionist marriage focuses on getting everything right -- the house, the schedule, the parenting, the social presentation -- and loses track of the actual leverage variable, which is love. The leverage-principles approach to marriage focuses on love and lets the rest follow. "Who cares what the house looks like if you forgot love?"

## How This Differs From Standard-Lowering

The leverage-principles approach is not the same as lowering standards. The three to five principles are held at a high standard. The other variables are not low-standard -- they are downstream of the principles, which means they get a reasonable level of attention but not optimization-grade attention. The work as a whole is high-quality because the high-leverage points are getting the attention they deserve, not because every detail is being perfected.

The perfectionist often hears "focus on three things" as "neglect the other things" and defends the perfectionism on the grounds that the other things matter. The defense misses the structural point. The other things do matter; they matter as downstream effects of the leverage principles. Getting the leverage principles right produces good versions of the other things. Trying to perfect the other things independently does not produce as good a version because the attention to the other things has been pulled away from the leverage principles that would have produced them well.

The two approaches produce different costs and different results:

- **Perfectionist approach:** high attention to all variables; high cognitive load; slow iteration; the work often fails to find the leverage point; the perfectionism is exhausting and the quality is lower than expected.
- **Leverage-principles approach:** high attention to three to five variables; manageable cognitive load; fast iteration; the work is organized around what actually matters; the quality compounds because the leverage points are improving and they propagate.

## Why This Is Hard for Perfectionists to Adopt

The perfectionist's resistance to the leverage-principles approach is not stupid. Several specific objections arrive:

**"What about the other variables?"** The perfectionist worries that the un-focused variables will be poorly handled. The answer is that they will be handled as downstream effects, which usually means handled well enough. But the worry is not silly -- some downstream variables genuinely need attention, and the leverage-principles approach requires the discernment to notice when an exceptional case arises.

**"How do I know I picked the right three?"** The perfectionist worries that the chosen leverage principles might not actually be the high-leverage ones. The answer is iteration: try three, see what propagates, adjust. The perfectionist tendency is to want the right three to be guaranteed in advance, which is not possible. The iterative selection of the principles is itself part of the work.

**"This feels like I'm not doing enough."** The perfectionist's habit is to feel that they are not doing enough unless they are working on everything. The leverage-principles approach feels lazy by comparison. The feeling is the perfectionism objecting to its own absence; the result usually disconfirms the feeling within a few weeks once the propagation effects become visible.

**"What if someone notices I'm not optimizing X?"** The perfectionist has been organizing partly to forestall criticism. Focusing on three leverage principles means there is more visible territory in which someone could find something to criticize. The non-perfectionist's response is that the criticism is the cost of doing the work well; the perfectionist's response is to try to eliminate the territory for criticism, which is what produces the over-optimization.

These objections are addressable but they do not go away easily. The transition from perfectionism to leverage-principles often takes time and several rounds of evidence that the propagation effect actually works.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not a recommendation to ignore most of the variables in the work. It is a recommendation to attend to most of the variables as downstream effects rather than as direct targets. The variables still get handled. They just get handled through the leverage principles, not in isolation.

It is not a license to choose the easy three. The leverage principles are the high-leverage ones, which are usually also the hardest ones to operate on directly. "Connection with the customer" is harder than "the email cadence" because connection is qualitative and ongoing while cadence is a knob you can set. The perfectionist who picks easy variables and calls them leverage principles has missed the structural point.

It is not the same as having no standards. The standards are high on the three to five principles. The standards on the downstream variables are "reasonable given the propagation" -- which is usually a high enough standard for the work to be good.

It is not a one-time selection. The leverage principles for a piece of work or a function evolve as the work evolves. The three Joe focuses on for coaching videos might be different in two years if the work has shifted shape. The principle is the orientation toward few-load-bearing-variables; the specific variables themselves are revisable.

## How to Use This in Coaching

For a leader or individual stuck in perfectionism, the question that opens this principle is:

- "What are the three to five things that if you get them right, everything else would fall out automatically?"
- "If you could only focus on three variables in this work, which three would they be?"
- "What is the actual leverage point here that all the detail-optimization has been distracting from?"

The question forces the person to look for the structural high-leverage variables rather than to keep optimizing the surface variables. The looking itself is often productive even before the variables are chosen, because the question makes visible the assumption-of-equal-importance that the perfectionism had been operating on.

For leaders specifically, the principle pairs well with [[principles-goals-metrics]] -- the leverage principles are the principles layer of that framework, which sits underneath the goals and metrics. The principles being clear is what makes the goals and metrics useful. The principles being absent is what makes the goals and metrics into a perfectionism trap.

## Tone

Practical, almost relieved. The principle is delivered as good news -- the work is actually simpler than perfectionism has been making it. There are three or five things that matter; the rest is downstream. The person can stop carrying the load of optimizing twenty variables and start carrying the lighter load of three or five.

The tone shifts when explaining the propagation effect. The propagation is the structural reason the smaller list of variables produces a better result than the longer list. Without the propagation, the principle would just be "do less." With the propagation, the principle is "do the right things, and the wrong things take care of themselves."
