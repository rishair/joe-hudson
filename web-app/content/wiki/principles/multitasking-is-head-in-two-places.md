# Multitasking Is Head in Two Places

## The Principle

What people call multitasking is two distinct things, both of which degrade output. The first is doing two tasks simultaneously, which is actually rapid context-switching between two tasks (the brain does not run them in parallel; it alternates fast, paying a switching tax on each handoff). The second, and the one usually missed, is doing one task while the head is on a different task -- the body is in one place and the attention is somewhere else.

The second form is more pervasive and more invisible. The person believes they are single-tasking. They are not. They are running the visible task with their hands and running a second task in their head. The second task is consuming attention. The visible task is being done with degraded presence. Both tasks suffer.

"Even if you're not multitasking by doing two things at once, you're multitasking by having your head two places at once, or having your head in a different place than your heart." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

## The Two Forms

**Form 1: Two tasks simultaneously.** The studies are clear. The brain does not run two cognitive tasks in parallel. It alternates. Each alternation costs time (the switching tax). The total time for both tasks done "simultaneously" is longer than the sum of the two tasks done sequentially. The accuracy on both is lower. The output quality on both is degraded.

"The studies on multitasking are pretty clear that multitasking is not as effective as doing one thing at a time." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

This is the well-known version. Most people will agree with it in theory and continue to do it in practice. The agreement does not produce the behavior change.

**Form 2: One task with the head elsewhere.** The driver is taking the kid to school. The body is driving. The hands are on the wheel. The eyes are on the road. The head is on the carpet cleaner that needs to be scheduled, the meeting that is at 11, the report that is due Friday. The visible task is single-task. The invisible task is the second simultaneous activity.

"Telling yourself that you have to be doing something, being constantly under attack in your head, especially do something that you can't do, like, oh, you're driving your kid to school, but you're also thinking about the fact that you have to get the carpets cleaned and blah, blah, blah, blah, blah. But you can't do it because you're driving your kid to school in that moment. That's also stressful." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

This is the hidden version. It is much more common. It runs in the background of most of modern life. The person does not notice they are doing it because there is no second visible task to point at. There is only the felt sense of being stretched, scattered, and never quite present.

## Why Form 2 Is the Bigger Problem

Form 1 is visible. The person can see they are checking email while on a call. The behavior is identifiable. The intervention is identifiable: close email during calls.

Form 2 is invisible. The person looks like they are single-tasking. They feel like they are single-tasking. The internal experience is the buzz of being-pulled-elsewhere, but they do not name this as multitasking because they cannot point at the second task. They name it as stress, as overwhelm, as "I'm tired," as "I can't focus." The actual mechanism -- two simultaneous attention-targets -- goes unnoticed.

Form 2 also produces the felt experience of [[time-scarcity]] more directly than Form 1 does. The body is doing the present task. The mind is in the future task. The mind cannot do the future task (the future task is not happening yet). The attempt to do it anyway produces the felt sense of "not enough time" -- because the mind is in a place where there is, in fact, not enough time, since the time has not arrived.

## The Body-Heart Split

Joe's specific formulation includes a third variant: head in a different place than the heart. The body is here. The head is on the next thing. The heart is on something else entirely -- the unspoken hurt from yesterday, the grief that has not been allowed, the want that has not been named. Three internal locations, one external body. The body is the only one that is fully present in the room. Everything else is somewhere else.

The signature: the person feels disconnected from what they are doing. Nothing lands. Conversations happen but no actual contact occurs. Meals are eaten without taste. Days pass without registration.

The remedy is not better time management. It is the practice of returning the head and the heart to where the body is. The body cannot be in two places. When the head and heart return to it, all three are in one location. From that location, presence is possible. Without that gathering, presence is not.

## What "Real Efficiency" Looks Like

Brett's compressed formulation of the alternative:

"The real efficiency comes from doing just the thing you're doing." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

Doing just the thing you're doing means:
- The body is here.
- The attention is here.
- The heart is here.
- The future is not being rehearsed in the background.
- The past is not being chewed in the background.
- Other tasks are not being mentally tracked in the background.
- There is one task and the whole person is on it.

The output of this state is higher quality, completed faster, with less depletion afterward. The person ends the task with more capacity than they started. This is the opposite of the multitasking state, which ends the task with less capacity than it started.

The state is also the felt-experience of flow. See [[flow-vs-managing-reality]] and [[presence-in-the-body-while-working]]. Single-presence-task is the working definition of flow when applied to one task.

## How to Notice You Are in Form 2

Several signals that the head has split from the body:

- You finish a task and cannot recall doing it.
- You have to re-read sentences while reading because the eyes moved but the attention did not.
- The conversation you are in has someone speaking and you have no idea what they just said.
- Your body arrived at the destination but you cannot remember the drive.
- You feel rushed during a task that has plenty of allotted time.
- You feel exhausted at the end of a day that, on paper, was not that hard.
- The thing you are doing is not enjoyable even though you usually enjoy this kind of thing.

Each of these is a signature of Form 2. The visible task has been completed with the body. The mind was elsewhere. The cost was paid in attention, presence, and the felt-experience of the day.

## The Intervention

The intervention is small and continuous. At any moment, ask: where is my attention right now? If the attention is not where the body is, return it. The return is the practice.

The return is not a one-time fix. The mind will wander again within seconds. The practice is the repeated return, not the single arrival. Over weeks and months, the wandering decreases and the return becomes faster. Over years, the default state begins to approach single-location presence.

The 5-minute pause every 2 hours ([[five-minutes-every-two-hours]]) is partly designed for this. In the pause, the head returns to the body. The body's location becomes the one location. From this gathered state, the next two hours of work happen with less attention-split than the previous two.

## Why People Resist the Single-Task Frame

The cultural commitment to multitasking is strong. Several reasons it persists:

- **Identity.** "I'm someone who can multitask." Letting go of this identity requires admitting the multitasking was not actually working.
- **Felt productivity.** The buzzing state of split-attention feels productive even when it is producing less. The felt-experience and the actual-output diverge. See [[overwhelm-reduces-productivity]] for the broader version of this divergence.
- **Avoidance.** Single-task presence requires being with what is here. What is here is often uncomfortable (the boring task, the difficult conversation, the underneath emotion). Multitasking is partly an escape from the present. Giving it up means staying with the present, including the parts that feel hard.
- **Fear of missing.** "If I don't keep track of all of these things at once, I'll drop one." The fear is real. The solution is to externalize the tracking (lists, calendars, notes) so the head does not have to hold it, freeing the head to be on the current task. The single-presence and the external tracking work together.

## When Someone Experiences This

- They feel scattered without being able to name what is scattering them.
- They get to the end of a day and have done many things but feel they have done nothing.
- They are present with their kids but not really present with them.
- They are in meetings but actually in the next meeting.
- They are eating but actually composing the next email.
- They think they are single-tasking and feel just as overwhelmed as when they were openly multitasking.
