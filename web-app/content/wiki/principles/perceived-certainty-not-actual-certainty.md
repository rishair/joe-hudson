# The Brain Needs Perceived Certainty, Not Actual Certainty

## The Principle

The anxious mind loops in pursuit of certainty about an outcome. The loop is unwinnable on its own terms: life does not offer 100% certainty about anything. The principle is that the brain does not actually require 100% logical certainty to function. It requires the felt sense of certainty. Most actions in daily life proceed because the person is *convinced* it will be fine, not because it has been *proven* fine.

This reframes what the anxious loop is doing. It is not failing to find real certainty. It is failing to produce the *feeling* of certainty. The chase is for the wrong thing in the wrong place.

"We don't actually need 100% certainty. We just need to be convinced that there's certainty. Meaning one person walks, goes outside of their house and walks to the grocery store. In their mind, there's 100% certainty that they're gonna get there and they're gonna get back. Intellectually, they might be like, yeah, there's a small chance, but they don't think about it... Where somebody who's scared to leave their house, they're not certain of that, right? So it's really not even that it's logically certain. It's far more about whether it's that perceived certainty is there." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

## The Grocery Store Example

The illustration that lands the point. Two people, same logical risk profile, walk to a grocery store.

- Person A walks to the store without thinking about it. Logically, they could be hit by a car, slip, have a heart attack, get robbed. They never run those calculations. They feel certain. They go.
- Person B, agoraphobic, cannot get out the door. The same trip terrifies them. Logically the risks are identical. The certainty-feeling is absent.

The difference between the two is not in the world. It is in the felt sense the system is producing. Both face the same probability distribution. One body produces certainty; one body does not.

This means the anxious mind's project -- to "make sure" before acting -- is misframed. There is no amount of analysis that produces what Person A has. What Person A has is a *body state*, not an *information state*. Adding more information does not generate the body state. Often it interferes with it.

## What This Reveals About Rumination

The principle clarifies what rumination is doing. The anxious person who circulates thoughts -- thinking and thinking before acting -- is treating the problem as informational. If they could just figure it out completely, they could act. But the bottleneck is not information. The bottleneck is the absent felt sense of certainty.

Brett names the trap: "we want to think about what to do, and then we circulate in that for a long time, thinking and thinking and thinking, because no action feels like it's actually leading to a safer place, because we're stuck in some local optimum and every way, every direction feels like it's worse before potentially it gets better, and we might not see that." -- Brett Kistler, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

The loop continues because thought cannot produce what is being sought. The felt sense of certainty is not produced by analysis. It is produced by the body's relationship to action and to the unknown. Adding more analysis only deepens the loop.

This is why thinking your way out of anxiety rarely works. The tool is mismatched to the target. See [[feel-over-figure]].

## The Implication for Action

If the brain only needs perceived certainty, the question becomes: what produces perceived certainty? Two answers operate together.

1. **The body has to be in a state that can produce the feeling.** A nervous system that is constricted will not produce certainty no matter what information is presented. This is why somatic work often precedes effective action: the system has to relax enough for the certainty-feeling to be available at all. See [[anxiety-as-constricted-life-force]].

2. **The certainty-feeling tends to arrive through action, not before it.** Person A walks to the store and the body confirms certainty by the walking itself. Person B, by waiting until certainty arrives before acting, has it backwards. The action produces the certainty; the certainty does not need to precede the action.

This is the structural reason behind the common coaching move of "take a small step." Not because the small step achieves anything in the world. Because it gives the body the data it needs to start producing the certainty-feeling that no amount of planning will produce.

## The "Results We Think We Want" Catch

A further turn on the principle. Joe adds that the results the anxious mind is pursuing certainty about are usually not the best results anyway.

"The results that we think we want are usually not the best results." -- Joe Hudson, Anxiety: A Signpost to Unmet Needs

This compounds the misframing. The loop is chasing certainty about an outcome that, even if achieved, would likely not be the best outcome. The whole apparatus is misaligned: wrong target (real certainty when only felt certainty is needed), wrong process (analysis when the certainty-feeling is somatic), wrong goal (an outcome that the deeper system would not actually choose).

See [[feared-consequences-rarely-happen]] for the related observation that the feared outcomes mostly do not materialize.

## Why This Is Liberating

The principle is liberating because it removes a goal that was unreachable anyway. The person trying to "be certain" before acting has been chasing something that does not exist on the terms they were chasing it.

Once the principle lands, the question shifts. Instead of "how do I become certain?" the question becomes "what would it take for my body to feel okay enough to act?" That question is answerable. The answer is usually some combination of feeling the anxiety, articulating the underlying need, and taking a small action without the felt certainty being fully present yet.

The willingness to act without the certainty-feeling is what produces the certainty-feeling. The reverse never works.

## Connection to the Anxiety Loop

The principle sits inside the broader treatment of anxiety as a loop. The anxious system is trying to address a real signal (an unmet need, per [[anxiety-three-definitions]]) by chasing a fictional resolution (logical certainty about outcomes). The chase generates more anxiety because the chase itself confirms there is something to be uncertain about.

The exit from the loop is to recognize what the loop is actually after (the felt sense of certainty), recognize that the loop cannot produce that (because analysis does not generate body states), and address the actual underlying signal (the unmet need, the unspoken request, the action that has not been taken). See [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]].

## In Coaching

When a person is stuck in a thinking loop about a decision, the principle is often the first move. The reframe: "you're trying to be sure before you act. The brain doesn't actually need to be sure. It needs to feel okay enough. Here's what that's about."

This often produces relief immediately, before any action. The relief comes from the person realizing they have been trying to achieve something that was not achievable. The pressure to "figure it out" was the pressure that was generating the loop.

The follow-up move is usually somatic. The person has been entirely in their head. The body has been ignored. Bringing attention to the body, feeling the anxiety as energy rather than tracking it as a thought, often produces enough of the certainty-feeling to act.

## What This Does Not Mean

The principle is not an argument for recklessness. It is not "just act, certainty is fake." Real risks exist and reasonable analysis has its place. The principle is specifically about the trap of trying to produce certainty through thought when thought cannot produce it. The intellect can run useful analyses. It cannot generate the felt sense that the system is treating as the requirement for action.

The skilled use of the intellect is to do the analysis it can do, then hand off to the body for the certainty-feeling that the analysis cannot produce. The trapped use of the intellect is to keep running analyses hoping one of them will finally generate the body state. The latter is the loop. The former is the function.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly amused. The principle has a "you're chasing your tail and here's why" quality to it that, when delivered well, lands as funny rather than as criticism. The person is not stupid for having been in the loop. The loop is structurally designed to be trap-like. Once seen, it is hard to stay in.
