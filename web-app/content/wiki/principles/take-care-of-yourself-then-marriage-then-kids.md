# Take Care of Yourself First, Then the Marriage, Then the Kids

## The Principle

The priority order for a family is the inversion of what most people say. Take care of yourself first. Take care of the marriage second. Take care of the kids third. Sometimes you have to sacrifice yourself for the marriage or the kids, but the default ordering matters because each upstream layer enables the downstream one.

"The most important thing in a marriage is to take care of yourself and then take care of the marriage and then take care of the kids. Sometimes you have to sacrifice yourself for taking care of the kids or taking care of the marriage, but the priority is always take care of yourself first, take care of the marriage second, take care of the kids third, because you can't be in a good marriage if you haven't taken care of yourself and you can't be a good parent if you haven't taken care of the marriage." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The structural argument: a marriage in which both partners have abandoned themselves cannot give the children what the children actually need. The children need parents who are alive, present, and connected to each other. Parents who are depleted and disconnected — even with the best parenting intentions — produce a household whose felt sense is depleted and disconnected. The kids feel it before they can name it.

## Where This Came From

Joe heard this at 25 or 26, before he met Tara, from an older couple in a bar. They had a marriage that was clearly alive and connected. He asked them how. This was their answer. The framing stuck for his whole life. See [[bar-couple-priority-order]] for the origin story.

## The Southwest Airlines Parallel

The same structure operates in good organizations.

"It reminds me oddly of like a Southwest Airlines thing where they say, employees first, customers second, shareholders third. Because if the employees are happy, the customers are happy. If the customers are happy, the shareholders are happy." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The logic is identical. The upstream layer is the precondition for the downstream layer. Inverting the order (shareholders first, customers second, employees third) produces a company that erodes its own ability to deliver what shareholders actually wanted.

The parental equivalent: putting kids first produces parents who have nothing left for the marriage, which produces a household whose adults have nothing left for themselves, which produces parents who eventually have nothing left for the kids.

## Why It Is So Hard to Do

The principle is simple. The execution is brutal, especially in the first 2 or 3 years after a child arrives.

"It's very hard to execute. Like it's very hard, like especially in the first 2 or 3 years to prioritize yourself. It's really, really hard." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The first years of parenting include sustained sleep deprivation, constant demand, and a cultural script that says good parents put the child first. Many parents do not realize they have stopped taking care of themselves or of the marriage until something breaks. Joe and Tara describe a period at around 18 months with their second child where they were questioning the marriage and could not fix anything. A friend told them they needed a weekend together. They figured out how to do it. By the end of the weekend, all the problems were gone. They had slept 18 hours for three days in a row and reconnected. See [[joe-and-tara-weekend-reset]] for the story.

That episode is the principle in action. The marriage had not died. The marriage had been starved. The fix was not therapy or harder work. The fix was returning to the upstream layer that had been neglected.

## What Taking Care of Yourself Means in Practice

The principle is not narrowly about indulgence. It is about whether you are actually a person, not just a function. The practical markers include:

- Are you sleeping enough to think clearly?
- Are you in your body, not just your tasks?
- Do you have time alone, without performing for anyone?
- Do you have time to feel what you are feeling?
- Do you have access to your wants, or has all of you been displaced by the wants of the household?

A parent who has not asked these questions in a year is most likely not taking care of themselves. The same parent will tell you they are doing fine. The body says otherwise.

## What Taking Care of the Marriage Means in Practice

Specifically not "doing things for the relationship." More like: returning to connection. The weekend together. The conversation that is not about logistics. The willingness to be honest about something hard before it has built up.

For the slow-burn version of what happens when this is neglected, see [[we-have-built-up-resentment]] and [[the-built-up-resentment-story]]. The marriage that has slowly lost its aliveness usually got there by way of two parents who put the kids first for so long that they forgot how to be in the marriage.

## The Practical Hacks Joe Recommends

If the basic principle is hard to execute, the practical workarounds are specific:

- **Get outside help if you can afford it.** Even 20 hours a week of childcare makes a structural difference.
- **Use grandparents, aunts, anyone who has wanted a baby.** However you can get the help, get it.
- **Take turns.** A parent who is working 50% can sometimes have more capacity than two parents working 100%. Joe has watched couples burn out, divorce, and then both end up working part-time as single parents. If they had done that in the marriage, they might not have gotten divorced.
- **Track who has the day off.** The mental load of "is the other person doing as much as me" is corrosive. Naming "tomorrow is your day, today is mine" eliminates the comparison.

The hacks all serve the upstream principle. They are not the principle. The principle is that you, the marriage, and the kids are an ordered set, not equal claims on the same finite resource.

## Why This Lands Hard for Some Parents

The principle inverts what many people were taught about being a good parent, especially mothers. The cultural script is: a good parent sacrifices. A good parent puts the child first. A good parent does not have needs.

The principle says: a parent who follows that script produces children whose modeled relationship to needs is to deny them. The child grows up to be the same kind of self-abandoner the parent was. The lineage of self-abandonment continues. The parent's actual job — if they want to give the child something different — is to stop being the model of self-abandonment.

This is consistent with [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]]: the kids absorb what the parents are, not what the parents say. If the parents are abandoning themselves while preaching self-care, the kids absorb self-abandonment.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent presenting exhaustion and the felt sense that something is "off" with the marriage but they cannot name what.
- A couple in the first three years of parenting who report no time together as a couple.
- A parent who feels guilty for wanting time alone or time with their partner.
- A parent whose self-care has reduced to nothing because "the kids need me."
- A coachee who is martyring themselves for the family and presenting as a burned-out caretaker.

## Tone

Practical. Direct. Not romantic. Joe presents this as something he has watched play out for decades and as something he knows from being a parent. He says explicitly that he and Tara were "not good at" carving out the weekend — which makes the principle land as something to actually try, not something to feel ashamed about not doing.
