# Treat Your Kid Like an Adult You Respect

## The Principle

The default parenting stance Joe holds throughout his daughters' lives: treat them the way you would treat an adult you respect. Not the way you would treat a friend's child, or a younger version of someone, or someone who needs to be managed. The way you would treat an adult whose presence in your life you value and whose autonomy you take seriously.

The principle has one caveat. Parents still draw boundaries with kids that they would not draw with adult peers — boundaries about safety, basic care, family contribution. Outside of that, the stance is the same.

"I want to treat you like the way I would treat somebody like an adult that I respect. Now, you want to draw boundaries, so that's not quite the same way, right? But besides that, and in every other way, I want to treat you like an adult that I respect. I have a job to teach you certain stuff, but like..." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

## Why This Stance Works

The respect stance accomplishes several things at once that the standard parenting stance does not.

**It avoids installing the not-okay signal.** A child treated as a respected adult is being told, structurally, that they are okay as they are. The opposite stance — the parent who corrects, manages, and adjusts the child throughout the day — is sending the inverse signal regardless of the parent's love. See [[dont-hate-your-kids-and-they-wont-hate-you]] for the long-form treatment of why this matters.

**It produces reciprocity.** The kid who is treated with respect tends to extend respect back, including the willingness to apologize quickly when they have been out of line. Joe describes this with Esme: "I really appreciate something about you is like if you do get all uppity with me, you know what I mean, on something — you apologize quickly." The respect goes both directions because the relationship is being run on the structural premise of mutual standing.

**It makes the child's pushback developmental, not relational.** When the kid does push back (and they will, especially as teenagers), the pushback is the normal pushing-off against the edge of the pool, not a fight about whether the parent will treat them as a person. The relationship survives the pushing because the structural ground was not pushed against — only the surface.

## What "Adult I Respect" Means in Practice

Concrete markers of what this looks like:

- When the kid enters the room, the parent makes themselves available the way you would for an adult you respect — not constantly chase, not perform indifference, just be present. See [[dont-chase-your-teen-stay-available]].
- When the parent has feedback, they deliver it directly, not through manipulation or hints. The same way you would speak to a colleague you respect, not through "have you really thought about" softening.
- When there is a conflict, the parent brings the whole problem to the kid for collaborative resolution, not just an edict. "How do we figure this out together?" See [[bring-the-problem-to-your-teen]].
- When the kid has a different worldview, the parent gets curious about it — the way you would about a colleague who sees something differently. See [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]].
- When the parent has to draw a boundary, they draw it directly and without escalation, the way you would with an adult — without the hardening that would communicate disrespect.

## The Mirror-Modeling Principle Underneath

The respect stance rests on a deeper principle: whatever you do to your kid, you are telling them is okay to do back. See [[if-you-do-x-youre-telling-them-its-okay-to-do-x]] for the full treatment.

"If you yell at your kid, you're telling them it's okay to yell back at you. If, if you don't like your kid, you're telling them it's okay not to like you. I want to treat you the way I would treat an adult that I respect." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The principle inverts the standard parenting calculus. Most parents think they are training the kid's behavior through their corrections. They are actually training the kid in the relational style they themselves are using. A parent who micromanages is training a kid to micromanage them back. A parent who treats the kid as respected is training the kid to extend respect.

## The Boundary Caveat (And Why It Does Not Violate the Principle)

The parent still draws boundaries that an adult-to-adult relationship would not require. The kid does need to be told they are not going out for the fourth night in a row. The kid does need to be reminded to take care of themselves. There are moments when the parent has to insist on something the kid does not want.

The principle holds even here, because the way the boundary is drawn still mirrors how you would set a limit with a respected adult. Direct. Without hardening. Without contempt. Without escalation. The boundary itself communicates respect by being delivered cleanly, not by being delivered with manipulation, shame, or punitive energy.

Joe describes the structural agreement: as long as the kid is taking care of themselves, participating in the family, and being good to people, they get as much freedom as they want. The boundaries exist within a structure that defaults to the kid's autonomy.

"My rule has always been... if you're taking care of yourself, if you're participating in the family and you're being good to people, you get as much freedom as you want." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

## What Esme Reports Back

The principle is articulated, in the conversation, by both parties. Esme names what the stance has produced in her experience of the relationship:

"It feels like a reciprocal relationship where I put in my part and then, like I get my part. Like it feels really balanced." -- Esme, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

This is the felt sense of being treated as a respected adult. It is reciprocal. It is balanced. The kid feels both their part and the parent's part. The relationship is not a one-way correction of the kid by the parent; it is a structure where both parties are accountable to each other.

## Where This Lands With Teenagers Specifically

The principle becomes especially load-bearing in the teenage years, because this is when the gap between "kid being managed" and "person to be respected" becomes operative. A teen who has been raised this way from young can absorb the teenage developmental work without breaking the relationship. The respect stance has been the default for years; teenage years just extend the autonomy lane that has always existed.

A teen who has been raised with the standard managing stance will often hit the teenage years and discover that the only way to claim their personhood is to fight. The fight is not really about the surface issues. It is about the structural question of whether they will be treated as a full person.

The reframe for parents anticipating the teen years: the cold rejection that older parents warn you about is largely the product of the managing stance arriving at the developmental moment when the kid can finally push back against it. The respect stance does not produce that cold rejection because there is nothing to reject.

## Tone

Joe states this matter-of-factly. The principle is not framed as aspirational or as advanced parenting. It is framed as the basic stance. The tone is "this is how I run this." The parent is not being asked to perform respect; the parent is being asked to recognize that respect is what produces the relationship they actually want. Hold that tone: structural, not exhortative.
