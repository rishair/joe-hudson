# Treat Something as a Threat and It Will Act Like a Threat

## The Principle

When a person or system treats something as a threat -- a colleague, a partner, an institution, a part of oneself, an emotion -- the treatment generates the conditions for the thing to actually act like a threat. The threat-perception is upstream of the threat-behavior. The behavior matches the perception.

"That threat is a gut thing, it's a nervous system thing. ... If I, if I react to it as if it's a threat, then I'm going to make a threat in the world. Meaning, if I treat you like a threat enough, you will act like a threat, right? ... And so when we treat the world as a threat, eventually it acts like a threat back." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The principle is the relational form of the [[what-we-fear-we-invite|Golden Algorithm]]. The Golden Algorithm names that the avoidance of an emotion creates the conditions for the emotion. This principle specializes that to the avoidance-by-threat-perception: treating something as a threat is one specific form of avoidance, and the threat-treatment is the production line for the threat itself.

## How the Loop Closes

The mechanism is observable. A person -- call them A -- perceives B as a threat. A's nervous system responds: vigilance, distance, defensiveness, micro-control, suppression, performance. A's behavior toward B carries the threat-perception even when A is trying to hide it. B's nervous system reads A's behavior. B's body registers being treated as a threat -- which usually produces some combination of frustration, defensiveness, and reciprocal threat-perception. B's behavior toward A now carries B's reactive threat-perception. A reads B's behavior as confirmation of the original threat-assessment. The loop is closed.

Neither party invented the threat from nothing. Each was responding to the other's response. But the loop was started by the original threat-perception, and the loop's perpetual existence requires only that the threat-perception remain active. Drop the perception on either side and the loop usually de-energizes within a few interactions.

## The Boss-Employee Example

Joe gives a concrete instance. A boss wants connection, performance, and the ability to work together with an employee. The employee is scared of the boss's response and is treating the boss as a threat. The employee's threat-treatment shows up as:

- Trying to be perfect to avoid the boss's anger
- Freeze, hesitation, second-guessing
- Stagnation, slow execution
- Holding back from being direct
- Avoiding the boss's attention

The boss is now living with an employee who is producing fear-stagnation-freeze instead of the connection and performance the boss wanted. The boss becomes frustrated. The frustration leaks. The boss now acts more like a threat than they would have if the employee had been direct from the beginning.

"I'm scared of your response as my boss. I'm going to treat you like a threat. Oh my gosh. And pretty soon you're going to be really fucking frustrated with me, and you're going to act like a threat. Like, I want connection, I want performance, I want us to be able to work together. I want all these things as your boss. I'm not getting any of them. I'm getting like fear, stagnation, freeze. I'm getting you trying to be perfect instead of actually like get the job done. Rrr, rrr. And then I act like a threat." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The structural irony: the employee's threat-treatment was meant to protect against the boss's anger. The protection produced the anger. The avoidance was the production line.

## Self as Threat: The Perfectionist Case

The principle applies internally as well. When a person treats themselves as a threat -- treats their own behavior, feelings, mistakes, or output as dangerous things that have to be managed and prevented -- the self-treatment generates the conditions for the self to actually become threatening to the person's life.

[[perfectionism]] is the clearest example. The perfectionist treats themselves as someone who has to be controlled, monitored, and corrected so they don't make a mistake. The self-treatment produces the very thing it was meant to prevent: chronic stress, frozen output, the inability to engage with the work, the relationship breakdown, the burnout. The self has now become a threat to the person's life. The threat-treatment produced the threat.

The same operates with [[fear-of-joy]]: treating one's own pleasure as a threat that has to be clamped produces a body that cannot relax, which produces the chronic depletion that actually does threaten the person's life. The clamp-as-protection becomes the production line for the depletion-as-threat.

The same operates with the [[voice-in-the-head]]: treating the inner critic as a threat to be defeated produces the war-with-self that consumes the energy that was meant for actual life. The treatment of the voice as a threat is what energizes the voice. See [[dont-fight-the-voice]].

In each case the structure is the same. The thing being treated as a threat (the perfectionism, the pleasure, the inner voice) becomes a more active threat because of the treatment. The work is not to defeat the threat but to stop treating it as one.

## The Way Out: Meet, Don't Threat-Treat

The corrective is not to ignore the thing that has been treated as a threat. It is to meet it with presence instead of with threat-response. The shift in stance changes what the loop does.

The boss who can be with the employee's fear (curious, present, not punitive) interrupts the loop on their side -- the employee's fear has space to differentiate and reduce, which produces less threat-treatment toward the boss, which produces less reciprocal frustration.

The employee who can be present with their own fear of the boss (feel it, name it, work with it) instead of acting from the threat-treatment can engage the boss directly. The directness produces a different relational signal, which produces a different boss-response.

The perfectionist who can meet their own perfectionism with curiosity instead of with more management ("what is this trying to protect me from? what would I have to feel if I dropped this?") interrupts the self-as-threat loop. The pattern de-energizes because the threat-treatment that was fueling it has stopped.

The general move: when you notice you have been treating something as a threat, pause. Feel what's underneath the threat-perception. Often the underneath is fear of a specific feeling (helplessness, abandonment, shame). Let the feeling differentiate. The threat-perception softens as the underlying feeling is felt. With the threat-perception softened, the behavior toward the thing changes. With the behavior changed, the thing stops responding like a threat.

See [[aggression-amplifies-against-conflict-avoidance]] for the specific case where avoidance amplifies the threat of an aggressive person. That principle is one application of this broader pattern -- the conflict-avoidance is the threat-treatment, the escalating aggression is the threat-response. The broader principle covers cases where the original "threat" was not aggression at all but anything the system has been organizing around in a threat-frame: a deadline, a partner, a customer, a part of self.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not the claim that nothing is ever a real threat. Some things are actual threats and the threat-response is appropriate. The principle applies to relational fields where the threat-perception is shaping the field. It does not apply to genuine physical danger, to actually-malicious actors, to circumstances where the threat-assessment is calibrated.

It is not a recommendation to be naive. The corrective is not to drop discernment. It is to drop the threat-frame as the organizing principle of the interaction. The person can still notice, calibrate, and protect themselves while not running the whole interaction through the threat-detector. The discernment improves when the threat-perception isn't dominating the cognitive field.

It is not the claim that the loop is the threat-treater's fault. Both parties are participating. The threat-treatment closes the loop; the response from the other side keeps the loop going. The corrective requires action on at least one side. The side that sees the pattern is the side that can act on it first.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, structural. Joe delivers this as an observation about how systems work, not as a moral teaching. The point is mechanical: this is what threat-perception does in relational fields. The mechanism is described so the listener can recognize it operating in their own field and choose differently.

In session, when this surfaces, there is often a felt-recognition moment when the person sees they have been generating the threat they have been protecting against. The moment carries some lightness -- the structure being seen is somewhat darkly funny once visible.
