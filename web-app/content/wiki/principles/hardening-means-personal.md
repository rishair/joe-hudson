# Hardening Is the Sign You Are Taking It Personally

## The Principle

The clearest external marker that a person has taken something personally is that they have hardened themselves to deal with it. The hardening can show as a wall in the body, a defensive posture, the closing off of emotion, the becoming-cold to handle something hot. Wherever that hardening shows up, the underlying state is personal, not impersonal, regardless of how the language frames it.

"If you have to harden yourself in any way, if you have to become defensive, build a wall between you and other people, you are taking it personally. That is a clear sign that you are taking it personally." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

## What This Reverses

The principle reverses a common assumption: that feeling sad, scared, or guilty is what taking something personally means. The opposite is true.

A person who feels fully -- who lets the sadness arrive when firing someone, who lets the fear arrive when asking for what they want, who lets the guilt arrive when setting a boundary -- is not taking it personally. They are feeling the emotion that is moving through them. The emotion completes and the person can stay in connection.

A person who hardens to avoid those feelings is the one taking it personally. The hardening is the protective response to a personal charge that the person does not want to feel.

"We somehow get in our head that if we take it personally, we're scared or we feel guilty or we're sad. But the clear sign of taking something personally is that you have shut yourself off to your own emotional experience." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

## How to Use the Diagnostic

In live coaching, watch for the moment a coachee hardens. The voice flattens. The face goes neutral. The body composes itself. The language becomes detached. The phrase "it's just business" or "it's not personal" or "I'm being objective" arrives.

That is the moment of taking it personally. The work is at the level of the emotion the hardening was deployed to avoid.

In self-investigation, the same diagnostic applies. The moment you notice yourself hardening to handle a situation -- composing yourself before the conversation, steeling yourself for the meeting, becoming professional instead of present -- something has hit you personally and you have decided not to feel it. The hardening is the move that locks the personal charge in.

## The Connection to Defense

This principle is one expression of [[defense]]. The defense is the body's automatic protection against an emotion it does not want to feel. The hardening is the visible signature of the defense in action.

The same instruction applies: dropping the defense is not done by trying to soften. It is done by allowing the emotion that the defense was holding back. See [[defense]] for the full mechanism. See [[defense-vs-love]] for the distinction between the defended and the open responses to the same situation.

## In Money Contexts

The principle has particular force around money because money so often produces a personal charge that the person refuses to acknowledge. Firing someone, negotiating a deal, raising rates, naming what work is worth -- each of these can trigger an emotion the person does not want to feel. The hardening response produces "it's just business" as the spoken defense and the cold professional as the embodied one.

See [[just-business-vs-impersonal]] for the distinction this principle enables. See [[money-is-impersonal]] for the contrasting state.

## In Other Contexts

The same principle operates outside money. Any conflict, any difficult conversation, any moment where one person has to deliver something hard to another. The hardened delivery signals the personal charge that the speaker is keeping out of awareness. The fluid delivery, even when difficult, signals the absence of that defended charge.

This is connected to [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]]. When emotion is unfelt, it gets either hardened-against or aimed-at-someone. Both are forms of doing emotion to others rather than feeling it. The third option -- feeling the emotion oneself and then communicating clean -- is the option the hardening forecloses.

## The Coaching Use

When a coachee describes a hard conversation they handled "professionally" or "without emotion" or "objectively," ask what they had to do to stay composed. The answer often reveals the hardening. The next move is to ask what they would have felt if they had not hardened. That answer is the personal charge that was operating the whole time.
