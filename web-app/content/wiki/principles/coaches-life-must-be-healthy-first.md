# Look at Your Life Before You Coach Anyone

## The Principle

Before entering the role of coach, the prerequisite is to look around at your actual life and ask whether the basic domains are functional. If your relationships are stable, your marriage is healthy, your kids are okay, your business or work is functioning -- the basic data is in order. You are now in a position from which the work can flow outward. If those domains are dysfunctional, the work you would offer is structurally off-source. The first work is the work on your own life. The coaching follows from a life that is actually working.

The mistake is reversed sequencing: someone has a transformative experience, becomes excited about the work, and immediately moves into the coach role -- while the marriage, the kids, the team, and the daily life are still dysfunctional. The excitement is the data. The dysfunction is also the data. The dysfunction wins. The coaching offered from that posture transmits whatever is unresolved in the coach.

"What I would hope is that somebody would actually do quite a bit of their own work and that they're actually looking around and they're like, oh, my life is actually, you know, actually, if I have an organization, it's healthy and functional. If I have a marriage, it's healthy and functional. If I have kids, it's healthy and functional. Now I'm ready to go and coach -- rather than, you know, I can't hold a relationship, my kids hate me, and my business is super dysfunctional, but I'm gonna go coach 'cause I had an ayahuasca experience." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The principle is empirical: your life is the mirror of your inner state. If the mirror is showing dysfunction, the inner state is producing that dysfunction. Going to coach others while your life is producing dysfunction means you are offering them the same wiring you have not been able to resolve in your own life.

## The Specific Check

The check has concrete fields. Each one is asking whether the structural domains of an adult life are actually working. Not perfect. Working.

- **Relationships.** Are your closest relationships functional? Can you be in them without managing yourself out of them? Do you have ongoing intimacy or have you settled into management?
- **Marriage or primary partnership.** If you have one: is it healthy and functional? Can both of you tell the truth? Are you connected or are you cohabitating around the dysfunction?
- **Kids, if you have them.** Are they okay? Are they connected to you? Are they thriving in the structure you are providing, or are they being shaped by your unresolved material?
- **Business or organization, if you have one.** Is it functional? Does the team you lead want to be there? Are you producing the result the structure is supposed to produce, or is the team absorbing the cost of your patterns?

These are not a checklist to pass before coaching. They are the diagnostic field. If multiple domains are dysfunctional, the dysfunction is information about what work is still unaddressed. The work in those domains is the same work the coaching would be asking clients to do. Doing the coaching while skipping the work in your own life is structurally backwards.

## The "I Had an Ayahuasca Experience" Pattern

Joe names a specific version of the wrong sequence: the peak experience that leads to the coaching pivot.

The pattern: someone has a powerful experience (psychedelic, retreat, awakening, breakthrough). The experience surfaces real material and produces real insight. The person becomes oriented toward the territory the experience opened. Then -- crucially -- they skip the years of integration that the experience was supposed to begin, and instead make the coaching pivot. The experience becomes the credential. The credential is then offered to clients while the integration is still entirely pending.

The peak experience is a beginning, not a qualification. The work that follows is what makes someone able to hold the role. The work has to happen in the actual fields of their life -- in the marriage that the breakthrough exposed, in the team that the breakthrough showed they have been controlling, in the kids whose behavior reflects the parent's unaddressed material. Doing that work over years is what produces the orientation that can hold coaching. Skipping it and going straight to the role does not work.

The same principle applies to any peak experience or insight: a powerful retreat, an awakening, a meditation breakthrough, a deep coaching session. The experience is data. The orientation the data produces is to begin the long work, not to skip it.

See [[dont-identify-as-awakened]] for the related anti-pattern at the level of identity. The coaching-pivot-after-awakening is the career-version of the same trap.

## The "25 Years" Reference Point

Joe's own reference point for what the long preparation looked like in his case:

"It was 25 years of deep internal practice that I went through before I even began to think that I was equipped. I don't think it has to be that long." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The 25 years is not the prescription. The prescription is the orientation: a sustained period of doing your own work before you take on the role of doing the work in front of others. Whether that is 5 years or 15 years or 25 depends on the person, their starting point, the support around them, and the depth of their commitment. The point is not the duration. The point is that the inner work has to be enough that the coach is not using the coaching to do the work they should have done before they took the role.

A specific structural form of this principle in Joe's organization: trainees who want to facilitate week-long programs work with the team for 7 years before being permitted to do so. The duration is not arbitrary. It is the time it takes for the relational field around the trainee to register their level of clarity, their pattern integration, their capacity to hold others. Faster paths exist in other lineages; the lineage being described has chosen this duration as the structural marker of what holding a week-long container actually requires.

"In our organization, we've had people who've been working with us, working before they got to us, working with us for 7 years before we would let them facilitate a week-long program. To see that their level of clarity to us is like an incredibly important part of it." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

## "It Comes With Grief, Not Judgment"

Joe is explicit that the principle is delivered without moral condemnation of people who get the sequence wrong. The orientation is grief rather than judgment.

"I say that all and it feels like it could come with some judgment and it doesn't. It comes more with grief because I've just seen the damage that can be done if you aren't perpetually looking at yourself and using this path as a reflection." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The grief is for both the would-be coach and their clients. The would-be coach is signing up for a role that will consume them. The clients will receive work that is not what they paid for. The grief is the appropriate response to seeing the pattern repeat, not the judgment that would put the teacher above the person making the mistake.

The structural implication: the principle is held firmly, but not punitively. People who hear the principle and recognize themselves in the wrong-sequence pattern are not being told they are bad. They are being told the sequence matters and that getting it right is in everyone's interest -- including theirs.

## Compatibility With Imperfection

Jerry adds a refinement that protects the principle from becoming an impossible bar:

"I would say that I think it's okay if you've done the work, if you've had those struggles in your life. I don't think you have to be perfect." -- Jerry Colonna, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

Joe affirms this. The principle is not "have a perfect life." That would be a different (and impossible) bar that would ironically install the same disqualifying identity the principle is trying to surface. The principle is about whether you have done the work, whether the work is ongoing, whether the basic domains of your life are functional enough that you are not using the coaching to compensate for what is broken there.

A coach with struggles in their life who has done the work and continues to do it is in the right orientation. A coach with a smooth life who has not done the work and is offering the smoothness as proof of qualification is in the wrong orientation. The work is the data, not the smoothness.

The dimension that matters is whether the coach is "perpetually looking at yourself and using this path as a reflection." The looking is continuous. The reflection is continuous. The dysfunction that arises in life is registered as material to work, not as a problem to hide. This orientation is what makes a coach. The smooth life that has avoided struggle is not the marker; the working orientation toward whatever life brings is.

## The Reverse Direction (Why Dysfunction in Your Life Predicts Damage in Your Coaching)

The structural reason this principle matters: your inner state produces your outer life. If your outer life is producing dysfunction, your inner state is the source. You will transmit that inner state into your coaching. The clients will receive it. They will not name it, but their work with you will be shaped by it.

See [[culture-outside-mirrors-culture-inside]] for the same principle applied to organizations. The principle generalizes: you cannot produce in the outer field what you cannot produce in yourself. The coach whose inner life is producing dysfunction cannot produce in clients an outer life that is functional. The coaching will transmit whatever is being run.

This is why the prerequisite check is not optional and not punitive. It is the structural condition for the work to be transmissible. Without it, the work being offered is something different from what is being named.

## A Specific Failure Mode This Principle Surfaces

The pattern-seer-becomes-coach failure mode: someone who can see their patterns articulately, who has the language of the work, who can describe what is happening in themselves and others -- and whose life is still organized around the patterns they can describe. They become a coach because they can describe. The describing is then offered to clients.

The describing is not the work. The integration is the work. See [[the-pattern-seer-using-pattern-knowledge-to-stay-stuck]] for the read that names this configuration. A would-be coach who has this configuration is exactly who this principle is for. The pattern-knowledge does not qualify them; it can mask that the actual work is still pending.

The check: are the patterns I can describe still operating in my life? If yes, the work is still here, the coaching role is premature. If no -- the patterns have been actually integrated such that they no longer organize my marriage, my parenting, my work -- the role becomes available.

## The Continuous Form

The check is not done once. It is done continuously. A coach who passed the check at year 5 of practice can drift into dysfunction at year 10. The drift is normal; the response to the drift is what determines whether the coach is still in the right orientation. The drift gets noticed, named, and worked. The coaching continues to be sourced in a working life because the working is continuous.

A coach who hides the drift, or who stops noticing it, or who insists their life is fine when the evidence says otherwise, has slipped out of the orientation this principle requires. The slipping is also normal; the question is whether it gets caught and corrected, or whether it calcifies into the new normal of someone who is now coaching from dysfunction without acknowledging it.

The structural protection for this is community -- people around the coach who can name what they are seeing. See [[surround-yourself-with-people-who-make-fun-of-you]] and the broader category of supervision and peer accountability in [[training-coaches-and-facilitators]].

## Tone

Grief, not judgment. Joe's delivery of this principle is slow and weighted with the memory of damage he has seen. He uses the word "grief" explicitly to position the teaching as compassion rather than condemnation. The tone matters because the principle could land as gatekeeping if delivered punitively; the grief frame keeps it landing as protection.

Jerry's complementary refinement -- "you don't have to be perfect" -- is delivered as relief, not as softening. The bar is real and the bar does not require perfection. Both can be true.
