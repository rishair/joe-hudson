# Forgiveness With Boundaries Invites the Other Into a Different Reality

## The Principle

Forgiveness with strong boundaries — the configuration of "I see your inherent goodness, I will not be around this behavior, I will not make you bad" — has more change-agent effect on the harm-doer than exile or held resentment does. The counter-intuitive truth: the harm-doer's behavior is more likely to shift in the presence of someone who has done the forgiveness work than in the presence of someone who maintains the bad-person frame.

"I would say that actually invites that person's behavior to change more than anything else. Like, oh, I'm going to forgive you. I see your goodness. I see your inherent goodness. I don't like this kind of behavior, but I'm not going to exile you forever. I'm going to just have these clear boundaries to take care of me, my family, my community. But I'm not going to make you bad. That, that is inviting the perpetrator into a different reality." -- Tara Howley, I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

This principle does not mean forgive in order to change them. The motivation has to be self-care (see [[forgiveness-as-self-care]]). The change in the other is a frequent side-effect, not a goal. Aiming the forgiveness at producing change reintroduces the manipulation the forgiveness was supposed to release.

## Why It Works

The change-agent function of forgiveness operates through a mechanism that exile cannot reach. The harm-doer is met as a full human being who is being seen — both their behavior (not okay) and their inherent goodness (acknowledged). The combination produces the conditions for the harm-doer to see themselves the same way.

Exile, by contrast, confirms the harm-doer in their existing self-perception. If they have organized their behavior around the belief that they are the bad guy, exile is the predicted response and reinforces the organization. Their behavior continues because the world is doing what their internal model says it does.

Forgiveness with boundaries breaks the predicted response. The harm-doer's model expected punishment or compliance. Instead they receive open-hearted refusal — the open heart contradicts the bad-guy expectation, the firm refusal contradicts the compliance expectation. The model cannot absorb both. Something has to change.

This is the same mechanism by which Joe's [[forgiveness-as-stop-making-them-monster]] operates in healing. The release of the monster-frame opens space for the harm-doer to be different. The monster-frame is what was holding them in their role.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

The form is: open heart + firm boundary + named behavior + acknowledged humanity.

- "I love you. I will not be in this dynamic. What you did is not okay. You are not the worst version of yourself."
- "I forgive you. I am not going to spend time with you. I see your goodness. I see your inherent goodness."
- "I will not pretend this did not happen. I will not pretend you are a monster. I will not be around this behavior. I am not abandoning my care for you as a human being."

The boundary is firm. The heart is open. The behavior is named as unacceptable. The personhood is acknowledged as intact.

"You don't condone the behavior, but you can understand and have compassion for where the behavior came from. And it does change you and it does change them." -- Tara Howley

## The Grandmother and the Murderer Example

The story Tara references: a grandmother whose grandchild was murdered. The grandmother went to the prison where the murderer was held. She forged a relationship with him — not to forgive in the abstract, but to do the work of opening her own heart. In the relationship she saw what had shaped him: a horrible childhood, a structure that had produced what he became.

"His behavior, you don't condone the behavior, but you can understand and have compassion for where the behavior came from. And it does change you and it does change them." -- Tara Howley

The relationship changed both of them. The grandmother's heart was no longer closed. The murderer was met as a human being for what may have been the first time. The boundary remained structural (he was incarcerated). The internal forgiveness work was separate from the external containment, and the open heart was what made the relationship possible.

The example holds the principle: the forgiveness work was for the grandmother (self-care), and a frequent side-effect of that work was change in the other person.

## The Sacred Possibility

Tara names this as "the sacred possibility" — not a guarantee, not a technique, not something to aim at, but what becomes possible when the work has been done and a relationship can be maintained.

"I do believe that that's the sacred possibility. Yeah. That doesn't mean don't grab the — you get the boundary, you get the boundary." -- Tara Howley

The "you get the boundary" qualifier is critical. The change-agent function works only when the boundary is also held. Open heart without boundary is the configuration that gets re-harmed; that is not what produces change in the other. Open heart with boundary is the configuration that meets the other as a full human being while refusing the harmful dynamic.

"In fact, you — it will not change if you do not have boundaries." -- Tara Howley

## What This Is Not

- **Not a technique.** Aiming the forgiveness at producing change reintroduces manipulation. The forgiveness has to be for the forgiver's heart, or it is not forgiveness.
- **Not reconciliation.** The change in the other can happen without the relationship being re-established. The grandmother story includes ongoing visits; many cases do not.
- **Not minimization.** The behavior is still named as unacceptable. The boundary is still firm. The harm is still real.
- **Not a guarantee.** Many people will not change regardless of the open-hearted forgiveness they receive. The principle describes what is possible, not what is certain.
- **Not for cases of active danger.** A person still being actively harmed should not be doing this work. Safety first, then if and when the survivor wants to, the work becomes available.

## The Implication for the Coach

When a person fears that forgiving will signal to the harm-doer that the harm was okay, the principle pushes back. The reverse is often true: the held resentment confirms the harm-doer in their pattern; the open-hearted firmness opens space for change.

This is one of the cases where the coach can offer the structural observation about how the world responds to internal states. The held charge feels like it is preventing the next harm. The open-hearted forgiveness with boundaries may actually be what prevents the next harm — both by not feeding the pattern and by inviting a different reality.

But the coach must not deploy this as a reason to forgive. The motivation cannot be "forgive so they will change." The motivation is "forgive so your heart can open." The change in them is what may follow, not what justifies the work.

## The Connection to "You Can't Change Another Person"

Joe teaches the principle [[dont-try-to-change-your-partner]]: you cannot change another person through your effort to change them. This principle is the apparent counter-example, but it is consistent:

- Direct attempts to change another person fail.
- Indirect effects on another person follow from your own state.
- Doing your own work (forgiveness, open heart, firm boundary) creates conditions where the other person's behavior may change as a side-effect.
- The change is theirs to make. You are not making it. You are creating the conditions in which their change becomes possible.

The same principle: stop trying to change them, do your own work, see what happens. The work-for-yourself is the only available path. The fact that it sometimes also changes others is a structural side-effect, not a goal.

## The Three-Layered Outcome

When the work is done well:

1. **The forgiver's heart opens.** This is the primary, certain outcome. The work is for this.
2. **The relational dynamic shifts.** The configuration of the relationship (or non-relationship) becomes cleaner. The boundary holds. The hostility releases.
3. **The other person sometimes changes.** Not always. Sometimes. The frequency is high enough that Tara names it as the sacred possibility, low enough that it cannot be guaranteed.

The coach orients the work toward the first outcome. The second and third are gifts that may or may not arrive.

## Tone

Tentative on the change-in-the-other claim. Tara holds this as a possibility that is sacred, not as a guaranteed mechanism. The tone is reverent rather than instrumental. The principle is offered as observation about what is possible, not as a technique to deploy.
