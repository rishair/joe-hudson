# Seeking Approval Pushes People Away (Because the Seeker Stops Listening)

## The Principle

The very act of seeking approval pushes away the approval being sought, and pushes away the relationship that would have been the source of it. Two specific mechanisms make this inevitable.

**First: the seeker has stopped listening.** When you are scanning the other person for approval, you are not in the conversation. You are in a meta-position — monitoring whether you are landing well, whether they like you, whether they will give you what you came for. Your attention is on the extraction, not on them. They feel the absence. They feel the management.

**Second: the seeker is extracting, not giving.** Asking for approval is structurally the same as asking someone for money. You are asking them to give you something they cannot easily give twice. After a while they pull back. "God damn it, buy your own lunch. Get your own approval."

"When you're seeking out approval, you stopped listening. You're not actually with the person. You're not present with the person. You want to extract something from them. It's like asking them for money, except for you're asking them for approval. And at some point they're like, 'God damn it, buy your own lunch. Get your own approval.'" -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

## Why This Is True

The internal experience of being on the receiving end of approval-seeking is the diagnostic. Joe gives it directly:

"The easiest way to understand how looking for people's approval pushes them away is think about the person who wants your approval, who's desperate for your approval, and notice what happens inside of you. You're like, 'Ugh, get away.' Or you're okay with it for a while, but then you're like, 'Oh, this is annoying.'" -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The reader is asked to verify the principle in their own body. They have been on both sides of this dynamic. They know what it feels like when someone is trying to extract approval from them. They know what it feels like to want to back away. The principle becomes self-evident the moment the perspective flips.

## Why High-Functioning Teams Cannot Run This Engine

The principle has a direct implication for groups. A high-functioning team or family is structurally incompatible with approval-seeking running in any member.

"Have you ever seen a high-functioning team where one person is like, 'Hey everybody, do you like me?' Like, it doesn't work. What happens in a high-functioning team or a high-functioning family is that people are listening to themselves and they're listening to each other. And when you're seeking out approval, you stopped listening." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The functioning team requires people who are present, who listen to themselves (so their contributions arrive from somewhere alive) and who listen to each other (so the work integrates). The approval-seeker breaks both. They are not present to themselves, so they have nothing to bring. They are not present to others, so they cannot receive what is being brought. The team registers the absence as a drag.

## The Trust Implication

There is a downstream consequence: you cannot trust someone who is running on approval-seeking. The person who is shaping their words to please you is not telling you what is true. They are telling you what will land. The relationship has no traction because nothing the person says can be relied on as their actual view.

"How can you be friends with or trust somebody who's just gonna do what you want because that gives them approval? Instead of somebody who's actually gonna show up as them, say what they mean, mean what they say. That's the people that you can trust." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The trustworthy person is the one who is willing to say what they actually think and feel, including the things you might not want to hear. The approval-seeker has subtracted themselves from the relationship. There is no one home to be in relationship with. The push-away is the field's natural response to that absence.

## The Pause Move

The principle by itself does work. Bringing awareness to the push-away mechanism slows the approval-seeking.

"Just see how when you look for approval, it's pushing people away. Feel the hurt of that pushing away. Just doing it in and of itself, bringing that awareness to the table is going to slow down your need for approval." -- Joe Hudson, STOP Chasing Approval

The seeking has been running as an automatic strategy. The body believed the strategy was producing connection. Seeing — and feeling — that the strategy is producing the opposite (the pushing away, the loss of trust, the absence of real contact) disrupts the automaticity. The body starts to have its own resistance to the move it was making.

The instruction is specifically *feel* the hurt of the pushing away — not just notice it intellectually. The felt sense is the lever. Knowing it conceptually does not change the behavior. Feeling what the seeking has cost does.

## Relationship to the Listening Principle

This principle is the approval-shaped version of [[wanting-others-to-listen-is-the-block]]. The general principle is: trying to extract a relational response from another person stops you from being in the conversation, and the absence of you in the conversation is what makes the response impossible. The variant for listening is "stop trying to get them to listen, become a listener." The variant for approval is "stop trying to get approval, give your own."

Both are special cases of [[craving-is-the-pushing-away]]. The grasp creates the gap that makes the thing being grasped at unreachable.

## What This Looks Like in Coaching

When a client describes their approval-seeking, the move is not to give them strategies to stop. It is to make the mechanism visible. Two specific moves work:

**The perspective flip.** "Think of the most approval-needy person you know. What do you feel toward them?" The client locates the "ugh, get away" response in themselves. The move has put them on the receiving end of their own behavior. The push-away is no longer abstract.

**The feeling-the-hurt move.** "Notice that you've been doing this for years. Feel the people who pulled back. Feel the absence of trust. Feel what it has cost you." The move is not to add shame to the dynamic. It is to let the body feel the actual cost of what the strategy has been producing. The body has been running the strategy as if it were producing connection. Letting the body feel that it has been producing the opposite is what unwinds the strategy.

After the mechanism is visible and the cost is felt, the work moves to the receiving (see [[let-it-move-through-and-clean-the-channel]]) and to the emotional substrate underneath (see [[putting-someone-above-blocks-emotion]] and [[chase-your-own-approval]]).

## Tone

Direct, slightly amused. The "ugh, get away" delivery is the tone marker. Joe is letting the reader hear the body's actual response to being approval-sought. The bluntness is required — softening it would let the listener continue believing the seeking is "just being nice" or "just caring what people think."

The follow-through is warmer. Once the mechanism is visible, the tone moves to the work of receiving — gentler, slower, somatic. The bluntness sets up the receiving; the receiving does the actual healing.
