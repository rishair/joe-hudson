# What Cracks You Open Shows What's Processable Now

## The Principle

In trauma work, the question of "what should we work on next" has a built-in answer: whatever piece of material the body is currently responding to is what is currently processable. The detail that produces the body's cracking — the catch in the voice, the hand movement, the body's protective posture, the tears that arrive without permission — is the system's signal about which piece is on the live edge. Not the piece that the mind thinks is most important. The piece that the body has chosen.

This shifts how a coach (or AI coach) navigates a trauma session. The map is not predetermined. The body draws the map in real time. The coach follows.

## The Diagnostic in Sean's Clip

Joe makes this explicit in the running commentary on the Sean clip. Sean describes massive abuse — being under the bed during his father's violence, watching the strangling, attacking his father with a guitar. He describes all of it with the body intact. The capacity to report is there. The processing is mostly complete on those pieces.

Then he mentions something offhand: as a kid, he used to scratch the inside of his cheek with his teeth, with so much anxiety, that he opened a hole in his gum. The detail is smaller than the violence. The system's response to it is bigger.

"The thing that breaks him down and makes him like really hurt is to feel the anxiety he used to feel. Like that's what cracked him. So think about that, that the, where he is right now is that the pain of this experience is to feel the anxiety that was in him. That's the thing." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The pattern continues. Sean mentions his brother taping knives to his leg, preparing to stab their father. The body responds again. The mind moves on to "anyway, I make a lot of money now," and the body is still showing the anxiety.

"It's like he's feeling the anxiety again. He's feeling like what had to be in his brother to want to stab the father, and the anxiety of like, oh, my brother's going to go to jail, my brother's going to stab my dad, and like all the anxiety that that brings up with him. That again starts really hitting his process heavily." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s in Pain

The mind would have predicted that the central violence would crack him. The body shows that the central violence has been processed. The anxiety inside the violence — the part of his experience that he could not feel as a child — is what is now live.

## Why the Body Surfaces What's Processable Rather Than What's Worst

The system has a logic. It does not surface material it cannot yet metabolize. The pieces that come up — that catch the voice, that move the hands, that arrive as tears — are pieces the system is ready to work on. The system has either built enough internal capacity to face them or external conditions (a safe listener, a stable life situation, an opening of permission) have shifted enough that the material can come up now.

Material that is not yet processable stays sealed. The system protects it. It does not surface in the way that breaks the body open. It is mentioned without affect, or it is referenced and then steered away from, or it does not come up at all. The protection is intelligent. The protection is allowing the parts of the work that can be done now to happen, while keeping the parts that cannot yet be done held in place.

The implication: if a coach tries to force the system toward what the coach thinks is the "real" issue, the system protects. The protection looks like the speaker withdrawing, getting more abstract, moving to dissociation, joking harder, changing the subject. None of these are the speaker being difficult. They are the system saying "not that piece, not right now."

If the coach follows what the body is surfacing, the system permits the work to continue. The piece that is up is the piece the system is ready for. The coach's job is to be present while it is processed.

## The Mind Often Disagrees With the Body

A common pattern: the speaker's verbal narration moves to a different topic at the moment the body has identified the live edge. The mind, sensing the body's response, redirects the conversation. The redirection is the configuration's defense.

Sean does this twice in the clip — moves to "life's good, I make a lot of money now" right after the body cracked on the anxiety material. The mind is trying to seal the piece the body just opened.

The coach's move at this moment is not to follow the mind. It is to follow what the body responded to. The mind's redirection is data — it confirms that the body's edge was real — but it is not the next direction.

This often looks like the coach gently returning to the moment that cracked open. "What was happening just before you said that?" Or simply being silent and present while the speaker's body continues to process what the mind is trying to seal. The body often wins this internal contest if the room stays available for it.

## How to Read the Body's Selection

The signals that the body has identified the live edge:

- Voice change: thinner, slower, catching, breaking, or unexpectedly louder
- Hand movement: fluttering, rubbing, wringing, drumming, moving with low-level anxiety the voice is not expressing
- Postural shift: head turning, head covering, leg coming up, body curling slightly
- Tearing or eye change: tears that arrive without permission, eyes that glaze, eyes that close, eyes that look down
- Breath change: shallower, faster, or temporarily suspended
- The verbal narration faltering: incomplete sentences, "I don't know," loss of the thread, unexpected silence
- The free-associated mention: an unexpected detail that the mind had not planned to bring up

When several of these arrive together on a particular piece of material, that piece is the live edge.

See [[body-language-of-someone-sharing-trauma]] for the developed read on these signals.

## What the Coach Does With the Diagnostic

Once the live edge is identified, the coach's job is to be present while the system works on it. The coach does not push, does not interpret, does not interrogate. The coach holds the room available for the work.

Specific moves:

**Stay with the moment.** When the body responded, the conversation should slow rather than speed up. The pace shifts to match the work.

**Do not move on prematurely.** If the speaker is willing to stay with the moment, the coach is willing to stay with it. If the speaker moves on, the coach lets them move on but may gently return.

**Notice what is being avoided.** When the mind redirects after the body's crack, the redirection is part of the data. The coach notices what is being avoided and holds it in awareness even if not naming it.

**Follow what surfaces next.** After the live edge is partially processed, the system often surfaces the next piece. This may be the surrounding material (see [[ancillary-trauma-needs-processing-too]]) or a related piece. Whatever surfaces is the next layer.

**Trust the system's pacing.** The system surfaces what it can metabolize. The coach's job is not to determine what should come up; it is to be the container while what surfaces gets processed.

## The Anxiety Layer in Particular

A specific pattern Joe identifies in the Sean clip: the live edge for someone who experienced sustained childhood violence is often the anxiety that was the constant background of their childhood, rather than the violence itself. The violence may have been processed. The anxiety — the chronic readiness, the body's perpetual brace, the inability to ever rest — has often not.

The anxiety is hard to process because it was not an event. It was an environment. The mind does not have a story to tell about it the way it has stories to tell about specific violent moments. The body, however, knows the anxiety intimately. The body lived inside it for the entire childhood.

When the anxiety surfaces as the live edge, the speaker often does not understand why this particular detail is what cracked them open. The discrepancy between the smallness of the detail and the size of the body's response is a signal. The body is processing the substrate state that the events occurred inside. The substrate is often the deeper piece.

The implication: if a speaker has done significant trauma work on the events and is still living as if the trauma is occurring (chronic anxiety, hypervigilance, inability to rest), the unprocessed piece is likely the substrate anxiety rather than another event. The work to do is the work of feeling the chronic background state that the child lived inside.

## Boundary: The Diagnostic Does Not Always Apply

There are situations where the principle is incomplete:

- When the speaker is in acute crisis, the priority is safety, not following the body's edge.
- When the speaker is so dissociated that the body is not signaling at all, the work is first to come back into the body before the live-edge diagnostic becomes available.
- When the speaker is in a highly defended state and the body's signals are being actively suppressed, the work may be first to soften the defense before the body's selection becomes legible.

In each of these cases, the principle is not wrong; it is just not yet operative. The conditions for the body to surface what's processable have to be in place first.
