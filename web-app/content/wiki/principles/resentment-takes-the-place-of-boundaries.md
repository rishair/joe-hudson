# Resentment Takes the Place of Boundaries

## The Principle

When the boundary that would protect you has not been drawn, the body fills the gap with resentment, blame, or sustained anger. The charge is doing the boundary's job — it is what is keeping you from being in the situation again. Once the boundary is actually drawn, the charge can release because the protection is now structural rather than emotional.

"It's almost like the anger or the resentment is taking the place of boundaries. So once you have the boundaries, then you can let go of all this sort of charge that you're otherwise holding on to." -- Alexa Anderson, surfaced and affirmed by Tara Howley in I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

This is why people refuse to forgive. The refusal feels protective because it is protective — the resentment is what is keeping them from re-entering the dynamic. Asking them to forgive without first giving them the boundary asks them to drop their protection.

## What Job the Resentment Is Doing

The resentment is the body's way of saying: "I will not let this happen again." Without the explicit boundary, this is the only protection available. The body holds the charge because it knows if the charge releases, the situation could repeat.

"I think the reason we don't want to forgive is because we want to make sure it never happens again. We want to make sure like, oh, that wasn't okay. And I'm going to be angry at them so that I never put myself in that position again. So that's actually appropriate. Those boundaries are appropriate." -- Tara Howley

The protective function is legitimate. The cost — the body holding sustained resentment — is what makes it unsustainable. The principle is: the protection needs to be moved from being held emotionally as charge to being held structurally as boundary. Once moved, the body can release the charge.

## How the Move Happens

The sequence: feel the feelings, get curious, draw the boundary. The boundary is what makes it safe to let the charge go. See [[forgiveness-three-steps-heart-head-gut]] for the full process.

The move is not "stop being angry." That instruction fails because the anger is doing a job. The move is "what is the boundary that this anger has been carrying?" When the answer arrives and is acted on, the anger releases on its own because its job is done.

"How do we have the boundaries without having to blame the other person or have hatred or resentment or lingering hurt in our own systems? And then with those boundaries, yeah, the heart can open again." -- Tara Howley

## The Cost of the Charge Doing the Boundary's Job

Holding resentment is expensive. The body pays in:

- Hormonal load. Sustained anger and blame keep stress hormones active over long periods.
- Heart-closure. The closed heart cannot do its other work (loving, sensing, being moved).
- Mental occupation. The story about the other person keeps cycling, taking up cognitive space.
- Identity hardening. The "I will never X" stance becomes a permanent self-feature.

"Blame eats at us. I actually think it releases all sorts of hormones that are not healthy for us long-term." -- Tara Howley

The cost is part of why the principle matters. The body cannot sustain charge-as-boundary indefinitely without damage. The structural boundary is cheaper to hold.

## Relationship to Resentment as Unspoken Want

This principle is a specific case of [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]]. The broader principle: resentment indicates an unfollowed want, an un-held boundary, or an unmade ask. This specific case: when the unfollowed want is "I do not want this situation to repeat," the boundary that would prevent the repeat is the missing piece.

Both principles converge on the same move: find what the resentment is pointing at and act on it. The difference is the specific shape of what the resentment is doing:

- In [[resentment-as-unspoken-want]]: the resentment indicates a want that needs to be named and followed.
- In this principle: the resentment is functioning as a substitute boundary that needs to be replaced by an actual boundary.

In most cases both are operating. The resentment is both indicating an unfollowed want and serving as a substitute protection. The work is the same: name the want, draw the boundary, and the resentment releases.

## The Iterative Discovery of the Real Boundary

The first boundary you reach for may not be the boundary that actually releases the charge. The boundary often has to be discovered iteratively, and the real boundary is sometimes larger than the specific instance.

"I might not get it right for the first 10 tries. Yeah. Right. And I'm going to keep going until my gut's like, that's the boundary. Oh, the boundary is that I get my boundaries. That's what I learned. Yeah. Oh, the actual boundary isn't about Saturday or Sunday. It's I get my boundaries." -- Tara Howley

The diagnostic for the right boundary: when you state it (to yourself or to the relevant other), the charge releases. When you state a boundary and the charge is still there, the boundary has not yet found its right size.

The deepest boundary is sometimes a stance rather than a rule: "I get to have my boundaries." That stance, once held, releases the charge that was holding the place where the boundary should have been all along.

## Why "Just Set Better Boundaries" Often Fails

The instruction "just set better boundaries" fails because it skips the emotion work. The body cannot draw a clean boundary while still holding the unprocessed anger; the boundary comes out as an ultimatum, a demand, or a yelled limit. See [[yelling-boundaries-instead-of-holding-them]].

The order is: emotions first, then curiosity, then boundary. See [[move-the-anger-then-draw-the-boundary]] for the inward-facing version (when you discover you are the one being passive-aggressive). The same sequence applies here: the anger has to move first, then the boundary that the anger was carrying can be drawn cleanly.

## Implications for the Coach

When working with a person who refuses to forgive, do not push the forgiveness. Push the boundary first.

The diagnostic question: "What boundary would make it safe to let this charge go?" The answer often reveals what the resentment has been carrying. Once the boundary is named and (where possible) acted on, the resentment usually relaxes on its own.

If the person says "I can't draw the boundary," the next question is "what would you lose if you drew it?" The answer reveals what the resentment has been protecting them from losing. The resentment is often holding a relationship, a position, an identity, or a hope in place. Drawing the boundary would force the loss to be felt. The resentment is the workaround for not feeling the loss.

## The Test

When the boundary is right, the charge releases. If you have what you think is a boundary and you are still carrying the charge, one of:

- The boundary is not actually drawn (you have stated it but not acted on it)
- The boundary is the wrong size (usually too small; the real boundary is larger)
- The emotion work is not done (return to step 1 of the three-step process)
- There is something the boundary would force you to feel that you are not yet willing to feel

The test is structural. The body knows.

## Tone

Practical. The principle is offered as mechanical observation about how the body works, not as moral exhortation. The held resentment is not bad; it is just expensive and unsustainable.
