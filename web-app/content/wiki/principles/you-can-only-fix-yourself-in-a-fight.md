# You Can Only Fix Yourself in a Fight

## The Principle

In a fight with a partner, the only person you can work on is yourself. Zero attention goes to fixing the other person. Zero attention goes to their health, their wellbeing, their growth, their pattern. All attention stays on what is yours to do.

"One of the main things that you learn in fighting well is that you can only fix yourself. You can put zero attention on fixing the other person. You can put zero attention on their health, their wellbeing, their— how do they get better, unless they ask you. You focus fully on yourself. And that's a really important part of fighting is that full focusing on yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "zero" is emphatic. Not "less attention," not "secondary attention." None. The fighting-well stance is full investment in your own internal work, with no investment in the partner's.

## What This Dissolves

The most common failure mode in tools-using couples is the trying-to-be-each-other's-therapist pattern. Each partner uses the tools they've learned to diagnose the other partner, suggest the other partner's work, name the other partner's patterns. The fight becomes a contest of "you're the one who needs to work on this."

"This constant processing usually involves trying to be each other's therapist. And you're still trying to fix one another." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The principle dissolves this directly. There is no version of "I am working on you" that is part of the fight work. If you are working on them, you are not in the fight work. You are in a different activity that you have given the same name.

The internal test: in the middle of a fight, what is your attention on? If your attention is on what they need to see, what they need to do differently, what their pattern is, you are out of position. The reset is to bring the attention back to your own state, your own pattern, your own work.

## What This Does Not Mean

This is not a principle of detachment from the partner. The partner is right there. You see them, hear them, are affected by them. The principle is about where the work is happening, not about emotional unavailability.

This is not a principle that the partner is wrong or untouchable. They are doing their own work or not. That is theirs to do. Your work is yours.

This is not a principle that you cannot give input ever. The carve-out is explicit: "unless they ask you." If the partner explicitly invites a perspective on their pattern, that is a different situation. The principle is about the unsolicited fixing, which is the default move that turns fights into mutual therapy projects.

This is not a principle that you should suppress observations you have about your partner. The observations may be accurate. The question is whether to act on them as fixing moves inside the fight. The answer is no. Hold the observations as data about you (what is being triggered, what is being noticed). Let them work themselves out in your internal work, not in their partner-aimed delivery.

## The Mechanism

Two reasons the principle works.

**First mechanism: the partner cannot receive the fixing.** When you are in a fight, both of you are likely in your trauma (see [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the trauma context). The trauma-state partner cannot integrate diagnostic feedback. Whatever you say lands in their trauma response, not in their integrating self. Even accurate observations land as attacks. The fixing move, however well-intentioned, escalates the fight.

**Second mechanism: your work on yourself is what actually frees the fight.** The fight is being held in place by both partners' patterns. When you start doing your own work on your own pattern -- noticing your defense, feeling what is firing in you, returning to your body -- the structural condition that held the fight in place starts to dissolve from your side. The partner often follows. Not because you fixed them. Because your shift changed the system.

See [[your-trauma-is-equally-matched-with-your-partner]] for why both partners are in the trauma. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] for why working your own side is what creates the conditions for both of you to heal.

## What Working on Yourself Looks Like in a Fight

The full focus on yourself involves a specific set of moves:

- **Noticing what is firing in your body.** Where is the activation? What is the defense doing?
- **Noticing what you want to make them be or do.** This is the projection material. Name it to yourself.
- **Noticing what you are not saying.** What truth are you holding back? See [[hold-back-from-relationship-is-what-needs-healing]].
- **Noticing what is older than this fight.** What is this reminding your body of? See [[name-im-in-my-trauma-as-couple-language]].
- **Noticing what you are doing to defend.** Where is the falling-into-non-defensiveness move available?
- **Noticing what you are wanting from them.** What want of yours have you been routing through them?

None of these are about them. All of them are about you. The fight becomes the surfacing-mechanism for your own material, and the moves you make in the fight (and the moves you do not make) are governed by what your material is asking for, not by what their material is doing.

## The Show-Up-Loving Move

Once the full attention is on your own work, a specific stance becomes available: showing up in a loving way to the partner without trying to make them do anything.

"You start to have these moments where you can see through the fight and show up in a loving way, which is the best way to be the therapist is being a non-therapist is just being loving and to be there loving with them." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "non-therapist" frame names what the move is. Therapy moves -- naming patterns, offering diagnoses, suggesting work -- are precisely what does not produce the shift. Loving presence -- contact, witness, warmth -- is what does. The principle of full focus on yourself includes the practice of being loving without it being a strategy to change them.

The risk is that loving presence becomes its own form of fixing. See [[loving-presence-as-defense]] for the failure mode where the loving presence is curated, performative, or used as a tool to produce a response in the partner.

## The Connection to "Make It All About You"

Key 4 of the [[4-keys-to-save-your-relationship]] framework is "make it all about you." This principle is the in-the-fight specification of that key. The 4-keys framework offers the orientation at the relationship level; this principle offers the operation at the moment-by-moment fight level.

The relationship-level orientation ("work on yourself, not on the partner") becomes hard to hold inside an active fight when the temptation to fix the partner is highest. This principle is the reminder that the orientation does not pause during the fight. It tightens during the fight.

## When This Principle Becomes Available

The principle requires some baseline of self-witnessing. A partner who cannot yet feel their own activation, name their own defense, or notice their own pattern in the moment cannot enact this principle in the moment. They will continue to find their attention pulled toward the partner's pattern because their own is not yet visible to them.

The principle is therefore both the orientation and the developmental work. As self-witnessing develops, the principle becomes increasingly available. As the principle is practiced, self-witnessing increases. Each reinforces the other.

In Joe's [[arc-of-fighting-maturation]], this principle comes online after the techniques-being-weaponized stage and is part of what transitions a couple from constant processing into actual healing.

## When Someone Experiences This

- Catching yourself in the middle of a fight trying to explain to your partner what their pattern is
- Realizing that the fight is mostly about who is more at fault rather than about what is firing in each of you
- Noticing that the more you "help" your partner see their pattern, the worse the fight gets
- Feeling exhausted from being your partner's therapist
- Recognizing that the things you keep telling your partner about themselves are the things they have not asked you to tell them

## Tone

Joe states this with the directness of a non-negotiable. The "zero attention" framing is delivered without softening. It is one of the clearest hard-line teachings in the fighting material. Not because the principle is moralistic but because the carve-outs ("unless they ask you") are precise enough that the rest of the principle can be stated as absolute.
