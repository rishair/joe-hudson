# Focus on the First Domino

## The Principle

At every level — company, project, life — there is one thing that, if done, makes everything else easier or irrelevant. The leader's job is to find it, name it, and make sure it gets done. Going to two things, much less three, dilutes the focus and reduces the leverage. One is the discipline.

"I think it's really important for leaders to be able to say, this is the first domino. What's the one thing or the two things and maybe the three things, but I like one. I try not to even go to two things that if I do that, everything else becomes easier or irrelevant. That's how I think about it. What's the one thing I can do? Everything else becomes easier or irrelevant." -- Joe Hudson

The principle is operational. It tells the leader what to spend their attention on and what to defer. The first-domino question is the one the leader returns to whenever the work threatens to spread across too many fronts.

## Why It Works

Most failure modes in companies and lives are failures to prioritize, not failures to execute. The work that does not get done is the work that was lower priority than the leader admitted; the work that does get done was higher priority than the to-do list ranked it. The first-domino principle forces the prioritization to be explicit and to be radical.

The reason for the radical narrowing: the leader's actual leverage is concentrated in a small number of decisions. If the company solves replicable revenue, almost everything else becomes addressable. If the leader builds their own clarity about what they want, almost every team conversation becomes easier. The one thing, identified correctly, makes the rest tractable.

"If I've got $10 million in revenue and I've grown revenue by 5x, I can forget about a lot of other things. Like a lot of other things can be going wrong, that going right, I'm okay. So you're looking for that. And once I have that right, then I have my ATM machine and I can go and fix everything else. And it's easier to fix it because I have the money to fix it." -- Joe Hudson

The metaphor is the ATM machine. The first domino, when it falls, produces the resources (financial, organizational, attentional) that make the rest of the work easier. The work was always going to require resources. Sequencing it so that the resource-generating thing comes first changes the difficulty of everything else.

## The Recursion: At Every Level

The principle applies at multiple scales simultaneously, and the leader has to be running it at each.

**At the meta level (the company):** what is the one thing for the entire company right now? Replicable revenue? Product-market fit? A specific hire? The company-level first domino is the single thing the leader is willing to bet everything else on.

**At the project level:** inside each project, what is the one thing? If the project is "build a sales story that works," the first domino is probably talking to ten or twenty or thirty customers and iterating on the story, not building marketing, designing the deck, or polishing the visuals. The leader runs the same question inside every project the company is doing.

**At the life level:** the principle extends past the company. Sometimes the one thing the leader needs is not a company thing. Earlier in 2026, Joe moved house. The first domino at that moment was settling into the new home so that the stability could support everything else he was trying to do.

"You know, as I'm working on several businesses, including AOA and my own, there's also just life. Sometimes the one thing that needs to happen — like earlier this year we moved. The thing that needed to happen was settling into a new home with having stable stability to be able to do all the other stuff." -- Joe Hudson

The leader who applies the principle only at the company level and not at the project or life level finds that personal chaos or project chaos still drags the company down. The principle has to be running at every level.

## Why One, Not Two or Three

The instinct is always to expand to two or three things. The leader feels that one is too narrow, that there are obvious second and third priorities, that focusing on one means abandoning the others. The discipline is to resist this.

The structural reason: human attention does not divide cleanly. Two priorities means each gets less than half (the cost of context-switching subtracts more). Three priorities means each gets a third minus context-switching costs. The first thing that would have gotten 100% of focus gets 30% or less. The leverage collapses.

The harder reason: most "second priorities" are actually addressable once the first is solved. The leader who insists on two priorities is often refusing to bet on which one is actually first. The refusal is the failure of leadership, not a sign of breadth.

The principle gives the leader permission to bet. The bet might be wrong, but a wrong bet is recoverable. A refusal to bet keeps the leader perpetually balanced across too many fronts to win any.

## How This Connects to Delegation

The first-domino principle is upstream of [[i-want-to-delegate-better]]. The leader who has not identified the first domino cannot delegate well, because the leader has not specified what actually needs to happen. The delegation problem dissolves partially when the first domino becomes clear, because the leader can name the specific thing the team needs to solve.

The principle is also what the leader brings into the [[set-solution-criteria-have-the-team-solve-within-them]] move. The criteria for the solution are derived from what the first domino requires. Without the first domino, the criteria are arbitrary. With it, the criteria are the operationalization of the strategic bet.

## The Question Form

The principle becomes a question the leader asks themselves and asks the team:

- "What's the one thing I can do that makes everything else easier or irrelevant?"
- "What's the first domino?"
- "If I do this one thing, what else becomes easier? What becomes irrelevant?"
- "What's the ATM machine? What's the thing that, if it works, generates the resources to fix the rest?"

The question can be asked at the company level in a strategy meeting, at the project level in a project kickoff, and at the personal level in a quiet moment of reflection. The same question generates different answers at each level. All of them point at where the leader's attention should go.

## What This Is Not

**Not a single-tasking principle for daily life.** The principle is about strategic prioritization, not about doing one thing at a time. The leader still has many tasks each day. The principle governs what those tasks are organized around, not how many of them there are.

**Not a refusal to delegate.** The leader who identifies the first domino still delegates the work on it. The first domino tells the leader what to delegate, not that they should do it personally. Often the first domino is "get the right person to own X" — and then the leader hands off X.

**Not an excuse for ignoring other priorities forever.** The principle is sequencing, not abandonment. Once the first domino has fallen and is producing its effects, the leader returns to the second priority — which is often easier now because the first domino solved adjacent problems.

**Not a static answer.** The first domino changes. The thing that was the first domino six months ago may no longer be. The leader has to keep running the question, not adopt one answer and ride it indefinitely.

## When the Principle Fires

- A leader is overwhelmed by the number of things they think they need to do
- A leader is trying to prioritize and producing lists of ten "top" priorities
- A company is making slow progress across many fronts and the leader cannot identify why
- A project is dragging because no one has identified what would actually unblock it
- A coachee is describing their life as full of equally important things and is exhausted

In each case, the question to ask is: what is the one thing that, if done, makes everything else easier or irrelevant? The answer focuses the conversation immediately.
