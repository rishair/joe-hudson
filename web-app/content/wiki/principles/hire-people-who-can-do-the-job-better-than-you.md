# Hire People Who Can Do the Job Better Than You

## The Principle

If you can do the job better than the person you hired to do it, get rid of them and hire someone you cannot do better than. The principle is the precondition for delegation. You cannot delegate to a person you do not trust to do the work. You cannot trust the work of a person you can outperform. The chain is broken at the hire, not at the delegation.

"If you can do the job better than anybody that you've hired, then get rid of them and get somebody who you can't do the job better than. And with that comes this recognition, oh, they know something that I don't know. I want them to do it because I know they can do it better than me. And they have wisdom that I want to learn from." -- Joe Hudson

The principle inverts the default frame of hiring. Most leaders hire people they can manage. The principle says: hire people who exceed you in the job. The leader's role then shifts from teaching them how to do the work to learning from them and resourcing them to do it.

## Why This Is the Delegation Precondition

The delegation problem looks like a delegation problem and is often a hiring problem. The leader who cannot delegate well often cannot delegate because the team is not actually capable of receiving the delegation. The leader has been compensating by doing the work themselves or by re-doing the team's work. Both reactions are honest responses to a team that is below the leader's standard.

The fix is upstream of delegation. Replace the team with people who exceed the leader at the job. Now the leader can hand the work off and the work gets done better than the leader could have done it. The delegation moves are real moves; until then, they are coping mechanisms.

This is why coaching on delegation often hits a wall. The leader is asking how to delegate more effectively. The actual answer is: you can't delegate this team better, because they aren't actually the right team. The work is to face the hiring decision, not to refine the delegation technique.

## What Comes With This Recognition

When the leader actually hires someone better at the job than they are, a specific shift becomes available. The leader stops being the source of authority on how the work should be done. The hired person knows things the leader does not. The leader's job is to listen, to learn, to resource, and to defend the criteria the work has to meet. The how belongs to the hire.

"And with that comes this recognition, oh, they know something that I don't know. I want them to do it because I know they can do it better than me. And they have wisdom that I want to learn from." -- Joe Hudson

The recognition is not abstract. It is the felt shift from "I'm telling you how to do this" to "I'm asking you how this should be done and I'm listening because you actually know." Until the leader has people they can have this experience with, the delegation problem will persist.

## The Inversion: Why Most Leaders Don't Do This

Most leaders hire people they can manage. The leader who is uncertain of their own authority or competence hires people who reinforce the leader's position by being below the leader. The team becomes a comfort zone. The company plateaus because the leader has built a team that cannot exceed them.

The principle is uncomfortable specifically because it requires the leader to be confident enough to hire people who outshine them. The leader has to be willing to look like the less-skilled person in the room on the specific dimension of the work. The leader has to trust their own contribution (vision, criteria-setting, resourcing) enough to not need to also be the most skilled at the doing.

The leader who cannot do this is leaking the discomfort into the hiring. They will keep hiring people they can outperform, and they will keep being the bottleneck on every piece of work, and the company will keep being bounded by their personal execution capacity.

## How This Connects to Trust

The principle reframes what "trust the team" means. The usual framing of trust is emotional: do you feel safe with this person? The structural framing is competence: do you believe this person will do this job better than you would? The latter form of trust is what makes delegation work.

"All of that is really critical in delegation. And if you don't have that in the delegate, like if you don't have that level of trust and/or if you want to have all the control to make sure that it goes right because nothing can be wrong, then, then you're going to have a problem." -- Joe Hudson

The leader who wants control because they don't trust the hire is going to micromanage. The leader who trusts the hire because the hire is actually better is going to delegate. The principle says the difference is upstream of the delegation: it is who you hired.

## What This Pairs With

The principle pairs with [[allow-mistakes-as-the-cost-of-real-delegation]]. Even people who are better at the job make mistakes. The leader has to be okay with that for the delegation to work. The hiring principle gets the right person; the mistakes principle keeps them in the role through the inevitable failures.

The principle pairs with [[intelligence-vs-wisdom]] and [[you-can-hire-anything-except-being-yourself]]. The leader is hiring wisdom, not just skill. The hire's wisdom is what the leader is leaning on. The hire might have less raw intellectual horsepower than the leader; what matters is that they have wisdom about the specific work the leader does not have.

The principle pairs with [[set-solution-criteria-have-the-team-solve-within-them]]. The leader sets the criteria; the team solves within them. The team that exceeds the leader at the solving is the team that can run this loop effectively.

## The Inverse Check

The principle gives the leader a diagnostic for the current team. For each person, ask: can I do their job better than they can? If the answer is yes, the hiring decision was wrong. The leader needs to either upgrade the person's role (move them to work the leader could not do better) or replace them.

The diagnostic is uncomfortable because it surfaces hires that need to be reckoned with. Most leaders have several. The principle gives the leader the question to make the reckoning specific.

## What This Is Not

**Not "hire people who intimidate you."** Better at the job is specific. The leader is not seeking people who outclass them in every dimension; the leader is seeking people who exceed them in the specific work being delegated. A leader who is good at strategy can hire a head of sales who is better at sales without needing the head of sales to also be better at strategy.

**Not abdication of the leader's authority.** The leader still owns the vision, the criteria, the direction. The hire owns the execution within those bounds. The principle is about delegation, not about reversing the chain of command. See [[set-solution-criteria-have-the-team-solve-within-them]].

**Not a license to surround yourself with adversaries.** The principle is about competence, not conflict. The hire who is better at the job and aligned with the leader's vision is the target. The hire who is better at the job and opposed to the vision is a different problem.

**Not a guarantee of delegation success.** The principle is the precondition. The leader still has to learn to delegate well — set criteria, ask the accountability questions, have the hard conversations, allow mistakes. The principle removes one structural barrier; the work of delegation continues.

## When This Comes Up

- A leader is asking how to delegate better and the diagnostic surfaces that the team is below their level on the work
- A founder is trying to scale and the company keeps plateauing at the founder's personal execution capacity
- A leader is re-doing their team's work and is trying to figure out why
- A leader has hired people they manage easily and is wondering why the company isn't moving
- A coachee is uncomfortable with the idea of being outshone by their hires

## Tone

Direct and structural. Joe presents this as a math problem, not a values claim. The math: if you can do the job better, the hire is wrong. Fix the math, then the delegation becomes available. The tone is not aspirational; it is operational.
