# Loving Someone Who Doesn't Love Themselves Is Like Dragging an Anchor

## The Principle

When a person does not love themselves, the people who try to love them feel the weight of it. The love does not flow into a person who is at war with themselves. It hits the self-rejection and bounces, or it gets absorbed and produces nothing. The lover is doing the work of two — loving the partner and trying to compensate for the partner's self-rejection — and the second half is impossible. The felt experience is of pulling a weight that does not move.

"It's really hard to love somebody who doesn't love themselves. It's a real complicated mess. And we've all done it. We all know what it's like to try to love somebody who's constantly not loving themselves. It's like dragging an anchor. It's just, 'Grrr,' the whole time. It's really difficult." -- Joe Hudson, Codependency: 3 Signs It's Not Love

## The Mechanism

The mechanism has two parts that interlock.

First, the person who does not love themselves presents an *image* instead of the actual self. They have decided the actual self is not lovable, so they offer a curated version for the love to land on. The partner's love, even when freely offered, cannot reach the actual person — it can only reach the image. The person experiences the love as not-for-them, because in a structural sense it is not. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]] and [[idea-of-self-vs-actual-self]].

Second, the person who does not love themselves cannot receive what arrives. Receiving requires being present in the body. The body is the place where love is felt. A person at war with their body and their self is not available to receive at that location. The partner's love is offered repeatedly and never lands.

The partner, doing this for months or years, accumulates the felt weight of trying. The trying is what produces the anchor sensation. Not the partner. The trying.

## What Makes the Anchor

The drag-weight is specifically composed of:

- The compliments that get rejected ("you're just saying that").
- The reassurances that do not stick (the partner says "I love you" and the rejection-loop produces "but you don't really know me").
- The accommodations made to avoid the partner's self-critical spiral (don't say this, don't bring that up, walk carefully here).
- The repeated reframes the lover offers about who the partner actually is, none of which the partner internalizes.
- The exhaustion of being the one steady source of esteem in the relationship.
- The grief of watching someone you love attack themselves and not being able to stop it.

None of these are the partner's failures of love. All of them are structural consequences of trying to deliver love to a person who is rejecting the receiver.

## The Symmetry: You Become the Anchor for Whoever Loves You

The principle is bidirectional. When you do not love yourself, you become the anchor for whoever is trying to love you. The codependence is not just hard on you. It is hard on the people closest to you in a specific structural way that they may not be able to name.

"And so when you are presenting yourself, you're making it really difficult for the other person to love you. And that is part of the thing of codependence. It's that when you are codependent, it makes it really hard for the other person to love you." -- Joe Hudson, Codependency: 3 Signs It's Not Love

This is one of the cleanest reframes of codependence as a relational cost rather than a personal failing. The person in codependence usually frames their behavior as "I'm trying so hard to be loved" or "I'm doing everything for the relationship." The structural fact is the opposite: the trying-to-be-loved-as-an-image is precisely what makes them hard to love.

The partner often does not have language for this. They just feel tired. They feel like the relationship is more work than it should be. They start pulling away without being able to say why. The pulling away is read by the codependent partner as evidence that they need to perform harder, which thickens the anchor.

## Why This Is Often Missed

The codependent's self-narrative is one of giving, sacrificing, putting the other first. The frame is "I am loving so hard, and they are not loving me enough back." From inside this frame, the felt heaviness in the partner is interpreted as proof of the partner's inadequacy.

The corrective frame: the felt heaviness in the partner is not proof of their inadequacy. It is proof that the love being offered cannot reach you, because you are not available to receive it. The work is not to demand more love from them. The work is to become available.

This is also the structural reason that some of the cleanest signs a relationship is recovering from codependence are reported by the *non-codependent partner*: "I feel like I can breathe again." "Loving you feels different now." "It's not as much work to be with you." These reports are direct readings of the anchor being released.

## The Exit

The exit is not on the partner's side. The partner cannot love harder out of the dynamic. The exit is on the side of the person who is not loving themselves: come back to the actual self, drop the presentation, let the love that arrives land on the person who is here rather than the version that is being performed.

See [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]] for the principle of why this exit is symmetric — coming back to love yourself expands your capacity to love them, and being available to be loved is what makes the loving easeful for them. See [[tell-yourself-you-love-yourself-in-a-mirror]] and [[love-yourself-the-way-you-always-wanted]] for the practices that begin the return.

The partner often does not need to wait for the full return. The first signs of the codependent partner turning toward themselves are felt as relief by the lover. The anchor lightens immediately. The dynamic begins to change before the work is complete.

## When Someone Experiences This

From the side of the person doing the dragging:
- The relationship feels like more work than it should.
- Compliments and affection seem to go in and disappear.
- The partner cycles through self-criticism, you reassure, the cycle repeats unchanged.
- You feel guilty for being tired of loving them, because they have done nothing "wrong."
- You start to wonder if you are the problem because the love you are offering does not seem to be helping.

From the side of the person being the anchor:
- A felt sense that the partner's love is for who they want you to be, not for you.
- An inability to take in compliments or expressions of love.
- An assumption that the partner will eventually see through you and leave.
- A constant low-grade performance of being lovable, exhausting to maintain.
- Loneliness inside a loving relationship.

## Tone

Plain and unsparing. The metaphor itself does the work. Joe delivers it without softening — the "Grrr, the whole time" sound makes the felt experience auditory. The bluntness is the kindness: the codependent person has rarely heard the dynamic named from this angle, and the recognition often produces relief rather than shame, because it finally explains why the people they love seem so tired.
