# Performance Is About State, Not Framework

## The Principle

In any high-stakes performance domain — speaking, coaching, parenting, leading, performing, intimate conversation — the quality of the output is determined more by the state the performer is in than by the technique they execute. State holds when the situation changes. Framework breaks at the first deviation.

"I think speaking is about state. It's about tapping into forgetting about the fact that you're speaking... The moment you start thinking about it and trying to hit a framework or trying to get the words right, you're out of the flow." -- Tristan de Montebello, Speaking Authentically with Tristan de Montebello

This is not an argument against frameworks. Frameworks are useful as scaffolding while the state is being developed and as orientation when the state is unreliable. But once the state is established, the framework recedes. The performer operates from the state directly, and the framework that was once needed becomes irrelevant — or worse, becomes the thing that pulls the performer out of the state.

## Why Frameworks Break and States Don't

A framework is a sequence. Step one, step two, step three. The performer executes the sequence. As long as the situation matches what the sequence anticipated, the framework works.

But situations rarely match. A speech runs long and a section has to be cut. A coachee says something the framework did not anticipate. A team meeting goes a direction the agenda did not include. A child has a meltdown the parenting book did not describe. In each case, the framework breaks. The performer who was relying on the framework has nowhere to go.

A state holds across situations. A speaker in flow can respond to any deviation because the flow is the source. A coach in presence can meet any client material because the presence is the source. A parent in connection can meet any child behavior because the connection is the source. The state generates the next move from whatever is actually in front of the performer, rather than from a pre-stored sequence.

This is why state-based performers look impressive in ways framework-based performers do not. The framework-based performer is impressive when the situation cooperates and disastrous when it does not. The state-based performer is consistent. The audience experiences this as competence, but the actual mechanism is the underlying state, not the visible execution.

## State and Framework Are Not Opposed

A subtlety worth holding: state and framework are not in opposition. They operate at different layers.

The framework is the substrate. The state is the integration of the substrate.

Tristan is explicit that the speaker who delivers without a script has done extensive preparation. The essence of what they want to convey is held clearly. The bookmarks along the way are known. The performer has done 100 reps in similar conditions. None of this is "no framework." It is framework that has been so thoroughly integrated that the performer is no longer consciously executing it. The framework is in the body. From the body, state-based performance becomes possible.

The trap is treating the framework as the destination. The framework is the floor, not the ceiling. The performer who has memorized every step and cannot move beyond the steps is operating below capacity. The performer who has integrated the steps into a state has integrated the framework into themselves and can operate from a fuller place.

## The Chain vs. The Tree

Tristan's specific image for this in speaking: most people think about a speech as a chain. A leads to B leads to C all the way to Z. Each link is a separate piece. If any link breaks, the whole chain is broken and the speaker is lost.

The state-based approach is the tree. There is one essence — the one thing the speaker wants to convey. There are bookmarks along the way the speaker wants to hit. But the rest is a tree of branches. If one branch breaks off, the tree is still a beautiful tree. The speaker still has access to everything else. The deviation is not a failure; it is just navigating a different branch.

"Now you had access to a tree with tons of branches where it doesn't matter if you snap one branch off, you still have a beautiful tree. So now you're just navigating this beautiful 3-dimensional thing that's your mind." -- Tristan de Montebello

See [[chain-vs-tree-thinking-for-content]] for the full development. The chain is the framework. The tree is the state. The state-based performer can do what the framework-based performer cannot: respond to whatever shows up without panic.

## The Domains Where This Applies

The principle generalizes:

- **Speaking.** State-based speakers discover what they are saying as they say it. Framework-based speakers read from a pre-written script. See [[speaking-authentically]].
- **Coaching.** State-based coaches follow what the client produces. Framework-based coaches execute a sequence. See [[presence-over-technique-in-coaching]] and [[coach-as-follower-not-director]].
- **Leadership.** State-based leaders meet whatever shows up. Framework-based leaders run their playbook regardless of context.
- **Parenting.** State-based parents respond to the actual child in the actual moment. Framework-based parents apply techniques. See [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]].
- **Negotiation.** State-based negotiators read the room. Framework-based negotiators run their tactic.
- **Decision-making.** State-based deciders feel through what is here. Framework-based deciders execute a decision tree.

In each domain, the framework-based performer can be effective in stable conditions and falls apart in unstable ones. The state-based performer handles instability as a default. This is why mastery in any of these domains tends to look like state, not like framework — by the time you are good, the framework has receded into the body.

## How State Is Developed

State is not bypassable. You cannot read about state and have state. The development is somatic and incremental.

The recipe Tristan describes for speaking generalizes:

- **Real reps in real conditions.** Practice in environments that approximate the actual environment. Not isolated reading. Not solo practice with no audience. The body has to encounter the actual stress for the state to develop the way it needs to develop.

- **Short reps with feedback.** Each rep should be short enough that the state can be felt and remembered. Each rep should be followed by feedback that names what was different. Many short reps with feedback develop state faster than a few long reps without.

- **Feedback that points at the state, not the technique.** "You looked nervous" is technique feedback. "You were rushing — what would it have been like to take a breath there?" is state feedback. The performer needs to learn what their state feels like, not what their performance looks like.

- **Welcoming the body sensations the channel requires.** The state cannot establish if the performer is fighting the nerves, the adrenaline, the exposure. The fight is what prevents the state. See [[the-emotional-experience-decides-the-channel]].

- **Trust that the subconscious will deliver.** The state requires faith that the butler will show up. See [[the-butler-metaphor]] and [[faithing-faith-as-verb]]. The framework-based performer is hedging against the possibility that nothing will come. The state-based performer is in the orientation where what needs to arrive will arrive.

## Why Coaches Should Care

This principle has direct implications for how coaching is delivered. A coach who teaches frameworks is producing framework-based performers. A coach who teaches state is producing state-based performers. The two outputs are different in kind.

The framework-teacher gives the coachee a script. The script is comforting. The coachee feels prepared. The script will work in stable conditions and break in unstable ones. The coachee will return for more frameworks when the first one fails.

The state-teacher gives the coachee a relationship to themselves. The relationship is uncomfortable at first because it has no script. The coachee has to develop the capacity to be with whatever comes up. Once developed, the relationship works in any condition. The coachee does not need to return for more — they have the substrate from which any technique can be derived as needed.

Joe's coaching is heavily state-focused. The frameworks (VIEW, the Matrix, the Golden Algorithm) are real and useful, but they are scaffolding for the development of state. The state — emotional fluidity, presence, listening, the relationship to oneself — is what the work is actually building. Once the state is in place, the frameworks have done their job.

## The Live Demonstration: Same Words, Five States

The cleanest live demonstration of this principle is the [[say-the-same-sentence-five-ways]] move. Have the speaker deliver the exact same sentence five times, each from a different state — armor, ego-protected, not protected, vulnerable, fully upright. The words are identical across all five deliveries. The listener's experience is dramatically different in each one.

In Joe's coaching session with Savannah, the same sentence — "What can we do to make sure that we both feel heard in this situation?" — was delivered five different ways. Joe's feedback during the exercise is the demonstration of the principle's effect on reception:

- After the armor version and the ego-protected version: "Both of those two make my skin want to crawl. I'm fucking out the door."
- After the un-protected version: "There's just the desperation coming through."
- After the vulnerable version: a softer reception, but still incomplete.
- After the upright version: "I'm with you now."

Same words. Five different reactions in the listener. The framework — what to say — was held constant. The state — what was present in the speaker — was the variable. The variable mattered more than the constant.

The implication is uncomfortable for anyone who has been trying to solve a relationship through better phrasing: the phrasing is not the lever. The state from which the phrasing is delivered is the lever. A perfectly-chosen sentence delivered from armor lands like armor. An imperfect sentence delivered from upright lands like upright. The state wins every time.

"You could probably map how much you're chasing for my understanding and how much I'm backing the fuck away." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?

The state-based teaching is most easily transmitted not by argument but by this kind of demonstration. The speaker hears in their own delivery, through the coach's feedback, that what changed was not the words but the place the words came from. The recognition is in the body.

## The Anti-Pattern

The opposite of this principle, and a common pitfall: treating the framework as the source. "If I just learn the right framework, I will be able to do X." This collapses the developmental work into a content-acquisition problem. The framework is consumed, the substrate is unchanged, the performance does not improve, and the consumer concludes they need more frameworks.

The right move when a framework is not working is usually not "find a better framework." The right move is "what state am I operating from when I try to use this framework, and is that the state from which this framework can do its work?" Most frameworks work fine when the user is in a useful state. Most frameworks break when the user is in a useless state. The intervention is at the state level.

## The Felt Sense of State

A state-based performance has a particular signature, both for the performer and the audience.

For the performer: time slows slightly. The body softens. The next move arrives without construction. There is delight, often surprise — "where did that come from?" Energy rises rather than falls. After the performance, the performer often cannot recall the details of what they did or said. The state was what was happening, not the content.

For the audience: the performance feels alive. The audience does not feel managed. There is responsiveness — the performer is meeting them, not delivering at them. The audience often experiences the performance as more meaningful than a content analysis of it would predict. They are receiving the state as much as the content.

A framework-based performance has the opposite signature. The performer is one beat ahead, executing a plan. The audience can feel they are being processed rather than met. The content may be technically correct and emotionally inert.
