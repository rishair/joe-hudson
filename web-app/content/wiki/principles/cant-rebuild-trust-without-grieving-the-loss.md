# Can't Rebuild Trust Without Grieving the Loss

## The Principle

When someone has been through a loss -- a breakup, the end of a marriage, a bad business partnership, a friendship that failed, any rupture of trust -- and finds themselves unable to trust again afterward, the standard response is to work on trust directly. Try to be open again. Try to give the next person a chance. Try not to scan for red flags. Work on the trust issue.

This is the wrong order. The inability to trust is the diagnostic, not the problem. The problem underneath is incomplete grief. When grief moves all the way through, what is left is not vigilance and self-protection; what is left is nothing to be afraid of. The fear that registers as inability-to-trust is the fear of what has not been felt. The grief is the work.

"When the grief moves all the way through, there's nothing to be scared of. So when I see people having a hard time trusting after a breakup or a marriage or bad business partnership or something like that, it typically means that they haven't grieved the whole thing." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The prescription is direct. When someone presents with an inability to trust after a loss and asks how to rebuild trust: the answer is grieve some more. The trust will not be rebuilt by an act of will or by deciding to trust. The trust returns by itself once the grief has actually moved.

## The Mechanism

What is happening when someone cannot trust after a loss:

The relationship (or partnership, or friendship) was held together by an identity in the person -- the way they needed to be in that relationship, the configurations they tolerated, the parts of themselves they brought, the parts they suppressed. When the relationship ended, that identity was injured. The injury is real. The natural response is to mourn the injury -- to grieve the identity that was structured around the relationship, the time invested, the version of self that no longer has a place to land.

If that grief is allowed to complete, the identity dissolves. The injury heals. The person who emerges from the grief is not the same person who entered the relationship. They have a different relationship to themselves and a different shape of available relating to others.

If the grief is not allowed to complete -- if the person rushes to "move on," refuses to feel the loss, tries to skip to trust -- the identity stays in place. The injury stays in place. The person is now trying to relate from inside an injured identity that has not been allowed to heal. The body knows the injury is there. The body's response is protection: hypervigilance, scanning for the next threat, inability to be open. The body is doing exactly the right thing given what it has been left with. The person reads this as "I can't trust." The body is reading it as "this identity is still injured and you are asking me to expose it again to the same kind of pain."

The grief is what completes the injury. Once the injury is completed, the body no longer needs to protect what was hurt -- because what was hurt has been allowed to die. There is nothing left to protect.

## The "Grieve Some More" Coaching Move

When Joe encounters someone in this configuration, the response is direct:

"If somebody's grieved and said, all right, now I'm -- had to find trust, I'd be like, you need to grieve some more." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The move bypasses the trust-question entirely. The trust-question is the symptom. The work is the grief. Going at the trust-question directly produces effort without movement; going at the grief produces the trust as a byproduct.

What "grieve some more" looks like in practice:

- Letting the body have the sounds it needs to make (often more anger, fear, sadness than the person expected)
- Not managing the timeline -- the grief takes as long as it takes
- Expecting the grief to move through multiple emotions, not just sadness (see [[grief-is-many-emotions-not-just-sadness]])
- Letting the grief expand outward -- often it starts with the immediate loss and ends up touching the childhood configurations that prepared the slot the loss filled
- Not requiring a "reason" to feel the grief; the body knows what it has not finished
- Stopping the scanning-for-threats (because the scanning is doing the work the grief should be doing)

See [[joes-friend-yuma-grief-drives]] for the canonical example of what full grief actually looks like when allowed.

## The Specific Domains This Applies To

The principle applies across multiple domains where trust has been broken:

- **Romantic relationships.** The breakup or divorce where the next person cannot be trusted. Joe names this directly.
- **Marriages.** The longer-term partnership that ended. The trust-issue often surfaces years after the marriage ended, not immediately.
- **Business partnerships.** Joe explicitly extends the principle to "bad business partnership." The structural mechanics are the same -- an identity organized around the partnership was injured by its ending.
- **Friendships.** The close friend who betrayed you, the long friendship that ended badly.
- **Family relationships.** Parent or sibling relationships that have ruptured.
- **Group or community trust.** The community that betrayed you, the team that turned on you, the organization that failed you.

In each case, the structure is the same: an identity was organized around the relational structure; the structure ended; the identity was injured; the injury needs to be grieved; without the grief, the inability to trust is what is left.

## The Vigilance-as-Stuck-Grief Reframe

A useful diagnostic: when a person describes their post-loss state, what do they report?

If they report vigilance ("I'm always scanning for red flags"), scanning ("I can't stop watching for the warning signs"), hypervigilance ("I see narcissists everywhere now"), or chronic openness-to-attack ("I can't let anyone in") -- this is the report of unfinished grief. The vigilance is doing the work the grief should be doing. The body is protecting an injury it has not been allowed to close.

If they report grief ("I'm still mourning what we had," "I'm still sad about this"), even if the grief is unwelcome or destabilizing -- the work is in progress. The recommendation is to keep going.

The two reports are structurally opposite even though both feel like difficulty after loss. The vigilance is the avoidance of the grief. The grief is the work.

See [[defensive-scanning-for-the-feared-threat]] for the read that names the vigilance pattern. The article specifically addresses post-narcissist hypervigilance, but the principle generalizes to all post-loss vigilance.

## The Inverse: When Trust Comes Back Easily

The principle's mirror form: when grief has completed, trust comes back easily. The person who has fully grieved is not trying to trust; they are just available. The wariness that should have been present has dropped because the body no longer has anything to protect from a re-injury. The injury has been allowed to be the injury and to complete.

This is sometimes a marker for the person themselves -- "wait, am I supposed to be more cautious?" The body has done the work and the mind has not caught up. The body has stopped needing to protect; the mind is still running the script that says it should be on guard. The body's report is the more accurate read.

The trust that returns after completed grief is not naive. The person can still see things clearly. They can still spot configurations that would harm them. What is missing is the chronic on-guard posture that confuses every possibility of contact with the threat of repeat-injury. The clear-seeing remains. The chronic protection drops.

## The Specific Application to Trust-After-Narcissism

A specific case Joe has worked with extensively in another transcript (see [[how-do-i-stay-open-after-narcissistic-abuse]] and [[without-grief-we-recreate-the-pattern]]'s narcissism-recovery section): after a narcissistic relationship, the standard advice is to learn the patterns of narcissism, watch for red flags, scan for warning signs.

The vigilance is the wrong work. The grief is the work. The relationship was organized around an identity slot that fit the narcissist. The slot is what gets injured by the ending. Without grief, the slot stays in place; the next narcissist who fits the slot can still arrive. With grief, the slot dissolves. The next narcissist registers as the wrong shape and produces no pull at all.

The same principle applies to trust after narcissism. The inability to trust is the slot in injured form. The grief is what dismantles the slot. Once dismantled, the person can spot a narcissist instantly (they always could) and loses interest, and can trust non-narcissistic partners cleanly because the slot that needed protection is gone.

The principle is one application of the broader rule. The broader rule: any post-loss inability to trust is unfinished grief. The narcissism-recovery case is a particular instance with particular features, but the structure is the same.

## When This Principle Comes Up in Coaching

- When someone presents the post-breakup, post-divorce, or post-betrayal inability to trust as the work
- When someone is asking how to be open again after being hurt
- When someone is doing trust-work directly (deciding to trust, trying to be vulnerable again) and reporting that it does not stick
- When someone is doing hypervigilance work directly (trying to stop scanning, trying not to look for red flags) and reporting that they cannot stop
- When the question "how do I learn to trust again?" is the presenting question
- When someone is asking why they keep choosing the same kind of partner / business partner / friend even after deciding they would not
- When the trust-issue has lasted longer than the person expected

## Tone

Direct, structural, with the matter-of-fact authority of someone who has watched the pattern play out enough times to know the answer is reliable. Joe delivers this teaching as a prescription, not as a possibility. The directness is part of the relief -- the person who has been trying to fix trust directly is being told that the work is elsewhere, that the trust will return on its own once the right work is done.

The teaching also carries a quality of permission. Many people are trying to stop grieving (because they want to move on) at the very moment when more grieving is what the system needs. The principle gives permission to do the grief, to take the time it takes, to expect more rather than less. The relief is in the redirection of effort to the place where effort actually works.
