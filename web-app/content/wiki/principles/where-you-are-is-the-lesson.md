# Where You Are Is the Lesson (Whatever You're Doing Is the Right Answer)

## The Principle

The question "is this the right path?" presupposes that there is a wrong path. There usually is not. The path you are on contains the lesson you are currently positioned to learn. Trying to figure out whether you should be on a different path is itself a way of leaving the path you are on, which means leaving the lesson it has for you.

"Whatever you're doing is the right answer, right? If you're living on top of the disco, that's the right answer. If you've left the disco, that's the right answer. It's like that both of them are paths to freedom. What, like, what are you going to choose to do?" -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

This is not a passive resignation. It is not "everything happens for a reason." It is a structural observation: the situation you are in is the situation through which growth is currently available to you. Other situations would have other lessons. The lessons are not ranked. You are not behind because you are in the situation you are in.

## The Ramana Story

The clearest formulation in the contemplative literature is attributed to Ramana Maharshi. Someone asks him: "Should I give up being a householder and go and join a monastery?" Ramana says no. The man gets defensive: "You don't know me, how could you possibly say that?" The defense is itself the data -- the man is identified with the should-I/should-I-not oscillation. Ramana's response: "You should be sitting here asking me a question is what you should be doing."

The answer points at what was happening in the moment of the asking. The seeker came looking for the answer to "should I leave?" The teacher answered the actual question, which was about the present moment, not about the future decision.

"That's like somewhat unsatisfactory, somewhat points to like the freedom that you're looking for is in this moment. Don't like -- what makes you leave the moment? Because it's right here, right now." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

The teaching is not that the seeker should never leave the householder life. It is that the seeker is currently a person sitting in front of Ramana with a question. The lesson is right there. The future decision will arrive in its own time, after the present lesson has been received.

## The Generalization

Once seen, the principle generalizes to any "should I be here or somewhere else?" question. The question is almost always a way of leaving the moment in pursuit of an imagined better moment. The leaving is the obstacle to whatever growth was available where the person is.

The principle does not say the person cannot leave. It says: be in this moment first. The leaving, if it is the right move, will become clear from inside the present moment. The leaving made from inside the should-I/should-I-not oscillation is a different kind of move -- it is a move motivated by the avoidance of being where you are.

"It's a much more productive thing to say, oh, right now is the lesson that I need to learn than it is to say, where am I supposed to be? What am I supposed to be doing? I, what if I lose the money? What do I need to get there? I need X, Y, and Z to get there. Far more productive to just be like, oh, the thing that I need to get there is presenting itself to me right now. This moment, not the next moment." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

## Why Trust Makes It Faster

The principle has an addendum: trust accelerates it. The person who can hear themselves and know which way to go covers the same ground faster than the person who can only move by external confirmation.

"Trusting yourself makes it a little bit quicker. Like, it's like, oh, I trust that I can listen to myself and know which way to go. It makes the whole journey a little bit quicker." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

The trust referred to is not a confidence that you have the right answer. It is more basic. It is the trust that wherever you are is where you are, and that being where you are is sufficient. From inside that trust, the next move emerges. From outside it, the person spins between options.

See [[trust-the-awareness-dont-add-effort]] for the related principle in the context of insight. The shape is the same: trust the seeing that is happening; do not add a layer of effort on top of it.

## What the Principle Does Not Mean

The principle does not mean magical thinking. It does not say "everything is exactly as it should be" in a mystical sense. It does not foreclose action. It does not say the current situation is good.

"I don't mean that like you're supposed to be like in a magical thinking kind of way. There seems to be some sort of interesting thing that occurs around, like, we find ourselves where we're supposed to be. But I mean it more of like, if that's your mindset, if your mindset is like, oh, this is where I'm supposed to be, this is where the opportunity is found to learn about myself, to grow, to lean in, then that's the most productive idea on how to face a moment, no matter what the moment is." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

The principle is operational, not metaphysical. It is the most productive stance to take toward whatever moment you are in. The alternative stance -- "I shouldn't be here, I should be somewhere else" -- is reliably less productive. It also tends to keep the person in the place they say they shouldn't be, because the resistance to where they are is itself the thing that holds them there.

The principle also does not preclude leaving. "I should be here, potentially to be here so I can learn to leave" -- that is fine. The principle is about the orientation, not the eventual movement. You can be fully present to a situation and choose to leave it from inside that presence. The presence is what makes the leaving clean.

## The Right Question Is Not Stay-or-Leave

A specific implication: the right question is rarely the stay-or-leave binary. The binary is the surface form of a deeper question, which is usually some version of "what is the lesson here, and am I willing to receive it?"

The person stuck in the stay-or-leave loop is often not actually weighing two options. They are using the loop to avoid being in the current situation while not having to actually leave it. The loop is the avoidance. The loop can run for years without resolving because resolving it is not its function. Its function is to keep the person from being where they are.

When this loop is named, the person often experiences relief. The pressure of "I need to figure out the right answer" lifts. The actual question -- what is being avoided in this moment? -- becomes available. See [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]] for the related principle that the felt experience of "needing to decide" is itself a signal of fear, not of a real branch.

## The Eventual Answer

A small note about timing. The seeker who comes to Ramana might receive the answer ten years later. The answer often arrives well after the question is asked, in a moment when the person had stopped expecting it. The mistake is to require the answer to arrive on the timetable of the asking.

"Maybe they needed to get the answer 10 years later." -- Joe Hudson, What We're Afraid to Lose

This is not a delay-tactic. It is an honest acknowledgment of how growth tends to work. The seeker who can hold the question loosely, continuing to be present to the moments as they arrive, often finds that the answer emerges in its own time. The seeker who grips the question tightly and demands resolution often blocks the very emergence they are demanding.

## In Coaching

When a person presents a stay-or-leave question, the move is not to help them weigh the options. It is to name the principle. The question itself is the dodge of the moment. The work is to be in the moment, find the lesson that is currently present, receive it. The stay-or-leave question will resolve itself from inside that work.

This is also a guard against the coach getting pulled into solving the surface question. Coaches who answer the stay-or-leave question reinforce the structure that produced it. The honest move is to redirect to what is happening now -- in the body, in the relationship, in the felt experience.

The Ramana version of this is uncomfortable for the seeker because it does not give them what they came for. The coach who can hold the discomfort of not-giving-the-answer is offering the seeker the thing they actually need. See [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]] for the related principle.

## Anti-Pattern

The principle can be misused as a justification for staying in actively harmful situations. "I'm supposed to be here" can become "therefore I should not act to change my circumstances." This is a misapplication. The principle is about orientation toward the present, not about the management of one's life. Mandela was in prison and was free, but when the door opened he walked out. He did not stay because he had been finding the lesson. He had been finding the lesson and then took the action that became available. See [[mandela-empowerment]].

The principle also does not say "endure abuse." If the situation is abusive, leaving it is often the work that is being asked for. The principle says to be present to the moment, which includes being present to "this is abusive and I am going to act on that." The presence does not require the staying.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A person presents a stay-or-leave question with significant gripping energy.
- A person asks "is this the right path?" with the assumption that there is a wrong one.
- A person is using the should-I/should-I-not loop to avoid being present to current circumstances.
- A person describes spiritual or career uncertainty in terms of "I'm not sure I'm doing the right thing."
- A person is restless inside their current life and assumes the restlessness is information about which path to choose.
- [[nothing-is-required-for-freedom]] has been named and the person asks "well then how do I know what to do?"

## Tone

Spacious, slightly cryptic. The principle is delivered with the tone of someone who has stopped trying to figure out the right path. Joe is reporting from a position of having stopped the should-I/should-I-not loop, not exhorting from the outside.
