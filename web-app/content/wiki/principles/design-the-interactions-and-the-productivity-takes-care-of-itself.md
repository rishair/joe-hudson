# Design the Interactions and the Productivity Takes Care of Itself

## The Principle

Productivity is mostly a problem of design, not a problem of discipline. The person who spends time designing how their interactions, their calendar, and their work-environment are structured ends up needing far less in-the-moment discipline to be productive. The person who keeps their design loose and relies on in-the-moment discipline ends up exhausted by the constant decisions and corrections.

The design layer is upstream of the discipline layer. Fix design and most of the discipline problems dissolve. Try to fix discipline without fixing design and the problems regenerate every day.

## Joe's Words

"I work off my calendar, and I have a very very clear and deliberate way in which my calendar gets made. Part of what was really important to me was that I didn't have weight in my productivity. I wasn't spending 2 hours a day organizing all my productivity tools so that I could be productive that day. I could not do Slack. So I just -- no, I will not do Slack. It is -- it was just so not productive for me. What I've noticed is that if I really spend a lot of time on the design of my interactions then most of the productivity is taken care of naturally." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

Two specific design moves are visible in this paragraph:

1. **Refusing channels that aren't productive.** "I just -- no, I will not do Slack." The refusal is the design decision. Once Slack is off the table, no daily discipline is required to avoid it. The decision is made once, structurally, and the productivity follows.

2. **No weight in the productivity tools themselves.** Most productivity systems require ongoing maintenance to keep working. Joe's design point is to keep the system light enough that maintaining it doesn't itself consume the time it was meant to save.

## What the Principle Catches

Several common productivity-failure modes are caught at the design layer:

- **The productivity-tool-bloat trap.** Brett names this directly: "You can go through your life stacking new productivity tools, hacks, tricks on top of one another until you have a palimpsest, one of my favorite words, like a bunch of spaghetti code of the ways that you structure your productivity and end up in a mess or lost in it." The design move here is to remove tools, not add them.

- **The constant context-switching trap.** A day that mixes coaching, podcasts, business meetings, and teaching produces more drain than the same hours grouped by type. The design move is to group by type at the calendar level. See [[context-switching-drains-energy]].

- **The decision-fatigue trap.** Every decision in the day costs. The design move is to make decisions once at the design level so they don't need to be remade in the moment. See [[avoid-the-decision-by-design]].

- **The discipline-required-to-resist-distraction trap.** Apps are designed to engage. Resisting them requires discipline. The design move is to remove access at the structural level so resistance isn't needed.

## The Reverse Pattern (Discipline Without Design)

The opposite shape is a person who is highly disciplined but exhausted by the discipline. Each day requires fresh acts of will: not to check email, to focus, to take breaks, to stop working on time. The discipline produces the productivity but at high cost. The cost compounds. Eventually the discipline breaks (or the body does) and the pattern collapses.

The diagnostic: a person who reports being "really disciplined" and also exhausted is usually missing the design layer. The discipline is doing work the design should be doing.

## The Three Patterns Joe Catches With Design

The same teaching surfaces three specific places Joe has used design to remove discipline-cost:

1. **No Slack.** Removed at the structural level. No daily decision about whether to check Slack today.

2. **Calendar-driven, not list-driven.** "I don't even work off of a list in a day. If I work off of anything, I work off my calendar." The calendar is the design. The to-do list, used as the daily driver, requires constant in-the-moment decisions about what to do next; the calendar pre-makes those decisions.

3. **Grouped-by-type days.** "If I can do one whole day of coaching and then one day of podcasts and then one day of teaching, I have far more energy than if I am having a business meeting followed by a coaching meeting." The grouping is a design choice that produces more energy without requiring any in-the-moment effort.

Each of these is a design move. Each removes a category of decision from the day. The cumulative effect is a day with much less weight on it.

## The Awareness-Replaces-Structure Long Arc

Brett observes the deeper development of the principle:

"For somebody who recognizes that they've gotten to say productivity bloat and they start taking things away, one of the things that I noticed that you start taking away is you stop engaging in certain kinds of patterns. Like what you described is you can join a meeting and you can see what is being productive and what isn't. And that sounds less like you're following a structure and more like you have developed the awareness of various patterns, those patterns, particularly the kinds of patterns that you might be prone to getting swept up in, then sort of the, the training wheels of structure to keep that from happening become less necessary until eventually they fall away." -- Brett Kistler, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

Joe confirms. The long arc: the design (structure) carries the early phase. As the person develops the awareness of what produces real productivity vs. spin, the structures become less necessary. The awareness replaces the structure. The structure was scaffolding for what the awareness now does directly.

This is a long-arc claim and easy to misuse. It does not mean "skip the structure if you're developed enough." It means the structure builds the awareness, and at some point the awareness can carry what the structure was carrying. The shortcut of skipping the structure without developing the awareness produces a person who is neither structured nor aware -- just busy.

## What This Principle Is Not

- It is not a productivity-system recommendation. The principle is meta: spend the energy at the design layer, not the discipline layer. The specific design choices vary by person.
- It is not anti-discipline. Discipline still has a role. The principle is that it should be applied to things that can't be designed out, not to things that should have been designed out.
- It is not the same as "be organized." Being organized usually means more structure (more tools, more systems, more processes). The principle here is often less structure: fewer tools, fewer channels, fewer recurring meetings, fewer commitments.

## When to Use the Principle

- When [[im-burned-out]] is present and the person reports being "really disciplined" while burned out.
- When [[i-keep-getting-stuff-done-but-nothing-changes]] and the person is reaching for more productivity tools.
- When the calendar is so packed that any in-the-moment design changes are impossible.
- When the person has accumulated a "palimpsest" of productivity tools that are themselves consuming hours per day.
- When the daily life requires constant willpower to resist distraction or maintain focus.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When [[productivity-as-fear-based-engine]] is running and the person is trying to fix it with more discipline.
- When [[context-switching-drains-energy]] has been named and the person needs the larger frame to act on it.
- When the productivity-tool-bloat has reached the point where the maintenance is the work.

## Tone

Practical, almost laconic. Joe delivers this with the energy of someone who has run the experiment and reports the result. The "I will not do Slack" is matter-of-fact, not embattled. The principle is presented as a structural observation: design fixes things that discipline cannot.
