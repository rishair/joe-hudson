# Doing Things for Love Is Prostitution, Not Love

## The Principle

Love produced by behavior is not love. The transaction structure that says "I do X, you give me love" cannot produce love regardless of how much X is performed. What it produces is a transaction. The person who receives the love is responding to the behavior, not to the person performing it. The person performing remains unloved while the behavior is paid for in love.

"There's no way that how much you produce or what you do for other people is going to get you more or less love. And if you say it that way, people immediately are, wait, no, that's — that can't be true. If I don't do things for people, they won't love me. If you're doing things for people to get them to love you, they're not loving you anyways. It's transaction. It's like saying giving somebody $100 to love you. It's not love, that's prostitution." -- Joe Hudson, You're Not Giving People the Chance to Love You

The metaphor is blunt because the structure is blunt. Sex paid for with money is not love because the structure is wrong. The same is true of love paid for with performance. The currency is different. The structural problem is identical. What is exchanged through transaction is not love because love is not the kind of thing that responds to transaction.

## Why People Resist This

The immediate response when the principle is stated is denial: "wait, that can't be true." The denial is structural too. The whole apparatus of doing-for-love is built on the assumption that the equation works. Discovering that the equation does not work invalidates the entire life-strategy built around it.

The fear: "if I don't do things for people, they won't love me." The fear is real-feeling because it traces to childhood, where the equation often actually held in some weakened form. Parents gave attention when chores were done. Teachers gave approval when grades arrived. Coaches gave inclusion when performance met the bar. The early environment trained the equation by partially reinforcing it. The strategy that was developed in response was rational given the data available at the time.

The strategy that was rational then is not rational now. The current environment includes the possibility of being loved without the performance. The strategy is blocking the recognition that this is possible. Dropping the strategy is what makes the alternative available. But the dropping is terrifying because the strategy has been the operating system for decades. See [[familiar-over-safe]] for the related dynamic.

## What Actually Happens in the Transaction

The mechanism of why transactional love does not produce felt love:

**The receiver is loving the behavior, not the person.** What the receiver is responding to is the dish being cooked, the help being offered, the listening being performed. The behavior is what reaches them. The person performing is hidden behind the behavior. The behavior gets the love. The person stays unmet.

**The person performing knows this somewhere.** The body registers the difference between being loved-as-self and being loved-as-performance. The performance-receiver does not feel met even when the performance is successful. The chronic dissatisfaction of the performer is the body's accurate registration that the equation is not delivering what it was supposed to deliver. The performer often interprets this as "I need to perform more" or "I need to perform better," which doubles down on the strategy and produces more of the same gap.

**The receiver eventually feels the transaction.** The receiver may initially accept the behavior as love. Over time, they often start to feel the absence of the actual person behind the behavior. They start to feel the performance, the management, the hidden ask. They may not be able to articulate what is wrong, but they pull back. The performer reads the pull-back as a sign that more performance is needed. The cycle accelerates while the actual contact recedes.

**The exchange is not symmetric.** Real love is bidirectional contact: two people present, each available, each receiving. The transactional version is unidirectional: the performer is giving, the receiver is receiving, and the performer is supposed to get love as compensation. But love is not the kind of thing that runs through compensation. It is the kind of thing that runs through mutual contact. The transactional setup precludes the mutual contact by setting one person in the giver role and the other in the receiver role.

## The Connection to Conflict Avoidance

The principle has a specific application to relationships where one partner is performing and the other is withdrawing. Conflict avoidance is one of the most common forms of doing-for-love: the avoider is suppressing what is true for them in order to keep the other person comfortable, in the hope that this comfort will be returned as love.

"What you do for other people is going to get you more or less love" — this is the operating equation in conflict avoidance. The avoider is doing the work of managing the partner's comfort. They expect, somewhere, that this work will produce love. It does not. It produces the absence of conflict, which is not the presence of love. The relationship slowly hollows out because the actual person of the avoider is not in it. See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]] for the dedicated principle on this dynamic.

The corrective: the truth has to be said. The truth-saying may produce conflict, which the avoider has organized their life around preventing. But the conflict-availability is what creates the space for the actual person to be in the relationship, which is what creates the possibility of love. The transaction has to be broken before love can arrive.

## The Connection to Caretaking and Obligation

The same principle is operating in [[i-caretake-people]] (caretaking as a love strategy) and [[obligation-as-management]] (acting from obligation rather than want).

In caretaking: the caretaker is performing care to receive love. The performance produces the appearance of love-giving but does not produce the experience of love either for the caretaker (who is busy performing) or for the recipient (who is being managed rather than met). The transaction structure prevents both sides from arriving at the actual love.

In obligation: the obligated action lacks the heart that the same action would have if it came from want. The receiver feels the difference. The obligated yes is not the loving yes. The performance is correct; the love is not in it. See [[guilt-cuts-off-natural-compassion]] for the principle that obligation severs the compassionate impulse from the action.

The pattern: any orientation that runs "I do X so that they will give me Y (love, approval, security)" is the transaction structure. The Y does not actually arrive. Even when the surface form of Y is received, the felt experience of Y is not.

## Why Real Love Cannot Be Transactional

The structural fact about love: love is responsive to who someone actually is, not to what they do. The behavior is a window the love may pass through, but the love is being received by the person, not by the behavior. If the person is not visible behind the behavior, the love has no target.

This is why the receiver who tries to love a behavior-performer reports a felt sense of "I can't quite reach them." The performer is hiding behind the performance. The would-be lover is reaching for the actual person and finding the performance in the way. The performer is reading this as evidence that they need to perform more, when the evidence is actually that they need to stop performing and let the actual person be visible.

The trade is the inverse of what the strategy assumes: stopping the performance reveals the person, which makes love possible. The strategy that was supposed to produce love is the structural blocker of love. The drop is what lets love arrive. See [[love-arrives-when-youre-not-scared-of-losing-yourself]] for the parallel structural account: love cannot arrive while the controller is still in charge.

## What Real Love Looks Like

Real love does not run on a contingency. It is not "I love you if you keep being X." It is "I love you," with the X dropping out of the equation. This does not mean the lover loves all behaviors equally; lovers can have preferences, can request changes, can name what they cannot tolerate. But the love itself is not gated by these.

The person doing the loving is responding to the actual person, including the parts the person did not perform for. The unloveliness, the awkwardness, the inconvenient truths — these are also being met. The love does not require them to be hidden. Often the love is deeper precisely because these parts are being included rather than excluded.

The performer who has been organizing life around the transaction cannot easily recognize this kind of love when it arrives. The performer is scanning for "are they loving the performance?" and the receiver is offering "I am loving you." The signal does not match the expected channel. The performer often dismisses the love or deflects it because it does not look like the love the strategy was reaching for. See [[i-cant-receive-compliments-or-love-from-my-spouse]] for the related dynamic.

The work of receiving real love includes letting go of the strategy that was supposed to produce it. The strategy has to be released for the recognition that real love is already available to land. See [[love-causes-annihilation]] for the deeper account of why real love is also annihilating: it dissolves the identity that was organized around earning it.

## What This Is Not

This is not a claim that you should never do things for the people you love. You should. Acts of care, service, gift-giving, presence — these are normal expressions of love. The principle is about the *orientation*. If the action arises from genuine care (no extraction expected), it is the natural expression of love. If the action arises from "I am doing this so they will love me," it is the transaction.

The test is internal: what is the felt motive of the action? If there is a "and then they will" attached to the action, the transaction is operating. If the action is happening because the doing-of-it is itself satisfying, the love is operating. The same surface action can be either, depending on the orientation.

This is not a claim that recipients of love-by-doing should call out the performer. The performer is often unaware that the strategy is operating. Calling it out tends to land as judgment. The corrective for the performer has to come from the performer's own recognition of the felt cost of the strategy. The recipient's role is to keep offering actual contact and to be honest about what they are experiencing, not to diagnose the performer.

This is not an argument against generosity, service, or care for others. The principle applies to the *motive*, not to the activity. Generous people are generous because they want to be, not because they are extracting love. The genuinely generous person and the love-extracting performer can look identical from outside. The difference is internal and felt by both giver and receiver, even when neither can articulate it.

## When This Principle Lands

The felt shifts in the person who has been running the transaction:

- **Recognition with grief.** "I have been doing this for years and it has not been working." The grief is the cost of seeing the wasted effort and the years of self-abandonment. The grief is part of the work; it does not need to be bypassed.
- **Terror briefly.** "If I stop performing, will they leave?" Some may. Some who would not have loved the actual person were always going to leave eventually. The terror is real and worth feeling rather than overriding.
- **A different kind of contact becomes available.** When the performer stops performing, the people in their life who are capable of loving the actual person can finally do so. The number of these people may be smaller than expected, but the quality of contact with each is dramatically higher.
- **Energy returns.** The energy spent on performance becomes available. Often the performer discovers they have far more to give than they thought, because they are no longer using their resources to produce the performance.
- **The need to give changes.** Giving from "this will earn me love" stops being interesting because the loop is exposed. Giving from genuine impulse becomes available because it is no longer contaminated by the extraction.

## The Operational Move

The practical move is the internal check: at the moment of doing something for someone, ask: "is there a 'and then they will' attached to this?" If yes, the transaction is operating. The action can still be done — but with the recognition that the and-then-they-will is not going to deliver. The action has to be evaluated for whether it is wanted for its own sake.

If the answer is "no, I want to do this regardless of what they give back," the action is the natural expression of love. Do it.

If the answer is "no, I do not want to do this regardless," the action is being done out of obligation or extraction. The question becomes: what is actually wanted here? Often the want is to be loved without having to perform. That want is real and worth feeling. The action that was going to be performed to earn the love is not going to produce the love. The want has to find another path, which usually involves [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] — saying the actual truth, including the want to be loved as-is.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the bluntness of someone naming a structural fact, not the warmth of someone consoling. The prostitution metaphor is deliberately strong. The strength of the language is calibrated to the strength of the denial the listener will reach for. A softer framing would let the listener slide off it. The bluntness keeps them with it.

The follow-up tone shifts. Once the structural truth is named, the work becomes gentle — the strategy is decades old, the body has organized around it, the dropping of it is hard. The naming is sharp; the holding of the person who is now seeing it is soft. Both registers are required.
