# Track Process Metrics, Not Outcome Metrics (Measure What People Can Control)

## The Principle

When designing metrics for a team or for yourself, track the inputs the person can directly control. Not the outputs that depend on factors outside their control. The outputs eventually matter; tracking them as primary metrics creates failure where the person had no leverage.

In sales: track the number of phone calls made, the number of pipeline moves, the number of meetings booked. The eventual revenue is what the company cares about, but revenue is downstream of dozens of factors the salesperson does not control. Phone calls are something the salesperson controls completely.

"Tracking sales data. Are you tracking how much money they brought in? Eventually you're gonna have to, but the best thing to track is how many phone calls they made. The best thing to track is how many moves down a pipeline did they make? Because then you're creating failure, you're creating success in something that they can actually do." -- Joe Hudson, Failure

## Why Outcome Metrics Produce Phantom Failure

An outcome metric measures whether a thing happened. Many things have to align for the outcome to happen: the salesperson has to make the call, the prospect has to be in the right mood, the budget has to exist, the timing has to work, the competitor has not just signed them, the product has to be a real fit. If any one of those misaligns, the outcome does not happen. The salesperson did their part. The outcome did not.

When the metric is the outcome, the salesperson's correct performance reads as failure. The salesperson made the call, ran the process, did everything they could do, and the metric says they failed. The metric is wrong. The metric was measuring something the salesperson did not control.

Over time, the metric installs the failure-frame on the person doing the right work. They begin to feel they are failing, which suppresses motivation, which reduces the calls, which reduces the eventual outcomes. The metric becomes the cause of the failure it was supposed to measure.

## Why Process Metrics Convert the Same Activity Into Success

A process metric measures the input. The salesperson made twenty calls today. Twenty calls happened. The metric is unambiguous. The salesperson can look at the day and see: I did the thing. The activity was performed.

Some of those calls will produce outcomes. Most will not. The base rate of conversion is what it is. But the salesperson's daily experience is now framed around what they actually did, not around what happened to land. The motivation stays available. The next day's calls happen.

Over time, the call count compounds. The conversion rate is what it is. The total outcomes grow because the total inputs grew. The outcome metric, which the company eventually needs to look at, looks better because the process metric was the one being tracked day-to-day.

## The Sequence Matters

"Eventually you're gonna have to" track outcomes. The company has to make money. The outcomes are real and they matter. The principle is not "ignore outcomes." The principle is about which metric drives day-to-day behavior and which metric is the eventual check.

The right sequence:
- Process metrics are primary. They drive the daily and weekly review. They determine recognition, conversation, and feedback.
- Outcome metrics are secondary. They are checked at longer intervals (monthly, quarterly) to confirm the process is producing the desired result over time.
- If the outcome metric stays flat while the process metric is healthy, the process needs to be examined. Either the wrong process is being executed correctly, or the conversion rate is genuinely too low.
- If the outcome metric is healthy while the process metric is weak, the team is lucky. Lucky is not a strategy. Strengthen the process.

The CEO who only tracks revenue is making decisions based on a metric that has a long lag and many confounding factors. The CEO who tracks revenue *and* the process metrics that produce revenue has both the eventual check and the daily lever.

## How This Principle Connects to the Failure Frame

The principle is the metric-design form of the failure-is-an-idea structural intervention. See [[failure-is-an-idea]] and [[iterative-mindset]].

If the only thing measured is outcome, every iteration that does not produce the outcome registers as failure. The habenula does its work. The team stops iterating.

If the iteration count itself is measured, every iteration registers as success regardless of outcome. The team keeps iterating. The base rate produces the outcomes over time.

The metric is one of the strongest interventions on the failure frame because the metric is what gets reviewed in performance conversations. The framing in the leadership memo can say "iterate, fail fast, etc." If the review conversation tracks only outcomes, the review conversation wins. The intervention has to happen at the metric level for the framing to operationalize.

This is structurally connected to [[reward-failure-in-the-organization]]. The reward intervention says "reward bold attempts." The metrics intervention says "track the attempts so the bold ones are visible and can be rewarded." Without the metric, the reward has no data to anchor to. With the metric, the bold attempt is countable, and what is countable can be celebrated.

## Where Else the Principle Applies

**Personal goals.** Lose weight is an outcome. Number of meals logged, workouts completed, hours of sleep, days of meditation: process metrics. Tracking the process metrics produces the outcome over time without the failure-frame attaching to any individual day.

**Writing.** Pages written today: process metric. Book published: outcome metric. The first is countable daily. The second arrives once over years. Tracking only the second is the recipe for the unfinished book.

**Parenting.** Did your kid succeed today is an outcome metric (and contains many things you do not control). Did you offer your kid the framing, presence, and attempts you wanted to offer is a process metric you control. See [[attempt-over-success-in-parenting]] for the family-system version.

**Coaching practice.** Did your client change is an outcome (depends on what they choose to do with the session). Did you stay in [[view]], notice what you noticed, ask the question that wanted to be asked is process. The first is partly out of your control. The second is what you do.

**Decision making.** Did the outcome of the decision turn out well is an outcome metric and partly random. Did you make the decision well at the time you made it is a process metric. See [[good-decision-is-how-you-make-it-not-the-outcome]] for the broader treatment.

## The Related Anti-Pattern: Metric-as-Goal

The principle here is about which metric to track. There is a parallel anti-pattern about what happens when any metric (process or outcome) is mistaken for the goal it was supposed to serve. The Instagram-followers-as-love-metric example, the wife's-current-mood-as-marriage-metric example, the $12M-as-goal example. In each case the metric was a proxy for an actual goal, the proxy got confused for the goal, the proxy got optimized, and the actual goal stayed flat or moved away.

"Oftentimes people confuse the metrics with the goals. ... For instance, people think the goal is I want to be loved. The metric is how many people are my friends on Instagram. And the metric becomes a goal, and then you're not loved." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The two principles work together. Choose process metrics rather than outcome metrics for the day-to-day tracking (this principle). And do not let any metric -- process or outcome -- become the goal it was supposed to serve (the related principle). See [[dont-confuse-the-metric-with-the-goal]] for the full anti-pattern, and [[principles-goals-metrics]] for the framework that keeps the metric in the dashboard role and the goal in the destination role.

## What This Principle Is Not

The principle is not "outcomes do not matter." Outcomes are how the world measures whether the activity was worth doing. The principle is about which metric drives the daily behavior. Outcomes still matter at the strategic review level.

The principle is also not a license for activity without purpose. Twenty phone calls a day to the wrong prospects, with the wrong message, in the wrong manner is not better than two calls done right. The process metric assumes the process itself is sound. The principle is "track the inputs to the process you have designed." If the process is broken, track its inputs and watch it stay broken.

The principle does not eliminate accountability. The salesperson who makes twenty calls but produces zero outcomes for a quarter is still accountable for examining what is going wrong. The accountability is at the process level (calls being made, conversion not happening, why not?) rather than at the outcome level (no revenue, you failed).

Joe delivers this principle with the cadence of practical advice. The voice is calm, almost engineering-like. The point is operational: here is the design choice, here is what it produces, here is why most organizations get it wrong. The conviction comes from having seen the failure mode at scale.
