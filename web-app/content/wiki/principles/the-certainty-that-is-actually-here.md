# The Real Certainty Is in the Now and in How You Want to Be

## The Principle

The mistake at the heart of false certainty is looking for certainty in the wrong place. The seeker looks at the future and asks "what is going to happen?" Then, finding no answer, they either invent one (optimism, pessimism, realism-that-collapses) or freeze.

The actual certainty available to a human being is not in the future. It is in the now, and in the choice about how to be inside the now.

"The certainty almost always lies in how you want to be, in what is available for you in the moment. That's where the certainty almost always lies. It's not going to be in the future. It's not going to be in the past. It is in the — I am certain I want connection with you right now. I am certain that this is how I want to be in this moment." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

## What Is Actually Certain

The catastrophizing brain catastrophizes because its problem-solving capacity has gone offline. The cortisol is too high. The certainty available to the body is being ignored in favor of the false-certainty being manufactured in the head.

What is actually certain — observable, real, available right now:

- You are here.
- You are safe in this moment (in most cases — the principle assumes the person is not in literal acute danger).
- You can identify who you love.
- You can identify the person in front of you who loves you.
- You can identify how you want to be in this moment.
- You can identify what you want right now (a glass of water, connection with this person, a deep breath).

These are the available certainties. They are small. They are real. They are stable across external chaos because they do not depend on external chaos.

"The brain catastrophizes because we've got too much cortisol going on. Our capacity to problem solve goes away. But if you can focus on the certainty, that often can relieve the system enough so that you can get into regulation." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

Naming the actually-certain is a regulation move. It is also a recovery of access to perception that the cortisol had occluded.

## The Stockdale-Style Internal Certainty

Beyond the moment-by-moment certainty of safety and connection, there is a second kind of certainty available: the certainty about how one is going to be inside whatever happens.

"I see a reality that I am going to live into today. I see a reality where we as human beings are better, more equipped, more heartfelt, more open-hearted in 20 years. And I'm gonna take this forest fire that's happening and I'm gonna use it to plant the next generation of seeds. And I'm gonna live like that, or I'm gonna live like I'm scared to death and that everything's gonna go to shit." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

This is not a prediction. The person who lives into "I am going to be open-hearted in this" does not know whether they will survive what is coming. They know how they intend to be inside whatever does come. This is the certainty Stockdale's surviving POWs had. They did not know they would get out. They knew how they would be while still inside.

See [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]] for the broader frame and [[stockdale-paradox]] for the canonical example. The principle here is the underlying mechanism: this kind of "I am going to be this way" commitment is a genuine certainty — not a fantasy about outcome, but a commitment about being.

## Why This Reframe Heals the Catastrophizing Brain

The catastrophizing brain is trying to find ground. The ground it is reaching for — "what is going to happen?" — does not exist. The reach itself maintains the cortisol that is preventing the brain from functioning.

When the person is shown the certainty that is actually available — that they are here now, that they are loved, that they can choose how to be — the reach for the impossible ground can relax. Once the reach relaxes, the cortisol drops. Once the cortisol drops, problem-solving comes back online. Now the person can actually respond to what is in front of them.

The sequence: false-certainty-seeking generates cortisol; cortisol generates dysfunction; dysfunction generates more false-certainty-seeking. The intervention is to name the actual certainty, which interrupts the seeking, which drops the cortisol.

This is the third move in the practice [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty]].

## What This Principle Replaces

It replaces the cultural default of looking for certainty in predictions. The default assumes that emotional stability requires certainty about outcomes. The default is wrong. Emotional stability requires contact with the certainty that is actually here, which is internal and present-tense.

It also replaces the spiritual-bypass version of "be here now." The principle is not telling the person to ignore the uncertain future. It is telling them that the resource they have been looking for in the future is actually available in the present, and that finding it there allows them to respond to the future more skillfully than they could from inside the false-certainty.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not denial that uncertainty exists. Uncertainty about outcomes is real and acknowledged. The principle is about where the available certainty actually lives.

It is not "positive thinking." Naming what is actually certain (you are here, you are loved, you can choose how to be) is not a reframing of bad facts into good ones. It is the recognition that the certainty being sought is in a different place than the seeker has been looking.

It is not a substitute for grieving what is uncertain or lost. The principle works alongside the grief, not in place of it. See [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty]] — the second move is to feel the avoided emotion. The certainty-noticing is the third move, not a replacement for the second.

## When This Principle Is Most Useful

- When a person is in chronic cortisol from sustained uncertainty
- When the conversation has been about future-prediction and is not producing anything useful
- When the person is asking "what is going to happen?" repeatedly
- When the person is dysregulated and needs a regulation move that is not "just calm down"
- When [[take-the-adventure-stance]] has been offered and the person needs to find the ground from which to take it
- When a leader is being pulled toward false certainty by people around them and needs to stay anchored in what is real

## When Someone Experiences This

The person is in a fear loop. The fear loop is about a future that has not arrived. Someone — partner, coach, friend — asks "what are you certain of right now?" The first answer is "nothing." The second answer is "I don't know." The third answer, with attention, is "I am here. I am safe right now. I love you. I want to be open-hearted through this."

The third answer changes the body. The cortisol drops. The chest opens. The available action becomes visible. Nothing about the external situation has changed. The relationship to the external situation has changed completely.

## Tone

Quiet, almost soft. The principle is delivered as a recognition rather than a teaching. Joe is pointing at something that is already there, not installing something new. The tone is the kind of voice one uses when reminding a panicking friend that they are, in fact, safe in this moment.
