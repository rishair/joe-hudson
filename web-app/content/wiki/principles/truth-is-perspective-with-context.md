# Truth Is Perspective With Context

## The Principle

In a fight with a partner, there is no right and wrong in the way the mind wants there to be. Each partner has a perspective. Each perspective contains a truth. Each perspective is partial. The shape of "truth" inside the psychological/relational domain is perspective-with-context, not a binary verdict.

The principle has two stronger and weaker forms. Joe offers both.

**The weaker form (more immediately graspable):** There is truth to each perspective. The two perspectives are not Truth vs. Lie. They are two partial views, each containing a real piece of what is there.

"You and I both look at the Bay Bridge, and you're in the Salesforce Tower looking down on it and I'm underneath it looking up, and I take a picture and you take a picture. And then we argue over, is this the Bay Bridge or is this the Bay Bridge? No, this is the Bay Bridge. But they're just different perspectives and they're both true." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

**The stronger form:** In the human/psychological domain, there is no right and wrong. Everything that registers as truth is context-dependent.

"Water boils at whatever, 100 degrees Celsius. Yeah, depends on the altitude. So it doesn't actually boil. At sea level, water boils at 100 degrees Celsius. On what planet? At sea level 10,000 years ago? At sea level today? Because there are different sea levels. So there's always context to whatever truth— there's no truth without that context as far as a human's perspective goes." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The weaker form is enough for most coaching applications. The stronger form is the underlying claim that makes the weaker form non-arbitrary.

## What the Principle Does Inside a Fight

The principle dissolves a specific failure mode in fights: the conviction that establishing who is right and who is wrong will resolve the fight. The conviction has the fight running on the wrong logic. The fight is not about which perspective is the true one. The fight is about being seen.

"What we really want is to be seen in a fight. What we're really looking for is to be seen." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

Once the principle lands, the move becomes: see the truth in their perspective without needing to collapse yours into theirs. Honor that they are seeing the Bay Bridge from where they are seeing it. Hold that you are seeing the Bay Bridge from where you are seeing it. The argument about which is THE Bay Bridge dissolves because the question itself was wrongly posed.

This does not require agreeing. It does not require acting as they would have you act. The carve-out is precise:

"It doesn't mean that you have to act a certain way because of their perspective. And that's where I think people get confused. They really need a perspective to be wrong if they think that they have to act a certain way because of it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The need to make a perspective wrong is often a function of believing that the perspective's truth would dictate an action. The principle separates the seeing-the-truth from the dictating-the-action. You can see the truth in their perspective without it forcing your behavior.

## The Most Important Move That Comes From the Principle

Brett offers a follow-up move that Joe identifies as the most important thing about this principle:

"What emotions would I have to feel to consider that perspective? What emotions am I avoiding by collapsing that perspective into right or wrong?" -- Brett Kistler, How To Fight Well

"That's the most important thing. We could argue over the semantics of truth, but what you just said is absolutely the most important thing, which is what do I have to feel if I can't collapse my partner into wrong or right? That's the profound bit." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The need to make the partner wrong is often the avoidance of a feeling. If their perspective is allowed to be partially true, something becomes feelable that has been being held off by the wrongness frame. The question "what would I have to feel if I couldn't collapse them into right or wrong?" surfaces what is being avoided.

This is the question that turns the principle from a philosophical position into a coaching instrument. The principle's purpose is not to settle who is right. The principle's purpose is to surface what the rightness-fight has been protecting against feeling.

## The Companion Principle: Your Perspective Is Also Incomplete

The principle has a second half that is easy to skip. Not only is the partner's perspective true. Your own perspective is incomplete.

"To realize your perspective is absolutely incomplete, or I would say wrong— if you need to put a fine point on it, there's something that I absolutely am not seeing. There's something I am missing. And that's, that's really important to see as well, because that makes a fight really, really simple. Oh yeah, there's something I'm not missing. There's some way I'm wrong." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The "I am missing something" stance is the operational form. It is not "I am wrong about everything." It is "there is at least one angle on this I have not seen, and that angle is what the partner is gesturing at." The stance is structural humility, not collapsed self-judgment.

Joe is careful about the framing:

"It's not wrong like I'm bad wrong. It's just like, yeah, I'm not right." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The shame version of "I'm wrong" is not the move. The clean version is closer to "my view is partial," which is structurally true for any human view of any situation.

## Why the Mind Wants Right and Wrong

The mind's need for right and wrong is a separation-and-judgment mechanism. It produces distance from the other person. It produces ranking. It produces a way to avoid the harder feeling underneath.

"As far as the human condition goes, as far as psychologically, right and wrong is something that we do to create separation and create judgment. And thinking that you're right, thinking that somebody else is wrong, thinking better or worse— all of those things are incredibly painful, not just for you, but for the other person." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The pain of being right is the same family as the pain of being wrong. Both require holding the partner at the distance the ranking creates. Both prevent the kind of contact that fighting well is supposed to produce.

See [[judgment-vs-discernment]] for the distinction between this mind-move and the genuinely useful capacity to see clearly. The principle is not against discernment. It is against the conversion of discernment into the binary rightness/wrongness that the mind likes.

## The Specific Couple Dynamic the Principle Addresses

The principle is particularly active in couples where one partner is the "intellectual" or "logical" one and the other is the "emotional" one. See [[intellectual-emotional-polarization-in-couples]] for the full pattern. The intellectual partner often holds the implicit case that they are more right because they are more rational. The emotional partner often holds the implicit case that they are more right because they are more in touch with the truth of what is happening.

The principle dissolves both cases. The intellectual partner's logic is partial; their context (what they are intellectualizing as a way to avoid feeling) is the missing piece. The emotional partner's empathetic read is partial; their context (what their emotion is empathically detecting) is true and partial too.

"There's usually a better-and-worse feeling, the conviction. Exactly, there's conviction here, we must— this must be the truth. And the person who's logical isn't feeling their emotions, so they're repressing their emotions through judgment of 'you're wrong' or 'I'm better.' So there's all like, it just feeds in perfectly to one another." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The principle, applied, asks each partner to drop the rank-ordering and look at what is true from the other's angle. The act of looking dissolves the felt need to make either one fully right.

## The Boundary: Math Bridges and Boiling Points Have Right Answers

Joe is careful not to over-extend the principle. There are domains where right and wrong work as the mind wants them to.

"In math, there's clearly this is an answer that's gonna build a stable bridge and this is gonna be an answer that— right? But as far as the human condition goes, as far as psychologically, right and wrong is something that we do to create separation and create judgment." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight Well

The principle is about the human/relational/psychological domain. It is not a universal epistemological claim. The bridge engineer needs the right answer about load-bearing capacity. The partner in a fight does not need the right answer about whose interpretation is correct. Different domains, different shapes of truth.

## What This Is Not

This is not "everything is relative" in the sloppy postmodern sense. The principle is precise: in the psychological/relational domain, perspectives are partial and context-dependent. It is not a claim that nothing is true or that all views are equally valid.

This is not a license for the partner to do anything. "Your perspective is also true" does not mean "therefore you get to behave however you want." Action accountability remains. The principle is about how to hold the disagreement about interpretation, not about whether boundaries and consequences apply.

This is not a claim that some things in relationships are not abusive or harmful. See [[verbal-abuse-as-controlling-with-anger]] for the boundary that this principle does not dissolve. Two perspectives being partially true does not mean abusive behavior is equally valid to its objection.

This is not a tool for avoiding hard truths. "There's truth in every perspective" can be deployed as "therefore I never have to engage with the specific way I am being wrong." That is a misuse of the principle. The companion move (my perspective is also incomplete) closes that loop.

## When Someone Experiences This

- You and your partner are in the same fight you have been in for years, each trying to establish who is right
- You feel the urge to look up evidence to prove your interpretation of an event
- You catch yourself rehearsing the case for why you are right and they are wrong
- You hear yourself say "but the truth is..."
- You realize the fight is not about the topic but about who gets to be right
- You ask yourself what you would have to feel if their perspective was also partially true and a feeling arrives

Joe offers the principle with two register shifts. The Bay Bridge example is offered playfully, almost as a joke (the back-and-forth "is this the Bay Bridge / no this is the Bay Bridge"). The water-boils example is offered more carefully, as the harder stronger-form version. When the principle reaches the most-important-question form, Joe's tone shifts to the rare register of "that's the profound bit" -- the principle's surface is the perspective frame, but the live edge is the feeling-avoidance underneath.
