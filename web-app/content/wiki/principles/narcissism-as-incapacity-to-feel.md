# Narcissism Is the Incapacity to Feel Emotions

## The Principle

The clinical category "narcissistic personality disorder" treats narcissism as a discrete condition some people have and others do not. The more useful framing for the work: narcissism is the incapacity to feel emotions, and it exists on a spectrum that includes everyone.

"All of us have some way in which we are narcissistic. The thing about narcissism in general... is that narcissism is an inability to feel emotions. It's the best definition I've ever heard of it. I think it was Lowen who said it." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

This is structurally the same teaching as [[stages-of-emotional-development]] Stage 2 and Stage 4 (repression and management). Narcissism is the locked-in version of those stages: the system has stopped being able to access the emotional movement underneath the defended self-image.

## The Three Components

When Joe describes narcissism in someone who is far out on the spectrum, three things are happening simultaneously:

**1. Incapacity to feel emotions.** The somatic and emotional machinery for feeling has been shut down. This is the root.

**2. The mental better-and-worse-than structure.** The conscious mind operates in comparison: "I am smarter than them" or "I am better than them." Underneath that, often subconsciously, is the opposite: "something is really wrong and broken with me." Both are running at once.

"What you notice is that they very much think they're smarter than everybody or most people. But they also feel like there's something really wrong and broken with them. And so both of those two things are happening simultaneously. One maybe is more subconscious, one is more extroverted." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

This is the same structural insight as [[idea-of-self-vs-actual-self]]: the person is in love with the idea of themselves and at war with the actual self underneath. The two run in parallel.

**3. The felt sense of being uncontained.** The narcissist's behaviors (yelling, manipulation, passive aggression) function in their system to test whether the environment will hold them. If the behavior works (people comply, get scared, give in), they do not feel contained. If the behavior does not work (boundaries hold, people remain calm and love themselves through the storm), they feel contained.

"They don't feel contained. So they often, when I mean containment, I mean that their trip isn't going to work. Like if their thing that they're going to do, I'm going to yell at you and get angry at you. If that works, they don't feel contained." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The behavior is not designed to win in the way it appears. It is designed to test for the boundary that would settle the nervous system. Without that boundary, the system never gets to rest.

## The Spectrum, Not the Category

The cultural use of "narcissist" as a label for other people functions as a way to separate oneself from the trait. "They're a narcissist; I'm not." This separation is itself a narcissistic move.

"The idea that there's somebody who's a narcissist and we're not is ridiculous." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

Everyone has places where they are unable to feel. Everyone has places where the mind runs better-and-worse-than comparisons. Everyone has places where the system seeks containment without being able to name what it is seeking. The label-as-separation is a way to avoid recognizing those places in oneself.

When a triggered person calls another person a narcissist, two things are usually happening at once:

1. The other person genuinely is showing the trait in this moment.
2. The triggered person is showing some version of the same trait by needing to make the other person the holder of it.

See [[every-trigger-with-your-kid-is-projection]] for the structurally identical move with parenting, and [[spiritual-narcissism]] for the version that hides inside spiritual language.

## What This Implies for Coaching

This frame does several things at once for the coach:

**1. It dissolves the "are they really a narcissist?" question.** They have some, you have some. The question is not the diagnosis. The question is the pattern in this relationship.

**2. It points the work toward feeling.** If narcissism is the incapacity to feel, the path out of any version of it (in self or other) is the recovery of the felt experience. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[stages-of-emotional-development]].

**3. It explains why standard relational tools fail with the far-end-of-spectrum case.** [[view]] depends on both parties being available to be moved by the connection. A person whose felt machinery is shut down cannot be reached by vulnerability. The vulnerability gets exploited rather than received. See [[contain-the-narcissist]].

**4. It explains the simultaneous grandiosity and shame.** Coaches who treat the grandiosity as the only feature miss that the shame is running underneath the entire time. Both have to be addressed.

**5. It locates the narcissist's own suffering.** The person on the far end of the spectrum is not having a good time. They are running a system that cannot feel and that does not feel contained. This is not an excuse; it is a recognition that the work, if they were to do it, would be the same work everyone else does — recovering the feeling.

## The Trigger Is a Gift

When a person with strong narcissistic features triggers you, the trigger is information about your own pattern. See [[own-your-narcissism]] and [[every-trigger-with-your-kid-is-projection]].

"If a narcissist is triggering you, like, great, there's all sorts of shit that you get to learn about yourself because you're being triggered, and there's the projections. And that's, that's like, that's great use of a narcissist, is to see all that stuff." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The narcissist in your life is one of the most efficient mirrors available for what you have not yet integrated. Not because they intend to be. Because their incapacity to feel surfaces, in sharp form, the places where you have not yet built capacity to feel through their behavior.

## Why It Is Still Okay to Separate

This framing does not require staying in relationship with someone whose narcissism is causing harm. It does the opposite: it makes separation cleanly available without requiring the additional move of dehumanizing the person.

"It's absolutely 100% totally wonderful and acceptable to just separate yourself from a narcissist, even if it's their parent... there's nothing that says that we have to interact with narcissists to be good people." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

You can recognize the narcissism as a real pattern, hold it as a human pattern present in some form in everyone (including you), set the boundary that protects your nervous system, and not have to make the person into a monster to justify any of those moves. See [[boundaries]].

## Tone

Even, factual. Joe does not perform compassion for narcissists or condemn them. The framing is clinical in the best sense: this is what is happening structurally, here is how to relate to it. The moralizing is absent.
