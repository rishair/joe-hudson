# The Issue Isn't the Issue, It's How You React to the Issue

## The Principle

What most people call the problem is actually the reaction to the problem. The original event, feeling, or condition is rarely the source of the suffering. The suffering is generated by the response to the event — the bracing, the management, the running, the suppression, the trying-to-fix.

This applies recursively. The reaction to the reaction is also part of the suffering. The judgment about the doubt is part of the doubt-suffering. The management of the anxiety is part of the anxiety-suffering. The trying to stop reacting is part of the reactivity-suffering.

"How much of the issue isn't the issue, it's how we react to the issue?" -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

"Or react to the reaction." -- Brett Kistler, completing the recursion

## The Mechanism

A feeling arises. The body produces sensation. The mind labels the sensation as a problem. The mind then produces a second response: management, suppression, justification, distraction. This second response is what is actually felt as "the problem."

The original feeling was a wave the body knows how to move through. The reaction is a clench against the wave. The clench is what stays. The clench is what gets called by the name of the original feeling. "I have anxiety" usually means "I have a chronic clench against the activation my body produces in certain situations." The activation passes; the clench does not.

The recursive version compounds the problem. The clench produces discomfort. The discomfort is judged. The judgment produces more clench. The clench produces more discomfort. The loop is self-feeding. What started as a brief feeling becomes a stable state of suffering, sustained entirely by the reaction-to-the-reaction structure.

## The Doubt Example

The session this principle is drawn from worked with a client who presented with stuckness around confidence. The reframe Joe offered:

"I have to be confident is running from the doubt. We all doubt. We, none of us move with 100% confidence. The question is how much can you welcome the doubt is the difference, not if you have it or not." -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The structure: the doubt is the original event. Everyone has doubt. The body produces it naturally when the stakes are real. The reaction is "I have to be confident" — which is the suppression of the doubt. The suppression is what generates the suffering. The confidence-pursuit is the second arrow.

Working at the level of "I have to be confident" cannot succeed because the confidence-pursuit is what is producing the felt-suffering. Working at the level of the doubt also cannot succeed in the usual sense because the doubt is fine. What works is shifting the relationship: welcoming the doubt instead of fleeing it. The doubt remains. The reaction changes. The suffering ends.

## Why This Matters for Coaching

The principle reorganizes the coaching conversation. The person presents the issue. The coach hears the issue but does not work at the level the issue is presented. The coach works at the level of the reaction. Because the reaction is where the suffering is generated, addressing the reaction produces the change. Addressing the issue does not, because the issue is not what is producing the suffering.

This is why the surface presenting problem is so often not the actual work. The presenting problem is usually a feature of the reaction-loop, named in a way that makes the reaction sound like the natural response to the issue. The coaching reads through this to find what the reaction is doing.

This connects directly to [[address-the-blocker-not-the-symptom]] (the same logic in a different vocabulary) and to [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] (the principle that the reaction-loop creates the very condition it was meant to manage).

## The Universal Application

In the breakdown of the doubt session, Joe extends the principle from doubt specifically to "everything":

"That's everything too, right? How much of the issue isn't the issue, it's how we react to the issue?" -- Joe Hudson, Doubt: The Key To Authentic Confidence

The generalization holds across emotional territory. Sadness becomes depression when the sadness is reacted against. Fear becomes anxiety when the fear is reacted against. Anger becomes resentment when the anger is reacted against. The first emotion is brief; the chronic suffering is the response that holds the emotion in place by trying to push it away.

It also holds across non-emotional territory. The relationship problem is often not the partner's behavior; it is the reaction to the partner's behavior. The money problem is often not the financial situation; it is the reaction to the financial situation. The work problem is often not the work; it is the reaction to the work. In each case, working at the level of the situation does not produce relief, because the relief is gated by the reaction, not by the situation.

## The Operational Test

A useful test for whether a presenting problem is the actual problem or the reaction to it: ask, "if you were not trying to manage this, how much of it would still be a problem?"

If the answer is "still a lot of a problem," the work is somewhere in the underlying material.

If the answer is "not as much," the reaction is the dominant load. The work is in the relationship to the experience, not in the experience itself.

Most of the time, the answer is "not as much." The client is surprised by their own answer. This is the principle making itself visible.

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not the claim that the original issue does not matter. The doubt is real. The fear is real. The activation is real. The principle says the suffering compounds at the level of the reaction, not that the original material is illusory.

It is not the claim that there is nothing to be done about external situations. Some problems require external action. The principle is about the felt-suffering layer, which is often confused with the external situation.

It is not a permission to bypass the original feeling. "It's just my reaction" is itself a reaction — the dismissal-of-the-feeling reaction. The principle requires actually noticing the reaction, not labeling it and moving on.

## The Recursive Trap

The principle's recursive form (reaction to the reaction) is what makes it hard to escape through ordinary effort. The person notices they are reacting badly. They react against the reaction: "I shouldn't be like this." That secondary reaction produces more clench. They notice that reaction and try to manage it: "I need to be less reactive." The tertiary reaction adds more load. Each layer of self-management is another layer of the original mechanism.

The exit is not at any of these layers. The exit is recognizing the structure and stopping the reaction-cascade by welcoming whatever is present, including the reactions themselves. The work moves from "fix the reaction" to "let the reaction be what it is." Once the reaction is no longer being fought, the chain stops generating new links.

See [[when-experiments-become-management]] for the related pattern in which a practice gets co-opted into the same reaction-management structure that the practice was supposed to dissolve.

## When This Principle Is Useful

- When a client presents a problem and the problem-as-described is something many people have without suffering from it (doubt, fear, frustration, conflict in relationships)
- When the client has been trying to fix the problem at the problem's level and reports the fixing has not worked
- When the surface problem feels disproportionate to the actual situation (a small event producing extended suffering)
- When the client describes a loop they cannot exit through normal effort
- When the client's reported experience includes management language ("I have to control this," "I need to stop doing this")
- When the chronic version of a brief feeling is what is being presented

## Tone

Direct and structural. The principle is delivered as an observation about how suffering is generated, not as a critique of reactivity. The recursive extension ("or react to the reaction") often produces a moment of recognition in the listener — the structure was sitting in plain sight but had not been named. The tone matches the relief that recognition produces.
