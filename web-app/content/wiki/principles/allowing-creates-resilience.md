# Allowing Creates Resilience (In Systems and in People)

## The Principle

Systems that allow are resilient. Systems that constrict are fragile. This holds across scales: economies, companies, nervous systems, individuals. The mechanism is the same. A system that has to control every degree of freedom can only survive in the conditions it was tuned for. A system that allows the parts to find their own movement adapts to whatever conditions arise.

The corollary in personal work: allowing emotional experience produces resilience. Constricting it produces fragility. The person who fights every uncomfortable feeling has a smaller operational envelope than the person who lets the feelings move.

## In Systems and Markets

The pattern shows up consistently across organizational forms.

**Economies.** Highly planned economies can grow for a while. They become fragile. Non-planned economies, where people follow their own inspiration, generate the resilience that lets them last for centuries rather than decades.

"If you look at economies that are highly planned, that don't allow, that don't have allowing in them, they are fragile. They might grow for a while, et cetera, but they become fragile, they become weak. Whereas if you have a non-planned economy where people are following their inspiration, you're doing a lot of allowing and that allowing creates a lot of resilience, decades and decades. It's not like 30 years of an economy, it's hundreds upon hundreds of years of an economy." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

**Open source vs. closed source.** Open-source platforms have a structural resilience that closed-source platforms cannot match. The allowing of contribution is what produces the durability.

**Uber vs. taxi services.** "The more they're just like, oh, we're allowing people to drive when they wanna drive, how they wanna drive, it becomes a far more resilient thing than a taxi service, which is far more controlled." The control of the taxi system optimizes for the conditions it was designed for. The allowance of the Uber system adapts to whatever conditions arise.

**Companies.** Decentralized companies are, in general, more resilient than centralized ones and often win competitively. Not in every case. But the pattern is strong enough to be relied on as a design heuristic. See [[valve-handbook-desks-on-wheels]] and [[buurtzorg-nursing]] for specific cases.

## In Personal Work

The same structural principle applies to a single nervous system. A person who constricts on every uncomfortable emotion has built a small operational envelope. The slightest emotional weather throws them out of function. A person who allows the emotions to move adapts to whatever arises and remains functional through it.

"That same resiliency is created by allowing the emotional experiences that we have, by not managing and constricting on those. They help us get through transformation much quicker. They help us heal much quicker. They allow us to make decisions much quicker." -- Joe Hudson, The Opposite of Trying

The allowing of emotion is the [[opposite-of-trying]] applied internally. It produces the same structural resilience that decentralization produces externally. The mechanism: the system that can absorb perturbation without breaking is the system that has not over-specified its operating conditions.

## Why Control Produces Fragility

A controlled system requires the conditions to match its tuning. When conditions match, the controlled system can outperform the allowed system. When conditions change, the controlled system is brittle. Its responses are pre-set. The new conditions are not in the response library.

The allowed system is generally less optimized for any specific condition. But it can find responses to conditions that nobody anticipated. Its components retain enough autonomy to adapt locally without needing the whole system to be redesigned centrally.

In personal terms: a person who has tightly controlled their emotional life can function well as long as the situations they encounter fit the control system. When something unexpected happens — grief, a relationship rupture, a health crisis — the control system has no response. The person collapses. The person who has been allowing emotion to move has resources for the unexpected because the resources are not pre-allocated to specific conditions.

## The Counterintuitive Implication for Capacity

A person who wants to build personal capacity for difficulty often thinks the way is to build more control: more discipline, more management, more self-regulation. This is the trying approach.

The actual move is to build more allowing: more capacity to feel what is happening, more permission for the emotion to move, less effort spent managing the internal state. The capacity that allowing produces is more durable than the capacity that control produces, because it does not require the conditions to stay within a narrow operating range.

This is also why personal-growth practices that focus on adding controls (more meditation, more positive thinking, more discipline) often hit a wall. The control structure is the friction layer. Resilience is on the other side of releasing it, not on the other side of building more of it.

## Pivot Capacity as a Specific Form

Brett's extension: allowing also produces capacity for pivots. Internally — adapting to your own changes in capacity, identity, life situation. Externally — adapting to changes in market, environment, relationship.

The person who has insisted on a fixed identity has to keep maintaining the identity against the changes. The person who has allowed identity to be fluid moves through life-stage transitions without the crisis. The same applies to a company maintaining a fixed business model against market changes versus a company that can read what is happening and reorient. See [[expanding-sense-of-self]] and [[transparent-identity-vs-rigid]] for the personal version.

## The Boundary Case

Allowing is not the absence of structure. The Uber system has rules. Open-source projects have governance. Decentralized economies have law. The allowing is at the level of how the components operate within the structure, not at the level of having no structure.

The same internally. A person who allows emotion to move is not a person without values or commitments. The allowing is at the level of internal experience. The external structure of life remains. See [[boundaries]] for how allowed-feeling expresses as clean action when action is called for, and [[structure-and-empowerment]] for the organizational version.

## A Reading on a Person, Family, or Team

When you encounter a system that feels brittle — one where small perturbations produce large dysfunction — the diagnostic question is: where is allowing missing? Where is the system trying to control something it could be allowing? The places where it is over-managing are the places where the brittleness lives.

For a person: what emotional experience am I not allowing? For a family: what truth is not being permitted to surface? For a team: what autonomy is being withheld from the parts? Restoring allowing in the right place often restores the resilience that was missing.

## When This Principle Is Useful

- When a person reports that they need to "be more disciplined" and is asking for more control as the solution.
- When a leader is trying to fix a fragile organization by adding more centralized management.
- When someone is asking how to handle the next crisis better and is reaching for more pre-planning.
- When emotional constriction has produced flatness and the person is asking how to feel things again.
- When a family or team has become brittle around the leader's anxiety and the leader is adding more rules.
- When a company is trying to control its way to durability and producing exactly the fragility it fears.
