# Welcoming Is Somatic, Not Verbal

## The Principle

Welcoming yourself is not something you say or think. It is something you feel — specifically, a direction of feeling. The feeling you have toward something you love (a pet, a child, a person you have a crush on) is what welcoming feels like. The work of self-welcoming is to direct that same feeling at yourself.

This is the operational answer to "how do I love myself?" The mind hears the question as a request for an action or a set of words. The body answers it as a request for a felt direction. The mind cannot solve the question because it is using the wrong instrument.

"How do you love yourself? Is paramount to the question of like, how do you feel? It is a sense. It's you're sensing yourself. And in this case, he's accessing it through welcoming. Oh, I'm — I am going to say, this part of me, you're welcome here. And that's, again, a visceral, somatic, sensual experience." — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

The reversal-of-the-flow framing captures it. Most of the time, the feeling-of-love flows outward (toward a pet, a child, a crush). Self-welcoming is the same feeling flowing inward — directed back at the self. The instruction is not to generate a new feeling. The instruction is to redirect an already-known feeling.

## Why This Matters Operationally

The most common mistake people make when they hear "love yourself" is to look for the right words or thoughts. They try affirmations ("I love myself"), self-talk scripts, positive self-imagery. These can support the welcoming but they are not the welcoming. A person can repeat "I love myself" a thousand times with no change in the somatic field; the body knows the difference between the verbal claim and the felt direction.

When the body actually does it, the change is visible. The person settles. The face relaxes. The eyes can meet others without bracing. In the source session, the person responds to the prompt "show me what it would be like to welcome yourself" with an immediate body shift — not a statement, a shift. The shift is the welcoming. The shift then stops (which is the work), but the shift was real, and it was somatic.

"There's this idea that it's some action or some words you say. It is a somatic feeling. The way that you feel when you're with a pet that you adore, or a child, or somebody you have a crush on is a little bit different, but it's very similar. There's a feeling that you have towards them. It's like, what is it like to direct that feeling towards yourself?" — Joe Hudson, *How Can I Feel Self-Love? Breakdown*

## The Pet/Child/Crush Reference

Joe uses the pet, the child, and the crush as access points because almost everyone has a direct felt-experience reference for at least one of them. The feeling toward a pet is widely available and clean (relatively few people have complicated feelings about their dog). The feeling toward a child carries the most pure form of welcoming in many people. The feeling toward a crush has the visceral, somatic quality that makes the felt direction unmistakable.

The instruction is: locate that feeling in your body. It is somewhere specific — a warmth, an opening, a softening, a direction. Now do the same thing toward yourself.

This is not a metaphor. The body actually does the same thing internally. The chest opens or warms or softens in the direction of the pet, and the same opening or warming or softening can be aimed at the body's own experience.

The variations exist because not all three references work for all people. Some people don't have pets. Some don't have children. Some don't currently have a crush. The coach uses whichever reference the person has access to.

## What This Rules Out

This principle rules out several common substitutes that look like welcoming but are not:

- **Verbal affirmation without the felt direction.** "I love myself" said with a tight chest and a held face is not welcoming. It is a verbal claim about welcoming.
- **Thinking welcoming thoughts.** Generating mental content about how welcome you are is not welcoming. The thoughts can support the welcoming or distract from it depending on whether they connect to the body.
- **Positive self-evaluation.** Deciding that you are good enough to deserve welcoming is not welcoming. It is permission-granting, which can precede welcoming but is not welcoming itself.
- **Tolerating yourself.** Accepting your existence without the warm direction of feeling is not welcoming. See [[acceptance-without-love]] for the related read.

The discriminator in every case: is the body doing something different, or are you just thinking different thoughts? Welcoming has a somatic signature. If the body is unchanged, the welcoming is not happening yet.

## The Coaching Move That Follows

Because welcoming is somatic, the coaching cannot deliver it through explanation. The coach has to occasion the body shift. The primary move is to ask the person to do it and watch what happens in the body.

"Show me what it would be like to welcome yourself right now." See [[welcome-yourself-in-the-conversation]].

The "show me" is exact. Not "tell me how you would welcome yourself." Not "imagine welcoming yourself." Show me — meaning, do it now, with your body, and let me see whether the somatic shift happens.

If the shift happens, the coach has the felt-experience to work with. If the shift doesn't happen, the coach can investigate what's blocking it without needing to teach the move first. Most people, asked to do this, can produce a brief shift. The work is not installing the capacity. It is meeting whatever interrupts the capacity.

## The Personal-Practice Implication

For someone practicing self-welcoming on their own, the principle says: don't reach for the words. Reach for the felt direction. Find the feeling you have toward something you love. Then direct that feeling at yourself.

If the felt direction is hard to find, find it toward an external object first (the pet, the child, the crush, a place you love, a piece of music you love, a memory of being loved). Once it is present in the body, slowly redirect it toward yourself. The redirection is the practice.

If even the external direction is hard to find, the work is at a different level — find what does open the body (a song, a sunset, the breath at the end of an exhale) and start from there. The principle holds either way: the felt direction is the substrate, the words are not.

## Why People Get This Wrong

Two reasons people default to the verbal version:

1. **Self-help language teaches it that way.** "Tell yourself you love yourself." "Repeat the affirmation." The verbal frame is what gets transmitted in books, classes, and apps. The somatic frame requires the body to be involved and is harder to put on a worksheet.

2. **The body is the territory that was shamed.** For most people who struggle with self-welcoming, the original shaming happened around their felt experience — they were too much, too needy, too emotional, too embodied. The body is where the wound lives. Retreating to the verbal level is a way to do the work while avoiding the territory where the work has to happen.

The principle pushes against both defaults. The work is in the body. The words can be helpful but are never sufficient.

## When Someone Experiences This

- "I keep saying I love myself but it doesn't feel like anything."
- "I do the affirmations but nothing changes."
- "I know intellectually I'm supposed to love myself but I can't feel it."
- "I love my dog more than I love myself."
- The person who can describe their self-love practice in detail but is visibly contracted in their body
- The person whose self-love work has gone abstract and stopped producing change

In every case, the principle says: drop the verbal layer and return to the somatic. Find the felt direction toward something you already love, and redirect.
