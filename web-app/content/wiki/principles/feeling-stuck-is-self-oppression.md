# Feeling Stuck Is Self-Oppression

## The Principle

The intellectual recognition that completes the work of unsticking is this: the trap you experience is being constructed by you. You are removing love from yourself, you are attacking yourself, you are silencing yourself -- and you are doing this in order to avoid having someone else do it to you. The avoidance has become the doing. You are not avoiding the catastrophe; you are running the catastrophe on yourself, every day.

"Your stuckness is basically trying to avoid an emotional state. And most likely that emotional state is the removal of love. Or we're going to get in trouble. So we're generally, in broad strokes, trying to avoid the removal of love or getting in trouble, getting attacked. That's what is actually happening. And the weird part is, if you intellectually see this, is that what's actually happening is you are removing love from yourself and you are attacking yourself. So you're actually not avoiding it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

This is the intellectual companion to the emotional and nervous-system work in unsticking. The principle isn't a substitute for the felt work. It is the cognitive frame that makes the felt work coherent.

## The Two Things Being Avoided

Joe names the two emotional states that almost all chronic stuckness is organized to avoid:

1. **The removal of love.** The fear that being-yourself, speaking your truth, expressing what's true will cause the people you love (and need) to withdraw their love.

2. **Getting in trouble.** The fear that being-yourself will cause attack, punishment, conflict, consequence.

Both fears were installed in childhood when they were sometimes literally true. The parent might have withdrawn love when the child was being themselves. The parent might have attacked when the child took up space. The fears were calibrated to a real environment.

In the adult, the original environment is gone but the fears keep operating. The fears organize the person's life around not-being-themselves, in order to avoid the removal of love and the punishment that were forecast.

## The Avoidance Is the Doing

Here is the structural inversion that makes the principle load-bearing:

"You are removing love from yourself and you are attacking yourself."

The avoidance of having love removed has become the active removal of love from the self. The person who will not speak their truth in their marriage to avoid losing their partner's love is, every day, withholding their own love from themselves -- denying themselves the expression that would let them feel connected to themselves. The partner has not done it yet. The self has been doing it daily for years.

The avoidance of being attacked has become active self-attack. The internal critic who polices everything the person says, who pre-emptively names what is unacceptable, who beats up the person for being-stuck-again -- this internal voice is doing to the person, every day, what the original attacker is feared to do. The attacker hasn't landed in years. The self-attacker has been landing every minute.

The person who is so afraid of being trapped by their partner is, by their own thinking, trapping themselves with their mind every day.

"Every day I am trapping myself with my mind every day. And what I see when people really solve this puzzle about feeling stuck all the time in their life, and one of the things that I notice that is really clear in them is that the only thing they were ever stuck with was the thoughts. It was a set of thoughts." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The trap is internal. The bars are made of thoughts. The thought-bars have been doing the work the feared external bars never did.

## The Specific Mechanics

Brett surfaces the operational shape:

"You are doing it first in the sense like you are choosing to remove the love from yourself by deciding that your needs don't matter enough to express. That your feelings aren't worth bringing to the relationship, that the relationship isn't going to be improved and the other person's life isn't going to be improved by you bringing up the scary wants, the scary truth for you. And you're also attacking yourself. You have to physically shut that down. You have to physically constrict and close down that impulse. And create stories about blame for yourself to keep it all locked in place in a little cage." -- Brett Kistler, How To Get Unstuck

The mechanics in sequence:

1. **Decide your needs don't matter enough.** This is the active denial of love-of-self. The choice not to bring your truth into the relationship is also a choice not to give yourself the experience of being met for your truth.

2. **Decide your feelings aren't worth sharing.** This is the active disqualification of your inner experience. The choice not to share your feelings is also a choice to treat your inner life as not-valuable.

3. **Decide the other person wouldn't benefit from your truth.** This is a story about the other that protects the self-suppression. The other has been recast as fragile or rejecting so that the self-suppression can be framed as care.

4. **Physically shut down the impulse.** The body has to do work to silence what wants to be said. Constriction. Tightness. Held breath. This is real physical self-aggression -- using your body against your own impulse.

5. **Create blame-stories.** The mind constructs narratives that lock the cage in place. "I shouldn't feel this way." "This is just how I am." "It's too late to change." Each story reinforces the bars.

"And that is self-aggression." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

## The Childhood Origin of the Move

The self-oppression started as a survival strategy. The child preferred to be the one doing the suppression because being-the-one-doing-it was the only available form of agency.

"That's typically what we do when we were kids, is what we did was we convinced ourselves, basically, it's easier, feels better to attack myself before I get attacked by somebody else." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The logic, as a kid: if I'm the one attacking me, at least I'm not surprised. If I'm the one withholding love, at least I have some control. If I keep the bad-news on the inside, at least my parents aren't completely incompetent -- because if they were the ones doing this without cause, the world would be unsurvivable.

"Because if it's not my fault, then my parents are fucking completely incompetent and holy shit, I'm only 3 years old. What the fuck?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The self-oppression preserved the parents as competent and the world as legible. The cost was that the child became the agent of their own oppression. The benefit was that the world remained operable.

The strategy was a real strategy. It worked. It allowed the child to survive. The principle being named here is the recognition that the strategy that worked then is now the thing doing the harm. The adult is not a kid anymore. The strategy can be updated.

## The Liberating Implication

The intellectual recognition does specific work in the unsticking. When the person sees that they are already doing what they're afraid of, the avoidance loses its function.

If the catastrophe being avoided is "they will withhold love from me" and the person sees clearly that they are withholding love from themselves every day, then continuing to avoid the catastrophe by withholding self-expression no longer makes sense. The catastrophe is happening. The avoidance is the catastrophe. Doing the avoided thing -- being themselves, expressing the truth -- could not produce a worse outcome than the current configuration. It could only produce a different one.

The recognition does not by itself dissolve the freeze. The emotional and nervous-system work still has to happen. But the recognition removes the rational basis for the avoidance, which is part of what allows the felt work to proceed.

"So when you can really see that click, like, oh, I'm feeling stuck because let's say that wife example or husband example, I can't say my piece because they'll attack me. And if I can't say my piece and I can't be happy in this marriage and I can't leave the marriage because then I won't have any love, that's basically that feeling of trapped. You're not giving yourself love by not speaking who you are, speaking your truth. You are attacking yourself by constantly beating yourself up for being stuck, right? Or for not saying your piece in the marriage or whatever the thing is. And so you're basically doing it all to yourself already." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The phrase "doing it all to yourself already" is the click. Once seen, the configuration is workable. Before being seen, the configuration is invisible.

## Depression as Self-Oppression

Brett extends the principle to depression:

"Maybe depression is some form of like self-oppression, like you are shutting down your own natural impulses, right? Which I don't even want to say you are, because that implies that it's a personal choice that you're making, right?" -- Brett Kistler, How To Get Unstuck

Joe's response surfaces a critical nuance. The framing as "you are" can convert the principle into more self-attack: "I'm depressed because I'm oppressing myself, so I'm bad for that too." This is the principle weaponized against the person it's supposed to free.

"If you say you are, that's great. You say you are, the person who is feeling that way is -- feels like -- we'll take that as more shame. Yeah, more I'm bad, and then they're more self-oppression. So it's usually like a horrible way to think about it." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The careful framing: the self-oppression is happening, and it is not a moral failing. The person is not bad for it. The pattern was installed; it is now running; recognizing it is part of changing it; weaponizing the recognition into more self-attack just adds another layer of the same pattern.

The corrective framing: this is something being done by a part of you that was trained to do it. The recognition is the beginning of the choice not to. The not-doing is the work. None of it is moral judgment of the person who has been running the pattern.

## The Cycle: Self-Oppression Produces More Depression

The pattern compounds. The more a person operates self-oppression, the more depressed they feel. The more depressed they feel, the more they retreat from being themselves, which produces more self-oppression. The cycle deepens.

"One of the things I notice is someone who's constantly feeling trapped, they're usually getting closer and closer to deeper and deeper depression." -- Joe Hudson, How To Get Unstuck

The cycle's exit is the same: drop the self-oppression by doing the thing it was protecting against. Be yourself despite the consequences. The doing is what breaks the cycle, not the thinking. The thinking can prepare the doing. The doing is what changes the state.

## When This Principle Operates

This principle is the right intervention when:

- The person has been doing emotional work and intellectual recognition is the missing layer
- The person can hold the principle without weaponizing it into self-blame
- The person is at a moment where they can see clearly what they have been doing to themselves
- The person is presenting trapped-stuck where the trap is interpersonal (marriage, family, work relationships)
- The person needs the cognitive frame that makes the next behavioral move (be-yourself-despite-consequences) coherent

It is not the right intervention when:

- The person is fragile and would convert it into more self-attack
- The person is in acute dysregulation and needs regulation first
- The work has not yet done the emotional movement that lets the intellectual frame land
- The stuckness is the 404 form (problem-stuck), not the trapped form

## What This Principle Is Not

It is not blame. The person is not at fault for the self-oppression. The pattern was installed by forces outside their control. Recognition is not judgment.

It is not the claim that all depression is self-oppression. Some depression has biochemical components, some has environmental causes that the person genuinely cannot change in the short term. The principle applies specifically to the kind of depression that runs on chronic suppression of self-expression, which is one type among others.

It is not "just change your thinking." The intellectual recognition is one of three layers (nervous system, emotional, intellectual). Recognition alone does not produce change. The emotional movement has to happen too.

It is not a license to dump on others. Being-yourself-despite-consequences is not the same as discharging at people. The expression can be clean -- "I have feelings I haven't been bringing into this marriage and I want to start bringing them" -- without becoming attack. See [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]].

## Tone

Direct but careful. The principle has weight and needs to be delivered without becoming weaponized. Joe holds the seeing as a kindness -- "if you can really see that click, oh, I'm doing it all to myself already" -- rather than as an accusation.

When Brett frames depression as self-oppression, Joe immediately steps in to nuance the framing. The careful nuancing is part of the teaching. The principle is precise: the self-oppression is happening, and the person is not bad for it being so. Both halves are required.
