# Iterate Your AI Use Toward Feeling Better

## The Principle

The way to use AI well is to keep iterating how you use it until you start feeling better after each interaction, not worse. The metric is not what the AI says it is doing for you. The metric is how you feel in your body when the conversation is over and then again hours later. Trend in the right direction is the only signal that matters.

"Keep on iterating with the way that you're interacting with AI until you start finding ways that make you feel better and better and better." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The principle is operational: it does not require any theory of AI ethics. It requires only the willingness to notice how you feel after a session and to change what you do based on what you noticed.

## The Two Reference Points

The principle has two anchor experiences that tell you what you are aiming for and what you are aiming away from.

**The aim-away-from experience is social media.** You scroll for an hour. The hour is full of low-grade engagement. When you put the phone down, you feel a pit in your stomach. You feel slightly ashamed. You think "I shouldn't have done that for so long." The body knows something was wrong even if the mind was occupied throughout.

"We all know you get off of social media and you feel a pit in your stomach and you kind of feel shitty and you feel ashamed and you think, oh, I shouldn't have scrolled for so long. That is how you feel when you feel disconnected from yourself." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

If your AI interactions leave you with that same feeling -- the pit, the slight shame, the "I shouldn't have done that for so long" -- the interaction was social-media-shaped. You are using AI to disconnect from yourself even if it felt productive at the time. Whatever you were doing is the thing to change.

**The aim-toward experience is the meditation retreat or the hiking trip with three or four close friends.** During the retreat itself, much of it sucks. The body is uncomfortable. The mind is restless. The hours drag. And then the retreat ends and you feel more connected with yourself than when you started. The hike with friends has the same shape: tiring during, integrative after, you feel more whole the next morning.

"Have a meditation retreat, might suck the whole entire time, but at the end of it, you're like, I feel more connected with myself. And so if you're interacting with AI in a way that leaves you feeling like social media, no bueno. And if you feel like you're interacting with OpenAI in a way that you feel like you just had a great hiking trip with like 3 or 4 friends, that's a pretty good sign that that's a way that you know." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The during-experience is not the metric. The after-experience is the metric. AI interactions that feel slightly difficult during but leave you more connected after are in the right shape. AI interactions that feel easy and engaging during but leave you depleted after are in the social-media shape.

## What "Feel Better" Does Not Mean

The principle is easily confused with a hedonic test: does this AI make me feel good in the moment? That confusion produces the worst use of the principle.

"I'd want to distinguish that from, am I talking to the AI in a way where it's trying to make me feel better? ... Eventually that won't feel good." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

The AI that is trying to make you feel better is the sycophantic AI -- the one that praises you, agrees with you, tells you you're great. In the moment, this feels good. Over time, it does not. The pit in the stomach arrives anyway. The shame arrives anyway. The depletion arrives anyway. The hedonic version of the metric collapses into the social-media failure mode.

The actual metric is longer-arc:

"Long-term trending, feeling more expansive, more authentic, more whole." -- Joe Hudson, How to Succeed in the Age of AI

Three checks on the actual metric:

- **Expansion.** Does the world feel bigger or smaller after the conversation? Bigger is the right direction.
- **Authenticity.** Do you feel more yourself or less yourself? More is the right direction.
- **Wholeness.** Do you feel more integrated or more fragmented? More integrated is the right direction.

If the AI is making you feel small, performative, or fragmented, the metric is failing even if the moment felt pleasant.

## What the Iteration Looks Like

The principle calls for active experimentation, not passive use. The default settings on most AI systems are not optimized for any particular user's flourishing. They are optimized for engagement, which is the social-media shape. The user has to do work to bend the system toward something better.

Practical moves the iteration can include:

- **Change the prompt.** Ask the AI to challenge you, not agree with you. Ask it to push back. Ask it to ask you the question rather than answer yours.
- **Change the use case.** Move from "give me the answer" to "help me think this through." Move from "validate this" to "what am I missing."
- **Change the session length.** Notice when sessions are dragging into social-media territory and stop earlier.
- **Change what you bring.** Notice when you are bringing approval-seeking, neediness, or avoidance to the AI. Bring something cleaner.
- **Change the AI.** Different systems have different defaults. Some will support the work you are trying to do; others will not. The metric tells you which.
- **Change the surrounding context.** Talk to a human about the things you talk to AI about. See [[dont-use-ai-self-development-in-isolation]]. The iteration includes what you do outside the AI session, not just inside it.

The iteration is ongoing. The principle is not "find the right setup and stay there." The principle is "keep checking and keep adjusting."

## Why the Principle Works

The body knows. The mind can be fooled by what an AI says it is doing for you, by what the experience feels like in the moment, by the relief of feeling agreed with. The body holds the actual cumulative signal of whether the interaction is integrating or fragmenting you.

The principle is just a way of asking the body what it is reporting and adjusting the inputs based on the report. The body's read is not infallible (see [[body-lies-as-much-as-mind]]) but it is usually more accurate than the mind's read of the same experience, and the trend over many interactions is reliable.

The same principle applies to many other domains. Diet, exercise, social activities, relationships -- in all of them, the iteration toward "I feel better over time" is the correct optimization target. AI is just the newest place to apply it.

## The Connection to the Cult Test

The principle is closely connected to the cult-detection test (see [[am-i-more-connected-or-more-isolated-test]]). Both ask the same underlying question: is this thing increasing my connection with self, others, and world, or decreasing it? Cults decrease it. Bad AI use decreases it. Good AI use, like good self-development work and good relationships, increases it.

The reason to have both as separate principles: the cult test is mostly about isolation patterns over months. The feel-better iteration is about the after-experience of individual interactions over weeks. They are different time-scales of the same fundamental check.

## Tone

Practical and matter-of-fact. The principle does not require any sophisticated theory. It requires only honesty about how you feel after the interaction and willingness to adjust. The tone is the tone of giving someone a useful tool they can use immediately.
