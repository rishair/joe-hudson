# Excuses Lower Confidence; "I Fell Down and I'm Getting Back Up" Raises It

## The Principle

When a team member has missed a commitment, the way they frame the failure tells the leader more than the failure itself. Two framings to watch for:

**Excuses lower the leader's confidence.** Twenty reasons why it didn't happen, all pointing outward, all positioning the team member as not the source of the response, tell the leader that the team member experiences the problem as interesting but the solving of it as not their responsibility. The signal is structural, not emotional.

**Falling down and getting back up raises the leader's confidence.** "I fell down. I'm getting back up. I'm going to make this happen." The framing positions the team member as the source of the recovery. The signal tells the leader that the team member owns both the failure and the next move.

"You give me 20 excuses tells me that you actually think that the problem is really sexy, but solving it is somehow not for you. ... It would, it would improve my confidence if you said, I fell down and I'm gonna get back up and I'm gonna make this happen. That would actually improve my confidence." -- Joe Hudson

The principle gives the leader a precise diagnostic. The same missed commitment can come with either framing. The leader's response is determined more by which framing the team member chooses than by the failure itself.

## Why This Works

The frame the team member chooses reveals where they place themselves relative to the work. Excuses place the team member outside the work — the problem is the work's, the obstacles are external, the team member is reporting from the sideline. Recovery talk places the team member inside the work — the problem belongs to them, the next move is theirs, the team member is on the field.

The leader bets on people, not on tasks. The leader's confidence is the bet they are making. Excuses tell the leader the bet was wrong; the person being delegated to is not actually the executor. Recovery talk tells the leader the bet is sound; the person is going to figure it out.

## The Mechanism on the Listener's Side

A second mechanism operates: excuses communicate to the leader that solving the problem is not the team member's job, that the team member is processing the failure as something that happened to them. Even when the excuses are factually accurate, they signal disengagement from the solving role.

"You give me 20 excuses tells me that you actually think that the problem is really sexy, but solving it is somehow not for you." -- Joe Hudson

The phrase "the problem is really sexy" carries the diagnostic. The team member finds the problem interesting enough to enumerate its features but does not find themselves to be the one whose job it is to address them. The mismatch is the diagnostic.

## How This Pairs With the Accountability Questions

The principle is the back-end of [[ask-the-three-accountability-questions]]. The leader asks "what made you not get that done?" with genuine curiosity. The answer comes. The leader then notices which form the answer is in.

If the answer is twenty excuses: the leader can stay curious about the answer and still name what it communicates. The leader has the right to say "this is lowering my confidence."

If the answer is "I fell down and I'm getting back up": the leader registers the framing as a credit deposit. The team member is operating from ownership. The leader's confidence rises, and the leader can move to the next two questions (what are you going to do, what support do you need) with more ground to stand on.

## What the Leader Can Say

The principle gives the leader a specific thing to say when excuses appear:

"It doesn't mean you're not curious. It doesn't mean you're not full of wonder. It doesn't mean, oh, what happened? Oh wait, those are excuses. I see that you're giving me excuses, and I can only tell you that that lowers my confidence." -- Joe Hudson

The move is to name what the excuses are communicating. The leader does not have to argue with each excuse. The leader names the signal the excuses are sending. "I notice this is a list of reasons. That's lowering my confidence."

The naming gives the team member real information. They might not have realized how the framing was landing. With the information, they can choose to reframe — or they can confirm that they really do experience this as not-their-problem, which is also useful information.

## What the Leader Can Hear Instead

If the team member, prompted, shifts to recovery talk: "Okay, I see. I dropped this. Here's what I'm going to do." The leader's confidence rises in real time. The conversation has demonstrated that the team member can move from the excuses frame to the recovery frame. The leader registers this capacity. The next time work is delegated, the leader has more reason to trust the delegation will hold.

If the team member cannot make the shift even when prompted: the leader has surfaced something important. The team member is genuinely not operating from the position of taking responsibility for the work. The accountability conversation is now in different territory. The work is not "how do we get this task done" but "is this person actually in the role I think they are."

## The Distinction Is Not Severity

The principle is not about how bad the excuses are. Some excuses are true. Some failures had real external causes. The principle is not asking the team member to accept blame for circumstances they didn't control. The principle is about the position the team member is operating from in describing the failure.

A team member can have entirely legitimate reasons for the missed commitment and still operate from the recovery frame: "Yes, the supplier missed their date and the design changed and the team got sick. I dropped this. I'm getting back up. Here's how I'm going to make it happen even with all of that going against me."

The recovery frame is not a denial of obstacles. It is a positioning of the speaker as the source of the response to the obstacles. The excuses frame is the same set of facts positioned as the cause of the failure rather than as the field the team member is operating in.

## What This Is Not

**Not a license to dismiss reasons.** Some reasons are real and need to be addressed structurally. The leader who hears recovery talk should still address the actual blockers. The principle is about reading the position the team member is in, not about ignoring the actual obstacles.

**Not a rule about how to talk.** The principle is descriptive of how the leader's confidence shifts based on framing. It is not a coaching script that says "people should always frame failures as recovery." Sometimes the team member does not yet know how to do the work, and the honest framing of that is more useful than performative recovery talk.

**Not a way to test for character.** The principle is about the moment, not about the person. A team member who defaults to excuses in one conversation can show up with recovery talk in another. The leader is reading the conversation, not the person.

## When the Excuses Frame Is Information

The excuses frame, when persistent, tells the leader something specific. It tells the leader that the team member has not yet taken on the role as the source of the response. The next moves are:

- Surface the pattern: "I notice we are talking about this in terms of what happened to it rather than what you are doing about it. Is that accurate?"
- Test for the role fit: "Is this work yours?" Sometimes the answer is no, and the work needs to be re-delegated.
- Address the underlying belief: the team member who chronically frames failures as external is often operating from a belief that they cannot affect outcomes. The work moves to the belief.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact and informative. Joe is not condemning excuses. He is naming what they communicate to him. The naming is information for the team member, not an attack. The leader who delivers this principle with disgust or contempt has missed the principle. The leader who delivers it with the same neutrality as "the room is cold" has gotten it right.
