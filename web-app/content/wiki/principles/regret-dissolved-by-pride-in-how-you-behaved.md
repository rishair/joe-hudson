# Regret Is Dissolved by Pride in How You Behaved

## The Principle

Regret has a specific structure: you wanted an outcome from a situation, you did not get it (or are unsure whether you got it), and now you walk away wishing you had done something different. The standard reading of this is that the difference would have produced the outcome. The structural reading is that no difference would have reliably produced the outcome -- but a different relationship to the outcome would have dissolved the regret.

The alternative: act in a way that you are proud of regardless of what the outcome turns out to be. If you can feel proud of how you behaved before you find out the result, the regret has no foothold. You still might want the outcome. You will not regret your behavior.

"Think about a moment where you regretted what you did. And then you almost always, there will be an outcome there that you didn't want. So now I want you to think, what is it that you could have done that would have made you have no regret in that interaction and still gotten the same results? You still didn't get the people to like you, you still didn't get the job, you still didn't get the money. But you think to yourself, I have no regrets." -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

## How Regret Actually Works

The mechanism that produces regret:

1. You walk into a situation wanting a specific outcome.
2. You contort yourself to do or say what you think will get the outcome.
3. You do not get the outcome (you can never always get the outcome you want).
4. You walk away regretting what you did.

The regret is not about the outcome per se; it is about having contorted for an outcome you did not get. The contortion is the cost. The unrealized outcome is what makes the cost visible.

"Typically what we're doing in those situations is we're thinking, this is the result I want, how do I get it? And we contort ourselves into doing or saying the thing that we think will get us what we want, and then we don't get it, and then we feel regret." -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

The standard solution -- "next time, contort better to actually get the outcome" -- does not work. Even when the contortion succeeds, the cost has been paid; the success has been achieved by the contorted self, not by the actual self. The actual self is still left out, just less visibly. And the next outcome will require its own contortion, and the next, and the next.

The structural solution: stop contorting. Be yourself. Walk into the situation able to be proud of how you behaved before you know the result.

## The Counter-Intuitive Test

The teaching's first move is a thought experiment that is supposed to feel impossible at first.

"Imagine that situation and you were 100% true to yourself. You were 100% upright in yourself. You were self-possessed so that before you found out what the result was, you felt proud of the way that you behaved. So if you really allow yourself to feel that, like, oh, I just walked in that situation, I am proud of the way that I behaved. How much does it matter that you got the result that you wanted? As far as regret goes, you still might want the result, but how much regret do you feel if you are proud of how you behaved?" -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

The thought experiment usually boggles the mind. The mind protests: "Wait, but the result -- I can't, huh?" The protest is the data. The mind is so organized around outcome-as-the-measure that it cannot initially process the question "would you regret it if you were proud of how you behaved?" The question makes the regret-machinery visible.

Once the experiment lands, the recognition is structural: the regret evaporates. The wanting may remain. The wanting and the regret are not the same thing.

## What This Is Not

It is not "lower your standards for outcomes." The principle does not ask the person to stop wanting outcomes. They can want what they want. The teaching is about where regret comes from, not about whether to want.

It is not "don't try to influence outcomes." Being yourself in a conversation can be very effective at producing good outcomes. The teaching is not against effectiveness. It is against the mechanism where wanting-the-outcome distorts the behavior into the contorted form.

It is not "perform pride after the fact." The pride has to be available before the outcome is known. Performing pride after losing is just rationalization. The principle requires that the behavior be of a kind you would have been proud of regardless of result -- which means the behavior has to be aligned with the actual self in the moment, not engineered for the outcome.

It is not the same as [[good-decision-is-how-you-make-it-not-the-outcome]] -- though they are closely related. The decisions principle is about how to evaluate decisions across a portfolio. This principle is about the felt experience of walking away from a single interaction. They share the same structural insight (outcomes do not measure deciding) and they reinforce each other, but they address different questions.

## The Behavior-Side Requirement

The principle requires that the behavior be such that you can be proud of it. This is not automatic. Most behaviors that the contorted person executes are not behaviors they would be proud of in retrospect. The performing, the people-pleasing, the strategic withholding, the manipulative wording -- none of these produce pride. The principle's downstream is therefore that the behavior has to change.

What behavior is proud-of-able?

- Saying what is true for you
- Wanting what you want without performing not-wanting
- Listening to the other person actually
- Making the request you want to make
- Naming the disagreement you have
- Being kind without sacrificing your truth
- Speaking from your actual stance instead of a calculated stance

See [[be-yourself-despite-consequences-undoes-stuckness]] for the operational specifics of what being-yourself looks like. The principle here is the regret-side framing of the same operation. The behavior change that being-yourself names is the same behavior change that pride-in-behavior makes available.

## The Outcome-Detachment Side

The principle also requires that the pride be available before the outcome is known. This is the harder half for most people. The contorted operator has the pride attached to the result: "I will feel proud if it works." The principle requires the pride to be detached from the result: "I am proud of how I am being, full stop."

This is structurally identical to [[no-attachment-to-the-outcome]] but reframed as the regret-side operation. The non-attachment makes the regret-dissolution possible. Without it, the pride remains contingent and the regret remains operative.

The detachment is not indifference. The person can still want the outcome very much. The detachment is specifically about the relationship between the behavior and the outcome: the behavior was the behavior I would be proud of, and the outcome will be the outcome. The outcome does not retroactively change the value of the behavior.

## Why This Designs a Life

The downstream implication: a life of proud behaviors accumulates as a life. The people who say yes to the proud behavior are the people who are saying yes to the actual self. The opportunities that respond to the proud behavior are the opportunities that fit the actual self.

"If we don't contort ourselves, if we say, no, this is how I'm proud of being, and I'm going to be this way, we don't feel that regret. And, and there's a great reason for that. And the great reason is that if we are ourselves, then the things that say yes to us are the things that are saying yes to us, not the contorted version of ourselves. The people who give us money for our job, the people who like us, or the people who like us for us, who we're proud of, not like us for the contorted sense of self that we did to try to please them. And so we actually design a life by being ourselves." -- Joe Hudson, Be yourself and watch your life rearrange

This is the connection to [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]]. The proud-behavior principle is the moment-by-moment operation. The world-reorganizing is the aggregate consequence.

## The Connection to the Regret-Fractal

The principle is the moment-by-moment exit from [[regret-as-fractal-conversation-day-year-life]]. If you can be proud of how you behaved in this conversation, you do not regret the conversation. A day of proud-conversations is a day you do not regret. A year of proud-days is a year you do not regret. A life of proud-years is a life you do not regret.

The fractal logic makes the per-interaction stakes higher than they look. The conversation you are about to have is not just one conversation; it is the unit-instance of the regret-fractal. How you behave in it sets the template for how you will behave in the year-aggregate of similar conversations. The teaching is not "individual moments matter symbolically." It is structural: the small-scale operation IS the large-scale operation, just at one scale of the fractal.

## What This Reframes

The standard frame: regret is the gap between what I did and what I should have done given the outcome.
The principle's frame: regret is the gap between the contorted-self that acted and the actual-self that was suppressed.

The standard frame's solution: do better next time (more accurate prediction, better strategy).
The principle's solution: be yourself this time (stop contorting, regret has no foothold).

The standard frame's failure mode: a lifetime of trying to get-the-outcome that ends in the deathbed verdict ([[dying-woman-what-a-waste]]).
The principle's success mode: a lifetime of proud-behaviors that ends in a deathbed verdict of "this was my life."

## Tone

Joe delivers this directly, almost matter-of-factly. The thought experiment is given as a real proposition, not as inspiration. The expectation is that the mind will protest ("wait, no, that's not what -- I can't, huh?"), and the protest is named as part of the teaching, not as resistance to overcome. The tone is "this is actually how it works; let me show you."

When the principle is offered to a client mid-session about a specific recent regret, the tone is investigative rather than corrective: "what would you have had to do to walk away from that without regret, even if the outcome were the same?" The question is given as a real puzzle, not as a rhetorical setup.
