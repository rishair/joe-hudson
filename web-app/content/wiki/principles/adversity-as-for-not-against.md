# Adversity Is For You, Not Against You (The Wheelbarrow Falls)

## The Principle

A particular kind of reframe operates as a load-bearing principle in transformation work: the adversity in your life is more accurately understood as having served you than as having been done to you. The losses that seemed disastrous, the prayers that went unanswered, the failures that ended what you wanted -- in retrospect, these often turn out to have been the precise events that produced who you became and what you actually have.

This is not the same as "everything happens for a reason" in the conventional consoling sense. It is more specific. The principle says that the part of you that wanted the thing you did not get was usually an identity-extension that was holding you back. The thing being protected by the loss was the part of you that actually could go forward.

Aaron Taylor's phrasing, after describing his reconnection with the father who had been absent through his childhood:

"And it felt like I fell out of the wheelbarrow and part of me died. But what I learned in that moment, Joe, was that thank God, God didn't give me what I wanted because I would have been selling myself short. That God doesn't do things to us, he does them for us. He was actually protecting me." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The specific instance: as a child, Aaron sat on a couch from 8 AM to midnight on the day his father was supposed to show up. The father did not come. Aaron's developmental conclusion at age 8: "Oh, I must not be good enough to love. I must not matter."

Years later, reconnecting with his father as an adult, Aaron discovered something different: "He was admittedly, in his own words, a disaster at that time when I wanted him to be there." The father not showing up turned out to have been protection. The version of fatherhood Aaron had been wanting would have done damage that the absence prevented.

## What the Principle Is NOT

The principle is often confused with several related but different things:

**It is not "everything happens for a reason" as a bypass.** The reason-attribution can become a way to skip the actual feeling of loss. The wheelbarrow story includes "part of me died" -- the death of an identity-extension that was attached to the imagined outcome. The principle does not erase the death. It reframes what the death produced.

**It is not victim-blaming.** Children do not deserve neglect. Trauma survivors do not "need" their trauma. The principle is not a moral judgment that the adversity was right or that the perpetrators were doing the right thing. It is a retrospective recognition that what happened produced something the alternative would not have produced.

**It is not gratitude for the abuse.** The healthy version is gratitude for what survived the abuse and what was forged through it, not gratitude for the abuse itself. Confusing these produces a particular spiritual sickness where people defend their abusers as having "taught them what they needed to learn."

**It is not a guarantee.** Some adversity does damage that is not retrospectively reframable as a gift. Some losses are just losses. The principle applies where it applies, and the application is recognized in retrospect, not asserted in advance.

## The Wheelbarrow Frame

Aaron's wheelbarrow story is the load-bearing image. He recounts a parable about faith:

"A guy goes to the circus and he looks up and he sees the guy on the high wire. [...] Then the guy comes out and gets a wheelbarrow and he's got, you know, like sacks of sand and he's pushing this thing across and it's a little bit more hairy, but the guy still believes [...] that this high wire walker is going to be able to make it from one end to the other. He said faith though is getting out of the stands, climbing up the ladder, and getting inside the wheelbarrow." -- Aaron Taylor, quoting a recovery story, Feel Your Way to Freedom

Faith is in the wheelbarrow, not in the stands. The wheelbarrow is moving across the high wire whether you are in it or watching.

The follow-up principle: sometimes the wheelbarrow falls. Sometimes you get in the wheelbarrow and you do not make it across. The damage is real. And -- this is the part of the principle that does the work -- what is destroyed in the fall is the part of you that could be destroyed. What survives is the part that could not. The fall does not refute the faith. It refines what you are.

Joe's reflection: "There are moments where I have done that. I've gotten in the wheelbarrow and then fallen and, you know, only to find out that the destruction that happens is a destruction of a part of myself that can be destroyed. Leaving me with the part of myself that can't be destroyed. Meaning, yeah, maybe it hurts, maybe it sucks, maybe it sucks for a good long while, but at the end of the day, I'm refined."

This makes the connection to [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]] explicit. The principle here is the same mechanism applied to lived adversity (not to deliberate feeling-work). The fall annihilates an identity-extension. What remains is the actual self. The actual self was protected by the fall, not damaged by it.

## When the Principle Applies in Retrospect

The principle is most useful in retrospect, when the person can see what they did not get and what they have instead. The questions that surface the principle:

- What did you want then that you did not get?
- Who would you have become if you had gotten it?
- What do you have now that would not exist if you had gotten what you wanted then?
- What part of you needed to die for what is alive in you now to be alive?

These questions do not erase the original pain. They reframe what the pain produced. The person can mourn what was lost and recognize what was protected in the same breath.

## When the Principle Does Not Apply

The principle does not apply in the middle of acute pain. Telling someone in the middle of grief that "this is for you, not against you" is usually an attempt to short-circuit the feeling, often by the person who cannot bear the witness. The principle is a retrospective reframe, not a real-time consolation.

The principle does not apply to all adversity equally. Some losses do damage that does not reframe well. The principle is a possibility, not a guarantee, and it lives in particular instances rather than as a general theology.

The principle does not apply when it is being used to defend ongoing harm. "I am benefiting from this adversity" cannot be used to justify staying in an actively damaging situation. The retrospective recognition that past damage produced something valuable does not extend to a prescription that future damage should be welcomed.

## The Mechanism: What the Loss Protects

The mechanism of the principle, when it operates well:

1. The person had an image of what they wanted (the present father, the desired outcome, the requested rescue).
2. The image was an identity-extension -- a story about who they would be if they got the thing.
3. The loss happened. The image was not realized.
4. The identity-extension that depended on the image had to be released over time.
5. What was released was something the person was actually held back by, not just held to.
6. What developed in its absence -- because the developmental energy went elsewhere -- is more aligned with who the person actually is than the realized image would have been.

In Aaron's case: the present father he wanted would have actively damaged the child Aaron was. The absence required him to develop resources (resilience, surrogate father figures in coaches, eventual choice of football) that produced the life he has. The image was an identity-extension calibrated to a fantasy of safety. The reality required something different and produced something different.

## The Fragility-of-Life Mechanism (Why Adversity Produces Gratitude)

A specific channel by which the principle often operates: severe adversity confronts the person with the fragility of life itself, and that confrontation forces a recognition of what is still here. The recognition is structurally aligned with the receive-state of gratitude.

"I think being confronted with the fragility of life is something that brings us closer to gratitude generally. Almost losing our life brings us closer." -- Joe Hudson, Gratitude

The mechanism is concrete:

1. The adversity interrupts the assumption of permanence. The body had been operating as if the breath, the body, the people, the conditions would all continue. The adversity shatters this assumption.

2. The shattering refocuses attention on what is still here. When much has been lost or could have been lost, what remains becomes visible in a way it was not visible when nothing was at risk.

3. The receive-state becomes structurally necessary. The person who cannot produce their own recovery has to receive the help that comes. The receiving becomes daily and embodied.

4. Many of the [[gratitude-as-a-condition-i-can-feel-it-once-x-happens|conditional anti-patterns]] dissolve. The conditions the person was waiting for are no longer available. The person can either be grateful for what is here or be unable to function. Many choose to be grateful.

See [[lottery-winners-vs-accident-survivors]] for the empirical reference Joe uses on this pattern: accident survivors often report more sustained gratitude than lottery winners, because the lottery winners never had to go through the fragility-of-life shift.

See [[base-jumping-broken-hand-gratitude]] for the in-the-moment version of the same pattern, where gratitude arrives in the hospital before recovery has begun.

This mechanism is part of why the principle works -- the adversity produces a specific physiological and perceptual shift that the alternative (continued comfort) does not produce. The shift is not magical; it is the structural consequence of the assumption of permanence being interrupted.

## The Practice Path That Does Not Require Adversity

A critical caveat the fragility-of-life mechanism produces: adversity is not the recommended path to gratitude.

"Please, you don't have to go through that to find gratitude." -- Joe Hudson, Gratitude

The structural fact that adversity often produces gratitude is not a prescription that one should seek adversity. The practice path -- the daily practice of [[gratitude-as-full-body-sport|felt gratitude]], the recognition that [[always-something-spectacular-happening|there is always something to be grateful for]], the deliberate cultivation of the receive-state -- offers the same access to gratitude that adversity sometimes forces.

The practice path has the advantage that the practitioner does not have to lose anything to find it. The adversity path is reserved for those for whom life has already chosen it. The principle here -- adversity is for you, not against you -- is for those people. The practice path is for everyone.

## What This Principle Permits in Coaching

The principle, used carefully, opens several specific moves:

- It allows a coachee to honor the pain of an adversity without being destroyed by the pain.
- It permits the question "what was protected in that?" which often reveals the developmental gift inside the loss.
- It supports the reframe from "this happened to me" to "this happened, and I became who I am partly because of how I met it."
- It pairs with [[fault-vs-responsibility]]: the adversity was not the person's fault, and the person can recognize what they made of it.
- It pairs with [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]]: the loss revealed what was actually them by destroying what was not.

The principle does not direct the work. It is a recognition that often arrives at a certain point in the work and reorganizes the relationship to past adversity.

## The Footprints Reference

Aaron also references the standard "Footprints" parable -- the dream in which the person sees two sets of footprints in the sand throughout their life, except during the hardest periods when only one set appears, and accuses God of abandoning them, only to be told that those were the times the person was being carried. The parable is overfamiliar from religious contexts, but Aaron uses it for a specific structural point: the adversity periods are not the absence of the holding. They are the holding in a different form than the person could see at the time.

The principle here is the secular version: the adversity periods often turn out, retrospectively, to have been the periods of greatest development. The absence of the desired outcome was the presence of something else that the person could not see at the time.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- A coachee describes a loss or adversity that they have not been able to integrate.
- A coachee is recounting their developmental story and the wounds in it.
- A coachee is asking why their hardest experiences happened.
- A coachee has done enough work to be able to see retrospectively what some losses produced.
- A coachee is wrestling with bitterness about something that did not go the way they wanted.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]]: the structural principle of what survives loss.
- Toward [[honor-the-patterns-intent]]: the related principle that the system that responded to the adversity was doing useful work.
- Toward [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]]: the related principle that the breaking opens capacity.
- Toward [[fault-vs-responsibility]]: the distinction that allows the coachee to recognize what they made of an adversity that was not their fault.

## Tone

The principle requires careful tone calibration. Used with the wrong timing or weight, it sounds like spiritual bypass or victim-blaming. Used with the right timing -- after the coachee has felt enough of the original pain to receive the reframe -- it can produce a substantial reorganization of the coachee's relationship to their own history.

The honest tone is: this is a possibility, not a verdict. It is offered for the coachee to test against their own experience. The coach holds the principle lightly enough that they could be wrong about its application to this person's specific instance.
