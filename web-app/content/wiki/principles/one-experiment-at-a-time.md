# Run One Experiment at a Time

## The Principle

Run one experiment at a time. The reason is not productivity. The reason is that learning lands more deeply, more accurately, and more usefully when the experimenter is focused. Multiple concurrent experiments dilute the attention and degrade the quality of what is learned from each.

A pragmatic exception: one experiment in a major life domain (business, say) and another in another domain (personal) are usually compatible. The constraint is on running multiple experiments inside the same domain at the same time.

"I usually just run one experiment at a time. Maybe I have a business experiment and then, and another experiment, my life experiment, but I try not to do too many so I can really savor, focus, understand what's happening, get the most out of the experiment. I just find it's more efficient that way." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

## Why It Works

Three reasons the focus produces more useful learning than the volume:

**1. Attribution.** When two experiments run simultaneously, the effects of each are confounded. A felt shift could be from experiment A, experiment B, the interaction of the two, or something else entirely. The experimenter cannot reliably attribute the result to the variable. One experiment at a time isolates the variable.

**2. Savoring.** Joe uses the word "savor." The texture of what the experiment produces is small and easy to miss. When attention is split across multiple experiments, the small textures get lost. The experimenter notices only the loud effects and misses the subtle ones, which are often where the real learning lives.

**3. Depth.** A single experiment, run with full attention, gets followed wherever it goes. The experimenter notices side effects, second-order consequences, unexpected pulls, places where the experiment fails interestingly. Multiple experiments turn each one into a checkbox; the experimenter does not have the bandwidth to follow any of them deeply.

## What This Replaces

The default tendency in someone who has discovered experimentation: pile on. The person finds that experiments produce learning and concludes that more experiments will produce more learning. They start three experiments simultaneously, then a fourth and fifth, expecting linear or exponential returns. The returns are sub-linear and often actually decline. Each additional experiment subtracts from the quality of all the others.

The principle is not about doing less. It is about doing more learning per experiment. The reduction in concurrent experiments increases the depth of each. The total learning is higher with focus than with volume.

## What Counts as "One Experiment"

The "one" is one focused inquiry at a time, not one action. A single experiment can include multiple components if they all serve the same inquiry. For example: an experiment on "what happens if I wake up and do breathwork in the morning" can include the breathwork, journaling after, and noticing what changed across the day. All of those are part of the one experiment.

What does not count as one experiment: simultaneously running "what happens if I wake up and do breathwork," "what happens if I change my diet," and "what happens if I start meditating in the evening." Each of those is a distinct inquiry. The three together turn the data into noise.

## The Building Pattern

Experiments often build on one another. The result of one experiment naturally suggests the next. Joe describes this as digging down -- one experiment opens the door to the next, and you keep going.

"Oftentimes you're building one experiment on the next. And so that's part of the attitude is, oh, I want to keep on digging. I want to keep on moving down. The tunnel or keep on going down the rabbit hole." -- Joe Hudson

The building pattern works because each experiment was run cleanly enough to know what it produced. If multiple experiments had been running simultaneously, the next one would be built on unclear data. Each clean experiment is the foundation for the next clean experiment. The depth Joe is describing -- digging down through layers of inquiry on the same theme -- requires the focus discipline at each step.

## When the Exception Applies

The "one in business, one in life" exception is honest about how human attention actually works. A person who has a business and a personal life has both running simultaneously regardless. Running an experiment in each domain does not split attention the same way running two experiments in the same domain does. The domains are already separate compartments in the person's cognition.

Other exceptions follow the same logic. If two inquiries are genuinely in different compartments of the experimenter's life (different people, different times of day, different settings), they can be run in parallel without degrading each other. The test is whether the experimenter would mix up which felt shift came from which experiment. If they would, the experiments are too close to each other and only one should be run.

## What to Do With the Other Ideas

The constraint of one-at-a-time produces a question: what do I do with all the other experiments I want to run? The answer is the list. Write them down. Keep the list. Run them sequentially. See [[write-50-experiments-before-starting]] for the practice that makes the list the structural protection.

The list does not need to be 50 long. The list needs to exist. The list is what lets the experimenter say "I will run that next" instead of "I will run that simultaneously." The next-experiment slot is the discipline. The list is the bench.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A person designs an experiment: "for two weeks, I am going to ask only questions in conversations." They run it. They notice things. At the end of two weeks, they reflect: what landed, what shifted, what surprised them. The reflection produces the next experiment: "for two weeks, I am going to speak with full passion every time I want to speak." They run that. Same reflection. The next experiment emerges from the second. Each experiment runs cleanly. The learning compounds.

What does not look like the practice: a person designs five experiments simultaneously on conversation -- asking questions, speaking with passion, being tentative, nodding, listening fully -- and runs all of them in a single week. The data is incomprehensible. The learning is shallow. The person concludes "experiments do not really work for me." The conclusion is correct for this version of running them.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the energy of a practical guideline learned through experience. The principle is not romantic. It is the recognition that the experimenter has limits of attention, and respecting those limits produces more learning, not less. The instruction is delivered casually -- "I just find it's more efficient that way" -- but the principle is operational.
