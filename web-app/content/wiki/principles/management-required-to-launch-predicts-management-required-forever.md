# The Management Required to Start Something Is the Management Required to Keep It Running

## The Principle

The amount of energy required to launch a deal, a hire, a relationship, or a project is the same amount of energy required to sustain it. If you have to push hard to start it, you will have to keep pushing to keep it running. If it starts with flow, it usually continues with flow.

This is not because the start magically determines the rest. It is because the level of fit was already present (or absent) at the start, and the start is where it becomes visible. The friction at launch is the friction in the system. The friction does not go away after launch. It just becomes ongoing.

"The amount of management that it took to make a deal happen was the same amount of management that I would have to consistently provide to make the deal work. So I just would not do the deal because it was the deals that happened with a certain amount of flow and ease that then continued with that same amount of flow and ease. Obviously there's ups and downs with everything, but generally that flow was far more likely." -- Joe Hudson, Enjoy Over Manage

## Where the Principle Applies

**Investing.** Joe learned the principle in venture capital. Deals that closed with ease continued with ease. Deals that closed with heavy negotiation, convincing, and management of the founders required the same management throughout the life of the investment. The pattern was consistent enough that he stopped doing deals that required heavy push to close.

**Hiring.** If you have to convince someone to take the job, you will have to convince them to do the job every day. See [[hire-by-dream-job]]. The candidate whose dream job already matches the role does not need to be convinced. They will not need to be convinced of the work tomorrow either.

**Romantic relationships.** Joe extends the principle implicitly: if you are managing the other person's mood in the early dates, you will be managing it for the duration. The patterns laid down in courtship persist. See [[subconscious-partner-selection]] for the related read on what is being negotiated in the first encounters.

**Emotional control inside an existing relationship.** A specific extension: the principle is also the structural argument against using emotion to control a partner. The control produces compliance. The compliance has to be maintained. The maintenance is exhausting. Eventually the system breaks. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for the full version. The connection: emotional control is functionally identical to a high-friction deal — the energy required to produce the behavior change is the energy required to keep producing it.

"If you have to control and cajole and push somebody into doing something so that you can be happy, you're going to have to maintain that pressure to keep them in that spot. It doesn't frickin' work. Finding what their truth is, finding where they want to move, and finding how that aligns with you, that's a much easier way to get to a place where you're together and at peace. It's why you see relationships where somebody dominates through control, those aren't happy relationships, whether it's controlling through big emotion sadness or big emotion anger or by withholding love. All of that, none of it leads to a happy relationship and just never works." -- Joe Hudson, How To Know If You Are In An Emotionally Abusive Relationship

The alignment alternative is the relational version of the low-friction deal: when both parties are moving from their own truth toward something that fits both, no maintenance pressure is required. The movement maintains itself.

**Business partnerships.** If the partnership requires heavy negotiation to start, the negotiations will continue. Each operational decision will require the same level of structuring that the original agreement required.

**Sales.** If you have to push hard to close, you will have to push hard at every renewal. The customer who is not enthusiastic when they sign will not be enthusiastic later. The amount of relationship management to maintain the account is the amount that was required to win it.

## The Mechanism

The principle is not magical. The reason it holds: launch energy is a direct sample of fit energy. Fit between two parties (a leader and a hire, an investor and a founder, a buyer and a seller) is present or absent before any specific interaction. The launch reveals the fit. Then the fit operates throughout the relationship.

If the fit is high, the launch is easy. The same fit makes the ongoing work easy. If the fit is low, the launch is hard. The same low fit makes the ongoing work hard. The launch was not the cause of the ease or difficulty. The fit was. The launch just made it visible.

This is why "we just need to get past the rough start" almost never works. The rough start is not a phase. It is the system showing what it is. After the rough start, the system continues to be what it is.

## Why This Is Hard to Honor

The principle would lead to walking away from many promising-looking opportunities. The deal that almost closes. The candidate who is almost perfect. The relationship that has potential. The principle says: if it requires push to land, it will require push forever. Walk away.

The difficulty: the push to close usually feels like dedication, professionalism, leadership. It feels virtuous. Walking away can feel like quitting, lacking commitment, leaving value on the table. The principle requires re-reading what "professionalism" means. Sometimes the most professional move is to recognize the fit is wrong and not force it.

This is why [[enjoy-over-manage]] is the upstream distinction. The leader who has internalized that management itself is a signal of inefficiency can read the push-to-close as the signal it is. The leader who still believes management is the work cannot see the signal because they are inside the very thing the signal is naming.

## The Inverse Tell

The principle also operates in reverse. When something starts with ease that exceeds expectation, that is not luck. It is signal. The ease is the fit being visible. The fit will continue to operate. The thing will likely continue to be easy. Honor the signal.

Joe describes this implicitly in his [[creative-career-story]]: when he stopped managing toward outcomes and started saying yes to whatever appeared with ease, the results compounded. The ease was the fit becoming visible. He stopped overriding the signal.

## What to Do When You Notice the Pattern Mid-Stream

The principle is most useful at the decision point. Once you are already in a deal, hire, or relationship that required heavy management to start and continues to require heavy management to run, the question is different.

Three options:
- **Exit.** Recognize the pattern, accept the loss, and move on. Often the best move because the cost of continued management is rarely accounted for.
- **Restructure.** Change the underlying conditions enough that the management requirement drops. Often very hard because the underlying conditions are usually the parties, and the parties usually cannot be changed.
- **Feel through it.** Sometimes the management resistance is your own unfelt emotion. The unfelt thing creates the perception of resistance where there isn't actually any. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]. This is the rarer case but worth checking before concluding the situation is unfixable.

The first option is usually right. The cost of remaining in a high-management situation compounds. The cost of exiting is paid once.

## What This Principle Does Not Mean

This does not mean every difficult start is doomed. There are real cases where the launch is hard because of external complexity rather than misalignment. The principle is about *management of the relationship between the parties*, not about external operational difficulty.

A startup launching in a hard market requires hard work. That is operational difficulty. The principle applies to whether you have to manage the other parties (the cofounder, the investor, the early hires). The internal alignment can be high even when the external task is hard. The principle is about the former, not the latter.

The diagnostic question: am I working hard *with* this person or *on* this person? Working with is fine and may be hard. Working on is the signal the principle is naming.

## Downstream (Where This Leads)

- Toward [[enjoy-over-manage]]: the broader distinction this principle operationalizes.
- Toward [[hire-by-dream-job]]: the specific move for hiring that avoids creating these situations in the first place.
- Toward [[follow-the-demand]]: the upstream business principle. If you follow demand rather than imposing supply, the launch energy is usually low.
- Toward walking away from things that look promising but require ongoing push. The discipline of walking away from "almost good fits" is what this principle produces in practice.
- Toward reading the launch energy as data rather than as the cost of starting.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact. Joe describes the principle as something he learned in investing, with the implication that the learning was empirical: he tried managing his way through hard deals, observed the pattern, and changed his behavior. The tone is "this is what works," not "this is what's virtuous."
