# What You're Chasing in Others' Approval Is the Feeling of Being Good With Yourself

## The Principle

The feeling that approval-seekers are chasing through other people's approval is identical to the feeling produced by being-good-with-yourself-right-now. The chase has been misdirected at an external substrate when the actual substrate is internal and always available.

This is not a moral claim. It is a structural one. The feeling — call it peace, or okayness, or being-good-with-self — has been the actual target the entire time. The strategy of getting it through others' approval has been operating on a flawed assumption: that the feeling has to be earned from outside. The assumption is wrong. The feeling has its source inside. The source has been available the whole time.

"How much of what you're looking for from others is this? How much of what you're looking for by not getting the judgment, by not getting the criticism, is actually you're just looking for this feeling, right? And notice that it never comes. Somebody doesn't judge you, but then they're going to judge you the next moment, or somebody doesn't criticize you today, but they criticize you tomorrow. But this thing, it's always available to you." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

## Why the External Substrate Cannot Deliver the Feeling

The chase fails not because the seeker fails but because the substrate is structurally incapable of producing the result on a sustained basis. Three structural problems:

**The substrate keeps changing.** The same person approves today and withholds tomorrow. The approver who delivered the hit yesterday is not the approver of today. The seeker cannot ever stand on solid ground because the ground keeps shifting underneath.

**The substrate depends on inputs you do not control.** Whether someone approves of you depends on their state when they receive your action — their sleep, their blood sugar, their unrelated stress, their conditioning. None of this is yours to manage. The same act lands differently depending on the receiver's state.

**The substrate's verdict is always provisional.** Even when approval is given, it can be revoked. The seeker who is paying attention knows this, which means the approval, even when received, is not actually doing the work of producing the felt-sense of being okay. The same vigilance that earned the approval is required to keep it. The vigilance is the absence of being-okay.

The internal substrate — being-good-with-yourself-right-now — has none of these problems. It does not change. It does not depend on inputs you do not control. Its verdict is not provisional because it is not a verdict at all; it is a felt-sense available through attention.

## How the Principle Maps to the Question

The principle is the structural claim. The operational form is the question [[what-do-you-need-to-be-good-with-yourself-right-now]]. The question is what makes the relocation of the chase from external to internal.

The sequence in the source teaching:

1. Joe names the chase: most people are spending their lives trying to avoid disappointment, judgment, criticism, and to get approval.
2. Joe asks the question: what do you need to do to be good with yourself right now?
3. The person finds the small felt-sense of being-good-with-self.
4. Joe names the principle: that feeling, that's what the chase has been about all along. And it is always available.

The order matters. The principle does not land as an abstraction. It lands as a recognition after the person has touched the internal feeling. The touching is what makes the principle real, not the explaining.

## What People Object With

When Joe states this, the predictable objection arises: "but I still have to act. I have a meeting. I have to make money. I have to be a husband. I have to be a wife. I have to be a parent."

The objection treats the principle as if it requires withdrawal from action. It does not. The principle reframes the source of action.

"Well, what if you did all that from this space, right? Instead of doing it from the space of, oh, I have to get everybody's love and approval. What if you do it from this place of, oh, what, what do I need to do to be good with myself right now? What are you chasing if you have that? What do you need to chase if you have that?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The question "what are you chasing if you have that?" is the key. The chase was operating because the internal feeling was assumed unavailable. Once the internal feeling is recognized as available, the chase has no target. The action continues — but as expression of the already-okay state, not as the strategy to produce the okay state.

The reframe applies to every domain of action. The job still has to be done. The kids still need to be parented. The meeting still needs to happen. The change is that all of these are done from a place where the doing is not load-bearing for the worth of the doer. The doer is already okay. The doing is what the okay-doer does.

## Why This Cures Approval-Seeking Where Willpower Cannot

A person who tries to stop seeking approval through willpower is trying to give up the chase without giving up the assumption that drives the chase. The assumption — "the feeling I need has to come from outside" — is what keeps the chase running even when the person decides to stop. The willpower fights the symptom without touching the cause.

The principle dissolves the cause. Once the person has felt that the feeling is internally available, the assumption that drove the chase is empirically falsified. The chase does not need to be stopped by willpower; it stops on its own because it has no target. The recognition is the cure.

This is why the operational instruction is not "stop seeking approval" but "ask the question right now." The question produces the recognition. The recognition is what stops the chase. The order is recognition first, behavior change second. Reversing the order does not work — behavior change first produces willpower-based suppression that fails predictably.

## Connection to Self-Possession

The principle is the internal half of [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]]. The self-possession principle describes what changes externally when the chase stops. This principle describes what is structurally true internally that makes the chase able to stop.

A person who has located the feeling internally is also a person who is no longer giving off the signal "I need your approval." The signal change is what produces the social-field change. The internal recognition is upstream of both the felt-sense relief and the relational reorganization. The two effects are paired because they have the same upstream cause: the relocation of the source of the feeling.

The bar/dating example Joe uses for self-possession ("the guy who was self-possessed was the one that women wanted generally; the women that were self-possessed were the ones that guys wanted generally") is the social-effect side of this principle. The reason self-possessed people attract is that they are not running the chase, which means they are not giving off the seeker-signal, which means they are not pushing people away through the extractive dynamic. The internal change produces the external change.

The principle pairs with [[speaking-truth-cures-approval-seeking]] as the other internal mechanism for the same shift. Speaking-truth cures approval-seeking because the speaking is the act of self-approval. This principle dissolves approval-seeking because the recognition that the chased feeling is internally available removes the need for the external substrate.

The two work together. The principle gives the recognition. The speaking is the daily enactment. The recognition without the enactment can drift back into the chase. The enactment without the recognition is performance. Together they install the shift in both the structural understanding and the daily practice.

## What This Is Not

- **Not "don't care what others think."** The principle does not require numbness to others. It relocates the source of the okayness-feeling from external to internal. Care for what others think can still be present as information; it is no longer the substrate of okay-ness.
- **Not withdrawal.** The principle does not require disengagement from relationship or from action. It changes the source from which engagement happens.
- **Not "you don't need anyone."** People are still wanted. The wanting becomes clean because it is not extractive. The wanting comes from already-okay-self toward others, not from incomplete-self toward others-as-completion-source.
- **Not instant.** The recognition lands instantly. The retraining of the default operating mode takes time. The principle is the structural understanding; the integration is the long arc.
- **Not a denial of injury.** People can still hurt you. Criticism can still sting. The principle does not anesthetize the response. It changes the substrate that the response is sitting on. The hurt arrives and moves through; the okayness remains underneath.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The first time the person asks "what do I need to do to be good with myself right now?" and feels a small okay-ness arrive in the body
- The recognition that the okay-ness has been the actual target the whole time, hidden underneath the strategy of trying to earn it from outside
- The discovery that asking the question and waiting for the answer produces the feeling more reliably than years of trying to earn the approval did
- The collapse of an old plan ("once I have X then I'll be okay") into the realization that the okay-ness was available all along without the X
- The strange relief of being able to do the same actions without the desperation that used to drive them
- The recognition, after a few weeks of running the question, that the approval-chase has quieted of its own accord — not from willpower but from the absent target

## Tone

Joe delivers this with quiet conviction and a noticing tone. The principle is offered as a structural observation, not as a sales pitch. The "notice that it never comes" line is delivered slowly — the noticing is the entry into the principle. The follow-up "but this thing, it's always available to you" is delivered with the small relief of someone naming what was always there.

The tone is not "I have the answer." It is "look at what is already true." The principle is presented as a discovery the listener can verify rather than a teaching the listener has to accept.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't use the principle to dismiss the difficulty of approval-seeking.** The chase is structural, installed in childhood, often defended by deep emotion. "Just be good with yourself right now" delivered without the question and the felt-sense touching is dismissive and produces shame.
- **Don't let it collapse into spiritual bypass.** The principle is not "you don't need anything." Emotions, needs, relationships are still part of being human. The principle relocates the source of okay-ness; it does not eliminate the rest of human life.
- **Don't convert it into a new performance.** "I'm so self-possessed now" is the chase wearing a different costume.
