# Wanting Is Aliveness

## The Principle

The act of wanting is what makes life feel alive. Not the having of the thing. Not the getting of the thing. The wanting itself. Strip out the wanting and you strip out the aliveness, even if the thing wanted is still available.

"Wanting is aliveness." -- Joe Hudson and Alexa Kistler, Ending The War With Your Wants

The principle is small and easy to underestimate. Its full weight comes from the thought experiment that operationalizes it: imagine you could have anything for the rest of your life but you were not allowed to want it. The aliveness drains out of the having. The thing is hollow without the wanting that pulled toward it.

## The Sex Thought Experiment

The cleanest demonstration:

"Could you imagine if I was to say to you, okay, for the rest of your life you can have sex, but you're not allowed to want it? Like, I'm just gonna stop. I'm just gonna — I'm gonna strip away all the wanting of sex, but you can still have it." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The response is immediate and universal. It's terrible. It's awful. The having without the wanting is not the same thing as the having with the wanting; it is something else, something hollow, something that misses what made the original thing the thing.

"Right, exactly. That's life. Like, if you don't get to want, you're pulling a lot of the joy out of life. And so it's interesting." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The thought experiment generalizes. Food without wanting it. Connection without wanting it. Art without wanting it. Money without wanting it. In every case the having is structurally available but the aliveness has been pulled out. The wanting was carrying most of the life.

## The Actor and the Director

A parallel demonstration from a different domain. Directors of actors discovered that emotional direction does not produce performance, but wanting-direction does.

"If you tell an actor, 'Do it happier, do it sadder,' like, never works. You never get the performance. But if you say to an actor, 'What you want in this scene is X, Y, and Z,' then the performance happens." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The actor in possession of a clear want generates the performance. The actor instructed in an emotional state generates an imitation of the state with no life in it. The wanting is what produces the aliveness in the performance. The state is the byproduct of the wanting, not a thing to be installed directly.

The principle for the actor is the principle for everyone. You cannot install aliveness. You can install wanting, and aliveness arrives as the byproduct.

## Why This Reframes Most Spiritual Confusion

Many spiritual traditions are read as "give up wanting." The principle reframes the traditions: they are pointing at craving (the gripping form of wanting that produces suffering), not at wanting itself. The clean wanting is what carries the aliveness the traditions are also pointing toward.

"On one level, what they're saying is very true. I just think there's some mistranslation, which is craving and aversion is the root of a lot of suffering... But to live in your wants, to feel your wants, that gives life life. That gives zest to life." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The mistranslation produces a familiar trap: a person reads "give up wanting" as a spiritual instruction, strips out their wanting, and discovers the result is not freedom but flatness. The aliveness left with the wanting. The instruction was for craving, not wanting; the strip-out went too deep.

See [[wanting-vs-craving]] for the full distinction. The corrective for the over-stripped person is not "want more things." It is "feel the wanting that is here, without trying to satisfy it or suppress it." The wanting is already there; it has just been disowned. Re-owning it restores the aliveness.

## The Operational Implication

When someone reports flatness, low aliveness, or "I don't feel anything anymore," the diagnostic moves first to wanting. Has the person stripped out their wanting in service of some self-improvement project? Is the wanting present but disowned? Has the wanting been routed entirely through don't-wants (see [[dont-want-is-a-want]])?

The corrective is not to introduce new wants. It is to allow the wanting that is already present to be felt as wanting. Most flatness in functional adults is not a wanting deficit. It is a wanting suppression.

See [[ive-lost-my-joy]] for the concern this principle responds to most directly, and [[joy-as-natural-state]] for the related teaching about joy as baseline rather than as a thing to be achieved.

## The Coaching Move

For someone who reports flatness or detachment: invite them to notice what they want right now, in this conversation, in this hour, in this week. Not the big wants, not the life-direction wants. The small wants. The wanting for the cup of tea. The wanting for the conversation to land. The wanting for the next song on the playlist.

Feel each one as a wanting, not as a goal. Notice what the body does when the wanting is allowed. The reanimation is usually fast. The system is not broken; the wanting just needed permission to register again.

## What This Is Not

- Not a claim that you should pursue every want. Wanting and acting on the want are separable. The principle is about the felt experience of wanting, which carries aliveness regardless of pursuit.
- Not a claim that all wanting is clean. The craving form is corrosive (see [[wanting-vs-craving]]). The principle applies to clean wanting — the feeling without the grip.
- Not a license to manufacture wants you do not have. The wanting that produces aliveness is the wanting that is actually present, allowed to be felt. Performed wants do not produce the same aliveness because they are not actual wanting; they are imitation of wanting.
