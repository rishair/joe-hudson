# Short-Horizon Planning in Rapid Change

## The Principle

The right planning horizon depends on the rate of change in the environment. In a stable environment, planning 5 years out is operationally useful. In a chaotic environment, planning 5 years out is mostly fiction. The planning horizon needs to match the predictability of the conditions.

In a period of rapid change, the operational horizon contracts to 3-6 months. Inside that window, opportunity is visible. Outside it, the system is producing speculative scenarios that the actual conditions will not match.

The leaders who find opportunity in rapid-change periods do not do so by trying to extend their planning horizon back to stability. They do so by shrinking the horizon to the window where actual opportunity is visible, and acting decisively inside that window.

"The way they find the opportunity isn't 10-year thinking or 5-year thinking, which you can do when there's some idea of stability, but they're finding the opportunity in a more like a 3-month-out period, a 6-month-out period." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## Why the Long Horizon Stops Working

Long-horizon planning depends on the environment being roughly predictable over the duration of the plan. The 5-year plan assumes that the conditions in year 4 will be a reasonable extension of the conditions in year 1. This assumption is roughly accurate in stable periods. It is false in periods of rapid change.

When the assumption fails, the long-horizon plan does several things:

- It produces decisions optimized for conditions that will not exist
- It locks the organization into commitments that the actual conditions cannot support
- It generates a felt sense of certainty (we have a plan) that masks the actual uncertainty
- It consumes the capacity that would otherwise be available to respond to what is actually happening
- It positions the organization to be surprised by events that the shorter-horizon attention would have seen

The leaders who insist on long-horizon planning during chaotic periods are not being responsible. They are being structurally unresponsive. The insistence is often a fear-management strategy: the long horizon feels safer than admitting that the actual horizon is short. The feeling is misleading. The actual safety is in matching the horizon to the conditions.

## Why the Short Horizon Works

The 3-6 month horizon is short enough that the conditions in the planning window will still resemble the conditions at the moment of planning. The opportunities visible at the start of the window are still likely to be opportunities at the end of it. The decisions made for the window can actually be executed inside the window before the conditions shift.

Inside that horizon, the leader can:

- Identify opportunities that are actually available
- Make decisions that will still be relevant when executed
- Allocate resources to actions that will produce results inside the window
- Run experiments whose feedback will arrive while the conditions that prompted them are still operative
- Pivot quickly when the next 3-6 month window reveals different conditions

The result is decisive action that is responsive to actual reality, as opposed to deliberate action that is optimized for imagined reality.

## Connection to the Two Ways Leaders Hold Uncertainty

Joe articulates this principle in the context of distinguishing two ways leaders hold periods of rapid change.

The first group holds the change in fear without feeling the fear. The fear leaks into the head as binary thinking and false-end thinking (see [[fear-in-body-vs-fear-in-head]] and [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]]). This group either freezes or makes increasingly drastic decisions based on collapsed-uncertainty stories.

The second group does the structurally different thing: they feel the fear, let it move through them, and then look for the opportunity. The opportunity they find is in the 3-6 month window, not in the 5-10 year window.

The pairing is important. The short-horizon principle is downstream of the fear-feeling. The leader who has not felt the fear cannot use the 3-6 month window effectively because the fear-driven thinking is still distorting what they see. The leader who has felt the fear can see the actual opportunities that are visible in the short horizon.

This is why the principle is not just operational. It is the practical form of the inner work being available for leadership in chaotic conditions. Without the inner work, the long-horizon insistence is the structurally available response. With the inner work, the short-horizon adaptability becomes available.

## What This Replaces

The conventional executive script insists on long-horizon planning as the marker of mature leadership. The good CEO has a 5-year vision. The good board governs to a 10-year strategy. The good investor evaluates companies on long-term thesis.

This script is correct in stable periods. It is also overgeneralized. In rapid-change periods, the same script produces worse results than honest short-horizon planning would. The long-horizon insistence is often the residue of the previous stable period; the practitioners have not updated their framing to match the new conditions.

The leader who can shrink the horizon when conditions require it -- and then expand it back when conditions stabilize -- is operating from an actual responsiveness rather than from a script. The flexibility is the marker of mature leadership in environments that are not stable.

## What This Implies for Practice

For a leader in a chaotic period:

- **Ask: what is the actual operational horizon right now?** Not what the previous plan assumed. What is currently visible?
- **Compress the planning cadence.** If you used to plan quarterly to a 5-year strategy, plan monthly to a 3-6 month horizon. The plans should be shorter, more specific, and more frequently updated.
- **Treat the long-horizon plan as a frame rather than as a script.** The directional intent can still exist over the long horizon. The operational decisions should be made in the short horizon.
- **Make decisions inside the window where they can actually be executed.** Decisions made for conditions 18 months out, when the actual horizon is 3 months, are wasted decisions.
- **Build pipelines of small decisions rather than one large decision.** The pipeline can adapt as conditions shift. The single large decision cannot.
- **Recognize that the people insisting on long-horizon planning during chaos may be fear-managing.** This includes you. Notice when the long-horizon thinking is a security blanket rather than a useful tool.

For a board, an investor, or an outside observer evaluating a company in a chaotic period:

- The CEO who has shifted to short-horizon operational planning is not failing to lead. They may be leading more accurately than the CEO who is still producing 5-year decks.
- The 5-year deck during a chaotic period is often the more suspect artifact.
- The board's job in chaotic periods includes giving the CEO permission to operate at the appropriate horizon.

## The Application to Personal Life

The same principle applies to personal life in chaotic periods. The person who is asking "what will my life look like in 5 years" during a period of major personal disruption is asking a question that does not have a usable answer. The same person asking "what is the next thing I can do in the next 3 months" is asking a question that has a usable answer.

The short-horizon framing is also less destabilizing emotionally. The 5-year question often produces catastrophizing or forced optimism -- both forms of collapsed uncertainty (see [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]]). The 3-month question stays close enough to the present that the body can actually engage with it.

See [[take-the-adventure-stance]] for the related framing about how to be inside a chaotic period. The short-horizon planning is one of the practical operational moves that the adventure stance makes available.

## Connection to Transformation

The principle is also structurally connected to the bigger pattern in [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]]. The chaotic period is not just to be endured. It is also the rare window in which transformation is structurally available. The short-horizon planning is what allows the leader to actually act on the transformation opportunities the period is presenting, instead of waiting for stability to return before acting.

The leaders who shrink to the short horizon during chaos are not just being adaptive. They are being positioned to take advantage of the once-per-cycle openings that chaos produces. The leaders who insist on the long horizon during chaos are positioned to miss those openings. The post-chaos sorting -- who came out stronger, who came out weaker -- often turns on this single difference in horizon-matching.

## Tone

Joe delivers this observation as a structural fact about how the leaders he works with operate. There is no moral framing -- short-horizon planning is not better than long-horizon planning in some absolute sense. The right horizon depends on the conditions. The tone is operational: this is what works when the ground is moving. The implicit teaching is that the leader who can match the horizon to the conditions, rather than defaulting to the script, has a structural advantage.
