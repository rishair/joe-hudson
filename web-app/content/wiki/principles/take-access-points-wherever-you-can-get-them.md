# Take Access Points Wherever You Can Get Them

## The Principle

The work operates on three layers — intellectual, emotional, nervous system (and arguably a fourth, the energetic). All three are connected. Anything that happens in one of them affects the others. Which means: the coach can enter the work from any layer that is open and the change will propagate to the others.

The principle: do not insist on a particular entry. Find the layer where the person has access right now and use that as the door.

"To me, you're addressing all three. And so I'll take access points wherever I can get them with somebody. So if the breath thing works to get enough access to the other two components, I'll take it. But I'll make sure that the work is done on all three levels as much as possible." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

The reason this works: the three layers are not actually separate. They are one system that we describe in three pieces for pedagogical convenience. Shifting one layer creates change in the others because they are dancing with each other constantly. A thought changes a feeling. A feeling changes a body state. A body state changes a thought.

## Why This Matters

The most common failure mode for a practitioner is forcing the work through their preferred door. A talk therapist tries to talk through every problem. A somatic practitioner insists on the body. A breathwork facilitator wants breath to be the answer. A meditation teacher wants stillness.

Each of these works some of the time. None of them works all of the time. The person who shows up with full access to their body and a complete inability to think clearly needs intellectual deconstruction first. The person who has talked themselves in circles for years needs to drop into the body. The person who is dissociated and numb needs nervous-system activation before anything else.

The coach who has only one tool is matching every person to that tool. The coach who can take access points wherever they show up is matching the tool to the person.

## Where to Start: The Most Common Patterns

There is a default but not a rule. Most Americans are head-dominant. The first move is usually to crack open the intellectual trap that is holding the system in place. Once the intellect has some space, the emotional and nervous-system work becomes accessible.

"In general, most people in society are far more head-oriented. So if you can crack open the intellectual trap that they put themselves in, that's often the first step for people, because that allows them to be able to pay attention to their emotion and/or their nervous system." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

But this is the default for the dominant cultural pattern, not a universal prescription. There are people for whom the nervous-system or emotional work is the right first step.

## The Reichian Body Type Exception: Hysteric

There is a body type the Reichian and Lowen tradition calls "hysteric" — not the colloquial sense of out-of-control, but a specific somatic configuration. People in this configuration are:
- More flighty
- More in touch with energy and crystals (in the cultural-marker sense)
- Bigger emotions on the surface
- More somatically aware

For these people, nervous-system work is the right first step. They are already so in touch with the body that they will feel the breath immediately and respond to it. The intellectual work that helps a head-dominant person will not land the same way; they are not stuck in the intellect, so deconstructing it does not free anything.

"If I see somebody who's very what I would call hysteric, not in a bad word, not hysterical — that terminology is from the Reichian and Lowen work — then that nervous system work is going to be really, really critical for them. Typically their emotions are bigger, that kind of human being, then nervous system work is a really good, important first step for them. Because they're so in touch with it that they'll feel that breath and immediately react and know, oh, this is a good thing that I can enjoy and do." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

The broader implication: body type and dominant intelligence shape where the work should start. The coach reads the person and matches the entry.

## The Discipline Requirement for Single-Layer Practice

A specific exception to the general "start with intellect" pattern: if the person is genuinely committed to a single practice — they have decided that breathwork is the work, or meditation is the work, or somatic therapy is the work — and they have the intellectual structure to be disciplined about it, then starting with that practice can work.

"Unless they're fully committed, unless for some reason or another they're like, 'okay, breathwork is there, I need to do breathwork, and I have the intellectual structure that allows me to have a disciplined approach to something' — which is not a huge amount of Americans anyways — then I would rather work on the intellect, deconstruct that enough, deconstruct the stories enough for them to be able to start finding the enjoyment in the breathwork, and then they'll do it naturally." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

This carves out a specific case. Most people will not stick with a single-layer practice without an intellectual on-ramp. A minority will. For the minority, you can lead with the practice. For the majority, you have to first deconstruct the stories that prevent them from enjoying the practice, because once they enjoy it they'll do it naturally.

## The Eventual Goal: All Three Layers

Taking access points wherever you can get them is the strategy for the entry. It is not the strategy for the whole work. The eventual goal is to do work on all three layers, because:

- Working on only one leaves the others holding the previous configuration in place
- The three layers are bound together (see [[head-heart-gut]] for the pencils-with-rubber-bands image)
- Lasting change requires all three to shift (see [[identity-changes-on-three-levels]])

"In general, the idea is to give them all three tools and allow them to work on all three tools instead of just doing one. And I think that's generally the issue with most work out there: they're working on one or maybe two of the places. And so, really effective for some people, but there's a missing component for others." -- Joe Hudson, 'WTF Is A Nervous System?' With Jonny Miller

This is also why a person who has done extensive work on two of the three layers can have a dramatic breakthrough when the third one finally gets addressed. The tension was already there. Releasing the under-worked layer creates rapid change in the whole system.

## In Practice

The coach's read in the first few minutes of a session:

- **If the person is intellectualizing and going in circles** — the intellect is the trap. Work there first to create space.
- **If the person is emotionally flooded and cannot think** — the emotional or nervous-system layer needs attention before any intellectual work can land.
- **If the person is dissociated and numb** — the nervous system is offline. Gentle activation and somatic contact before any inquiry.
- **If the person is somatically awake and emotionally fluid but stuck in a story** — the intellect needs the work.
- **If the person is somatically wise and ready** — nervous-system practices land immediately.

The principle is responsive, not prescriptive. The coach reads what is open and walks through that door.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A person walks into coaching expecting talk therapy and finds the coach asking them to feel their feet on the floor
- A person comes for breathwork and the session turns into deconstructing a story
- A person has been doing meditation for years and the coach suggests they get angry on their commute
- A person has been in talk therapy for years and the coach asks them to shake their body
- The work that helps them is not the work they expected
