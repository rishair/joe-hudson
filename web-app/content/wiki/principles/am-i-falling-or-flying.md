# Am I Falling or Flying? (The Felt Test of the Right Edge)

## The Principle

In transformative work, you are doing it right when the felt sense is ambiguous between falling and flying. Too little stretch and you are just sitting on a couch. Too much and you are panicking. The right edge — your personal edge, which is different from anyone else's — produces the specific sensation of not being sure whether you are falling or flying. Both at once. That is the diagnostic that you are working at the threshold where actual change can happen.

The image comes from Caroline, a MasterClass alumna three years out, attributing it to someone in her small group: a bunch of people skydiving together. "Sometimes it feels like you're falling, but you're really flying."

The instruction extracted from the image: take what would be a leap for you, given who you actually are. Not someone else's leap. Not the bigger leap because it sounds more transformative. Yours. The right leap is the one that produces the felt question "am I falling, or am I flying?"

"It was about jumping in the way that feels like a big leap for you so that you can feel this sense of, whoa, this is a little big for me. And that's gonna look different for everybody... You have to get beyond playing it safe... You have to take the context of your life, look at what your safe kind of space is and push to the edge of it and then see how it feels to do the course. Like going beyond what the you who came into the course is comfortable with, because then you're gonna see that you're able to do more than you thought." — Caroline, What Changed After Master Class?

## Why the Test Is Felt, Not Decided

You cannot pre-decide what your edge is. The edge is a sensation, not a thought. You find it by getting near it and noticing the felt shift. The body knows the difference between "this is still my comfort zone" and "this is the place where I might do something I have not done before." The thought does not always know.

Two failure modes when the test is decided rather than felt:

**Underestimating the edge:** The person makes a careful calculation about what is "appropriate" stretch and lands well inside the comfort zone. They do the course or the practice exactly as designed and get less out of it than was on offer. They are not falling and they are not flying. They are just doing the thing.

**Overestimating the edge:** The person decides their edge should be where someone else's edge is. They take a leap that is too big for their current capacity. They flood, they panic, they cannot integrate. The leap was not theirs.

The felt test corrects for both. If you are sitting on the couch, you do not feel the falling-flying sensation. If you are flooding, you feel only falling — no flying. The specific signature of being at the right edge is the both-at-once quality.

## The Both-At-Once Quality

The principle is closely related to [[fear-vs-excitement]]: the same energy can show up as constricted fear or expansive excitement depending on the body's relationship to the breath and the situation. At the right edge, both are present. You are afraid and excited. You are stretching and not torn. You are uncertain whether what you are doing will work and you are doing it anyway.

In Hebrew there are two words for fear: yir'ah (the fear that warns of threat) and pachad (the fear of growth into a bigger room). See [[two-words-for-fear]]. The falling-flying sensation is pachad — the fear of becoming who you are being invited to be. It is friendly fear. It is the fear that says you are at the door of your next capacity.

If the sensation is only falling, you have probably accessed yir'ah — the threat-fear — and the edge is too far. Back up. If the sensation is no fear at all, you have not actually approached your edge. Go further.

## Caroline's Application

Caroline gives a concrete example from her own life. She used to write detailed question lists before podcast interviews. The preparation made the conversation predictable. She had a sense that something was being lost in the over-preparation, but the not-preparing felt impossible. Eventually she stopped preparing questions. The first time she did not prepare, she would have thrown up nine years earlier. Now she would not do it any other way.

The arc tracks the principle. The unprepared interview was at her edge — falling and flying. The prepared interview was inside her safe zone. Once she found that her edge was movable, the new edge became the place she works from, and a new edge appeared further out. The shift from "this would make me throw up" to "I wouldn't do it any other way" is what happens when you keep meeting the falling-flying sensation rather than retreating from it.

"I used to write all these questions for guests, and I still get people. I had somebody who was like a New York Times columnist, I didn't prepare a single question. I was just like, I'm just going in and I'm going to see what we find... That would have made me throw up when I started the show 9 years ago, but now I wouldn't do it any other way." — Caroline, What Changed After Master Class?

She is also explicit about the prerequisite: "You have to trust yourself that you can handle going off the map." The falling-flying sensation is only tolerable if there is some baseline trust that you will land. That trust is itself something you build by meeting the edge repeatedly. Each time you do not crash, the trust deepens. Each time you do crash and recover, the trust deepens differently — you learn that the crash is also survivable.

## What This Looks Like Across Domains

**In a workshop or course:** Doing the practice that scares you, not the one that feels comfortable. Sharing the thing you almost did not share. Saying yes to the partner exercise instead of opting out.

**In a coaching session:** Letting the topic that is hardest to talk about come up first, not the topic that feels easier. Following the question that lands in your body, not the question that sounds smart.

**In an ongoing practice:** Doing slightly more than you were planning to do, not because of effort, but because the slightly-more is where you can feel the edge. If the daily practice is meditative and feels safe, the edge is in the harder practice you have been avoiding.

**In a conversation:** Saying the thing that pucks you, not the thing that is comfortable to say. See [[pucker-and-still-say-it]] for the coach-side version of this principle.

**In a life decision:** Choosing the option that produces the falling-flying sensation, not the option that feels like clear flying (no risk) or clear falling (sure crash).

## What This Doesn't Mean

**It does not mean always at your edge.** Sustained falling-flying is exhausting and not actually integrative. The body needs rest. See [[dont-go-after-every-emotion-at-once]] and [[rest-is-an-accomplishment]]. The principle is about how to recognize the edge when you are at it, not about being at the edge constantly.

**It does not mean other people's edges are your edges.** A leap that produces falling-flying for one person produces panic for another and boredom for a third. The image of "skydiving together" is not "everyone do the same thing." It is "we are all in the air, each at our own jump."

**It does not mean the bigger leap is the better leap.** A leap that floods you is not a leap that integrates. It is a leap that traumatizes. The right leap is the one that produces the both-at-once sensation, which is not necessarily the most dramatic-looking leap from the outside.

**It does not mean you should never play it safe.** Some moments require safety. The principle is about the moments when growth is what you are trying to do. In those moments, playing it safe is the failure mode.

## The Connection to Authenticity

Caroline links the principle to the larger arc of the work: "Going beyond what the you who came into the course is comfortable with, because then you're gonna see that you're able to do more than you thought." The leap is not about the leap. It is about the discovery of capacity that was already there but was not visible because the self-image had been calibrated to a smaller version of you.

The falling-flying sensation is also the felt experience of the self-image being slightly out of date. The "you who came into the course" was a self-image that thought it was the whole picture. The leap reveals that it was not. There is more in you than the previous self-image accounted for. The next self-image will absorb the new evidence, settle, and then need to be stretched again. See [[improvement-vs-authenticity]] for the underlying spiral.

## Tone

Caroline delivers this with a quality of having lived it. The image is shared with affection — she remembers it weekly. She does not present it as a technique; she presents it as a way to know whether you are doing the right kind of work right now. Joe affirms it as "really, really well said" and then loops it into his own framing: "needs to feel a little bit like falling, a little bit like flying. That's the place." The principle is meant as a navigation aid, not a standard to live up to.
