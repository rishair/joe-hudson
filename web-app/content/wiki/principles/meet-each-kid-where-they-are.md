# Meet Each Kid Where They Are (Don't Compare Younger to Older)

## The Principle

With multiple kids, a parent's habit becomes to expect the younger one to be where the older one was. The older sibling sets the template; the younger sibling is measured against it. Joe names this as a parenting failure mode and the corrective: meet each kid where they actually are, not where the older sibling was at that age.

"There's this habit of people, comparing the younger one to the older one, kind of thinking that the younger one should be where the older one is, which is totally — instead of going, oh yeah, like she, she, she's younger. And so meeting you're where you're at and in the interest that you have. And you know, what you want to talk about and what Una wants to talk about are completely different, totally different things. Yeah, different, totally different things. And to be in your world, to go down the rabbit hole in your world, is really an important thing to me." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and Esme — The Teenage Years

The two halves of the move: (1) drop the comparison to the older sibling; (2) meet this kid in their actual interests, their actual age, their actual world.

## Why the Comparison Happens

The comparison is automatic. The parent has already done the older kid's development once. They have a felt sense of "what a 12-year-old should be capable of," "how a kid this age talks," "what is reasonable to expect" — and all of that felt sense is built from the older sibling. The parent does not realize they are using one kid as the calibration for the other.

The comparison is also amplified by efficiency. Treating siblings as similar requires less parental attention than treating each as their own person. The parent can run the same systems, give the same talks, expect the same behaviors. Treating them as individuals requires more bandwidth.

The comparison is also amplified by the parent's projection. The parent often wants the younger to share the older's strengths. "Your sister is good at math, you should be too." "Your brother handled this at your age, why can't you?" The wish that they were more alike becomes the corrective pressure that makes each kid feel they are being measured against the wrong yardstick.

## What the Younger Kid Experiences

The younger kid receives a structural message: who you actually are is not what is expected of you. The expectation is calibrated to a different person — your older sibling — and you are failing to meet it. The younger kid's developmental work has to be done in the shadow of the comparison.

This produces several common outcomes:
- The younger kid develops a chronic sense of inadequacy against an older sibling they cannot outrun.
- The younger kid develops a contrarian identity — refusing the older sibling's path simply because it is the path they are being compared to. Their identity becomes shaped by reaction rather than by their own actual interests.
- The younger kid suppresses their own interests because their interests are not the older sibling's interests and therefore not what the parent is calibrated to register as valuable.
- The younger kid loses the parent's curiosity about their actual world because the parent is busy comparing it to the older sibling's world.

None of this serves the kid. None of this is what the parent intended. The mechanism runs without anyone choosing it.

## What Meeting Each Kid Looks Like

The corrective is structural. The parent has to actively notice when they are calibrating against the older sibling and re-orient to the actual kid in front of them.

Joe describes the practical move: "What you want to talk about and what Una wants to talk about are completely different, totally different things." The acknowledgment itself is the move. The parent registers that the two kids occupy different worlds. The parent then commits to being in each world separately rather than treating them as variations of one world.

The behavioral form:
- Each kid gets their own [[go-down-the-rabbit-hole-in-your-kids-world]]. The parent learns each one's interests as a separate inquiry.
- Each kid gets their own [[connect-trip-with-your-kid]]. The connection time is one-on-one, not a family group.
- The parent's expectations for what is age-appropriate get calibrated to the actual kid, not to the older sibling at the same age. Different kids hit different milestones at different times. The kid who is reading at 4 and the kid who is reading at 7 are both fine.
- The parent's metric for whether a kid is doing well is the kid's own felt sense and capacity, not a comparison to the older sibling's trajectory.

## The Younger-Sibling-Specific Application

A specific failure mode for the second (or third, or fourth) kid: the parent is operating from a sense that they have "done the work" of figuring out parenting with the older kid, and the younger kid should benefit from the wisdom. In some ways this is true. But the parenting wisdom is largely about parenting that specific older kid, not about parenting in general. The younger kid often needs different things.

The parent has to be willing to start over. The fact that the older sibling responded to a particular approach does not mean the younger sibling will. The fact that the older sibling needed structure does not mean the younger needs structure. The fact that the older sibling wanted space does not mean the younger wants space. Each kid is a fresh case.

## The Older-Sibling-Specific Application

The older sibling is also affected by the comparison habit. They become the benchmark against which the parent measures. They carry the structural weight of being the template. They often inherit the parent's first-time-parent mistakes, their first-time-parent anxieties, their first-time-parent over-corrections.

The corrective for the older sibling: they are also a specific kid, not just the version of the older sibling that exists in the parent's calibration system for the younger. The parent should periodically check that they are still meeting the older kid where they are, not where they were when the parent was figuring out how to parent them.

## Connection to Liking the Kid

This principle is a structural condition for [[liking-your-kid-vs-loving-your-kid]]. The parent who is constantly comparing one kid to another cannot fully like either, because the parent is not actually looking at either kid — the parent is looking at the gap between them. Liking requires meeting the actual kid. Meeting the actual kid requires dropping the comparison.

The principle also connects to [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]]. An adult you respect is not measured against their sibling. The respect is for them as a person, on their own terms. Extending the same to the kids means meeting each kid as a person, on their own terms.

## The Larger Principle Underneath

The principle is a parenting instance of the broader principle that comparison is not the right tool for meeting people. See [[comparative-mind]] for the underlying teaching. The mind that compares is the mind that is not present with what is actually here. The parent comparing siblings is the parent not present with either of them.

The corrective in both cases is the same: come back to what is actually in front of you. The actual kid. The actual interests. The actual conversation. The actual moment.

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent realizing they routinely compare their younger kid to the older
- A parent realizing the younger kid is contrarian against the older and the parent has been part of that dynamic
- A younger adult sibling recognizing they grew up being measured against an older sibling and how it shaped them
- A parent of a second baby realizing they need to start over with this kid rather than apply what worked with the first
- A parent who feels closer to one kid than the other and wonders why

Matter-of-fact. Joe names the comparison habit as a thing parents do, not as a moral failure. The corrective is described as a recognition, not as a discipline.
