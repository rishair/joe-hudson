# Everything Goes Away Anyway

## The Principle

A specific objection arises when the client begins to let themselves feel something good — excitement about a wanted thing, love for a person, joy in a moment. The objection sounds reasonable: "I shouldn't let myself feel this because it's going to go away." The reasoning: feeling the good thing now will only make its disappearance more painful. Better to not feel it. Better to defer.

The principle is the corrective. The good thing is going to end whether you feel it or not. The ending is not contingent on your feeling. The only thing your not-feeling controls is whether you got to feel the good thing while it was here. The not-feeling does not prevent the loss; it just adds a second loss (the unfelt-good-thing) to the first one (the now-ended-thing).

Joe's compressed delivery of the principle:

"It's gonna go away no matter what. Everything goes away. Yeah, it's not about whether you feel excitement or not. It just— shit goes away. Everything ends." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The follow-up that makes the absurdity of the objection visible:

"I'm not gonna love— I'm not gonna love you because you're going to go away. I'm not going to love my kids because they're going to go away. I'm not going to coach people because they're all going to go away." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The client's recognition: "No, that doesn't make sense."

Joe's confirmation: "Doesn't fucking make any goddamn sense."

The principle works through the reductio: when the impermanence objection is generalized to the rest of life, the absurdity becomes obvious. No one would refuse to love their kids on the grounds that the kids will eventually leave or die. The same logic applied to excitement, joy, or any good feeling is just as absurd. The absurdity, once seen, dissolves the objection's grip.

## Why This Works

The impermanence objection is one of the most common defenses the system uses against allowing wanted feelings. The defense has a structure: it feels like wisdom (everything does end), but it is using the wisdom to prevent the very feeling that would make life worth living.

The principle does not argue with the wisdom. It accepts the premise — yes, everything ends — and then asks what follows. The conclusion the objection draws (therefore I shouldn't feel the good things) does not follow from the premise. The premise is true; the conclusion is a non sequitur. The principle exposes the non sequitur by carrying it to its logical conclusion. If "it will end" is a reason not to feel something, then it is a reason not to feel anything ever, which is no one's actual position.

Once the non sequitur is visible, the objection loses its authority. The client can still notice the impermanence and feel the worry — but the worry is no longer functioning as a reason to defer the feeling. The deferring has been exposed as not following from the noticed impermanence.

The reductio also bypasses the intellect's defense. If Joe argued against the impermanence objection directly ("but feeling it now doesn't make the loss worse"), the intellect would generate counter-arguments. By extending the objection to absurd domains (kids, partners, work), the intellect cannot defend the position without obviously contradicting itself. The recognition is somatic, not just intellectual — the client feels the absurdity.

## The Deferral Pattern

The principle addresses a specific behavioral pattern: deferring the feeling of a good thing to manage the loss of the good thing in advance. The client believes they are protecting themselves. What they are actually doing is converting the future loss into a present loss while keeping the future loss intact.

Joe's articulation of this in the source session, after the client has reported the deferral pattern explicitly:

- **Client:** "I see that when I'm able to just be in that excitement, I'm like a magnet for opportunities and stuff. And then I'm like, oh, this thing is so good, like, I feel like it's gonna go away. And then I don't let myself feel the excitement, and then it goes away, and it just defers."

The client's report shows the full mechanism. The excitement attracts the opportunities. The fear that the excitement will end leads to the deferring of the excitement. The deferring is also the deferring of the opportunities. The future loss the deferring was meant to prevent has been recreated as a present loss.

The deferring also does not prevent the future loss. The opportunities, once deferred, do not return as undeferred opportunities later. They are gone. The thing the client tried to protect against has happened, just earlier than it would have happened anyway.

The principle exposes the math. The deferring costs the present feeling. The deferring does not prevent the future loss. The transaction is purely negative.

## The Disappointment Underneath

The impermanence objection is often a disguise for an avoided feeling: disappointment. The client has, somewhere in their history, had a good thing end. The ending produced disappointment. The disappointment was not metabolized — it was felt as too much. The system installed a protection against future disappointments by preventing the good things from being felt strongly in the first place.

The disappointment underneath is the actual material to work. The impermanence objection is the surface defense; the unmetabolized disappointment is what the surface defense is protecting against.

In the source session, after the principle has been delivered, Joe moves directly to the disappointment:

"And that disappointment, I wonder what it's going to be like when you breathe into that too, when you learn to enjoy that. Maybe it's also not what you think it is." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The pivot has a specific shape. The principle has dissolved the impermanence objection. The dissolution exposes the disappointment underneath. Joe pivots to the disappointment as the next material, applying [[breathe-into-the-emotion-to-enjoy-it-not-to-get-rid-of-it]]:

"Go to the disappointment. Go to the place where you get disappointed because the thing goes away. Go to a memory where it happened. Feel the disappointment and breathe into it." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The client breathes into the disappointment and reports: "It feels like I went to acceptance and then I went to dropping it off like a weight." The disappointment, met without removal-agenda, transforms into acceptance and release.

The principle and the disappointment work are paired. The principle dissolves the surface defense; the disappointment work metabolizes the feeling that the defense was protecting against. Together they remove the architecture that the impermanence objection was holding in place.

## The Deeper Frame

The principle rests on a deeper teaching that Joe sometimes makes explicit in other contexts: the same energy that allows the feeling of the good thing is the energy that allows the feeling of the loss. Capping one caps the other. A person who has decided not to feel excitement to avoid future disappointment has also decided not to feel anything fully. The cap is general.

The corollary: people who have done the work to feel their losses fully are the same people who can feel their joys fully. The two capacities are the same capacity. The work of metabolizing past disappointment is what makes future excitement possible.

See [[joes-funeral-poem-for-kase]] for the related teaching on this in the context of death. The poem articulates the same principle at the existential level — we cannot survive this life; the only question is how fully we get to be in it while we're here.

The principle is also related to [[what-we-fear-we-invite]]. The not-feeling of the good thing, motivated by fear of its ending, is itself a way of pre-living the ending. The fear-driven deferral creates the experience the fear was trying to prevent.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- He does not deny the impermanence. The premise is granted. Yes, everything ends. The principle takes the premise seriously and then exposes the non sequitur.
- He does not promise the loss won't be painful. The loss will be painful when it comes. The principle is not about avoiding pain; it is about not adding a second pain (the unfelt good) to the first.
- He does not lecture the client on impermanence philosophy. The principle is operational, not philosophical. The point is to dissolve the present defense, not to teach Buddhism.
- He does not let the client convert the principle into a new should ("I should feel the good things even though they'll end"). The principle is a corrective, not a new requirement. The point is freedom to feel, not obligation to feel.

## What This Is Not

- It is not [[the-minds-stories-about-emotions]]. That principle addresses the three specific myths about emotions (I'll be sad forever, I'll destroy what I love, I'll become incapable). This principle addresses the specific defense of deferring-feeling-to-manage-future-loss.

- It is not a denial of grief. Grief is appropriate when things end. The principle is not against feeling the loss when it comes. The principle is against pre-feeling the loss now to avoid feeling the good thing now.

- It is not a denial of healthy caution. Some things should be invested in cautiously because the chance of loss is high. The principle is for the situations where the client is refusing to feel a good thing they are already in because of generalized impermanence, not because of specific elevated risk.

- It is not [[disappointment-with-self-is-the-safe-bet]]. That principle addresses the trade of unknown high-intensity emotion for familiar low-grade emotion. This principle addresses the specific deferral of currently-available feelings to manage future loss.

## Coaching Path

The principle is operational when the impermanence objection has surfaced as a defense against allowing a feeling. The standard sequence:

1. The client reports the impermanence-based defense: "I shouldn't let myself feel this because it will go away."
2. The coach states the principle: "It's going to go away no matter what. Everything goes away."
3. The coach extends to the reductio: "I'm not going to love you because you're going to go away. I'm not going to love my kids because they're going to go away."
4. The client recognizes the absurdity: "No, that doesn't make sense."
5. The coach confirms: "Doesn't make any sense."
6. The coach pivots to the underlying disappointment: "And that disappointment, I wonder what it's going to be like when you breathe into that too."
7. The disappointment work begins. See [[breathe-into-the-emotion-to-enjoy-it-not-to-get-rid-of-it]].

The whole sequence is fast — often under a minute — because the principle works through immediate recognition rather than through extended teaching. The brevity is appropriate; the principle is a clearing move, not the work itself.

## Tone

Direct, deadpan, slightly absurd. The reductio relies on the absurdity being delivered straight. "I'm not going to love my kids because they're going to go away" works because it is said with the same matter-of-fact tone as the original objection. The matching tone exposes the underlying logic as the same.

The follow-up "doesn't make any goddamn sense" is delivered with a small smile. The smile shares the recognition with the client. The two of them are seeing the absurdity together. The shared recognition is what dissolves the objection's grip — the client no longer has to defend it because they are now in on the joke.

The principle does not lecture. The principle does not moralize. The principle just reflects the objection back in its generalized form and lets the absurdity do the work.
