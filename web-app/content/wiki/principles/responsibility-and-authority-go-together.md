# Responsibility and Authority Go Together

## The Principle

If a person is responsible for an outcome, they need the authority to act on it. The common organizational anti-pattern is to assign responsibility without authority — making someone accountable for results they cannot actually shape because the decisions belong to someone else. This produces predictable dysfunction: resentment, learned helplessness, decisions made too far from the work, and a slow eviction of agency from the organization.

The corrective is structural: pair them. The person responsible for the outcome gets the authority to make the calls. This is rare in practice because it requires the formal authority to trust distributed decision-making, but it is the design that makes distributed leadership actually work.

"In our company, we even give the authority that way, which is rare in a company. We actually say if you're responsible, you get the authority." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

## Why The Pairing Is Required

Responsibility without authority is the most common failure mode in organizations. It looks accountable on paper. In practice it produces a person who is asked to deliver outcomes they cannot drive. The dynamics:

- **The decisions get made by the wrong people.** The person with authority is not the person closest to the work. The decision is upstream of the relevant information.
- **The responsible person becomes the conduit for blame.** Outcomes belong to them; decisions do not. So they end up explaining why decisions made elsewhere produced bad outcomes. The pattern installs the [[fear-triangle]] structurally.
- **Real authority leaks elsewhere.** The decisions still get made somewhere — usually by whoever does have the authority, who is typically removed from the consequences. This produces decisions optimized for the decision-maker's context, not the consequence-bearer's.
- **The responsible person learns to disengage.** Why care about an outcome you cannot shape? The pattern erodes ownership over time.

Authority without responsibility is the inverse failure: a person with the power to decide but without ownership of the consequences. This produces reckless decisions, no learning loop, and the decision-maker positioning themselves to take credit for upside and pass downside to whoever holds the responsibility.

The pairing eliminates both failure modes. Whoever holds the consequence holds the decision. The pairing is what makes accountability real instead of theatrical.

## Why The Pairing Is Rare

Joe notes that the pairing is "rare in a company." The reasons are structural:

1. **Formal hierarchies are built around centralized decision-making.** Distributing authority requires the formal authority to actively let go of decisions they could otherwise make.
2. **Responsibility is cheap to assign; authority is expensive.** Assigning responsibility costs the assignor nothing. Assigning authority requires giving up the option to override.
3. **The fear of bad decisions made by distributed authorities.** If the work is high-stakes, the formal authority worries that distributed authority will produce mistakes the centralized form would have prevented. The worry is sometimes valid and often overweighted.
4. **The role-of-the-leader confusion.** Many formal leaders believe being the leader means making the calls. Distributing the calls feels like distributing the role. See [[lead-and-follow]] for the distinction that resolves this.

The pairing requires the formal authority to trust that distributed decision-making produces better outcomes than centralized decision-making most of the time, and to design the organization around that trust.

## What The Pairing Looks Like

Joe's company example: leadership rotates by domain. One person leads on the strategy. Another leads on the producer side. Another leads on operations. Each gets the authority over their domain because they hold the responsibility for the outcome in that domain. The formal CEO does not override domain decisions outside of clear principle violations.

"You are a leader on some things. I am a leader on some things. Mattia is a leader in some things. Mun is a leader in some things. And they get to be that leader as long as they're stepping into that leadership." -- Joe Hudson, Are You Avoiding Your Own Power?

The "as long as they're stepping into that leadership" qualifier is important. The authority is not assigned and forgotten. It is sustained by the person actually taking the leadership move. If they do not, the authority remains available but uninhabited, and the leadership emerges from whoever does take the move (see [[leadership-emerges-from-confident-direction]]).

## The Connection To Following Conviction

The pairing supports the rule [[we-follow-leadership-not-blame]]. When someone takes the leadership move on a problem they identified, the pairing means they get the authority to actually fix the thing. Without the pairing, the leadership move dies on contact with the authority gap — the person is told to lead and then cannot execute because the decisions still live elsewhere.

The combination of "leadership gets followed" and "responsibility gets authority" produces the durable form of distributed leadership. Either rule alone is insufficient. Both together create the conditions for the organization to function as a collection of leaders rather than a hierarchy with a single decision-maker.

## What The Pairing Is Not

**Not anarchy.** The pairing is structured. The formal leader still holds the organization's principles, the budget envelope, the strategic direction. The distributed authority operates within those constraints. See [[if-you-dont-stand-into-authority-others-will-vie-for-it]].

**Not consensus.** The pairing does not require everyone agreeing on a decision before it gets made. It requires the person with the responsibility to decide. The decision can be wrong. The responsibility for the wrongness lives with them. The learning loop runs.

**Not the absence of escalation.** Decisions that exceed the local authority escalate. The pairing is about the default location of decision-making, not about removing all escalation paths.

## The Anti-Pattern: Lobbying The CEO

A specific anti-pattern the pairing is designed to prevent: team members lobbying the CEO to override domain leaders' decisions. The pattern undermines the distributed authority and reinstalls centralization. The corrective is the CEO consistently routing decisions back to the domain leader: "this is their call." The pattern dies when it stops being rewarded.

The same pattern in reverse: domain leaders bringing decisions up to the CEO they should be making themselves. The corrective is the CEO declining to make the call: "you have the authority on this, what do you want to do?" The pattern dies when the CEO stops absorbing the decisions.

## When This Comes Up In Coaching

- A founder asking how to scale without becoming the bottleneck
- A leader exhausted from making every decision in the organization
- A team member who feels accountable for outcomes they cannot affect
- A team member with authority but no clear accountability for what they do with it
- A founder who has assigned roles but is still being asked to make the role-holders' decisions
- An organization where everyone is responsible for everything and accountable for nothing

## Tone

Joe describes this as one of the more uncommon organizational design choices. The tone is direct and operational: this is how it works in his company, this is the consequence, this is what other companies usually do instead. There is no moral pressure to adopt it — just clarity about what the choice produces.
