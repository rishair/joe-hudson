# The Wanting Is What Matters, Not What You Want

## The Principle

The act of wanting is the engine. The specific content of what is wanted is not. The wanting is the directional pull of evolution. What you happen to want at any given moment is one of many possible strategies for that pull. The strategies are inconsequential. The pull is everything.

"Wanting is critical. What you want is really inconsequential. What you want is directionally correct, but it is not the end-all be-all of anything. That wanting is the natural pull of evolution, of authenticity. What you want is a strategy to get there, and there's 10 or 20 strategies. So what you want is inconsequential, and there's no reason to attach to it. But it is to follow your wanting and then to watch how your wanting changes and watch how what you want changes." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## What This Resolves

A common confusion when working with wants: people start to feel that if they follow a specific want and it does not pan out, the whole framework has failed. They want X, they pursued X, X did not happen, therefore the wanting principle was wrong.

This principle resolves the confusion. The wanting was correct. X was one strategy. The strategy did not pan out. The wanting did not fail. The wanting continues, now informed by what X taught.

This is also why coaching can sit with someone whose wants are shifting rapidly without anxiety. The shifting is the principle working, not the principle breaking. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]].

## What You Want Will Change

People always assume their goals will hold steady. They never do. Joe has seen many things not change as people go through this work and many things change, but the goals always change.

"I've never seen the goals of a person not change through the journey. What's often interesting is the goals that they used to have just get met naturally without any effort because they become just a step in what's necessary for them to evolve into their authenticity." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

Sometimes the goal is met as a byproduct (the person who stopped caring about money and then money came in). Sometimes the goal dissolves because the underlying want refines. Either way, attaching to the specific content of the want is attaching to a strategy that is going to change anyway.

## The Practical Reframe

In coaching:
- Do not try to get the want "right" before following it.
- Do not treat changing wants as instability.
- Do not over-invest in the specific content of any single want.
- Do invest in the practice of noticing the wanting itself.
- Do notice how the wanting refines as it is followed.

In personal life:
- The job, the relationship, the project, the goal: these are vehicles.
- The wanting that pulled you toward them is the actual engine.
- When the vehicle stops serving the wanting, get a different vehicle. The wanting keeps going.

## The Connection to the River

In [[improvement-vs-authenticity]], authenticity is described as a river. The river has a way it wants to flow. Next year the river will run differently. The principle here is the river-equivalent at the level of wanting: the wanting is the river. The thing you want this year is the channel the river is currently running through. The channel will change. The river continues.

## The Connection to Holy Confusion

When the wanting principle is taken seriously, what someone wants starts changing more rapidly. This can feel disorienting. The fear shows up: "I don't know what I want anymore."

The reframe: not knowing what you want at the strategy level is fine if you are still in touch with the wanting at the directional level. The wanting is what stays. The wants come and go.

"The goals shift and then the goals disappear and then there's no goals for a while. And then after there's no goals for a while, there's very specific goals. And then there's just this movement that's like, the goal is to live principled because you know that living principled will make you happier than any goal that you could ever achieve." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

See [[im-afraid-of-stagnating]] for the holy confusion territory.

## Eventually, Principled Living

The endpoint of following the wanting is not a final want that solves everything. It is principled living that becomes choiceless. The pleasure of living in alignment with one's principles outweighs the pleasure of any specific achievement. It stops being a choice because the alternative is too painful.

"It becomes choiceless at a point. It becomes outside of your power at some point. It's like, I just can't not live principally because it's too damn painful." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

See [[principles-free-you]] for the related principle.

## What This Does NOT Mean

- It does not mean wants are irrelevant. The specific want is the current expression of the wanting. Follow it. See [[wants-deepen-as-you-follow-them]].
- It does not mean you should not pursue what you want. The pursuit is how you discover whether the strategy serves the wanting.
- It does not mean wants are interchangeable. Some wants are clean (see [[wanting-vs-craving]]). The wanting is best honored by clean wants.
- It does not mean detachment in the sense of not caring. The wanting is full caring at the directional level, with looser grip on the specific destination.
