# Imposter Syndrome Projects Incompetence Onto the People Who Hired You

## The Principle

Imposter syndrome contains a hidden projection. To feel like an imposter in a role you were selected for, you must implicitly assume that the people who chose you did not know what they were doing. You must assume the person who hired you, the board who approved the hire, the panel who awarded the position, the audience who showed up to see you -- all of them -- got it wrong. The imposter feeling is structurally bound to a judgment that others are imposters too.

"If somebody hired me -- you know, I got hired to do a BASE jumping commercial. And for me to feel like I'm an imposter there, I have to also assume that the person who hired me doesn't know what they're doing. And so I have to project that other people are imposters to interact with me and to trust me and like depend on me." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

The projection often goes further than one level. If the people who hired you are also imposters (which the imposter feeling requires you to assume), then the people who hired *them* are also imposters. The chain runs up. The structural logic of imposter syndrome turns into a sweeping judgment that no one in the selection process knew what they were doing.

"If you're an imposter, then the person who hired you is an imposter... whoever hired them. Yeah, and who hired them." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

## Why This Reframe Cuts Through

Making the projection visible exposes a tension the imposter cannot easily defend. The person who feels like an imposter usually has respect for the people who chose them. They were grateful for the opportunity, impressed by the person who hired them, honored to be selected. They cannot easily hold both "I respect the judgment of the people who chose me" and "those people made a mistake choosing me."

The standard imposter syndrome interpretation requires them to hold both. The reframe makes the contradiction obvious. Either the people who chose you knew what they were doing (in which case you are not the unqualified-fraud the imposter feeling reports), or they did not (in which case you have a problem with their judgment, not with your qualifications). The first option dissolves the imposter feeling. The second option is one most people, on inspection, do not actually want to assert.

The reframe is not "they were right about you so you should feel qualified." That would still be in the worth-via-external-validation frame the imposter syndrome lives in. The reframe is structural: the imposter syndrome cannot operate without the projection, and once the projection is visible, the syndrome loses one of its structural supports.

## The Three Cascading Levels

The full projection has three levels:

1. **The hirer level.** The person who chose you did not know what they were doing.
2. **The hirer-of-the-hirer level.** Whoever put that person in the position to choose you also did not know what they were doing.
3. **The systemic level.** The whole system of selection and validation is operating on incompetence.

Most imposter syndrome operates only at level 1 in the person's awareness. The deeper levels are implicit. Making them explicit ("if the person who hired you was wrong, what does that say about whoever decided they were qualified to hire?") often produces a small cognitive break where the person sees the absurdity of the projection.

The break does not always cure the imposter feeling, but it does loosen the cognitive scaffolding that held it in place. The person becomes more willing to consider that the people who selected them might have been competent at the selection, which means the selection might be informing about something other than the imposter feeling.

## What Joe Does With This in a Session

The move is to name the projection and let the client sit with the implication. The coach does not have to argue the client out of the imposter feeling. The naming does the work.

A representative sequence:
- Client: "I just don't think I should be in this role."
- Coach: "Interesting. Who decided you should be?"
- Client: "Well, [person] hired me."
- Coach: "And what do you think of their judgment, generally?"
- Client: "They're really sharp."
- Coach: "So they're really sharp, and they made the call to hire you. Which means for you to be the imposter you say you are, they were not sharp at this moment. Which one is true?"

The client usually pauses. The tension is now visible. They can either revise their assessment of the hirer's judgment (which most do not want to do) or revise their assessment of their imposter status. The second is more available than they realized.

## A Note on When This Move Doesn't Fit

The projection move is most useful when the person was clearly chosen by someone -- hired into a role, given an award, asked to take a position. It does not fit as cleanly when the person put themselves into the role (founder, artist working independently, someone whose imposter syndrome is about something self-initiated). For those cases, the other moves are usually the better entry points.

For the self-initiated cases, the related move is to ask "what do you assume about the people who are showing up for your work?" The same projection dynamic often appears one layer over -- the founder assumes the customers must not know what they're doing for trusting the product, the artist assumes the audience must not know what they're doing for appreciating the work. The reframe is structurally the same: making the projection visible exposes a tension the person cannot easily defend.

## Tone

Light and slightly playful. The projection move works best when delivered with curiosity rather than confrontation. The coach is pointing at something the client probably has not noticed, not catching them in a contradiction. The discovery should land as "oh, that's interesting" rather than "oh, I'm caught."

The cascading version ("and who hired them") has a slightly comic quality that helps. The full chain of incompetence the imposter projection requires, when made explicit, often produces a small laugh. The laugh is part of how the projection loosens.
