# Nature Improves Itself

## The Principle

Growth, learning, and improvement are not things that must be added to a person. They are the nature of being alive. The oak tree grows without trying to improve. A 6-year-old learns constantly without being told to. Improvement happens. The improvement project is friction layered on top of a process that would happen anyway.

"Our nature is to grow. Our nature is to improve. Our nature is to learn. A 6-year-old kid is constantly wanting to learn and grow, and it doesn't stop unless someone has kicked the love of learning out of us." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The Oak Tree

The recurring image. The oak tree grows. It does not check its growth against a model. It does not measure its progress. It does not ask whether it is improving fast enough. It just grows because that is what oak trees do. Growth is its nature.

"I always use this metaphor of an oak tree because when I look out my window, there's an oak tree. The oak tree grows, the growth happens. Does it need to? No. Is it looking to improve itself? No. It's just its nature." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

The principle: humans are like the oak tree. The growth happens. The improvement project is what the oak tree does not need.

See also [[inherent-goodness]] for the oak tree's other use: when is an oak tree good enough?

## Children as Proof

The clearest evidence that growth is natural: children. Babies smile most at faces that come at them sideways, because the sideways face signals play, and play is learning. The drive to learn is wired in.

"All humans are. And somehow or another, we can put them into a school system, tell them that they have to improve and get A's, and then they stop wanting to learn." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

The kicker: about 47% of highly intelligent kids fail high school. The improvement frame, applied to a being whose nature is already to learn, can break the learning. Joe references the psychological studies: tell a child they are smart, and when they try and do not succeed, they will stop trying, because trying without success threatens the smart identity. The improvement frame produces the stagnation it claims to prevent.

## The Unschooling Evidence

Kids pulled from traumatizing school environments and given the rule "no destructive activities, but no required work" often do no work for 3 to 6 months. Then they decide they want to learn math. Then they learn from fractions to calculus in approximately 3 to 5 months.

The conventional frame would call this stagnation followed by sudden acceleration. The accurate frame: the kids stopped fighting their nature and then their nature took over. The fast progress was always available. The improvement project was suppressing it.

"They want to learn. Because it is their desire to learn in that direction, and they want to do it, and they will do it." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The Hacky Sack Demonstration

Joe's own story: smoking was the conscious improvement project and barely moved for decades. Hacky sack was just fun, not framed as improvement at all, and accomplished a developmental transformation (addressing sensory processing difficulty) that Joe did not even know he needed.

"My nature, my authenticity, improved me in ways that I didn't even know were happening." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

See [[joes-hacky-sack]] for the full story.

The pattern Joe sees in clients: if they are following their intuition, they pick the right next move every time. The intuition has access to information the conscious improvement project does not.

"I will watch people, and if they're just following their intuition, they will just pick the next thing. And it'll be like, oh my God, they picked it perfectly again." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

## The Holiday Effect

Most adults have witnessed this directly. Take two weeks off. Come back. Performance is better, smoother. Better calls get made. Because you stopped actively trying to improve for two weeks. The improvement project was friction. Removing the friction produced the improvement the project was trying to manufacture.

See [[dont-improve-for-two-weeks]] for the explicit experiment.

## The Tree Without a Goal

A second framing Joe uses when working with a person who is afraid that without a scorecard or goal, evolution will stop:

"A tree doesn't have a goal, but it keeps on evolving. The idea that you could stop evolving is — the only way you could stop evolving is by dying." -- Joe Hudson, What's Your Scorecard?

The image specifically rebuts the assumption that movement requires intention. The tree does not intend its growth. It is not aimed at a destination. It does not check its progress against a target. It grows because growth is what trees do. The growth happens regardless of whether the tree has a goal.

The implication for humans: evolution does not depend on having a goal. The body, the mind, the capacity to love, the capacity to perceive — all of these keep developing whether or not the person is consciously trying to develop them. Joe sometimes pairs the tree image with the [[compass-not-map]] distinction: the compass points without specifying the destination, the tree grows without specifying the height. Neither requires a goal to produce direction.

This framing is especially useful when the person has a scorecard-organized identity and is worried that releasing the scorecard will produce stagnation. The fear is "if I stop pushing, I'll stop growing." The tree-without-a-goal teaching makes visible that growing was never what the pushing was producing in the first place.

## Striving and Evolution Are Not Correlated

A specific corollary, often delivered in the same session as the tree teaching:

"You did the most evolution in your life from 0 to 8 years old, and you weren't consciously trying to get someplace. You probably did your least evolving in [your career] between 28 and 38 years old, when you were striving the whole time. They're not correlated. They're sometimes correlated, sometimes not." -- Joe Hudson, What's Your Scorecard?

The point: the period of most development in a person's life is the period before they had a scorecard. The period of least development is often the period of most striving. The correlation between effort and growth is not what the person has assumed.

This rebuts a specific defense of the scorecard. The defense: "the scorecard is what produces my growth, so if I drop it, I'll stop growing." The counter-evidence: the most growth in the person's life happened with no scorecard. The least growth often happened with the heaviest scorecard. The scorecard was never producing the growth; it was producing the experience of "I'm doing something to grow."

The teaching does not say striving is useless. It says striving and growth are not the same axis. They can co-occur or not. Releasing the striving does not release the growth.

For the K-session application: K was afraid that if he made self-trust his scorecard instead of money/likes, he would stop evolving. The tree teaching and the 0-to-8 framing addressed the fear directly. The most evolution was already not correlated with the scorecard he had. The fear of stagnation without the scorecard was unfounded.

## The Direction of the Movement

The shape of natural human improvement, when allowed:
- Toward deeper compassion.
- Toward greater capacity.
- Toward the recognition that love and empowerment converge.

"As it turns out, most humans, when they understand themselves more and more, there's a deeper and deeper compassion that shows up. There's a deeper, deeper amount of empowerment that shows up. The things that you think are opposite, such as love and being empowered, they turn out to be the same thing. At the pinnacle of loving is empowerment. At the pinnacle of empowerment is loving." -- Joe Hudson, Authenticity over Improvement

The directionality is not arbitrary. Authentic growth produces these specific qualities. This is one of the reasons inherent goodness is not a hopeful belief but an observation: when you let people grow without forcing them, they grow toward goodness.

## What This Principle Does NOT Mean

- It does not mean effort is bad. Effort applied to the wanted thing is part of how that thing happens. Effort applied to forcing oneself to want something different is the friction.
- It does not mean self-development is pointless. It means self-development works when it is recognition rather than improvement. See [[undoing-not-doing]].
- It does not mean passivity. The oak tree is not passive. It actively grows. The point is that the activity does not require management.
- It does not mean any current state is the final state. Growth keeps happening. The principle is about the mechanism, not the outcome.

## When Someone Experiences This

The felt sense: relief, often surprised. The improvement project was heavy. Discovering it was not necessary lightens what was being carried.

The first instinct: panic. If I don't push myself, won't I stagnate? See [[ill-become-lazy-without-shoulds]] and [[im-afraid-of-stagnating]].

The second instinct, after testing it: amazement. The growth happens anyway. Often faster. The person realizes they have been working against themselves.
