# Of Course You Had to Be Numb

## The Principle

When a person is coming back to life from a long-held numbness, the integrating frame is not "you've been numb and now you're not" — it is "of course you had to be numb." The numbness was the body's wisdom. It was the adaptation that worked. Honoring the adaptation at the moment it is releasing is what makes the release portable.

The phrase carries three things simultaneously:

1. **Of course** — the recognition that the numbness was necessary. Not a defect to be ashamed of; the right strategy for the original conditions.
2. **You had to** — the temporal precision. The pressure was on the past-self, not the current-self. The current-self can be different because the conditions have changed.
3. **Be numb** — the precise name for what was done. Not avoidance, not weakness, not failure to feel. Numbness: the active strategy of damping down the aliveness so the system could survive.

"Yeah, that's right. And of course you had to be numb." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

"So you're coming back to life from a numbness that was required." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

## Why This Move Matters

When the numbness is releasing, the body has been doing something for decades that was protecting the system. The release is real but it is also disorienting. The body had a working strategy, and now the strategy is dissolving. Without the integrating frame, the release can feel like loss: "what was I doing all these years? what's wrong with me that I have to undo all this?" The shame about having been numb defeats the work.

The "of course you had to be numb" frame inserts the right interpretation at the exact moment the body is updating. The numbness was not pathology. It was adaptation. The adult body that is now releasing the numbness is not correcting a defect — it is updating from a strategy that fit the original conditions to a strategy that fits the present conditions.

This is the in-the-moment version of [[honor-the-patterns-intent]]. The pattern (numbness) had an intent (protect the child from material the small body could not hold). The intent was wise. The pattern served. The releasing of the pattern can happen without rejecting the past-self who built it.

The framing matters most at the threshold moment. The body discovers the aliveness underneath the numbness, and the next thing the body wants to know is "why didn't I have this before? what was wrong with me?" The "of course" arrives before the shame-question can form. The body receives the answer before it can finish asking the question.

## The Mechanism of the Original Adaptation

The numbness in the source session had a specific origin: the small child could not hold the rage that the trauma produced. The child's body did the only available thing — it left. Dissociated. Went numb. The energy that would have shaken a small body apart was prevented from moving by going offline.

"At the bottom of it is deep, deep rage that the little me that endured tremendous trauma couldn't hold in her body, so she left." -- client, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

The leaving was the wisdom. A small body cannot hold an adult-sized rage. The energy has to go somewhere. The body's option was: damp the entire system so the energy that cannot be held also cannot move. The numbness was the technology that allowed survival of an event the body had no other tools for.

This is not metaphor. The somatic mechanism is real. The body that held the trauma still carries the numbness as its default state. The adult who reports being unable to feel was not failing to develop the capacity; the capacity was actively suppressed because the original conditions made suppression survival.

The "of course" honors this. It does not say "you are now allowed to feel." It says "your previous strategy of not-feeling was correct for the conditions you were in, and the conditions are now different."

## The Two-Beat Structure: Honor, Then Acknowledge the Update

The integrating move has two beats in sequence:

**Beat 1: "Of course you had to be numb."** Honor the original adaptation. The past-self did the right thing. The numbness was not failure.

**Beat 2: "You're coming back to life from a numbness that was required."** Name the current update. The past-self's strategy is being released because the present-self can hold what the past-self could not.

The two beats are not redundant. Beat 1 prevents shame about having been numb. Beat 2 establishes the current direction. Without beat 2, the honoring becomes endorsement of the numbness ("yes, stay numb"). Without beat 1, the update becomes correction ("stop being numb"). Both together make the update an evolution rather than a correction.

In the source session, the beats arrive in the order above and produce immediate integration. The client says "of course, of course, of course" with each beat — the body recognizing what it has been doing and why, in real time.

## When to Use This

The move is most effective at specific moments:

- **When the numbness is releasing.** This is the primary use-case. The body is updating; the integrating frame goes in here.

- **When the client surfaces shame about having been numb.** "I've been like this for so long, what's wrong with me." The "of course" preempts the shame by reinterpreting the duration.

- **When the work has produced a felt-recognition that something underneath the numbness is alive.** The aliveness is unfamiliar; the framing legitimizes the strangeness as "coming back to life."

- **When the original trauma is named and the client is connecting their adult patterns to it.** The "of course" links the past-cause to the past-strategy without making either pathological.

- **When the client says any version of "I should have done this work sooner."** The numbness was not failure to do the work. It was the work. The release is what the work has been making possible.

It is less effective:

- **Before the release has begun.** The honoring of the numbness, delivered too early, can confirm the numbness as identity rather than as transitional strategy. The frame works once the strategy is already coming apart.

- **As a way to reassure a client who is not actually releasing.** "Of course you had to be numb" said to someone who is still fully numb does not help them release. It is integration framing, not initiation framing.

## The Universal Form

The principle generalizes beyond numbness. The structure works for any adaptive pattern that is releasing:

- "Of course you had to compose yourself." (See [[composing-yourself-as-self-suppression]].)
- "Of course you had to caretake everyone." (See [[i-caretake-people]].)
- "Of course you had to overachieve." (See [[overachieving-is-survival-mode]].)
- "Of course you had to lock down your sensitivity." (See [[locked-down-sensitivity-is-the-gift]].)
- "Of course you had to become the fearless one." (See [[im-not-an-anxious-person]].)

In every case the structure is the same: name the pattern, affirm its wisdom for the original conditions, frame the current update as evolution rather than correction. The "of course" carries all three.

This is the same move-shape as [[say-of-course-i-just-want-to-be-seen-here]]'s self-acknowledgment version. The "of course" phrase is a coaching unit in Joe's toolkit; this principle is one specific application of that broader move.

## The Body's Confirmation

In the source session, the client's body confirms the framing the moment it lands.

- **Joe:** "Yeah. So you're coming back to life from a numbness that was required."
- **Client:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "And there you are again."
- **Client:** "I feel it in my fire."
- *(Source: 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage)*

The "I feel it in my fire" is the body's own articulation of what is now available. The aliveness that was numbed is back. The fire is there. The frame "you're coming back to life from a numbness that was required" gave the body permission to claim the aliveness without simultaneously having to reject the numbness that had preceded it. Both are honored. The transition is clean.

## What This Principle Is NOT

It is not permission to stay numb. The frame is for the moment of release. It honors the past adaptation as part of the release. It does not endorse continuing the adaptation when the conditions have changed.

It is not a substitute for the body's work. The numbness releases through the body's actual experience of moving the held material (rage, fear, grief). The "of course" is the framing that integrates the work; it is not the work itself.

It is not dismissal of the original cost. The numbness was required AND it cost the person decades of dimmed aliveness. Both are true. The framing does not minimize the cost; it just refuses to add the additional cost of shame about having paid the first cost.

## Tone

Warm and certain. The "of course" is not hesitant. It is delivered as obvious recognition, not as comforting reassurance. The body needs the witness to confirm the wisdom of what was done, not to soothe the person about it.

In the source session, the "yeah, that's right" before the "of course you had to be numb" is the tonal anchor. The witness is in the same body-truth as the client. The framing comes from the same place the body just spoke from. The integration is therefore felt as recognition rather than as instruction.
