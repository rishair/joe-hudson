# Don't Meet Aggression With Aggression

## The Principle

When you are on the receiving end of aggression — whether direct or passive — the move that locks the dynamic and produces more of it is to meet aggression with aggression. This includes the subtle versions you might not recognize as aggression: trying to change their behavior, walking on eggshells, trying to convince them they are wrong about how they are being.

Each of these is a move to control their state. Each is therefore itself aggression, just dressed up in a different costume. The dynamic does not dissolve. It tightens.

"Don't meet aggression with aggression. Typically, occasionally you need to, but typically meeting aggression with aggression leads to a power dynamic. So, leads to more fear triangle. And one, the most common form of aggression that you would have is that I want to change you. So trying to change the person's passive aggression is, is you're like, it's, you're entering the dynamic with them either by I'm gonna be angry at you to change you, or I'm gonna walk on eggshells to change you, or I'm gonna try to convince you to change you. Like any anything like that. That's really the dynamic." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## The Three Hidden Forms of Aggression-Back

The forms most people do not recognize as aggression because they look like the opposite:

**1. Trying to change them.** Any move whose purpose is to produce a different behavior in them is a move of control. Wearing the costume of self-help language ("I just want you to grow") does not change what it is. See [[dont-try-to-change-your-partner]] and [[dont-weaponize-the-make-it-active-move]].

**2. Walking on eggshells to change them.** The fear-based version. You adjust your behavior to prevent their reaction. The hope is that if you tiptoe well enough, they will eventually be different. This is a control move executed in the body. They feel it. The dynamic does not change; it gets quieter and more held.

**3. Trying to convince them.** The intellectual version. You argue, present evidence, explain why they should see it differently. The energy is aggressive even when the words are reasonable. The other person feels it.

All three share the structure: I am operating on you to produce a state in you. The structure is what makes it aggression, not the volume or the words.

## Why It Lands as More Fear-Triangle

The fear triangle (see [[fear-triangle]]) is structurally about trying to control through power-over. When you respond to aggression by trying to change them, you have entered the triangle yourself. Now both of you are in it. The roles flip and rotate. The dynamic intensifies.

"Trying to change the person's passive aggression is you entering the dynamic with them" — the entering is the problem, not the specific role. Once both parties are inside the triangle, neither can leave it without the other leaving, and neither wants to leave while the other is still in.

## What To Do Instead: Own Your Own Experience

The replacement is not the absence of action. It is the shift from operating-on-them to owning-your-own-experience. You can:

1. **Say ouch.** Report what landed without trying to change them. See [[say-ouch]].
2. **Draw a boundary.** Not about their behavior, about yours. "When I am being guilt-tripped, I am going to walk away." Boundaries about what you do, not demands about what they do.
3. **Name what is happening.** "That feels like a guilt-trip." A neutral naming, not an accusation requiring a response.
4. **Be in VIEW.** Bring vulnerability, impartiality, empathy, wonder to the moment. See [[view]]. The state itself shifts the dynamic.

The thread through all of these: you are reporting on yourself or owning your own action, not operating on them.

"You can own your own experience. So one of the ways to do that is, ouch, that hurt. One of the ways to do that is to draw a boundary. You're like, oh, I'm not really interested in being guilt-tripped. One of the ways to do that is just own what's happening. Oh, hey, that feels like you're guilt-tripping me. What would make you guilt-trip me right now? Be in view. Any of those things." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

## The Crucial Caveat: Empowerment

Any of these moves can themselves be made aggressive if delivered from a place of trying to change them. "Ouch" said as a complaint to make them stop is aggression. A "boundary" said to punish or control is aggression. Naming "that feels like a guilt-trip" said as a verdict is aggression.

The thing that makes the moves work is not the script. It is the empowerment behind them. The person speaking is making a choice they own. They are not trying to make the other person do anything. They are doing the thing that is true for them and accepting whatever consequence follows.

"It's really easy to make any of those things passive-aggressive. So the only way to do it is to really be in touch with your own empowerment, which is, I think, the most critical piece, which is you are making a choice to avoid a certain consequence and to own that choice. Like, you don't get to control the consequences, but you get to control the choices that you make." -- Joe Hudson, Passive Aggression

The diagnostic: am I doing this to produce a change in them, or because it is the true move for me? If the former, the move is aggression with a new wrapper. If the latter, the move is empowerment, and the dynamic begins to dissolve because one party is no longer participating in it.

## The Occasional Exception

Joe acknowledges that occasionally aggression-back is what is needed. The exception is real but is not the rule. The default is to not enter the dynamic. The exception is for cases where the other person is escalating in a way that requires a clear, direct, undefended firmness — which is itself not the same as the controlling-aggression-back this principle names. (See [[aggression-amplifies-against-conflict-avoidance]] for the related but different mechanism around containment.)

The exception is rare enough that pointing to it is not a useful instruction for most situations. The principle holds: by default, do not meet aggression with aggression.

## In Coaching

When a person reports they have been responding to chronic aggression by trying to fix the other person, this principle is the entry point. The move is to name the trying-to-change as itself aggression, even though it does not feel like aggression to the person doing it.

When a person reports they have drawn many boundaries and the dynamic has not improved, check whether the boundaries are actually about their own behavior or are disguised demands for the other person to change. Many "boundaries" people set are actually the third hidden form of aggression-back.

When a person reports they are exhausted from walking on eggshells, this principle names the cost of the second hidden form. The eggshells are not non-engagement; they are continuous control-effort directed at the other person's state.

## Boundary Conditions

The principle does not apply to physical safety. In situations of actual physical threat, removing yourself or defending yourself is not entering the dynamic — it is responding to a threat. The principle is about emotional and relational aggression, not about physical violence.

The principle also does not say you must accept aggression. The opposite. The principle is about how to refuse to accept it without using aggression to do so. The refusal can be total. The refusal can include leaving the relationship. The refusal just cannot run on the same engine as the thing being refused.

## Tone

Joe is direct about this principle. The matter-of-factness is part of the teaching: the temptation to fix the other person is strong, and a soft framing would not interrupt it. The "don't" is not punitive. It is structural — the thing does not work for mechanical reasons, regardless of how appealing it feels.
