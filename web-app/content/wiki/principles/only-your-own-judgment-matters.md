# The Only Judgment That Matters Is Your Own

## Definition

The judgment you are looking for from outside -- the one that will tell you whether you are good enough, qualified enough, real enough, not an imposter -- does not actually arrive from outside. The only judgment that ends up mattering is your own judgment of how you lived your life.

This is not a moral claim about self-trust. It is closer to a structural observation about whose voice is in the room at the end.

## The Story Joe Uses

A friend of Joe's was thrown out of a van and had a near-death experience. He saw the light at the end of the tunnel, walked through it, and saw two figures coming toward him. He thought: "Oh, I'm going to be judged for my life now. These two people are going to come and judge me, and I'm going to see if I'm good enough." When the two figures arrived, one of them looked at him and asked, "Did you do good enough?"

The judgment was not delivered. The judgment was requested. The judging entity was him.

"When they arrived, these two people, they arrived, one of them looked at him and said: Did you do good enough? And it was only his own judgment that mattered." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

## In Practice

The principle dissolves the imposter-syndrome search for external validation. The person is looking for someone to tell them they are qualified, that they belong, that they are not faking it. The principle says: that voice you are waiting for is your own. There is no outside committee that will eventually rule on your competence.

The corollary: how you feel you have lived is the relevant evaluation. Not what you accomplished. Not what other people thought. Not whether the metrics were hit. The internal experience of having lived your life is the verdict.

"When you realize we're all imposters and that all that really matters is how we feel like we've lived our lives, in that moment, imposter syndrome just can't matter at all." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome

## When Someone Experiences This

When you find yourself reaching for someone-who-knows to tell you that you are okay, that is the moment this principle applies. The reach is not wrong. It is just looking in the wrong direction. The validation is not going to arrive from there because that is not where it lives.

Connects to [[whats-the-scorecard-that-cant-be-taken-away]] -- the internal scorecard that cannot be revoked. The external scorecards (status, money, approval) keep moving and can be lost. The scorecard that ends up running the verdict is the one inside.

## Upstream (What Leads Here)

- When someone with [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] is looking for external proof that they are not an imposter, this principle reframes where the relevant judgment lives.
- When someone says "I just want someone to tell me I'm doing this right," this principle names who that someone has to be.
- When someone is collapsed into [[i-need-a-scorecard]] and reaching for any external metric to value themselves, this is the deeper move underneath the question [[what-if-there-was-no-scorecard]].

## Downstream (Where Joe Goes From Here)

- After this principle lands, Joe often moves to the body. The imposter feeling is fear; the fear is excitement without the breath. See [[fear-vs-excitement]] and the practice [[say-im-excited-ten-times]].
- This principle pairs with [[everyone-doing-something-real-is-an-imposter]] -- the two together fully dissolve the imposter frame. The first says no one is on the other side. The second says no one outside is going to judge you in.
- Connects to [[wants-over-shoulds]] and [[how-do-you-want-to-be]] -- if the only judgment that matters is your own, the orientation becomes how you want to live, not how you should live.

## Tone

The story carries the principle. Joe tells the near-death story plainly, almost casually, then lets the punchline ("did you do good enough?") land. The tone should not be elevated or mystical. The point is structural, not spiritual.

## Boundary Conditions

This principle is not a license to dismiss feedback or to disregard impact on others. It is an answer to the specific question: whose verdict on your life will arrive at the end? The principle does not say feedback doesn't matter. It says that the integrating voice that issues the final verdict is your own.

The principle is also not the same as "do whatever you want." See [[wants-over-shoulds]] for how following wants is different from doing whatever you want -- and how the want-following requires honesty about what you actually want, which only your own judgment can establish.
