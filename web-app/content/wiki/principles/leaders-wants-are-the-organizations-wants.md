# A Leader's Wants Are the Organization's Wants

## The Principle

A leader's individual wants and needs are not private. They are organizational data. The leader's resentment, discomfort, satisfaction, and desire are signals about what the organization itself wants and needs. Suppressing them suppresses information the organization requires to function.

The reframe inverts the usual model. The leader's wants are typically treated as personal preferences that must be subordinated to organizational requirements. The actual relationship is the reverse: the leader is positioned in the organization in a way that makes their internal state a sampling of the organization's state. Suppressing the sampling does not make the organization not want the thing. It just removes the leader's ability to see what the organization wants.

"I've come to learn that my wants and needs are the wants and needs of the organization. So I feel that it's my responsibility to express them, whereas before I pushed them down." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

## Why the Leader's Wants Are the Most Aligned

There is a structural reason the leader's wants merit the weight the principle gives them. The leader's position is the only one in the organization that sees the whole. Every other role sees a slice. The leader's wants are the only wants that arise from the felt sense of the whole. That does not make them objectively correct. It makes them the wants most aligned to what the organization needs.

"The interesting thing about being a leader of an organization is that your wants, your specific wants, not everybody else's, are the best aligned wants to the wants of the organization. It doesn't mean that everybody else's wants aren't important, but your wants are the most important wants because they are the only ones that see the entire organization." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The reframe is precise. It is not "the leader's wants are more important than the team's wants because the leader is more important." It is "the leader's wants are the most aligned to the organization's wants because the leader's position is the only one with the field of view that makes alignment to the whole possible." The principle is structural, not hierarchical.

## Why This Is True

The leader's position in the organization aggregates signals from across the system. The leader hears from many functions, sees patterns across teams, and absorbs the cumulative felt sense of how the organization is doing. The leader's body is processing organizational data whether the leader recognizes it or not.

A want that arises in the leader is often a want that has been arising across the organization. The leader is the first or most articulate place it surfaces because of position. Suppressing it as "just my preference" misreads the signal. The leader is the antenna.

This is also true downward. A team member's wants are often the team's wants. The leader's job is to surface these and to model the surfacing of their own.

"When my team is expressing their wants and needs, and sometimes I have to pull it out of them, I literally validate them by saying, hey, if you are feeling a certain way, it's because your team or your organization feels that way. And you owe it not only to yourself, but to them to say it out loud." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

## Wants Include Feelings, Not Just Preferences

The principle extends from explicit wants to the leader's internal state more broadly. The leader's fears, frustrations, and discomforts are also organizational data. Hiding them is the same failure as hiding wants: depriving the team of information they need to function.

"You see that all the time, particularly with executives, particularly with CEOs, is that they don't share with their team how they're feeling because they have this idea that they need to take care of everybody, that they're responsible for everybody. So they have to handle it all on their own. And so they're not saying to their team, hey, I'm really scared about X, Y, and Z, or the way you said that doesn't work for me, or the way this team is functioning is off for me, because they think that they have to fix it. And therefore their team can't trust them. Then their team is sitting there going, okay, when's the bad mood going to happen? When's the reaction going to happen?" -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The leader believes hiding fear protects the team. The team experiences the hidden fear as the leader's mysterious moods, the unpredictable reactions, the inexplicable strategic shifts. The team spends substantial energy trying to predict what is actually going on with the leader. The energy that should be going into the work is going into psychological speculation about leadership.

The structural failure: the team cannot trust a leader whose actual state is hidden. Trust requires being able to see what is going on. See [[trust-as-multifaceted]]. The hidden internal state of the leader is the same betrayal of trust as the hidden want, with the same downstream consequences.

## The Safety Failure for the Team

A different angle on the same dynamic: a team without visible leader-wants does not just lose trust. It loses orientation. The team members do not know whether they are doing a good job, whether they are aligned, whether they are even in the right direction. The leader's hidden wants leave the team in a continuous low-grade vertigo about whether the work is what is wanted.

"If you're leading an organization and people don't know what you want, then they feel things like, 'Oh my gosh, who's leading the organization?' Or they feel things like, 'I don't know if I'm doing a good job.' Or they don't know if they're in alignment or not in alignment. And so without cleanly saying what you want, it's very hard for anybody to feel like they know what they're doing or that they're doing the right thing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The unstated leader-want produces a felt-state in the team that the team then tries to manage with their own work. They overproduce, hedge their bets, build the wrong thing carefully, ask in roundabout ways. The energy that should go into the actual work goes into the meta-work of trying to figure out what the leader wanted. The leader's silence becomes a tax on the whole organization.

## The Mistaken Self-Reliance

The leader who hides their feelings often experiences it as responsibility. "I have to carry this so the team does not have to." The frame is heroic. The actual effect is the opposite of what the frame describes.

The team is already carrying it. They are reading the leader's state, calibrating their behavior to it, working around it. The "protection" does not remove the weight from the team; it just makes the weight invisible and unaddressable. The team carries the weight without the information that would let them respond to it.

When the leader names the state, the team can either help carry it consciously or stop carrying the misattribution. Either is better than the previous condition. Brett's articulation:

"This brings me back to some of my experience as a CEO where I would take things on from a self-reliance perspective of this is mine to carry, this is my fear, I don't need to let this trickle down through the team. And there's a way that that's not trusting them to handle the reality of a scary situation." -- Brett Kistler, Building a Functional Team

The reframe: the self-reliance is also a failure to trust the team. The leader is asserting that the team cannot handle the truth. That assertion is itself a betrayal of trust in the reverse direction. The leader's hiding signals "I don't trust you to handle this," and the team registers the distrust.

## The Cleanly-Said Want and the Loyalty It Builds

How the want is said is as important as that it is said. A want stated cleanly — owned, undefended, without the apologetic hedging or the aggressive "you'd better" pressure — is what produces the loyalty effect the principle predicts. A want stated with shame or with armor produces the opposite.

"The more hard you are in the way that you ask for what you want, the less likely somebody's gonna be able to give it to you or want to give it to you. Also, if you ask for it like, 'Oh my gosh, you're never gonna wanna do this for me,' even the hand motion of, 'Oh, you're never gonna wanna do this,' is a no. It's like, 'Don't give me what you want.' So asking for what you want is just a very clean process. They have the choice to say yes or no." -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The two failure modes both pre-load a refusal into the request. The hard-ask pre-loads the refusal because the team's compliance feels coerced; even when granted, it costs the relationship. The squished-ask pre-loads the refusal because the asker has already conceded that the want is unreasonable; the team's job becomes confirming the asker's prediction. The clean ask is the one where the wanting and the asking are not contaminated by either defense.

See [[asking-with-shame-invites-refusal]] for the deeper version of this teaching. The leader version adds that the loyalty produced by clean asking is large enough to do work the leader otherwise has to do through pressure. The executive Joe describes in the next section made this visible empirically.

## The Loyalty Example: Workers Who Stayed Without Pay

The empirical evidence Joe reaches for when teaching this principle: an executive whose clean asking built so much loyalty that her team worked without pay during a cash shortage. The example is not romantic; it is structural. The combination of cleanly stating her wants and genuinely supporting her people produced a level of team commitment that traditional management cannot purchase.

"One of the executives I was working with got really good at asking for what she wanted. Anytime she felt any discomfort, she said, 'Oh, this is what I want.' She was also exceptionally good at being supportive of other people. She was constantly making sure that the people that she had working with her had the support they needed to do a great job. And as her company was growing, they had a cash shortage. They didn't have enough money to pay everybody. And everybody stuck around and worked without pay for a little while. They had so much loyalty to this leader. So think about it like this: If people are willing to work without pay, how much do you really need to manage them?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Lead Without Managing

The diagnostic question Joe leaves at the end of the example is structurally important: if the loyalty produced by clean want-stating is large enough that people will work without pay, the management overhead the leader thought was necessary was an artifact of unclean wanting, not a feature of working with humans. The principle, fully applied, replaces a substantial portion of what most leaders think of as the work of leadership.

## The Reframe of Duty

The standard frame: stating personal wants at work is self-indulgent or unprofessional. The reframe: stating personal wants at work is a duty. The leader who hides their wants is failing the team.

This inverts the morality of want-suppression. Most leaders have been trained that suppressing wants is mature, responsible, and team-oriented. The actual effect of the suppression is the opposite: it deprives the team of information they need, generates politics where information should flow, and breeds resentment in the leader.

See [[sublimated-wants-become-politics]] for the mechanism by which suppressed wants surface sideways. The principle here is the moral inversion: stating wants is not selfishness; it is service.

## The Team-Wins-When-Leader-Wins Framing

Another angle on the same principle: the leader's wants getting met is the highest-probability route to the team's wants getting met. If the leader's wants get met, the team's wants are most likely to get met. If one team member's wants get met, the rest may or may not get met. The leader's position makes their wants the most aligned to the team's collective wants.

"As a leader, your wants are the wants that are most aligned with the company's wants. If your wants get met as the leader, then your team's wants get met or most likely to get met. If one person in your team's wants get met, then maybe your team's wants get met, but probably not. So there's something that happens where once you recognize that the whole team wants to win and that your wants getting met are what's going to allow the team to win, then there's this like a natural flow that happens that requires — that, that, that is less control, less force, less management, and like a natural alignment can happen if everybody also feels listened to." -- Joe Hudson, "I Block 3 hours a Month for Hard Conversations"

The framing has a specific consequence for how the leader engages the team. The leader is not in competition with the team's wants. The leader's wants are the team's wants' best representative. When the leader names them clearly and gets them met, the team's wants are met by the same motion. The naming-the-leader's-wants work is also the meeting-the-team's-wants work.

This is why suppression of the leader's wants is so costly. The leader who hides their wants is hiding the wants most aligned to what the team needs. The team then has to operate without the data that would let them succeed. The leader's "modesty" about their own wants is actually depriving the team of orientation.

## The Connection to Less-Management Through More-Wanting

A further consequence: when the leader's wants are clear and met, the natural alignment that emerges in the team reduces the need for management. The team is oriented around the actual want; they do not need to be controlled or forced or managed into the alignment because the alignment is already there.

This is one of the operational reasons that wants-clarity reduces management overhead. The leader who has done the wants-naming work has less management to do because the team is already aligned. The leader who has not has more management to do because the team is constantly trying to figure out what the leader actually wanted. The wants-clarity is upstream of the management load.

See [[i-want-to-delegate-better]] for the related concern this connects to. The leader who wants to delegate better often has not done the wants-naming work; once they do, the delegation problem partially dissolves because the criteria for what they want become available.

## The Bodily Diagnostic Implication

The principle extends to bodily discomfort. If something in the leader's system is resistant or in friction, that is information about a thing the organization needs to address. The body is not generating noise. The body is reading the field.

"How cool is it that your body shines a light on the very thing that you need to deal with? So if there's discomfort in your system, if you literally don't feel good about something, if there's resistance or friction in you, that's a thing we need to go deal with. Let's go unlock that." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, quoting an L12 colleague, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The leader's discomfort is the organization's diagnostic surfacing. Ignoring it is not stoicism. Ignoring it is closing the diagnostic.

See [[anxiety-as-unarticulated-need]] for the related principle that anxiety is information about an unspoken want or boundary.

## The Coaching Reframe

When a leader presents a want and asks whether to express it, the question reveals the suppression frame is still operating. The reframe is not "should you express this want?" but "what would it take to recognize that not expressing it is depriving your team?"

The shift is from permission-seeking ("am I allowed to want this?") to responsibility-taking ("the team needs to know what I want, and I have been withholding it").

## What This Is Not

This is not license for the leader to dump all internal states onto the team. The principle is that wants are organizational signals. The principle is not that every fleeting emotion is an organizational mandate. See [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] and the discipline of releasing emotions before processing them with others.

This is also not "the leader's preferences override democratic input." See [[wants-vs-partiality]]. Owning a want is not the same as imposing it. The expression of the want opens a conversation; it does not close one.

This is also not "leadership wants are objectively right." The principle is that the wants are signals worth surfacing. The signals are then interpreted, debated, and integrated with other signals. The leader who treats their own wants as final has misread the principle.

## The Cascading Permission

When the leader models naming wants as duty, the team learns it is permitted. The naming becomes contagious. The organization develops the capacity to surface what it actually wants rather than what people think they should want.

"That gives permission for those around you that you care about to articulate their wants and needs. And it's one of the best ways to drive a true meaningful relationship with someone, whether it's a work colleague or for a friend or family member." -- Carla Piñeyro Sublett, Finding Compassion in Self-Indulgence

The cascade is the principle scaling. The leader's naming makes the team's naming possible. The team's naming makes the organization's naming possible. The organization develops a vocabulary for wants where previously there was only the vocabulary of strategy and goals.

## When to Reach for This

- A leader is sitting on a want and treating it as personal preference.
- A team member is suppressing a want and the suppression is generating dysfunction.
- An organization has high politics and low directness.
- A leader is feeling resentment toward their team and the resentment is being held as a personal failing.
- A leader is in a coaching session asking whether to bring up something that bothers them.

## When Not to Reach for This

- When the want is actually a fleeting emotion that has not been processed. Process first; then check whether the want is still there.
- When the want is a craving rather than a want. See [[wanting-vs-craving]]. Cravings dressed as organizational signals create dysfunction.
- When the suppression has a structural basis the leader is trying to override. If the role does not have the authority to enact the want, the move may be to surface the structural issue rather than to enact the want unilaterally.

## Tone

Direct and serious. The reframe is a significant inversion of how most leaders have been trained. Delivered lightly, the reframe lands as a tip. Delivered with weight, the reframe lands as the structural shift it is.
