# Everything I Say They Contradict

## What Joe Notices

The signature read is content-independent friction-seeking. Whatever the other person says, the contradicting person finds something to argue with. The content keeps shifting; the underneath-impulse to contradict does not.

The behavioral markers:

- The partner (or kid) is responding to every statement with a contradiction or critique. "Why did you do it that way?" "That doesn't seem like —" "Why would you ever do that?"
- The contradicting is unlike their usual behavior. The person who is doing it is not normally this way. The pattern is anomalous.
- An offered kindness gets reframed as something objectionable. The hug becomes "you're trying to control me." The compliment becomes evidence of an agenda.
- Anything you say is heard as criticism, regardless of how it was offered.
- The fight-seeker emits a felt state of being on a hair-trigger — there is no comfortable place to put the conversation.
- The receiver of the contradicting has the felt sense of "there is no way out of this fight" or "here it comes."

"I just noticed everything I said she contradicted. And it was like more subtle obviously than like an 8-year-old, but it was like everything I said, I was like, 'Oh yeah, this thing.' 'Well, why did you do it that way? That doesn't seem like—' And then I said, 'Why did you do it?' I was just like, one criticism after another, which is very unlike Tara." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The shared signature across kid-version and adult-version: the contradicting itself is the function, not the content of any particular contradiction.

## The Confirming Test

The clearest way to confirm the read is to contradict yourself, change position, or agree with the person — and watch what happens. If they contradict you anyway, the contradicting is not about content.

"I even tested it. I was like, 'Oh, I'm gonna contradict myself and I'm gonna agree with her.' And I did, I agreed with her. And then she was like, 'No, why would you ever do that?'" -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The test is diagnostic: when the person contradicts both your position and its opposite, what they want is the friction, not the resolution. The reach is for the discharge, not the agreement. See [[the-contradict-myself-test]] for the test as a deliberate move.

## What This Implies

What Joe understands from this read: the person is in a state of [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]] specifically of the "looking for a safe place to discharge" variety. Something got loaded earlier — a hard day, an interaction at work, an accumulated charge that did not get released — and the body is now seeking the discharge through the safest available container.

The relationship is the safe container. The partner has consistently been there the next day. The love is reliable. The body knows the relationship can absorb friction. So the body reaches for the friction in the place that has been built to survive it.

This is the same machinery as the kid melting down after school. The kid held it together at school. Home is safe. The discharge happens at home. See [[kids-release-emotions-where-they-feel-safe]] for the parallel principle and [[looking-for-a-fight-means-self-disconnection]] for the principle as it operates in adults.

## What Joe Does

There are three available moves depending on the relationship and the readiness:

**Move 1: Name the structure to yourself.** If you are the one being contradicted, the read changes how you experience the interaction. You stop trying to win the contradicting. You recognize the discharge-seeking. You can then choose to either offer the container or step out of the discharge surface role. The recognition itself often reduces the urgency you would otherwise feel to defend.

**Move 2: Offer the explicit alternative.** "It looks like we want to get in a fight. Let's just go yell and then we'll come back." See [[go-to-separate-rooms-and-yell]] for the full move. This works when the relationship has enough capacity for one partner to name the dynamic without making the other defensive about it.

**Move 3: Name it back to the person if they can hold it.** "I think you might be looking for a fight. Did something happen today?" This depends entirely on whether the relationship has the foundation for this kind of naming. Many relationships do not, and the naming itself becomes the new content to fight about. Joe's preferred move in many cases is the third-room option (move 2) rather than the diagnostic naming.

## When the Read Is About You, Not Your Partner

The same read is available in reverse. The signal is that you are the one contradicting everything.

"I came home and I had a shitty day and I was like, I'm contradicting everything Tara says. I'm like, I'm looking for that fight." -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The body-cue for self-recognition is specific: the wound-up state, the friction-seeking quality of attention, the felt sense that you would find something to contradict about regardless of what was said. Once you have noticed it once, the cue becomes available.

The self-recognition is the opening for the principle [[looking-for-a-fight-means-self-disconnection]]. The internal question becomes "what do I really need?" — see [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]] — rather than continuing to discharge through the partner.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe (narrating his daughter):** "She came home from school and she was just ready for a fight. It was like whatever I said, she was hearing as criticism. Whatever I would do, I'm like, 'here, give me a hug.' 'Ah, you're trying to control me.'"
- **Joe (then on the move):** "We had realized like, oh, they just need this emotional release. So we'd say okay. And then we would draw some boundary. No, I'm not gonna stop telling you that we gotta do the dishes. And then I have this big meltdown. They yell, scream. And then they would be back in connection and they'd be like, 'Oh, actually what happened at school today, I got made fun of.'"
- *(Source: How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal)*

The kid-version is the clearest illustration because the discharge is visible. The adult-version is the same shape with more sophisticated wrapping.

## What Not to Do With This Read

Do not use this read to dismiss legitimate complaints from your partner. The read is not "every time my partner is upset, they are just discharging." Some fights are about what they are about. The read applies to the specific signature where the contradicting is content-independent — where the discharge is preceding any specific material, where the reaching is for friction itself rather than for any particular resolution.

If you suspect the read but cannot tell, the contradict-myself test is the disambiguation. If they contradict your reversed position, the read applies. If they engage with the reversed position substantively, the fight is about content and this read does not apply.

The other anti-pattern: using the read as a way to feel superior. "I see what you're doing — you're just discharging." This converts the read into an attack and creates the very fight it was meant to prevent. The read is for the reader, not for the diagnosed. See [[dont-jump-to-compassion-before-anger]] for the analogous trap with a different read.

## Tone

When Joe describes the read, the tone is warm recognition rather than diagnostic detachment. He names himself doing it. He names his wife doing it. The tone is "this is what bodies do, and once you see it, you have options." Not "this is what dysfunctional couples do." The pattern is universal enough that recognizing it is a relief, not an indictment.
