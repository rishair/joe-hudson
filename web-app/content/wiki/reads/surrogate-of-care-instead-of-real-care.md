# Surrogate of Care Instead of Real Care

## What Joe Notices

The signature: the person describes being constantly helped by someone in their life and frames themselves as the put-upon victim. They feel smug or self-righteously irritated about how much the helper is "always trying to fix everything." They have not asked the helper to stop. They have not asked for what they actually want. They are complaining about the help while continuing to accept it.

The structural picture: the receiver is in [[the-helper-trap]] just as much as the helper is, but from the other side. The helper is dissipating their fear by helping; the receiver is accepting a surrogate of care because they are unwilling to ask for the real thing.

"If you're on the other side of that and you're listening to this video and you're like, oh yeah, that person resent, da da da da da, like, why does he always try to do that? And you're feeling all smug about yourself help, you're absolutely part of the pattern, just to be clear." -- Joe Hudson, When You Help Others, You're Asking Them to Hold Your Fear

The "feeling smug" is the giveaway. The receiver who has cleanly disengaged from the helper's pattern is not smug; they are clear. The smugness is the residue of being on the receiving end of something they have not refused but also have not received cleanly.

## What This Implies

The mechanism: the receiver gets something out of accepting the surrogate. Specifically, they get the *proximity to care* without having to do the work of asking for the actual care they want. The helper's stepping-in feels close to care even when it does not deliver care. The receiver settles for the proximity because asking for what they actually want feels riskier.

"What you're doing is you're getting a surrogate of care with this helping. Instead of asking for the care that you really want — hey, this is what I really want from you — instead of saying like, hey, I'm not going to hold your fear, I'm just going to be able to get the job done if you can treat me this way, and this is the care that I need. Instead, you're sacrificing what your truth is for some close proximity to care." -- Joe Hudson, When You Help Others, You're Asking Them to Hold Your Fear

The phrase "sacrificing what your truth is for some close proximity to care" is the precise read. The receiver has a truth (what they actually want from this person). They are not speaking it. In its place they are accepting what the helper offers, which is close to but not equal to the truth. The sacrifice produces a particular kind of low-grade dissatisfaction — a sense of being loved but not seen, helped but not met.

The receiver's complicity is not pathology. It is a learned strategy, often paired with the helper's strategy in the same relational system. Where the helper learned to manage others to stay safe, the receiver often learned not to ask, because asking produced consequences in their original family. Both strategies are childhood-installed safety moves running in the adult body.

## What Joe Does With This Read

The move is to gently make the receiver's complicity visible without dismantling the legitimate complaint about the helper. Both can be true: the helper's pattern is doing real damage AND the receiver is also part of the pattern. The "absolutely part of the pattern, just to be clear" line is the model — direct, unsoftened, no out-clause.

The follow-up move is to surface what the receiver actually wants. Not the help they have been getting. The care they have not been asking for. The question often opens a specific dimension the receiver has not articulated to themselves: what would actual care from this person look like? What have I been settling for that is close to it but not it?

"Hey, this is what I really want from you" is the operational form of the move from the receiver's side. The receiver becomes responsible for naming what they want rather than for resenting what they are getting.

See [[ask-what-they-want-before-responding]] for the helper-side mirror of this move and [[the-helper-repair-conversation]] for the full repair from the helper's end.

## The Specific Receiver-Side Script

A specific operational form Joe gives the receiver to use with the helper:

"Hey, I'm not going to hold your fear, I'm just going to be able to get the job done if you can treat me this way, and this is the care that I need." -- Joe Hudson, When You Help Others, You're Asking Them to Hold Your Fear

The structure of the line:
- **"I'm not going to hold your fear"** — explicit refusal to accept the fear-transfer that the help has been delivering. Names what the help has actually been functioning as.
- **"I'm just going to be able to get the job done"** — affirmation of the receiver's capacity. Returns the agency to where it belongs.
- **"If you can treat me this way"** — names the change the receiver wants from the helper.
- **"This is the care that I need"** — names what the receiver actually wants instead of the surrogate.

The line is a complete repair from the receiver's side. It can be delivered without the helper having yet recognized the pattern. The receiver does not need the helper to lead.

## Why the Smugness Is the Diagnostic

The smugness is the receiver's defense against seeing their own part. As long as the helper is "the problem," the receiver does not have to confront their own unwillingness to ask. The smugness preserves the position.

The receiver who has actually moved out of the pattern is not smug. They have asked for what they want, named the dynamic with the helper, and either gotten what they asked for or moved on. The cleanness of having spoken removes the residue of complicity that produces smugness.

A useful self-test: if you find yourself describing the helping person in your life with a tone of superior irritation, you have not yet done your part of the work. The tone is the signal that you are still in the pattern, just from the side that gets to feel morally clean about it.

## Dialogue Example

A compressed exchange illustrating the read in practice (constructed to fit Joe's stated framing, not from a specific coaching session):

- **Receiver:** My partner is exhausting. He's always jumping in to help with things I have under control. He'll start cleaning the kitchen while I'm in the middle of cleaning it. I just want him to back off.
- **Joe:** Have you told him what you actually want from him?
- **Receiver:** I've told him a million times to stop helping.
- **Joe:** That's not what you want. That's what you want him to stop doing. What do you actually want from him?
- **Receiver:** ...I want him to be with me. Not to be busy near me.
- **Joe:** Okay. Have you told him that?
- **Receiver:** No.
- **Joe:** Yeah. So while you're complaining about his help, you're not asking for what you want. You're getting his version of care, which isn't what you want, and you're refusing to ask for the version you do want. You're not just the victim here.

The move does not let either party off the hook. The helper's pattern is real. The receiver's failure to ask is also real. Both have work to do.

## What This Read Is Not

The read is not a way to deflect from the helper's responsibility. The helper still needs to do [[the-helper-repair-conversation]]. The principle that uninvited help erodes trust still applies. See [[uninvited-help-erodes-trust]].

What the read adds is the symmetry. The full pattern has two players. Coaching the receiver as a pure victim leaves them without agency over the situation. The read returns the agency: there is something you can do, and it is not to make the helper change. It is to name what you actually want.

The read also distinguishes between two presentations on the receiver side:
- **The receiver who has not yet asked.** The work is to name and ask. The read applies.
- **The receiver who has clearly and repeatedly asked and is not being heard.** This is a different situation. The helper is non-receptive. The work is closer to [[treat-them-like-an-adult]] applied to the helper, or in extreme cases to the question of whether the relationship can sustain the pattern.

The distinguishing question: have you actually said, cleanly and without performance, what you want and what you do not want? If yes, this is a different problem. If no, this read applies.

## Tone

Joe delivers this read with the same directness as the helper-side principle. Both sides get the same treatment. The refusal to give the receiver a free pass is part of the read's power. The receiver who is treated as a pure victim cannot exit. The receiver who is treated as a participant has a way out.

The tone is not harsh; it is symmetrical. If the helper is being asked to see their part, the receiver is being asked to see theirs. The fairness is the medicine.
