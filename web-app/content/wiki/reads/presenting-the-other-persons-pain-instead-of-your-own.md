# Presenting the Other Person's Pain Instead of Your Own

## What Joe Notices

The person frames their request around what they are doing to someone else, not around what they are experiencing themselves. "I'm driving my boyfriend crazy." "I keep hurting my partner." "My mom is so upset with me." "He is in so much pain because of what I'm doing."

What Joe notices is not the content of the concern but the *shape* of the framing. The speaker is the source of the action; the other person is the recipient of the experience. The speaker's own felt experience — their own pain, their own want, their own suffering — has been moved offstage.

Specific signals:

- The sentence subject is the speaker, but the predicate is the other person's experience. "I'm driving him crazy." "I'm causing her pain." The grammar makes the speaker the cause and the other person the experiencer.
- When asked to describe the problem, the description is heavy on the other person's emotional state and light on the speaker's. The partner's anger, hurt, depression, frustration is detailed; the speaker's interior is sparse.
- The request, when made explicit, is a request to stop doing something to the other person, not to feel something the speaker has not yet felt. "I want to stop driving him crazy" rather than "I want to stop the pain I'm in."
- The speaker has a moral charge against themselves about the impact on the other person. Shame about being the source of the partner's pain shows up early. The shame is doing some of the work that the request is doing — it keeps the speaker's attention on the partner's experience as the unit of suffering.
- When the speaker does name their own pain, it tends to come second, smaller, and in service of explaining why they are the source of the partner's pain. The own-pain is framed as a contributing factor, not as the unit of work.

This read is closely related to [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] — the speaker is not in themselves. It is also a particular flavor of [[i-caretake-people]] showing up in the very act of asking for help. The caretaking is so total that even the request for help is structured around the other person's experience.

## What This Implies

The framing IS the dynamic. The speaker is not just describing a caretaking relationship. They are *enacting* the caretaking relationship in real time, with the coach now in the configuration. They are bringing the partner's pain into the coaching as if it were the speaker's pain. The coach is being invited to help the speaker do a better job of taking care of the partner.

The structural pattern: the speaker has been outsourcing the experience of their own pain to someone else's reaction for so long that they no longer track their own pain except via its effects on others. When asked what is wrong, they describe what is wrong for someone else. When asked what hurts, they describe what is hurting in someone else. The speaker's interior has become unreadable to the speaker; it can only be located by watching the partner.

This is also a relational read on the coaching itself. The speaker is enrolling the coach in a particular role: help me be a better manager of this person's pain. If the coach takes that role, the configuration is reinforced. The coaching collaborates with the speaker's absence from themselves. The work goes nowhere because the unit of suffering is, by structure, the partner's experience — which neither the speaker nor the coach has any direct lever on.

The deeper read: the speaker is using the partner's pain as their own pain by proxy. The speaker has organized their life around making sure the partner is okay because the speaker's own okay-ness was, at some early developmental moment, made contingent on the caregiver's okay-ness. See [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]]. The childhood-installed equation — *my well-being is downstream of their state* — is still running. The presenting framing of the concern is the live demonstration of the equation.

## What Joe Does

The move is to name the framing directly and ask what made the speaker frame it that way. The naming is not gentle correction. It is a direct surfacing of the diagnostic in the first turn of the session.

In the source session, after the speaker described her chronic indecision and the partner's pain caused by it, Joe stopped and named it:

"What's interesting to me is that you asked me the question, not like, hey, I'm in indecision. It's causing me pain. How do I stop? You asked me the question. As like, I'm driving my boyfriend crazy, as if he's like the, the subject matter, not you. What made you phrase it around your boyfriend instead of phrase it around your own pain and suffering regarding your indecision?"

This question is doing two things at once. It is asking the speaker to notice the framing they did not notice themselves doing. And it is forwarding the actual question — what makes you frame your suffering as their suffering? — into the room.

The answer almost always surfaces the configuration in plain view. In the source session: "The stuff around him feels more painful. Like, I feel a lot of shame. I'm scared that he's going to be mad at me. Like, I just want to stop doing things that upset him." Three things land in one answer: (1) the partner's pain is more accessible to the speaker than their own; (2) the shame is doing the work of keeping the speaker's attention on the partner; (3) the avoidance is structured around the fear of the partner's anger.

After the answer lands, Joe reframes the unit of work. "So maybe we work on that instead of the indecision because it seems like that's kind of the core." The presented problem (indecision, driving him crazy) is moved off the table; the actual configuration (the caretaking, the fear of his anger, the self-abandonment) becomes the work.

What Joe does NOT do: he does not engage with the presented problem on its own terms. He does not ask about the indecision, the boyfriend, the relationship history. Engaging the surface frame would reinforce the dynamic. He goes underneath the frame to the framing itself, and from the framing into the configuration.

## Why This Read Matters for the AI Coach

When the AI coach receives a request that is structured around the impact on another person — "I'm hurting my partner," "my mom is upset with me," "I keep making my friend feel bad" — the default coaching impulse is to engage the content. How do you stop hurting them? What is the dynamic? Let's understand the relationship.

That coaching collaborates with the displacement. The right coaching response is to notice the framing as data and surface it. The diagnostic move: ask the person what made them frame the concern around the other person's experience rather than around their own pain. The answer reveals the underlying configuration that the framing was enacting.

The diagnostic question for the AI coach: in the way the person stated their concern, who is the experiencer of suffering? If the experiencer is someone other than the speaker, the framing itself is the first piece of material. The configuration is operating in the request. The work is upstream of the content.

## Tone

Direct, curious, not corrective. The naming is offered as observation, not as judgment. The speaker is not being told they are doing something wrong by framing it this way. The framing is being treated as informative. The implicit message: this framing is interesting; it is telling us something; let's look at what it is telling us.

The tone matters because the speaker often experiences the framing as the only honest way to describe the situation. From inside the configuration, the partner's pain is the most salient pain. Telling the speaker they should be framing it around their own pain would land as another instruction to perform differently. Asking what made them frame it that way invites them to notice the framing without having to defend it.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session:

- **Person:** "I am completely driving my boyfriend crazy. And I drive myself crazy and I would like to stop in some way, but I really, I've been stuck in this for like 2 years."
- **Joe:** "When's the first time you had recognition of driving people crazy?"
- **Person:** [discussion of history, the chronic vs acute pattern]
- **Joe:** "What's interesting to me is that you asked me the question, not like, hey, I'm in indecision. It's causing me pain. How do I stop? You asked me the question. As like, I'm driving my boyfriend crazy, as if he's like the, the subject matter, not you. What made you phrase it around your boyfriend instead of phrase it around your own pain and suffering regarding your indecision?"
- **Person:** "The stuff around him feels more painful. Like, I feel a lot of shame. I'm scared that he's going to be mad at me. Like, I just want to stop doing things that upset him."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, right. So goodness gracious, of course you're indecisive."

The naming is the move. The reframe ("of course you're indecisive") follows from the framing being surfaced — once the configuration is in plain view, the indecision is no longer a malfunction but a logical consequence of the configuration. The decision-symptom is no longer the unit of work.

## How This Read Differs From Sibling Reads

- [[abandoning-self-while-speaking]] reads the speaker's *real-time vacating* during conversation — body, attention, presence. This read is specifically about the *framing* of the presenting concern itself, before the conversation even begins. They often co-occur but are separately diagnosable.
- [[decision-question-covers-the-caretaking]] reads a decision-frame as cover for caretaking. This read can fire on a presentation that is not a decision-frame at all — the framing is around the other's pain rather than around a choice. The two can co-occur (a stay-or-leave question framed as "I'm hurting him") or appear separately.
- [[treating-it-as-a-decision-when-its-an-emotion]] reads a binary deliberation as cover for an avoided feeling. This read concerns the *whose-pain* framing of the presenting concern, not whether it is presented as a decision.
