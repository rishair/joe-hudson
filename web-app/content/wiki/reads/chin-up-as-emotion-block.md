# Chin Up (and Jaw Clamped) as the Somatic Signature of Emotional Repression

## What Joe Notices

Two of the main physical mechanisms humans use to stop emotion from being felt are observable in real time on a face:

1. The chin lifts slightly. The head tilts up. The neck extends. The whole posture becomes a small variant of looking down on something.
2. The jaw clamps. The teeth come together. The muscles around the jaw tighten and stay tight.

Either alone is a signal. Both together are nearly diagnostic of active emotional suppression in the moment.

The chin lift is the more universal of the two. It is visible in posed photos of famously narcissistic public figures -- the chin always slightly up. It is visible in actors playing arrogant characters in films -- the chin goes up. It is visible in people in a coaching session at the exact moment they are about to feel something they do not want to feel -- the chin goes up just before the feeling would have arrived.

The position is functional, not stylistic. The lifted chin physically interrupts the somatic channels through which emotion would otherwise be felt. The jaw clamp does the same thing through a different mechanism. Both create just enough mechanical interference to keep the felt experience from reaching awareness.

Observable signals:

- Chin position is up rather than neutral.
- Jaw is tight. Teeth may be slightly clenched even when not speaking.
- The person becomes more articulate, more head-driven, faster in their speech as the chin lifts.
- A felt-sense report goes from "I can feel something here" to "I can't really feel anything" as the chin moves from down to up.
- The opposite movement also confirms the read: when the chin lowers, the person reports increased somatic access almost immediately.

## What This Implies

The person has organized a long-running, automatic somatic strategy to keep certain emotions from being felt. The strategy is not chosen in the moment. It is a default. The body has been doing this for so long that the position has become normal posture, the clamp has become resting muscle tone.

The implication is not that the person is suppressing this one feeling in this one moment. The implication is that the person has a sustained, body-level architecture for not feeling. The chin lift in front of Joe is one frame of a film that has been running for decades.

This connects to [[childhood-imprinting]]: the body learned the strategy early as adaptation to a context in which feeling was not welcome. The body kept the strategy after the context changed. The person who cannot feel their emotions (see [[i-cant-feel-my-emotions]]) is usually running some version of this somatic architecture without knowing it.

The good news embedded in this read: the architecture is physical. It can be physically adjusted. Dropping the chin and softening the jaw -- even a few degrees, even for a few seconds -- often produces an immediate increase in felt experience. The change does not require years of therapy. It requires a small physical shift, repeated until the new position becomes the new default.

## What Joe Does

Joe names the read explicitly and teaches the mechanism in a few sentences. The teaching is short, intellectual enough to satisfy a head-dominant person, and immediately testable on the body.

The standard sequence:

1. Notice the chin (or jaw) position out loud. "I notice your chin is up." Or: "Your chin just lifted right then."
2. Offer the small movement instruction. "I want you to lower your chin and tell me what happens in your ability to feel in your body." Or: "Soften the jaw and notice what changes."
3. Receive the report. The person almost always reports that the feeling is stronger when the chin is lower or the jaw is softer.
4. Give the brief teaching frame. "One of the two ways we stop emotions in our body is to raise our chin. Which is why a definition some people have for narcissism is being unable to feel your emotions. If you look at a famous narcissist, their chin is up. It's part of the deal. The other one is holding the jaw, keeping it tight. That's the other one people use to not feel emotions, to clamp the jaw."
5. Move on. The read has done its work. The person now knows the mechanism. They will catch it themselves later.

The naming is matter-of-fact. There is no judgment of the lifted chin. The chin came up to do a job. The person is being shown the mechanism, not criticized for having it.

## The In-the-Moment Variant: Chin Down As Emotion Is Arriving

A second use of the same read appears when Joe sees emotion starting to come up and the chin lift as the active block being deployed in real time. The instruction is the same -- chin down -- but the timing is different. It is not a diagnostic experiment; it is an intervention to keep an arriving emotion from being caught and stopped.

"I see the emotions coming and I see you kind of evading the emotion. So chin down, bring your chin down, chin down, chin down. Yeah, there we go." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Feel Financially Safe?

The repetition of "chin down" multiple times in quick succession is the signature of this variant. The chin keeps trying to lift; Joe keeps redirecting it down. The emotion is at the threshold, the body is trying to stop it, and the chin instruction is the small physical interruption that holds the doorway open.

The diagnostic for when to use this variant rather than the teaching variant: the emotion is *coming*, not absent. The person is on the edge of feeling something rather than reporting they can't feel. The chin-down instruction here is operational, not pedagogical. Joe is not explaining the mechanism in the moment; he is using it to keep the feeling-channel open just long enough for the emotion to arrive.

The follow-up after the chin lowers and the feeling arrives is the same as any moment of emotional arrival: stay with the feeling, do not redirect, let it move. The chin instruction has done its job once the feeling is in the body. From there the work is on the emotion itself.

## Dialogue Example

After Ali had been giving fast, articulate, intellectual answers, Joe noticed the head position and ran the small experiment.

- **Joe:** "I just want to teach you one more little itty-bitty trick. Do the same thing again and go ahead and feel what's in your -- and now I want you to lift your chin up, like lift your chin a little more. Yeah. Like that. Great. And now, I want you to lower your chin and tell me what happens in your ability to feel in your body."
- **Ali:** "I feel like the feeling is stronger when I'm sort of doing this."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Yes. That would be most of the literature would say that. And, what I noticed is when you talked about -- I love that literature piqued you. You're like, 'literature?' when you did the first time, when you were feeling into the loss of money in your system, your chin was doing this. Your chin was raising. And when you were like, let me find the real answer, your chin went down. So if you watch back the video, you'll just notice."

*(Source: Ali Abdaal coaching session)*

The structure of the move is worth noting. Joe did not theorize first and then test. He had Ali experience the difference first -- chin up, chin down, what changes -- and then offered the framework. The somatic evidence had already landed before the intellectual frame arrived.

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, slightly clinical, with no edge of criticism. The naming is delivered as anatomy, not as character analysis. The person is not being told they are narcissistic; they are being told that one mechanism for stopping emotion produces a head position that the culture associates with narcissism. The distinction matters. The first lands as judgment; the second lands as useful information.

Often the teaching is offered with a small smile. The body has been doing this for years and the person did not know. There is something almost funny about a mechanism that has been operating in plain sight the whole time.

## What Joe Does Next

After the chin adjustment lands and the person reports increased somatic access, Joe usually continues with whatever feeling was about to arrive before the chin came up. The chin adjustment is a doorway, not a destination. The next move is into the feeling that was being blocked.

If the person tries to use the chin-down position to perform feeling rather than to feel, see [[manufacturing-the-feeling]]. The adjustment of physical position is not the same as the arrival of the emotion. The position makes the emotion possible. The emotion still has to arrive on its own.

If the chin keeps lifting back up despite the instruction, the underlying emotional material is large and the body's protective system is reasserting itself. Do not push. Honor the body's signal. The read becomes useful information about which territory will need to be approached more slowly. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]].
