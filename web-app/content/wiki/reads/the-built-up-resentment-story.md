# The Built-Up-Resentment Story

## What Joe Notices

When someone tells the story of their marriage, their company, their co-founder relationship, or their job in a particular trajectory shape -- "it started great, we were happy, and over time there's this built-up resentment, this walking on eggshells, this not entirely happy with one another" -- Joe hears conflict avoidance immediately. The shape of the trajectory and the specific vocabulary are the read.

"Hey, we got married, it was really great, we're happy, and over time there's like this kind of built-up resentment. There's this like walking on eggshells. There is this like we're not like entirely happy with one another, that whole thing. And sometimes I hear that story in marriages, sometimes I hear this story between founders, sometimes I hear the story inside of a job. And, and to me, usually there's a couple key words in this that are that will point me to the idea that this is all created by conflict avoidance." -- Joe Hudson, Addressing Conflict Avoidance

The recognition is fast because the story is the same regardless of the relationship type. The substance varies; the structure does not. Founders, spouses, employees in a long tenure -- all of them produce this trajectory under sustained conflict avoidance.

## The Signature Vocabulary

The phrases that signal the read:

- "built-up resentment"
- "walking on eggshells"
- "not entirely happy with one another"
- "it was great at the beginning"
- "things have gotten heavy"
- "the aliveness is gone"
- "I don't want to go home"
- "it's hard to be there"
- "we just don't fight like we used to"
- "we're more polite now"

The vocabulary signals the read because each phrase points to the same underlying mechanism: tension has been accumulating, it has not been metabolized, and the relationship has become organized around its avoidance.

## What This Implies

This trajectory is one of the most reliable presentations of [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-healing]] in long-term partnerships. The mechanism:

1. The relationship started with low accumulated tension and a high willingness to be open.
2. Friction emerged. Both parties chose to manage rather than address.
3. The unaddressed friction became more friction. The management got more complex.
4. The relationship organized itself around the management. Both parties became increasingly hidden from each other.
5. The accumulated unsaid material is now heavy enough that bringing any of it up feels dangerous.
6. The honesty that was the original ground has been replaced by surface peace and underground resentment.

The cost is hidden until late. The early years of the relationship have masked the development of the pattern. The presenting concern usually arrives only after the cost has become large enough that the surface explanation ("things are just hard right now") has stopped working.

## What Joe Does

The standard moves:

1. **Name the pattern as conflict avoidance, gently.** Not as accusation. As frame. "What I'm hearing is a relationship that started in honesty and has been organized around conflict avoidance. Most people don't see it as that, but that's what produces the trajectory you're describing."

2. **Surface the specific things that are not being said.** See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]]. The general pattern lives in specific suppressed truths. Find them.

3. **Name the cost.** See [[conflict-avoidance-prevents-being-loved]]. The person can usually feel that they are not loved despite the relationship being intact. The reframe -- that this is what they have produced through the avoidance -- lands hard but lands honestly.

4. **Distinguish from genuinely healthy relationships.** Some long relationships do get easier and quieter without resentment. The diagnostic is the felt aliveness, not the absence of conflict. See [[we-never-fight]] for the version of this read where the person presents the relationship as fine despite the same trajectory being underneath.

5. **Address the actual suppression.** Most often this means starting with what the person is afraid to say. Move the anger in private first ([[get-angry-in-the-car]]) so the conversation can be kind without being hedged.

## How This Differs From "We Never Fight"

[[we-never-fight]] is the same underlying pattern with a different presentation. In "we never fight," the person reports the relationship as fine and may be unaware of the cost. In the built-up-resentment story, the person knows the relationship has gone flat and is presenting it that way. The trajectory is the same. The self-awareness is different.

When this read is present, the coaching can move more directly to the mechanism. When [[we-never-fight]] is present, the coaching has to first surface what is being missed.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "We got married, it was really great, we were happy, and over time there's this kind of built-up resentment. There's this walking on eggshells."
- **Joe:** "I've heard this story a lot. Sometimes in marriages, sometimes between founders, sometimes inside a job. The players change. The pattern is the same. To me, this is conflict avoidance."

The recognition is immediate. The person often experiences relief that the pattern has been named. The relief itself opens the door to the next move.

## Tone

Joe delivers the recognition matter-of-factly. He has heard this story many times. The point of naming it as a recognizable pattern is to remove the personal-failure interpretation the person has often been carrying. "This is one of the most common configurations I see" lands differently than "you have done this wrong."
