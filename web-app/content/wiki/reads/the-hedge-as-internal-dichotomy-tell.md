# The Hedge as the Internal-Dichotomy Tell

## What Joe Notices

A small word — "kind of," "just," "a little," "niche," "minor" — placed in front of a description of a thing the person has been stuck on for years. The hedge demotes the thing in language while the behavior continues to treat the thing as massively important. The mismatch between the hedge and the loaded behavior is the read.

The classic form Joe names in the breakdown: the client describes his unstarted project as "a kind of niche website." The hedge ("kind of niche") arrives in the same sentence as the disclosure that he has been trying to build it for years. The cost of the not-building (working for someone else, limited income, not providing for the kids the way he wants) will turn out to be enormous. The defensiveness with which the importance is hedged is inversely proportional to the actual operating load: the heavier the load, the smaller the hedge tries to make it.

> "There's this little itty-bitty clue that he just dropped right there." -- Joe Hudson, Overcoming Procrastination (Coaching Session Breakdown)
>
> "It's this kind of niche website."
>
> "But we're going to find out it's incredibly important to him, but he's like, it's a kind of niche website. So there's like, that's like the dichotomy inside of him. Like he's telling us what that dichotomy, that fight inside of him is. Here's this important thing, but it's not that important, but it's really important, but it's not that important." -- Joe Hudson, Overcoming Procrastination (Coaching Session Breakdown)

The hedge is the verbal trace of the internal fight. The person is at war with themselves about how important the thing is — and the hedge is the moment that war shows up in the room.

## What This Implies

The hedge is doing protective work. It is keeping the loaded importance from being fully owned — by the client, by the coach, by the witnessing audience. The unspoken contract is: if I demote this verbally, I don't have to face how much I have loaded onto it. The protection works at the level of explicit articulation. It does not work at the level of behavior, where the loading continues to operate.

The fight the hedge encodes is between two sides of the person:

- One side has loaded the thing with enormous importance (it's supposed to define them, free them, provide for the kids, prove they can work for themselves)
- The other side is trying to demote the thing so the loading doesn't have to be felt or owned

The behavior (years of not-starting) reports the loading. The language (the hedge) reports the demotion. Both are true reports of the two sides of the war. The read is to see both at once.

The mechanism that follows: as long as the hedge keeps the importance unowned, the importance cannot be deconstructed. See [[own-the-importance-before-seeing-through-it]]. The hedge is therefore the operative defense the loaded importance has installed to keep itself running. Pierce the hedge, and the importance becomes available for the work.

## What Joe Does

The move is to flag the hedge as the tell and then to set up the [[prove-why-its-important]] confrontation. The hedge gets named (sometimes only in the coach's own attention, sometimes out loud in a way that the client can hear). Then the work moves to force the ownership of the importance the hedge has been hiding.

In the breakdown, Joe explicitly calls back to the hedge later in the session, after the importance has been forced into the open: "This is where that niche thing is, I'm calling back the niche thing." The hedge was the early signal; the late move makes the hedge visible as having been the early signal. The client can now see in retrospect that they were demoting in language what they were loading in operation.

The immediate next moves after spotting the hedge:

1. Note the hedge internally (or aloud if appropriate)
2. Continue with the work, but with the hedge held as the orienting datum
3. Surface the loaded importance through the prove-it move
4. Compress with "it's so important that it makes you stuck"
5. Optionally call back the hedge: "remember when you said it was just a niche website?"

The callback is potent because it makes the gap visible to the client. They said "niche." They listed financial freedom, fatherhood, and identity. The two cannot be reconciled inside the hedge. The hedge has to drop. Once it drops, the work can proceed.

## Dialogue Examples

The breakdown captures the early hedge and the late callback:

- **Person:** "I've got a website I'm building out, just a niche website."
- **Joe:** [internally: itty-bitty clue. The hedge is the tell. This is going to turn out to be incredibly important.]
- **Joe:** [aloud, later in the session, after the importance is in the open] "This is where that niche thing is, I'm calling back the niche thing. Right? So, to some degree, there's some freedom in him owning it, like to actually say the thing, and then — because until you can say it, you can't see through it."
- *(Source: Overcoming Procrastination Breakdown)*

The arc is visible: hedge early, force the ownership in the middle, call back the hedge at the end to make the move legible.

## Tone

The read is held quietly, almost amused. The hedge is not a flaw the client is being caught at; it is a signal the coach is reading. The internal posture Joe describes is closer to "oh, there it is" than to "gotcha." The friendliness of the read is part of what allows the subsequent confrontation to land without producing defensiveness. The client is not being indicted for hedging; they are being met where the hedge is already operating.

## Variations of the Hedge

The "niche" hedge is one form. Other linguistic hedges that do the same work:

- "Just a small project"
- "Kind of a side thing"
- "A little hobby"
- "Nothing really, just an idea I've been kicking around"
- "It's just for fun" (when the not-starting behavior contradicts the "for fun" framing)
- "It's no big deal"
- "Whatever, it's just a website / app / book / business"
- "Not a real thing, more of a concept"

The diagnostic across variants: the demotion is verbal and the behavior is loaded. The size of the gap between the two is the size of the importance that needs to be owned.

## Upstream Cues That Surface This Read

- The person describes a specific thing they are stuck on
- The description includes a small demoting word ("just," "kind of," "a little," "niche")
- The behavior contradicts the demotion (years of not-starting, chronic perfectionism, heavy avoidance)
- The cost of the not-doing, when surfaced, is enormous
- The hedge is repeated or doubled ("a kind of niche website, just a small thing")
