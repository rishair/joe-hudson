# Two Ways Leaders Hold Uncertainty

## What Joe Notices

Leaders show up to periods of rapid change in one of two structurally different ways. The same external conditions produce two different patterns. The differentiator is the leader's relationship to their own fear.

**Pattern 1: Fear without feeling the fear.** The leader is afraid. The leader is not feeling the fear. The unfelt fear leaks into the head as binary thinking ("good or bad, black or white, right or wrong") and false-end thinking ("if X happens then doom, if Y happens then the company collapses"). The leader experiences these thoughts as analysis. The thoughts are actually fear in cognitive form. See [[fear-in-body-vs-fear-in-head]] and [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]].

This leader is typically tight, decisive about the wrong things, and prone to large moves driven by the collapsed-uncertainty stories the fear is generating. The team experiences the leader as either freezing or thrashing. The decisions made from this state are systematically distorted by the unfelt fear, even when the leader does not recognize that fear is what is operating.

**Pattern 2: Feeling the fear, then finding opportunity.** The leader is also afraid. The leader is letting the fear move through them. The fear, allowed to move, releases the cognitive distortion it was producing. From the post-fear state, the leader can look at the same conditions and see what is actually available. What is actually available, in a rapid-change period, is opportunity: the structural openings that the chaos has created. The leader operates inside the 3-6 month window where the opportunities are visible (see [[short-horizon-planning-in-rapid-change]]) and treats the period as a transformation opportunity rather than as only threat.

Specific signals:

- Pattern 1: tight language, urgent voice, binary frames ("we have to either X or Y"), catastrophic stories ("if this doesn't work we're done"), 5-10 year planning attempts that feel forced, refusal to admit uncertainty out loud.
- Pattern 2: softer language, normal voice, multi-option frames, realistic acknowledgment of difficulty, 3-6 month planning that feels responsive, willingness to name "I don't know" or "I'm scared" out loud.

"It seems like there's two ways in which it's being held. The first one is that they're holding it with a lot of fear. And so typically what that does is that creates binary thinking. So they, it's good and bad, black or white, right and wrong. When people are in the fear without feeling the fear. And the other thing that's happening is there's a lot of false end thinking that goes on. So if this happens, then doom, or if this happens, then the company will collapse, et cetera, et cetera. And the propensity to fall into that fear is based more in different parts of the market. The other group of folks that I work with see this as a massive opportunity at these moments of transition that we are inevitably going to go through as a society, as people, as a business, that it allows you to redefine institutions, allows you to have a lot of growth just the same way a marriage would or a divorce would. And those folks, they're deeply feeling into their fear, allowing that to move through them, and then looking for the opportunity." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

## What This Implies

The two patterns are not different leaders. They are the same leader in two different relationships to their own fear. Pattern 1 can shift to Pattern 2 if the leader does the work of actually feeling the fear. The shift is structural, not stylistic.

This matters because the conventional advice to a Pattern 1 leader is to "stop being so scared" or "be more decisive" or "think more strategically." None of these work, because they treat the symptom rather than the cause. The symptom is the binary thinking and the false-end thinking. The cause is the unfelt fear. The treatment is the feeling, not the cognitive override.

It also matters because the cognitive override often makes things worse. The leader who is told to stop being scared and to think more clearly often produces even more binary thinking, because the override is itself a fear-management strategy that is now layered on top of the original fear. The leader gets more frozen, not less.

The reverse holds. The leader who is given permission to actually feel the fear (rather than override it) typically becomes more capable, not less. The fear, allowed to move, releases its grip on the cognition. The clear thinking becomes available because the substrate has been freed. The opportunity becomes visible because the noise has dropped.

## What Joe Does

When Joe is working with a leader in Pattern 1, the move is not to fix the cognitive symptoms directly. The move is to bring the leader into the felt experience of the fear that is producing the symptoms.

"Most of what happens is people's natural response is, 'how do I get out of the fear?' And so if you want to, the best way is to get into the fear. And so typically you can feel that fear at any moment if you allow yourself to. And if you fully allow the emotion and the expression of that fear in your system, then you'll get to a feeling of greater capacity." -- Joe Hudson, What It's Like To Live Your Purpose

The instruction inverts the standard intuition. The leader wants to get out of the fear. The work is to get into it. The getting-into-it is what releases the cognitive distortion that was the visible symptom. The cognitive distortion does not have to be addressed directly. It dissolves when its source is felt.

Once the leader has done this work, the operational moves of Pattern 2 become available: shorter planning horizons (see [[short-horizon-planning-in-rapid-change]]), willingness to acknowledge uncertainty publicly (see [[welcoming-fear-in-leadership]]), responsiveness to actual conditions rather than to imagined ones, and the capacity to see the opportunity that the chaos is presenting (see [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]]).

## The Differential Across Markets

A specific observation Joe adds: "The propensity to fall into that fear is based more in different parts of the market."

Some industries, some company types, and some market conditions are more conducive to Pattern 1 than others. This is partly because the felt risk is higher (smaller companies, more concentrated personal stakes, less diversification of identity). It is partly because the cultural script of the industry rewards the binary-thinking leader more than the feeling-the-fear leader.

The read is not "this leader is good or bad." The read is "what is the operative pattern right now, and what would shift it." The shift is always toward feeling the fear that is currently being held without being felt.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Leader (Pattern 1):** "Look, this market is either going to recover by Q3 or we're going under. I don't see any middle ground."
- **Joe:** "I notice the binary. What's the fear underneath that?"
- **Leader:** "I'm not afraid, I'm being realistic."
- **Joe:** "Okay. What's it like in your body when you say the company might go under?"
- **Leader:** *(pause)* "...tight."
- **Joe:** "Stay there. Just feel that for a second."

The redirect is not from analysis to more analysis. It is from cognition to body. The cognition was the symptom. The body is where the work happens.

## Tone

When working with a Pattern 1 leader, Joe's tone is patient and direct. The patience is for the leader who is doing the best they can within the script they have. The directness is in naming the structural pattern -- the binary thinking is the fear, the false-end is the fear -- without softening it to make the leader more comfortable. The comfort would reinforce the avoidance. The directness invites the work.

When working with a Pattern 2 leader, the conversation often turns to the specifics of the operational moves -- what is in the 3-6 month window, what experiments are worth running, where the opportunity is. The work is downstream of the felt-fear; the conversation can be operational because the underlying substrate is regulated.
