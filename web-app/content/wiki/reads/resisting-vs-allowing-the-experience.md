# Resisting vs. Allowing the Experience

## What Joe Notices

When someone is in an awakening process, the single most operative variable for how the process will go is their stance toward the experience. Two people having structurally the same experience will end up with completely different outcomes depending on whether they are resisting it or allowing it.

The same is true at smaller scales for any intense experience. The roller coaster ride is the same physical event for the person with their arms in the air and the person gripping the bar. The experience is opposite.

"Two people are going down the roller coaster and one person's like, 'This is great,' one person says, 'This is horrible,' they're gonna have very different experiences on the roller coaster." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

"One's got their arms in the air, the other one's got their, like, gripping." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

"Same thing with awakening. If somebody is resisting it, it's gonna be a very different experience for them. And so, that's a really important thing whenever I'm working with somebody who's had this experience is to normalize it as much as possible." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The read is operative because the stance is what the coach can affect. The experience itself is not interventionable. The stance toward the experience is.

## What This Implies

The amount of resistance correlates directly with the amount of suffering. This is a stronger claim than "resistance contributes to suffering." Joe is direct that the resistance is largely producing the suffering, not the experience itself.

"The amount of resistance correlates directly with how rough the integration will be... If you get Zen sickness and you say, oh, okay, this is normal, this is Zen sickness, this passes, no big deal, you have a very different experience than if you're like, what the hell is happening? How do I get it to stop?" -- Joe Hudson, Integrating a Transformative Experience

The implication: the work is on the stance, not on the experience. If the person can shift from gripping to allowing, the experience itself becomes navigable. If the person stays in gripping, the experience produces escalating distress regardless of what is happening at the level of the recognition.

This is one of the structural reasons the [[zen-sickness]] failure mode is so durable. The person in zen sickness is in continuous resistance to the experience. The resistance produces the suffering. The suffering reinforces the conviction that something is wrong, which deepens the resistance, which deepens the suffering. The cycle is self-perpetuating until the resistance is named and released.

## Specific Signals of Resistance

The signals that the person is resisting rather than allowing:

- Repeated requests for the experience to stop ("how do I get this to stop?", "how do I make it go away?").
- Catastrophizing about implications ("am I going crazy?", "will I be able to function?", "what about my job?", "what about my marriage?").
- Cognitive grasping for explanation as a way to control ("I just need to understand it").
- Physical bracing: tight jaw, shallow breath, raised shoulders, clenched hands.
- Trying to figure out what to "do" about the experience rather than letting it move.
- Diagnosis-seeking ("is this a psychiatric thing?", "do I need medication?").
- Comparison to imagined "right" awakenings ("this isn't like the books describe it").
- Withdrawal from contact with others, attempting to handle it alone.

## Specific Signals of Allowing

The signals that the person is allowing rather than resisting:

- Curiosity about the experience ("oh, that's interesting -- the visual field flattened").
- Permission for the experience to be whatever it is ("okay, this is happening").
- Permeable body language: open chest, slow breath, relaxed limbs.
- Presence of laughter or playfulness even amid the disruption.
- Asking for support without demanding intervention ("can you just sit with me?").
- Welcoming sensation rather than fighting it.
- Capacity to describe what is happening without urgency.
- Recognition that they are still functioning, still operating.

The signals are often subtle and mixed. A person can be allowing in one domain and resisting in another -- accepting the perceptual changes but gripping against the loss of certainty, for instance.

## What Joe Does

When Joe reads resistance:

1. **Normalize the experience.** The primary intervention. See [[normalize-the-experience-its-happened-to-others]]. The resistance is largely driven by the story "something is wrong with me." Replace the story and the resistance often releases on its own.

2. **Point at the still-operating life.** "You finished the show. Nobody noticed. So you can still live your life." The diagnostic that the system is still functional often releases the catastrophic interpretation.

3. **Address the resistance directly.** Sometimes naming "you're trying to make this stop -- what if you just let it be here?" lands. Sometimes naming the gripping (literally, with the body) produces the release.

4. **Suggest enjoyment of the sensations.** This is the strongest reframe Joe offers. The resistance is fueled by the conviction that the experience is bad. Suggesting the person look for what is enjoyable about the experience often disrupts the conviction.

   "Get out of your brain about what it means for the future of you and just notice that there's like a lot of freedom happening in your body. There's a lot of opening that's happening in your body. How do you enjoy that? How do you lean back and deeply enjoy that experience?" -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

5. **Ground the body.** See [[ground-the-body-during-awakening]]. A grounded body can allow what an ungrounded body cannot.

When Joe reads allowing:

1. **Confirm and support the allowing.** "You're doing it right. Just keep allowing it."
2. **Stay present.** Less intervention required. The person is metabolizing the experience naturally.
3. **Be available for the next phase.** When the disruptive phase passes, the integration phase begins and other support becomes useful.

## Why the Allowing Stance Is Hard

The bounded self that is dissolving has every reason to resist its own dissolution. The resistance is not a character flaw or a sign of poor preparation. It is the structurally appropriate response of the self to the experience of its own loosening.

"There is a place where when your ego disintegrates or your identity disintegrates, there's a fear that comes with that. Like, what I know to be me is going to die on some level. You can call it death, you can call it transformation, whatever, but it's, you're gonna cease to exist as you know yourself. And so there is this fear that happens and it happens for everybody." -- Joe Hudson, The Handbook for Accidental Awakening

The fear is universal. The resistance is the natural response to the fear. The coaching work is to name the fear, normalize the fear, and offer a different stance -- not by demanding it, but by modeling it.

The coach who is unbothered by the experience demonstrates the allowing stance. The coachee picks it up by witnessing it. This is one structural reason the coach's own state matters more than the coach's words. See [[normalize-the-experience-its-happened-to-others]] for the move that operationalizes this.

## Tone

When Joe makes this read, his tone is not corrective. He does not shame the resistance. The resistance is treated as the natural response to a frightening experience. The reframing is offered as an invitation to a different stance, not as a critique of the current one.

The shift the coach is hoping to produce is small in operation but large in effect: from "this should not be happening" to "this is happening." The shift can happen in a single sentence or take days. The coach's job is to keep offering the invitation without pushing for the shift.
