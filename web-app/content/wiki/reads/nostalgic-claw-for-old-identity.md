# Nostalgic Claw for an Old Identity (Reaching Back While Trying to Escape Forward)

## What Joe Notices

The person is in their current life, often anxious or stuck. They begin to describe a previous moment in their life when they had less than they have now. The previous moment is described with a particular wistful quality: that was when I was truly free. That was when I was alive. That was the real me.

The story has a specific tell. The previous moment was not actually a moment of freedom. It was a moment with its own constraints, its own anxieties, its own ordinary difficulties. The current narration smooths these out. What is left is a romanticized image of a self that the present self is no longer.

The nostalgia is doing double work. It is criticizing the current life and reaching back to a previous identity. The reach is a claim: "that person, the one I used to be, was the real one." The implicit corollary: "the current one is the false one."

## What This Implies

The reach is not actually about the past. The past is functioning as ammunition against the present. The person is trying to leave the current identity but does not yet know how. The previous identity, romanticized, provides a temporary alternative -- a version of self they can claim while the work of being present to the current one is being avoided.

This is one of the more subtle forms of identity attachment. It does not look like identity attachment because the person is criticizing the current self. The defense is being a critic. But the critic is operating in service of a different identity claim -- the previous one. The person is not actually releasing identity; they are exchanging one for another.

The structure is most visible when the previous moment, examined in detail, was not actually freer than the current one. The current self is reading the past through a filter that produces freedom out of what was, at the time, just life. The filter is the identity move.

## Brett's Version

Brett describes this happening to him during a period of high wealth-anxiety. He had more money than he had ever had, and he was more anxious about money than he had ever been. In a moment, he looked back nostalgically at a previous time in Cape Town when he had been completely broke, writing his name on napkins to find freelance work.

"I nostalgically went back to a moment in my 20s where I was completely broke in Cape Town, South Africa, and I like wrote down my phone number and my email address on napkins and started passing them around to hostels in the area, seeing if anybody would do what -- like needed some web work. And I nostalgically looked back on that time from that moment as like, ah, that's when I was truly free." -- Brett Kistler, What We're Afraid to Lose

He himself caught the move:

"It's interesting, there's a way that I was like clawing back to an old identity at the same time as I was like trying to get out of my current one. And also there was like a deep recognition of the, like the bullshit of the story that I was in." -- Brett Kistler, What We're Afraid to Lose

The phrase "clawing back to an old identity" is the read in his own words. He was reaching for the previous self as a way of being something other than the current self. The previous self was not actually free; he had been scrambling for survival. But the current narration produced freedom from the memory.

See [[brett-cape-town-napkins]] for the original event being romanticized. The actual moment was about discovering capacity, not about freedom per se. The current nostalgia overwrites the actual content of the moment with a different content that serves the present identity move.

## What Joe Does

The move is not to argue with the nostalgia. The romanticized past is doing a job for the person. Arguing with it gets defensive.

The move is to ask whether the previous moment was actually free. Almost always, the person can recall, with prompting, that the previous moment had its own anxieties. The Cape Town moment was scary. The college days had their own loneliness. The young version had its own fears. The freedom narrative does not survive close examination.

Once the previous moment is restored to its actual texture, the question becomes: what makes you read it as freedom now? The answer usually points at what is currently being avoided. The previous moment is freer in retrospect because the current moment is the one that is uncomfortable. The discomfort projects backwards into "anywhere but here was free."

The work then moves to the actual moment. What is happening right now that makes you want to be somewhere else, even an imagined version of somewhere else? The answer is the work.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** Back when I was traveling with nothing, I was so free. I just lived day to day. I didn't have any of this stress.
- **Joe:** What was actually happening in your body then?
- **Person:** I mean, sometimes I didn't know where I was going to sleep. I would get sick and not have insurance. There was a lot of anxiety about money.
- **Joe:** So what makes that period feel like freedom?
- **Person:** I guess... it's not the period that feels like freedom. It's more that it feels free compared to now.
- **Joe:** Yeah. What about now feels like the opposite of freedom?

The first move is to restore the past to its actual texture. The second move is to redirect to what is currently being avoided that the nostalgia was a way of escaping.

## The Two Things It Can Mean

The nostalgic claw is not always about identity attachment alone. It can also mean two distinct things:

**Genuine recognition that something has been lost.** The person actually had access to something then that they have lost contact with now. The previous moment was not freer, but the previous self was more in touch with something the current self has armored against. The nostalgia is pointing at a real thing, even if the explanation (it was the freedom of having less) is wrong. The work here is to find what is actually missed and access it from inside the current life.

**Pure identity move.** The person had the same architecture then as now and is using the past to escape the present. The romanticized version of the past does not survive contact with the actual past. The work here is to dissolve the claim that the previous self was different in any structurally important way.

The two are not always distinguishable in the moment. Joe holds both possibilities open and lets the inquiry sort which one is operating.

## Tone

Curious, gentle. The nostalgia is treated with respect even as it is being inquired into. The person is not foolish for having it; the move is universal. The work is to see what is underneath, not to make the person wrong for the move.

## What This Read Does NOT Mean

The read does not mean the past was bad. It does not mean nostalgia is always avoidance. People can have honest, sweet relationships with their past selves that are not in service of escaping the current self. The read is specifically about the claw -- the reach with grip, the function of the reaching being to provide an alternative to the present. The honest memory does not have that grip.

The distinguisher: does the memory bring the person closer to themselves now, or does it pull them away from now? The honest memory tends to integrate. The claw tends to displace.
