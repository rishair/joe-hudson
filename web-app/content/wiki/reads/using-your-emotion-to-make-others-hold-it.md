# Using Your Emotion to Make Others Hold It

## What Joe Notices

The signature: the person is having an emotion, and the way they are having it is inviting (or demanding) that other people in the room also have it. The emotion is not being felt by them privately first; it is being deployed outward in a way that recruits.

Concrete forms across emotions:

- **Anxiety:** they walk into a meeting saying "I'm really, really worried about this" with an energy that needs the others to also become worried. If the others stay calm, the anxious one cannot settle. They need company in the anxiety to feel safe.
- **Sadness:** the classic guilt-trip. The sadness is presented in a way that requires someone to do something about it. If the receiver does not change behavior or perform care, the sadness escalates.
- **Anger:** the angry person who does not feel angry alone — they feel angry at someone, with the felt-need that the someone respond, comply, or get scared. The anger needs an audience to land on.
- **Fear:** "if you do this thing I won't feel safe." The fear is being used as the lever. Someone else has to change for the fear to subside.
- **Love (or what looks like love):** love-bombing. Affection that is loaded with the requirement of a particular response. If the love is not received the right way, the love-as-pressure becomes visible.

The common shape: the emotion is happening with a centripetal pull. It wants to draw the room in. It is not being processed by the person having it.

## What This Implies

The structural insight that makes this read useful: someone who is doing this is not actually feeling the emotion they appear to be having. Brett's framing of the mechanism:

"If you're sad at somebody, it's not really sadness that you're feeling. You're projecting the sadness. You're using the sadness to manipulate, which is a way of avoiding feeling it. So it seems like that might be true for any of these emotions where if you are using them to manipulate, you are essentially self-manipulating your experience of the emotion. Which is one reason why it might just continue showing up in your life in the same way without really moving and transforming." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

Joe's confirmation:

"That's a beautiful point, right? Someone's guilting you with sadness. They're not actually feeling their sadness... They want you to feel the fear for them. But it also means that they're not fully feeling it because if they were fully feeling it, they wouldn't be trying to get it out of their system onto you." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

So the read works two ways at once. It tells you something about what they are doing relationally (deploying the emotion at the room). It also tells you something about their inner state (they are not actually in contact with the emotion they appear to be having). Both are happening.

## The Diagnostic Question

Joe offers a precise test:

"When you're — when — if you look and you're like, oh, if that person doesn't change their behavior at all, am I still okay? And if the answer is no, then you know you're in that place of doing it at somebody." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3

The test works in both directions. As a check on yourself: if you remove the other person's response from the equation, can you sit with the emotion alone? If not, the emotion is being used at them. As a read on someone else: are they having the emotion in a way that would still hold if you completely stopped responding to it?

## What This Has to Do With Recycling

This is also the structural answer to why some emotions cycle in a person without moving. If the emotion is being deployed outward instead of felt inward, it does not get processed. The body keeps producing it because the loop never closes. The person keeps being anxious, sad, or angry about the same thing because the feeling has been outsourced rather than experienced.

"If it's cycling like an addiction, it's probably a sign that I'm not actually getting it." -- Brett Kistler, Q&A #3

The same emotion, fully felt by the person alone, often clears on a much shorter time scale than the same emotion deployed at others for weeks or years.

## What Joe Does With This Read

The response depends on whether the person doing it is the client or someone in the client's life.

**If the client is the one doing it.** Joe's move is gentle redirection from the audience to the self. The question becomes "if you didn't need anyone else to also feel this, what would you do with the feeling?" The work is the recovery of the felt experience that the deployment has been substituting for. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]] and [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]].

**If someone in the client's life is doing it to them.** Joe's move is the boundary that does not absorb. The client does not need to hold the emotion that is being thrown at them. Naming what is happening without judgment ("I notice you're worried; I'm not going to also be worried about this") is the containment. See [[what-makes-you-care-about-their-emotional-state]] for the question that surfaces the dynamic, and [[dont-do-emotion-at-people]] for the principle the boundary serves.

## Why It Is Not Cruelty to Decline the Recruitment

A common worry: if I do not match their anxiety, am I being cold? The reframe: matching the anxiety does not help them either. It just confirms the recruitment loop. The most useful thing for the anxious person is for someone in the field to not also become anxious. That gives their system a different signal — they can feel anxious without it being universally true.

The same is true for sadness, anger, fear. The person who can hold their own state in the presence of the dynamic offers something the recruitment cannot: a way out of the loop.

## Tone

Even, without contempt for the person doing the recruitment. They are not failing morally; they are failing to feel. The read is diagnostic, not judgmental. The response is firm but warm: I will not also feel this for you, and I am not punishing you for trying to make me.
