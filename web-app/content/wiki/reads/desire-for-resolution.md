# Desire for Resolution

## What Joe Notices

A pull in the conversation toward conclusion. The person starts moving the dialogue toward wrap-up, summary, takeaway, neat ending. Often before any actual resolution has occurred.

Observable signals:

- The person asks "so what should I do?" or "so what's the answer?"
- A subtle leaning forward in tone, as if reaching for an exit.
- The person summarizes what was just said in a way that closes it down rather than opens it up.
- A list-making move: "okay so the takeaways are..."
- Energy that wants to package the conversation and put it on a shelf.
- Phrases like "so the upshot is" or "alright, so" or "where does that leave me?"

The pull is often unconscious. The person is not aware they are reaching for resolution; they think they are participating in arriving at it.

## What This Implies

Underneath the desire for resolution is the belief in a finish line. The same childhood imprint that produced an arrival fantasy ("when I'm enlightened / saved / done / good enough") is producing a session-level fantasy ("when this conversation has resolved, I'll be okay"). The pull for resolution is the residue of [[no-finish-line]] still playing out.

In coaching specifically, the desire for resolution often appears just before the actual material would surface. The mind senses the proximity of the unfelt feeling and reaches for the off-ramp. Resolution is the polite version of avoidance.

It can also indicate the manufactured-feeling has been completed in the person's internal accounting: "I felt the thing, now let's move on." The internal checkbox has been ticked. The actual feeling may not have moved at all. See [[manufacturing-the-feeling]].

## What Joe Does

Joe names the desire and uses it as material. He does not satisfy it.

- "I can sense like a desire for resolution."
- "What's the resolution going to get you?"
- "And how is that not here right now?"

The question "what's the resolution going to get you?" is the key move. The person names what they think resolution will provide (alignment, joy, accomplishment, connection). Joe then points out that what they want from the resolution is already present in the conversation: "I feel connected to you. I'm enjoying the conversation." The resolution would not produce what the person already has.

This often produces a small confusion. The person had been operating on the assumption that they were missing something. The realization that the resolution would not deliver anything new collapses the resolution-fantasy and opens the question: "what is it that you want the resolution for?"

The answer is often: to feel like it's done. Like complete. Like I got there. At which point Joe asks: "What if that never comes? What if there is no complete, no done, no heaven?" See [[what-if-there-is-no-finish-line]].

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "So right now it's like you — it like — I can sense like a desire for resolution."
- **Brett:** "Yeah, yeah, there's a strong desire for resolution, which I'm recognizing is wanting myself to change."
- **Joe:** "And what's the resolution going to get you?"
- **Brett:** "Alignment, joy, accomplishment, connection."
- **Joe:** "And how is that not here right now?"
- *(Source: Allowing Yourself to Change)*

The sequence does not require many turns. Naming the desire, asking what the resolution would provide, and pointing at the present moment is usually enough to dissolve the pull.

## Tone

Gentle, observant, not corrective. The naming is descriptive: "I can sense" rather than "you are doing." The questions that follow are genuine inquiries, not Socratic traps.

## What This Often Reveals

- An active finish-line story still operating.
- A childhood arrival fantasy (often religious) being replayed in real time.
- The next layer of avoidance: closing the conversation before the felt experience can occur.
