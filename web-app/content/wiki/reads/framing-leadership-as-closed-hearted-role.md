# They're Framing Leadership as the Closed-Hearted Role

## What Joe Notices

The person is refusing leadership not because they cannot lead — they often visibly can — but because they have an internal model in which leadership equals closed-heartedness. The refusal is a moral move from their perspective: they are preserving their warmth, their kindness, their care for people. They are choosing love over power.

Observable signals:
- They reference a specific authority figure (parent, boss, coach, teacher) as what they do not want to become
- They use language like "I don't want to be the asshole"
- They describe their non-leadership as a values choice rather than as an inability
- They become uncomfortable when asked what they want and frame the asking itself as harsh
- They visibly soften when asserting any preference — almost as if the softening is itself the protection
- Their words about leadership have a slight contempt to them, even when they admire effective leaders
- They equate "stepping into power" with "becoming controlling" or "becoming cold" without distinguishing the two

The read also surfaces when the person describes other people's leadership: their critique tends to focus on the perceived coldness or controlling quality rather than on actual outcomes. They have a sensitive antenna for closed-hearted authority and they recoil from it consistently.

## What This Implies

The person carries an inherited binary: love and power, kindness and leadership, warmth and authority are opposites. The binary was installed by direct exposure to an authority figure who modeled the closed-hearted form, often during childhood, sometimes later. The exposure was empirically correct for the person — they saw the cost of the closed-hearted form and they made the rational choice to refuse to repeat it.

The error is generalization. They have not yet seen the integrated form — empowered AND loving, in charge AND warm, leading AND attuned. So the integrated form does not exist in their internal field of options. The binary is not a confusion; it is the absence of a third option in their experience.

This connects to the broader inheritance pattern. The way the person related to their original authority figure becomes the template for all authority — including the authority they could take in their own life. See [[how-you-related-to-parents-is-how-you-relate-to-god-and-money]]. The closed-hearted parent becomes the closed-hearted-leader template, which becomes the model the person is refusing to embody, which becomes the reason they refuse leadership.

The implication for the work: the binary is the structure to dissolve. Arguing that the person should lead more is missing the point — they will not lead from inside the binary because doing so would mean becoming the figure they are protecting against. The work is showing them the third option (see [[empowered-vs-power]], [[love-requires-empowerment]]) and giving them the body-level experience of it (see [[feel-love-and-empowerment-as-the-same]]).

## What Joe Does

The response sequence depends on which layer of the binary is operating.

**For the surface layer (intellectual recognition):** Joe names the binary directly. "It sounds like you have a frame in which being a leader means being the asshole, and you don't want to be that, so you don't lead." The naming makes the frame visible. The person often nods immediately — they recognize the frame as theirs.

**For the developmental layer (the inheritance):** Joe traces the inheritance gently. "Where did you learn that being in charge meant being like that?" The person typically names a parent, a boss, a coach. The tracing is not for explanation — it is for distancing the inherited model from the person's own potential leadership. The inheritance is not them. It was a particular person.

**For the protective layer (the warmth being defended):** Joe acknowledges what the refusal is preserving. The warmth is real. The protection of it makes sense. The teaching is not "your warmth is the obstacle." The teaching is "your warmth is the foundation — and it can lead." This is a different relationship to the warmth than the person has been holding.

**For the integration layer (the third option):** Joe introduces the field of possibility. Empowered AND loving. In charge AND warm. The research on empathetic leaders being more successful. The examples of leaders the person admires who have the integration (Martin Luther King is the canonical reference Joe uses). The third option is named, demonstrated through examples, and offered as the work to move toward.

The integration is not delivered as instruction. It is offered as a possibility the person has not yet inhabited but can begin to. The body-level practice (see [[feel-love-and-empowerment-as-the-same]]) is what makes the integration available beyond intellectual recognition.

## What Joe Does Not Do

- Joe does not argue that the person should "just lead." That move re-installs the binary by treating the refusal as the problem rather than the binary itself.
- Joe does not invalidate the person's experience of closed-hearted leaders. The data they have is real. The error is in the generalization, not in the observation.
- Joe does not push the person toward formal leadership roles. The work is the integration first; the formal roles can follow or not.
- Joe does not contrast their warm refusal with a more "decisive" alternative as if the alternative were the goal. The decisive-without-warmth alternative is the figure they are protecting against. Offering it as the goal would confirm the binary.

## Tone

The reading is gentle. The person carrying this pattern is sensitive — usually more sensitive than average to closed-hearted dynamics, which is part of why the model landed so hard. Joe meets that sensitivity with matching softness. The teaching is offered, not asserted. The person has been protecting something real, and the protection is honored even as the binary is dissolved.

The tone has a specific quality of "you are not wrong about what you saw — and there is more." The "more" is the third option that has been missing from the field.

## Dialogue Examples

A representative exchange from the source episode:

- **Brett (synthesizing what often shows up in clients):** "I have a sense that there's a common experience for somebody who's scared to step into leadership is that they want to be loving, but they don't want to be the asshole. There seems like a false binary between really good love and power."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Yeah. I think the underlying thing is, if I'm a leader, I'm potentially going to be an asshole or I have a job to do and therefore I'm gonna stop paying attention to people, I'm gonna stop empathizing. Even though all the research shows that empathetic leaders are more successful leaders."
- **Brett:** "I think many people just had the examples of whoever was in a leadership or in charge role in their life did so from a closed heart. And so they believe that they're going to have to close their heart to step into making something happen — into power."
- **Joe:** "Or they close their hearts in themselves to make something happen. Often the case is that their authority figure, either external when they were a kid or their internal authority figure, is an ass."

The exchange demonstrates the read and the response in one move: naming the binary, locating the developmental origin, and pointing toward the third option without yet introducing it.
