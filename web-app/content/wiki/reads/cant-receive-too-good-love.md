# Can't Receive Love That's Too Good

## What Joe Notices

The person is in a relationship — or was in a relationship — with someone who is unmistakably loving toward them. The love is not in question. The partner is good to them. The person is not having a normal experience of being loved well. Something is going wrong. Specific signals:

- The person breaks up with, or sabotages, the person they obviously deeply care about. They cannot say why.
- The person becomes visibly self-attacking after receiving care. They are upset with themselves. They are beating themselves up. The trigger was the love, even though the surface explanation is something else.
- The person reports discomfort with being loved well. "It makes me uncomfortable." "I don't know what to do with this." "It feels like too much."
- The person reports not deserving the love. "I don't deserve this." "I don't know why they love me." "It's too much for what I am."
- The person says the partner is "too good for them" — usually as a way of explaining a decision to leave that the person cannot otherwise justify.
- The person engineers conflicts with a loving partner, then experiences the conflict as evidence the relationship is not actually loving.
- The person prefers, in their selection patterns, partners who do not love them well — calling these partners "exciting," "interesting," "real," "with chemistry" — while finding loving partners "boring," "lacking spark," "missing something."
- The person reports feeling more like themselves with partners who treat them badly than with partners who treat them well.

The read fires when good love is reliably producing the wrong response. The love is real. The response is the trained response. The body is doing what the body learned to do.

## What This Implies

The person has a love-imprint that does not include "love that is just love." Their original love-configuration was bundled with something else — abuse, criticism, conditionality, engulfment, abandonment. The body learned that love is the bundle. When love arrives now without the bundle, the body experiences the gap. The missing components are not registered as relief; they are registered as wrongness. The body completes the pattern itself, often by producing the missing component (self-attack, conflict, withdrawal) so the configuration matches.

"That happens a lot for people who have any kind of abuse in their life, whether that's critical mental abuse, emotional abuse. If they get love that's too good, they can't accept it. It makes them feel very uncomfortable. And it's all because they feel like subconsciously they don't deserve it. There's a lot of shame going on, and shame is anger turned inwards." -- Joe Hudson, I've Coached 1000s of People

The "can't accept it" is precise. The person is not refusing the love by choice. The body is unable to process the love as love. The intellectual mind may want the love. The body's pattern-matching system does not recognize it as the thing it is looking for, and so cannot complete the receipt.

The shame the person reports about not deserving the love is the felt-form of the wiring mismatch. The body experiences "this does not match my pattern" as "there is something wrong with me." The wrongness gets reified as a story about deservingness. The story is downstream of the body's mismatch; the body's mismatch is upstream of the story. Working on the story does not change the wiring. Working on the wiring eventually changes the story.

See [[love-pattern-mimics-childhood-love-pattern]] for the underlying principle. See [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] for the specific mechanism by which the wiring mismatch produces the self-attack.

## What Joe Does

The move is not to convince the person they deserve the love. The convincing operates at the level of the story, which is downstream of the wiring. The wiring updates through repeated experience of love-without-the-bundle, not through cognitive argument.

Several moves Joe makes when this read fires:

**Name the wiring.** "Your body learned that love came with abuse. So when love arrives that doesn't have abuse with it, your body doesn't know what to do. It tries to complete the pattern by attacking you, or attacking the partner, or ending the relationship." The naming gives the person a frame for what they are experiencing that is not "I am broken." The frame is structural. The person can hold a structural fact without it becoming another shame-object.

**Stay with the love without forcing.** When the person is visibly self-attacking after receiving care, Joe does not rush to fix it. He does not say "you deserve this love." He stays present, keeps the heart open, and lets the person be in the discomfort of receiving without trying to manage it on the person's behalf. See [[keep-the-heart-open-and-repeat-the-truth]] for the structural move.

**Surface the underneath.** The self-attack that follows the love is the surface form. Underneath is often anger — at the original parent or caregiver who paired love with abuse — that has been turned inward as shame. The work moves toward letting that anger find its outward direction. See [[shame-is-anger-turned-inward]] for the release mechanism and [[get-angry-in-the-car]] for the practice form.

**Refuse to confirm the wiring.** If the person becomes combative as the work deepens (because the body's wiring includes "love produces conflict"), Joe does not retaliate, withdraw, or shame the combat. He holds the open heart. The non-confirmation of the wiring is the new information the body needs.

What Joe does NOT do: argue with the person about whether they deserve love. The argument operates at the story level, where the wiring is winning. The argument also gives the person something to argue back about, which is more efficient at maintaining the wiring than receiving the love would be. The argument is the speed bump.

## Dialogue Examples

In the Good Will Hunting scene, the read fires after Will admits he broke up with Skyla. Will is visibly upset with himself. The therapist's response is not to reassure Will that he deserves Skyla. It is to begin delivering the underneath message: "It's not your fault." The "fault" is the surface story. Underneath is the shame. Underneath the shame is the anger. Underneath the anger is the pain that was never felt.

- **Will:** "So, you know, what is it like? Will has an attachment disorder? Is it all that stuff? Fear of abandonment? Is that why I broke up with Skyla?"
- **Therapist:** "I didn't know you had."
- **Will:** "I did."
- **Therapist:** "You wanna talk about it?"
- **Will:** "No."

*(Source: I've Coached 1000s of People)*

The "no" is the moment to stay close to. Will is intellectualizing the breakup (attachment disorder, fear of abandonment) and refusing to feel what is underneath. The therapist's "you wanna talk about it" is the offer; the "no" is the refusal. The work then moves through the next several beats, eventually arriving at "it's not your fault" — which addresses not the intellectual diagnosis but the shame-story underneath.

## Tone

The tone for this read is patient and unhurried. The person is in the middle of an experience their body cannot complete cleanly. Rushing them produces more bracing. The coach has to slow down to the speed of the body's processing, which is slower than the intellectual speed the person is presenting at.

The tone is also warm without being too warm. Too much warmth can flood the system the same way the original good love is flooding the system. The warmth is steady, available, undemanding. The person can take as much of it as they can take. The coach does not push more.

The tone is most precisely described as the tone Robin Williams takes in the Good Will Hunting scene: low-pressure presence, willingness to repeat, no eagerness to fix, no withdrawal in the face of the response.

## What This Read Is Not

This is not [[dont-call-them-a-liar-with-deflection]]. That anti-pattern is about the social move of deflecting compliments politely. This read is about the deeper structural inability to receive love as love. The deflection may be present, but the read is about what is upstream of the deflection.

This is not [[speed-bumping]]. Speed-bumping is the intellectual-acknowledgment defense against substantive observations. The can't-receive-too-good-love read is about the body's refusal to integrate love specifically. The two can co-occur — the speed-bumping may be the verbal form the refusal takes — but the read is about the wiring, and the anti-pattern is about the live in-session defense.

This is not "they don't really want love." See [[love-pattern-mimics-childhood-love-pattern]] for the explicit distinction. The person wants love. They do not have a body that can yet receive it. The wanting and the receiving are different operations.
