# Confidence Only Where Approval Isn't Needed

## What Joe Notices

A person describes a long list of high-status accomplishments and admits to never having felt confident inside any of them. Hall of Fame, Super Bowl, marriage, parenting, professional career: not confident in any. Then they name one specific context where they do feel confident -- often a small one. A men's circle. A 12-step room. A specific friendship. A therapy session.

The signal is the *contrast*. The person is competent across every domain that the culture would call success, and their felt confidence sits inside the one domain that has no external achievement structure attached to it.

The specific phrasing to watch for:

- "You know what? I feel confident in [specific small context]."
- "When I'm in spaces where I feel permission to be my authentic self."
- "I don't have to perform there."
- "Nothing to hide there."

Aaron Taylor, after listing the College Football Hall of Fame, Super Bowl ring, marriage, fatherhood, and 14 years at CBS Sports, none of which produced felt confidence: "I feel confident in circle. I feel confident in circle, meaning when I'm in spaces where I feel permission to be my authentic self."

## What This Implies

The confidence is not absent from the person. It is located precisely where the person is not seeking approval. Everywhere else, the confidence is being eaten by the approval-seeking. The accomplishments did not produce confidence because the accomplishments were running on the engine of approval-seeking. Each new accomplishment fed the engine that prevented confidence from arriving.

The diagnostic insight: confidence and approval-seeking are inversely related. Where the person needs the room's approval, confidence cannot land. Where the person does not need it (because the room is structured for honesty rather than performance), confidence arrives easily.

This is why the person can be a top performer in their field and still report no confidence inside it. The performance produced the external markers but the underlying mechanism (performing for approval) is the opposite of what confidence requires.

## What Joe Does

Joe makes the read visible by naming what is structurally different about the one context where confidence lives. He does not congratulate the person on having found a good men's group. He uses the contrast to reveal the mechanism operating everywhere else.

His response to Aaron: "It seems like what you just said really illuminates that in the fact that probably in circles, the only time that it's not about somebody else's pat on the butt, it's not about somebody else's telling you you did a good job."

Joe's move is to expose the structure: the difference between the high-achievement contexts and the circle is not the level of accomplishment but the absence of approval as the currency. Where approval is the currency, the person's worth is contingent on receiving it. Where it is not the currency (because the room is structured for truth-telling), the worth can be felt directly.

Joe then extends the read into a parenting implication, which is one of his typical moves -- using the read to widen what the person is working with: "To have any kind of confidence, which also to me relates to what you were saying about your kids, which is for me, the whole thing about being a parent of teenagers is to have them learn to hear their own voice of good job."

The implicit teaching: the goal is not to find more circles. The goal is to bring the internal "good job" -- the capacity to hear one's own voice -- into all the other contexts.

## Dialogue Example

- **Aaron:** I'm in the College Football Hall of Fame, I've won a Super Bowl, I won the Lombardi in college as the most outstanding offensive lineman, but football, I never felt confident, ever. In my marriage? No, not really. How about as a father? Uh, no. An employee of CBS? [...] You know what? I feel confident in circle.
- **Joe:** [later, after Aaron describes the childhood pattern of "good job, good play, good read, good recovery, good boy"] Something that just clicked for me is that you were saying earlier on, you know, did you have that full sense of confidence in ball? Did you have that full sense of confidence at CBS or with your marriage? And it seems like what you just said really illuminates that in the fact that probably in circles, the only time that it's not about somebody else's pat on the butt, it's not about somebody else's telling you you did a good job.
- **Aaron:** [pause, recognition]

The move's elegance: Joe waits for the autobiographical material that explains the read (the "good job, good play" pattern from childhood) and then makes the connection back to the present-day report. The read becomes self-evident once the structure is named.

## Tone

Joe makes this read with warmth and slight relief, as if naming something the person almost said themselves. Not diagnostic. Not corrective. The tone is "of course it makes sense that you can only feel confident there." The implication that this is true of *everywhere else* is delivered as a co-discovery, not as a verdict.

## Why This Read Matters

Without the read, the person's report -- "I feel confident in circle" -- registers as a happy footnote. With the read, it becomes the diagnostic that explains the entire lack of felt confidence elsewhere. The single confident location becomes the data point that reveals what is structurally true about every other location.

This is also why the read is more useful than asking the person why they don't feel confident at work. That question keeps the inquiry inside the work context, where the answer is some version of "I don't know, I should feel confident, I've accomplished a lot." The read locates the answer outside the work context, in the structural comparison with the one place where confidence is felt.

## The Intimacy-Bifurcation Variant: When the Confidence Read Applies to Connection-with-Specific-People

A specific operational variant of this read shows up when the bifurcation is not between accomplishment-contexts and an intimate-group, but between safe-rooms-where-the-person-can-be-themselves and high-stakes-rooms-where-they-freeze-around-a-specific-other. The structure is identical to the canonical read; the surface presentation is different.

The signal: the person is currently being intimate, emotionally available, even tearful in the coaching room — and is presenting a concern about being unable to be intimate with the specific person whose response matters to them (a woman they want to date, a parent, a boss, a romantic partner). The contrast between in-room aliveness and out-of-room frozenness is the data.

In the source session, the young man was visibly emotional and intimate with everyone on a Zoom call (including strangers) while presenting a concern that he could not be intimate with women he wanted to date. Joe's first move was to make the bifurcation explicit:

"What I notice right now is that you're being intimate with everybody on the call. You're on the edge of tears. You're emotional. So how are you doing it right now, or how were you doing it a couple minutes ago?" -- Joe Hudson, I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me

The naming locates the capacity in the present. The capacity is intact. What is missing is not the ability to be intimate; it is the ability to be intimate when the person needs validation from the other party.

The deeper move: identify what the high-stakes context has that the safe-room does not, and what it does not have. The thing the high-stakes context has: a person whose validation the seeker wants. The thing the safe-room does not have: that need for validation. The need is the freezing agent. Where the need is absent, the capacity is available. Where the need is present, the capacity is blocked.

The young man's own articulation when Joe asked what made the safe-room different: "I know I'm winning right now... I know I'm doing the thing I want to do." The frame surfaces the same structural insight as the canonical read in different vernacular: the confidence (or in this case the intimacy capacity) is contingent on the absence of seeking. Where the person is acting from their own want, they are intimate. Where they are acting from the need for another's response, they are frozen.

The structural identity between the variants: this is the same read as the Aaron Taylor case, applied at the level of immediate interpersonal availability rather than at the level of career-context felt-confidence. In both cases, the reservoir of the capacity is located precisely where the approval-engine is off. The work in both cases is to relocate the verdict-source from outside to inside — so the capacity becomes available everywhere, not just in the rooms where approval-seeking is structurally absent.

The corrective move that follows from this variant: name the bifurcation, surface the wanting-of-validation as the freezing agent, and route to [[action-toward-what-you-want-is-the-self-validation]] — the principle that the doing of the thing IS the self-validation, and the not-doing IS the un-validation that no external approval can undo.
