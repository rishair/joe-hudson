# I'm Wrong, You're Right

## What Joe Notices

After the coach offers an observation or a reframe, the person responds with an immediate self-correction: "I'm wrong. You're right." Or some variant: "Oh my goodness, you are right. You're actually right. Yeah, I'm wrong. You're right." The response is fast, snapped-out, and total. The person does not consider the observation, weigh it against their own experience, or partially agree; they convert the observation into evidence that they were wrong and the coach was right.

The self-correction is the agreement-form of someone who learned to position themselves as the one who got it wrong as the path to being in good standing with the people they needed. The correction is what made them safe. The configuration continues into adulthood. The coach offers a reflection; the person immediately performs the I-was-wrong response because that is the form of relating they know.

Signals:

- The person says "I'm wrong, you're right" or "you're actually right" with surprising speed after a coach's observation
- The acknowledgment is total — no nuance, no partial agreement, no "let me think about that"
- The person produces the self-correction before they have had time to feel into whether the observation is true for them
- The face shifts to a "got it" expression that performs understanding before understanding has had time to land
- The person uses self-correction as the entry into every shift in the conversation — "oh you're right" repeated across the session
- The self-correction has a quality of relief — being wrong is more comfortable than holding a perspective the coach has not validated
- The person does not push back on observations they have reason to push back on
- The body posture often slumps slightly into the correction, as if the correction is a small collapse

The decisive marker: the self-correction is immediate and total, and it happens regardless of whether the observation is accurate to the person's felt-experience. The pattern fires whether the observation is on or off target. It is not actually responding to the content of the observation; it is responding to the structure of the relationship.

## What This Implies

The self-correction is the configuration of someone who learned that relying on themselves meant relying on themselves for self-correction. The original environment had a parent or caregiver who needed the child to be the one who got things wrong so that the parent did not have to be. The child learned to organize their relating around the self-correction as the entry into being in good standing. The self-correction was their job.

"You can see the pattern again. 'I'm wrong. You're right.' That's what his mind does. His mind is constantly correcting himself because that's how somebody who needed to rely on themselves instead of their parents — this is how they learn to operate." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The configuration is a specific signature of [[self-reliance-emotional-signature|active self-reliance]]. The self-reliant person had to rely on themselves for the self-correcting work that a caregiver would normally do. Nobody else was going to tell them they were okay, so they had to constantly check whether they were wrong and correct it themselves. This produced an internal voice that is in a near-permanent state of self-correction. The voice never rests. The coach's observation is one more occasion for the same operation: I must have been wrong, the coach is right, I will correct myself.

The implication for the coaching relationship: the self-correction is the pattern the coach is most likely to elicit by being a coach at all. Anything the coach says will be received through the self-correction filter. The person is not actually engaging with the content of the coach's observations; they are performing the agreement-form they know. The work is not visible if the work depends on the person disagreeing or holding ground.

The implication for the person's internal life: the self-correction is happening continuously inside them, not just in coaching. They are correcting themselves throughout the day. The voice that does the correcting is the voice of the configuration. The voice does not have to be loud to be exhausting. The exhaustion is one of the signatures of [[im-depressed|depression]] that the configuration produces.

## What Joe Does

**Name the pattern when it surfaces.** Joe does not let the I'm-wrong-you're-right exchange pass as agreement. He names what it is. "You can see the pattern again. 'I'm wrong. You're right.' That's what your mind does." The naming is delivered as observation, not as criticism. The person needs to see the pattern in order to stop performing it as if it were genuine agreement.

**Refuse the agreement when the agreement is the pattern firing.** When a person snaps to "you're right" after an observation, the coach can decline the agreement. "I don't actually need you to agree with me. I want you to check whether what I said is true for you." The refusal of the agreement is the interruption of the configuration. The person is being asked to do something they have not had to do: hold their own ground, even momentarily.

**Notice the speed.** The pattern is partly diagnosed by speed. The agreement that arrives before the person has had time to feel into the observation is the configuration firing. Slowing the person down (see [[mind-narrative-bypasses-felt-experience]]) gives the felt-data time to register and the genuine response time to arrive.

**Find the place the pattern installed.** The configuration came from somewhere. Usually a parent or caregiver who needed the child to be the wrong one. Locating the installation is part of the work that lets the configuration dissolve. The person needs to see that the I'm-wrong-you're-right is a learned form, not who they are.

**Let the person not know.** A specific corrective: when the person does not know what they think, do not push for an answer. The pushing reactivates the configuration ("I should know, I'm wrong for not knowing, what does the coach want me to say"). Sitting in the not-knowing without making it a problem allows the person to discover what they actually think, which is what the configuration has been overriding.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

He does not accept the self-correction as genuine agreement. Accepting it would reinforce the configuration. The coach who lets the person say "you're right" without noticing the pattern is being used as the parent figure the person has organized around. The coach has to decline the role for the work to happen.

He does not shame the pattern. The pattern was protective. The person ran the pattern because it kept them safe in an environment where being wrong-and-correcting was the entry into relationship. Shaming the pattern would produce more of it (the person would correct themselves for having the pattern).

He does not try to teach the person to disagree with him. The pattern is not solved by performing disagreement. The pattern is solved by the person noticing what they actually think and feel underneath the correction-impulse. Disagreement-as-performance would be the same configuration in a different costume.

## Connected to the Relational Field

The self-correction pattern also organizes how the person interprets the coach's interventions. Whatever the coach does, the person interprets through the lens of "what does the coach want me to do, so I can do it right." This is the [[caretaking-the-coach-during-intensity|caretaking-the-coach]] dynamic from a different angle — but here the caretaking is specifically through self-correction rather than through other forms of softening or managing.

"He's interpreting whatever I do into something that he has to do to please me or to get it right." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The implication: the coach has to be very careful about what they offer, because anything offered will be received as an instruction the person has to follow correctly. The corrective is to keep returning the person to their own felt-experience rather than to the coach's frame. "What do you notice?" matters more than "what do you think of my observation?" The work has to keep landing in the body, where the configuration has less control.

## The Connection to "How Do I" Questions

The self-correction pattern often pairs with the [[how-question-as-defense-against-having|how-question]]. The person who self-corrects also asks how. "I did it wrong, you're right. How do I do it right?" The two patterns reinforce each other. The self-correction creates the I-must-have-been-wrong premise; the how-question creates the I-need-instructions response.

Working with one without working with the other is incomplete. The full pattern is: I was wrong + please tell me how to be right + I will then perform the correction. Each part has to be interrupted for the configuration to soften.

## Dialogue Example

The canonical instance from the source session — Joe naming the pattern after the 18-year-old has just said "Oh my goodness, you are right. You're actually right. Yeah, I'm wrong. You're right":

- **Person (after Joe shows him what just happened with the loving):** "Oh my goodness, you are right. You're actually right. Yeah, I'm wrong. You're right."
- **Joe (in commentary):** "And you can see the pattern again. 'I'm wrong. You're right.' That's what his mind does. His mind is constantly correcting himself because that's how somebody who needed to rely on themselves instead of their parents — this is how they learn to operate."
- *(Source: "This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown)*

The structural point: Joe is reading the *speed and totality* of the self-correction as the diagnostic. The agreement is real on the surface — the person is genuinely seeing what Joe is pointing at. But the *form* of the agreement (the immediate self-blame, the "I'm wrong") is the pattern firing. The genuine seeing could have been "yeah, I see that" or "oh, interesting" or even just a slow nod. It came out as "I'm wrong, you're right" because that is the configuration's default response to any new piece of information from a person in authority.

## Tone

Curious, structural, gentle. Joe names the pattern as a feature of how the person's mind operates, not as a fault. The naming creates room for the person to see the pattern without having to defend against it. The tone communicates that the pattern is recognizable, common, and changeable — not damning.

The naming has to be done without producing the next iteration of the pattern. If Joe says "you just did the I'm-wrong-you're-right thing" and the person responds "you're right, I did," the configuration is firing on the naming itself. Joe will sometimes pre-empt this by saying the naming sideways: "this is what your mind does," locating the action with the mind rather than with the person, so that the person can observe their mind doing it rather than have to agree about themselves having done it.
