# Abstract Framing as Defense

## What Joe Notices

The person opens the session with abstract pattern-language rather than a specific situation. They describe what they do, in general, rather than what is happening, specifically. They use words like "I keep," "I always," "I have this thing where," "the pattern is," "I get in my own way." They describe themselves as the analyst of themselves.

The first sentence has the texture of a summary, not an opening. As if the person has already done the work of figuring out what is wrong and has come to the session to confirm the diagnosis.

Specific signals:

- The opening sentence contains the words "pattern," "always," "keep," "tend to," "have this thing where," or "in my own way."
- The opening does not contain a specific instance — no dates, no concrete actions, no names of people involved.
- The person speaks about themselves in the third person of self-observation: "I notice I do this," "I see that I keep," "I recognize that I."
- The grammar is generalized — "we resort to distraction" or "I go to what I know how to do" rather than "yesterday I opened my laptop and started organizing files instead of writing."
- The vocabulary is borrowed from self-help, therapy, or coaching content: self-sabotage, avoidance, distraction, pattern, identity, getting in own way.
- The body language matches the abstraction — composed, observational, slightly above the material rather than in it.
- The person frames their relationship to the problem as one of recognition rather than confusion. They already know what they are doing. They just cannot stop.

## What This Implies

The abstraction is the defense's preferred medium. As long as the conversation stays at the meta-level — discussing the pattern, naming the avoidance, analyzing the self-sabotage — the defense never has to engage with anything concrete enough to actually change.

This read is distinct from [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]] though it shares some surface texture. Intellectualization uses conceptual sophistication to outflank the felt material; abstract framing uses general pattern-language to avoid the specific particulars where action could happen. A person can be in abstract-framing without being intellectually sophisticated. The defense is simpler — it just keeps everything one level up from the doing.

The abstraction is often produced by years of consuming self-help content. The person has absorbed the vocabulary of patterns, defenses, and avoidances. The vocabulary has become the medium they think about themselves in. The medium is the defense — it floats the conversation above the specific where the work would have to happen.

The person is usually self-aware enough that asking "do you recognize this pattern?" produces immediate yes. The self-recognition is the pattern firing one more time. The recognition has been substituted for the action the recognition supposedly enables.

## What Joe Does

The primary move is to interrupt the abstraction by asking for the specific. The interruption has to happen early — within the first minute or two of the session — because every additional sentence at the abstract level reinforces that the abstract level is where the work will happen.

The interruption form Joe uses is direct and slightly impatient:

"It's very abstract. It's too abstract for me. Tell me, what's the — what's — okay, you're getting in your own way. How?" -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

Three things happen in this sentence. First, Joe names the abstraction as abstraction ("it's very abstract"). Second, he locates the failure in himself rather than the client ("too abstract for me"). This keeps the interruption from landing as a criticism. Third, he repeats back the abstract frame the person used ("you're getting in your own way") and immediately demands the concrete underneath it ("How?").

The structural form of the interruption: name the abstraction, locate the demand-for-the-concrete in your own need to understand, then ask the operationalizing question. The form keeps the client from getting defensive about having been abstract.

The "how?" is doing specific work. It refuses the why-question the client expects ("why do I do this?") and demands the mechanism ("what specifically do you do?"). The how-question forces concrete particulars to come out. See [[how-what-questions]] for the broader principle.

If the client returns to abstract framing on the next answer, Joe repeats the move. The pattern can fire many times in a session. Each time it fires, the same interruption is appropriate. The work cannot proceed while the abstract level is being maintained.

Once the concrete particulars are in the room (the specific business, the specific avoided action, the specific should), the rest of the coaching becomes available. The interruption is the gateway move that converts the session from analysis-about to engagement-with.

## What This Read Is Not

It is not a criticism of the client's self-awareness. Most clients running this pattern are accurately observing themselves. The problem is not that the observations are wrong; it is that the observations have become the substitute for action.

It is not the same as [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]]. Intellectualization is more sophisticated — the client uses conceptual frames, philosophical detours, and multi-layered reframes. Abstract framing is simpler — the client just keeps everything generalized. Both reads can be operating simultaneously, and the interventions overlap, but the abstract-framing intervention is more elementary: just ask for the specific.

It is not [[in-the-story]]. Story-mode is when the client narrates a sequence of events ("and then... and then... and then..."). Abstract framing is when the client summarizes a pattern without ever entering the events. Both modes avoid the felt material, but they avoid it differently. The story-mode client is at least in the particulars; the abstract-framing client has not yet arrived at the particulars.

It is not a sign the client is being evasive on purpose. The abstract framing is almost always automatic. The client believes they are explaining their problem; they have not noticed that their explanation is the form their avoidance takes.

## Dialogue Example

The cleanest demonstration from the source session:

- **Client:** "Okay, so basically what I'm trying to figure out is how I get myself out of my own way. I just keep going back to that and then it's like, okay, I recognize that's the problem. And then we resort to distraction to avoid actually getting our hands dirty in the fucking problem."
- **Joe:** "It's very abstract. It's too abstract for me. Tell me, what's the — what's — okay, you're getting in your own way. How?"
- **Client:** "I go to what I know how to do instead of doing what I want to do, because what I want to do feels like — yeah."
- **Joe:** "And what is it that you want to do?"
- **Client:** "I want to become my own business owner."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

In this exchange, the interruption produces movement within two questions. The client begins in pure abstraction ("how I get myself out of my own way"). Joe names the abstraction. The client gives a still-abstract answer ("I go to what I know how to do"). Joe pushes one level more specific ("what is it that you want to do?"). The client produces the concrete content: starting a business.

The session has moved from "I have this pattern of self-sabotage" to "I want to start a business" in approximately twenty seconds. The concrete content is the material the rest of the session can work with.

## Tone

Direct, slightly impatient, friendly. The impatience is not unkind — it is the impatience of someone who can see the conversation is about to go somewhere unhelpful and wants to redirect it before it does. The locating of the failure in the coach ("too abstract for me") keeps the warmth intact.

The naming of the abstraction can be playful. "It's too abstract for me" said with a small smile lands differently than the same words said as a critique. The smile signals that the abstraction is being met with curiosity, not judgment.
