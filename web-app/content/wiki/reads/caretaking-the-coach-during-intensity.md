# Caretaking the Coach During Intensity

## What Joe Notices

When the client is asked to do something big — let the rage move, take up space, dominate, yell — and they do it small instead. Not because the rage is not there. Because they are managing the coach's experience of them in the moment.

Specific signals:

- The client does the motion at half-volume. The body produces a thin version of the requested move.
- The client wants to do the move silently or internally rather than out loud. "I want to just do it in my head silently."
- The voice has a quality of pleading or convincing rather than asserting. "I feel like now I'm trying to convince you."
- The eyes seek the coach's approval after each attempt. Did that one count? Was that enough?
- The client tracks the coach's reaction more than their own felt state. The internal attention is at the meta-level (how is this landing) rather than at the body-level (what is happening in me).
- A small victim-quality enters the voice. "No, don't tell me I'm a train wreck." The motion has become a request for the coach to confirm rather than an assertion that has no need of confirmation.

The signature is the client doing what they think a person doing-this-work should look like, performed for the coach, rather than the client doing the actual work.

"So there's like a little victim in it, right? There's a little like, no, don't— you're not going to tell me I'm a train wreck. No, this is dominate." -- Joe Hudson, 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage

The client may or may not be aware they are caretaking. Often they are not. The pattern is automatic — the same caretaking they do in every other relationship is being directed at the coach. The work is in the same room as the wound.

## What This Implies

The client is performing the work for the coach's comfort rather than doing the work for themselves. The motion is being shaped at the edge to be small enough that the coach is not bothered, big enough that the coach can see they tried.

Underneath: the same dynamic that produced the original suppression. The client was probably trained, in childhood, that their full expression was too much for whoever was caring for them. The body learned to make the motion only at the level that the caregiver could absorb. The training transfers to every helper-figure including the coach.

Specifically, the caretaking-of-the-coach is doing several jobs at once:

1. **Protecting the coach from the size of the rage.** The client may believe (often without articulating it) that the actual size of their rage would harm the coach. The small version is a courtesy. The client is managing what they think the coach can handle.

2. **Preserving the coaching relationship.** If the rage came out at full size, the relationship might not survive. The client knows this from experience. Every previous relationship has been organized around making sure the size was titrated. The same calibration is operating with the coach.

3. **Avoiding the actual move.** The performed-for-the-coach version is technically compliance with the instruction without the somatic event the instruction was pointing at. The client can report "I tried" without having to undergo the annihilation that the actual try would produce. See [[annihilation-reveals-what-is-real]].

4. **Recreating the wound's structural condition.** In the original wound, the rage could not have a witness who could absorb it. The caretaking-of-the-coach silently confirms the same conditions are operating now. The client is, in the present moment, demonstrating their own thesis that there is no one who can take it.

## What Joe Does

Joe names the caretaking directly. The naming is the intervention. The client cannot continue to caretake the coach while the coach is actively pointing at the caretaking.

The naming is paired with explicit reassurance that the coach can take it. Not as performance — as factual statement. "I'm not asking you to caretake me. I can take it." The instruction restructures the field: the client now has a witness who is explicitly available for the full size of the move, and the caretaking has lost its operational purpose.

"Yeah, that's right." — Joe Hudson, in response to the client saying "I know you can take it too. I don't have to caretake you."

In the source session, Joe also uses the read to push directly into the body. "Look at me in the eyes and say it to me." The eye contact removes the option of doing the move at distance. The client cannot caretake the coach while looking the coach in the face and making the move at the coach. The eye contact forces the relational frame into the room, where it becomes available to be addressed rather than avoided.

The push is also delivered with explicit confidence in the client's capacity: "I am pushing you right now. What makes that the case? Because I fucking know. I know you're capable." The coach is not pushing because the coach is mean or impatient. The coach is pushing because the coach knows what the client can do, and is refusing to participate in the dynamic that says the client cannot.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't accept the caretaking version of the move.** "Great, that's a good start" rewards the performed-small version and confirms the client's read that the coach needs them to be small. The work stops at the surface.

- **Don't push without naming the dynamic.** Just pushing harder ("come on, do it bigger") without naming the caretaking leaves the client trying to comply with the new instruction in the same caretaking mode. They do a slightly bigger small version. The pattern is intact.

- **Don't apologize for pushing.** "I'm sorry, I know this is hard" reactivates the caretaking — now the coach is caretaking the client back, and the dynamic is mutual. The room is back to standard caretaking equilibrium.

- **Don't soften your own presence to make the asking gentler.** The client needs to feel that you can take whatever they bring. A soft, careful coach is unconvincing as a target for the dominating-form. The body needs evidence that the witness is solid.

## Dialogue Examples

From the source session, the explicit naming:

- **Client:** "I want to just do it in my head silently, and I know that's not an option. That's sidestepping. It's, it's my body just wants to rage. It can't even be put into words, you know."
- **Joe:** "It can. Fuck you, I'm not a train wreck."
- **Client:** "Fuck you, I am not a train wreck."
- **Joe:** "Still not dominating."
- **Client:** "I feel like now I'm trying to convince you."
- **Joe:** "That's right. That's— no, you're trying to convince yourself and me and the world. That's not what I'm asking you to do."
- *(Source: 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage)*

The "trying to convince you" is the client's own naming of the caretaking. Joe receives the naming and refuses the frame: not "convince me," just "do it."

Later, after the rage has moved, the client surfaces the relief of having a witness who can take it:

- **Client:** "Oh, I know you can take it too. Yeah, I really appreciate it. I don't have to caretake you."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, yeah, that's right."
- **Client:** "And too many people have to be caretaken."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so we're here with you."
- *(Source: 50 Years of Numbness Dissolved in 15Mins of Rage)*

The "too many people have to be caretaken" is the client recognizing in real time that the pattern is structural in their life — and that the present moment is one of the rare exceptions. The naming of the structural pattern is part of what makes the move portable. The client now has a felt-sense of what it is like to do a big move with a witness who is not asking to be managed.

## Tone

Joe's tone when naming the caretaking is direct but not punishing. The naming is delivered as observation, not as accusation. The client is doing something the body trained them to do; it is not a moral failure; the coach is naming it so it can stop operating in the room.

The push that follows the naming carries more energy. The push is loving but it is not soft. The client needs to feel the coach's actual presence — solid, not fragile, not in need of management. The push delivers this through tone as much as through content.
