# Seeking the Young Help by Having a Problem

## What Joe Notices

In the middle of a coaching session, the person who has been moving forward starts producing a new problem. The new problem is often a variant of the old one ("I don't know how to do it," "I'm confused," "I can't quite get there") and arrives at a moment when the session was about to land or move on. The function of the new problem, on the surface, is to be solved. The function underneath the surface is to keep the helper engaged.

The person is not consciously doing this. They are running a configuration that installed in childhood: there is a problem, I need someone to help me with it, the help is not arriving. The configuration kept itself ready for the help that did not come. As an adult, when the person finds themselves in a relationship with someone who is actually able to help, the configuration fires — and one of the ways it fires is by producing a problem so that the helping can continue.

The problem is real to the person. The mechanism is not cynical. It is the unmet-need amplification of childhood help-seeking arriving in an adult context where the help could land but the person cannot let it. Instead of letting the help land, the person creates another problem to be helped with. The pattern produces what looks like need but functionally pushes the actual help away by keeping the helper in the helping-role rather than letting the relationship be the help.

Signals:

- The person produces a new problem just after a breakthrough or shift, often within the same session
- The new problem is structurally similar to the original but presented as if it were different ("I get it now, but I don't know how to do it long-term")
- The person asks "how" questions repeatedly even after demonstrating they know how (see [[how-question-as-defense-against-having]])
- The person reports not understanding a teaching they have just successfully applied
- The person shifts to a new struggle when the current struggle has just lifted
- The questions begin to have an asking quality that goes beyond the actual content — there is something to be received in the asking itself, not just in the answer
- The person seems to need the coach to keep responding regardless of whether they need information
- There is a quality of grasping in the questions — as if losing the helper's attention would be the loss

The decisive marker: the new problem is functioning to maintain the helping relationship rather than to be solved. If the helper offered to keep listening without solving anything, the problem-producing would slow down. The problem is the entry-fee the person learned to pay for being attended to.

## What This Implies

The configuration installed in an environment where the help the child needed did not arrive. The help required was specific — not just feeding and shelter, but the kind of help that a young child needs from a caregiver: attunement, presence, being seen, being soothed. The help did not come, or did not come in the form the child needed. The child organized around the unmet need.

"That's something to know, that because you never got the help that you really needed, there's this very young help that you're still looking for that nobody can give you. Because it's the kind of help you give to a young person, not a young adult person or an adult person." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The crucial point: the help being sought is *young help*. It is the help a small child needs from a parent. No adult can give this help to another adult, because the help is structurally for a child. The adult who is seeking it from other adults is seeking something that cannot be provided by the people they are seeking it from.

The result is the *amplification* of the help-seeking. Every adult relationship that approximates the original parental relationship triggers the configuration. The configuration tries to extract the young help from the adult relationship. The adult relationship cannot provide it (because no adult relationship can). The configuration interprets the non-arrival of the young help as the same non-arrival from childhood and amplifies. The person ends up seeking ever-more-intensely from the very people who are most attuned to them.

"It gets amplified in other relationships, which ends up being a golden algorithm for this guy, which means that like grasping for the attention is often going to push it away. Instead of him trusting that he is awesome, amazing, that people are enamored with him, instead of trusting all that stuff, he's going to keep on like hunting for that, seeking for that validation for that, particularly by having a problem that he — somebody can help him solve, to be cared for in that way." — Joe Hudson, *"This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown*

The "golden algorithm" phrasing matters. The configuration is self-perpetuating: the grasping pushes away what the person most wants, which produces the felt-confirmation that the help is not arriving, which intensifies the grasping. The cycle does not break by getting more help (because the help being sought is structurally unavailable) and does not break by getting less help (because then the configuration intensifies further). The cycle breaks by recognizing what the configuration is and beginning to receive the love and attention that actually is available, in the form it is available, from a self-loving foundation.

## What Joe Does

**Name the pattern without making it the person's fault.** Joe surfaces what is happening structurally: the person is seeking a kind of help that no adult can provide, and the seeking is amplified by the unmet original need. The naming is delivered as information about how the configuration operates, not as criticism. The person needs to see the structure to begin operating outside of it.

**Locate the help-need in the body and treat it with self-love.** The young help the person is seeking can be partially provided by the person themselves, in the form Joe calls [[love-the-worst-feeling-part-like-your-parents-should-have|loving the worst-feeling part the way you wanted love from your parents]]. The person becomes the source of the parental-template love. The young help that the adult cannot provide can be provided by the adult to the part of themselves that still needs it.

**Refuse the new problem when it is the pattern firing.** When a person who has just had a breakthrough produces a new problem, Joe will sometimes decline to solve it. "I'm not going to buy that you don't know how to do it. You already did it." See [[how-question-as-defense-against-having]]. The refusal of the new problem is the interruption of the configuration's mechanism. The person now has to encounter the discomfort of the help-seeking without the help-seeking being rewarded with helping.

**Trust the person's awesomeness over the person's problem.** A specific move: when the person produces a new problem, Joe sometimes responds not to the problem but to the person's actual capacity. "You've got it. I see that you've got it." The response refuses the premise of the problem (that the person needs more help) and offers the alternative the configuration has been preventing: trust that the person already has what they need. The trust is itself a form of the relating the person has been seeking, delivered in a way the configuration cannot convert into the next round of help-seeking.

**Make the configuration visible as a feature of the self-reliant pattern.** Joe names this as one of the predictable features of the self-reliance configuration. The person is not unique for having it. Naming it as a common configuration normalizes the work and reduces the shame that often comes with seeing it.

## What Joe Does NOT Do

He does not feed the configuration by providing more and more help. Continuing to solve each new problem reinforces the configuration. The configuration learns: produce a problem, get attended to, produce another problem. The pattern intensifies under help.

He does not shame the help-seeking. The help-seeking is the form the unmet childhood need has had to take. Shaming it would produce more shame on top of an already-overloaded system. The person is being honored as someone who needed help they did not get, not criticized for asking for it now.

He does not interpret the new problem as evidence the previous work did not land. The previous work landed. The new problem is the configuration firing, not the work failing. Treating it as evidence the work failed would produce another round of "you need more help" that reinforces the configuration.

## The Coach as the Object of the Pattern

This read is particularly relevant in the coaching relationship because the coach is the contemporary analog of the parental figure the configuration formed around. The person is not just abstractly seeking help; they are specifically seeking it from the coach in the way they once sought it from a parent. The coach has to be aware of this projection in order to coach effectively.

If the coach receives the help-seeking at face value and tries to satisfy it, the coach is in the structural position of the parent who could not provide what the child needed. The coach will fail (because the help being sought is structurally unavailable) and the failure will be experienced as confirmation of the original non-arrival.

If the coach refuses the help-seeking entirely, the coach is in the structural position of the rejecting parent who would not engage at all. This will also be experienced as confirmation of the original non-arrival.

The coach has to do something more precise: receive the seeking, name the structure, redirect to what is actually available. The available thing is the relationship itself — present, attentive, not asked to provide the impossible help. The presence is the medicine. The help-as-information is sometimes incidental.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session — the 18-year-old, after a successful loving move, asks "how do I do this?" Joe's response and commentary:

- **Person (after demonstrating the loving):** "I always — I mean, I tried to do it earlier, but I've always had confusion when you said love, how to do that. I heard welcome. I don't know how to do it though."
- **Joe:** "You did it."
- **Person:** "I did it? So literally just put my attention there?"
- **Joe:** "No, and then you loved it. You didn't ask me how to do it. You didn't ask me how to do it the first time, so I'm not gonna buy that you don't know how to do it."
- **Joe (in commentary):** "So there, this is like the beginning of — it's been there a little bit, but there is like an attention-seeking thing going on here as well. Which is like, I'm going to create a problem so that we get to continue to be in this relationship. Like, which is usually the unresolved thing, which is there's a problem, I need to take care of it myself. I want my parents' help. I've been wanting my parents' help for a long time, or caregiver, whoever it is, but I'm not going to get it. And so there's a way in which that itch never got satisfied. So it gets amplified in other relationships, which ends up being a golden algorithm for this guy, which means that like grasping for the attention is often going to push it away."
- *(Source: "This Is How You Love Yourself" Coaching Session Breakdown)*

The structural point: Joe is treating the new "how do I do this?" question as the configuration firing, not as a genuine question. He refuses the question. The refusal is the interruption of the pattern. He pairs the refusal with the explicit recognition that the person has the capacity already ("you did it"). Both moves together — the refusal of the problem and the affirmation of the existing capacity — are the corrective.

## Tone

Warm, structural, kind. The configuration is treated as a recognizable feature of someone who needed help they did not receive, not as a manipulation. Joe's voice carries the recognition that the person is doing the best they can with the configuration they have. The naming is done with the protective shame turned off — the person is not being caught at something but rather being shown a structure they have been operating inside.

The refusal of the new problem is delivered firmly but kindly. "I'm not gonna buy that you don't know how to do it." The firmness is necessary to interrupt the configuration. The kindness is necessary to prevent the configuration from reading the firmness as the parent's rejection it was originally trying to circumvent.
