# Treating It As a Decision When It's an Avoided Emotion

## What Joe Notices

The person presents a fork. Two paths, neither obviously correct, with the request: help me figure out how to choose. The framing feels reasonable. It would be normal to spend the next 45 minutes laying out the considerations on each side.

What Joe notices is the structure underneath the framing.

- The person has been "sitting in the question" for months or years. The deliberation has not converged. Real decisions made by humans converge.
- Both options have been described in roughly symmetric terms. The person is presenting two paths as if they were genuinely comparable rather than as if one obviously called.
- The energy is intellectual. The person is in the head, comparing trajectories, asking how to weigh them. The body is not in the room with the choice.
- The question being asked is one the person has clearly asked themselves many times. The presentation has the polish of rehearsal.
- The verb is "choose" or "decide." Not "I'm scared to act on what I know" or "I can't bring myself to do what wants to be done." The framing puts the difficulty in the choosing rather than in the doing.
- The person frames their inability to choose as a problem to be solved by better thinking. They have not entertained that the inability to choose might be information rather than dysfunction.
- A fear has been named on one side ("I don't want to burn out again") but no equivalent fear has been named on the other side. Or both sides have fears named but they have not been put next to each other.

The read names what is actually happening: the decision-frame is doing the work of avoiding the emotion that would resolve the situation if it were felt. The person is using the fork as a place to live so they do not have to feel what would arrive if they actually moved on either path.

## What This Implies

A real decision is a moment, not a residence. People who actually decide do so quickly because the deciding mechanism (the emotional center) has either landed on an answer or has not. If the emotional center has landed, the person knows what to do. If the emotional center has not landed, the person is sitting with the not-knowing rather than the deciding.

The person who has been "deciding" for months is doing something other than deciding. The intellect is being asked to do what only the emotional substrate can do. See [[decisions-are-emotional]]. The intellect has no way to produce an answer because the postulates of the analysis are emotional and the emotions are being avoided rather than felt.

Specifically, when both options are scary in symmetric ways, the symmetry is usually itself a sign that the same avoided emotion is showing up on both sides in different costumes. The person reads the two options as different paths. From inside the body, the two options are the same path, both leading toward the feeling that needs to be felt. The decision-frame is the workaround that keeps the feeling deferred no matter which path is picked.

This is a relational read as well. The person is enrolling the coach in the deliberation. The coach is being asked to play a particular role: thoughtful adjudicator of pros and cons. If the coach plays that role, the avoidance is reinforced. The coach has joined the strategy of treating the emotion as a thing to be navigated around through smart choosing.

## What Joe Does

The move is to refuse the decision-frame and surface the avoided emotion. Joe asks [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]] on each side of the fork. The question is asked the same way on both sides. The symmetry of the asking surfaces the symmetry of the answers.

When the avoided emotion turns out to be the same on both sides (or close to the same), Joe names it. "How does burnout not do the same thing? If you burn out on your career, how does that not also equal having a long time not doing something?" The question is rhetorical but is asked as if it were genuine. The person, looking at it, recognizes that the two fears are one fear.

Once the symmetry is visible, Joe reframes the question. The actual question is not which path. The actual question is how to do anything at all without running from the feeling. "Maybe you're asking the wrong question." The reframe pulls the work out of the fork and into the relationship with the avoided emotion.

What Joe does NOT do: he does not adjudicate the pros and cons of the two paths. He does not help the person make a better list. He does not offer his own opinion about which option seems wiser. Any of those moves would be playing the role the person enrolled the coach in. The coaching is the refusal to play that role.

## Tone

Direct, ground-level, warm. The redirect is not a critique. It is a turn. Joe is not telling the person they are wrong to be considering the choice. He is offering a different question that turns out to be the actual question.

The tone has the quality of someone gently moving a conversation toward what is actually live. Not "stop doing that" but "let's go over here, where the thing actually is." The person usually feels relief when the redirect lands, because the deliberation itself has been exhausting and the new question is closer to where the body has been all along.

## Dialogue Example

- **Person:** "I had a terrible burnout for the last year, and I decided to quit my job and have several months to rest. And right now is the moment when I already feel the energy to start something new, to proceed on my career. But the problem -- not a problem, but the situation is that I am choosing between two paths. Like, my husband and I, we are thinking about having a child, just thinking about it. So I don't know how to combine these two things... So I'm choosing and I don't know how to choose because I just feel confused of this situation and I'm seeking of some guidance. How do I make this decision? Should I make it at all?"
- **Joe:** "What's -- what if you didn't make the decision? What would that look like? You just said, what if I don't make it at all? How would that look?"
- **Person:** "I will just go with the flow, like let something happen to me. But this seems to me kind of irresponsible..."
- **Joe:** "What's the emotion you're trying to avoid? We have this course called the Decisions Course, and one of the things that we teach in that is that big decisions are usually just avoided emotions. We make decisions in the emotional part of our brain. If I took the emotional part of your brain out, you would take 4 hours to decide where to have lunch, but your IQ would be the same, neurologically speaking. So what's the emotion that you're trying to avoid in both of these two decisions?"

*(Source: How Do I Not Burn Out Again?)*

Joe's first move ("what if you didn't make the decision?") is itself part of the read in action. By asking what not-deciding would look like, he gives the person a chance to notice that not-deciding is felt as irresponsible. The "irresponsible" framing reveals that the decision is functioning as a way to feel responsible. The fork is doing identity work, not choice work.

The second move is the question that opens the avoided emotion. He does not work the decision. He goes underneath it.

## The Relationship-Decision Variant

A specific form of this read fires when the binary is "should I stay with this partner or leave?" — and the relationship has gone heavy under sustained caretaking of the partner's emotional state. The decision-frame covers the caretaking dynamic. The person is asking how to decide about the relationship when the actual work is the caretaking pattern that has made the relationship dead in its current configuration.

The structural signals for this variant: the person describes the partner's emotional state extensively (depression, anger, shame, mental health), describes themselves as the one managing that state, reports more aliveness during a recent breakup or separation than during the relationship, and frames the question as "do I want to be with this person?" rather than "what's happening between us?"

The reframe move is [[redirect-from-stay-or-leave-to-self-love]]: the work is not the decision; the work is self-love, and under conditions of self-love the decision loses its weight. The diagnostic question is [[what-would-it-take-for-you-to-not-take-care-of-them]]: surface the caretaking by asking the person to imagine being with the partner without managing them.

See [[decision-question-covers-the-caretaking]] for the dedicated read for this variant, [[should-i-stay-with-my-ex]] for the concern, and [[qa-trial-separation-client]] for the canonical example.

## The Habit-Change Variant

A specific form of this read fires when the person presents a binary about a habit they cannot stop ("should I keep drinking or stop?", "should I keep eating sugar or stop?", "should I keep scrolling or quit?"). The binary often arrives with mild urgency or self-frustration but rarely with crisis. The framing is "fun in the moment vs. consequences later" — a present-future tradeoff.

The structural signal: the person has been carrying the question for a while, has cycled through stop-and-restart attempts, and has the question polished into a clean binary. The drinking case has the cleanest form because alcohol is socially calibrated as moderable; the binary "stop completely vs. drink a little" stays open indefinitely because both options are defensible.

What is being avoided in this variant is usually a [[blocker-emotions]] — shame, guilt, judgment — that the habit is providing relief from. The reframe move is [[reframe-the-question-to-the-blocker-emotion]]: "what if the question was, should I give up shame and guilt or not?" The reframe collapses the binary into the actual question, which is about the relationship with the avoided emotion, not about the substance.

See [[should-i-stop-drinking]] for the canonical concern, [[heart-in-a-box-want-to-drink]] for the specific access-mechanism the substance is often providing, and [[hidden-rebellion-under-the-habit]] for the second layer (the rebellion against the inner should that is keeping the habit alive against the conscious attempt to stop it).

## The Should-I-Be-In-A-Relationship-At-All Variant

A specific form of this read fires when the person is single (often post-divorce, post-breakup, post-long-period-alone) and presenting a binary about whether to be in a relationship at all. The framing: "I want to but it's too much work / I don't have time / it'll take away from the other things I want to do." Both voices have been running on each other for months or years. The deliberation has not converged.

The structural signature for this variant: the person describes both an internal "for" voice and an "against" voice, but only the against voice has been fully elaborated. The for voice has been mentioned and then dismissed. The decision-frame is doing the work of letting the dismissal continue — as long as the question is being asked, the dismissive voice gets to keep dismissing.

What is being avoided in this variant is usually [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]] in its recursive form. The "other" being managed is the inner voice that wants the relationship. The "manager" is the voice that says it's too much work. The pattern is also often active in the present in a difficult ex relationship or co-parenting situation, which generates the endurance-frame the person is then projecting onto future relationships.

The reframe move is [[reframe-from-whether-to-how]]: convert the binary about whether to be in a relationship into the configuration question about what kind of relationship would not feel like endurance. The supporting move is [[how-do-you-abandon-yourself]]: surface the active self-abandonment with both the ex and the inner voice. The recursive recognition that surfaces is [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]]: the asking of the should/shouldn't question is the abandonment in real time.

See [[should-i-be-in-a-relationship-at-all]] for the dedicated concern, [[relationships-as-test-of-endurance]] for the read about the endurance frame this variant almost always carries, and [[use-your-strength-on-the-real-work]] for the close that often lands the session.

## What This Read Is Not

This is not the same as the person being genuinely undecided about a real choice. Real undecidedness is also a thing. The diagnostic for genuine undecidedness is that the person can usually feel the not-knowing as alive, can describe what each path would feel like in the body, and is not stuck in a loop. The person operating in the decision-as-avoidance pattern is producing the same conversation about the choice repeatedly, has done so for months or years, and the body is absent from the deliberation.

It is also not [[performing-growth]]. The decision-frame can be performed (a person trying to look thoughtful by deliberating), but the read here is structural rather than performative. The decision is functioning as a maneuver regardless of whether the person knows it is.
