# Deflection Onto Others' Needs

## What Joe Notices

The person, asked what they want or need, redirects to what someone else wants or needs. The someone-else is usually a person the speaker loves and is responsible for: their kids, their partner, their team, their parents.

The redirect can be subtle or overt:

- "I'm not really worried about me. I'm worried about my kids not getting their needs met."
- "I'm fine. My partner is the one going through a hard time."
- "I don't really have needs. I just want to make sure my team is okay."
- When asked "what do *you* want," the answer arrives in the form of what would benefit someone else
- The person describes their own life almost entirely in terms of caretaking responsibilities, with no mention of personal want, desire, or longing
- When pressed, the person says their own needs are met — and then cannot name a specific need that has been met or how
- The signature follow-up: when the direct question is repeated ("but what about *you*"), the person shuts down rather than answering

A diagnostic test: ask the person what they would want for themselves if they had a free day with no responsibilities. The deflection-onto-others read is confirmed if the answer is some version of doing something for someone else, or going blank, or pivoting to "I don't even know."

## What This Implies

The deflection is not generosity. It is a learned strategy for handling the felt impossibility of having one's own needs. The structural belief: *my needs are not okay to have / will not be met / are dangerous to name*. The strategy: route attention to others' needs, take care of them, and hope that one's own needs will be met by proxy when the others are taken care of.

"Which is another way of trying to take care of your needs. So if my needs directly aren't safe, aren't welcome, aren't good, then if I take care of yours, then by proxy, my needs might get taken care of." — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen* (Brett naming the mechanism, which Joe confirms)

Joe's response is precise: "Right. Except for it never works, right? Or eventually doesn't work." The proxy strategy fails because the other person cannot reliably read what is being asked for sideways. The wife caring for the kids in the hope that someone will eventually notice she has needs will not get her needs met unless someone explicitly asks her. The asking is the thing she has been avoiding for the same reason she is using the proxy strategy.

This read is structurally the same operation as [[im-scared-to-be-seen]] — both are forms of the shame about wanting to be seen / wanting one's needs met. See [[two-layers-of-shame-about-being-seen]]: the wanting itself feels shameful, so the wanting gets routed through caring for someone else's wanting, which feels permissible.

The read is also closely related to [[caretaking]] and the [[care-vs-caretaking]] distinction. Caretaking is care performed in service of a felt internal contraction; the strategy is to manage one's own state by managing the other's. The deflection-onto-needs is one of caretaking's specific operational forms.

## What Joe Does

Three moves, often in sequence:

1. **Ask directly about the person's own needs.** Despite the deflection, ask. The asking itself can be the intervention. "What about you? Are your emotional needs being met?" The directness is required because anything indirect will be re-routed by the deflection-strategy.

2. **Stay with the shutdown when it comes.** The person often shuts down when asked directly. The shutdown is data. Joe does not push past it; he names it gently and lets the system have what it needs. The work of opening the shutdown is slower than one conversation. See [[exposure-as-cure-for-shame-around-being-seen]] for the practice that addresses the shutdown over time.

3. **Name the equivalence between deflection-onto-others and shame-about-own-needs.** Make explicit that caring excessively about others' needs is the same operation as believing your own are not okay. This is not delivered as criticism; it is delivered as a structural observation. The naming, when it lands, gives the person a different way to see what they have been doing and a different door to walk through.

The follow-up question that begins the redirect: *what do you need? what is yours?* — see [[what-do-i-really-need-right-now]] for the question form Joe uses across multiple presenting concerns. The redirect from others-orientation to own-need is one of the core operations Joe runs across many configurations of caretaking, people-pleasing, and fear-of-being-seen.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I'm not really worried about me. I'm worried about my kids not getting their needs met."
- **Joe:** "Really? Emotional needs being met?"
- **Person:** "No." [shuts down]
- *(Source: Fear of Being Seen, dinner with husband-wife team)*

The exchange compresses the entire read. The deflection (worried about kids), the direct question (what about you), the body's truth (no), and the shutdown that follows. The shutdown itself is the diagnostic — the person knows the answer is no, and the not-okay-ness of having that answer produces the shutdown rather than the further exploration.

See [[the-hidden-wife-at-dinner]] for the fuller version of this example.

## Tone

Gentle, direct, not-pushing. The read is named without urgency. The person who has been deflecting onto others' needs for years cannot suddenly admit their own; the coach is not trying to force the admission. The work is to introduce the question, let it land or not land in the current moment, and trust the slow work to do its part.

The tone is also not therapeutic-grave. Joe sometimes laughs about it, and the person can laugh too — recognition can be both serious and light. The lightness keeps the moment from becoming another performance of seriousness about being-seen-work, which would itself be the pattern playing out.
