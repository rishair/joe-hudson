# Purpose as Approval-Seeking in Disguise

## What This Read Notices

The person presents the purpose question. They want help finding their purpose. They describe their search. They want a clear answer. But underneath the question, the structure is approval-seeking wearing purpose-language. What they actually want is to know what they can be approved for, recognized for, told they are doing well at.

Joe names this directly:

"Oftentimes people confuse this. Oh, I have a sense of purpose. What they're really asking is, what can I be acknowledged for?" -- Joe Hudson, The Myth Of Finding Your Purpose

The person believes they are asking about purpose. They are asking about approval. The two questions feel similar from the inside; they are structurally different. Purpose is sourced from within. Approval is sourced from without. A question that is structurally approval-seeking cannot find purpose, no matter how much purpose-language it wears.

## The Detection Cues

Signals that the purpose-question is actually an approval-question:

- **"How will I know it's my purpose?"** — the question assumes external validation will arrive to confirm the purpose. The framing of recognition is built in.
- **"I want to do something meaningful"** — meaningful to whom? If the answer is "meaningful to others/the world/society," approval is in the structure.
- **"I want to make a difference"** — for whom? If the desire is to be seen as someone who makes a difference, that is approval-seeking.
- **The person asks for examples of other people's purposes** — they are looking for templates to be matched against, which is a form of being-told.
- **The person describes wanting a "clear sign"** — a sign from outside, from the universe, from a mentor, from circumstances. The wanting-to-be-told is built into the request.
- **The person rejects answers that do not have prestige** — if the purpose-answer would not be impressive, it does not count. The prestige-filter is the approval-filter.
- **The person already mentions audience while describing their purpose** — "I want to be the kind of person who..." with "the kind of person" being a category that gets approved.

When these are present, the question being asked is not the question the person thinks they are asking.

## What This Implies

The person is in the [[approval-seeking-from-authority]] pattern (see [[the-four-authority-positions]]) but has framed the dynamic as a purpose-question rather than an authority-question. The coach has been positioned as the authority who will validate the purpose. Answering the question — generating possible purposes for the person — would feed the approval-loop.

The deeper dynamic: the person believes their value depends on being recognized for something. The search for purpose is the search for the form of recognition. The form does not matter; the recognition does. Until the recognition-seeking is named, the person will keep restructuring the search, looking for a better form, never satisfied because no form delivers the recognition they actually need.

The recognition they actually need cannot be given by others. It is self-recognition — the for-yourself orientation. See [[do-it-for-yourself-to-find-purpose]].

## What Joe Does With This Read

The move is not to give the person their purpose. Doing so feeds the loop. The moves:

1. **Name the underlying question.** "I think what you're actually asking is: what can I be acknowledged for? Is that closer?" The naming is the work. The person often immediately recognizes the truth of it. Once named, the question can be addressed for what it actually is.

2. **Refuse to play the authority.** Do not generate possible purposes. Do not suggest paths. Do not validate the search frame. Each of these positions you as the authority who can grant the recognition the person is seeking. Refusing the role is the move.

3. **Turn the question inward.** "What feels more in your purpose right now?" — see [[what-feels-more-in-purpose-right-now]]. The shift from external answer to internal felt-sense reverses the approval-extraction structure.

4. **Pivot to the for-yourself orientation.** Once the approval-seeking is named, the alternative orientation can be offered: what would it be like to do the thing for yourself rather than for the approval? See [[do-it-for-yourself-to-find-purpose]].

5. **Name the purpose-is-how reframe.** If the person is searching for the right what, the search itself is built on the approval-frame (specific activities are recognizable). The shift to how-of-purpose collapses the search. See [[purpose-is-how-not-what]].

## Dialogue Shape

A compressed version of how this often goes:

- **Person:** I just want to find my purpose. I've been trying everything and nothing feels like it.
- **Joe:** What is it about a purpose that you want? What would having one give you?
- **Person:** I would feel like I was doing the right thing. Like I was on the right path.
- **Joe:** And how would you know you were doing the right thing?
- **Person:** [pause] I guess... it would feel good. I would feel acknowledged for it.
- **Joe:** Yeah. So what you might actually be asking is: what can I be acknowledged for? Not: what is my purpose. The acknowledgment-question is real, but it's a different question than the purpose-question.

The naming opens the actual question. The actual question is then workable. The purpose-question, while it was being held as the question, was unworkable because it was the wrong question.

## Tone

Direct, warm, slightly amused. Not accusatory. The mis-framing is common and not a personal failure. The person is doing what most people do with the purpose-question. The naming is offered as a structural correction, not as a criticism of the person. The lightness lets the person see the structure without defending against it.

When the person resists the naming, the tone stays warm. The resistance is not the problem. The person is feeling the implication that they have been asking the wrong question for a long time. That is hard to receive. Stay with it. The recognition lands eventually, often quietly.

## The Framework-Fit Sub-Variant

A specific sub-form of this read is when the person is not asking the coach for the approval — they are asking a framework. They have encountered StrengthsFinder, Zone of Genius, Wealth Dynamics, Human Design, Enneagram, or another classification system, and are trying to fit themselves into its categories. The framework-fitting feels different from approval-seeking because no specific person is being asked, but the structure is identical: the validating-label is external, the question is "which of these labels am I?", and the search-energy is contortionist.

This sub-variant gets its own read at [[contorting-into-someone-elses-framework]]. The diagnostic question is "from a business perspective" or similar contextual marker — the person specifies that they need a framework-shaped answer, not a felt-shaped one. The full coaching path involves the same moves (refuse to play authority, name the underlying pattern, redirect inward), with the additional move of naming the framework specifically as foreign and offering the apology to self for the contortion. See also [[apologize-for-contorting-into-frameworks]].
