# Walking Away From the Fight Feeling Bad About Yourself

## What Joe Notices

The coachee describes the recurring fight, and the pattern of how they feel afterward. The felt-residue is reliable. They walk away feeling small, ashamed, depleted, or unsure of themselves. They may have technically "won" the fight (the partner conceded, dropped it, walked off), and they still feel bad.

The diagnostic claim Joe makes:

"Nobody walks away from a fight feeling like, oh wow, I feel great about myself." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The reverse:

"If a fight starts and you go, 'oh, I've done nothing wrong,' and you can see yourself clearly and listen to the other person, you actually walk away feeling great being in a fight." -- Joe Hudson, How To Overcome Shame In Conflict

The felt-residue is the read. A fight that ran on shame leaves both people worse, regardless of who "won." A fight that ran without shame leaves both people more themselves, regardless of how intense it was.

## What Joe Hears Underneath

When the coachee describes feeling bad after fights, several things are likely happening that need separating:

- **Shame was being passed during the fight.** The hot potato was running. Both people are now holding shame. The felt-residue is the shame that did not get acknowledged and metabolized.
- **The coachee was defending.** Defense requires partial agreement with the accusation. The post-fight bad feeling is partly the residue of agreeing with the accusation that was being defended against.
- **The fight was not actually about what it was about.** The content was a substitute for some other unresolved material. The coachee walks away with the unresolved material still unresolved, plus the additional pain of the fight.
- **The coachee betrayed themselves during the fight.** They said things they did not mean, took stances they did not believe, performed certainty they did not have. The post-fight bad feeling includes the felt-cost of self-betrayal.

The post-fight bad feeling is the body's report on all of these simultaneously. The read is not "you are a bad fighter." The read is "the fight was running on shame, and you were inside the loop, and the loop is what produces this felt-residue."

## What This Implies

The coachee is in a fight pattern that is running on the [[shame-hot-potato]] mechanism. The defenses they fire are the throws of the potato. The defenses fire because they want to be seen, and they think defending is the way to be seen. The post-fight bad feeling is the recurring body-report that the strategy is not working.

The coachee likely believes that the bad feeling is just the nature of fighting. "Nobody likes fighting." "Fights are uncomfortable." "That's how it is." This belief is what keeps the loop alive — it normalizes the felt-residue rather than reading it as a diagnostic.

The reframe: the felt-residue is not the nature of fighting. It is the nature of fighting from shame. Fighting from a place of inherent goodness, with the urgency to be seen released, leaves a different felt-residue entirely — Joe's claim is that it actually feels good. See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]].

## What Joe Does

Three moves are typically available depending on what the coachee is ready for.

**Move 1: Name the read directly.** "When you describe how you feel after the fights — that small, ashamed, depleted feeling — that is information. The fight is running on shame, and you are inside the loop. The bad feeling is the body telling you the loop is running." The naming alone often produces relief because it locates the suffering in a structure rather than in the coachee's identity.

**Move 2: Offer the reverse case as an aspiration.** "There is a way of being in a fight where you walk away feeling more yourself, not less. It is available. Most people have not experienced it because they have never been in a fight without being in shame." This opens the possibility that fighting could be different, which is often a new thought.

**Move 3: Locate the entry point.** Once the read is named and the reverse case is on the table, the question becomes which specific intervention to start with. Usually one of:
- [[i-dont-need-to-be-seen-right-now]] — for the mid-fight urgency that drives the defense
- [[drop-the-hot-potato]] — for the broader frame of the shame-game
- [[non-defense-is-the-ocean]] — for the somatic stance underneath
- [[see-yourself-when-they-cant-see-you]] — for the case where the partner cannot do the corresponding work
- [[inherent-goodness]] — for the foundational recognition the work rests on

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "We had another fight last night. I keep ending up in this place where I feel terrible about myself afterward."
- **Joe:** "Notice that. That feeling — that you keep ending up there — is the data. Nobody walks away from a fight feeling good about themselves when shame is running. The bad feeling is the body's report on what was happening, not a verdict on you."

- **Person:** "But I was right. I made my case and he eventually agreed."
- **Joe:** "And still you walked away feeling bad. So the winning didn't deliver what you thought it would. What was it actually like in the fight? Were you defending?"
- **Person:** "Yeah, I guess I was. I felt like he didn't see what I was actually saying."
- **Joe:** "Right. The wanting to be seen was firing the defense. The defense was the throw of the shame. You can win the fight and still walk away with shame because the winning was on the wrong axis."

- *(Source: How To Overcome Shame In Conflict, drawing on the framing of the post-fight diagnostic.)*

## Why This Read Matters

The read is one of the most accessible entry points for the broader shame-in-conflict work. Most people who have recurring fights have never thought of the post-fight feeling as diagnostic. They think it is just how fighting is. Once the felt-residue is reframed as data, a whole structure becomes visible:

- The bad feeling is shame.
- The shame is from the loop.
- The loop is run by defenses.
- The defenses are driven by the urgency to be seen.
- The urgency can be released.
- The defenses can stop firing.
- The shame can stop being passed.
- The post-fight feeling can change.

The entire shame-in-conflict curriculum unfolds from the one observation about how the fights feel afterward.

## Tone

Joe delivers this read with a kind of friendly precision. The bad feeling is not made into a moral failing or a sign that the coachee is broken. It is treated as ordinary information about a structural pattern. The tone is "you have been getting useful data this whole time and reading it as something else." There is often relief in the coachee when this lands — they have permission to take the felt-residue seriously, and they have a frame for what the residue means.
