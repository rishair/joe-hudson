# Complicating It Is the Distraction

## What Joe Notices

The person presents a simple structural problem and immediately makes it complicated. The complication can take many forms: a story about why the problem is hard, a multi-layered psychological analysis of why they keep doing what they do, a meta-frame in which the real problem is figuring out what's wrong with them. The shape is consistent: every move toward the simple action of solving the problem produces a new layer of analysis that postpones the action.

When asked directly "what's wrong with the question 'how do I start my business?' — what makes it more complicated than that?" — the person defends the complication. The defense is the diagnostic. The person believes the complication is legitimate. They have not noticed that the complicating itself is the distraction.

Specific signals:

- When the problem is simplified ("you want to do X — what's wrong with just doing X?"), the person introduces a new layer ("but I get in my own way" / "but I have a hard time stepping into the identity" / "but there's something deeper going on").
- The person frames "figuring out what's wrong with me" as a precondition for action.
- "Big-ass thing I have to solve before I can start" — the person has constructed a meta-problem (their psychology) that has to be resolved before the actual problem (the action) can be addressed.
- The complication is sophisticated enough that engaging with it would take significant time, conveniently delaying the action.
- The person uses self-help vocabulary fluently — distraction, avoidance, self-sabotage, pattern, identity — and the fluency is the form the complication takes.
- The "but" or "yeah, but" arrives every time the conversation moves toward the simple action.
- The person's energy goes up when discussing what's wrong with them and goes down when discussing what they would actually do.

A clean diagnostic question: ask the person to imagine the simple action happening, then notice what their mind generates. If the mind immediately generates a new obstacle, a new layer to figure out, a new pattern to address — the complicating is operating.

## What This Implies

The complicating defense has a specific architecture. The person has noticed they are avoiding the thing they want to do. The avoidance has produced uncomfortable feelings. To manage the uncomfortable feelings, the person has created a new project: figure out why they are avoiding. The new project is presented as the route to the original goal, but it is in fact a more sophisticated avoidance of the original goal.

The structure recurses. If the new project (figure out why I'm avoiding) is named as also avoidance, the person can create a new new project (figure out why I keep doing this meta-thing). The recursion can go indefinitely. Each level produces a feeling of work being done while the action remains untaken.

This is the read that surfaces specifically in clients who have done significant self-development work. The work has installed the apparatus that the complicating defense now uses. The vocabulary of patterns, defenses, and avoidances becomes the medium through which the new layers of complication get constructed.

The complicating is often experienced by the person as productive struggle. They believe they are getting somewhere. The somewhere they are getting is one more iteration of the loop that has been running for years. The feeling of progress is the loop's payoff.

Underneath the complicating is usually a simple unfamiliar action that would require the person to become someone different. The complicating preserves the current identity by ensuring the unfamiliar action does not get taken. See [[i-keep-getting-in-my-own-way]] for the broader concern this read serves.

## What Joe Does

The primary move is to name the complicating directly. Not as criticism. As observation. The naming converts the invisible defense into a visible move the person can now see themselves making.

"Even the psychological 'what's wrong with me, I'm distracting myself' — all that is also a way to not start your business." -- Joe Hudson, Getting Out of Your Own Way

The naming has a specific shape. It includes both the obvious distraction (the comfort-work the person does instead of the wanted-work) and the less obvious distraction (the psychological analysis of why they avoid). The combined naming exposes the whole architecture as the avoidance, not just the surface part.

Joe often makes this move twice in a session. The first time, he names the comfort-work as the distraction. The second time, after the person has tried to engage with the question "why do I do this?", he names the why-question itself as another distraction. The two namings together cover the architecture's two main layers.

A specific form Joe uses: "And it sounds like one of the things you do is you make things complicated as a way to distract yourself." This form names the complicating as a habit, not as a one-time event. The naming gives the person a way to recognize the complicating when it fires in the future. The recognition is operational — the person can notice themselves complicating in real time and choose differently.

When the person, after the complicating has been named, generates yet another layer ("now it's like, okay, if I can't get to my business, what can I get to before?") — Joe names this too. "So you are now complicating doing your business?" The naming continues for as many iterations as the complicating produces. The work is to keep exposing each new layer as it arrives until the person can see the architecture clearly.

The redirect that follows the naming is always to the simple concrete action. Not to a new layer of analysis. The redirect is structurally simple — "go put out ads," "tell all your friends," "decorate someone's house for free" — and the simplicity is the medicine. The complicating defense cannot operate on simple, immediate, concrete instructions.

## What This Read Is Not

It is not [[intellectualizing-as-the-form-of-control]]. Intellectualization uses conceptual sophistication to outflank felt material. Complicating-as-distraction uses any kind of additional layer — psychological, practical, logistical — to postpone the action. The two reads can co-occur but they are not the same. Intellectualization tends to be conceptual; complicating is operational.

It is not [[in-the-story]]. Story-mode is the narration of past events. Complicating is the construction of additional present-tense problems. Both delay action but they delay differently.

It is not a sign that the person's analysis is wrong. The patterns the person has identified are often accurate. The accuracy is irrelevant. The point is not that the analysis is incorrect; it is that the analysis is being substituted for the action. A perfectly accurate analysis can still be the distraction.

It is not [[abstract-framing-as-defense]] though it often follows it. Abstract framing is the opening posture (presenting the pattern rather than the situation). Complicating is what happens after the specific has been named — the person agrees that yes, the specific thing is the issue, and then immediately produces a new layer of complication around the specific thing. The two reads run sequentially: abstract framing keeps the conversation from arriving at the specific; complicating builds new abstractions around the specific once it has arrived.

## Dialogue Example

The full sequence from the source session:

- **Joe:** "And so the question seems to be like, how do I start my own business?"
- **Client:** "Sort of, yeah."
- **Joe:** "Okay, great. What's wrong with the question, how do I start my own business? What makes it more complicated than that?"
- **Client:** "I don't know. I think it just, it sounds too simple because we're forgetting about the like part about I get in my own way, but maybe that's just a story I continue to tell myself."
- **Joe:** "Isn't, is, is the story that you're telling yourself part of the way you get in your way and distract yourself? Look at this big-ass thing I have to solve before I can start my own business."
- **Client:** "Yeah, yeah, it's constantly, okay, what can I work to solve that I already know how to do instead of facing this other problem that I don't know how to tackle."
- **Joe:** "Right. So even like the psychological, what's wrong with me? What, I'm distracting myself, da da da, all that is also a way to not start your business."

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

In this exchange, Joe makes two distinct namings. First, he names the "I get in my own way" story as itself part of the way the person gets in their way. Second, he names the psychological self-analysis ("what's wrong with me, I'm distracting myself") as itself a way to not start the business. The two namings together expose the architecture's recursive structure.

The client's response between the two namings is telling: "what can I work to solve that I already know how to do instead of facing this other problem that I don't know how to tackle." The client has, in their own words, articulated the underlying mechanism — solve the familiar instead of the unfamiliar. The naming Joe does is helping the client see that even the activity of trying to understand the avoidance is one more instance of solving-the-familiar.

A later iteration in the same session:

- **Client:** "I just feel like the thing with the business, it's exemplary of so many other areas of my life. It's the same fucking story for everything."
- **Joe:** "So you are now complicating doing your business?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, because now it's okay if I can't get to my business. Oh, what can I get to before?"

*(Source: Getting Out of Your Own Way)*

Joe catches the complication mid-construction. The client had been working on a feeling in the body and immediately produced a new generalization ("it's exemplary of so many other areas of my life") that would have shifted the conversation away from the specific present-moment work. Joe's "so you are now complicating doing your business?" surfaces the move in real time. The client confirms the diagnosis and articulates exactly what the complicating was buying ("okay if I can't get to my business").

## Tone

Direct, observational, slightly amused. The naming is delivered as a noticing, not as a critique. The "even the psychological 'what's wrong with me'" line is often said with a small smile — the smile signals that Joe finds the loop's sophistication recognizable rather than condemnable.

The "so you are now complicating doing your business?" form is a question, not a statement. The question allows the person to confirm the observation themselves, which preserves their agency. Naming the move as theirs to do or not do, rather than diagnosing them with the move as a condition, is the difference between an opening and a closing.
