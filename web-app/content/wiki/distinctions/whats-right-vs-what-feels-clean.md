# What's Right vs. What Feels Clean

## The Distinction

**What's right** is a moral question. It asks "what is the correct thing to do here according to some standard outside of me?" The standard might be religious, cultural, social, philosophical. The answer locates the action in a framework of right and wrong. The frame is in the head.

**What feels clean** is an embodied question. It asks "what do I need to do or say so that my body feels at rest at the end of the day? What do I need to feel good about, to keep my heart open, to be honest with my interconnectedness?" The answer locates the action in the felt experience of integrity. The frame is in the body.

When the question of what to do is asked in the right/wrong frame, the actual boundary that the body knows becomes inaccessible. When the question is asked in the clean/not-clean frame, the boundary surfaces.

"I think the 'when is it right to' can fuck us from feeling the real boundary. That's my easiest way to fuck myself from finding the boundary, is for me going, 'Oh, is that the right thing?' Because then I'm in, like, right/wrong as opposed to what's my boundary?" -- Tara Howley, I Learned Forgiveness by Refusing to Forgive

## Why the Right Frame Blocks the Boundary

The right-frame organizes the question around an external standard. The standard is universal in form — what is right is the same regardless of the specific person. But the boundary the body needs is specific to the person. The mismatch is structural: a universal frame cannot produce a specific answer.

When a person tries to find their boundary through the right-frame, they end up with one of:

- An abstract answer that does not apply to their actual situation
- An answer that matches what they think they should want rather than what they actually want
- Paralysis because the right answer is unclear or contested
- A boundary that comes out as moralized demand rather than self-care

The clean-frame, by contrast, organizes the question around the felt experience of the person. The body knows what it needs. The clean-frame is the framing that lets the body's knowledge speak.

## The Restated Question

The reframe Tara offers:

"At the end of the day, what can I live with? So it's kind of different than a boundary. It's like, what do I have to feel good about at the end of the day to keep my heart open to myself and others?" -- Tara Howley

The question "what do I have to feel good about at the end of the day" is a different question from "what is the right thing to do." The first surfaces the actual need. The second produces moral analysis.

Alexa's rephrasing:

"How do I know that I'm living my truth in such a way that I feel good and clear and I can occupy the whole space of me?" -- Alexa Anderson

The marker of the clean answer: the body feels at rest. The mind has clarity. The person can occupy their whole space. The right answer, by contrast, often leaves the body still uncertain even after the mind has reached a verdict.

## The Sales Manager Case

Tara's example: someone who had a bad experience with a salesperson and was deciding whether to communicate the warning to the next employer. The right-frame question would be "should I warn them?" — which becomes a moral debate about loyalty, second chances, fairness to the salesperson, fairness to the next employer.

The clean-frame question is "what do I need to do so my body is at rest about this?" The answer that arrived: "I'm going to tell the head of sales. What they choose to do with it is their responsibility. But my system is gonna feel clear if I share it."

The right-frame would have produced an argument that might or might not have resolved. The clean-frame produced a clear, actionable, body-known answer.

## How This Fits With Right and Wrong Falling Away

This distinction is the practical application of [[right-and-wrong-flips-from-head-to-action]]. Joe teaches that as a person's consciousness matures, right and wrong releases its grip at the level of thought and re-emerges as commanding at the level of action. The thought-frame "what is right" stops being useful; the body-frame "what is the action that arises from this state" becomes very precise.

The clean/right distinction is how this looks in the moment. The right-frame is the thought-frame; the clean-frame is the body-frame. Asking what is right keeps the question in the place where it has lost its grip. Asking what is clean moves the question to where it actually has answers.

This is why the distinction matters even for people who are still working with right and wrong in general. In the specific moment of trying to find a boundary, the right-frame is the wrong tool. The clean-frame works regardless of where the person is in their general relationship to moral frames.

## The Stress Test: Does the Right Answer Survive Body-Test?

When in doubt, the test is: state the right answer to yourself and notice what the body does. If the body softens, the right answer and the clean answer are aligned. If the body remains tight, uncertain, or braced, the right answer is not the clean answer — and the clean answer is the one that will actually serve.

Often the right answer is what the person has been told they should do. The clean answer is what their body has been quietly knowing they need to do. The two diverge frequently. When they diverge, the clean answer is the one to follow.

## When the Right Frame Is Useful

The distinction is not "right is bad." The right-frame is useful for thinking through implications, considering ethics, weighing systemic effects. It is the wrong tool for finding a personal boundary because boundaries are about the body's needs, not about universal ethics.

The two frames can work together: use the right-frame to think through what is involved, then use the clean-frame to find what to actually do. The order matters. If the right-frame runs after the clean-frame, it can second-guess the body's knowing into paralysis.

For decisions of personal action (boundaries, what to say, what to do with someone), the clean-frame is primary. For decisions about systemic structures or actions affecting others' lives, the right-frame has more legitimate role. The boundary-question is squarely in the first category.

## The Coach's Move

When a person is stuck trying to figure out what to do and frames the question as "is it right to..." or "should I...", the coach's move is to reframe to the clean question. Forms:

- "What would let your body rest about this?"
- "What do you need to do so your heart stays open here?"
- "What can you live with at the end of the day?"
- "What does your gut say?"
- "If you take should out of it, what do you want to do?"

The reframe often produces a different and clearer answer than the right-frame was producing. The person frequently knew the answer all along; the right-frame was what was blocking them from accessing it.

## The Two Wrong Boundaries

The clean-frame distinguishes two boundaries that the right-frame conflates:

- The boundary the person thinks they should draw (from the right-frame) — often abstract, often performative, often doesn't release the charge
- The boundary the person's body actually needs to draw (from the clean-frame) — specific, embodied, releases the charge when stated

The diagnostic [[resentment-takes-the-place-of-boundaries]] applies here: if the charge has not released, the boundary stated is the right-frame boundary, not the clean-frame boundary. The work is to keep asking what would actually feel clean until the body says yes.

## Tone

Practical. The distinction is offered as observation about how the body responds to differently-framed questions. There is no moral judgment of people who use the right-frame; the point is that the right-frame is the wrong tool for this specific job.
