# Bottom-Up vs. Top-Down Goals (Both Work When the Want Is Real)

## The Two Sides

**Bottom-up goals:** Each team determines its own goals. The CEO's goals are aggregated from what the teams want to accomplish. The alignment is built from the wants of the people doing the work.

**Top-down goals:** The CEO articulates what they want and what the important things are. Everyone else aligns around it. Salesforce is Joe's named example -- the CEO sits down, says "this is what I want, and these are the important things," and then everybody else aligns around it. Within that frame, each team is asked how they participate in a way that works for them.

Both directions can produce well-functioning organizations. The direction is not the deciding factor. The deciding factor is whether the want behind the goal is real somewhere in the system.

"Oftentimes what I see, like in a company, for instance, is goals that are bottom up. This team determines what are the goals of this team, determine all the way up to the top with the CEO's goals. They are often really effective goals because they're built from the wants of people. It's also really effective the other way. Salesforce has this thing. The CEO sits down and says, this is what I want, and these are the important things, and then everybody else can align around it. The alignment is really, really important. The want is also really, really important, and the want has to come from somewhere." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

## The Common Failure Mode

The failure mode is not direction-specific. The failure is when nobody actually has the want.

"When the want isn't there, when everybody's doing it for somebody else, I'm doing it for the board, and then I'm doing it for the CEO, and there's nobody who actually has that want, it's very difficult." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The shape of this failure: the board wants the CEO to want it; the CEO has installed it as a goal because the board wants it; the executives are pursuing it because the CEO has installed it; the operators are executing because the executives told them to. At no point in the chain does an actual want exist. The goal is real in that it has been written down and metrics are being tracked, but nobody is the source of the wanting.

This is the organizational version of [[the-inner-board]]. The voice in the head issuing goals that none of the subsystems actually want. The same dynamic at scale: a goal-setting layer issuing goals that none of the operating subsystems actually want.

## What Bottom-Up Looks Like When It Works

The teams know their own work. They can see what is possible, what is interesting, what is worth pursuing in their domain. When they are given authority to set their own goals, the goals emerge from their actual investment in the work. The wants are real because they belong to the people doing the work.

The aggregation upward then produces a CEO-level goal that is the sum of the wants of the people who will execute it. The alignment is automatic because the goal was built from what everyone already wants. No one is pursuing somebody else's goal.

The failure mode of bottom-up goal-setting: the teams set goals that are too narrow to add up to a coherent company strategy. The teams may also set goals that are too low because the want at the team level may not include the larger ambition. The CEO has to do additional work to integrate the team goals into a coherent direction without overriding what the teams want.

## What Top-Down Looks Like When It Works

The CEO has a clear vision of what they want and where the company is going. They articulate it cleanly. The clarity is itself an invitation; people who share the want gather around it, and people who do not share it find this out quickly and either leave or are filtered out.

Salesforce is Joe's example here. The CEO's want is clear. The alignment around it is voluntary in the sense that the people who don't share the want self-select out over time. The people who remain have made the CEO's want their own, or have found their version of it within the larger frame.

The crucial qualifier Joe names: "everybody is being asked how they participate in a way that works for them and their team." Even in the top-down version, the operators have agency about how the want gets executed. The top-down structure is the want; the bottom-up structure is the implementation. Both layers have to be real.

The failure mode of top-down goal-setting: the CEO's want is treated as the only legitimate want, and the operators are reduced to executors. The energy drains. The execution becomes mechanical. The people who would have offered better implementation paths if asked are not asked. The company achieves the goal on paper while becoming a worse version of itself.

## Why the Direction Is Not the Question

The framing trap is to argue about whether top-down or bottom-up is correct. The argument is irrelevant. Both work when the want is real. Neither works when the want is fake.

The right question is: where is the real want located, and how does the goal-setting structure honor it?

If the real want lives in the teams (because the work requires close knowledge of the domain), bottom-up will harness it best. If the real want lives in the CEO (because the vision is genuinely something only the CEO has the perspective to see), top-down will harness it best. Most companies have some of both, and the goal-setting structure should reflect that distribution.

## How This Applies to the Person

The same dynamic operates inside a single person. The goal can be set top-down (the head issues the goal) or bottom-up (the body, the heart, the underlying self surface what they want). Both work if the want is real somewhere in the system.

The internal failure mode is the same as the organizational one: the head issues goals because some authority (parental voice, cultural script, the inner board) says it should, and the rest of the system goes along. Nobody actually has the want. The goal is pursued without enthusiasm, fails or succeeds without joy, and produces the oscillation between goal-setting and goal-rejection that [[goals-make-me-miserable]] describes.

The internal alignment work is the same as the organizational alignment work: locate where the real want is, and let it set the goals. If the want lives in the body, listen to the body. If the want lives in the heart, ask the heart. The "what does your heart want?" question Joe asks in offsites is the bottom-up move at the personal level. See [[the-inner-board]] and [[goals-must-come-from-actual-want]].

## The Friction Variant

A specific note Joe makes about goals at the organizational level: there is always going to be some friction in goal-setting. There should be. The friction is the sign that something real is being negotiated.

"I'm sure there's struggle in it. There's always struggle. There's always some friction, and there should be some friction in goals because there's friction in getting to the next level of the video game. There's friction in taking your marriage to the next level. Anything that we're taking to the next level, there's going to be some friction in. There is a place where when people don't want friction, they start turning away from their goals. They're also turning away from aliveness in that." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The friction-free goal is a sign that the goal is fake. Either nobody actually wants it (so there's no friction because there's no engagement) or the friction has been suppressed (and the goal will quietly fail later). Real wants produce real friction. The friction is the data that the want is real.

This connects to [[friction-is-required-for-life]]: the same principle that operates at the cellular level operates at the organizational level. Friction is the structure of being alive. A friction-free organization is structurally the same as a corpse.
