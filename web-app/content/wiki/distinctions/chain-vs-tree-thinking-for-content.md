# Chain vs. Tree Thinking for Content

## The Distinction

Two ways to hold content in preparation for delivery.

**Chain.** The content is a sequence. Word follows word. Step follows step. A leads to B leads to C all the way to Z. Each link in the chain is a separate piece. The whole thing is the chain.

**Tree.** The content is an essence with branches. There is one thing the speaker wants to convey. There are bookmarks they want to hit along the way. Everything else is a tree of branches, accessible in any direction from any point.

The distinction matters because the failure modes are completely different.

A chain has one failure mode: any broken link and the whole chain is broken. The speaker who has memorized the speech and loses their place panics. There is nowhere to go. The chain has stopped functioning. Without the next link, the speaker cannot proceed.

A tree has no equivalent failure mode. If one branch snaps off, the tree is still a tree. The speaker still has access to the essence, the bookmarks, and all the other branches. The snapped branch is not a catastrophe. It is just a branch.

"You're memorizing word after word or step after step. And if you think about it that way, then any chain link that breaks and you're, and it's gone and there's nothing beyond and you're lost and now you're, it's full panic mode. What you had to do was come back to the very, very essence of what it is you were gonna talk about. Like, what's the one thing I want to convey?... Now you have access to a tree with tons of branches where it doesn't matter if you snap one branch off, you still have a beautiful tree." -- Tristan de Montebello, Speaking Authentically with Tristan de Montebello

## Why Chain Thinking Is the Default

Most preparation defaults to chain thinking because chain thinking feels safe. The chain is reassuring: every word is accounted for, every step is mapped, no improvisation is required.

The reassurance is misleading. The chain only works as long as nothing goes wrong. The moment anything goes wrong — and something always goes wrong in any real performance — the chain reveals itself as fragile. The speaker is left with no recovery path.

The tree feels unsafe in preparation because the tree does not promise specific outputs. The speaker prepares the essence, holds the bookmarks, and trusts that the words will arrive. This is harder to put down on paper. The preparation does not produce a memorizable document. The preparation produces a state.

This is why most novice speakers prepare as chains. The chain is what they can show their nervousness — "look, I prepared." The tree cannot be shown the same way. The tree shows up as preparedness only when delivered.

## When the Chain Actually Works

The chain is appropriate for very specific kinds of content. A toast at a wedding where the exact words matter. A scripted speech designed to be quoted. A poem being recited. A line in a play. Content where the precise sequence is itself the value.

The chain is also appropriate for the first 30 seconds of any presentation, when the speaker needs to ground and the opening words establish tone. A memorized opening can carry the speaker through the initial nerves.

But for the body of most performances — speeches, presentations, lectures, coaching sessions, podcasts, interviews — the chain is the wrong tool. The content is not the precise sequence. The content is the substance the speaker is conveying. That substance is better held as a tree.

## The Tree in Practice

Holding content as a tree requires three things:

**The essence.** What is the one thing you want to convey? Not three things, not five things — one. The essence is the trunk of the tree. Everything else grows from it. If the speaker can articulate the essence in one sentence, the tree has somewhere to root.

**The bookmarks.** What are the few specific moments you want to hit? Maybe a story, maybe a particular formulation, maybe a question for the audience. These are the major branches. The speaker wants to be sure these are reached, but the path between them is not fixed.

**The trust that the rest will arrive.** Between the essence and the bookmarks, the words are not pre-decided. They are discovered. The speaker is faithing — see [[faithing-faith-as-verb]] — that the next sentence will be there when it is needed. The butler will deliver. See [[the-butler-metaphor]].

The preparation is minimal in word count and maximal in substrate. The speaker has spent time with the material until the essence is clear, the bookmarks are felt, and the substrate is rich enough that the butler has plenty to draw on. From there, the delivery is more like exploration than recitation.

## What the Tree Enables That the Chain Doesn't

A tree-prepared speaker can do things a chain-prepared speaker cannot:

- **Respond to the audience in real time.** A question, a facial reaction, a moment of confusion can be addressed by branching off and coming back. The chain has no branches to branch off from.
- **Drop sections that aren't landing.** If a story isn't working, the tree-speaker can skip it. The chain-speaker has to deliver it or lose their place.
- **Add what arrives in the moment.** A new connection that occurs to the speaker mid-talk can be incorporated. The chain has no slot for new content.
- **Run shorter or longer.** A 20-minute slot becomes 12 minutes if needed, or 30 if the audience is engaged. The chain is fixed in length.
- **Recover from interruption.** A noise, a heckler, a technical failure can be handled. The chain breaks under interruption.
- **Discover something new while speaking.** This is the deepest gift. The speaker can find something they did not know they were going to say. The chain cannot produce this; everything was pre-decided. The tree can. The butler arrives with a connection the speaker did not see in preparation.

Joe's experience of giving an extemporaneous 20-minute speech at a conference after his presentation slides were lost is the canonical example. See [[joes-extemporaneous-vc-speech]]. He had the outline of what he was going to say. The slides were the chain. With the slides gone, he was forced into the tree. The result was better than anything he had ever delivered. From that moment on, he never prepared a chain-style speech again.

## The Connection to State

Chain thinking and framework thinking are the same trap at different levels. The chain is the in-speech version of the framework. Both produce comfort in preparation and fragility in delivery. Both are pulled out from under the performer at the first deviation.

The tree, like the state, is what holds when the conditions change. The tree is the content equivalent of the state. See [[state-not-framework]] for the parallel principle at the broader performance level.

The relationship: state is what the speaker is. Tree is how the content is held. The two work together. A state-based speaker holding chain-based content can still be derailed by deviation, because the chain breaks. A tree-based content with no state still wobbles because there is no integration. The speaker who is in state and holding a tree is the speaker who can speak about anything from anywhere.

## How to Convert Chain to Tree

For a speaker accustomed to chain preparation, the move to tree preparation is uncomfortable. Some practical steps:

**Find the essence.** Before preparing details, write the one sentence that captures what you want to convey. If you cannot, the rest of the preparation is premature. The essence is the root.

**List the bookmarks.** Three to five moments you want to be sure you hit. Stories, formulations, questions. Not in sequence — just listed. They will find their order in delivery.

**Do not write the connecting tissue.** Resist the temptation to write paragraphs that explain how you get from bookmark to bookmark. That writing locks you into a sequence. Trust that the connections will be made in delivery.

**Speak the material out loud, multiple times, slightly differently each time.** This builds the substrate. The same content delivered in different sequences across rehearsals teaches the body that the content is held in many configurations, not one.

**Stop rehearsing before you are comfortable.** A speaker who rehearses until comfortable has memorized the chain. The discomfort of not-knowing-what-will-come-out is the discomfort of being in the tree. Stay with it.

**Take reps in conditions where you cannot rely on the chain.** Live conversations, podcasts, conference panels, classroom discussions. Anywhere the chain cannot help. The body learns the tree by being forced into it.

## Boundary Conditions

Tree thinking is not universally superior. There are domains where chain thinking is the correct approach: precise quotation, legal language, technical specifications, anything where the exact sequence is itself the deliverable.

There are also speakers for whom the chain is a developmental phase that has to be passed through. The novice speaker often cannot hold a tree because the substrate is not deep enough. The chain gives them a way to deliver while the substrate is being built. As they grow, the chain can be dropped. Trying to drop the chain prematurely produces a speaker with neither chain nor tree — just confusion.

The signal that a speaker is ready to drop the chain: when delivery from a memorized script feels lifeless to them. When the speaker hears themselves and notes that something is missing. That is the moment the tree becomes possible. Before that moment, the chain is still doing its developmental work.
