# Non-Personal With the Body In vs. With the Body Out

## The Distinction

Seeing yourself from the outside can be done two ways. With the body in the game: an embodied, refreshing, refreshing form of impartial observation where the personal commentary drops away and the news arrives as fact. With the body out of the game: dissociation, where the system protects itself by thinning the connection to felt experience. The two states look similar from the outside. They are structurally opposite.

The defining quote:

"There's a way of looking at yourself with all the personal. There's a way of looking at yourself in the non-personal. And without going into dissociation, see what it's like to just live in the non-personal for a minute. You don't have to be outside of yourself. It's just non-personal." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

The dissociation is the protection. The non-personalness is the gift. The body's presence is what separates them.

## The Mechanism

The same observational stance can be achieved by two different routes. The first route reduces the personal commentary by leaving the body — the body is what generates the commentary, and if the body is absent the commentary stops. This is dissociation. The cost is the loss of contact with felt experience.

The second route reduces the personal commentary by widening the awareness while keeping the body present. The body still feels what it feels. The narrating voice, the judging voice, the explaining voice — those go quiet. What remains is the felt experience and a clear seeing of it.

"All the judgment that you usually hold for all that shit and all the talking, why aren't you, and all that's gone. From that outside perspective, you can see it as is, not as a commentary. Like you're getting the news as a blurb instead of like MSNBC or Fox News and all the fucking commentary, right? You're just getting like, oh, this is the state of affairs." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

The MSNBC/Fox metaphor is the key. The personal mode is a news event covered with commentary about what it means and who is at fault. The non-personal mode is the news as a blurb — what is happening, without the editorial layer.

## Why the Body Has to Stay In

Without the body, the non-personal observation is structurally indistinguishable from dissociation. The system reports back the same way: "I see myself from the outside." But the felt experience is different. Dissociation feels thin, distant, slightly numb. Embodied non-personal observation feels alive, present, "like a wave of warm honey" — as the source client described it.

The reason: dissociation is the body's specific protective response to overwhelming material. The protection works by reducing the felt-sense channel. When the felt-sense channel is reduced, the body's natural intelligence is also reduced. The system loses contact with what it knows.

Embodied non-personal observation keeps the felt-sense channel open. The body still knows what it knows. What changes is the relationship to the knowing — instead of being identified with each sensation as "me" and each judgment as "true," the system observes the sensations and judgments as events happening in the field of awareness.

The first state is a defense. The second state is a vantage.

## The Coaching Move That Reveals the Distinction

A person who has been dissociating their whole life often discovers the distinction by accident in coaching. They are asked to come back into the body, they do, and then they are asked to see themselves from the outside while staying in the body. The combination is what produces the embodied non-personal state.

The instruction Joe uses:

"That place where you disassociate, where you see yourself from the outside, I want you to go there but with no effort. So stay in your body. You're not dissociating, you're just seeing yourself from the outside. So it's seeing yourself from the outside without the dissociation." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

The instruction asks for the observational position without the leaving. The person who has only ever achieved the observational position through leaving has to discover that the position is available without the leaving. The discovery is the work.

A specific refinement Joe adds: when the person attempts the embodied non-personal and uses just a little bit of dissociation to manage the felt content (a slight cutting-off at the core), Joe names the residual: "Don't cut out — you're cutting a little bit off of your core. Like, don't, don't — you're using a little bit of the dissociation in your core. See what it's like to just let the core be completely open while you do this." The residual dissociation often hides in the gut or the chest. Naming it allows the body to release the last bit and produce the fully embodied non-personal state.

## What the Embodied Non-Personal Produces

In the source session, after the client achieved the embodied non-personal state, his report was:

"What — it's refreshing. Yeah, um, I, I know I hold on to a lot of trauma from when I was younger of just judgment and bullying and a lot of that. And it's not who I am anymore, but it's just so ingrained in much of my life that it's hard to remember that people don't see me that way anymore. Yeah, I'm a different person, but it's refreshing to open my body up to that perspective."

Then, when Joe asked what it felt like:

"It is. Yeah, it's like a, like a wave of warm honey."

The "wave of warm honey" is characteristic. The embodied non-personal state has a positive felt quality — warmth, expansion, gentle aliveness. Dissociation has no such quality. The body's report is what distinguishes them in real time.

The other characteristic output: the person sees their own pain (the anger, the resentment, the fear) without identifying with it. "I see a lot of anger and resentment and fear" — said from the embodied non-personal position — is structurally different from the same words said from inside the anger. In the non-personal position, the seeing is the relationship. In the personal position, the having-the-emotion is the relationship.

## Why This Distinction Matters for the Coaching Brain

A person who reports observing themselves from outside is not automatically in a useful state. The report does not disambiguate which form of observation is happening. The diagnostic has to include the body.

The questions that disambiguate:

- "What does it feel like in your body right now?" — embodied non-personal produces specific positive sensations (warmth, expansion, settling). Dissociation produces flatness or numbness.
- "Are you in your body or out of it?" — the person usually knows once asked directly.
- "What's the felt quality of seeing yourself this way?" — refreshing, alive, warm = embodied. Distant, thin, neutral = dissociated.

The coaching move differs depending on the diagnosis. The embodied non-personal state is the target — stay there, deepen it, let the rest of the session work from it. The dissociated non-personal state is a way station — name it as dissociation, run [[amplify-the-dissociation-then-opposite-with-no-effort]] to land the body, then re-attempt the non-personal observation from the embodied position.

## The Gift Inside the Defense

The teaching that often follows this distinction in a session: the dissociation was developed for a reason and contains real intelligence. The non-personalness — the ability to see oneself without identification with every passing sensation — is a genuine capacity. The system installed it as protection because the protection was needed.

The work is not to throw out the capacity. The work is to keep the capacity while putting the body back in. The same person who learned to see themselves from outside as a child to survive can, as an adult, use that observational capacity with their body present. The result is something better than either dissociation or naive identification with every feeling — it is the embodied witness.

"The reason I'm saying this is because all that disassociation has an amazing gift in it if you keep your body in the game. Disassociation without the body is one thing. Dissociation, or the non-personalness — the dissociation, that's the protection, is the non-personalness. But if you do it with the body in the game it's refreshing. It's quite sweet." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

This is the [[honor-the-patterns-intent]] principle applied at a precise level. The pattern (dissociation) had a function (non-personal observation). The function is keepable. The cost (leaving the body) is removable. The work is to separate the two.
