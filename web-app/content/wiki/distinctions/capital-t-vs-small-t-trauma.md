# Capital-T vs. Small-t Trauma

## The Distinction

Both kinds of experience produce trauma. Both run the same machinery. The distinction is what the wounding event looked like and what makes it harder or easier to recognize.

**Capital-T trauma** is the kind that is culturally legible as trauma. A discrete, intense event. A war zone, a car accident, an assault. The body learned in a short window that something specific was dangerous, and the body now reactivates that learning when the environment rhymes. PTSD is the textbook form. The veteran who hears a car backfire in Cleveland and is back in Fallujah is the standard image.

**Small-t trauma** is the kind that is not culturally legible as trauma. There was no event to point at. There was a sustained pattern, often a repeated message: a child was scared and the parent said "be strong"; a child was angry and was told "don't be that way"; a child cried and was made to feel ashamed. The body learned, across thousands of micro-instances, that a particular emotion was not allowed. The learning is just as deeply installed as PTSD learning. The reactivation works the same way. The mechanism is identical.

"This is a big capital T trauma, PTSD, war, Fallujah, but there's other traumas, like, you know, every time I was scared, my mom was like, be strong. So I never actually got nurtured in my fear. And so fear became the enemy. That's a different kind of trauma." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

## Why the Distinction Matters

The distinction is not academic. People with small-t trauma routinely refuse the word "trauma" because they have no qualifying event. "Nothing bad happened to me. My parents were good people. I don't have trauma." The refusal blocks the work, because the small-t trauma is still firing, still generating triggers, still producing the puppet feeling in fights, still organizing the person's life. See [[i-lose-myself-in-the-fight]] and [[trigger]].

The refusal is most common in people raised in functional-looking families. The message "be strong" was delivered by a parent who loved them and was trying to help. The message "don't be sad, it's not that bad" came from someone who genuinely wanted the child to be okay. The absence of malice in the delivery makes it harder for the adult to call the outcome trauma. The body, however, does not care about the parent's intent. The body just knows that a particular emotion was not allowed and that it learned to not have that emotion.

Naming the small-t version as trauma is what allows the same healing mechanism to apply.

## What Each One Looks Like When It Fires

**Capital-T fires:** A sensory cue rhymes with the original event. A loud sound, a smell, a face, a movement. The body floods. The person is partially or fully back in the original moment. They may lose contact with the present environment. The reaction is large and obvious.

**Small-t fires:** A relational cue rhymes with the original pattern. A tone of voice, a withdrawal of attention, a particular kind of criticism. The body activates a familiar reaction — the trigger — that operates more subtly. The person may not realize they are in trauma. The reaction can be repressed (a half-inch nod, see [[repressed-trigger-in-the-body]]) or large. The signature is the recurring nature of the activation in similar relational situations.

The fight in a long-term relationship is most often small-t trauma firing on both sides. The puppet feeling does not need a war story to explain it. The story of "fear was not allowed in my house" is fully sufficient. See [[the-fight-is-asking-you-to-feel-what-you-couldnt]].

## How the Be-Strong Trauma Operates Downstream

Joe traces a specific path for the "be strong, don't feel fear" version of small-t trauma to show how installed it is and how it shapes adult relationships:

"When I'm scared, fear is the enemy. I won't feel that. I won't feel it in my partner. My partner's scared. I'm trying to control them so that they don't feel fear, so I don't feel it because I don't want to feel it. And that's a different kind of trauma. And I'm back to my mom saying, 'Hey, you have to be strong. You're not allowed to have fear.' But what the trauma's asking of me to heal is to actually feel, to feel that fear that I didn't get to feel when I was a kid." -- Joe Hudson, The Real Reason You Lose Control In A Fight

The cascade is precise:
1. Original message: fear is not allowed.
2. Internalization: fear becomes the enemy.
3. Internal application: I will not feel my own fear.
4. External application: I will not let my partner feel fear (because their fear activates mine).
5. Control behavior: I manage the partner so they do not have feelings that would activate the forbidden feeling in me.
6. The partner experiences this as control, and the fight begins.

The fight that looks like it is about the partner's behavior is actually about a forty-year-old prohibition against a particular emotion. The mechanism is the same as in PTSD: the body is reliving the original conditions, and is asking for the emotion to finally be felt.

## What the Distinction Does Not Imply

Capital-T trauma is not "real" trauma and small-t trauma is not "lesser" trauma. Joe is explicit that the mechanism is the same. The distinction is about the form of the wounding event, not about the severity of the wound or the legitimacy of the response.

The distinction is also not a hierarchy of priority. A person with small-t trauma does not need to "earn" their right to do the work by demonstrating a discrete event. The body's reactivation pattern is the data. If the puppet feeling is happening, the trauma is firing, regardless of whether the original event is socially recognized as traumatic.

## The Shared Healing Mechanism

Both kinds of trauma resolve through the same mechanism: the emotion that could not be felt at the original time is felt now, in a container that allows the feeling. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

For capital-T: the veteran feels the terror, the helplessness, the grief that was suspended in the moment of the original event. The body completes the loop. The reactivation pattern dissolves.

For small-t: the adult feels the emotion that was originally not allowed — the fear that was met with "be strong," the anger that was met with shame, the sadness that was met with dismissal. The body completes the loop. The reactivation pattern dissolves.

The relational dimension also matters in both cases. See [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]]. The aloneness in the original wound was part of the wound. Being present with someone — therapist, partner, group — while the emotion is felt addresses the aloneness directly.

## Why Joe Draws This Distinction Explicitly

The distinction is drawn at the start of a conversation about losing control in fights because the audience is mostly people whose trauma is small-t and who will refuse to identify with the frame if only PTSD is described. By naming both kinds of trauma in the same sentence, the door opens for the small-t person to recognize themselves.

The pedagogical move is to make the small-t version impossible to dismiss as "not really trauma" by placing it next to the capital-T version and showing the identical mechanism. The "be strong" example is chosen carefully — it is mundane enough that nearly everyone has a version of it, and it is presented with the same gravity as Fallujah.

## Tone

Joe holds both with equal weight. There is no minimization of capital-T (the Fallujah image is delivered with care for what it represents). There is also no diminishing of small-t (the "be strong" example is named with the same gravity). The point of the distinction is to give the small-t person access to the same work the capital-T person already has access to.
