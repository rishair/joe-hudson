# Observing the Emotion vs. Moving the Emotion Through the Body

## The Distinction

There are two distinct things often called "feeling your emotions" and they are not the same.

**Observing the emotion** is sitting with awareness on what is arising. The meditation form: notice the sadness, breathe, watch it. This is a real practice with real benefits. It reduces identification with the emotion. It cultivates the capacity to be with what is uncomfortable. It produces distance from being controlled by the emotion.

**Moving the emotion through the body** is letting the muscles do what they need to do. Tears running, body shaking, jaw releasing, voice making sound. This is the somatic discharge that the body has been holding. It releases the muscular pattern, not just the mental relationship to the pattern.

"There's forms of meditation that say feel your emotions and observe the emotions... I'm not saying that that's bad, I think that's actually really a great thing. There's also having the emotions expressed and move through your body, and that's an incredibly critical piece of it because the emotional repression is held in our muscles." -- Joe Hudson, Stages of Emotional Development

## Why Observation Alone Is Not Enough

Emotions live in muscles. See [[emotions-live-in-muscles]]. When an emotion is held back, a specific muscular pattern carries the holding. The pattern persists until the muscles get to make the movement they have been preventing. Observing the emotion does not move the muscles. The chronic holding pattern remains, even if the person has become more comfortable with sitting in the presence of the emotion.

"The muscles aren't going to learn from just sitting in meditation position and allowing yourself to feel the emotion, though that's incredibly helpful. There is a need for the emotion to actually move your body, to allow it to move your body and move through your body." -- Joe Hudson, Stages of Emotional Development

A person who has meditated for years may have learned to be peacefully present with sadness without ever having cried it out. They are good at the observation. The body has not yet been given the movement.

## What Moving the Emotion Looks Like

The body knows. Joe's principle across this work: do not direct the movement, allow it. The forms it takes:

- Crying, sometimes loud, sometimes silent, sometimes for sustained periods
- Body shaking. See [[let-your-body-shake-it-out]]
- Voice making sound: yelling, groaning, growling
- Pacing, curling up, stretching, rocking
- Laughing
- The jaw releasing, the shoulders dropping, the belly softening

The motion does not have to be dramatic. A small somatic shift counts if it is the actual movement the body was holding back.

## The Two Together

Joe is not against meditation. He is precise about what it does and does not do. The observation is helpful; it is the complement to the movement, not a replacement for it. People at Stage 4 of [[stages-of-emotional-development]] often substitute observation for movement and feel they are doing the work. They are doing some of the work. The other half waits for the body.

"I did it for years. I still do it. I love it. And there's, there's just, we're human. We need to go to the bathroom and we need to cry. It's just how we are as people. And so, yeah, moving those emotional states through the system is, if it's not critical, it's definitely something that expedites the whole damn thing." -- Joe Hudson, Stages of Emotional Development

## A Diagnostic for Practice

If you have been doing emotional work for some time and the body still feels rigid, chances are you are observing without moving. If you can sit with sadness for an hour but cannot remember the last time tears ran down your face, the practice has been observation only.

The fix is to make space for the movement to happen. Privacy, time, willingness. See [[daily-emotional-yoga]].

## Why People Default to Observation

Observation is easier. It does not require letting the body do something the person finds embarrassing or undignified. The image of a competent adult does not include yelling into a pillow or shaking on the floor. Observation preserves the image. Movement breaks it.

The other reason: observation is often what the body knows how to do because the suppression learned in childhood is so strong. The body literally has not been allowed to make the motion for years or decades. The body needs to relearn the shape. See [[joes-crying-story]]: Joe faked crying in the woods for three months before real crying returned.

## Tone

Practical and inviting. Joe holds the meditation tradition with respect while being clear about its limit. The point is not to discredit a practice but to ensure the body's part of the work also gets done.
