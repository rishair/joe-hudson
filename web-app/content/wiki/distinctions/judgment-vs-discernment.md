# Judgment vs. Discernment

## Definition

Discernment is the knowing of a distinction. Looking at the data clearly and seeing what is there. Judgment is the preloaded version: an emotion the person is avoiding has biased the read on the data. The data is no longer being seen on its own terms.

"Discernment is just a knowing of distinction. But if it's judgment, then you're not clear. You're not looking at the data clearly. You have preconceived notions of the data and then you can't make great decisions." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## How to Tell Them Apart

The clearest test: is the read on the person or situation stable across emotional states, or does it shift when your emotional state shifts? Discernment holds steady. Judgment moves with your unfelt material.

A second test: is there energy in the read? Discernment is matter-of-fact. Judgment carries a charge. The charge is the unfelt emotion underneath.

A third test: when you imagine feeling the avoided emotion, does the read soften? If yes, the read was a judgment. If no, it was discernment.

## Why Judgment Corrupts Decisions

Decisions made through judgment are decisions made through unfelt emotion. The judger is not deciding based on what is happening. They are deciding based on what they cannot bear to feel. This is why people make confident decisions that are obviously wrong to outside observers: their confidence is the avoidance, not the clarity.

Discernment-based decisions tend to be quieter, more flexible, and more accurate. The person can be wrong without it threatening their identity. They have not loaded the decision with avoided emotion.

## The Mechanism

Every judgment maps to a specific avoided emotion. Joe gives examples:

- Judging someone for being angry: avoiding the feeling of being out of control, or the feeling of potentially losing them.
- Judging someone for being uptight: avoiding the feeling of being controlled, or the feeling of being rigid.
- Judging someone for being needy: often avoiding the feeling of being needy oneself.

The judgment is the way the avoided emotion gets externalized. See [[unfelt-emotion-leaks]].

## What to Do When You Notice a Judgment

The intervention is not to suppress the judgment or to tell yourself you should not judge. It is to follow the judgment back to the avoided emotion. "What might I have to feel if this person were not the way I am saying they are?"

Once that emotion is felt, the judgment usually dissolves. What remains is either nothing (the judgment was entirely a projection) or discernment (a clean read on what is actually happening that was being amplified by the avoided emotion).

## Why This Matters in Coaching

A coach making judgments about a person they are coaching cannot read them clearly. The judgment tells the coach more about themselves than about the coachee. The coach's own work is to feel what is being avoided so that discernment is available.

This is also why [[impartiality]] is foundational. Impartiality is the absence of agenda. Judgments carry agendas. When the coach has no agenda, judgments do not arise. What arises is discernment.

## Tone

Plain, neutral. Joe is not moralizing about judgment. Judgment is just data: when you find it, you have found an unfelt emotion. The instruction is investigative, not corrective.
