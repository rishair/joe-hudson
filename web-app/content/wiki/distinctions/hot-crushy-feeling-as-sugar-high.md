# The Hot Crushy Feeling Is a Sugar High vs. Real Love Is the Deeper High

## The Distinction

The intense early-relationship feeling that the culture treats as the proof of love -- the "ooh, I'm so crushy on you" feeling, the four-to-five-month chemical infatuation -- is a sugar high. It is real, it is enjoyable, and it is structurally bounded. It does not last. It cannot be re-extracted by finding another partner. The person who chases this feeling across partners is doing a version of [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]]: the chase ends each relationship before the deeper kind of love can arrive.

The deeper kind of love -- the felt high of a mature relationship -- is structurally different. It is what becomes available in a partnership where two people have worked through hard material together, who have been with each other through thick and thin, who have allowed each other to see the inside. The felt quality is not the same as the crushy feeling. It is deeper, slower, less ah-ah and more ah.

"There is no relationship where that hot, like, ooh, I'm like so crushy on you, like, stays. That shit's gone in like four or five months. And you see people trying to find that over and over again. That's another way in which they push away love, because that is like a sugar high. And you want that real good stuff. You want the really good love. That happens with a mature relationship where you have worked through shit and you have been with each other through thick and thin. That's when that love shows up." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

## What "Sugar High" Names

The early relationship high is chemical. Oxytocin, novelty, projection, the rush of selection-confirmation. It is real in the sense that it is felt. It is not real in the sense that it tells you anything stable about the relationship.

The sugar-high label captures three properties:

**1. It feels great.** The pleasure is genuine. The early high is one of the more pleasant felt states humans get to have.

**2. It is structurally bounded.** The chemistry does not sustain. Four to five months, give or take, the felt intensity tapers. The taper is not a relationship failure. It is the chemistry doing what chemistry does.

**3. It is addictive in the same way sugar is.** The person who has been on the sugar high and is now at the taper craves the high. The craving is satisfied most quickly by starting over with a new person, because new-person novelty re-triggers the chemistry. The repeated cycle of crushy-feeling-then-end-it-when-it-fades is the sugar-cycle in romantic form.

The chasing of the crushy high is one of the most common forms of [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]]. The person ends each relationship around the time the chemistry fades because they read the fading as the relationship being wrong. The deeper love that would come on the other side of the fading is the love they keep just missing.

## What the "Really Good Love" Is

The high of the mature relationship is a different chemistry and a different felt experience. It is built, not chemical. It requires both partners to have worked through the material that would otherwise constrain the relationship. It requires both partners to have been with each other through thick and thin. It requires the trust that has been built by surviving the conflict.

The felt quality is not the early ah-ah of the crushy feeling. It is closer to ah. A deeper register, a slower vibration, a held-ness that the early high did not have because the early high had not yet been tested. The mature love is less about the chemical novelty and more about the felt presence of someone who actually knows you and is still here.

Joe describes the felt difference:

"I haven't experienced the end [of the deeper high], but what I notice is that the high is different. That the high becomes less like, ah, ah, and more like, ah. Like, it's just a different high." -- Joe Hudson, Finding a Romantic Partner

The notation -- ah-ah vs ah -- is the closest verbal handle for a felt distinction that is hard to describe and easy to recognize once it has been felt.

## How the Mature Love High Replenishes

The early high is one-shot. Once the chemistry tapers, it does not return in the same form for the same relationship.

The mature love high renews. Every time the relationship goes through a major piece of work -- a conflict resolved, a piece of armor dropped, a deeper truth allowed -- the high returns in a more grounded form. The relationship does not have to be lower-energy at year three than at month three. It has access to a renewable form of the high that the early relationship did not.

See [[nre-returns-in-grounded-form-after-each-healing]] for the developed treatment of this mechanism.

Brett's variant of the same observation:

"There's, there's like that initial four to five months. But I also notice that every time we break through a new level of intimacy, like breakthrough into a new level of intimacy, suddenly that comes back for that for a period of time. There's like, and it's almost like we're stepping more and more into the relationship. And every time we do that, there's a new kind of phase of, wow, I've never experienced this before. So it gets to feel like that crushy phase. Even in year 3, year 4." -- Brett Kistler, Finding a Romantic Partner

The renewal is not exactly the early high. It is the deeper high arriving in a form that includes the freshness the early high had. The breakthrough is what triggers it.

## Why the Distinction Matters in Coaching

For the person chasing the crushy feeling:

- The chasing is one of the forms of [[push-pull-dynamics-in-dating]]
- The chasing keeps the person away from the deeper love by ending each relationship before that love is available
- The chasing is the body's strategy for getting the felt good of love without the threshold-crossing the deeper love requires (see [[fear-of-losing-yourself-in-love]])

For the person whose relationship has lost the early high:

- The loss is structural, not a sign the relationship is wrong
- The deeper high is available on the other side of the work the relationship is asking for
- The cold relationship is not at the end of its chemistry; it is at the edge of work it has not done

For the person who thinks long relationships are necessarily low-energy:

- The model is wrong. The model treats the early high as the only high. The deeper high is also a high, in a different form, and it renews with each piece of work.

## What This Distinction Is Not

It is not anti-chemistry. The early high is fine. It is a real and pleasant feature of how relationships begin. The principle is not "you should not enjoy the early high." The principle is "the early high is not the thing, and if you chase it as the thing you miss what is actually possible."

It is not "settle for the lower-grade feeling." The deeper high is not lesser. It is different and, in the felt experience of those who have known both, often described as more. The crushy feeling is not the gold standard the mature relationship is supposed to imitate; the mature relationship has its own register.

It is not a denial that some relationships should end. The principle does not say "stay through the fading." Some relationships should not continue past the chemistry, because the chemistry was the only thing holding them together. The principle is about distinguishing the chemistry-tapering from the relationship-being-wrong, and about recognizing that the chasing of new chemistry is itself often the form of the push-pull.

## When This Distinction Comes Up

- A person describes a pattern of ending relationships around month four or five
- A person reports the chemistry has died in a long relationship and is asking whether the relationship is over
- A person is single after a long period and reports being "addicted to the beginning" of relationships
- A person believes long relationships are necessarily companionate and lower-energy
- A person in a mature relationship reports a sudden return of intense feeling and is confused about what produced it

## Tone

Plain. Joe is not romanticizing either form. The early high is named with affection ("that shit's gone in like four or five months" has a slight amused fondness). The deeper high is named with the matter-of-fact tone of someone who has it and is reporting what it is like. The distinction is delivered as information, not as a moral about which is better.
