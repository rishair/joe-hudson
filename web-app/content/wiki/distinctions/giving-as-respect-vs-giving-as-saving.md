# Giving as Respect vs. Giving as Saving

## The Distinction

Two acts of giving can look identical from the outside and have opposite effects on the receiver. The first kind treats the receiver as a full person with their own capacity, including the capacity to give something back. The second kind treats the receiver as an object of pity who lacks the capacity to give and therefore must only receive. The first kind is generosity. The second kind is hierarchy disguised as generosity.

The distinction is in the structure of the giving, not in the size or sincerity of the gift.

## The Two Sides

**Giving as respect:**

- Treats the receiver as a full person with their own capacity
- Includes a path for the receiver to give back -- a trade, a contribution, a recognition
- Sees specific worth in the receiver and the receiver's situation
- Does not require the receiver to perform gratitude or smallness
- Leaves the receiver feeling larger, not smaller
- Tone: equal-to-equal, with the gift being one move in an ongoing relationship
- Often involves [[generosity]] of seeing as well as material giving

**Giving as saving:**

- Positions the giver as having what the receiver lacks
- Asks nothing of the receiver because the receiver is presumed to have nothing to give
- Sees the receiver primarily through the lens of what is missing
- Often requires the receiver to perform gratitude as the only available form of reciprocation
- Leaves the receiver feeling smaller, validated as someone-who-cannot-provide
- Tone: above-to-below, with the gift being a one-time descent from a higher position
- Often involves moralistic framing -- "those poor people" -- that does the harm

## Joe's Formulation

"He's not generous in such a way where he's putting himself above, which is another way that people are generous. 'I'll help those people out because I'm better than them.' They, poor them, I'll help them out." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The "I'll help those people out" tone is the diagnostic. It signals that the giver is using the giving to confirm their own elevated position. The receiver is the prop in the giver's identity-construction.

By contrast, generosity that lands as real treats the receiver as someone who has capacity and gifts of their own. The giver's giving is not the apex of the relationship; it is one move in a sequence in which the receiver also has moves.

## The Canonical Demonstration

Joe tells the story of meeting a group of Canadians in Nicaragua who had brought clothing to give to poor Nicaraguans. The giving was one-directional and asked nothing in return. Joe asked what they were doing for trade. They responded that they were not asking for anything -- they had everything and were giving freely.

Joe's response: "Oh, so it seems to me that you're just teaching them that they can't do stuff for themselves. You're saying to them that you're not capable of taking care of yourself. We need to take care of you." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The Canadians asked what they could possibly ask in return. Joe pointed to a Navy SEAL who was working alone trying to save turtles in that part of Nicaragua. He suggested the locals could help with the turtles in exchange for the clothing. The locals would get the clothing, get to give of their labor toward a cause, and the structure of the exchange would treat them as people with capacity rather than as objects of charity.

"They get to give. The idea is that the person who's receiving somehow, that's the gift, as if it's not the giving that's the gift. My experience is giving feels just as good, if not sometimes better, than receiving." -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

See [[canadians-in-nicaragua-with-the-clothing]] for the full example.

## Why The Receiver Must Get to Give

The structural insight: giving is itself empowering (see [[generosity]] on this). If giving is empowering, then a giving arrangement in which one party always gives and the other only receives is an arrangement in which one party gets the empowerment of giving and the other does not. Over time, this trains the receiver into the role of one-who-receives, which is the role of one-who-cannot-provide. The "generosity" has produced disempowerment.

The fix is structural: build a path for the receiver to give. The path does not have to be equivalent in size or kind. The locals do not have to give back clothing for clothing. They can give labor for clothing, recognition for clothing, witness for clothing. What matters is that the receiver gets to occupy the position of one-who-gives, even briefly. The empowerment of giving runs both ways, the relationship becomes an exchange rather than a charity, and the giving lands as real.

## The Real-Life Indicators

The receiver of giving-as-saving usually reports one or more of these felt states:

- Discomfort taking the gift even when the gift was needed
- A vague sense of being made smaller by the gift
- Resentment toward the giver despite the giver's apparent kindness
- A need to defend their dignity by minimizing how much the gift mattered
- Difficulty receiving in general, possibly extending to other relationships

The receiver of giving-as-respect usually reports:

- Felt warmth that integrates without leaving a residue
- A sense of being seen as a full person
- Motivation to contribute back in their own way
- Lasting capacity to ask for help in the future without shame
- The giving becomes part of an ongoing relationship rather than a one-time event

The giver-as-saving usually reports:

- A vaguely puzzled feeling that the receivers were not as grateful as expected
- A sense that the receivers do not understand what was done for them
- A drift toward feeling unappreciated, possibly extending to a pattern across multiple recipients
- Identity reinforcement around being "a generous person"

The giver-as-respect usually reports:

- Felt mutuality with the receivers
- Loss of any sense that they were doing something special
- Curiosity about who the receivers are as people, not just as objects of help
- Relationships that continue past the giving event

## Why People Default to Giving-as-Saving

Several factors:

- Cultural narratives of charity often have above-below structures built into them
- The pleasure of feeling like a benefactor is real, even if the benefactor effect harms the receiver
- Moralism around helping installs the obligation but not the capacity to design empowering structures
- Most teaching about giving focuses on the giver's intention, not on what the receiver experiences
- The structural design work (how do they get to give back?) takes more thought than just handing over the resource

The fix is not to give less. The fix is to design the giving such that it includes a path for the receiver to be a giver too. This usually requires asking the receiver something, not just giving to them. The asking is the respect.

## In Coaching

When someone presents helping others as a path to happiness, the question is not just whether their giving is from desire (see [[giving-from-desire-not-obligation]]). It is also whether the structure of their giving leaves the receivers larger or smaller. If they describe their recipients with pity -- "those poor people" -- the structure is likely giving-as-saving and is not producing the generosity benefits.

When someone reports that their charity work feels unrewarding even though they are doing the right things, this distinction is often the explanation. The activity is giving-as-saving, the receivers are not being treated as full people, the relationship has no mutuality, and the giver is not experiencing the empowerment that real generosity produces because real generosity is not happening.

The coaching move is to redirect to design: what would it look like to ask something of the people you are helping? What gifts do they have that you could receive? The Canadians-in-Nicaragua intervention is the model -- a small redesign that included the receivers as givers changed the whole structure of the relationship.

## The Self-Application

The same distinction applies when receiving help. If you receive in a way that asks nothing of you and treats you as someone-who-cannot-provide, the receiving will disempower you over time. Letting yourself give back -- even something small, even something different -- protects your dignity in the receiving relationship. This is part of why people who have received much from a particular helper often need to find some way to give back; the giving is the rebalance that lets the receiving land.

## When This Distinction Is Active

- A person describes their charity or volunteer work and the language is condescending
- A person reports their giving feels good for them but the recipients do not seem grateful
- A person is in a one-way receiving relationship and feeling smaller for it
- A person is designing a charitable program or business with a giving-back component
- A person is in a helping profession and noticing the receivers are not benefiting as expected
- A person is in a long-term relationship where one partner has been the constant giver
- A person describes someone they admire whose generosity is delivered without hierarchy

## Tone

The distinction can be delivered playfully when the example is light (Canadians with clothing, Joe pointing it out and suggesting the turtle exchange). It should be delivered gently when the person being coached has been doing giving-as-saving for years; the recognition can land as shame if not held carefully. The redirect to design is the antidote: this is not about you being bad at generosity; this is about the structure of the giving needing one more move to land.
