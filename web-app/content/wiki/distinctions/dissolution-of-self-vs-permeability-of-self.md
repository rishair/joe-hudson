# Dissolution of Self vs. Permeability of Self

## The Distinction

Two different ways the work of "less self" can be lived. Both are real. They are not the same.

**Dissolution of self.** The frame where freedom comes from the recognition that "this isn't me, it's moving through me." The personal is set aside. The teacher, coach, or facilitator becomes the channel. The wisdom is not theirs. The praise is not theirs. The criticism is not theirs. They get out of the way and let what wants to move move. The Fools Crow hollow-bone frame. See [[channeling-quality-of-truth]] and the phase-2 description in [[the-hollowing-out-process]].

**Permeability of self.** The frame where freedom comes from the self getting so large that there is more space, not from the self disappearing. The personal is fully present. The teacher feels the impact of their work in their own body. The praise lands. The criticism lands. The lives changed by their work break their heart on a consistent basis. The self is not gone; it is porous enough to let the world all the way in and through.

The distinction matters because dissolution alone, held too long, becomes the cover for unowned authority and lost empathy. Permeability is the corrective.

## Joe's Naming of the Shift

Joe describes this as the next phase of his own work. The dissolution phase served him for years. It is the phase he was inhabiting when he could say "it's not mine, it's coming through me, I'm a clean vessel" — and meant it. The phase produced a non-defended relationship to praise and criticism, and the depth of work the dissolution phase made possible exceeded anything available before it.

What he is finding now is the second move:

"There's I realize that somebody like a Donald Trump or a Sam Altman or a Bono or whatever, whoever the Bono is of today, they hold like a lot of people's energy... it's like, this thing, and I see the way that a lot of people hold that really, like, doesn't work out for them in the long run." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The way to hold the growing focal-point energy without it destroying the person is not more dissolution. It is permeability. Joe's own statement of the move:

"Up until now, the way that the journey has been is it's become less me through dissolution, meaning the recognition that it — the teaching isn't mine, it's coming through me, that it's not personal, that it's um, that it's like a gift. And that's all — it's always kind of been how I've been able to see more clearly through the process and be a better conduit for what wants to move. ... What I'm getting to now is what I'm realizing is that actually the personal has to be reincorporated back into it for, for me to be able to be healthy if this thing continues to grow." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

"So now instead of the freedom coming from like a dissolution of self, it's almost more like a permeability of self. It's like it's becoming so large that there's more space." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The frame shifts from getting smaller (less me) to getting larger (more permeable). The freedom that used to come from absence-of-self now comes from porousness-of-self.

## The Two Hot Springs Encounters That Crystallized the Shift

Joe describes two encounters at a hot springs that named the shift for him.

**Encounter one — the recognition.** Joe wakes up, walks shirtless toward the bathroom half-asleep. Six women see him. One says, "Joe Hudson?" The recognition cascades. "Oh, I took your course." "I heard about your course." Joe's internal experience: "I just want to go to Vipassana alone." This is the focal-point experience in miniature — being recognized in a private moment by strangers because of the work.

**Encounter two — the man at Walmart.** Later at the same hot springs, Joe and a stranger are chatting. The man tells a single story: he had a moment at a Walmart where he helped a woman interpret a dream and it made a difference in her life. As he tells the story, he starts weeping. Joe's recognition: "You can't do that if it's not personal."

The first encounter is the structural reality of growing influence Joe is having to learn to hold. The second is the corrective. If the work has to be impersonal — "I'm just a channel" — to be holdable, the practitioner is shielded from the kind of impact the weeping man at Walmart was reporting. The shield protects the practitioner from the audience-capture trap. The shield also protects them from the connection that the work actually requires.

"To be intimate with that experience is incredibly personal. To allow that to break my heart, I know that, that, that to allow that in on a personal level, on an intimate level, is gonna be required for me not to become dismissive of people, to not — there's this whole thing about power that like, as you have more power, you lose empathy." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The permeability is the structural response to the power-erodes-empathy problem. The dissolution frame, held alone, lets the practitioner accept growing influence without having to feel the actual impact of the work landing in actual people. The permeability frame requires the impact to be felt — on the body, in the chest, as heartbreak when the work has landed in someone — as the price of remaining a coach rather than becoming a guru.

See [[let-it-break-your-heart-on-a-consistent-basis]] for the developed principle and [[power-erodes-empathy]] for the underlying problem.

## How To Tell Which Frame Is Operating

In oneself or in a person being coached.

**Dissolution-only signatures:**
- The work is described as "coming through" but the practitioner does not feel the impact of it landing
- Criticism is deflected with "I'm just a channel, that wasn't me"
- The praise is also deflected, sometimes with the same language
- The practitioner has a hard time crying about the people their work has changed
- Encountering a person whose life shifted from the work produces a kind of clean appreciation rather than a felt break in the body
- "I'm just the conduit" starts to function as a defense

**Permeability signatures:**
- The work is described as channeled AND the practitioner feels the impact in their body
- Praise is received as praise, with the felt weight of being seen, while still being held with the channel-frame
- Criticism is received as information, with the felt weight of being challenged, while still being held with the channel-frame
- The practitioner can be moved to tears by an encounter with someone changed by the work
- The reports of impact are not abstracted into "the work has impact" but felt as "this person, in their actual life, is different"
- The practitioner becomes more rather than less able to be touched by the people they encounter

The signatures overlap. The both-and is the point. Dissolution provides the structural safety; permeability provides the contact. Neither alone is sufficient.

## Why Dissolution Alone Becomes Dangerous

A teacher or coach who has lived in pure dissolution for years can accumulate influence without remaining accountable to the people the influence touches. The dissolution frame ("it's not me, it's the work") provides a structural exemption from feeling the impact. The exemption is initially useful — it lets the practitioner hold the role without ego-distortion. Sustained too long, the exemption becomes a numbing.

The numbing is the precondition for losing empathy. The studies Joe references on power and empathy work through this mechanism: when the impact is no longer felt, the consideration of impact in future decisions stops being available. The leader makes decisions about people they have stopped being affected by. The teacher gives teachings to a body of students whose actual lives have receded into abstraction.

The dissolution frame can be the mechanism that makes this numbing palatable to the practitioner. They are not callous; they are just "letting the work move through." The frame is true, and it is also the cover.

Permeability is the corrective because it forces contact. The impact cannot be abstracted because it is felt in the body. The number of people changed by the work cannot remain a statistic because each one, when encountered, breaks the heart again.

See [[the-hollowing-out-process]] for the developmental arc that puts both frames in sequence (phase 2 dissolution, phase 3 the both-and including permeability) and [[dont-claim-the-wisdom-as-yours]] for the principle that prevents phase 3 from collapsing into self-aggrandizement.

## What This Means Operationally

For a coach or facilitator who has been working in pure dissolution and is feeling the cost of it:

- Stop deflecting the praise. Let it land. You can hold both that the work is moving through you AND that you are real and the praise is for you.
- Stop deflecting the criticism. Let it land. The same both-and.
- When someone tells you about a change in their life that came from the work, do not just appreciate it from a distance. Let it actually move you. Cry if it produces tears.
- Notice when "I'm just a channel" is becoming a way to not feel something.
- The growing number of people the work has touched is a number that can become abstract. The permeability move is to keep at least some of them concrete. Pick one. Feel them. Let it matter.
- The both-and is the practice: you are a channel AND you are personal. You are not the source AND you are the specific person through whom the work takes its shape. Both, all the time.
