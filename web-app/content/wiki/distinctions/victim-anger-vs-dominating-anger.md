# Victim Anger vs. Dominating Anger (Both Required for Clarity)

## The Distinction

When releasing anger at a circumstance, an illness, a situation, or an internalized injury, two distinct shapes of expression need to move. Both must be available. Skipping either one leaves the anger incomplete.

**Victim anger** is "you have done this to me." It names what was taken, ruined, stolen. It speaks the grievance. The body is upright but recounting harm.

**Dominating anger** is "you will not take this from me anymore." It refuses the future the situation is trying to write. The body asserts itself over the thing. "Fuck you, you will not fucking take my fucking life anymore."

Both are required if the anger is to land into clarity. Either alone is partial.

"Both of these two things is really important to be able to fully express what I would call, like, the victim anger. Like, you've done this to me and fuck you. And then the fucking dominant anger, both of those two things are really important expressions if we want to get to clarity, right?" -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Hold It All Anymore

## What Each Sounds Like

**Victim anger (the grievance form):**
- "You have ruined my life."
- "You have taken away traveling. You have taken away cozy dinners. You have taken away movie nights, events with my family."
- "You have crushed my spirit. You have changed my goals."
- "You have stolen everything I want."

The structure: the object of the anger is the subject of the destruction. "You did X. You took Y. You stole Z." The speaker is in the position of the one who has been done to.

**Dominating anger (the refusal form):**
- "Fuck you, you will not fucking take my fucking life anymore."
- "I will fucking own you."
- "I am done taking your shit."
- "You're not gonna ruin any more of my life. I'm going to do what I want to do."
- "You can stay in your dark wormy hole and die."

The structure: the speaker is the subject. The object of the anger is being addressed as something that will be dominated. "I will not allow X. You will not do Y to me. I will take back Z."

## Why Both Are Required

Each form moves a different layer.

**Victim anger releases the grievance.** It names what happened, what was lost, what was taken. Without this, the harm is not acknowledged by the body's own voice. The body still carries the unspoken accounting.

**Dominating anger releases the future.** It refuses the trajectory the situation is trying to write. Without this, the body still anticipates more of the same. The victim form alone leaves the speaker as a recipient of what is happening.

Together they produce the clarity described in [[visceral-clarity-from-anger]]: a felt sense of "I know what to do" that is structured around the self rather than around the other.

## Which One Tends to Be Harder

For most people, one form is easier than the other. The harder one is usually the more important to practice.

In the source session, Sadie found the victim form first ("You have ruined my life... you're just continually stealing it"). The dominating form took an example before she could find it. Joe demonstrated: "Fuck you, you will not fucking take my fucking life anymore... I will fucking dominate you."

Christopher, by contrast, slipped from anger into sadness ("I'm just tired of feeling like this") rather than into domination. Joe redirected: "That's the sadness, and that's usually where your anger goes... let's do the dominant one. I want you literally to feel like you are dominating it."

The pattern: people whose anger gets repressed into compliance (the "be a good girl/boy" pattern) often find the victim form first because they have permission to be hurt. The dominating form requires going against the childhood injunction that anger is impermissible. People whose anger collapses into sadness or self-pity often need explicit coaching toward the dominating form so the energy does not just rerun the collapse.

## The Test of Each

For both forms, the test is the same: does it feel good? Does it produce relief? Does it produce some flicker of clarity?

Sadie after the dominating form: "It felt good. Like, I kind of wanted to, you know, stab something, maybe. Yeah, a little violent. Yeah, yeah, it felt really nice, actually."

The feeling of "wanting to stab something" is not a problem here. It is the body confirming the dominating anger has begun to move. The release is happening through the body, not at a person. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] and [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]].

## When Anger Is Stuck in Sadness

If a person tries to access anger and consistently slides into sadness, the dominating form is what is being avoided. The sadness is the more permissible feeling. The anger underneath the sadness is the dominating refusal that the system has not yet been allowed to make.

"Yeah, so that's the sadness, and that's usually where your anger goes." -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Hold It All Anymore

The move is not to stop them from being sad. The move is to name the pattern (anger slides into sadness) and then explicitly direct toward the dominating form: "Let's do the dominant one. I want you literally to feel like you are dominating it."

The body may need a demonstration first because it has no template for the motion. Modeling the dominating form gives the body permission and shape. See [[fake-the-anger-to-let-it-move]] for the broader principle.

## What This Distinction Replaces

Many people in anger work get stuck because they assume there is one shape of anger to express. They access the form that is more familiar, run it for some time, and find the anger keeps recycling without producing the landing of clarity.

Reading the situation as needing both forms changes the practice. If the victim form has been running and clarity has not arrived, the dominating form is the missing piece. If the dominating form has been running and there is still grievance underneath, the victim form is the missing piece.

This is not the same as [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]], which addresses anger that is covering grief or hurt. The both-forms distinction addresses anger that is genuinely the right emotion but has only had access to half of its full expression.

## Mode of Use in Coaching

When directing anger release work:

1. Start with whichever form arrives first. Do not push for the other.
2. Watch what happens at the end of the first form. Did clarity arrive? Did the body settle?
3. If not, direct toward the other form. Demonstrate if necessary.
4. Check the body after the second form. The clarity often arrives only after both have moved.

The role of the coach in the source session is instructive: Joe modeled the dominating form when Sadie's first attempt at "dominate" still sounded like grievance. The modeling was not an instruction; it was a demonstration of the body's motion. Once Sadie heard it, she found it: "I'm gonna do what I want to do and when I want to do it. Yeah, you're done ruining my future. I'm going to have fun, I'm going to travel, and you can't do shit."

## Tone

Joe's tone when directing this work is direct and energized. The dominating modeling carries the actual energy of the dominating form: "Fuck you. I will fucking dominate you. You can do whatever the fuck you gotta do." He does not perform a calmer version. The body needs to hear the actual shape.

After the release, the tone returns to warm: "How'd that feel?" The transition signals that the release was a contained motion, not a state to live in. The anger moved; now the conversation continues.

## What This Is Not

This is not anger at a person. The anger in this distinction is directed at the situation, the illness, the internalized pattern, the circumstance. The "you" being addressed is the chronic illness, the depression, the situation, the internalized voice -- not the partner, the parent, the boss.

If the dominating anger is being directed at a person who is in the room, this is the anti-pattern [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]]. The whole structure of this distinction assumes the anger is moving through the body privately or in a contained co-creative setting, not as discharge at another person.

## Variations

In couple settings (as in the source session), both partners can move through both forms together, directing the anger at the shared situation. The shared object of anger means the release deepens connection rather than fracturing it. See the example of Sadie and Christopher releasing anger at his chronic illness together.

The move "let's get angry together at the thing" is a powerful corrective to relationships where the anger has been getting directed at each other. The same energy, redirected at the shared circumstance, produces bonding rather than damage.

## When the Dominating Form Collapses on Contact With a Person (The Need-Suppression Layer)

A specific failure mode of the dominating form: the client accesses it cleanly toward an abstract target (a circumstance, an illness, a pattern), and the energy collapses the moment a real person enters the sentence. The client starts in dominating posture — "I have something to say, you're going to listen, you're going to back up" — and the body drops the energy as soon as "you" actually means somebody.

"And what just happened is you — as soon as you made a demand of somebody else —" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Stuck In Life When I Know My Patterns?

This is not the [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] pattern. The anger is not covering grief or hurt. The dominating form is hitting a structural block: the client was trained, usually in childhood, that it was not safe to make demands of anyone. The dominating anger requires the directed-at-someone structure, and the directed-at-someone structure is what was foreclosed.

The diagnostic combination: the client can find dominating anger toward an abstract object, but the moment the target becomes a specific human (even an imagined one), the anger collapses into appeals, justifications, or victim-form. The collapse is the surface of the need-suppression substrate.

The intervention is [[make-demands-of-me-experiment]] — the in-session exercise that demonstrates the structural block by asking the client to make aggressive demands of the coach (with the explicit setup that the demands will not be granted). The difficulty is the finding. The client discovers in real time that the demand-channel was never installed, which surfaces the need-suppression layer the dominating anger had been crashing against.

Once the need-suppression layer is named, the work can address it directly. The dominating anger then has a clear path: not just "fuck you, you will not take this from me" toward a circumstance, but "I have a need and I am allowed to want it expressed" toward a real other. The full release of the dominating form requires both — the energetic refusal and the permission to direct that refusal at a person.

This variant adds a third element to the distinction: in addition to the two anger forms, there is a structural pre-condition (the demand-channel) that determines whether the dominating form can land on a human target. For self-reliant types and compliance-trained types, the work on the dominating form often has to include rebuilding the demand-channel as part of the practice.

## When the Dominating Form Gets Aimed at the Internalized Identity Itself (The Train-Wreck Case)

A different application of the distinction: the target of the dominating form is not a circumstance or an illness but the internalized belief about who the person IS. The brokenness-identity. The "I'm a train wreck" or "I'm broken" or "I'm a mess" that the client opens the session by stating as if it were factual diagnosis.

The setup is the same as the abstract-target case (the dominating form has a structural target other than a present person), but the target is internal — specifically, the introjected sentence that someone, somewhere, once said about the client and that the client has been repeating since.

The move is [[fuck-you-im-not-X-against-the-introjected-belief]]: "Fuck you, I'm not a train wreck." The dominating-form is aimed at whoever installed the belief — parent, society, abuser, "I don't care," in Joe's phrasing — never at the self.

The failure modes are the same as in the abstract case but show up earlier:

- The client tries to convince rather than dominate. "I feel like now I'm trying to convince you." Joe's response: "That's right. You're trying to convince yourself and me and the world. That's not what I'm asking you to do."
- The client does the move with a victim-quality. "No, don't tell me I'm a train wreck." Joe: "So there's like a little victim in it, right? There's a little like, no, don't— you're not going to tell me I'm a train wreck. No, this is dominate."
- The client drops volume, makes the move silently or in their head. "I want to just do it in my head silently."

The move requires the same modeling and the same pushing into the dominating-form. The eye contact instruction ("Look at me in the eyes and say it to me") and the explicit confidence in the client's capacity ("I am pushing you right now. What makes that the case? Because I fucking know. I know you're capable") are the tools for getting the body past the convincing-form and into the dominating-form.

When the dominating form lands on the internalized identity, the body shakes. The held charge of decades of agreeing with the introject discharges. The client may then spontaneously add content: "I never gave permission. I never said okay." The dominating energy, having moved, becomes available for ongoing claim of agency.

The integration that follows often pairs with [[you-are-going-to-get-annihilated-as-affirmation]]: the identity that was being dominated is, in fact, getting annihilated. The fear that arrives as the move lands is accurate. The witness affirms the annihilation rather than soothing the fear, and the work continues.
