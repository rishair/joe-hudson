# Triggered vs. Big Emotion (Trigger Is Any Disproportionate Reaction, Not Just Anger)

## The Distinction

A trigger is any emotional reaction that is bigger than the present situation warrants. The trigger is defined by its source (the past) and its size (disproportionate), not by its surface form (anger, sadness, freeze, anxiety, etc.).

This matters because the cultural shorthand uses "triggered" to mean "got angry." A person who never gets angry can therefore conclude that they are never triggered. Often they are triggered constantly; the form is just not anger.

"Triggered doesn't mean that you get angry. Trigger might be that you're like, oh, I'm just repressing that thing. Or it could be passive aggression, or it could be like extreme sadness or a lot of fear or anxiety. Trigger can be any emotional response." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

## The Marine Restaurant Story

The clearest illustration. Joe was at a restaurant. A couple sat down with a friend. The wife told the husband what to eat, talked over his choices, dominated every interaction. The husband -- "this big, like, Marine guy" -- never raised his voice. His facial expression was what Joe describes as "repressed rage." His responses were "like the most minute nods. Like, yes was like, uh, half an inch up."

If you asked him whether he had been triggered, he could honestly say "no, I never got angry." He was triggered the entire meal. The trigger response had taken the shape of containment and repression rather than expression.

"He could easily have said like, 'Oh, I was never triggered by this.' But you could see that he was like, I would almost say, violently shut down. Like, he hardly spoke. He, he was like, the facial expression was repressed rage." -- Joe Hudson, Triggered

Brett caught the implication immediately: "It could be a subtle freeze." Joe: "Yeah, exactly."

## The Forms a Trigger Can Take

The catalog from the transcript and adjacent territory:

- **Anger** (the prototypical form, but only one form)
- **Repressed rage** (the visible-but-contained form; tight jaw, half-inch nod, flat tone)
- **Passive aggression** ([[passive-aggressive-vs-aggressive-aggressive]] -- anger taking a covert route)
- **Extreme sadness** (the trigger taking grief form)
- **Fear or anxiety** (the trigger lands as threat-system activation)
- **Freeze** (the body shuts down rather than mobilizing)
- **Dissociation** (the person leaves the room without their body moving; see [[disassociation-as-numbness-layer]])
- **Hyper-rationality** (intellect kicks in to cover the felt response)
- **Sudden withdrawal** (the person becomes quiet, distant, or unavailable)
- **Compulsive caretaking** (the trigger response is to manage the other person harder)
- **Performance** (the trigger lands as putting on the social face; see [[the-happiness-filter]])

Any of these can be the trigger. The diagnostic is not the surface emotion. The diagnostic is the size and origin of the reaction.

## Why The Distinction Matters Diagnostically

The single most common error in self-diagnosis: "I never get angry, so I am not triggered." This error keeps the person from finding their triggers because they are looking for the wrong shape.

The same error appears in coaching when the coach reads only for visible anger and misses the repressed forms. The Marine in the restaurant looks unbothered. He is more triggered than the person actively yelling, because his trigger is fully contained and therefore unprocessed. See [[kinked-emotions]] for the related mechanism: the visible "anger" is often the kink in the hose, not the emotion itself.

The coaching move when this error is present: stop asking "what triggers you?" and start asking "what do you not say?" The withholding is the trigger's visible form when the felt emotion is contained. See [[say-the-thing-youre-not-saying]].

## What This Says About "Calm" People

A person who appears unflappable is not necessarily un-triggered. They may be the most triggered person in the room, with the trigger response so contained that no one (including them) can see it. The calm is the trigger.

This is one face of [[everyone-is-pretending]] applied to the trigger frame. A culture that valorizes "not getting triggered" produces people whose triggers all take repressed shapes. The triggers do not vanish. They go underground and emerge as physical illness, cold relationships, slow withdrawal from life, or sudden inexplicable collapses.

The reverse is also true: a person who is visibly angry is not necessarily more triggered than the calm person next to them. They may just have less repression.

## Reading Triggers in Yourself

For self-diagnosis, the more reliable signal than the surface form is the body. A trigger has a felt signature that is the same regardless of which emotion is on top:

- Sudden constriction in the chest, throat, or stomach
- Tightening of the jaw or shoulders
- A subtle pulling-back even when externally still
- The sense of "this is bigger than the situation"
- A historical flavor: "I have felt this before, in a different room"

When you notice any of these, you are triggered. The visible emotion (or its absence) is incidental.

## Reading Triggers in Others

The same applies to reading another person. Watch for:

- Sudden facial change that is too small for the moment (the half-inch nod)
- A flat or controlled tone that does not match what is being discussed
- The person stopping looking at you
- Repetitive small movements (clenching, tapping, foot bouncing)
- Sudden over-pleasantness or over-helpfulness
- Subject changes that arrive too smoothly
- A shift in the air that you can feel but cannot name

These often signal trigger more accurately than the words being said.

## When Someone Experiences This

- The realization, after years of saying "I'm not a triggered person," that you have been triggered constantly in a repressed form
- The discovery that the partner you read as "unbothered" was actually in containment the whole time
- The shift in how you notice your own state once you stop looking only for anger
- The recognition that calm and repressed look the same from outside
- The relief of being able to name the trigger you have been having that did not look like a trigger
