# Redundant vs. Safe

## The Distinction

Doing what your boss wants is not the same as becoming safe. The two are often opposites.

The strategy that feels like safety -- doing the tasks the boss approves of, staying in the assigned role, being seen as a "good employee" -- often produces the opposite outcome: it makes the person redundant. They become the most replaceable version of themselves. The reorg, the layoff, the AI implementation comes, and the person who has been "safe" is the easiest to remove.

The strategy that actually produces safety usually feels less safe in the moment: taking on the role no one else is taking, learning the skill no one else has yet, becoming the person who solves the problem the organization does not yet know how to solve. This strategy is uncomfortable because it requires doing the unknown task. See [[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]].

## What Makes the Distinction Hard to See

The fear-based engine of [[productivity-as-fear-based-engine]] is wired backwards on this question. The body interprets "boss is happy" as a safety signal. The relief of avoiding the boss's displeasure feels like safety being produced. The actual mechanism producing structural safety -- becoming uniquely valuable -- is invisible to the fear-engine because it requires moving toward discomfort, not away from it.

The result is that millions of people running productivity-as-safety produce redundancy-as-output. They feel like they are doing the right thing. The system around them is converging on a moment when their "right thing" will be the wrong thing.

## Joe's Words

"What most people were doing was making sure that they were doing the tasks that their boss would be happy with. Not making them safe, making them redundant." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

The bluntness of the statement is the work. The two outcomes (safe vs redundant) are not subtly different. They are inverted. Naming the inversion is what makes it visible.

## The 100-Middle-Managers Anchor

The clearest version of the inversion in action: [[100-middle-managers-redundant-vs-safe]]. AI was coming to take half the jobs. The safe move was to become the person who implemented AI. The boss-happy move was to keep doing the tasks the boss approved of. The 100 managers, by and large, did the boss-happy thing, which was the redundancy-producing thing.

The anchor example is useful because the inversion is concrete and the consequences are predictable. Anyone listening can see what would happen next.

## Where the Distinction Applies

The inversion appears in many domains:

- **Corporate employment:** boss-pleasing tasks vs. organization-transforming tasks.
- **Entrepreneurship:** investor-pleasing decisions vs. product-market-fit decisions.
- **Academia:** publication-game tasks vs. field-advancing tasks.
- **Coaching/consulting practice:** client-pleasing scope vs. client-transforming scope.
- **Relationships:** partner-pleasing accommodation vs. relationship-deepening honesty. (The inversion is real here too: long-running partner-pleasing tends to produce the rupture it was meant to avoid.)
- **Parenting:** kid-pleasing parenting vs. kid-developing parenting.

In each domain, the strategy of pleasing-the-authority feels like the safe path and is structurally the path to a future of unsafety. The strategy of developing-the-genuine-value feels less safe in the moment and is structurally safer over time.

## What to Do With the Distinction

The diagnostic question, once the distinction is visible: "How is my current productivity actually aimed at safety, structurally?" If the answer is "by pleasing the people above me," the strategy is most likely producing redundancy. If the answer is "by becoming the person who solves problems that aren't being solved," the strategy is producing actual safety.

The work is not to stop doing the boss-pleasing tasks. Some of them are required to maintain the position from which the safety-producing work can be done. The work is to add the safety-producing tasks consciously, and to recognize that those are the leveraged ones, not the boss-pleasing ones.

This is the same shift the [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]] practice produces. The three things that would make most of the to-do list unnecessary are usually also the three things that would make the person less replaceable. The redundancy-reducing tasks and the leverage-producing tasks are the same tasks.

## When This Distinction Lands

- When [[100-middle-managers-redundant-vs-safe]] has just been told and the person can see the parallel in their own work.
- When the person has been in boss-pleasing productivity for years and is starting to feel that something is wrong even though everything looks fine from the outside.
- When the company is announcing layoffs and the person is shocked to be on the list despite their "good performance."
- When the AI/automation conversation has surfaced for their role and the person is suddenly aware that the boss-happy tasks are exactly the ones that will be automated first.

## What This Distinction Is Not

- It is not anti-boss. The distinction is structural, not relational. A good relationship with the boss is compatible with the safety-producing strategy. The distinction is about where the productivity is aimed, not about the quality of the relationship.
- It is not pro-rebellion. The point is not to stop doing what the boss wants. The point is to recognize that doing only that is the redundancy strategy. The safety strategy includes the boss-pleasing as one component but is not exhausted by it.
- It is not about ambition. The distinction applies regardless of whether the person wants to advance. The person who wants to stay in their current role for 20 years still benefits from doing the safety-producing work, because the current role itself may be made redundant.
