# Being of Service vs. Wanting the Moniker of Coach

## The Distinction

Being of service and being a coach are two different things. Service is a way of being available to other people that can be expressed in every aspect of life -- in friendship, in parenting, in work, in community membership, in any encounter with another person. The role of coach is a specific professional structure with particular authority, exposure, and projections. The two get conflated. The conflation is the source of one of the most common errors in deciding to become a coach.

When someone says "I want to coach because I want to be of service," the question is whether they want to be of service or whether they want to be called a coach. The two are not the same. If service is what they want, service is available to them right now in every interaction; they do not need the role. If the role itself is what they want -- the title, the recognition, the structural identity of "coach" -- that wanting is something different, and treating it as service obscures what is actually being sought.

"If you want to be of service, you can do that in every single aspect of your life. So you don't need to coach to be of service. If you want the moniker over the being of service, I would say that that's a pretty good sign that something's wobbly." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

## The Two Sides

**Being of service:**
- A quality of how one is in any interaction
- Available in every domain of life right now
- Does not require a role, a title, or compensation
- Does not require an audience or a setting
- Cannot be taken away or threatened by outside circumstances
- Is itself the reward; produces no need for further recognition
- Compatible with any vocation, including non-helping vocations
- Does not need anyone to call it anything

**Wanting the moniker of coach:**
- A wanting for a specific identity-marker
- Requires the role to be conferred and recognized
- Often requires compensation as confirmation
- Requires an audience or setting that recognizes the title
- Can be threatened (by criticism, by loss of clients, by reputation hit)
- Produces ongoing need for the title to be reaffirmed
- Is a specific profession with structural constraints and exposures
- Lives or dies by being called the thing

The two can coexist. A person can want to be of service AND want the coach role for legitimate reasons (the role is the instrument through which they can be of service in the specific way they are called to). The distinction matters because the wanting-the-moniker can pose as wanting-to-be-of-service when it is something different underneath. The diagnostic is what falls away if the role is removed.

If you cannot be called a coach but you can still be of service to your friends, your family, your team, and your community -- and you are at peace with that -- service is what you wanted. If you cannot be called a coach and you feel diminished, deprived, or unfinished -- the title is what you wanted, not the service.

## The Wobbly Signal

The specific signal Joe names: when the wanting for the title rises above the wanting for the service, something is structurally off.

"If you want the moniker over the being of service, I would say that that's a pretty good sign that something's wobbly." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

The wobble points at one of several configurations:
- The title is being used to provide an identity the person does not otherwise have
- The role is being sought for the recognition it confers, not for the work it enables
- The coaching is meant to validate something about the person rather than to serve the client
- The role is being chosen as a strategy to feel important, needed, or seen
- The aspiration to coach is downstream of an unmet need that the coaching is supposed to address

See [[purpose-as-approval-seeking-in-disguise]] for the related read in the purpose-question domain. The same configuration: a wanting that looks oriented toward the work but is actually oriented toward what the work would confer.

The signal does not require shaming. It requires noticing and inquiry. If the wanting for the title is operating, the question is what the title is being asked to do for the person. That question is the work. Doing that work may reveal that the underlying want has nothing to do with coaching; it might be a want for recognition, for purpose, for a defined identity, for proof of value. None of those need to be solved by becoming a coach. They need to be solved as the things they are.

## "Be of Service Everywhere" as the Redirect

The redirect for would-be coaches whose primary want is service: do it. Now. Everywhere. Without the title.

This is not a consolation prize. It is the actual practice that, over time, may reveal whether coaching is the right specific instrument or whether service is the answer in its own right. The person who can be deeply of service in their existing life -- in their marriage, in their parenting, in their team, in their community -- is the person whose service is sourced in something real. That person may or may not become a coach; either way the service is happening.

The person who cannot find a way to be of service in their existing life but is convinced they need the coach role to do it -- that person is showing that something is being projected onto the role. The role is being asked to provide what the person cannot find in their existing life. The role will not provide it; the role will amplify whatever pattern made the existing service-finding hard.

## Why This Distinction Protects the Coach Role

If the distinction is held, the coach role attracts people who have already been being of service in their lives, who have already developed the felt sense of what service is, who are turning to the role because the work they are already doing has called them into a specific container for it. These coaches are sustainable in the role because the role is downstream of their existing way of being, not the source of it.

If the distinction is not held, the coach role attracts people whose primary want is the title and whose service-orientation is downstream of getting the title. These coaches will not be sustainable in the role because the role's day-to-day reality (people in pain, projections, depletion, exposure) does not provide the recognition the original want was for. The role is structurally bad at being what they wanted it to be.

The distinction therefore protects both directions: it protects the role from being filled by people for whom it is the wrong answer, and it protects those people from a role that will consume them while not giving them what they actually wanted.

## How to Tell Which Is Operating in Yourself

A set of diagnostic questions:

- Where in your current life are you already being of service? If you cannot name multiple places: the service is not yet the operative quality; the want for the role may be doing work the service should be doing.
- If you were told you could never be called a coach but could still help everyone in your life: would that satisfy you? If yes, service is what you want. If no, something else is operating.
- If your closest people told you "you are deeply of service to me in our friendship," would that land? Or would it land as insufficient because it is not the formal coaching relationship? The reaction is the data.
- When you imagine introducing yourself as a coach, what feeling arrives? If the feeling is "this finally fits how I have been showing up for years," the role is in the right direction. If the feeling is "now I am someone," the role is being used for identity.
- When you imagine doing all the same work but without the title, does the work itself still hold meaning? Or does the work lose its meaning without the title?

These questions are not pass-fail. They are diagnostic. They surface what is actually operating. The honest answer to them is more important than the right answer; the work is on what surfaces, not on producing a clean response.

## The Money Parallel

A related signal: if the engine is making money through coaching, the same wobble is present in a different form.

"If you're doing this to make money, I would also say there's something that's wobbly." -- Joe Hudson, The Cost of Ignoring What You're Called to Do

Money is not wrong as a downstream effect. The wobble is when money is the engine. The person whose engine is money will optimize the coaching for what produces money rather than for what serves the client. The structural pressure will distort the work. The same is true for the title-engine: the person whose engine is the title will optimize for what reinforces the title rather than for what serves the client.

The clean engine is the call. With the call as engine, money and title arrive (or do not) as downstream effects. Without the call, money and title become the engines, and the work is distorted to serve them.

## The Practical Form

For someone considering coaching: spend a year being of service in every domain of your life that is available. Friendship. Parenting. Marriage. Team. Community. Strangers. Without any title. Without any compensation. Without any audience. See what becomes alive in you when you do this. See where the energy moves easily and where it does not. See whether the service-orientation actually fits who you are or whether you have been performing it because it sounds right.

After a year of this, the coaching question answers itself. Either the work has been so naturally happening that the role becomes obvious because you are already doing it and a structure would help -- or the work has not been happening and the want for the title is now visible as the want it actually was. Either way the question is clarified.

Direct, clarifying, not punitive. Joe delivers this as a useful diagnostic, not as a moral test.
