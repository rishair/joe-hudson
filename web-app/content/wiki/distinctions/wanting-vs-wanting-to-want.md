# Wanting vs. Wanting to Want

## The Two Sides

**Wanting:** The actual pull of the system toward something. Felt in the body. Produces energy. The want is there before the thought about whether to have it. The system is already moving toward the thing.

**Wanting to want:** The wish to have the want. The thought "I should want this." The performance of wanting without the actual pull underneath. The system is not moving toward the thing; the head is just declaring that it should.

The distinction is subtle because the second can pass for the first in self-report. The person asked "do you want X?" can say "yes" while only wanting to want X. The yes is real; the want is not.

"Do you want the perfect mate, or do you want an ideal of perfection? ... Or are you wanting to want the mate? ... Do I want to want to get to lose weight, or do I actually want to lose weight?" -- Brett Kistler / Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

## Why This Distinction Matters

Goals built on "wanting to want" do not run. They cost energy without producing motion. The person commits to the diet, the workout, the new relationship, the business plan, and the commitment quietly drains away. The head keeps re-declaring the want. The system never agrees.

The conventional reading of this drain is "I lack discipline" or "I'm not motivated enough." The reframing is that the want was never there. What was there was the want to want -- which is a real thing, but it is not enough to move the system.

This is the diagnostic version of [[the-inner-board]]. The inner board issues goals the rest of the system does not endorse. The person consciously wants to want the goal -- the voice says "yes, this is what I should want" -- but the heart, the body, the underlying self never signed on. The want-to-want is the conscious overlay. The actual want is missing.

## How They Sound the Same on the Surface

The person who wants X and the person who wants to want X often give the same answer to "do you want X?" Both say yes. The verbal response is identical.

The difference is detectable by attention to what surrounds the yes:

- The yes from real wanting comes with body energy. The voice softens or strengthens. The eyes brighten. The breath moves. There is a felt commitment.
- The yes from wanting-to-want is flat. It comes from the head. The body does not participate. The voice is recitation. The yes is the right answer to the social question.

The diagnostic Joe uses is to check whether the wanting was felt, not whether it was claimed. See [[did-you-actually-feel-the-wanting]] for the move.

## Why Someone Wants to Want

The want-to-want is usually a response to a should. Somewhere in the person's history (parental voice, cultural script, peer comparison, romantic narrative) there is a story that says "you should want this." The story is internalized. The person, wanting to be the kind of person who would want this, generates the wish to want it.

This is not bad. The wanting-to-want can be the first stage of an actual want forming. Sometimes the wanting-to-want is the system noticing what it might genuinely want if it allowed itself to. Sometimes it is the precursor to the real want surfacing.

But the wanting-to-want, treated as the real want, will not produce the outcomes the real want would produce. Goals set from wanting-to-want fail in the same predictable way: with declining energy, increasing self-attack, eventual abandonment, and the conclusion "I'm not the kind of person who can do this."

## What to Do With a Wanting-to-Want

Three moves, depending on what is underneath:

1. **Excavate to see if a real want is buried under the wanting-to-want.** Sometimes the wanting-to-want is a cover for a real want the person is not letting themselves have. The classic example: "I want to want a healthy relationship" can sometimes be cover for the wanting of the healthy relationship that has been gated by fear ("my parents' relationship was shit; I'm afraid to want this because I'm afraid to fail at it"). The work is to feel the fear, which lets the real want surface. See [[find-the-want-behind-the-should]].

2. **Honor the wanting-to-want as data without converting it to a goal.** Sometimes the wanting-to-want is information about a direction the system is curious about but not committed to. The person can stay in the curiosity without setting it as a goal. The actual want may form later. Or not. Either is okay.

3. **Drop it.** Sometimes the wanting-to-want is purely inherited. There is no real want underneath. The system has no pull toward this thing. The work is to let the wanting-to-want go and find what the actual wants are. The relief of dropping the borrowed want is often the entry point to discovering the actual wants the system has been pursuing without permission.

## The Relationship Example

The mate example illustrates the distinction in a domain where it matters most.

"What happens in a human system is people will jump to, yep, I want it really quickly, but it's not actually allowing themselves to feel that whole process in their system. And when they feel the whole process in their system, they might find out something like, actually, I'm really scared to be in a relationship because my relationship between my mom and dad was absolute shit, and I don't want to recreate it. And the last two relationships I had, I kind of recreated it, and I don't want that. Now what you want is a healthy relationship. So is what you want a mate, or is what you really want to learn how to have a healthy relationship?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The "I want a mate" is the surface yes. The wanting-to-want is the felt experience underneath: the desire to have the desire, complicated by the fear of repeating a damaging pattern. Once the fear is felt through, the actual want surfaces -- and it is different from the surface want. The actual want is to learn how to have a healthy relationship. The mate goal, set from the surface yes, would have failed predictably. The healthy-relationship goal, set from the actual want, has a chance of running.

## The Weight Example

The same applies to the perennial "I want to lose 20 pounds" goal.

"Do you want to lose 20 pounds? Yes, I absolutely want to lose 20 pounds. Yeah. Okay. You said it. Yeah, right away. I didn't see you want it. I didn't see you feel the wanting of it." -- Joe Hudson, Why Your Goals Are Making You Miserable

The yes is immediate. The wanting was not felt. The goal is built on wanting-to-want. The diet starts; the diet ends; the weight is the same. The pattern repeats across years because the want-to-want never converts into an actual want and the actual want (which might be about energy, or about feeling at home in the body, or about something else entirely) never gets surfaced.

## What This Implies for Coaching

When someone names a goal in a coaching session, the move is not to take the goal at face value. The move is to check whether the want is real. The check is somatic: did the body show up when the want was named? Did the voice change? Did the energy move?

If the want is real, the work is to support its expression and removal of obstacles. If the want is wanting-to-want, the work is excavation: what is the actual want underneath, or is there one? Sometimes the answer is no, and the relief of recognizing that is the entire move.

The diagnostic question Joe sometimes asks: "Are you wanting to want this?" The question itself surfaces the distinction. Most people, when asked it cleanly, can tell which one is operating. The recognition is usually enough to start the real work.
