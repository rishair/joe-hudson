# Trust vs. Safety

## Definition

Safety and trust both name something important about being able to function in a team or relationship. They are not the same. Safety is the easier word, the more often used, and the more often weaponized. Trust is the harder word, harder to manipulate, and the better organizing concept.

"Safety's a weird one. I talk about trust. I steer away from safety a little bit, but it is a really important thing." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

## The Two Failures of Safety

**Too much safety.** A challenge is by definition not entirely safe. If a team or activity feels entirely safe, either it is not actually a challenge or the safety is being faked. Excessive safety produces flat, untuned function the way a guitar string strung too loose produces no music.

"There's a level of safety where, if you give too much safety, it's kind of like a string on a guitar. If there's too much safety, then it's strung too tight and it's out of tune. And it's not enough safety and it's too loose and so it's out of tune." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

**Safety as control.** A person says "I don't feel safe" and the implied demand is that others change their behavior. The word "safety" carries enough moral weight in current discourse that the demand often goes uncontested. The mechanism is manipulation dressed as vulnerability.

"The other thing that happens with safety is people, especially in this, in the modern culture, they use safety as a form of control. So they say, I don't feel safe, and then that means other people need to change their behavior. So that's a whole nother level of dysfunction that can happen within that word of safety." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

See [[dont-use-safety-as-control]] for the explicit anti-pattern, and [[vulnerability-as-control]] for the parallel weaponization of vulnerability.

## What Safety Actually Means in a Team

The legitimate use of safety in a team context is specific. There are things about the challenge that are inherently unsafe (you might lose the game, the company might fail, you might die on Mount Everest). Those cannot be made safe; trying to make them safe is denial.

Then there is everything else. Everything that is not the inherent unsafety of the challenge should be safe. You should feel safe to express your opinion. Safe to say "this isn't working for me." Safe to ask for help. Safe to be honest about your state.

"You need to feel safe to be able to express your opinion. You need to be able to feel safe to say, okay, I'm having a hard time, I need some help. All those things are necessary to feel safe, but you're not going to get the safety of the fact that you might not die on Mount Everest." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The football team analog: players on the field are not going to feel safe (the game is by definition unsafe). They can feel safe in the locker room talking about strategy, asking for support, admitting fear. The two are different. Conflating them produces either teams that try to make the game safe (which fails) or teams that prevent the safety of the locker room because it seems too soft (which also fails).

This connects to [[safety-vs-defensiveness]]: the body's signal of feeling unsafe is distinct from the body's signal of feeling defensive. Both deserve attention; they need different responses.

## Why Trust Holds Up Where Safety Fails

Trust resists weaponization in a way safety does not. You cannot demand trust the way you can demand safety. The asymmetry is structural.

"It's very hard to control somebody out of trust. You need to be more trustworthy by X, Y, and Z usually is a pretty solid, like, yeah, you've stolen from me and I can't trust you. So this is gonna end the relationship, or you need to figure out how to convince me to trust you again. So it's harder to manipulate other people with trust." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The conversation about trust naturally points toward what behavior would build trust. The conversation about safety often points toward what other people should stop doing. The first is generative; the second is defensive.

This is one reason Joe organizes team work around trust rather than safety. Trust requires the trustee to show up. Safety can be invoked to demand others show up differently for the requester. Trust orients the work toward mutual accountability. Safety in its weaponized form orients the work toward unilateral demand.

## When Each Word Is Right

**Safety is the right word when:**
- The body is genuinely registering threat (physical, emotional, or relational risk)
- A specific environmental factor needs to be addressed (an abusive person in the room, an unaddressed harassment pattern)
- The conversation is about establishing the conditions under which the harder work can happen

**Trust is the right word when:**
- The work is about how the relationship is actually functioning across multiple dimensions
- The question is whether two people can rely on each other
- The team is trying to understand its own dynamics
- The leader is trying to understand why their team is or is not engaged

Safety is upstream of trust. You need a baseline of safety to begin building trust. But trust is the longer-running and richer organizing concept for what makes a team work.

## What This Looks Like in Practice

A team member says "I don't feel safe bringing up the issue with X."

The safety frame says: X needs to change behavior so the team member feels safe.

The trust frame says: There is a missing dimension of trust here -- either conflict trust (the team member doesn't trust the team to handle the issue) or communicative trust (the team member doesn't trust X to receive it). The work is to surface what specifically is missing and build it.

The trust frame leads to action by the speaker (what would build trust here?). The safety frame leads to demand on others (what should they do differently?). The first produces movement; the second produces stalemate.

This is not to say the team member's experience is invalid. It is to say that the framing determines whether the experience becomes the start of work or the end of it.

## The Deeper Problem: Safety Isn't a State, It's a Story

There is a structural reason the word "safety" is so available for manipulation: safety is not actually a state of the world. It is a story we construct to feel comfortable enough to act. Brett Kistler names this directly from a context far from team dynamics -- BASE jumping, where the conventional question "is this jump safe?" has no answer because nothing is.

"Safety is an illusion. Nothing is safe. We will call something safe if we feel comfortable, if we feel like we can be in flow, if we feel like we are capable of navigating the environment... if we feel like we are going to survive it." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

The framing transfers to the interpersonal domain. When someone says "I don't feel safe," they are reporting a story they are telling about the situation. The story may track reality well or badly. The discrimination is whether the felt safety (or unsafety) is built from contact with what is actually present or from a construction that excludes parts of what is present.

This is why "I don't feel safe" can be wielded as a demand. The phrase reports an internal state, not a fact about the world. The implicit move is that the demand follows from the state. But the state is also a story, and the story can be one that requires others to change or one that is the speaker's own work to do.

See [[safety-that-connects-vs-disconnects-from-reality]] for the full development of this point. The principle in shorthand: the same word "safety" can refer to either a felt sense built from full contact with reality, or a felt sense built by excluding part of reality. The two are opposites, but share the word.

## Why This Matters for Teams

In a team context, the conflation of the two senses of "safety" produces predictable confusion. A team member says "I don't feel safe to bring this up." The leader hears this and treats it as a fact about the team environment. But the unsafety could be:

- A real environmental issue (a colleague's aggression, a punitive leadership pattern, a history of retribution) -- the safety-as-contact form, where the felt sense matches a real condition
- A story being constructed because the team member doesn't want to face the discomfort of bringing the issue up themselves -- the safety-as-dissociation form, where the unsafety is being maintained by the avoidance, not by an external threat

Treating both as the first kind always creates a different problem from treating both as the second. The discrimination is part of the work.

The trust frame helps here too. "What would build trust here?" routes the conversation toward the specific work to be done. "What makes you not feel safe?" can route into either a real diagnostic or a story about what others should change. The first form of the question is harder to wield. The second is easier.

## Tone

Joe is measured here. He acknowledges safety is real and important. He is making a careful distinction, not dismissing safety. The point is that the word is weaker as an organizing concept than trust, and is more available for weaponization.
