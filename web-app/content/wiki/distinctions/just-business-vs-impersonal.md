# "It's Just Business" vs. Truly Impersonal

## The Distinction

These two stances look similar from the outside and are opposites in practice.

**"It's just business"** is a defended performance of impersonality. The person has taken something personally, has reached an emotional charge they do not want to feel, and has hardened themselves to push the charge down. The phrase signals the shame or guilt that the speaker is unwilling to feel. It tells the listener that the speaker does not trust either of them to handle the real reason.

**Truly impersonal** is a stance in which the personal charge has actually been felt and is no longer present. The person stays open, names what is actually happening, and remains in connection with the other through the hard conversation. No hardening is required.

The reliable marker: hardening. If the speaker had to harden themselves to deliver the message, the message is personal. The hardening is the tell.

"Saying 'it's just business' means that you have taken it personally. You have some level of shame or something to that effect, and you want to push that down. You want to push that emotional experience down." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

## The Firing Example

Joe uses the example of being fired to make the distinction concrete.

**The "it's just business" version:** "We had to let you go. It's just business. Nothing personal." The speaker is protecting themselves from feeling bad about firing the person. The phrase asks the listener to also not feel anything. The communication is closed.

Brett's reframe of this: "It's nothing personal, which is really personal because it's like, I don't trust you to be able to handle my telling you the real reason that I'm firing you."

**The sad version:** "I feel really sorry that we couldn't make it work. We wanted to and we tried." The speaker stays in their emotion. This is more honest than the just-business frame, though it still centers the speaker's feelings.

**The truly impersonal version:** "This was our expectation. We made it clear. The expectations aren't being met. My experience is that you're either capable of doing what we asked, in which case you're choosing not to, which means you know on some level this isn't the right job for you. Or you're not capable of it, in which case you must be feeling like you're not doing a good enough job, which isn't great for your world either. So it's clearly not a match. How do we move forward? How do we help you get where you want to go? How can we be of service in a way that's mutually beneficial?"

This is the least personal of the three options. It states the situation, names what is true for both people, and stays in collaboration even through the ending. No hardening is required because the speaker is not protecting themselves from anything.

See [[three-ways-to-fire-someone]] for the full example.

## The Diagnostic

The clearest diagnostic for whether a stance is truly impersonal or defended impersonal: did the person have to harden, build a wall, or close down to take the stance?

"If you have to harden yourself in any way, if you have to become defensive, build a wall between you and other people, you are taking it personally. That is a clear sign that you are taking it personally." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

This corrects a common misconception: the assumption that being scared, sad, or guilty is what "taking it personally" means. The opposite is true. Feeling the emotion is the opposite of taking it personally. Shutting down to the emotion is what taking it personally looks like in action.

"We somehow get in our head that if we take it personally, we're scared or we feel guilty or we're sad. But the clear sign of taking something personally is that you have shut yourself off to your own emotional experience." -- Joe Hudson, Money Can't Oppress You

## Why People Choose "It's Just Business"

The hard conversation triggers an emotion the speaker does not want to feel: guilt, sadness, fear, the discomfort of someone else's pain in response to one's action. Rather than feel it, the speaker hardens. The hardening protects the speaker from their own emotion. The hardening also closes the listener out of any real contact.

This is the same defense mechanism described in [[defense]], applied to business and money contexts. The speaker is not protecting themselves from the listener. They are protecting themselves from what they would feel if they stayed open.

## Why Truly Impersonal Works Better

The truly impersonal stance produces better outcomes in difficult conversations because it preserves the relationship through the difficulty. The fired person is more likely to leave feeling seen rather than dehumanized. The negotiation produces a better deal because no one is bracing. The boundary is held without resentment because no resentment has been generated.

This connects to [[defense]] and [[boundary-with-vs-boundary-against]]. The clean boundary, the clean ending, the clean refusal can all be delivered with an open heart. The closing of the heart adds nothing to the boundary and subtracts a great deal from the relationship.

## In Coaching

When a coachee uses "it's just business" or similar phrases ("it's not personal," "this is just how things work," "I have to be objective here"), the read is that the personal charge is present and the speaker is pushing it down. The coaching move is to invite the underlying feeling. What would it be to actually feel guilty for firing this person? What would it be to actually want what you are afraid to ask for? Once the emotion is felt, the conversation can be conducted without the defensive frame.

## The Tolerating-Underperformance Inversion

A specific application of the distinction that emerges in business-delegation contexts: the phrase "it's not personal, it's just business" gets the structure backwards when applied to tolerating an underperforming employee. The clean impersonal business move is to address the gap — name the situation matter-of-factly, hold the standard, make the change. If the leader is *not* making the clean move, something is personal — caretaking, fear of conflict, identity protection, over-responsibility. See [[tolerating-underperformance-is-always-personal]] for the dedicated treatment of this inversion.

The distinction's general teaching applies: if the leader had to harden up to be "just professional" about the underperformance, they were taking it personally and pushing it down. If the leader can name the situation plainly and stay open, they have actually arrived at the impersonal stance — and the decision becomes available.
