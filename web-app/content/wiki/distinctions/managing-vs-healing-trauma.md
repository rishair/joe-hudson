# Managing vs. Healing Trauma

## Definition

Managing trauma means reducing symptoms, coping better, meeting fewer diagnostic criteria. Healing trauma means resolving it: the person no longer identifies as defined by the wound. The right outcome is not "symptoms are less" but "it's over."

Joe draws this distinction explicitly: "How do you define the difference between managing PTSD and healing it?" -- Joe Hudson, A Discussion with Dr. Bessel van der Kolk & Joe Hudson

## The Two Sides

**Managing:** Keeping symptoms under control. The trauma is still present but contained. The person functions better but the underlying dynamic remains. Academic research typically measures this: symptom reduction, no longer meeting diagnostic criteria.

**Healing:** Resolution. The person can say "it's over." Not that it did not happen, but that it no longer organizes their present experience. "I used to be an abused and neglected kid, but now I feel loved inside." -- Bessel van der Kolk (in conversation with Joe)

## Why This Matters for Coaching

The coaching orientation is always toward resolution, not management. The question is not "how do I cope with this pattern?" but "what would it take for this to be complete?" This distinction shapes every intervention: the goal is not to help someone manage their triggers but to help them feel what they have been avoiding so the trigger dissolves.
