# Direct Intervention vs. Structural Change

## The Distinction

There are two main forms of leverage when changing a culture. Most leaders default to the first, which is far less powerful than the second.

**Direct intervention** is action taken on a specific situation, person, or relationship. Hire someone. Fire someone. Coach someone. Give a bonus. Sit down with the team and dress them down. Each direct intervention addresses the instance in front of you. The intervention is bounded to the moment, the person, or the project.

**Structural change** is changing how the organization itself operates. How meetings run. How decisions get made. What goes on the walls. How communication is structured. How accountability flows. Each structural change addresses every future instance of the pattern, not just the present one.

"There is two main forms. One is what I would call like a direct intervention. So that would be something where I'm going to hire somebody, I'm going to fire somebody, I'm going to coach somebody, I'm going to undress or talk down this team or something to that effect. I'm going to give a, bonus. Those are direct interventions. Those are far less powerful than structural changes. This is how we do meetings. This is how we make decisions. This is what we put on the walls. This is how we communicate." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

## Why Structural Beats Direct

A direct intervention solves one problem one time. A structural change solves the same class of problem every time it would otherwise occur. The leverage ratio compounds with every future instance the structure prevents.

A leader who fires a problem employee has solved one problem. A leader who designs hiring criteria, performance standards, and onboarding so problem employees are screened out, surfaced early, or developed past the problem has solved every future instance of that problem at the same time.

A leader who coaches an executive to stop being defensive in meetings has handled one person. A leader who establishes a structural norm that defensiveness gets named in the meeting and the meeting takes a break has handled every future instance across every executive. See [[coach-defensive-behavior-out-of-the-meeting]] for the structural form.

## The Faffing Example

A canonical illustration. Joe noticed a pattern in internal correspondence: "we could kind of do this or this, I don't know. And somebody else going, oh, that's a cool idea, I guess." He named the pattern "faffing." The direct intervention would have been to talk to the people in the specific email thread, coach them on clarity, model better communication. Instead, the structural change was made: every internal correspondence ends with "action needed:" followed by what the action is, who is supposed to do it, and by when.

"And so we just put one structural change in, which is at the end of every correspondence, you write action needed, colon, what the action is, who's supposed to do it, when they're supposed to do it by. And in doing that, all the, almost all, maybe 90% of the faffing stopped." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The 90% number is the point. A direct intervention on each instance of faffing would have addressed each instance and missed the rest. The structural change reduced the entire category by an order of magnitude with one design decision. See [[require-an-action-at-the-end-of-every-email]] for the full practice this references.

## The Question That Generates Structural Change

The mental move that produces structural change instead of direct intervention is a question the leader asks when they encounter a frustration: "how do I change this so it never happens again?" not "how do I fix this one?"

"Anytime that I'm frustrated inside the company, I don't say, how do I change this? I say, how do I change this so it never happens again in the company? You know, if I see incompetency somewhere, I don't say, how do I help that person not be incompetent? I say, how do I make it so that incompetency doesn't happen inside the company generally?" -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

The question moves the leader's attention from the instance to the structure. The same frustration that would have produced a direct intervention now produces a structural redesign. See [[ask-how-do-i-change-this-so-it-never-happens-again]] for the move version of this question.

## What Determines Whether Structural Change Works

Structural change is more powerful but also more demanding. It works when two conditions are met:

**It empowers people.** The structure gives people more agency in their roles, not less. The faffing rule does not infantilize anyone -- it asks everyone to be specific about what they want and what they're committing to.

**It ensures quality.** The structure prevents the failure mode the direct intervention would have addressed. The faffing rule actually reduces faffing because the structural pressure operates on every message.

If the structural change empowers without ensuring quality, you get chaos. If it ensures quality without empowering, you get bureaucracy. The two together are the design target.

"The way to think about what, how you make those tools, however, is how do I empower people and how do I ensure quality? If you can do those two things in a decentralized way, then, then it works." -- Joe Hudson, The Secret to Thriving Company Culture

See [[structure-and-empowerment]] for the broader application of this design criterion.

## When Direct Intervention Still Matters

Direct intervention is not useless. It is the right move when:

- The structural change has not yet been designed and the instance needs handling now.
- The instance is the diagnostic that reveals the missing structure -- the direct intervention surfaces the pattern, then the structural change captures it.
- The person in front of you needs to be met as a person, not as a category. Some moments require direct human engagement that no structure can substitute for.
- The structural lever is unavailable (e.g., you are not the leader, or the structural decision-makers are not present).

The error is not in using direct intervention. The error is using direct intervention as the only lever, instance after instance, when the same pattern keeps recurring. Each recurrence is information that the structure is missing.

## Why Most Leaders Default to Direct

Direct intervention is faster to do, more visible, and more satisfying. Firing the problem person creates immediate change. Talking down the team produces an immediate response. Coaching the executive feels like progress because the conversation happens.

Structural change is slower, less visible, and less personally validating. The design conversation about how decisions get made takes longer than just making the decision. The new meeting format takes weeks to land. The action-needed rule does not produce visible drama. The leverage of structural change is realized over time, not immediately.

Leaders who optimize for immediate visibility default to direct intervention. Leaders who optimize for compounding leverage default to structural change. The latter produce dramatically different organizations over years, even when the daily activity looks less impressive.

## How the Two Interact

In practice, the two forms work in sequence. A direct intervention happens because something needs handling now. After the intervention, the question is asked: how do I change this so it never happens again? The answer is the structural change. The next instance is prevented by structure rather than by another direct intervention.

This sequence is the leader's daily discipline. Every direct intervention is a candidate for a structural follow-up. Most do not get one, which is why most leaders are perpetually intervening directly on patterns they have already addressed multiple times.

## The Measurement Precondition for Large-Scale Cultural Change

At the scale of large organizations (Joe references companies of 100,000 people, or even countries), the principle has a precondition that smaller organizations can sometimes skip: you have to be able to measure the culture and measure your experiments on it. Without measurement, structural change at scale is unverifiable, and most attempts fail without the leader knowing why.

"You have to have a way to measure the culture. You have to have a way to run experiments on the culture and measure the results of that experiment so you know if you're having any impact. So typically, people will do a cultural thing where they'll say, okay, we're going to do this new perf, which is a performance review, or a new KPI format, and we'll whatever, we'll do bonuses a different way and it'll change the culture. But they don't know if it actually did. Like they're just kind of listening to the ground. And so you need some way to measure what's gonna happen." -- Joe Hudson, "I Block 3 hours a Month for Hard Conversations"

The pattern Joe describes: large-organization cultural interventions (new performance reviews, new KPI formats, new bonus structures) are launched without measurement infrastructure. The leader does the experiment, listens to the ground, and forms an opinion about whether it worked. The opinion is mostly noise, because the underlying measurement does not exist.

The structural-change discipline at scale requires the discipline of measurement at scale. The leader who wants to change a large culture has to first build the apparatus to measure whether the changes are working. The apparatus is itself a structural intervention — measurement-as-a-system, rather than measurement as a one-time exercise.

"As far as the tools, there's literally hundreds of tools that you can experiment with. But if you don't have a way to measure your experiments, it's never going to work." -- Joe Hudson

The measurement-of-experiments principle is the broader form. At smaller scale, the leader can sometimes see the effect of a structural change directly (the faffing-stopped example was visible without formal measurement). At larger scale, the effect becomes invisible without instrumentation. The leader has to invest in the instrumentation first, then the structural changes become legible.

## The Three Levers at Large Scale

The principle, at large-organization scale, expands from two levers (direct intervention, structural change) to three:

1. **Direct intervention** — addressing the specific situation
2. **Structural change** — addressing the category of situation through institutional design
3. **Consciousness work on the leader** — addressing the cascade through the leader's own state

The third lever is most explicit at large scale. "Consciousness of the leadership is usually reflected in the organization. So especially if the leader's been there for a while. ... So on some level, just your consciousness, taking care of your own consciousness, will affect the culture of the company." -- Joe Hudson, "I Block 3 hours a Month for Hard Conversations"

The leader's own internal work is a culture intervention, separate from any specific action they take. The leader who is doing their own consciousness work is by that fact alone shifting the company. See [[spit-at-the-top-is-a-waterfall-at-the-bottom]] for the mechanism.

The three levers operate together. The leader who relies on only one — only direct interventions, only structural changes, or only their own consciousness work — leaves the other two on the table. The leader who runs all three (with measurement to verify the work) has the full apparatus for cultural change at scale.

## What This Maps to in Personal Work

The same distinction applies inside one person. The direct version is "I'll try not to do that next time." The structural version is "I'll redesign the situation so the choice doesn't appear in the same form." See [[avoid-the-decision-by-design]] for the personal version of the same principle.

The leader who has internalized the structural-over-direct discipline at the personal level is the leader who applies it at the organizational level. The leader who is constantly battling their own willpower one instance at a time tends to also be constantly intervening directly in the organization one instance at a time. The pattern transfers.

## Boundary Conditions

The distinction is not about whether structural change is always available. It is not. Some situations require direct intervention because no structural lever exists at the time. The distinction is about which lever to reach for first when both are available, and which question to ask after any direct intervention.

It is also not a claim that structural change is always elegant. Bad structural changes exist -- bureaucracy, surveillance, micromanagement-by-process. The empowerment-and-quality test (above) is the screen. Structural change without it produces a worse organization than no structural change at all.

## Tone

Joe describes this distinction matter-of-factly, almost diagnostically. He is not selling structural change as an inspiration. He is describing the leverage math: one form produces more change per unit of leader-energy than the other. The leaders who don't see this are leaving most of the available leverage on the table.
