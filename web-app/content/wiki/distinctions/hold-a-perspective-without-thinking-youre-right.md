# Hold a Perspective Without Thinking You're Right vs. Hold a Perspective and Think You're Right

## The Distinction

Both are available. Both are clean.

**Hold a perspective and think you're right.** A natural human state. You have arrived at a view, you find it compelling, you operate as if it is correct. This is normal. There is nothing necessarily wrong with it.

**Hold a perspective without thinking you're right.** A subtler state. You have arrived at a view, you find it compelling, you operate from it — and you also know it is your perspective, not objective truth. You can say "this is right for me" without saying "this is right in the world."

The first stance is the default. The second is the freedom most people do not realize is available.

"You can hold a perspective and not think you're right. And it's an interesting nuance and you can hold a perspective and think you're right. That's fine. And what I notice is holding a perspective and saying, oh, right, this is a perspective that's dear to me and I can't believe that it's right. I just know it's the one that's right for me. There's a lot of freedom in that, both freedom of action and freedom of not taking things personally." -- Joe Hudson, Q&A #3 — Common Questions, Uncommon Answers

## Why the Second Version Is the Freedom

When a perspective is held as objective rightness, three things become entangled:

1. The perspective itself.
2. Identity (being right about this is part of who I am).
3. The ego defense of being right (proving I'm right, getting agreement, refusing contradiction).

When the perspective is held as "right for me, not necessarily right in the world," all three remain present but separate. You still have the perspective. You still act from it. The identity-defense and the need to convince others are no longer attached.

Two specific freedoms follow:

**Freedom of action.** You can act from your perspective fully without the constant background load of having to defend it as universally true. The energy that was going into being right is freed up for actually living the perspective.

**Freedom of not taking things personally.** When someone disagrees with you, they are disagreeing with your perspective, not with you. The disagreement does not threaten your identity because the perspective was not held as your identity. See [[humility]] as the state where this is the default.

## The Bay Bridge Image: Two True Perspectives, Same Object

A concrete image for the distinction-as-applied-to-relationships: two people look at the Bay Bridge from completely different positions. One is in the Salesforce Tower looking down on it. The other is underneath it looking up. Each takes a picture. Each picture is the Bay Bridge. The pictures look nothing alike. Both are true.

"You and I both look at the Bay Bridge and you're in the Salesforce Tower looking down on it and I'm underneath it looking up and I take a picture and you take a picture and then we argue over 'Is this the Bay Bridge or is this the Bay Bridge?' 'No, this is the Bay Bridge, but they're just different perspectives and they're both true.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The image makes the principle land in couples conflict specifically. The fight that says "this is what happened" -- "no, this is what happened" usually has both people taking accurate pictures from different positions. Both pictures are real. Both are partial. Neither is the bridge. The bridge does not collapse into one picture; the bridge has both pictures and many more.

The relevant move for the fight is to recognize that the partner's perspective is also a real picture of the bridge, taken from where they were standing. It is not made-up. It is not wrong. It is a different vantage point on the same situation, and the situation is large enough to hold both.

"So there's no right or wrong. There's no, this isn't the Bay Bridge. This is the Bay Bridge. There's really learning. And that's really important because what we really want is to be seen in a fight. What we're really looking for is to be seen." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The Bay Bridge image, paired with the want-to-be-seen recognition, gives the fight a different shape. The fight stops being about who has the correct picture and becomes about whether each person's picture is being seen. The seeing is what was actually being asked for; the rightness fight was the indirect way of asking for it.

## The Even-Harder Version: There Is No Truth Without Context

Joe goes further than "both perspectives are true." The harder version: there is no truth at all about most human-relevant questions, only truth-in-context. Even physics-flavored facts are context-dependent.

"Water boils at whatever, 100 degrees Celsius, whatever. At sea level, water boils at 100 degrees Celsius. On what planet? At sea level 10,000 years ago? At sea level today? Because there are different sea levels. So there's always context to whatever truth -- there's no truth without that context as far as a human's perspective goes." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The implication: in relational disputes, the "facts" each partner is defending are almost always heavily context-dependent. The context includes their childhood, their emotional state at the time, what came before, what came after, what they were attending to, what they were missing. Two partners with identical access to the same situation will pull different facts because they were in different contexts when the situation happened. Neither is lying. Both are reporting accurately from inside their context.

This is the underlying reason perspective-as-not-objective-truth is structurally accurate rather than merely useful. Even what looks like factual disagreement is usually context-dependent reporting from two different positions.

## Your Perspective Is Also Incomplete

The corollary: your own perspective is not just one of many; it is necessarily incomplete. There is always something you are not seeing. Naming this is part of what releases the defense-of-rightness.

"Both of them are incomplete, I think is just as important of a realization, but to realize your perspective is absolutely incomplete, or I would say wrong, if you like need to put a fine point on it, there's something that I absolutely am not seeing." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The realization "I am missing something" is not the same as "I am wrong." It is structurally weaker and more accurate. The partner who can hold "I have a perspective, I trust it, and I know I am missing something" is in a different stance than either "I am right" or "all perspectives are equally true." The middle position is the working position.

"Yeah, there's something I'm not missing. There's some way I'm wrong. Guarantee. I guarantee that like I can only look at this thing from this angle and therefore there's an angle I'm missing. There's something I am missing there." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

This is the felt-sense practice of the principle. Going into the conversation already knowing you are missing something disarms the defense-of-rightness in advance. The partner is no longer the threat to your correctness; the partner is the candidate to show you the angle you cannot see.

## What Holding This in a Fight Requires You to Feel

Joe and Brett surface a deeper move beyond the perspective-recognition: the willingness to feel what you would have to feel if you cannot collapse the partner into "wrong."

"What do I have to feel if I can't collapse my partner into wrong? Or right." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The collapse-into-wrong is the defense against feeling. As long as the partner is wrong, you do not have to feel: their pain (because they brought it on themselves), your own grief (because you are still in the fight), the love that has been hidden under the conflict, or your own implication in what produced the situation. Collapsing them as wrong protects you from all of it.

The stance of "this is my perspective, I am not collapsing them into wrong" requires you to feel everything the collapse was protecting you from. This is the structural cost of integrating the distinction. The intellectual move is easy. The emotional move is the actual work.

"That's the most important thing. We could argue over the semantics of truth, but what you just said is absolutely the most important thing, which is what do I have to feel if I can't collapse my partner into wrong?" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

## This Is Not Saying "All Perspectives Are Equal"

A common misreading: this distinction collapses into "everything is relative, all views are equal." It does not. You can hold a perspective with full conviction, act on it strongly, work for what it implies in the world — and still hold the meta-position that it is yours, not universal. The conviction lives. The claim of objective rightness drops away. Both at once.

The Tibetan Buddhist principle Joe references elsewhere captures the same posture: [[mind-wide-as-sky-action-fine-as-barley]]. The mind is wide enough to know that at some level no perspective is the truth. The action is precise and committed to what one has determined to do.

## The Connection to VIEW

This distinction is what makes [[view]] compatible with having strong perspectives. The standard misreading of VIEW is "be impartial, do not have opinions." The actual teaching: VIEW is impartial about the *truth-claim* attached to perspectives, not about the perspectives themselves. The person in VIEW can have strong perspectives and care deeply about them and still not be defending them as universal truth.

A person who has confused VIEW with not-having-perspectives ends up exhausted, because they are trying to suppress the natural human activity of arriving at views. The exhaustion is what produces the [[what-if-i-dont-want-to-be-in-view]] concern. The release is recognizing that perspectives are fine; the only thing VIEW asks you to drop is the identity-attachment to being objectively correct.

## The Connection to Identity

This distinction is one of the cleanest tests of where identity has solidified around a view. Try the test: take a perspective you hold. Can you say "this is what I think and I don't know if it's right"? If the answer feels easy, the perspective is not identity. If the answer feels impossible — if you find yourself wanting to argue for the objective rightness of the perspective even in private — the perspective has become identity. See [[identity]] for the broader frame.

The work then is not to drop the perspective. It is to soften the identity-attachment so the perspective can be held without the defense load.

## What It Sounds Like

The state where this distinction is alive in someone's speech:

- "Here's how I see it. I'm not going to tell you it's the right way to see it. It's how I see it."
- "I'm committed to this. I'm doing this. I don't think anyone else should necessarily do this."
- "This is what's true for me. It might not be true for you."
- "I'm going to act from this, and I'm open to being wrong."
- "I disagree with you and I'm not trying to change your mind."

The state where the distinction is collapsed (and the perspective has become identity):

- "Anyone who actually thinks about this honestly would agree with me."
- "The truth is..."
- "I just can't understand how someone could think otherwise."
- "If they really understood, they would see I'm right."
- "I have to make them see."

## Three Reasons This Is Hard

The distinction is hard to inhabit because the rigid form (perspective-as-identity-as-rightness) provides three things the open form gives up. Each can be named explicitly:

1. **You usually have to feel something you don't want to feel.** Holding both sides of an opposition means feeling the discomfort, fear, or loss in each. The defended position protects you from the felt material of the opposing side. Letting both be true means feeling both.

2. **The way you create distinction is the way you define your personality.** To hold both the right and the left as partially true requires letting go of some of the identity you have built on the distinction. The identity defends itself by refusing the both-and.

3. **You don't get to be right anymore.** You can't believe the voice in your head as universal truth. You can't trust your own thoughts as the final answer. The certainty-ground dissolves.

"One is you usually have to feel something you don't want to feel. Two is the way that we create distinction is the way that we define our personality. So to be able to see the wisdom of the Biden side and the Trump side. Whoa, man, you're gonna have to let go of some of your personality. You're gonna have to feel certain things... The other reason it's scary is because you don't get to be right anymore. You can't believe the voice in your head. You can't believe your own thoughts." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free (Coaching Reaction Video)

The cost is real. People who have not yet integrated this distinction are not being unreasonable; they are protecting themselves from the felt cost. The work is not arguing them into the position. The work is helping them feel what they have been avoiding feeling, so the protection is no longer needed.

## Being Right Is Wired to Being Loved (Culturally)

A deeper reason the rigid form is hard to release: in many cultures, being right is associated with being loved, accepted, and safe. Releasing the claim to rightness threatens not just identity but the perceived availability of love.

"In our culture, being right is tied with being accepted, being loved. So if we're not right, it's not just that it feels uncomfortable and groundless, it's also that our entire body has associated it with a loss of love." -- Brett Kistler (in conversation with Joe), How Saying "No" Sets You Free (Coaching Reaction Video)

Joe extends the observation: letting go of rightness requires being willing to feel both the love that has been lost and the love that cannot be lost. The second is even scarier than the first.

"That's right, that's right. Feeling the love that can't be lost, which is even scarier. And the love that has been lost." -- Joe Hudson, How Saying "No" Sets You Free (Coaching Reaction Video)

The asymmetry matters. Most people understand that releasing rightness will trigger the grief of love-lost. Fewer understand that it will also trigger contact with love-that-cannot-be-lost — the unconditional ground of being that is not contingent on being correct. The second is scarier because it dissolves the entire transactional structure of the self. There is nothing to defend because there is nothing being earned.

This is why the distinction is not just an intellectual move. The felt work it requires goes all the way down to the structure that conditions love on performance. See [[whatever-you-think-you-need-to-be-lovable-is-self-rejection]] and [[wanting-got-wired-to-unlovability]] for the underlying childhood wirings.

## The Coaching Application: Pointing at Truth From Where the Listener Stands

This distinction has a specific application for coaches and teachers. A coach who has integrated it can deliver opposing teachings to different people without internal contradiction, because the coach is not identified with the specific content of any teaching. The coach can say "money is the sun" to one person and "capitalism is wrong, just simply" to the next, because the freedom being pointed at is the same.

See [[point-at-truth-from-where-they-stand]] for the principle that makes this coaching move available. The principle requires the distinction; the distinction is the felt-sense capacity that lets the principle be lived rather than merely understood.

A coach who has not integrated this distinction will accidentally teach their identification with their teachings, no matter what the teachings say. The student receives the identification, not the freedom. A coach who has integrated it can deliver any content cleanly, because the content is an instrument and the freedom is the goal.

## When This Distinction Helps

- When someone is in repeated conflicts about a perspective and the conflicts feel exhausting.
- When someone is afraid to have strong perspectives because they think it means they cannot be in VIEW.
- When someone is collapsed into "everything is just a perspective" and has lost the ability to act from conviction.
- When someone is in a relationship with someone who disagrees with them on something central.
- When someone has built an identity around a worldview and the identity is starting to be questioned by life.

## The Both-And Move

The mature form of this distinction is the both-and. You hold the perspective strongly. You act from it. You do the work it implies. And you also know it is yours, and you do not require others to share it, and you do not defend it as objective truth. Both halves are alive.

This is the same structural move as the both-and of authority and equality described in [[own-your-narcissism]]: "I understand the power I hold and I am not better than anyone." Different content, same structure. You can occupy your perspective fully and also be in equality with people who disagree.

## Tone

Joe holds this distinction with care. He is not advocating that everyone drop their convictions or hold all perspectives loosely. He is naming a specific freedom that is available to people who have been bound by the assumption that conviction requires the claim of objective rightness. The naming opens the door. The walking through it is up to the person.
