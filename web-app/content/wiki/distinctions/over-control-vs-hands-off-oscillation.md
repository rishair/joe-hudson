# Over-Control vs. Hands-Off (The Leader's Oscillation Trap)

## The Distinction

There is a binary that catches leaders who are working on their controlling tendencies. The two ends are:

**Over-control:** the leader sees a team member about to fail and jumps in to rescue them. Catches them before the fall. Provides the answer, takes the call, redoes the work. Disguised as care. Often felt as responsibility. The motivating story: "I can see what's about to happen. It would be irresponsible not to act."

**Hands-off:** the leader knows over-controlling is the failure mode they have been working on, so they correct in the opposite direction. Disengage. "You're empowered. You do you." When the team member fails, the leader keeps their distance. Disguised as respect for autonomy. Often felt as restraint. The motivating story: "I am letting them learn."

Both are wrong. They are the two ends of the same fear-driven binary. The leader oscillates between them because both feel like the corrective for the other.

"You can take it too far the other way, which is like, you do you, good luck. And that's not helpful either." -- Jaime Waydo, Putting My Needs First

"When I'm on the path, I'm often kind of vacillating between either over-controlling and trying to save them from the inevitable failure that I see them walking into, whether or not I'm actually correct about that, or I'm just hands-off and I'm just like, okay, they're just gonna learn on their own. And then I miss opportunities to give them really clear, you know, something that I see that might be difficult for them to hear and be able to deliver it lovingly and without fear and without like talking down to them." -- Brett Kistler, Putting My Needs First

## What Each End Costs

**Over-control costs:**
- The team member does not develop the capacity to handle the failure that was prevented.
- The team learns to bring everything to the leader because the leader will catch them.
- The leader becomes the bottleneck for development on the team.
- The team member receives the implicit message that the leader does not trust them.
- See [[caretaking-prevents-the-bottom]] for the structural mechanism.

**Hands-off costs:**
- The team member fails in a way that could have been informed by the leader's experience without being prevented.
- The team feels abandoned. They learn that the leader is not actually engaged.
- The leader misses real opportunities to offer clear observation.
- The team's trust in the leader's care drops because care without engagement is hard to distinguish from indifference.
- The leader withholds their gold (see [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]]) under the false belief that withholding is the corrective for installing.

## Why the Oscillation Happens

The leader who has been over-controlling reads the over-control as the problem. The corrective offered (or self-prescribed) is usually framed as "let go." So the leader corrects by letting go. The letting-go produces failures the leader was previously preventing. The leader experiences this as confirmation that they should be more involved. They re-engage. The re-engagement looks like over-control. The cycle continues.

The oscillation is itself fear-driven. Both poles are inside the [[fear-triangle]]. Over-control is the savior. Hands-off is the savior wearing a non-savior costume — the savior who has decided that the most responsible thing to do is nothing. The decision to do nothing is still a fear move. The fear is now of being the over-controlling leader again.

## What the Distinction Reveals

The two poles share a structural feature: in both, the leader is operating from fear and binary thinking rather than from clarity. See [[binary-thinking-signals-fear]]. The choice between rescuing and abandoning is a false choice. There is a third position the binary obscures.

The third position has several names depending on context: present without prescribing, engaged without controlling, witnessing what you see while leaving the action to the person, telling the truth without taking the responsibility. The third position is what [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] describes positively: you can be there, you can speak, you can love, without robbing either the pain or the gold.

The binary is the trap. The third position is the work.

## What the Third Position Looks Like

The leader who has found the third position can:

- See the team member walking toward a likely failure and not jump in to prevent it.
- Also see the team member walking toward a likely failure and offer a clear observation: "I see X happening. Here's what I notice." Without prescribing.
- Tell a difficult truth lovingly, without fear, without talking down. See [[love-vs-nice]].
- Let the team member receive the observation and make their own choice.
- Be present through the failure, not because the failure needed to be prevented, but because the relationship continues through it.
- Trust that the failure may be the strengthening event, both for the person and for the team.

"Maybe they're not gonna fail and I'm wrong. Maybe the failure is the learning that they need. Or maybe like them being weak in that role is actually what's going to strengthen the team by having the team see it and come around. ... I need to really mean that and let them kind of go and forge their own path." -- Jaime Waydo, Putting My Needs First

## The Corkscrew Application

The oscillation is itself a place on the [[development-as-corkscrew]] arc. Most leaders who are working on this distinction will get the third position right sometimes, wrong sometimes, and the wrongness will alternate between the two poles. The development is not "stop oscillating." It is "the oscillations become smaller and the third position becomes more available."

Each turn of the corkscrew presents a subtler version of the same choice. Early on, the over-control is loud (taking over meetings, redoing work). Mid-development, it is medium (sending the "have you thought about" email). Late development, it is sentence-level (the one extra suggestion at the end of a clean delegation). The hands-off pole has matching subtle versions.

The work is not to perfect the third position. The work is to develop the sensitivity to notice when you have swung to one of the poles and to come back to the middle. "I think I've done it right a few times and wrong a few times lately." -- Jaime Waydo, Putting My Needs First

## Where the Distinction Comes Up

- A leader is reporting that their team isn't growing despite being given autonomy. Check for the hands-off pole.
- A leader is reporting burnout from constantly catching their team. Check for the over-control pole.
- A leader has recently corrected from one of the poles and is reporting unexpected problems. They have likely swung to the other pole.
- A leader is asking "should I step in here or not?" The question itself is a sign of the binary. The third position does not feel like a step-in-or-out choice.
- A leader describes themselves as "classic savior" and is now trying not to be one. The not-being-one is likely already the savior wearing different clothes. See [[dont-modify-behavior-to-exit-the-triangle]].

## What the Distinction Suggests Doing

- Notice when you are framing the choice as either rescue or step back. The framing is the trap.
- Ask: "What would it look like to be fully here without taking responsibility for the outcome?"
- Practice the move of stating what you see without prescribing what to do about it. See [[never-rob-pain-or-gold]] for the form.
- When you have swung to one pole, do not correct by swinging to the other. Find the center.
- Trust that some failures are the development. The team member's capacity to handle the failure is built in the failing, not in the prevention.
