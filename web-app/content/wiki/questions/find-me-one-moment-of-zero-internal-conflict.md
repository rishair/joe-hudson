# Can you tell me one moment in the last year where there was zero internal conflict?

## The Question

The form Joe uses:

- "Can you tell me one minute, one moment in the last year where there was zero internal conflict?"

The follow-up after the person says no:

- "But yet you've acted in the last year. So how are you going to get out of this mess where you're not supposed to act? What's the level of internal conflict that you're allowed to act on?"

The question and its follow-up are doing one thing together: undermining the rule that says "I shouldn't act unless I feel internally clear." The rule is doing the work of preventing action. The question collapses the rule by showing the rule's premise is never satisfied. The follow-up forces the person to either name a threshold (which they cannot, because there isn't one) or admit the rule was never a real rule.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person reports they cannot act because they feel internal conflict, agitation, frustration, fear, or any not-clear internal state. The implicit logic is: "I shouldn't act when I feel this way." The question surfaces this logic and falsifies its premise.
- When the person says some version of "I'm holding back because I can feel the battle inside" or "if I feel any agitation, I shouldn't unleash it."
- When the person is using internal state as a permission gate. The question converts the gate into something visible that can be examined.
- After the calibration of the projected behavior has been done (see [[show-me-what-aggressive-looks-like-in-your-mind]]). The question handles the next layer of the prohibition: even if "aggressive" is recalibrated, the person may still be using "but I feel internal conflict" as the reason to hold back.
- When the person is using the inner state as evidence that they are not ready. The question shows that the readiness will never arrive on this metric.

## What It Produces

**The person says no.** Almost always. The chronic state of low-grade internal conflict means there is no clean moment in recent memory. The person cannot find one.

**The rule's premise visibly collapses.** Once the person has said no, the rule has nothing to operate on. Either the person never acts (which is not what they actually do), or the rule was never the real reason for the holding back.

**The follow-up forces a threshold answer.** "But you've acted in the last year." This is a fact. The person did act, despite the chronic conflict. So what was the threshold? The person cannot specify a clean answer. The threshold turns out to be situational, often interpersonal ("if there's trust and safety with the person, I let out a little more"). The threshold is revealed to be socially calibrated, not internal-state calibrated. The whole frame of "I can't act until I'm internally clear" was a cover.

**The person sees that the apparent rule is actually a position.** "I shouldn't act unless I'm clear" was the architecture of perpetual deferral. Once that becomes visible, the deferral cannot operate the same way. The person now has to choose what they actually want to do, without the rule providing the deferral for them.

**The pivot to the real question becomes available.** Once the rule has collapsed, the work moves to: what are you actually waiting for? What would let you act? The answers are usually about safety, trust, fear of consequences — material that can be worked with directly. The "internal conflict" frame was the wrapper.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person says no (the usual response):** move to the follow-up immediately. "But you've acted in the last year. So what's the actual threshold?" Do not let the moment pass. The person needs to feel the rule collapse, not just hear the question.

**If the person tries to find one moment (rare):** allow it. "Okay, when?" The person almost always finds a moment that was either trivial (no stakes) or anomalous (a singular event). The moment usually does not stand up to "and you acted from no internal conflict because of that moment?" The rule is still in trouble.

**If the person says "well, the threshold depends":** push for what it depends on. "On what?" Usually the answer is interpersonal. "If there's trust and safety with the person, I let out a little more." The answer reveals that the threshold is calibrated socially, not internally. The "internal conflict" frame was the cover for a more specific calibration about relational safety.

**If the person retreats to "I should be able to find clear moments":** the should is the rule re-asserting. Stay with the data. "But you can't right now. So what does that mean about the rule?" The data has the authority. The rule does not.

**If the person uses the response to escalate ("so I'm just always conflicted, that's the problem"):** redirect. "Maybe that's not the problem. Maybe the rule that you have to be internally clear before you act is the problem." The reframe is the actual move. The internal conflict is not the issue; the rule about what to do with it is.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session:

- **Person:** "I don't want to unleash that when I can feel that internally."
- **Joe:** "I see. So, so what you're saying is that if you feel internal conflict, you're not supposed to act."
- **Person:** "Correct."
- **Joe:** "Correct. Okay, can, can you tell me one minute, one moment in the last year where there was zero internal conflict?"
- **Person:** "No."
- **Joe:** "Okay, but yet you've acted in the last year."
- **Person:** "That's true, that's true."
- **Joe:** "Okay, so how are you going to get out of this mess where you're not supposed to act? Or what's the level of internal conflict that you're allowed to act on?"
- **Person:** "Um, in part it depends on who I'm interacting with."
- **Joe:** "Okay."
- **Person:** "If there's like a level of trust and safety with the person, then I let out a little bit more."

The structural details:

- Joe first surfaces the rule explicitly ("so what you're saying is that if you feel internal conflict, you're not supposed to act") to make sure both people are working with the same rule.
- The person confirms the rule ("correct").
- The question lands the falsification immediately.
- The follow-up ("how are you going to get out of this mess") forces the person to grapple with the inconsistency.
- The answer reveals the actual threshold (interpersonal trust and safety), which was the hidden calibration the "internal conflict" frame was covering.

## Tone

Casual. The question is delivered as if the coach is genuinely curious, not as a trap. The "okay, but yet you've acted" is a friendly observation, not a gotcha. The "how are you going to get out of this mess" has a slightly amused quality — the coach is naming the structural impossibility of the rule with light recognition that the person has built themselves into a corner.

The lightness matters. If the question were delivered as challenge, the person would defend the rule. If it is delivered as curiosity, the person can see the rule collapse without having to defend it.

## Variations

**The energy version.** "Can you tell me one moment in the last year where you felt totally relaxed?" For the person whose rule is "I should be relaxed before I can do this thing."

**The certainty version.** "Can you tell me one moment in the last year where you were totally certain about what to do?" For the person who is waiting for clarity before acting.

**The not-scared version.** "Can you tell me one moment in the last year where you weren't scared of anything?" For the person whose rule is "I need to not be scared first."

**The calm version.** "Can you tell me one moment in the last year where you were totally calm?" For the person whose rule is the inverse of what this transcript surfaces — that they need to feel calm before they speak.

In each variation the underlying move is the same: surface the rule that says "I need to feel X before I can act," then demonstrate that X never reliably arrives, so the rule is the architecture of perpetual deferral.

## What Not to Do

- **Don't accept the rule on its own terms.** "It's hard when there's internal conflict" lets the rule keep operating. The move is to falsify the rule's premise.
- **Don't substitute your own threshold.** "Just act when it's only a little internal conflict" replaces one rule with another. The person needs to see that the threshold-based rule itself doesn't work, not just adjust the threshold.
- **Don't make the person prove they could find a clean moment.** The question is genuine. If the person can find one, treat it as data, not as something to overturn. But usually they can't.
- **Don't let the rule re-form with new clothes.** After this question collapses the rule, watch for it to re-form as "I should at least be more clear" or "I should at least have less conflict." Name the re-formation if it appears.

## When Not to Use This Question

- When the person is in acute distress and the question would land as challenge. The move requires enough regulation for the person to look at their rule without defending.
- When the rule is actually doing protective work. Some "I shouldn't act when I'm dysregulated" rules are appropriate. The question is for when the rule is being used to defer ordinary expression, not when it is preventing actual harm.
- When the trust between coach and client is not established enough for the falsification to land as curiosity rather than as attack.
