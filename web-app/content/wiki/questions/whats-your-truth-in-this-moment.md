# What's Your Truth in This Moment?

## The Question

"What's your truth in this moment?" The variations stay close to the base form:

- "What's your truth in this moment?"
- "What's your truth right now in this moment?"
- "What's your truth of this moment?"
- "So what's your truth in this moment? Allowing yourself to feel all of it."

The question has two structural features that make it distinct from the broader "what's true for you?" question:

1. **"In this moment"** locates the question in present-tense felt experience, not in general autobiography. The person is not being asked what's true about their life; they're being asked what's true right now, this second, in this body.

2. **"Your truth"** signals that the answer is the person's own perception, not an objective claim. The truth being asked about is the felt truth of the present, not a verifiable fact.

Together, the framing produces a question that can only be answered by checking with the body. The head doesn't know the answer in advance. The answer arrives by the person noticing what's actually present.

## When Joe Uses It

- After the speaking-from-inside-the-discomfort move ([[walk-into-the-discomfort-and-speak-from-there]]) has produced an initial shift and the work needs a way to keep returning to the present-tense felt state.
- As the repeatable instrument across a session — asked multiple times, each repetition giving the person another chance to find their truth and another chance to notice the re-control that follows.
- When the person has been operating from a planned or rehearsed version of themselves and needs a question that can only be answered by improvising from the body.
- When the person says they don't want to be feeling the discomfort and the question can demonstrate that another mode is available: the question doesn't require them to manage the discomfort, it asks them to report from inside the present moment regardless of state.
- When the work has been at the level of general patterns and needs to drop to the level of right-now-this-moment.
- As the question that gets re-asked after [[name-the-shift-everyone-felt]] — the shift was real, the question tests whether the truth-state is still available.
- When the person needs a portable practice they can use outside the session. The question is short, repeatable, and works in any moment.

## What It Produces

- **An immediate check-in with the body.** The question can only be answered from the body's report of the present. The head can try to answer it but the head's answer will feel hollow; the body's answer will have weight.
- **An opening, often before any words arrive.** Joe noticed in the source session: "every time I ask, it's getting — here you are." The asking itself locates the person in their present-tense self before they've even spoken. The opening is what's available first; the words are downstream.
- **Surprising content.** The truth in this moment is usually different from what the person has been performing. When they actually check, the answer is often something like "I am uncomfortable but also invigorated" or "I am grateful" or "I don't want to be telling my story" — answers that surprise the person.
- **The re-control becomes visible.** When the question is asked, opening happens. When the answering starts, control often re-asserts. See [[the-control-after-the-truth]]. The question can be re-asked, and the re-asking lets the person notice the difference between the opening (the question-moment) and the closing (the answering-moment). The contrast is the practice.
- **Recognition that the truth-in-this-moment is not the same as the truth-about-life.** The person had been operating on a frame in which truth is a settled thing they discover. The question reveals that truth is a present-tense felt-state report that changes moment to moment. The frame shift unsticks the person from trying to find the truth and lets them simply notice it.
- **An accessible practice for everyday life.** Unlike most coaching questions, this one doesn't require a coach. The person can ask themselves the question in any moment. The portability is part of the design.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person answers from the head:** Joe usually doesn't accept it directly. Either he asks again, slower, or he goes nonverbal (see [[go-nonverbal-and-let-the-nonverbal-talk]]) so the head can't run the answer.
- **If the person answers from the body and the answer is a felt-state (joy, anger, sadness, gratitude, confusion):** Joe receives it. The receiving installs the felt-state as a valid answer to the truth-question. Then, often, asks again — the truth in this moment may have already changed.
- **If the person produces an answer with weight and then immediately produces a second sentence with control:** Joe names the re-control. See [[the-control-after-the-truth]]. The naming makes the cycle visible.
- **If the person says "I don't know":** Joe treats it as data, sometimes the most honest answer. The not-knowing is the present-tense state. Sometimes Joe stays there ("then that's your truth in this moment — not knowing") and lets the not-knowing itself open something.
- **If the answer surprises the person:** Joe often follows with "yeah, there you are" or similar acknowledgment. The recognition that the truth was different from what they expected is part of the wiring.
- **If the question keeps producing the same answer:** the answer may be more stable than the question's frame suggests, or the person may be defaulting to a familiar answer. Joe shifts the asking: nonverbal, slower, with explicit invitation to let the answer surprise them.

## The Practice Frame

The question is meant to be re-asked. Not once. Many times. The repetition is the practice.

"What's your truth in this moment? Allowing yourself to feel all of it." -- Joe Hudson, What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?

Each asking is a new instance. The truth in this moment is different from the truth in the moment ten seconds ago. The repetition trains the person to keep returning to present-tense felt access rather than settling into a static answer.

In the source session, Joe explicitly hands the question to the person as their practice for the days and weeks ahead: "what is it to speak your truth in this moment so you're not doing the self-harm? And all it requires is the same thing I'm doing, which is just asking, like, what's your truth in this moment?"

The question is a portable practice. The person doesn't need a coach to use it. The question generates its own next-question through the answering and re-asking cycle.

## Dialogue Examples

From the source session, after the first truth-emergence had been named and the woman was navigating the cycle of openings and re-controls:

- **Joe:** "So what's your truth in this moment? What's your truth of this moment? I don't care about the addiction or anything. Like, what's your truth in this moment?"
- **Person:** "Um, I am, um, I'm enjoying this conversation. It's a challenging conversation, but it's, uh, very, um, invigorating."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So even in that, that was beautiful because you actually allowed the truth out for a minute. And then there was like, then the control, like you started with the control, it came out and then a little bit more control."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The question is asked. The answer comes from a true place ("invigorating") and immediately gets recomposed ("um, very, um, invigorating"). Joe names both the opening and the re-control. Then the question gets re-asked.

A later instance, with the explicit instruction to feel all of it:

- **Joe:** "What's your truth in this moment? Allowing yourself to feel all of it."
- **Person:** "Yeah, there. Yeah. I feel, um, like very, uh, like sweaty. Like hot."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. Yeah. That's common. Just by the way, that's common in your, in your, in your, where you are right now in this conversation. That's a very common thing."

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The question, asked with the added "allowing yourself to feel all of it," produces a somatic-level answer (the sweat, the heat). Joe normalizes the somatic response so the person doesn't read it as a sign something is wrong. See [[heat-and-sweat-as-process-marker]] for the underlying physiology.

And the version where Joe goes nonverbal because the verbal access keeps reintroducing control:

- **Joe:** "Let's do the experiment again. It's changing just by you being aware of it. So what's your truth right now in this moment?"
- **Person:** "I'm confused. I'm recognizing something about the movement from being embodied... using nonverbal as a communication to verbal, that there's some juncture."
- **Joe:** "So go nonverbal for a minute and let the nonverbal talk. I know that's weird to say, but like, I'm going to say, what's your truth right now in this moment? And don't even think that you have to say anything. So what's your truth in this moment?"

*(Source: What's Beneath Your Fear of Being Seen?)*

The question is asked with the explicit removal of the verbal requirement. The answer that arrives is felt, not spoken — and Joe can read it from across the conversation: "so much power you are holding back. Holy fuck, you must have been just like this willful, powerful little girl."

## Why It Works

The question works because it's structurally impossible to answer dishonestly while remaining in present-tense check-in mode. The person can lie about their general autobiography. They can manage what they say about their childhood, their work, their relationships. They can't, in the same way, lie about what's true in their body right now — because the question itself locates them in the body, and the body's report is what arrives.

If the person tries to bypass the body and produce a head-answer, the answer has a recognizable hollowness that both the person and the coach can feel. The hollowness is itself useful data — the bypass is happening, the question can be re-asked.

The repetition is also part of why it works. A single asking can be evaded. Repeated askings, especially across a session, install the question as a frame. The person starts to anticipate the question, and the anticipation itself produces present-tense check-in even before the question arrives.

The portability is the third mechanism. Because the question is short and requires only the present moment to answer, it can be used by the person in their own life. The coaching has installed the question; the person can run it on themselves indefinitely. This is the deeper meaning of "all it requires is the same thing I'm doing" — Joe is teaching the question by using it, knowing the person will take it home.

## Tone

Quiet, slow, spacious. The question is delivered with very little pressure. The person needs room to actually check rather than performing an answer. If the question is rushed, the answer is rushed; if it's spacious, the answer can arrive from somewhere unrushed.

The repetition is even-toned. Joe doesn't sound impatient when he asks again. The question is being asked because the moment has changed, not because the previous answer was wrong. The evenness across repetitions is part of what makes the practice landable.

The follow-up to the answer is acknowledging, not evaluative. "Yeah, there you are" or "yeah, that's it" — the recognition that the answer landed somewhere true. Joe does not say "good answer" or "that's the right one" — both would install performance pressure on what should remain a felt check-in.
