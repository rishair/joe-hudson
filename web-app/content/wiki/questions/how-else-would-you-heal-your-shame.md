# How else are you going to heal your shame?

## The Question

The question Joe asks after the person has begun to see that conflict has value and that being themselves will inevitably offend some people. The question forces a confrontation between two things the person believes simultaneously: I want to heal my shame, and I want to avoid the conditions that produce shame.

The variations:

- "How else are you going to heal your shame?"
- "How else could you heal your shame?"
- "If it isn't inviting conflict? What's another approach?"

## When Joe Uses It

After the person has experienced the shame in the room (often through [[trigger-the-shame-live]]) and has agreed somatically that the shame is bearable. The question is timed for the moment when the cognitive frame is still rebuilding itself. The person has felt that shame is survivable but has not yet seen that their old strategy was preventing the healing they wanted.

Particularly useful when:

- The person presents [[im-afraid-of-offending-people]] and has begun to soften on the absolute frame.
- The person has been talking about "wanting to heal" while organizing their life around not feeling.
- The person has framed conflict as the enemy of growth when it is the medium of growth.
- The person is in [[the-shame-avoidance-loop]] without seeing the structure.

## What It Produces

A productive collapse. The person tries to answer and finds the answer is not coming.

"How am I gonna heal? It's good. I know it's always a good spot when I like lose all thoughts." -- Guest, Healing Shame by Being Ourselves

The lack of thought is the data. The shame avoidance strategy has been functioning as a substitute for healing. When the person tries to imagine an alternative path, the strategy is exposed as occupying the slot that an actual path would occupy.

Sometimes the person produces an answer that further reveals the structure:

- "I could shame myself." (They are already doing that.)
- "I could feel it in the desert with no people." (Shame is relational; the alone version is incomplete.)
- "I could project my parents." (They are already doing that too.)

Each attempted answer points back to the fact that real shame healing requires conflict with actual others, and that the strategy of avoiding others has been the obstacle.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the person loses all thoughts: stay there. The blank is the move. Joe affirms: "Right." Then gives the broader point: "If you love the shame, invite the shame, can't wait for the shame because that's the chance to get to know the shame better and to integrate it."

- If the person produces a non-conflict answer: take it seriously and follow it through. The shadow paths usually fail in observable ways. The person discovers the failure themselves.

- If the person tries to find a way to do the work in private without offending anyone: Joe gently shows that private work has limits. Shame is a relational signal; it heals in relationship.

- If the person responds with the breakthrough — "To heal my shame, I need to feel it and feel through it and learn about it" — Joe lets the person arrive there. The arrival is the work.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "Here's a weird question. How else are you going to heal your shame?"
- **Guest:** "How else am I going to heal my shame? Other than conflict, making — other than conflict and distancing myself from some friends in the process."
- **Joe:** "Maybe. Yeah. Who knows what they're gonna do? That's — that you can't control that."
- **Guest:** "How am I gonna heal? It's good. I know it's always a good spot when I like lose all thoughts."
- **Joe:** "The question is, how else could you heal your shame? If it isn't inviting conflict? What's another approach?"
- **Guest:** "To heal my shame, I need to feel it and like feel through it and learn about it."
- *(Source: Healing Shame by Being Ourselves. The question collapses the strategy and produces the actual prescription from the guest's own mouth.)*

## Tone

Joe asks the question lightly, as if it is just curious. "Here's a weird question." The lightness disarms the defenses that would otherwise meet the question with reasons. The lightness is also honest: the question is interesting more than it is heavy.

## Anti-Patterns

- Do not ask the question while the person is still defending the avoidance strategy. The question requires that the person already has one foot out of the strategy.
- Do not provide the answer. The question only works if the person tries to answer and discovers the structure for themselves.
- Do not use this in early-stage work where the person has not yet felt shame somatically. The question is for after the body has had a sample.
