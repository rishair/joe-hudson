# Which came first?

## The Question

When a client describes a producing-engine pattern (overachievement, perfectionism, chronic helping) alongside a wound (feeling misvalued, unseen, not enough), the coach asks them to trace the chronology:

"So which came first, the misvalue or the perfectionism or the overachievement?" — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The question follows up if the client cannot answer immediately:

"If you had to guess... did you feel deeply valued for who you were as a child? Or did you like, you know, 3 years old, you're like building the best fucking fairy houses in the goddamn planet. You're like, no, it's gotta be 10 stories, not 9 stories. Let's go." — Joe Hudson, You're Deflecting What You Want Most

The question is not about getting a precise answer. It is about getting the client to feel into the structure that links the wound and the pattern. The answer almost always exposes that the two are not independent — the pattern formed in response to the wound, and the wound was about being not-valued, and the pattern is the surviving-and-trying-to-be-valued solution to the wound.

## When Joe Uses It

The setup:

- The client has described both a pattern (producing, achieving, perfecting) and a wound (being misvalued, feeling unseen)
- The client has not yet linked the two in their own awareness
- The session needs to expose the developmental origin of the producing-engine without making the discovery feel like analysis
- The client is in a state where the wondering can produce a felt response, not just an intellectual answer

The question is a wondering question, not an interview question. The coach is not collecting biographical data. The coach is asking the client to feel into a chronology that the client has been carrying in their body without making explicit.

## What It Produces

The most common response is "I don't know which came first." This is data, not failure. The not-knowing usually means the two have been so fused for so long that the client cannot separate them. The fusion itself is what needs to be seen.

When Joe gets the "I don't know," he offers the follow-up that gives the client a concrete prompt: did you feel deeply valued for who you were as a child? The yes-or-no question lands faster than the chronology question because it points at the structure directly.

In the source session:

- **Joe:** "Did you feel deeply valued for who you were as a child? Or did you like, you know, 3 years old, you're like building the best fucking fairy houses in the goddamn planet. You're like, no, it's gotta be 10 stories, not 9 stories. Let's go."
- **Client:** "I always wanted to make things better. Like always. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Great. Great. And your parents saw that and felt you were just like, no."
- **Client:** "No, they were like struggling immigrants and they were like, is that useful? You know? So yeah."
- **Joe:** "Right. Okay, great."
- **Client:** "I mean, they're loving, but so they did come pretty similarly, same time."

*(Source: You're Deflecting What You Want Most)*

The answer that surfaces: the producing impulse ("always wanted to make things better") was there early; the parents could not value it for itself because they were in survival mode themselves ("is that useful?"); the wound and the pattern arrived "pretty similarly, same time." The fusion the client could not name explicitly was named through the asking.

The next move Joe makes is the reflective compliment ("I see you put a lot of effort into being in survival mode" — see [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]]). The reflective compliment lands harder because the chronology has just exposed the depth of the producing-engine's relationship to the wound.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client traces the wound earlier than the pattern: the work is to feel through the original wound directly. The pattern was the adaptation; the wound was the cause.

If the client traces the pattern earlier than the wound: the work is often to look at whether the pattern itself produced the conditions for the wound (e.g., the kid who was already producing got valued only for producing, which made the producing-wound deeper). In this case the pattern and the wound have a feedback loop.

If the client says "they came at the same time": this is the most common answer for the overachiever-from-survival-mode configuration. The pattern formed in response to the wound and the wound formed in the context where the pattern was needed. The fusion is the structure.

If the client cannot answer at all and gets stuck in head-mode: Joe sometimes drops the question and goes to the reflective compliment. The chronology was not the point. The exposure of the structure can happen through other moves.

## What This Question Is Not

- **It is not a therapy question.** The chronology is not being asked for the purpose of doing childhood-origin processing. The question is being asked to expose the present structure that the chronology produced.
- **It is not a request for precision.** The client's guess is fine. The work is the wondering, not the accuracy.
- **It is not the same as [[what-was-going-on-at-eight-years-old]] (if exists).** That kind of question opens specific developmental territory. This question opens the chronology between two present-day patterns.
- **It is not for every client.** A client who is highly defensive about their childhood, or who is not yet open to seeing the present pattern as developmental, may not benefit from the question. The coach has to read for the readiness.

## Tone

Curious, slightly leaning-in, with the readiness to follow up if the first version of the question doesn't produce a response. Joe's "so which came first" is delivered as a wonder, not as an interrogation. The follow-up with the fairy-house example is more playful — the imagined 3-year-old building a 10-story fairy house gives the client a concrete image to feel into, which is easier than abstract chronology.

The "did you feel deeply valued for who you were as a child" version of the question is delivered more gently. The directness is balanced by the softness of the tone. The client is being asked to feel into something significant.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the chronology answer surfaces the wound: move to [[reflect-effort-back-as-the-compliment]] to credit the survival-cost
- If the chronology answer surfaces the pattern-first: move to looking at how the pattern produced the conditions for the wound
- If the answer is "same time": this is data that the producing-engine is structurally inseparable from the wound; the work is the receiving that addresses both at once
- If the answer brings up childhood emotion: stay with the emotion before moving on

## Tone

Curious and slow. The question is delivered as a wondering. The space is left for the answer to arrive. The follow-up with the fairy-house example is more playful, designed to give the client a concrete image to feel into. The overall energy is collaborative — Joe and the client are looking at the chronology together, not Joe extracting information from the client.
