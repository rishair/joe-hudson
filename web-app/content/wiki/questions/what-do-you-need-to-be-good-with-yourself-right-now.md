# What do you need to do to be good with yourself right now?

## The Question

A present-moment self-okayness question that is the operational entry-point into self-approval. The question is asked with the explicit constraint that the answer cannot be in the future or the past.

Variations:

- "What do you need to do to have peace inside?"
- "Right now, at this moment, what do you need to do? Who do you need to be? To have peace with yourself right now?"
- "Just take a deep breath for a second and ask yourself this simple question: what's required in this moment to just be good with myself?"
- "What do you need to do to be good with yourself right now?"

The question is paired with a constraint and a noticing:

"And if you're not good with yourself right now, that means your brain has already gone into the future or into the past. So I'm just asking you right now, what do you need to do to be good with yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The "right now" is load-bearing. The same question without "right now" routes the mind into planning, into achievement, into preconditions ("I have to do this, then I'll be good"). The "right now" closes those routes and forces the answer into the present.

## When Joe Uses It

The question is offered when:

- The concern [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] is active and the person is reaching for approval as the path to being okay
- The person is describing the chase for love, judgment-avoidance, or recognition as the thing they need to feel okay
- The person has accepted intellectually that external approval is not the answer but does not yet have a felt-sense alternative
- The person is in [[i-have-to-X-to-be-free]] — the precondition trap, where the "good with myself" has been deferred to some future condition

It is also the entry-point question for the broader teaching that what someone seeks from others' approval is actually the feeling of being good with themselves — a feeling that is always available right now.

The question pairs with [[chase-your-own-approval]] as the operational form: chase-your-own-approval is the long-arc orientation; this question is the in-this-moment instantiation.

## What It Produces

The first response is typically silence or confusion. The mind does not have a pre-built answer because the mind has been running on the externalizing question. The pause is part of the work.

When the answer arrives, it is usually small. A breath. Permission to set the meeting aside for a minute. The decision to drop a piece of self-criticism. The recognition that nothing is required. The smallness is the point — the felt sense of "I am good with myself right now" does not require a large action because it is not actually contingent on action. It is contingent on the relocation of the question.

The follow-up that often opens: "And this feeling that you're starting to gather now, this feeling of like, oh, right now I can be good with myself — how much of what you're looking for from others is this?" The question retroactively reveals that the entire approval-chase has been about this internal feeling that was always available without the chase.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person says they don't know: Joe takes that as data, not as a failure of the question. The not-knowing is the discovery — the muscle of asking-self has been disused. He may slow down the question, offer the breath, repeat the constraint that the answer is right now.

If the person's answer is in the future ("I need to finish this project," "I need to lose weight"): Joe redirects to right-now. "That's the future. What about right now?" The redirection itself is the practice.

If the person's answer is conditional ("I need them to stop criticizing me," "I need to get the promotion"): the same redirection. "Those are conditions. What if the conditions don't change? What do you need to do right now?"

If the person finds the answer and a felt-sense of peace arrives: Joe makes the follow-up move explicit — "how much of what you're looking for from others is this?" The recognition that the chased thing was always available without the chase is the unlock.

If the person says "but I still have to act, I have a meeting, I have to be a parent, I have to make money": Joe answers the objection directly. The question is not asking the person to stop acting. It is asking them to act from this place instead of from approval-seeking. "What if you did all that from this space, right? Instead of doing it from the space of, oh, I have to get everybody's love and approval. What if you do it from this place of, oh, what do I need to do to be good with myself right now? What are you chasing if you have that? What do you need to chase if you have that?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

## The Structural Insight Behind the Question

The question is not a coaching technique. It is the operational form of a structural observation: the feeling people chase through approval-seeking is identical to the feeling produced by being-good-with-self-right-now. The chase has been misdirected at an external substrate when the actual substrate is internal and always present.

"How much of what you're looking for by not getting the judgment, by not getting the criticism, is actually you're just looking for this feeling, right? And notice that it never comes. Somebody doesn't judge you, but then they're going to judge you the next moment, or somebody doesn't criticize you today, but they criticize you tomorrow. But this thing, it's always available to you." -- Joe Hudson, Why Self-Possessed People Get Everything They Want

The "always available" is the load-bearing claim. External approval has structural unavailability — the same person who approves today may withhold tomorrow, and you cannot control the inputs. Internal good-with-self has structural availability — it requires only the relocation of the question. The question is what makes the relocation.

## Why "Right Now" Is the Specific Constraint

The default approval-seeking mind operates in two tenses: past (the evidence of past approval, the wounds of past withholding) and future (the planning to earn the approval, the dread of future judgment). The present is missing from this configuration. The mind has nowhere to land in the present because the approval-question requires a verdict that cannot be issued in the present.

The "right now" constraint forces the question into the only tense where good-with-self can actually be located. The location is not aspirational. It is immediate. The question is doing the relocating work, not just asking about it.

This is also why the question functions as a diagnostic. If the answer is in the future, the diagnostic is "the mind has left the present." The leaving is the pattern. The diagnostic does not require analysis; it requires noticing.

## Tone

Quiet, slow, slightly inviting. Joe delivers this question with explicit pacing — "Just take a deep breath for a second and ask yourself this simple question." The slowness is functional. The mind that has been running the approval-chase is fast; the question has to be delivered slow enough for the mind to land in the present long enough to ask it.

The tone is also slightly hopeful in the follow-up. "This thing, it's always available to you" is said with the small relief of someone naming a way out. The conviction comes from having found this himself, not from selling the practice.

## Anti-Patterns

- **Don't ask it as a quick check.** The pause is part of the work. A quick ask with no breath produces a quick answer that is still in the approval-mind.
- **Don't accept a future answer without redirection.** If the person answers in the future, the question has not landed yet. The redirection is the practice.
- **Don't let the person convert it into an achievement.** "I'll meditate every morning so I'm good with myself" converts the question into a precondition. The question is asked to dissolve preconditions, not install new ones.
- **Don't use it as a deflection from feeling.** If a feeling is moving — grief, fear, anger — the question can be used to skip the feeling. The right move when feeling is moving is to feel; the question is for when the person is in their approval-chase, not when they are in genuine emotion.
