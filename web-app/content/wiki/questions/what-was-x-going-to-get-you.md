# What was X going to get you?

## The Question

The question takes whatever the person has been chasing -- perfection, achievement, the promotion, the company, the money, the body, the relationship, the title -- and asks what that thing was supposed to deliver. The chasing has a target underneath the target. The pursued thing is rarely the actual goal. The actual goal is the felt-state the person believes the pursued thing will produce.

The signature exchange, from the Stephanie Harrison conversation:

- **Joe:** "What was perfection going to get you?"
- **Stephanie:** "Happiness."

Two turns. A long history of self-improvement, achievement, and rash-producing pressure named in one word. The pursued thing (perfection) was always a surrogate for the felt-state (happiness). The naming makes the chain visible.

Variations:

- "What was perfection going to get you?"
- "What was the promotion going to get you?"
- "What were you actually after?"
- "If you had gotten there, what would that have given you?"
- "What was the achievement supposed to deliver?"
- "What was the X going to get you?"

## When Joe Uses It

The question appears when:

- The person has been describing a long-running pursuit (perfection, achievement, money, success, body, status) and is reporting it has not produced what they expected.
- The person is locked into the pursued thing as the goal itself, without having examined what the pursuit was actually for.
- The person has reached the named goal and discovered the expected feeling did not arrive — the post-bestseller drift, the empty top of the mountain. See [[whats-next-after-i-reached-my-big-goal]].
- The person is presenting the felt-state absence (unhappiness, emptiness, lack of meaning) but is still organized around the strategy that was supposed to produce the felt-state.
- The person has been doing the work for years and is still chasing the same thing without seeing that the chase itself is the problem.

## What It Produces

A surprisingly short answer. Most pursuit-chains collapse to one or two words when asked directly. The answer is almost always a felt-state:

- Happiness
- Safety
- Belonging
- Worthiness
- Love
- Peace
- Freedom

The brevity of the answer is part of what makes the question work. The person has been operating an elaborate strategy whose underlying goal turns out to fit in a single word. The disproportion between the size of the strategy and the simplicity of the goal exposes the strategy as a surrogate.

The second thing the question produces: the recognition that the strategy was not actually a route to the felt-state. Perfection does not produce happiness. Achievement does not produce safety. Success does not produce belonging. The strategy was misdirected at the level of mechanism, not just at the level of intensity. More of the same strategy will not produce the felt-state.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the answer is a felt-state (happiness, safety, love, peace):** Joe lets the recognition land. The person can now see they were chasing a felt-state through a strategy that does not produce it. The work moves toward the actual mechanism of the felt-state, which is not the strategy they were running. In the Stephanie case, the "Happiness" answer opens directly onto Joe's structural teaching: "what I see with a lot of people, they think that happiness has to be achieved or earned." The misframe is exposed; the alternative orientation can be introduced. See [[how-do-i-be-happy]] and [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]].

**If the answer is another concrete thing (money, status, the promotion):** The question recurses. "And what was the money going to get you?" The chain keeps drilling until it lands on a felt-state. The recursion is the work. Each layer surfaces a smaller, more honest target. See [[what-was-really-more-important-than-you]] for the related drilling move applied to childhood scenes.

**If the answer is "I don't know":** The not-knowing is data. The person has been engaged in significant pursuit aimed at an outcome they cannot articulate. Joe stays with the not-knowing rather than supplying an answer. The discovery of "I don't actually know what I was after" is often itself the recognition that opens the work.

**If the answer is a stated goal but said flatly:** The flatness is a signal that the answer is still rehearsed rather than felt. Joe may sit with it longer, ask the person to feel into whether the answer is true, or restate it back: "So you've spent ten years chasing X, and what you were really after was Y." The restatement can surface the absurdity that the strategy and the actual goal do not match.

## Distinct From Adjacent Questions

This is not [[what-are-you-trying-to-control-for]]. That question takes a specific currently-running controlling behavior (jealousy, micromanagement) and asks what outcome the behavior is engineered to produce in a specific situation. This question takes a longer-arc pursuit (perfection, achievement, success) and asks what felt-state the pursuit was for.

This is not [[what-was-really-more-important-than-you]]. That question drills past a parent's surface-object priority in a childhood scene to surface the parent's avoided emotion. This question drills past the client's own surface pursuit to surface their assumed-felt-state goal.

This is not [[what-do-you-want]]. That question asks the person to locate desire in the present, often when they are out of touch with it. This question takes an already-running pursuit and asks what it was for.

The three questions are related instances of the same underlying move: locate the actual target underneath the apparent target. They differ in what the apparent target is (a controlling behavior, a parent's prioritization, a long-running pursuit) and in what gets surfaced (the surrogate-outcome, the parent's avoided emotion, the assumed felt-state).

## Dialogue Example

From The Plight Of Perfection / Happiness, the New Way:

- **Stephanie:** [describes pursuing achievements as a way to justify self-worth] "It was to be as perfect as I possibly could, and of course, no matter what I did, never got close to being perfect."
- **Joe:** "And what was perfection going to get you?"
- **Stephanie:** "Happiness."
- **Joe:** "So yeah, which I think is the — what I see with a lot of people, they think that happiness has to be achieved or earned."

The exchange demonstrates the speed of the move. The question is one turn. The answer is one word. The teaching that follows ("happiness has to be achieved or earned") names the misframe the answer exposed. The whole sequence takes three turns.

## Why the Question Works

People build strategies around assumed endpoints they have never examined. The strategy gets sophisticated; the endpoint stays unexamined. The unexamined endpoint is what holds the strategy in place — the strategy persists because the person still believes the endpoint will be produced if they execute the strategy correctly.

Surfacing the endpoint allows two tests:

1. **Is the endpoint actually what the person wants?** Often the named endpoint, once spoken aloud, sounds less compelling than the strategy implies it should be. "Happiness" as the answer to "what was perfection going to get you" exposes that the entire perfection project was for a state the person could not produce by being perfect.

2. **Will the strategy actually produce the endpoint?** Almost always, the answer is no. Perfection will not produce happiness. The achievement will not produce worthiness. The wealth will not produce safety in the way the strategy promised. The mismatch between strategy and endpoint is now visible.

Once both tests are run, the strategy loses its rationale. New strategies become considerable. The person is no longer locked into a pursuit that does not work because the pursuit's grip depended on the unexamined assumption that it would work.

## The Specific Case of Pursuit-of-Happiness

The question is particularly powerful when the answer comes back as "happiness." This is the most common endpoint and the most structurally compromised one. Happiness pursued is not produced; happiness produced is not pursued. The "happiness as goal" framing is itself the problem. See [[how-do-i-be-happy]] for the concern this exposes and [[5-ways-to-stay-unhappy]] for the structural teaching about why pursuit-of-happiness fails.

The Stephanie session is a clean demonstration. She built her entire life around a pursuit she thought would produce happiness. The pursuit produced rashes, panic attacks, and a breakdown on her bedroom floor. The endpoint never arrived. The question makes this visible in one turn: the pursuit was always for the endpoint, and the endpoint was never going to come from the pursuit.

## Tone

Direct, casual, gentle. The question is not therapeutic in its delivery. It has the quality of curiosity rather than confrontation. "What was perfection going to get you?" is asked the way someone might ask "what was that meeting for?" — as a real question about what the thing was meant to accomplish, not as a rhetorical exposure of futility.

The gentleness matters. The person has spent significant time and pain building the strategy. The question is not "your strategy was foolish" but "what was it for?" The non-judgment in the framing makes the answer easier to give honestly.
