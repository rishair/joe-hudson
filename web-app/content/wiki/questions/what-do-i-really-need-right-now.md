# What do I really need?

## The Question

A question the person asks themselves when they notice the body-state of looking-for-a-fight. The question redirects attention from the surface urge (the fight) to the underneath need (which is rarely a fight).

The phrasings Joe uses:

- "What do I really need?"
- "What do I really need? A cuddle? Do I really need to go into another room and just fucking yell for a while and then come back?"
- "Instead of going to, let's have a fight, go to what do we need to be connected?"

"To be able to start recognizing, oh, this feeling, this sensation, this somatic experience that I'm having right now, that's how I feel when I'm looking for a fight. And what do I really need? What do I really need? A cuddle? Do I really need to go into another room and just fucking yell for a while and then come back? You don't have to yell at anybody to get the emotional release, right?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The framing is small. Not "what is my deepest unmet need in life." Just: in this moment, when the body is doing the fight-seeking thing, what does it actually need? The answers are usually concrete and small: a cuddle, twenty minutes of yelling in private, food, sleep, naming the day to someone who will listen.

## When to Use It

The trigger is the body-cue of fight-seeking. The signature is described in [[everything-i-say-they-contradict]]: the wound-up state, the friction-seeking quality of attention, the felt sense that whatever the partner says you would find something to contradict about.

The trigger sequence:

1. You notice the body-state of looking-for-a-fight (the wound-up quality, the about-to-contradict-anything quality).
2. The noticing itself is the cue. The recognition that what is happening is the displaced-discharge dynamic of [[looking-for-a-fight-means-self-disconnection]].
3. You ask yourself: "what do I really need?"
4. The answer is allowed to be concrete and small. Cuddle. Yelling. Solitude. Food. Naming the hard day.
5. You move toward the actual need rather than the surface urge.

The question is part of a self-coaching pair. The companion question [[what-am-i-not-feeling-right-now]] operates inside the fight after it has started and the superiority position has appeared. This question operates before or during the fight-seeking phase, when the urge is present but the fight has not yet escalated. The two questions belong to the same family of self-asked redirects, deployed at different moments in the same arc.

## What It Produces

The question typically surfaces one of a small number of recurring answers:

- **A cuddle / contact.** The underlying need was for connection, which the fight was a corrupted way of reaching for. The reach was correct; the form was wrong.
- **Private discharge.** The body is carrying held charge that needs to move. The need is not for the partner; it is for somewhere to yell or move the body. See [[get-angry-in-the-car]] and [[go-to-separate-rooms-and-yell]].
- **Solitude.** The need is for the day to settle without input. Twenty minutes alone with no demands.
- **Food / rest / basic body.** The body is dysregulated by a missed meal, low sleep, dehydration. The fight-seeking is the body asking for care it cannot articulate.
- **Being heard about the day.** The need is to name what happened — the boss, the meeting, the failed thing — to someone who can listen without fixing. Often the partner can be this someone if the request is named.
- **Crying.** The held material wants to come out as sadness rather than anger. The fight-seeking was the surface displacement of something underneath.

The question is not a quiz with a right answer. Even a vague answer ("I think I just need something not to be a fight right now") is sufficient to redirect attention. The redirection is the work. Once the attention is on the need, the urge to fight loses its function and softens.

## What to Do With the Answer

Move toward the actual need. The specific move depends on what surfaced:

- If the need was for contact: ask for the cuddle, the touch, the moment of connection. "I'm wound up. Can you just hold me for a minute?"
- If the need was for private discharge: announce the timeline and go. "I'm going to yell in the other room for ten minutes. I'll be back." See [[withdrawal-with-vs-without-a-timeline]] for why the timeline matters and [[go-to-separate-rooms-and-yell]] for the couple-level version where both partners go.
- If the need was for solitude: name it as solitude, not as anger. "I need twenty minutes by myself. It's not about you."
- If the need was for basic care: handle it. Eat. Drink water. Lie down for fifteen minutes.
- If the need was for being heard: ask. "Can I tell you about my day? I just need you to listen, not fix anything."
- If the need was to cry: let the body do it. The partner can often hold this if the structure is named.

The throughline: the need gets met directly. The fight, which was a corrupted attempt to meet the need, becomes unnecessary.

## Why This Question Works

The fight-seeking state is a misdirected reach for something. The body needs something; the most-rehearsed pathway to get it (in many adults) is conflict, because conflict produces discharge and discharge produces a brief return to baseline. The mind in the heat of the moment cannot see other pathways.

The question opens the field of available pathways. "What do I really need?" is mechanically simple: it pauses the auto-pilot, names the underlying state, and invites the body to reach for the thing it actually wants rather than the displaced version. Once the actual need is named, the alternatives become visible.

This is the same machinery the principle [[looking-for-a-fight-means-self-disconnection]] describes. The principle names the structure. The question is the in-the-moment operationalization of the principle.

## Why Not Just "What Do I Want?"

The companion question [[what-do-you-want]] is broader — it asks about desire in general. This question is narrower and more situated. It is specifically about the in-the-moment fight-seeking state, and the answers it surfaces are concrete and small.

The narrowing is part of what makes the question deployable in the moment. "What do I want?" is a question that requires reflection, sometimes years of reflection. "What do I really need right now?" is a question the body can answer in seconds: "a hug, food, a yell, some quiet."

The two questions belong to the same family. The desire question is the larger arc. The need question is the in-the-moment self-coaching move. Both are versions of redirecting attention from the surface position to the underneath material.

## The Couple-Level Version

The question can be asked together as well as alone. When both partners are in the fight-seeking state, the joint version of the question is "what do we need to be connected?"

"Instead of going to, let's have a fight, go to what do we need to be connected?" -- Joe Hudson, How to Use Relationship Fights to Heal

The shared version frames the work as a joint inquiry rather than each person managing themselves separately. The same answers apply: contact, private discharge, food, naming the day, time. The shared inquiry tends to produce shared moves — both partners go to separate rooms, both partners take a walk, both partners eat a meal before having the harder conversation.

The shared version requires both partners to have at least passing familiarity with the framework. Without that, the question can land as deflection. With it, the question is a relief: a different way to organize the moment that does not require either partner to be the bad one.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not the in-the-fight question [[what-am-i-not-feeling-right-now]]. That question operates after the fight has started and the superiority position has appeared. This question operates earlier — when the body is in the fight-seeking state but the fight has not yet escalated. The two are sequential moves in the same arc.

This is also not the existential "what do I need" question that comes up in larger life inquiries. The scope here is the next twenty minutes. The answers are small and bodily.

This is also not a way to suppress the urge. The urge is information. The question does not say "stop wanting to fight." It says "yes, you want to fight; what is the actual underneath need?" The yes-and structure is what makes the question work. A question that started with "you shouldn't want to fight" would produce defense rather than inquiry.

## Tone

Quiet, curious, almost tender toward yourself. The question is asked of yourself with the same energy a friend would use if they were checking in on you. Not "what's wrong with you?" or "why are you being like this?" Both produce shame and shut down inquiry. Just: "what do you actually need right now?"

Joe's tone when describing the question is gentle: "Do I really need a cuddle? Do I really need to go into another room and just fucking yell for a while and then come back?" The honesty about the range of possible needs — including the messy ones like yelling — signals that no need is off the table. The question's openness to any answer is what makes it safe to ask.
