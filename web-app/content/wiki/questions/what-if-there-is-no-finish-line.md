# What if there is no finish line?

## The Question

When the person is operating on the assumption that there is an arrival point — a state in which the work will be done, the pattern resolved, the self fixed — Joe asks them to consider what it would be like if that arrival never comes.

Variations:

- "What if there is no complete, no done, no heaven?"
- "What if that never comes?"
- "What happens to your sense of self if you deeply accept that there is no finish line, there is no — you're never done?"
- "What if this is just the rest of the letting go of the story? Like the finish-line part of the story is now gone."

The question dismantles the arrival fantasy without arguing against it. It asks the person to feel into the alternative.

## When Joe Uses It

- When [[desire-for-resolution]] has been surfaced and the person has admitted they want resolution to feel "like it's done."
- When the person's biography contains a religious or arrival-based imprint (Christianity, enlightenment-seeking, achievement culture).
- When [[improvement-vs-authenticity]] has been named and the person is still tracking progress toward an imagined end state.
- When the person is describing the present moment with a "yes, but I still need to..." structure.
- Particularly powerful when the person has already dismissed an earlier finish line (e.g., left a religion, abandoned a goal) but is still operating on the assumption that a finish line exists.

## What It Produces

A specific bodily relaxation. The person often reports it directly: "I just feel a lot more relaxed in my body now." The sense of perpetual sprinting toward a horizon loosens.

It also surfaces what the arrival fantasy was protecting against. Often the person realizes they have been treating their current life as preparation for a future life that was supposed to start once arrival happened. Without the finish line, the question becomes: what is left to do here, now?

The answer is usually: nothing in particular. Or: whatever shows up. The realization is not nihilistic. It is the opposite. The current life stops being a rehearsal.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person relaxes immediately:** Joe stays. Lets the relaxation register. Often follows with: "what's left to do about this perceived pattern?" — letting the person notice that the pattern they were so worried about is much less interesting once the arrival is gone.

- **If the person resists ("but then nothing matters"):** see the meaning-making trap in [[im-afraid-of-stagnating]]. Joe addresses directly: meaning is a project of the mind, not a requirement of life.

- **If the person notices their old religion connecting:** Joe makes the link explicit. The current finish-line story is the residue of the older one. The person already dismissed one version. This is dismissing the rest.

- **If the person tries to convert "no finish line" into a new finish line ("so the work is to live without a finish line, then I'll be enlightened"):** Joe names the conversion gently. The finish line keeps trying to re-install itself. Each re-installation is another thing to notice and release.

## Dialogue Example

- **Joe:** "What if that never comes? What if there is no complete, no done, no heaven?"
- **Brett:** "I mean, it would be boring if everything was done."
- **Joe:** "I mean it even back in the day, right? Like the thing that you dismissed is that there was a finish line when you dismissed the religion."
- **Brett:** "And there was also a way that in the religion I was really hanging, like, holding on to this idea of a finish line."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, that's right."
- **Brett:** "Like, I actually, when I was a kid, my logical brain was like, well, I'm a Christian. Ideally, I could just kill myself right now and go straight to heaven. Why not do that?"
- **Joe:** "What if this is just the rest of the letting go of the story? Like, the finish line part of the story is now gone."
- **Brett:** "Yeah, the finish line part of the story has continued to be there all along."
- **Joe:** "What happens to your sense of self if you deeply accept that there is no finish line, there is no — you're never done?"
- **Brett:** "I just feel a lot more relaxed in my body now."
- *(Source: Allowing Yourself to Change)*

Joe takes time with this question. It is asked in multiple forms. He references the person's specific finish-line history (religion) to make the question concrete rather than abstract. The body response is what he is listening for, not the verbal response.

## Tone

Quiet, spacious. Not provocative. The question is genuinely asked, not rhetorically deployed. There is room around it. Joe waits.

## What Not to Do

Do not turn "no finish line" into a teaching. The temptation is to explain why finish lines are illusions, cite Buddhist sources, etc. That would re-engage the mind. The question is meant to be felt, not understood.

Do not move on too fast if the person says something but is not visibly affected. If the body has not shifted, the question has not landed yet. Stay with it.
