# What's the question?

## The Question

A meta-question Joe asks when a client has been narrating their own situation, often with significant self-awareness about the pattern, but has not actually asked Joe anything. The question forces the client to convert the self-narration into the actual inquiry the client is bringing.

"If you know that you're burning out because you're trying to prove yourself and you're putting too much pressure on yourself and then you're under constant attack and your adrenals get fatigued and you burn out, what's the question?" — Joe Hudson, Why Your Boss Never Sees You

The form: summarize back what the client has already laid out, then ask "what's the question?" The summary acknowledges that the client's narration was complete. The question then asks them to do the part they have not yet done — formulate what they are actually wondering.

## When Joe Uses It

The question fires when a client opens a session by describing their situation in some depth without asking anything. The client typically reports the problem, names the pattern, identifies the structure they think is operating, and stops. The client has used the session opening to perform self-awareness rather than to bring a question.

The performance is usually not conscious. The client has practiced talking about their problem in this analytical way many times. The talking has become a substitute for the asking. The pattern is especially common in clients who are intellectually strong, well-read in psychology or coaching content, and have done significant amounts of self-work. The pattern is what their self-work has produced — they can now describe themselves accurately without that description producing change.

The move arrives early in the session. Joe does not let the self-narration continue indefinitely. After the client has named the basic shape of the problem, Joe interrupts with "what's the question?" The interruption shifts the session out of report-mode and into inquiry-mode. The shift is the work the question does.

In the source session, the client had named: 3-4 burnouts, the cause as "proving myself to an authority figure," the gender-specificity to male managers, the pattern of pressure leading to adrenal fatigue. Joe summarized it back and asked: what's the question? The client produced two: "Why exactly am I trying to prove them?" and "Why am I not able to trust myself?"

Joe took the first one ("that's enough, that's a good one") and went to work. The session had been converted from a narration into a coachable inquiry within thirty seconds.

## What It Produces

The client produces a question, usually within a few seconds. The question is almost always at the level the client has been thinking. It is often a "why" question, which Joe will then redirect (see [[why-questions]]) but the redirect is downstream — first the question has to be elicited so the coaching has a target.

Sometimes the client produces multiple questions and Joe selects one. The selection criterion is usually whichever question has the most heat, the most aliveness, the most somatic charge. Joe often signals the selection clearly: "that's enough, that's a good one." The signal tells the client which question is being worked. The client can then settle into the work rather than continuing to generate more questions.

Sometimes the client cannot produce a question and falls into more narration. This is data. The client has been using the narration to avoid the inquiry. Joe may then ask the question more directly: "What do you want to know?" Or he may name the avoidance: "You keep telling me about it — what are you actually asking?"

Occasionally the client says they don't have a question — they were just hoping Joe would tell them what to do. This is also data. The client has been hoping the coach will produce the inquiry for them so they can stay in the receiving position. Joe will often redirect: the coaching does not work that way; the question has to come from the client.

## Why This Works

The question works because coaching needs a target. A client who has been narrating their situation without asking has not given the coaching a direction to move in. The coach can listen for what seems important, but the listening is guessing. The question shifts the target into the client's hands. The client knows, at some level, what they actually want to know. The question asks them to name it.

The question also works because the formulating-the-question is itself coaching work. The act of converting a diffuse situation into a specific question requires the client to find the heat — the part of the situation that is actually pressing on them. The heat is often different from what the client has been narrating. The narration has been organized around what the client has already understood. The question often surfaces what the client has not yet understood, which is where the coaching can produce movement.

The third reason this works is that the question respects the client. The standard coaching opening — the coach guessing at what to focus on and offering it back — implicitly says "I know better than you what you should be working on." The "what's the question?" opening implicitly says "you know what you're actually here for; tell me." The respect creates a different working relationship. The client is the source of the inquiry. The coach is the support for the inquiry. The hierarchy of expertise is inverted from the start.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the client produces a clean question: Joe takes it and begins the work. Often with a brief signal of acceptance ("that's enough, that's a good one") and then a substantive response that opens the inquiry.

If the client produces multiple questions: Joe selects. The selection is usually the one with the most charge, the one closest to what the client cannot easily think their way through. The unselected questions are often related and will be addressed downstream as the work moves.

If the client produces a "why" question: Joe will often hear it as the right question even though "why" is generally a less productive form than "what" or "how." See [[why-questions]] and [[how-what-questions]]. The "why" question has the heat the client is asking from; the coaching can take that heat and convert it into a "what's underneath this" or "what does it produce" inquiry that the work can move in.

If the client produces a question that is intellectualized to the point of being abstract: Joe will sometimes redirect. "That's an interesting question but I notice it's a head question. What's the body's question?" The redirect uses the head/body distinction to surface the version of the inquiry that the body is actually asking, which is often shorter, more vulnerable, and more workable.

If the client cannot produce a question: Joe may ask "what do you want to know?" or "what are you hoping happens here?" The simpler forms sometimes unstick the client. If even these don't produce an inquiry, the work shifts to surfacing what the avoidance of the question is doing. The avoidance is itself the data.

## Dialogue Example

The compressed exchange from the source session:

- **Client:** "I got burned out 3 to 4 times by this time in my life... So I understand that majority of this burnout came because of, uh, I proving myself to an authority figure, whenever it is a male manager, I can see this kind of a pattern."
- **Joe:** "So then what's the question? If you know that you're burning out because you're trying to prove yourself and you're putting too much pressure on yourself and then you're under constant attack and your adrenals get fatigued and you burn out, what's the question?"
- **Client:** "Yeah. Uh, why exactly I'm trying to prove them and, uh, why I'm not able to trust myself."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So, uh, and one of the —"
- **Client:** [starts to continue narrating]
- **Joe:** "Wait, that's enough. That's a good one. What makes it that you're trying to prove yourself to them?"

*(Source: Why Your Boss Never Sees You)*

The exchange shows the move's complete arc: summarize what the client has narrated, ask for the question, accept the first clean version, redirect from continued narration back to the question, begin the coaching. The session is fully launched within sixty seconds.

The "wait, that's enough, that's a good one" is part of the move. The client tried to keep narrating after producing the question. Joe stopped them. The narration was over. The question had been formulated. The work could begin. Letting the narration continue would have re-established the report-mode the question had just interrupted.

## Tone

Direct, slightly impatient, warm. The impatience is not unkind — it is the impatience of someone who knows the client has more than they are bringing and is gently pushing them to bring it. The warmth keeps the directness from landing as critique. The combination signals to the client that the coach is taking them seriously enough to interrupt the narration. The interruption is a form of respect.

The "that's enough" signal at the end is also direct without being harsh. It tells the client to stop a behavior (continued narration) without making the behavior wrong. The implicit frame is: you have given me what I needed, the next thing is the work, let's go.

## What This Question Is Not

- **It is not [[whats-the-game]].** That question asks for the underlying engine of the activity. This question asks for the inquiry the client is bringing. They serve different points in the session: this one converts narration into inquiry early; that one surfaces the structural engine later.

- **It is not [[whats-the-scariest-question-to-ask]].** That question asks the client to identify which of several questions has the most charge. This question asks the client to produce a question in the first place. The other one is a refinement; this one is the initial elicitation.

- **It is not a request for "the right" question.** The question the client produces is the question being worked, even if it is not the question the coach would have selected. The client owns the inquiry. The coach works what is brought.

- **It is not for every client.** Clients who arrive with a clear question do not need this move. Clients who are emotionally activated and need to be met where they are also do not need this move — interrupting their narration to demand a question would be a misread. The move is for clients who are in analytical mode and are using the analysis to avoid the inquiry.
