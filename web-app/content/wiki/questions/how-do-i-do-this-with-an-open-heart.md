# How do I do this with an open heart?

## The Question

- "How do I do this with an open heart?"
- "How do I do this job with an open heart?"
- "How do I be of service in this moment?"
- "How do I love more completely right now?"

The core form is "how do I do this with an open heart?" The variants substitute for the specific situation. The job version is for ongoing professional contexts. The moment version is for in-the-moment decisions.

## When Joe Uses It

The question is the primary in-the-moment intervention for imposter syndrome and the I-I-I-I doubt loop. It is asked of the situation that is generating the imposter feeling. The person can ask it of themselves at any point -- before a meeting, during a presentation, when imposter syndrome flares up during a difficult conversation.

Joe brings the question in when:
- A client is stuck in self-evaluation about whether they are qualified
- A client is in the I-I-I-I doubt loop and the cognitive reframes have not landed
- A client has principles about service in the abstract but no concrete way to act on them in the moment
- The "build confidence" or "convince yourself you're qualified" strategies have been tried and have not worked
- A client asks "what do I actually do when imposter syndrome hits?"

The question is also useful as a preventative orientation. A client can ask it at the start of a meeting they are dreading, before walking into a high-stakes conversation, when starting a new role. It sets the attentional default before the imposter loop has a chance to fire.

"How do I do this job with an open heart? And if you ask yourself that question, it's a simple question. Imposter syndrome comes up and say, how do I do this job with an open heart? The imposter syndrome just can't exist in it because it puts you into the act of service. It puts you into humility because you think about yourself less, because you're in a place where you're in communion with other people." -- Joe Hudson, The Counterintuitive Cure for Imposter Syndrome

## What It Produces

The question relocates attention from self-evaluation to service-orientation. The most common immediate effect: a felt-sense shift in the chest area -- a slight opening, a slight relaxation of the gripping that the doubt loop produces. The person becomes physically softer.

The cognitive effect: the imposter-evaluation loop becomes hard to sustain. The mind cannot easily simultaneously run "am I qualified" and "how do I serve open-heartedly." The service-orientation pushes the self-evaluation out of the operative slot.

The behavioral effect: the person typically becomes more available to the people around them, asks better questions, listens more, and is more willing to acknowledge what they don't know. The performance overhead drops because the performance has become irrelevant to the new orientation. See [[maintaining-false-persona-costs-more-than-incompetence]].

The non-effect (important): the question does not produce a feeling of being qualified or having figured it out. The not-knowing remains. What changes is the relevance of the not-knowing to the action. The action is now oriented toward service; the not-knowing is just data, not a verdict.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The question is usually self-administered, but if asked in coaching, Joe stays close to the felt-sense response:

- **If the client softens noticeably:** name what happened. "Notice what just changed when you asked that. The imposter-feeling didn't get solved -- something else moved into the seat it was in." The naming installs the felt-sense as a reference point the client can return to.
- **If the client tightens or grips:** the question landed in the codependent-service version, not the open-hearted one. Joe moves to [[open-hearted-service-vs-codependent-service]]. The orientation needs the distinction to be live before the question can work.
- **If the client says "I don't know what that even means":** make it concrete. What does this person across from you actually need from you right now? What would it look like to attend to that, without managing whether they like you for attending to it?
- **If the client says "but I do want them to like me / approve / hire me":** name the hidden contract. The contract is the codependent version operating. Separate the two: the open-hearted service is available without the contract; with the contract, it stops working.
- **If the client says "but I don't know what to do":** that's the point. The not-knowing is the imposter feeling, and it does not need to be resolved before the service can begin. The next service-move is available from inside the not-knowing.

## The Mechanism

The question works because of the structural claim in [[open-hearted-service-cures-imposter-syndrome]] and [[doubt-is-self-focus-arrogance-is-self-focus]]: imposter syndrome and doubt are forms of self-focus. The question redirects attention to others. The redirect occupies the same attentional slot the self-focus was using. The slot is filled; the imposter loop has nowhere to run.

This is not a suppression. The imposter feeling can still be present. The question does not require eliminating it. It just gives the attention something else to do. The feeling becomes peripheral rather than central.

The question is also load-bearing on the open-hearted part. "How do I do this for them" without the open-heart qualifier is often codependent service ("how do I do this to get them to approve of me"). The "open heart" specifies that the orientation is not toward extraction of return but toward presence. The qualifier protects the question from collapsing into the codependent version.

## Where It Sits in the Larger Path

The question is the practical in-the-moment intervention. It sits alongside several other moves that work on imposter syndrome:

The cognitive moves dissolve the frame. The somatic moves work on the activation. This question works on the orientation. The full coaching path for imposter syndrome typically includes moves from all three categories. See the coaching path in [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]].

## Tone

Quiet, simple. The question should land as small, not as profound. Its power is in how often the person asks it, not in any single dramatic moment of asking. The coach delivers it as a tool the client can use right now and tomorrow and next week, every time the imposter loop fires.

When demonstrated in session, Joe asks it lightly, almost casually. The lightness matters -- a heavy delivery would invite the client to make it into another performance ("I must now serve open-heartedly"), which would re-install the codependent version. The lightness keeps it as a simple orientation rather than a new identity to defend.
