# What's the game?

## The Question

A two-word structural question Joe uses to surface the underlying engine a person is running. Asked when a person describes the absence of something ("there would be no game") or when a person is exerting massive effort on a configuration whose purpose has not been named.

- "What's the game?"
- "So what's the game?"
- "What game are you playing?"

The question presumes there is a game. The question does not argue with the person about whether they are playing one. It asks them to name it.

## When Joe Uses It

The question arrives at a specific moment in the structural mapping of a presenting concern. The person has been describing a chronic difficulty (burnout, exhaustion, conflict with authority, repeated failure in a domain). Joe has been narrowing the structure by asking what would change if a key piece were absent. The person says, in some form, "there would be no game" — meaning the activity would lose its driver, its tension, its meaning.

At that moment Joe asks: what's the game?

The configuration looks like this:

- **Joe:** "What if you couldn't feel one was better or one was worse, and neither you nor them were better or worse? What would change, if anything?"
- **Client:** "Maybe I feel that there's no game and purpose or meaning to it."
- **Joe:** "You would feel like there was no game. Is that what you said?"
- **Client:** "Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, cool. What's the game?"

*(Source: Why Your Boss Never Sees You)*

The client's own word ("game") is the door. Joe walks through it. The next answer is usually the operational engine the entire concern is being driven by. In the source session: "To be seen."

The question also works without the client's own word as setup. When someone has been describing massive effort or repeated chronic struggle around a configuration with no obvious payoff, "what's the game?" asks them to make the implicit payoff explicit. The framing of activity-as-game does not trivialize the activity — it names that the activity is structured, that it has a goal, and that the goal has not yet been spoken aloud.

## What It Produces

The answer is usually short. Sometimes one word. Often the word arrives faster than the person expected. The person has been holding the answer the whole time and has not been asked the question that would let them say it.

In the source session, the answer was "to be seen." Two words. The client delivered them quickly. The entire burnout structure the session had been mapping reorganized around those two words. Producing for the boss, proving himself to male authority figures, the 3-4 burnouts in the career, the inability to trust himself — all of these reconfigured as moves in the see-me game.

Joe's response to a clean game-answer is usually appreciation. "Oh, that's — wow, look at you. Look at you. You, you, look at you." The recognition is for the client having seen their own game. The game was not hidden from them at the deepest level; it was hidden behind the conscious frame the activity was being justified by.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Once the game is named, Joe moves immediately to the next move: testing whether the game is being played right now, in the session.

"So is that the game we're playing right now?"

The question takes the game-answer from the abstract level (it's the game I play with bosses) and pulls it into the present (is it the game we're playing here, in this room, right now?). The answer is almost always some version of "not exactly" or "I don't think so." The client has not yet seen that the same game runs in every situation that has the same shape — including the coaching session.

Joe then demonstrates that the game is in fact being played in the room. He does this by giving the client the thing the game is supposedly trying to win. In the source session, after the client said "to be seen," Joe immediately said: "Cool, I'll see you. I see that you've done a lot of introspective work. I see that you're incredibly smart." Then he watches whether the client lets the seeing land.

If the client does not let it land — which is the predictable outcome — Joe names the contradiction: the game is to be seen, and you are not letting yourself be seen, so the game is unwinnable. See [[you-cant-win-the-see-me-wont-be-seen-game]] for the principle this exchange demonstrates.

The game-naming question is, in this structure, the setup for the demonstration that the game cannot be won as currently configured. The question opens the field; the in-session demonstration shows the field has no exit through the moves the client has been making.

## Why This Works

The question works because most chronic difficulty is sustained by an unnamed engine. The person is doing massive amounts of work without the activity producing the outcome the work was supposed to produce. The standard frames the person uses to describe the activity (it's just my job, it's just how I am, this is what is required) hide the engine. The engine is the thing the person is actually trying to achieve, which is structured differently from what they say they are achieving.

Naming the game makes the engine visible. Once visible, the engine can be examined. The question of whether the engine is winnable becomes askable. The question of whether the cost is worth it becomes askable. The question of whether the game even needs to be played becomes askable. None of these questions can be asked while the engine is unnamed because the unnamed engine looks like the work itself rather than like a game being played within the work.

The framing of activity-as-game is also somatically lighter than the framing of activity-as-life. Life feels heavy. A game feels like something one can stop playing. The reframe from "this is my situation" to "this is the game I am playing" gives the body a small opening that the heavier frame did not allow.

## Tone

Light, slightly playful, curious. The question is not asked as a gotcha. It is asked as a wonder. The two-word brevity matters — long versions ("so what would you say is the underlying driver of what you've been describing?") sound like therapy. The short version sounds like a friend asking what you are actually up to.

The lightness is necessary because the answer will be heavy. The client about to name "to be seen" as the game underneath the burnout is about to surface the original survival wound of the unseen child. The question that opens that door has to be light enough that the body can actually open it. A grave question produces a defensive answer. A light question produces the truth.

After the answer arrives, the tone shifts to recognition. "Look at you" is the appreciation Joe offers to a clean game-answer. The appreciation rewards the body for having spoken. The next time the question is asked, the body will be slightly more willing to answer because the previous answer was met with welcome.

## Variations

- "So what's the game?"
- "What game are you playing?"
- "What's the actual game?"
- "If there were no [thing the person says would disappear], what would change?" (the setup question that often produces "there would be no game" as the answer, which opens the door to "what's the game?")

The variation that asks the setup first ("what if X were not true — what would change?") and lets the client introduce the word "game" themselves is the cleanest version. The client owns the framing because they introduced the word. Joe then just repeats the word back as a question. The structure presents itself.
