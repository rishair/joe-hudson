# What's the for part?

## The Question

The question is asked when a person has presented a deliberation in which they have given full voice to the "against" side and only a sketch of the "for" side. The question asks the for voice to speak with the same fullness the against voice has.

Variants:

- "What's the thing that says you want to? What does that sound like?"
- "What's the for sound like? What is the for part?"
- "Tell me what the voice that wants the relationship is actually saying."
- "Let me hear the for side."
- "What does the voice that wants this sound like to you?"
- "What's the for voice saying?"

The question is structural. It treats the inner debate as having two voices, both of which should be in the room before the work can proceed. The against voice has usually been talking for years; the for voice has usually been dismissed.

## When Joe Uses It

This question is reached for early in a session when the person has presented a deliberation but has only fully voiced one side of it. The clearest tells:

- The person describes their concern as a push-pull or a two-voices structure, but only details one voice.
- The against voice has specific content ("it's too much work, I don't have time, it'll take away from the other things I want to do"). The for voice has been mentioned generically ("part of me wants to...") without detail.
- The person introduces the for voice and then immediately moves past it to elaborate the against voice.
- The deliberation has been running for a long time without convergence. The non-convergence is often the for voice being underweight in the asking — the against voice is fully dressed and the for voice is in a thin sketch.

The question also fires when [[the-question-itself-is-the-abandonment]] is the active read. Asking the for voice to speak is the move that interrupts the dismissal pattern. It forces the abandoning voice to step aside long enough for the abandoned voice to be heard.

The question is also useful when [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]] is going to be the next move. Surfacing the for voice first gives Joe the material from which the avoided hurt can be located — the hurt is usually adjacent to what the for voice wants.

## What It Produces

The for voice, when invited to speak, usually arrives quickly. The person has known what it wants. They have just not been letting it say so.

In the source session, after a long account of the against voice ("it's a lot of work, I don't have time, am I going to be able to do that and business and train for sport"), Joe asked "what's the thing that says you want to? What does that sound like?" The for voice immediately said: "I love relationship. I love spending my life and sharing experience with a person and people."

Two short sentences. The for voice had been there all along. It just had not been asked.

The for voice's answers tend to be simpler than the against voice's. The against voice is usually arguing — producing reasons, weighing considerations, justifying the dismissal. The for voice is usually just naming — saying what it loves, what it wants. The simplicity of the for voice's answer is often diagnostic: the want is real; the elaboration was unnecessary; the deliberation was being run on the against side because the for side did not need to be argued, only acknowledged.

Sometimes the for voice produces tears. The person has been dismissing this voice for months or years. Being asked to give it space, and discovering it speaks readily once given space, can be moving. The grief is for the time the voice has spent being silenced.

Sometimes the for voice cannot find words. This is also information. It may mean the voice has been so thoroughly dismissed that it has fallen silent. The follow-up is to give it more space, to ask a smaller version of the question, to let the not-finding-words be okay.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the for voice speaks clearly: Joe lets it be heard. He may not respond directly to its content. The hearing is the work. Joe then often moves to the next question that uses the symmetry — [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]], or "what's the hurt you're trying to avoid?", or "how do you abandon yourself with this voice?"

If the for voice speaks tentatively: Joe gives it more space. "Say more about that. What else is in there?" The tentativeness is often the residue of the dismissal pattern. Slowing down lets the voice find its full size.

If the for voice cannot find words: Joe waits, then asks a smaller version. Not "what does the for voice say?" but "what would the for voice say if you let it have one sentence?" The smaller container can hold what the bigger one cannot.

If the for voice produces an immediate intellectual answer: Joe redirects to the body. "How does that feel when you say it?" The intellectual answer is often the against voice in disguise — using the for voice's vocabulary to keep the structure intact.

After the for voice has been heard, Joe usually pivots to surface the avoided hurt that is producing the dismissal. The for voice and the against voice both have hurt underneath them. The question "what's the hurt you're trying to avoid, if any?" goes there. See [[whats-the-emotion-youre-trying-to-avoid]] (the broader question), and note that this transcript uses the specific variant "what's the hurt" rather than "what's the emotion" — the variant matters when the underlying material is grief, loss, or pain rather than fear, shame, or guilt.

## Dialogue Example

The man opens with the against voice elaborated: "I've heard myself often say like, it's too much work. I don't want to get back into a relationship. I don't have time. So there's like almost like a push-pull, like I want to, but then I hear myself say these things and how it's going to take away from the other things I want to do."

- **Joe:** "What's the thing that says you want to? What does that sound like?"
- **Person:** "Like what is the specific reasons why kind of thing?"
- **Joe:** "Well, this one is I don't have time, da da da, like I won't get to do the things I want to do. What's the for sound like? What is the for part?"
- **Person:** "Yeah, the like for it sounds like, yeah, I love, love, like I love relationship. I love spending my life and like sharing experience with a person and people. And yeah, along those lines."
- **Joe:** "So what's the hurt you're trying to avoid, if any?"

*(Source: Why He's Afraid To Commit)*

The question had to be asked twice. The first asking ("what does that sound like?") got a confused reply because the person did not yet understand that the for voice was being asked to speak in the same register as the against voice. The second asking ("what's the for sound like? what is the for part?") made the symmetry explicit by referencing how the against voice had already been described.

The "I love relationship. I love spending my life and like sharing experience with a person and people" came out simply, in two sentences. The against voice had taken five minutes to articulate. The for voice took ten seconds. The asymmetry is the diagnostic: the for voice had been underweight in the deliberation, and once given space, said its piece briefly and clearly.

Joe then moves immediately to the avoided hurt. The for voice has spoken; the structure of the deliberation is now symmetric; the next question can be asked.

## Tone

Warm, curious, slightly insistent. The first asking is gentle. If it gets a confused reply, the second asking has a little more push — making it clear that yes, this is the question, and yes, the for voice is being asked to speak fully. The push is not impatience; it is the holding-open of the space for the voice that has been dismissed.

When the for voice speaks, Joe receives it without commentary. No "wonderful" or "yes!" or any other validation. The receiving is itself the validation. The voice is being heard; that is enough.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not [[what-do-you-want]]. That question asks for the want directly. This question asks for the voice that has been carrying the want — and acknowledges that there is also a voice that has been dismissing it. The two-voice structure is the unit of work in a way it isn't for the bare want question.

This is not "what's the upside of being in a relationship?" That question produces a list of features. This question produces the voice. The difference is structural: a list of features can be argued with by the against voice; a voice cannot be argued with, only listened to or dismissed.

This is not [[whats-the-scary-truth]]. That question goes for what is being avoided in speaking. This question is more specific: it asks for the speaking that has been getting dismissed inside the deliberation.
