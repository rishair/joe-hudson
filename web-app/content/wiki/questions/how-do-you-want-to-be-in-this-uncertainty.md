# How do you want to be in this time of change?

## The Question

The uncertainty-specific application of [[how-do-you-want-to-be]]. The general question asks how someone wants to be — period. The uncertainty variant locates the question inside a specific moment of disruption and asks what stance they want to bring to it.

Variants:

- "How do you want to be in this time of change?"
- "How do you want to be in this moment?"
- "How do you want to be in this uncertainty?"
- "How do you want to be in this chaos?"
- "How do you want to be in this transition?"
- "I'm going to take this forest fire that's happening — how do I want to be in it?"

The question relocates the reference point. The default reference point in uncertain times is the external situation: "the markets are down, the markets are up, AI is rising, the election is coming." That reference point produces dysfunction. The reference point this question installs is internal: "this is how I want to be in this time of change." Internal reference points are stable across external chaos.

"How do I want to be in this moment? I want to be like I'm flying through the crash. How do I want to be? I want to be like I can create something better. This is how I want to be in it. It's uncertain. I'm not going to pretend that it's not uncertain. I'm not going to be unrealistic in the uncertainty, but I'm also going to say, how do I want to be in this time?" -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

## When Joe Uses It

- When someone is in [[how-do-i-navigate-this-uncertain-time]] and the conversation has been about prediction
- When the person has been asking "what is going to happen?" repeatedly and the question is not producing anything
- When [[take-the-adventure-stance]] has been offered and needs the operational follow-on
- When the person is in any of the three artificial collapses — optimism, pessimism, or realism (see [[optimism-pessimism-realism-vs-ownership]]) — and needs the question that exits all three
- When a leader is being pulled toward false certainty by people around them and needs to anchor in their own being
- When the person is making a major decision in uncertain conditions and is trying to find a basis for it in the external situation
- When the person is dysregulated and the cortisol is high, but is regulated enough to receive an open question (if too dysregulated, see [[three-moves-when-frozen-by-uncertainty]] first)

## What It Produces

The question often produces a pause. The person was prepared for the conversation to be about the external situation — markets, politics, technology, the diagnosis. The question moves the conversation into the only domain where they have actual agency.

After the pause, the answers tend to be quieter and more honest than the answers to "what should I do?" Not "I should hedge my investments" but "I want to be present with my family while this plays out." Not "I should pivot the business" but "I want to be in this with my team." Not "I should figure out AI" but "I want to be creative in a way I haven't let myself be."

The answers tend to be the actual wants that have been suppressed by the focus on the external chaos.

In Joe's COVID account:

> "When we were stuck in the house, I'm like, what I want to do is I want to be close with my family. What I want to do is be creative. Like, this is how I want to be in this time. It's when AOA online courses were born. The depth of intimacy I got with my kids during that time was amazing. I had all this time and space to create MasterClass. We started this podcast. All of this happened because we were — we knew how we wanted to be in this situation, and it wouldn't have happened without COVID."

See [[joes-covid-creative-explosion]] for the full example. The pattern: ask the question, get the genuine answer, follow the answer.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person gives a character-level answer** ("I want to be present, creative, generous, calm"): the work is to test whether they are already being that, or what is in the way. The "how" is available now; it does not have to wait for the external situation to resolve.

- **If the person gives another outcome** ("I want to be successful through this"): Joe redirects. Successful is an outcome, not a how. "What is the quality of you that makes the success possible? How do you want to be such that the success becomes downstream?"

- **If the person says "I don't know"**: Joe stays with the not-knowing. The cultural training is all about outcomes. The "how" question is unfamiliar enough that not-knowing is appropriate. Stay. The answer often arrives after the person sits with the question for a minute.

- **If the answer is reactive** ("I want to be safe / not afraid / not stressed"): the answer is a negation. Joe asks what they do want, not what they do not want.

- **If the answer is grand** ("I want to be Gandhi-like through this"): notice and check. Sometimes this is the genuine answer and the person is reaching for something true; other times it is performance and the actual answer is smaller and more honest.

- **If the person gives a felt answer** (their body shifts, their voice softens): Joe stays quiet and lets the felt-answer continue to land. The question has done its work.

## Why This Question Works in Uncertainty Specifically

In stable times, the question "how do you want to be?" produces answers about life as a whole. In uncertain times, the question becomes urgent. The external chaos that had been mostly invisible is now in the foreground, and the question "how do I want to be in this specifically?" cuts through the noise in a way that the general question does not.

There is also a leadership dimension. Joe is explicit:

"If you look at the people who've done that — say, this is how I want to be in this time of change — whether it's Winston Churchill or Martin Luther King or Gandhi, that's what congeals humans. That's what creates movements. That's the thing that drives companies, is that level of, this is how I want to be in this situation." -- Joe Hudson, Making the Most of Uncertain Times

The question is therefore not only a personal-coaching question. It is the question that produces leadership in chaotic times. The answer is the leadership.

## What Makes This Question Different From "What Should I Do?"

"What should I do?" requires knowing what is going to happen, so that the action can be calibrated to the outcome. In genuine uncertainty, "what should I do?" has no answer, because the dependency it requires is not available.

"How do I want to be in this?" requires nothing external. The answer is available immediately. The answer also makes the action question solvable — once the how is clear, the what often becomes obvious. Joe wanted to be close to his family during COVID; that made the long mornings at home into time with the kids rather than time stuck. The how converted the same situation from waiting-it-out to using-it-well.

## Tone

The question is asked quietly and warmly. The pace is slow. The question is offered as a relief from the prediction-seeking, not as another demand for an answer. The pause after asking is part of the question.

If asked too fast or too clinically, the question lands as another item on the to-do list. If asked with space, the question lands as an invitation to stop trying to figure out the future for a moment.
