# How do you NOT trust it?

## The Question

- "How do you NOT trust it?"
- "How do you NOT trust your intuition?"
- "How is that not trust?"

The follow-ups when the person resists:
- "You just spoke. How did that happen?"
- "It's happening right now."
- "If you're talking, you're trusting your intuition."

The pacing of the questions matters. They land one at a time, with space. Each one is a separate small invitation to notice what is already happening.

## When Joe Uses It

Used when the person presents a concern in the form "how do I trust myself / my intuition / my body?" The form of the concern presupposes that trust is missing. This question inverts the presupposition.

Specifically:
- The person says "I can't tell if this is intuition or fear"
- The person says "how do I know if I can trust my gut?"
- The person describes contradictory signals and asks how to choose between them
- The person treats trust as something they have to construct or earn rather than something already happening

The question is a refusal to engage the original framing. Instead of "let me help you figure out how to trust yourself," the move is "you are trusting yourself constantly, so the question is malformed, what's actually going on?"

This belongs in a small family of Joe's inversion questions that show the person what they already are doing rather than asking them to do something they're not. See [[how-is-that-not-true-about-you]] and [[how-is-that-not-here-right-now]] for related inversions on different topics.

## What It Produces

Confusion first, then recognition. The person initially can't compute the question. They came in to ask "how do I trust?" and the response was "what do you mean you don't?" The brief disorientation is the work happening.

What follows is usually:
- "Huh. That's an interesting way to put it."
- "I guess it just happens."
- A small laugh of recognition

Once the recognition lands, the original question collapses. The person can see that they are continuously trusting themselves to speak, to breathe, to move, to make a hundred micro-decisions a minute. What they were calling "not trusting myself" was something else.

That something else is usually fear about an outcome. The person isn't failing to trust themselves; they're afraid of what will happen if they act on what they know. The trust frame was a misdiagnosis. The real material is fear of consequences. This opens the path to [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the recognition lands, Joe presses further. "So how is the question even a question? You're trusting yourself right now."

If the person doesn't see it, Joe demonstrates concretely. "You just made a hundred decisions in the last five minutes -- what to say, where to look, how to breathe. They didn't feel like decisions. But there's this big decision that comes up, or it feels big." This anchors the abstract claim in observable fact.

If the person says "yes but those don't count, the real decisions do" -- Joe goes to the next move: "And as soon as you think there's a decision, you're in fear." The trust question dissolves into a fear question. See [[if-you-think-youre-deciding-youre-in-fear]].

If the person produces a more sophisticated form of the original concern ("but I'll have a gut reaction and then another contradictory one"), Joe goes to: "Yeah. So how is that just not fear?" Same move, deeper layer.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I'm trying to figure out: how can I trust my intuition? How can I recognize intuition from fear or anxiety or projection?"
- **Joe:** "How do you NOT trust it?"
- **Person:** "How do I not trust it? Because all these other parts are now speaking up and I'm like, well, which one of those things was true?"
- **Joe:** "Yeah, how did you just say that?"
- **Person:** "What do you mean?"
- **Joe:** "You just spoke."
- **Person:** "I just spoke."
- **Joe:** "How did that happen?"
- **Person:** "I guess I've developed a mental model from my experience..."
- **Joe:** "It's happening right now. All these parts exist. None of them are true. All of them have some truth. And here you are talking. How is that work? How is that happening?"
- **Person:** "I guess it will happen whether I want it to or not."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. So how do you not trust your intuition? If you're talking, you're trusting your intuition."
- *(Source: How Do I Trust Myself? -- The inversion dismantles the original frame.)*

## Tone

Curious, slightly playful, slightly stubborn. Joe holds the inversion. He doesn't soften it or back down. The question is asked with a small smile. The pacing is patient -- he gives the person time to compute the question rather than rushing to clarify.

Joe doesn't make the person wrong for asking the original question. He just refuses to answer it on its own terms. The refusal is friendly.
