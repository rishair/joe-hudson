# What Would Be Left to Do?

## The Question

When the person has been describing fix-the-wrongs activity as the structure of their life, the question removes the fixing hypothetically and asks what would remain.

"What is the point then? What's the point of your life if your job isn't to fix the wrongs of the world?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

And in the more compressed form, immediately after:

"Fix the wrongs of yourself, fix the wrongs of the world. What, what if, what if you couldn't do that? What would be left to do?" -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The question takes the entire structure the person has built their life on and pulls it out from under them in a single sentence. The answer that arrives is the data of what the structure has been protecting against.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person has named fix-everything as their identity ("that's what drives me," "that's my role").
- When the person is morally framing the fixing as the right thing to do ("if I don't, who will?").
- When the fix-pattern has been operating long enough that the person cannot easily imagine life without it.
- When [[everything-i-touch-fails-drives-fixing]] has not yet been surfaced and the descent through purpose is the cleaner entry to the underneath material.
- When the person has been describing the world's injustices as their personal mandate and the conversation needs to pivot from the moral content to the structural function.

The question is in the same family as [[if-you-couldnt-feel-this-whats-underneath]] but operates on activity-as-identity rather than on emotion-as-blocker. The structure is the same: temporarily remove the load-bearing element, and ask the system what remains.

## What It Produces

**Often: "Nothing."** The first answer to "what would be left to do?" is frequently a stark "nothing." The starkness is informative. The system has been organized around the fix-activity to the point that without it, no alternative organization is available.

**The sadness underneath the nothing.** "Nothing" is rarely just an absence. It is often the felt-sense of a loss the person has not let themselves register. The activity has been a substitute for living with what is. Without the substitute, the absent thing — meaning, connection, peace — is suddenly accessible as a felt lack. The felt lack often arrives as sadness.

In the source session, the woman said "nothing" and Joe immediately named what arrived with it: "Yeah, but what I notice is there's a sadness that hits you now."

**The reach for philosophy.** The person often tries to escape the felt material by reaching for big philosophical questions. "Why are we here?" "What's the meaning of life?" "Who am I?" These are the fix-pattern reapplied to the meaning-question. The mind has been deprived of its usual fix-targets and is generating new ones.

In the source session: "It's very sad because, I mean, where do you go? Like, why are we here? It goes into that realm. I know you do a lot of like, what's my purpose? Who am I? Like the ego identification."

Joe declines the philosophical detour and brings the person back to the body and the room. The philosophy is another version of the fixing.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person says "nothing":** Joe stays with the nothing and notices what arrives with it (usually sadness). The sadness is the next material.
- **If the person reaches for philosophy:** Joe declines the philosophy and brings them back to the present body. See [[make-the-session-itself-the-demonstration]]. The philosophy is another fix-target; the work is to drop it and feel what's actually here.
- **If the person says "support people instead":** Joe notes the recurrence of the same pattern in different clothes. "I don't know how that's much different" is often the person's own response when they listen to their reformulation. The pattern wants to survive in any form.
- **If the person tries to find a concrete answer:** Joe holds the question lightly and does not require an answer. The not-finding is the work. The system is being asked to sit with the not-knowing instead of producing the next fix-target.

## Why It Works

The question works because the fix-pattern is not just an activity; it is the structure that has been organizing the person's existence. Asking the person to imagine life without the structure produces the felt sense of what the structure has been holding off.

The thing that has been held off is usually one of:
- The grief of being a person without a redemptive project.
- The terror of having no role.
- The peace the person has been protecting against. See [[peace-is-what-we-resist-the-most]].
- The aliveness available in the present that the activity has been preventing.

Each of these is uncomfortable enough that the system has been working full-time to avoid it. The question forces a brief encounter. The encounter is the door.

The question is also a small disconfirmation of the moral framing of the fix-pattern. The pattern often justifies itself with "if I don't fix this, who will?" or "the world needs me to do this." The question hypothetically removes the activity without asking whether anyone replaces the function. The question is interested in what is left for the person, not in what is lost to the world. The shift in frame is itself part of the work; the moral framing was part of the pattern's protection, and stepping outside the moral frame loosens the pattern.

## Joe's Specific Move with the Actions-Haven't-Changed-Much Frame

Before the "what would be left to do?" question, Joe sometimes deploys a small reframe that prepares the ground. In the source session:

"In the grand scheme of things, all of your actions have really not changed much. ... All those things that you've done to right all the wrongs, to make sure that all those things have happened — in the grand scheme of things, the world is still going around the sun. The politics haven't, you know, there's been no decisive shift in the war in Ukraine or the upcoming war with China." -- Joe Hudson, Why Am I Always Trying To Fix Myself & Others?

The reframe is small and slightly provocative. It does not argue with the person's values. It just observes that the activity, evaluated against the scale at which the person has framed it (the world's injustices), has not moved the needle. The reframe sits next to [[purposelessness-is-freedom]]'s anthill view at the cosmic scale, but operates at a smaller domain — it is specifically about what the person's fix-activity has accomplished, not about the cosmic meaninglessness of all activity.

The function of the reframe is to remove the protection the moral framing provides. The fix-activity has been justified as effective; the reframe says it has not been. Without the effectiveness-justification, the pattern is exposed as activity that does not produce what it claims. The question "what would be left to do?" then lands on more honest ground.

## What This Question Is Not

- It is not a recommendation to stop trying to make the world better. The question does not assert that the world should not be improved or that injustice does not matter. It just asks what would be left for the person if their identity-as-fixer were not in place.
- It is not nihilism. The question opens a space; what fills the space is up to the person. Often what fills it is more grounded engagement with the same concerns, but from a different posture.
- It is not a permanent injunction against fixing. The question is a tool for the descent into what the fixing has been protecting against. After the descent, the person can engage with what they value, including the world's problems, but from a different place.

## Tone

Curious, slightly insistent, warm. The question is asked the way one would ask a friend to imagine retirement — not as a threat but as a wondering. "If you weren't doing all this, what would you do?" The wondering quality is what makes the person willing to imagine the alternative rather than defending the current configuration.

The insistence is in not letting the person escape the question. If they reach for philosophy, the question is re-asked in the present moment. If they say "support people instead," the question is asked again about that. The persistence is gentle but it does not relent. The question is what is being worked, and the question requires an answer that lands in the body, not in the mind.
