# What's the real question?

## The Question

The exact form Joe uses, delivered immediately after the person's opening presentation:

"So what's the real question?" — Joe Hudson, I Can't Feel Anything

The question is short. There is no preamble. There is no acknowledgment of the content of what was just said. Joe receives the opening and then asks for what's underneath.

The phrasing variants Joe uses:
- "What's the real question?"
- "What are you actually asking?"
- "What do you really want to work on?"

A specific variant, used when Joe can hear two possible questions inside the opening framing:

"Is the question how do you be authentic, or is the question how do I not be self-conscious? What's the question? Just to be clear." -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?

The binary-clarification form has the same purpose as the open form, but is reached for when Joe can already hear two distinct questions hiding in a single phrasing. The person is asked to choose between two specific options rather than to produce a question from scratch. The choosing makes the actual concern visible and reduces the risk that the person will continue working on a question that is structurally different from the one they came in with.

## When Joe Uses It

The question gets deployed when the person's opening report sounds like a story rather than a request — when they have described a situation, a life history, a set of facts, without naming what they want to do with it in the coaching session.

In the source session, the person opened with a long contextual report: mother died at age 5, decades of compartmentalizing emotions, recent attempts to reconnect with feelings, the inability to label what is happening internally. The report was rich in detail but contained no question. The person was describing a state of being, not asking for work.

Joe's response was to ask for the question. The implicit teaching: the contextual report is not the work. The work begins when there is a question that can be entered. Without a question, the conversation could continue indefinitely in the contextual layer without ever reaching the felt material.

Specific situational triggers:
- A long opening that describes circumstances without naming a present concern
- An intellectual framing of a life situation that does not include the person's current emotional position
- A description that ends with "and..." or "so..." without resolving into an ask
- A presentation that sounds like a written essay rather than a spoken request for help
- A person who is articulate enough to keep describing indefinitely if not interrupted

## What It Produces

Several characteristic responses:

1. **The real question surfaces immediately.** The person already knew what they wanted to work on; they had been circling it. The direct ask cuts through the circling. In the source session, the person's answer to "what's the real question?" was: "I'm struggling, you know, to find pleasure in like what to do next or anything. Like everything I do is, you know, just because that's the right thing to do and not because I want to do this." That was the real question. It came out cleanly because Joe asked.

2. **The person realizes they didn't have a question.** They were processing out loud, not asking. The question prompts them to formulate one. The formulation itself is part of the work.

3. **A different question surfaces than the one the person came in with.** The opening describes one thing; the real question is about something else. The opening was the cover; the real question is the work. See [[treating-it-as-a-decision-when-its-an-emotion]] for the related read.

4. **The person says "I don't know" — and that's data.** The not-knowing means the work in this session will begin with finding the question, not answering it. The finding can happen through the body. The first move might then be a felt-sense question to surface what wants attention.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the real question arrives cleanly: Joe takes it as the contract for the session. The session works on the real question, not on the contextual material that preceded it. The earlier context becomes background; the real question becomes the foreground.

If the real question is meaningfully different from the opening: Joe may briefly name the difference ("so the question isn't actually about X, it's about Y") to make sure the new framing is shared. Often the act of speaking the real question changes what the person thought the session was about.

If the person cannot find a real question: Joe shifts to the felt-sense layer. "What is in your body right now?" Or he stays with the opening material but names the absence of a question as part of the work — "let's just start with what's most alive."

## Why This Question Works

The structural mechanism: people often present coaching concerns the way they present problems to themselves — as a context to think about rather than as a question to answer. The thinking-about can continue indefinitely without producing change. The asking-and-answering produces movement.

Asking for the real question shifts the person from third-person mode (describing a situation) to first-person mode (asking for what they need). The shift is small but decisive. Once they have asked for something, the rest of the conversation can be the answering. Without the question, the conversation has nowhere to go.

The word "real" is doing work too. It implies that the person's opening framing was not the real question — without judging it. The "real" acknowledges that there is more underneath, and invites the person to bring that more forward. It is gentler than "what's the actual question," which can read as a correction.

## The Binary-Clarification Variant

When Joe can already hear two distinct questions inside the opening framing, the question gets deployed as a binary rather than as an open ask. The person is given two options and asked to choose. This is faster than the open form when the options are already audible.

The form: "Is the question X, or is the question Y? What's the question? Just to be clear."

When this variant is used:

- The opening contains a complex framing that smuggles two questions together (often a how-to question and a feeling question).
- The two questions would produce two structurally different sessions, and the difference matters for what move comes next.
- The person is articulate and likely to keep producing more framing if not given a specific binary to land on.

The binary works because it removes the labor of formulating the real question and reduces it to a choice. The choice exposes the actual concern. In the source session, the person opened with "how do I be my authentic self, especially on stage?" Joe heard two questions inside that — an authenticity question and a self-consciousness question. He named both and asked the person to choose. The person chose self-consciousness. That choice became the contract for the session.

The variant has the same risk as the open form: if the person chooses the option that is not actually their real question, the work goes in the wrong direction. The mitigation is that the binary form makes any miscategorization more visible — the person knows they had to choose, so the chosen frame is more available for revision later if it stops fitting.

The "just to be clear" closing matters. It signals that the question is administrative (getting the contract right) rather than therapeutic (probing for hidden meaning). The administrative framing keeps the person from feeling that they got the opening wrong.

## Dialogue Example

The full opening sequence from the source session, showing the question's function as the conversation-starter:

- **Person:** [opens with extended report] "I'm just completely blank on, you know, where to go next. Um, for context, I lost my mother like pretty early, like when I was 5 years old. And then I tried, you know, not to feel anything because at that age I couldn't like understand what was going on or, you know, manage was happening to me. I had no answers. And so for pretty much like the whole of my life, I've been thinking of emotions as these like useless, ineffective things. And finally I'm in a place to, you know, connect with them. But I don't know, like, I cannot like label emotions. I don't know what's happening. You know, ever after like listening to your podcast, I can like find some sensations, but most of them are just like, you know, pressure in the chest or like a spoke through the chest, and nothing else comes up. I'm very blank."

- **Joe:** "So what's the real question?"

- **Person:** "I'm struggling, you know, to find pleasure in like what to do next or anything. Like everything I do is, you know, just because that's the right thing to do and not because I want to do this."

*(Source: I Can't Feel Anything (Coaching Session With Master Coach))*

Note what Joe did not do. He did not respond to the mother's death. He did not respond to the inability to label emotions. He did not respond to the pressure in the chest. He bypassed every potential entry point in the opening and asked for the question. The opening was given respectfully — the question was asked respectfully — and the person provided a different concern as the real one: the loss of want, the everything-from-obligation. That concern is what the session worked on.

A second example, showing the binary-clarification variant in use:

- **Guest:** "My question is, um, how do I be my authentic self, especially on stage? Because I've gotten a lot of feedback from people, like, what's your—"
- **Joe:** "What's, what's your— what, what, uh, stage doing what?"
- **Guest:** "Like stand-up comedy."
- **Joe:** "Oh, it's an interesting question, right? There's like, with comedy in particular, you're, you're putting on an act and yet you still want to be authentic, right? It's like there's both things happening, right?"
- **Guest:** "Right."
- **Joe:** "Um, is the question how do you be authentic, or is the question how do I not be self-conscious? What's the question? Just to be clear."
- **Guest:** "Yeah, I think it would be how to not be self-conscious..."

*(Source: How Do I Overcome Self-Consciousness?)*

Joe heard two questions inside one framing — an authenticity question and a self-consciousness question — and asked the person to choose. The choice was decisive. The whole rest of the session worked on the self-consciousness question, not the authenticity question. The binary form took less time than the open form would have because Joe had already located the candidate questions; the work for the person was just choosing.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not "what do you want to work on today?" That question can be answered with any topic and does not require the person to locate what is actually live. The real-question phrasing specifies that there is something more underneath, which forces the person to drop down to that layer.

This is also not a challenge to the person's opening. It is not "your opening wasn't a question, give me one." The tone is curious, not corrective. The implicit message is: "I heard everything you said, and now I want to know what you came here to ask."

It is also not a shortcut to bypass the contextual material. The context matters. The mother's death at age 5 matters. The decades of compartmentalization matter. But they are not the question. They are the background to whatever question is actually being asked. The question and the context are different things and need to be distinguished.

## Tone

Direct, brief, warm. The question is one short sentence. There is no softening, no leading-up, no acknowledgment. The brevity is structural — a long preamble would let the person stay in the explanatory mode they were already in.

The warmth is in the tone of voice, not in extra words. The implicit message: "I'm listening, I trust you to know what you came here for, and I want to hear it."
