# How Do We Want To Be Together?

## The Question

The forward-looking question Joe substitutes for the backward-looking blame question. When a couple is locked into "who was wrong," the question that breaks the lock asks what they want to build instead.

The question has three forms, often delivered as a sequence:
- "How do we want to be together?"
- "How do we want to love each other?"
- "How do we get there?"

"The question is, how are you going to move forward? The question is focusing. How do we want to be together? What's our shared vision? ... Anybody can have their point of view. The question is, are we able to be together? How do we want to be together? How do we want to love each other and how do we get there? That moves you forward." -- Joe Hudson, Why Blame Is So Corrosive

The question is offered as the structural replacement for the blame frame. Not as a soft alternative ("let's be positive instead"), but as the question that the blame question was preventing from being asked.

## When Joe Uses It

- After a blame loop has been identified and the energy on "who was wrong" needs to be redirected
- When a couple is in a [[shame-hot-potato]] dynamic and each side is mounting evidence
- When a person is in stage two of [[three-stages-of-the-blame-path]] and the work needs to move past blame altogether
- When [[blame-is-not-real]] has been delivered and the listener now needs a new question to ask in place of the blame question
- When a coaching session has reached the limit of useful diagnosis and needs to turn forward
- When [[4-keys-to-save-your-relationship]] is being introduced and the orienting question is required
- When a partner has been describing what is wrong with the other partner at length and needs to be redirected

## What It Produces

The first effect is disorientation. The person has been organized around the blame question for so long that the forward question feels foreign. They often do not have an immediate answer. The lack of answer is data, not failure.

The second effect, when the question is held, is the surfacing of wants. The person discovers they actually do have a vision for what they want the relationship to be -- it has just been buried under the blame. The vision was being prevented by the energy spent on "who was wrong."

The third effect is operational. Once "how do we want to be together" has an answer, "how do we get there" becomes a workable question. The relationship has direction. Direction makes movement possible.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person can't answer:** the question is held. The not-knowing is the first response. Joe does not rush to give them a vision. He waits. The wants emerge if the silence is held.

**If the person gives a list of what they don't want:** Joe redirects to what they do want. Lists of don't-wants are the blame question in disguise -- still organized around what the partner is doing wrong. The question requires the positive form to do its work.

**If the person gives a vision but it is about controlling the partner:** Joe surfaces this. "I want him to listen to me more" is not yet a shared vision. The work is to find what is wanted in the relationship, not what is wanted from the partner.

**If the person gives a genuine vision:** Joe moves to "how do we get there" -- the operational question that the vision now makes available.

## How The Three Parts Work Together

The three parts are not interchangeable. Each does something the others do not.

- **"How do we want to be together?"** establishes the *state* the couple wants to inhabit. The texture of the relationship. The way it feels.
- **"How do we want to love each other?"** establishes the *action* of love specifically. The particular way care wants to be expressed and received.
- **"How do we get there?"** establishes the *path*. The operational moves that turn the vision into the lived reality.

Without the first, there is no destination. Without the second, the destination has no specifics about care. Without the third, the vision stays abstract and does not produce action.

The sequence matters. If "how do we get there" is asked first, the question is empty because there is no destination yet. If "how do we want to be together" is asked and then dropped, the vision floats and does not become real. The sequence is the practice.

## Why This Question Works Where Blame Doesn't

Blame organizes attention backward, toward what happened. The energy goes into reconstructing the past, assigning fault, defending against fault, mounting evidence. Nothing changes about the present or the future. The couple stays in the same dynamic because the question they are asking has no exit.

The forward question organizes attention toward what is possible. The energy goes into description of the desired state, the operational moves toward it, the testing of whether the couple is actually in alignment about what they want. The dynamic shifts because the question has changed.

The forward question also requires both partners. The blame question can be asked unilaterally -- one person can spend hours building the case that the other is wrong. The forward question cannot be answered alone. "How do we want to be together" requires a we. The asking pulls the couple back into the same room.

## Tone

Joe asks this with a kind of brightness, a pivot in energy. The blame discussion is heavy; the forward question is offered as the way out of the heaviness. The brightness is the tonal signal: "we don't have to keep doing that thing, we can do this other thing instead." The brightness is part of why the question lands -- it offers relief by demonstrating that the heaviness was optional.

In a more depth-oriented coaching context, the question can also be asked with quiet spaciousness. The brightness is one delivery; the gentle pause is another. Joe matches the delivery to the room.

## What the Question Is Not

It is not "let's just look forward and forget what happened." The blame frame is being set down because it does not produce movement, not because the past does not matter. The past is allowed to be present; it just is not the organizing question.

It is not "let's both pretend we agree on everything." The question makes disagreement visible. Two partners can have different visions for how they want to be together. Surfacing that is the work. The question reveals the gap and gives the couple something to work with that is more workable than blame.

It is not a one-time question that produces a one-time answer. The question is asked again as the relationship evolves. The vision changes; the operational moves change; the question stays available as the orienting instrument.

## Related Questions in Joe's Toolkit

- "What do you want?" -- the individual version, asked when someone is in their shoulds rather than their wants
- "What are you noticing in your body?" -- the moment-to-moment somatic version
- "How do you want to do this?" -- the application of the forward orientation to a specific decision
- "What's underneath this for you?" -- the depth version, asked when the surface question is masking the actual material

The forward-looking relationship question is the couples version of "what do you want." The individual question asks one person what they want; the couples question asks both partners what they want together, recognizing that "together" is itself a thing that has a wanted shape.
