# How much of this game is to avoid the feeling?

## The Question

After a feeling has been named in the body (usually surfaced by [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-you-soften]]), ask what percentage of the entire long-running pattern is the defense against this specific feeling. The question reframes the pattern from "a problem I have" to "the structure I built to avoid this."

The phrasings Joe uses:
- "How much of this game is just to avoid the sadness?"
- "How much of the pressure is just to avoid the feeling?"
- "How much of this whole thing is the defense?"
- "How much of the whole game is to keep this feeling away?"

The word "game" is doing work. It signals that the entire pattern — the pressure, the rebellion, the loop, the years of trying to stop — is one move, not many. It is the move of avoiding this specific feeling. Naming it as a game makes it light enough to be put down.

The word "how much" rather than "is" is also doing work. It invites a percentage answer the body has access to even when the head is still defending the pattern. The body can say "ninety percent" before the head can say "no, the pattern is real."

This is the present-tense game-form of [[how-much-of-this-is-fear-of-feeling-the-hurt-you-already-have]]. The other question redirects future-fear to unfelt past pain. This question redirects a present chronic pattern to a present unfelt feeling. Both share the same structural mechanism: a defense is exposed by asking what feeling it is defending against.

## When Joe Uses It

After a specific feeling has been named in the body. The feeling usually surfaces from [[what-do-you-have-to-feel-when-you-soften]] or from a similar opening that asks the body to consult itself about what is underneath a defense.

The typical sequence:
1. The pressure-pattern or defensive pattern is in the room.
2. The recursion has been mirrored and the fix-question dropped.
3. The person has softened and the underneath-feeling has arrived (usually sadness, sometimes fear, sometimes anger).
4. **This question** reframes the entire pattern as the avoidance of this feeling.

The question can also work as a standalone diagnostic when a long-running pattern is in the room and a candidate underneath-feeling is visible from context. The question floats the candidate and lets the body verify or refuse.

## What It Produces

The most common response is a felt agreement — sometimes the words "yeah," sometimes a longer breath out, sometimes a fresh wave of the feeling. The body usually answers before the head. The percentage given is often high: "huge," "most of it," "all of it."

A secondary response is the next layer surfacing — the fear of the feeling itself. "If I let myself feel sad, I'll never stop." This is the mythology of the feeling and is its own work — see [[the-minds-stories-about-emotions]]. The fear surfacing in response to the question is data: it confirms that the feeling is the thing being defended against and shows what the defense was protecting against (not the feeling but the imagined permanence of it).

From the source session:

- **Joe:** "And what do you have to feel when you soften?"
- **Client:** "I feel, I feel some sadness."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. How much of this game is just to avoid the sadness?"
- **Client:** "Yeah, I feel like, uh, there's some fear that just came up around that I'll just like feel sad, feel sad. I just won't stop feeling sad."
- *(Source: The Question That Ended Her 30-Year War With Herself)*

The client's answer is structurally complete: she affirms the diagnosis ("yeah") and immediately surfaces the next layer (the fear of permanence). The two-part answer is common. The question lands as a diagnosis the body has been waiting to confirm.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **When the body confirms with "yeah" or a percentage:** the reframe has landed. The pattern is now visible as a defense. The next move depends on what surfaces — often [[the-minds-stories-about-emotions]] for the mythology, or staying with the feeling itself if no mythology arrives.

- **When the fear-of-the-feeling arrives:** name the mythology. "That's what our mind does. If I allow myself to be sad, I'll be sad forever. If I allow myself to be angry, I'll destroy what I love. If I allow myself to be scared, I'll become incapable." Don't argue with the mythology; name it as universal, then return to the feeling.

- **When the person resists the percentage ("not really, the pattern is real"):** the question has not landed yet, often because the head has rejoined to defend the pattern. Wait. Try again later in slightly different form. Sometimes the body will answer the question even when the head is denying it; watch for the body's answer.

- **When the response is tears or release:** stay. The question has done its work. The release is the loosening of the defense the question has just exposed.

- **When the person tries to convert the recognition into a task ("so I just need to feel the sadness"):** this is [[insight-converted-to-task-in-real-time]]. The head has caught the recognition and converted it into a new pressure. Joe usually names this gently and returns to the feeling.

## Why It Works

The pattern has authority because the person believes the pattern is the problem. The authority depends on the pattern being seen as primary and the feeling underneath being seen as either nonexistent or secondary. The question inverts the hierarchy: the feeling is primary, the pattern is the defense.

The inversion does two things. First, it deflates the pattern. The pressure-and-rebellion loop, when seen as a game to avoid sadness, becomes light. The years spent inside it are not wasted but they are also not load-bearing — they were the cost of avoiding the feeling. The cost is now visible.

Second, it makes the feeling the place to look. The person has been trying to solve the pattern for years. The pattern was solvable only by feeling the feeling. The question redirects the entire attention from "fix the pattern" to "let the feeling arrive."

The percentage framing matters. "Is this game to avoid the sadness?" can be answered with yes/no. "How much of this game?" requires a quantitative consult with the body, which is harder for the head to deflect. The number that comes back is usually surprising — usually higher than the person expected. The surprise is part of the question's effectiveness.

The word "game" matters. The pattern was being held as something serious and intractable. "Game" demotes it to something the person has been playing. Games can be stopped playing. Patterns require effortful solutions; games just require seeing the game.

## The Difference From "Is This About Avoidance?"

A weaker form: "is this whole thing about avoidance?" That question can be answered with "no, the pattern is real." The yes/no structure lets the head defend the pattern.

"How much of this game is to avoid the feeling" presumes that some of it is avoidance and asks for the percentage. The presumption is structurally hard to refuse. The person can verify the percentage at any level, but the question has already established that avoidance is part of the operation. Even a "twenty percent" answer is the recognition.

The phrasing also names the specific feeling. Without a specific feeling, the question is generic. With a specific feeling — "the sadness," "the fear" — the question has a precise target. The precision is part of why the body responds.

## Tone

Warm, slow, light. The "game" word is delivered with some lightness — not mocking the years of suffering, but inviting the person to see the structure as smaller than they have been holding it.

Joe often pauses before the question. The pause is the room for the just-named feeling to settle. The question then lands into that settled space rather than into the previous defensive activity.

The percentage framing is delivered as if asking the body, not the head. "How much of this game" is asked the way one might ask "how much do you weigh" — as a fact-finding question about something the person knows or can find out by checking.

## Variations

- **For chronic productivity-pressure:** "How much of the productivity is to avoid the feeling of [stillness/grief/uselessness]?"
- **For chronic people-pleasing:** "How much of the pleasing is to avoid the feeling of [being unwanted/being alone]?"
- **For chronic anger:** "How much of the anger is to avoid the feeling of [hurt/fear/helplessness]?" See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] for the related principle.
- **For chronic fixing:** "How much of the fixing is to avoid the feeling of [being-with what you can't fix]?"
- **For chronic story-telling:** "How much of the story is to avoid the feeling that's there without the story?"

The structural form is constant: name the chronic pattern, name the candidate feeling underneath, ask the percentage.

## What This Question Is Not

- It is not an accusation that the person has been wasting their life. The lifetime of the pattern is honored as the cost of the avoidance, not as evidence of stupidity.
- It is not a one-time question. The same question is asked at different points in a session and across sessions as new defenses surface and new feelings become available.
- It is not designed to produce a verbal percentage. The percentage is a vehicle. The real answer is the body's recognition. Sometimes the verbal answer is "I don't know" while the body has clearly answered "all of it." Joe trusts the body.
