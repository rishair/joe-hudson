# Would You Criticize Someone Else for It?

## The Question

A symmetry-test question used to surface the asymmetry between how the person treats their own behavior and how they would treat the same behavior in someone else. The question is most useful when shame is doing the criticism and the person believes the criticism is fair.

The phrasings:

- "Would you criticize somebody else for it?"
- "If it wasn't me, would I treat myself this way?"
- "Are they an asshole? Are they bad? Do they need to be fixed?"

The question's punch comes from the person they have in mind. Joe specifies: someone they care a lot about. The someone-else has to be a real person the speaker has affection for. Generic "would you say this to a stranger" is weaker than "would you say this to your sister, your best friend, your daughter."

"Would you criticize somebody else for it? For some people, that's going to get rid of like 80% of it. Oh, so somebody else was scared as a kid and they learned to hide — you think that if it's somebody they care a lot about — are they an asshole? Are they bad? Do they need to be fixed? That's another way to just really quickly say: oh wait, if it wasn't me, would I treat myself this way?" — Joe Hudson, *Fear of Being Seen*

## When Joe Uses It

The trigger is when the person is actively shaming themselves for a pattern that is structurally human. This includes:

- Self-shaming for being scared, frozen, anxious in social situations
- Self-criticism for "still" having a trauma response after years of work
- Beating themselves up for hiding, walking on eggshells, people-pleasing
- The retrospective spiral ("I should have said this, I should have spoken up, I should have been different")
- Any pattern that, if observed in someone they love, would receive compassion rather than criticism

The question is also useful when:

- The person has just shared something they call a "flaw" and is bracing for judgment
- The person describes their own behavior in the third person and the description is harsher than they would tolerate from anyone else about anyone else
- The person reports the [[the-eye-of-sauron]] criticism running on themselves
- The person is in [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]] mode and is using "growth" as the justification for self-attack

The question is precise and quick. It does not require the person to feel into anything deep. It is a thought experiment that surfaces the asymmetry, and the surfacing usually does substantial work on its own.

## What It Produces

The question typically produces several recognizable responses:

- **Immediate softening.** The person realizes the criticism they are running on themselves is not a criticism they would apply to anyone else. The recognition can produce visible release in the body — shoulders drop, breath returns, eyes soften.
- **80% drop in shame for the issue at hand.** Joe's specific number — not every issue, but many. The question is leveraged enough that one application can move the bulk of the shame around a specific pattern.
- **The defensive move.** Sometimes the person resists the symmetry: "Yes, but I have higher standards for myself." This is data. The resistance is the shame protecting itself; the work shifts to looking at why the higher standard is being applied. See [[self-improvement-is-shame-inducing]] and [[i-need-to-improve-myself]].
- **Specific compassion.** Sometimes the person can name exactly what they would say to a friend with the same pattern, and the contrast with what they are saying to themselves becomes the work.
- **Tears.** Sometimes the question lands at the level of grief — the recognition that the person has been treating themselves with a cruelty they would never aim at anyone they love. See [[recognizing-self-treatment-produces-grief]].

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the question lands and the shame softens: the work continues with the pattern, but the shame is no longer in charge of the conversation. The person is now able to look at their own behavior with something closer to the compassion they would extend to someone they love. The whole rest of the work becomes easier.

If the person resists with "but I should hold myself to a higher standard": the work pivots to examining what is driving the higher standard. Usually it is one of: an installed parental voice that did the same thing, a fear that without harsh self-criticism the person would become lazy or selfish, or a shame layer about *being someone who has shame*. The double-shame loop ("I shouldn't even feel shame anymore at this stage") is one of the common ones. See [[shame-after-spaciousness]] for a related pattern.

If the person can't find someone they care about to imagine the scenario with: this is meaningful data about isolation. The work may shift toward connection and exposure (see [[exposure-as-cure-for-shame-around-being-seen]]) before this question can do its full work.

If the person says "but they wouldn't have this problem to criticize": the question can be sharpened. "Imagine someone you love had exactly this pattern — same fear, same hiding, same freezing. What would you say to them?" The specificity prevents the abstraction-escape.

## Why This Works

The shame layer functions by claiming the deficiency is specific to the person — "*I* am the broken one." The work of any therapy that addresses shame is partly the work of locating the pattern in the common-human-experience category instead of the I-am-uniquely-broken category. See [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] and the related principle [[the-only-cure-for-shame-is-its-universality]] (when it exists).

The someone-else question performs this relocation operationally. It forces the person to consider the behavior abstracted from the specific identity. Once abstracted, the behavior almost always reveals itself as ordinary — something a kind person would respond to with compassion. The compassion is then made available for the person's own pattern. The shame layer's grip relies on the asymmetry; the question dissolves the asymmetry, and the shame loses its support.

This is structurally the same machinery as the [[treat-yourself-as-you-would-a-friend]] practice, but in question form for in-the-moment use rather than as a daily practice.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Person:** "I just keep hiding. I can't believe I still freeze up like this. It's pathetic."
- **Joe:** "Would you criticize somebody else for it? Like, somebody you really care about — they were scared as a kid and they learned to hide. Are they pathetic? Are they bad? Do they need to be fixed?"
- **Person:** [pause] "No. I'd just... I'd want them to be okay."
- **Joe:** "Yeah."
- *(Source: Fear of Being Seen, paraphrased structure)*

The work after this kind of exchange is much softer. The person can now actually look at the pattern rather than defending against the shame about it.

## Tone

Quick, light, almost casual. The question is not delivered as a heavy intervention. The lightness is part of why it works — the person can answer it almost reflexively, and the reflexive answer is the very thing the shame layer cannot survive.

When the person resists, Joe stays light. The lightness is a refusal to participate in the shame frame the person is offering. The refusal is itself part of the teaching.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not an argument for permissive self-treatment in all things. Joe is not claiming that every self-criticism is illegitimate. The question targets the *shame* layer — the corrosive, self-attacking kind. Functional self-correction ("I notice I didn't speak up, I want to next time") is different. See [[untruth-in-every-thought]] and the distinction between functional feedback and the abusive recurring voice.

This is also not a request to lower standards. The person can still want to grow. The question challenges the *cruelty* of the criticism, not the existence of a desire to grow. See the parallel teaching in [[recognition-with-open-heart-moves-pattern-recognition-with-shame-entrenches-it]].

This is also not a way to bypass real ethical concerns. If the person is genuinely harming someone, the someone-else test would still produce concern about the harm. The test does not eliminate accountability; it eliminates shame-based self-attack that is operating on top of ordinary human patterns.
