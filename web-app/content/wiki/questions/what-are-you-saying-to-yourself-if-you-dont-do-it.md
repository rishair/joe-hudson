# What are you saying to yourself about yourself if you don't do the thing you want to do?

## The Question

A question that surfaces the self-message embedded in the act of not doing the thing the person wants to do. The question refuses the usual cover story ("I didn't want to," "I wasn't ready," "I was scared") and forces the person to see that the not-doing itself was delivering a verdict about their worth.

Variations:

- "What are you saying to yourself about yourself if you don't approach the woman?"
- "What does that action communicate to yourself about yourself?"
- "What is it you're saying about yourself to yourself by not reaching out to the woman that you know you want to reach out to?"
- "What did you do? What does that action communicate to yourself about yourself?"
- "What would be the thing about yourself that makes you scared and not feel ready to approach somebody that you want to approach?"

The question has a specific grammar: "you" / "yourself" / "yourself." The triple-self construction is load-bearing. It locates the verdict-giver and the verdict-receiver as the same person, and surfaces the action as the message between them.

The companion question that often follows once the first one lands: "What would be the thing about yourself that makes you scared?" — this routes from the surface self-message ("I'm not ready") to the underneath ("there's something about me I don't want to show").

## When Joe Uses It

The question is offered when:

- The person describes wanting to do something and not doing it, particularly with a person whose response matters (approaching someone they want to date, speaking up to a parent, saying the hard truth to a boss)
- The person has just deflected with a cover story ("I just didn't want to," "I wasn't ready") and Joe needs to push past the deflection
- The person is presenting [[i-want-connection-with-people-im-afraid-will-judge-me]] or [[how-do-i-stop-seeking-approval]] and the entry point is the specific instance of inaction
- The person is treating the not-doing as a neutral event ("I just didn't approach") rather than as a self-communication
- The person has named winning/not-winning as their internal state and Joe wants to surface the structure of the un-winning

The question is particularly useful when the person describes a recent moment of inaction with some affect (regret, frustration, confusion about themselves) but cannot yet articulate what was being decided. The affect is the data that something was decided; the question makes the decision visible.

## What It Produces

The first response is typically confusion or the cover story.

"Like, what would I say to myself?"

"Uh, I didn't want to."

The cover story is the diagnostic. The person genuinely does not see the not-doing as a self-message because the cover story has been doing the work of obscuring the message for years. Joe's typical response is to refuse the cover story directly: "No, no, you're not. You're fucking with me." The directness is required. Without it, the cover story closes the question and the work cannot proceed.

When the cover story is refused, the next response is often "I'm kind of confused, but..." followed by the actual self-message: "I'm scared and I don't feel ready." The "scared and not ready" is closer but still surface. Joe goes one more layer: "What would be the thing about yourself that makes you scared?" The deeper answer is some version of "there's something about me I don't want to show her" — which is the actual self-message the not-doing has been delivering. The person is not approaching because they have already decided they are not approvable as they are.

The recognition that lands: the not-doing was telling the person they were not worthy. The person was waiting for the woman's validation to confirm they were worthy. But the not-approaching had already issued the verdict in the opposite direction. The validation-seeking strategy is structurally self-defeating because the strategy itself (not acting) is the un-validation.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person gives the cover story ("I didn't want to"): refuse it. "You're fucking with me." The refusal is direct because the cover story will keep the question from landing. Joe is not arguing with the person about what they wanted; he is naming that the response is a deflection from the question being asked.

If the person says they don't know what they're saying to themselves: slow down and re-anchor in the specific moment. "Just let that sit for a second." The not-knowing is often the body trying to feel something that the mind has been suppressing. The pause lets the body answer when the mind cannot.

If the person produces the self-message at the cover-story level ("I'm scared," "I'm not ready"): take the surface message and ask for the underneath. "What would be the thing about yourself that makes you scared and not feel ready?" The question pushes from the affect-statement to the self-claim that the affect is protecting.

If the person produces the actual self-message ("there's something about me I don't want to show her"): Joe reflects it back and names the structural implication. "So you're not validating who you are in that moment by not approaching. You're not winning." The reflection connects the specific self-message to the broader structural fact that the not-doing has been the un-validation all along.

If the person attempts to fix the situation by deciding to approach next time: Joe redirects to the felt-sense first. The fix-frame is the same approval-seeking move with a new costume (now the approval is being sought from the self-improvement narrative). The work is not to start approaching; the work is to stop blocking the natural flow of the self, of which approaching is one expression.

## The Structural Insight Behind the Question

The question is the operational form of [[action-toward-what-you-want-is-the-self-validation]]. The principle: every action toward what you actually want is itself the validation of your wanting it. Every action away from what you want is itself the un-validation. There is no separate "validation" step that happens after the action; the action IS the validation. The not-doing is therefore the un-validation, automatically and structurally.

The question makes this visible at the level of a specific instance. Once the person sees that not-approaching said "I'm not worthy," the loop becomes visible everywhere. Every held-back truth, every unsent message, every withheld want is the same operation. The self-message is constant, low-grade, and corrosive. It is not heard as a message because it is delivered by the absence of action rather than by words. But it is heard. The body keeps the score.

This is why external validation does not satisfy the approval-seeker. The seeker is delivering the un-validation to themselves continuously through their own not-doing, and no amount of external approval can outweigh the constant self-issued verdict. The cure is not to extract validation from others; the cure is to stop issuing the un-validation. And the way to stop issuing the un-validation is to start doing the thing — not for the validation, but because the doing is the validation.

## Why the Question Works on People the Standard Approval-Seeking Frame Does Not

For some people, the abstract teaching about approval-seeking does not land. They hear it as another self-improvement principle to fail at. The specific-instance question bypasses the abstraction. It is not asking the person to consider how they relate to validation in general; it is asking them what they told themselves about themselves in the specific moment they did not act.

The specificity is what produces the felt-recognition. The person can deflect a general principle. They cannot easily deflect a specific instance of their own behavior with the question pointed directly at it. Once the specific instance has produced the recognition, the general principle becomes visible.

## Dialogue Example

The canonical exchange from the source session:

- **Client:** *(presenting the concern about wanting connection with people he's afraid will judge him)*
- **Joe:** "What are you saying to yourself about yourself if you don't approach the woman?"
- **Client:** "Like, what would I say to myself?"
- **Joe:** "No, what did you do? What does that action communicate to yourself about yourself?"
- **Client:** *(pause)* "Uh, I didn't want to."
- **Joe:** "No, no, you're not. You're fucking with me."
- **Client:** "Oh, I actually— I truly thought that was the answer."
- **Joe:** "Okay, okay. So I said you wanted to reach out to the woman and you didn't. What is it you're saying about yourself to yourself by not reaching out to the woman that you know you want to reach out to?"
- **Client:** "Oh, I'm kind of confused, but I'm going to say like, I'm scared and like, I don't feel ready."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. And what makes it that you're scared and don't feel ready in that moment? What would be the thing about yourself that makes you scared and not feel ready to approach somebody that you want to approach?"
- **Client:** *(after thought)* "It's like, I don't want to cry when I'm approaching a woman I'm attracted to."
- **Joe:** "Yeah, so what I'm hearing on some level is that there's something about me that I don't like, that I don't want to show to this woman."
- **Client:** "For sure."
- **Joe:** "Right, so you're saying that you're like, on some level, you're not validating who you are in that moment by not approaching. You're not winning."

*(Source: I Want Connection with People I'm Afraid Will Judge Me | Coaching with Joe)*

The sequence shows the full arc of the question: cover story → refusal of cover story → surface self-message → deeper self-claim → reflection back as the structural fact (not-doing = un-validation). The arc takes about 90 seconds in live time. The recognition is the move.

## Tone

Direct, slightly insistent. The question carries the energy of "I am not going to let you off the hook with the easy answer." Joe's "you're fucking with me" is delivered without anger but with the firmness required to break the cover story. The tone is the tone of a coach who knows the actual answer is one layer below where the client is offering, and who has the conviction to wait there until the actual answer arrives.

After the actual answer arrives, the tone shifts to warmer reflection. "So you're saying that you're like, on some level, you're not validating who you are in that moment by not approaching. You're not winning." The reflection is gentle. The person has just seen something hard about themselves; the gentleness lets the recognition settle without shame.

## Why This Question Is Different From "Why Didn't You Do It?"

A coach might ask "why didn't you approach her?" The question lands in the analysis frame. The client produces reasons — fear, readiness, timing, doubt about the response. The reasons close the question. The work does not proceed.

Joe's question — "what are you saying to yourself about yourself if you don't?" — refuses the reasons frame. The not-doing is not framed as having a cause to be explained. It is framed as a communication that has been delivered. The communication has a content. The question is asking for the content.

The grammatical move (action-as-communication rather than action-as-effect) is the key. Once the action is heard as a communication, the speaker and the recipient are both the self. The self-communication is the territory of the work. The cause-analysis is the avoidance of the territory.
