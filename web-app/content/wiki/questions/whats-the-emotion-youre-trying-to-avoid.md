# What's the emotion you're trying to avoid?

## The Question

The question is asked tactically when a person has presented a decision they cannot resolve. It is asked on each branch of the fork, not just one.

- "What's the emotion you're trying to avoid?"
- "What's the emotional reality that you don't want to deal with?"
- "What's the emotion you're trying to avoid in both of these two decisions?"
- "And if you went and had a baby, what's the emotional experience that you're avoiding there?"

The question follows a setup. The setup is the principle that legitimizes the question: "Big decisions are usually just avoided emotions. We make decisions in the emotional part of our brain. If I took the emotional part of your brain out, you would take 4 hours to decide where to have lunch, but your IQ would be the same, neurologically speaking." With that grounding, the question stops sounding like a soft sidestep and starts sounding like the actual diagnostic.

## When Joe Uses It

This is the move when the person presents a decision as the thing to work on. Not a decision they have already made; a decision they are stuck inside. The setup is some version of: "I am choosing between A and B, and I don't know how to choose." The question diverts from solving the decision and goes underneath it.

Specific situations:

- The person frames their concern as a choice between two paths and asks for help choosing.
- The person has been "sitting in the question" of the decision for months or years without resolution.
- The person presents pros and cons of each option and the conversation is at risk of becoming intellectual analysis.
- The person has just rested from burnout and is now standing at a fork that both options are coloring with the burnout fear. See [[im-burned-out]] and [[im-afraid-ill-revert-to-my-old-pattern]].
- The person has named a fear of one option (the obvious aversive) but has not named the fear of the other option (the option that seems wanted). The question forces the symmetry.
- The person is in [[treating-it-as-a-decision-when-its-an-emotion]] -- presenting a decision-frame for what is actually an avoidance.

The crucial timing: ask the question early in the session, before getting drawn into adjudicating the decision. Once the conversation starts comparing the merits of A vs B, the decision-frame is being honored and the deeper question gets buried.

## What It Produces

People usually answer this question on the first pass. The avoided emotion is not deeply buried; it has been operating right under the decision. The decision frame was the cover, not the burial.

In the live session this article is sourced from, the person was choosing between starting her career again and starting a family. Joe asked the question on each side:

- On the career side: "I don't want to experience burnout again."
- On the family side: "Feeling like I'm missing on something because if I'm having a child, then I will not have my career. And that's like fear of missing out. I think that I will have a long time not doing something, not having progress."

What looked like two different fears ("burnout" vs "FOMO") on first reading were the same fear in different costumes when Joe held them next to each other: both were the fear of "a long time not doing something." Once the symmetry was visible, the decision-frame dissolved. There was nothing to choose between because both options had the same underneath.

This is the predictable shape of what the question produces:

1. The person names a different fear on each side.
2. The two fears turn out to be the same fear with two costumes.
3. The decision-frame loses its grip.
4. The actual work becomes visible: how to relate to the avoided emotion itself, not how to choose the path that avoids it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The first move after the person names the avoided emotions on each side is to surface the symmetry. Joe does this by asking how the two are different.

"And when -- how, how does burnout not do the same thing? If you burn out on your career, how, how does that not also equal having a long time not doing something?" -- Joe Hudson, How Do I Not Burn Out Again?

The person, looking at it, usually says some version of "it's the same." That recognition is the work landing. The decision was not a decision. It was a maneuver to find an option that would avoid the feeling, and the maneuver was failing because both options have the feeling on the other side.

The second move is to reframe the question. The actual question is not which path to take. The actual question is how to do anything at all without running from that feeling. "So what -- maybe you're asking the wrong question. If you don't think you can guarantee not to burn out, it means that you like -- no, no choices where you want to go. So what if the question is, how do I work, or how do I have a baby with no burnout? What's the answer there?"

The reframe takes the person out of the fork and into the deeper question: what is your relationship to the avoided emotion such that no path is its escape? That question opens onto the real work. In the live session it opened onto self-attention and not managing other people's opinion -- the actual engine of the original burnout. See [[caring-what-others-think-is-the-burnout-engine]].

If the person resists the question and tries to keep working the decision-frame, the move is gentle reassertion: "Yeah, you want to feel achievement and progress, and you don't want to feel non-progress, stuck, and burnout, right? Yeah, yeah. And, uh, what makes you think that you could either have a baby or, um, have a career and guarantee that you don't feel burnout?" The question that surfaces the impossibility of avoidance via choice. Once that lands, the decision-frame can no longer hold.

## The "Hurt" Variant

When the deliberation is about a relationship (entering one, staying in one, leaving one) and the avoided material is grief, loss, or pain rather than fear, shame, or guilt, Joe often substitutes "hurt" for "emotion." The variant: "What's the hurt you're trying to avoid, if any?"

The substitution matters. "Emotion" is generic and can be answered with a list. "Hurt" is specific and points at the wound the avoidance is organized around. The asker who substitutes "hurt" is telegraphing that they expect the answer to be a pain — usually a pain that has already happened and is being prevented from happening again.

The "if any" appended to the question matters too. It removes the assumption that there must be a hurt. The person can say no, there isn't one. Most often there is, and the "if any" gives the person space to find it without being told it's there.

In the source session for this variant, Joe asked "what's the hurt you're trying to avoid, if any?" after the man had named his ambivalence about being in another relationship. The answer surfaced his marriage history — 10 years married, then divorced, then a 3-year relationship that ended, then a year single. The hurt was the recurring loss. The deliberation about a new relationship was organized around not re-encountering that hurt. See [[should-i-be-in-a-relationship-at-all]] for the concern this variant most often serves.

The variant is also useful when the person is post-something (post-divorce, post-loss, post-major-life-transition) and the avoided material is anticipated grief rather than abstract fear. The hurt-language acknowledges what has happened in a way the emotion-language does not.

## What This Question Is Not

This is not [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]]. That question goes for the bottom — the deepest abyss the person has organized their whole life around avoiding. This question is tactical. It asks about the specific feeling being avoided in this specific decision. The deeper-abyss question is a closer in a session. This question is an opener.

This is also not a generic "how do you feel about each option?" That question invites narration. This question is sharper: it names that there is an avoidance happening and asks for the avoided feeling directly. The framing matters. "How do you feel about A?" produces story. "What's the emotion you're trying to avoid in A?" produces the avoided emotion.

## Tone

Direct, ground-level, not soft. The principle that decisions are emotional grounds the question in neuroscience, which gives the question weight. Joe asks it the way a doctor asks "where does it hurt?" — clinically, without drama, expecting an answer.

The follow-up ("and on the other side?") is asked the same way. The symmetry move is the move; the tone is what allows it to land without feeling like an attack on the legitimacy of the decision the person is wrestling with.
