# What if there was no scorecard?

## The Question

- "What if there was no scorecard?"
- "If you just literally couldn't believe in a scorecard. Every scorecard, you were just like, it's not at all accurately assessing in your heart, in your body. If there's no scorecard, what would you have to sit with?"
- "Let's say you actually find out with 100% certainty that there is no scorecard. There's no way of describing your worth accurately."

The full delivery has a specific structure: the question, followed by a clause that closes the escape route ("if you just literally couldn't believe in one"), followed by the "what would you have to sit with" prompt. The third part is what makes the question land somatically rather than intellectually. The intellect can answer "I'd find a new one." The body has to answer "what would I have to feel?"

## When Joe Uses It

When the person has surfaced a scorecard (money, likes, status, achievement, recognition) and is reporting their self-worth oscillating with it. The read that triggers this question is [[scorecard-keeping-as-identity-structure]]: the scorecard is doing the work of identity, not just metric. The question is the move that makes the structural function visible.

Specifically:
- The person describes mood tracking an external metric ("the market's been up a few days, I feel a little better about everything").
- The person reports having already swapped scorecards once ("I went from chasing money to chasing likes") and the new one is doing the same job.
- The person uses comparative language about self-worth and external position ("I'm better because I have more X").
- The person has gotten "to the top" of a scorecard and reports the dissatisfaction.

This is not a question for someone whose relationship with a scorecard is functional. It is a question for someone whose self has structurally organized around the metric. The question dissolves the organization to reveal what is underneath.

This question belongs in a small family of Joe's destabilizing questions, alongside [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]] and [[what-are-you-defending]]. All three operate by removing the structure the person is using to navigate, then asking what is left.

## What It Produces

A somatic drop, usually. The person describes a roller-coaster-stomach feeling, a punch in the gut, a sense of falling. This is the body responding to identity dissolution, not to the loss of any actual resource. The drop is the diagnostic: it shows the scorecard was doing identity work, not just metric work.

The verbal answer is usually:
- "I'd be lost."
- "I'd be rudderless."
- "I'd be wayward / meandering."
- "I wouldn't know who I am."
- "I'd be scared."

Each of these is a name for the felt absence of the orienting structure. The work then is to stay with the felt absence rather than to rescue it with a new scorecard.

Often the person tries to escape into intellect ("well, intellectually I know I'll be fine"). Joe usually deflects this — "I don't care about the intellect, tell me about the emotional component" — and brings the question back to the body.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person says "I'd be lost":** Joe stays with the felt sense. "How does it feel to be rudderless?" The body answer (stomach drop, falling sensation) is the data. He may compare it to a known sensation: "How is that feeling different than freedom? Let's say you bungee jump and you have that exhilaration of like, oh, I'm flying. How is it different?" The contrast surfaces that the rudderless feeling is fear, not loss-of-direction.

**If the person tries to escape into intellect:** Joe redirects. "I don't care about the intellect. Tell me about the emotional component." The intellect can construct a new scorecard quickly. The body has to actually feel the absence.

**If the person says "I'd be scared":** Joe asks what specifically is scared, or what is trying to survive. The question excavates downward. "What specifically is trying to survive?" usually surfaces the body, then survival, then loneliness, then the realization that no scorecard helps with what is actually scared.

**If the person tries to find a way the scorecard would still help:** Joe closes the escape. He has multiple variations that close different escapes:
- The dark cave: "Imagine you're in a cave forever with a mask on. You can't see anything. Your needs are met. What's the scorecard?" This removes the social field, making visible that the scorecard does not help with what is actually scared.
- The 30-year safety guarantee: "Let's just assume that for 30 years, you're physically safe. What's left to be scared of?" This removes survival, making visible that the scorecard is not protecting against actual death; it is protecting against something else.
- See [[strip-away-externals-thought-experiment]] for the full move.

**When the person arrives at the felt absence:** Joe pivots to the inversion: [[whats-the-scorecard-that-cant-be-taken-away]]. The destabilizing question dissolved the external structure. The inversion question invites the internal one. The scorecard impulse stays. The substrate shifts to something that cannot be revoked.

## Dialogue Example

- **K:** "There's still some kind of scorecard that I keep in my head. And it kind of — self-worth kind of oscillates with that. Like the market's been up a few days, I'm like, ah, I just like feel a little bit better about everything."
- **Joe:** "Great. Okay. So, what if there was no scorecard?"
- **K:** "Hmm."
- **Joe:** "If you just literally couldn't believe in a scorecard. Every scorecard, you were just like, it's not at all accurately assessing in your heart, in your body. If there's no scorecard, what would you have to sit with?"
- **K:** "I think I'd be lost. Yeah. I think I would be rudderless."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. That's great. How does it feel to be rudderless?"
- **K:** "It's funny, you like, uh, as you said that, there's like — this, you know, like when you drop on a roller coaster and your stomach catches up with you on the drop. That's like, that's what I felt in that moment."
- *(Source: What's Your Scorecard? — the question, the somatic-drop response, and the staying-with-the-feeling sequence.)*

## Tone

Calm, slightly playful, completely undefended. Joe asks the question as if it might just be true that there is no scorecard. He doesn't load it with weight or treat it as a heavy revelation. The lightness is part of what makes the body let in the question.

The pacing is critical. Joe asks the question, then waits. He does not fill the silence. The "what would you have to sit with" prompt is given after the first response has had time to land. The question is not rushed.

When the somatic drop arrives, Joe meets it with curiosity, not concern. "That's great." The drop is what is supposed to happen. Treating it as good keeps the person in the body rather than launching them into "what's wrong with me that this feels so bad."
