# What's the scariest question to ask?

## The Question

A specific opening-intake question used when the person has multiple questions and the coach needs to identify which one to work on. The criterion is not which question is most important or most strategic — it is which one is scariest, which one is making the person almost cry right now.

Joe's exact form:

"I don't even know which one to choose, but the one that the rest hinges on. The one that's scariest to ask, the one that's making you almost cry right now." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

The question is delivered when the person opens with some version of "I have a lot" or "I don't know where to start" or lists multiple topics. The standard response is to ask which is most pressing. Joe's response is to ask which is scariest — and to use the body (almost crying) as the signal that identifies the right one.

## When Joe Uses It

The question applies when:

- The person opens with multiple potential topics and cannot choose between them
- The person is visibly close to tears or emotionally loaded
- The body is producing a signal (eyes wetting, voice tight, breath caught) that one of the questions matters more than the others
- The standard "what would you like to work on" question would invite the person to pick the most strategic-sounding topic rather than the actually live one
- The session has time for one deep thread rather than several surface ones

The question does NOT apply when:

- The person has clearly named a single question already
- The person is dissociated and not producing any body signal that would distinguish the questions
- There is a topic that has structural priority (a safety issue, a time-sensitive decision) that needs attention regardless of which question is scariest

## What It Produces

The question relocates the selection from the head to the body. The person was about to choose intellectually — which topic seems most important, which one I should work on, which one will be most productive. The question redirects to the felt criterion — which one is scariest, which one is loaded right now.

The body usually knows immediately. The question that the body has been holding most heavily surfaces as soon as the criterion shifts to body-loading. The person produces the question, often with a small surprise that this was the one — they had been about to pick a different one as the "right" choice.

In the source session, the person had said "Um, I got a lot." Joe asked the scariest-question version. The person produced: "How do I love myself and express my emotions properly?" The question came out tearful. The body had been holding that question. The intake question identified it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

Once the scariest question has been produced, Joe takes it as the contract for the session. The other questions the person was holding are not engaged unless they surface as part of the work on the chosen one. Often the chosen question turns out to be the structural ancestor of the others — work on it dissolves several of the others without addressing them directly.

The selection itself is usually emotional. The person crying or almost crying while producing the question is part of the data — the body has confirmed that the right thread has been pulled. Joe stays with the emotional loading rather than redirecting away from it.

The follow-up is usually a clarifying question on the chosen topic. In the source session, the person said "how do I love myself and express my emotions properly?" and Joe immediately asked: "What do you mean properly?" The clarifier surfaced the suppression frame that was running underneath the question.

## Why This Variant Matters

The standard intake question ("what would you like to work on?") invites the person to use their head to pick a topic. The head will often pick the topic that is most respectable, most articulable, most productive-seeming. The actually live material — the thing the body is holding — often gets bypassed because it is not the most respectable thing on the list.

The scariest-question variant overrides the head's selection criterion. The body's signal (almost crying) becomes the indicator. The result is that the session works on what is actually live rather than what is intellectually preferred.

This is structurally related to [[whats-the-real-question]] but operates differently. Whats-the-real-question is for when the person has given an extended context and never named a question — the move is to ask for the question that wasn't asked. The scariest-question variant is for when the person has multiple candidate questions — the move is to filter by body-loading.

The two questions can be sequenced. The scariest-question variant identifies which question the body is most loaded around. If that question turns out to be a cover for a deeper question, whats-the-real-question can follow.

## The Body as the Selector

The deeper teaching the question operationalizes: the body knows which question matters. The intellect produces many candidates because it doesn't know which is load-bearing. The body has been carrying one of them more heavily than the others, and the carrying produces visible signs — tears in the eyes, a tightness in the throat, a breath catch.

The signs are the data. The intake question uses the data to make the selection. The person does not have to articulate why one question matters more than another. They just have to follow the body's signal to the one that is loaded right now.

This is the principle [[the-body-knows-the-truth]] applied to the question of "what should we work on." The body knows. The intake question gives the body permission to be the selector.

## What This Question Is Not

- It is not asking the person to perform their most dramatic concern. The criterion is the body's actual loading, not a performance of intensity.
- It is not asking the person to be brave. The framing is what makes you almost cry — not what would be hardest to discuss. The body's involuntary signal is the marker, not the volitional choice to discuss difficult material.
- It is not appropriate for someone who is already dissociated. If there is no body signal because the body is offline, the question has no input to work with. In that case, the first move is somatic reconnection, then the intake question.

## Dialogue Example

The full opening of the source session:

- **Joe:** *(after greeting)* "So..."
- **Guest:** "Um, I got a lot."
- **Joe:** "Um, I don't even know which one to choose, but the one that the rest hinges on. The one that's scariest to ask, the one that's making you almost cry right now."
- **Guest:** "I mean, how do I — how do I love myself and express my emotions properly, or learn to express my emotions? I—"
- **Joe:** "What do you mean properly?"
- *(Source: 2025-04-18_Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time)*

The exchange shows the question working as designed. The person opens with the indication that they have many questions. Joe uses the scariest-question filter. The person produces a single question tearfully. Joe immediately enters the clarifying work on that question. The whole intake takes under thirty seconds.

## Tone

Direct, curious, and slightly playful. The "I don't even know which one to choose" is delivered as if Joe is helping the person solve a navigation problem they both have. The framing "the one that's scariest" is offered as an option, not as a demand — the person can choose whether to follow the body's signal or to stay with the intellectual selection.

The "making you almost cry right now" is delivered as observation, not as confrontation. Joe is naming what he is already seeing in the person's face. The naming gives the person permission to use the visible emotion as the marker rather than to override it with a more respectable choice.
