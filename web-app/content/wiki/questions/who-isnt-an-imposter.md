# Who isn't an imposter?

## The Question

- "Who isn't an imposter?"
- "Who do you think actually knows what they're doing?"
- "Can you name one person who's doing something real and knows how to do it?"

## When Joe Uses It

When someone is in the loop of [[i-have-imposter-syndrome]] -- trying to prove to themselves that they are not an imposter and losing the argument. The question does not settle the argument. It dissolves the frame.

The frame says: there are real-people-who-know and there are imposters-who-don't, and I am ashamed to be in the second category. The question forces the person to try to populate the first category. The category turns out to be empty.

Joe uses this when the person is gripping for an example of someone who has crossed the line into qualified knowing. Often they reach for someone famous -- Elon Musk, a favorite writer. The follow-up moves complete the work: Elon did not know how to build a spaceship when he started and is still iterating. The favorite writer did many edits. The reach for the qualified example is itself the proof that the category is empty.

## What It Produces

- A pause. The person tries to name someone and the example does not hold up.
- Often a small shift in posture: the gripping for evidence relaxes when the search comes up empty.
- A specific disconcerting feeling -- "it's disconcerting because it means that there's nobody who knows exactly what they're doing." -- Joe Hudson, Imposter Syndrome. The ground falls out. Joe stays with that.
- A relief, eventually. If everyone is an imposter, the shame around being one collapses. The kindergarten frame becomes available: it does not bother you to be a kindergartner if everyone is a kindergartner.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- If the person reaches for an example (Elon Musk, a writer, a CEO): walk them through how that person was also figuring it out, also failing, also iterating. The example collapses.
- If the person says "I don't know": treat that as the work. The category they were defending is empty. Stay with the discomfort of the ground falling out.
- If the person says "But I'm worse than the others": that is the shame layer. The question has done its work; now address the shame directly. See [[shame-drives-behavior]].
- If the person says "But it still feels real": of course it does -- it is real. The principle [[everyone-doing-something-real-is-an-imposter]] does not deny the feeling. Move to the body. The feeling is fear, which is excitement without the breath. Offer [[say-im-excited-ten-times]] and [[fear-vs-excitement]].

## Tone

Curious and a little playful. Not interrogating. The question should land like an honest invitation to think it through, not like a gotcha. The dissolution that follows is more powerful if the question feels open.
