# What makes you care about the outcome?

## The Question

- "What makes you care about the outcome?"
- "If you're going to be who you're going to be and the outcome's going to do what it does, what makes you concerned?"
- "What's the core, like right down into it -- what's the reason that you care about the outcome?"

A companion question that often precedes it:
- "If I was to say to you, you get to determine all of your results, or you get to determine how you are as a person -- which are you going to choose?"

The companion question almost always produces "how I am" as the answer. Then the main question reveals the gap: if how-you-are is what you want, what makes you care so much about the outcome?

## When Joe Uses It

This question is used when:
- The person has named fear of an outcome that's driving their behavior
- The person is trying to make a "decision" that feels heavy because they're attached to a specific result
- The person is in the second-guessing loop and the loop is fueled by outcome attachment
- The fear of result is producing rigidity that's likely to produce the feared outcome (see [[what-we-fear-we-invite]])
- The "trying" quality is high and the "doing" quality is low

The question comes after the surface fear has been named. It is a downstream question. It is asked once the person has agreed that they are afraid of something. The question then asks: why does that outcome matter so much?

It's also used when the person has named values they want to protect (connection, integrity, authenticity). The question becomes the doorway to noticing that those values live in how-they-are, not in the outcome.

## What It Produces

A pause, then often a value-statement. Common answers:
- "Because if it doesn't go that way, I'll lose connection with X."
- "Because if it goes badly, I'll feel like I failed."
- "Because I have these attachments to outcomes and I want to make sure I have this thing."
- "Because there's fear of a loss of something that's a value of mine -- a value of connection, or authenticity, or integrity."

The answers are almost always one of: relational (I'll lose someone or someone will see me differently), identity (I won't be the kind of person I want to be), or material (I'll lose something I have or fail to get something I want).

What the answers reveal: the person has located their okay-ness in the outcome rather than in their being. The outcome has become the placeholder for something that was supposed to live in them. The fear is not about the outcome itself. The fear is about what the outcome would mean about them.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The move depends on what the person names:

**If they name a value (connection, integrity, authenticity):**
"But that's all in how you are. I can be connected if everybody's spitting on me, and I can be disconnected if everybody's loving me." This shows the person that the value is not actually contingent on the outcome. The value lives in how they show up. The outcome can go any way and the value remains intact.

**If they name a relational fear (losing someone):**
Often the same move -- the connection lives in how-they-are, not in whether the other person reciprocates. "I can feel connected to someone without them being connected to me." This is hard to receive but it lands.

**If they name an identity fear (I won't be who I want to be):**
Joe usually flips to the deeper question: "What if you're all of that?" The identity-fear is binary (I am that or I'm not that), and Joe dissolves the binary. The person is all the things they fear being and all the things they want to be. The fear is what makes them treat the contents of self as exclusive options.

**If they can't go further:**
Joe asks the next-level question: "What's the ultimate thing you're running from?" See [[whats-the-ultimate-thing-youre-running-from]]. The outcome-attachment is the surface layer; the question is what's underneath it.

## The Pivot This Question Performs

This question performs a specific pivot. Before the question: the person is trying to manage the outcome. After the question: the person is looking at why the outcome matters. The before is impossible (outcomes can't be controlled). The after is workable (the attachment can be felt and released).

This is the same pivot Joe uses across many topics. "What if you can't change the result and you can change how you are?" The pivot reorients the person from result-management to how-management. The how is where the actual freedom is. See [[how-do-you-want-to-be]].

## The Annoying-Question Acknowledgment

Joe often pre-acknowledges that the question is annoying. "Again, an annoying question, but what makes you wanna result?" The pre-acknowledgment matters. It signals that Joe knows he is asking the person to look at something they have been avoiding looking at, and that he's going to keep asking anyway.

The pre-acknowledgment also disarms the response. The person is less likely to dismiss the question with annoyance if Joe has already named that it might feel annoying. The shared acknowledgment of the discomfort makes the question more receivable.

## Dialogue Examples

- **Joe:** "If I was to say to you, you get to determine all of your results, or you get to determine how you are as a person, which are you going to choose?"
- **Person:** "Probably how I am. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "So then what makes you care about the results?"
- **Person:** "Yeah, that's a great question. I have these attachments to outcomes..."
- **Joe:** "What's the core, like right down into it, what's the reason that you care about the outcome? If you're gonna be who you're gonna be and the outcome's gonna do what it does, what makes you concerned?"
- **Person:** "There's fear of a loss of something that is a value of mine. Like fear of a loss of connection or fear of a loss of authenticity or integrity."
- **Joe:** "But that's all in how you are. Like I can be connected if everybody's spitting on me, and I can be disconnected if everybody's loving me."
- *(Source: How Do I Trust Myself? -- The question reveals that the values being protected live in how-being, not in outcome.)*

## Tone

Patient, slightly insistent, with the friendly acknowledgment that the question is annoying. Joe doesn't ask the question once and accept the first answer. He asks variations, presses, comes back to it. The persistence is friendly, but it doesn't let the person off the hook.

The pacing is slow. Each version of the question is given time to land. Joe waits for the body answer, not the head answer.
