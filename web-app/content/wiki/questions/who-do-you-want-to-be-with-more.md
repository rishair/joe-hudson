# Who do you want to be with more?

## The Question

"Who do you want to be with more? This or the person who's pushing?"

The question is asked immediately after the client has briefly touched the connecting/present mode in the session — usually for only a few seconds. The two modes (the one they just briefly were, and the one they have been most of their life) are made into a binary choice. The client is asked to feel which one they want to be with.

Variations:
- "Which version of you do you want to be with?"
- "Which one do you want to be in the room with?"
- "Who do you prefer being — this you or the pushing you?"
- "Which one of these would you rather hang out with?"

The question can also be addressed about another person ("which version of your partner do you prefer?") but its primary use is about the client themselves, asking them to be the witness to their own two modes.

## When Joe Uses It

The question is deployed at a specific moment: after the client has briefly demonstrated the new mode in the session and the coach wants the client to feel their own preference for it. The timing is important. Too early (before the new mode has actually arrived in the room), the question is abstract — the client cannot feel what they are choosing between. Too late (after the moment has passed), the question becomes a memory exercise rather than a live preference.

Joe deploys it when:

- The client has been operating in a [[proving-instead-of-connecting]] mode and briefly drops into the connecting mode during the session
- A binary contrast between two felt states is available and recent
- The client needs to access their own preference for the new mode as the leverage for sustaining it
- The defensive system would resist a direct instruction to be in the new mode but cannot resist a question about preference
- The connection to client work or relational work needs to be made — the client's preference for one version of themselves implies the same preference in their customers/partners/kids

The question is not asked to extract a particular answer. The answer is almost always obvious (the connected version), but the work of the question is in the felt-experience of choosing, not in the verbal answer.

## What It Produces

**An immediate felt preference.** The client almost always answers without hesitation. The "Oh, this" answer carries somatic weight — the body has registered the difference between the two modes and knows which one feels better.

**The leverage for change.** Once the client has felt their own preference, the work has a referent. The new mode is not an instruction imposed by the coach; it is something the client just chose. The same mechanism that produced the preference will reproduce it as the client keeps practicing.

**The bridge to client work / relational work.** Joe often follows the answer with "your clients also" or "your partner also" or "your kids also." The implication: if the client prefers the connected version of themselves, the same preference is being held by everyone they relate to. The client's customers prefer the connected version. The proving version is what has been pushing them away.

**The collapse of the "but how" defense.** When the client has touched the new mode and confirmed they prefer it, they cannot then claim the new mode is unavailable or unwanted. They were just there. They just chose it. The proof and the preference are both present. The only remaining work is to repeat.

## What Joe Does With the Response

The response is almost always "this" or "the connected one" or "this version." The work is in what comes next.

- **If the answer is "this":** mirror back what they just demonstrated and connect it to who they want to be with at work or in relationships. "Apparently your clients also." Or: "Your partner would also prefer this version of you." The implication propagates the preference into the practical context.

- **If the answer is hesitant:** the hesitation is the data. The client may have a counter-preference for the pushing version because it produces a result they want (productivity, control, the appearance of competence). Ask about the hesitation. "What's the reason for the hesitation?" The answer reveals what the pushing version is doing for them that the connecting version would have to do another way.

- **If the answer is "I don't know":** the client may not have actually been in the new mode long enough to feel the difference. Go back to the mode-shifting work. Get them into the connected mode for longer. Then ask again.

- **If the client tries to convert the answer into a new project ("so I should be this version more"):** redirect to the felt preference. "Not should — feel which one you'd rather be." The should-version of the answer recruits the same proving-engine that was the problem. The felt-preference version bypasses it.

## Dialogue Examples

The canonical sequence from a coaching session with a fitness coach who had spent years pushing himself in his business:

- **Joe:** "Just right now, let's not do it. There you go. Who do you want to be with more? This or the person who's pushing?"
- **Client:** "Oh, this."
- **Joe:** "Oh yeah. I apparently your clients also."
- *(Source: 2025-10-02_Why Can't I Get My Business Off The Ground)*

The follow-up bridge from "your clients also" was the entire point of the question. The client's instant preference for the connected version of himself was applied immediately to the customer relationship. The customers had been getting the pushing version. The customers had been leaving. The same preference the client just felt is what the customers had been expressing by walking away.

## Tone

Quiet, almost casual. The question is asked at low volume. There is no pull toward a particular answer. The casual tone matters because the question is asking the client to consult their own felt experience, not to produce the right answer for the coach. A heavy or insistent tone would tilt the client toward performance.

The follow-up ("apparently your clients also") is delivered with the same lightness, slightly amused. The amusement carries the recognition that the client has just demonstrated to themselves what their clients have been demonstrating to them by leaving. The amusement is gentle, not mocking — the client has been doing their best, and the recognition is offered as relief, not as exposure.
