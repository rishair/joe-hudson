# How would you talk to them if you knew they were good enough?

## The Question

The question in Joe's words:

- "How would you talk to him if you knew he was good enough?"

The variant in the source session was structured as a follow-up after the coachee defended her encouragement of her husband. Joe asked first whether she would deliver the same encouragement to a peer ("Would you say that to me? No. Right."), and then offered the reset question:

"How would you talk to him if you knew he was good enough?" -- Joe Hudson, Watch her Lifelong Anxiety Shift in Real Time

The question can be deployed with any pronoun and any relationship: how would you talk to her, your child, your direct report, your mother, if you knew they were already what they need to be?

## When Joe Uses It

The question fires when:

- The coachee has been describing a partner (or any close relationship) using encouragement-language that assumes the partner's deficit. See [[encouraging-them-agrees-with-their-self-attack]] and [[project-language-about-the-partner]].
- The coachee has been recognized as making the partner a project. See [[making-your-partner-a-project]].
- The coachee has named that they have been "trying to help" the partner and has just received the read that the helping is structurally control.
- The coachee asks (often defensively) "well what would I say then?" in response to the read that their encouragement perpetuates the partner's self-criticism.

The question opens a different conversation. It does not tell the coachee what to say; it asks them to find what they would say from a different stance. The stance shift is the work.

## What It Produces

The question is structured to short-circuit the cheerleader/critic dyad. Most of the language the coachee has been using assumes the partner needs to be brought up to where they should be. The question removes that assumption and asks the coachee to find language from a different starting point.

What tends to happen when the question lands:

- The coachee struggles for a moment. The encouragement-form had been so automatic that the absence of it leaves a silence. The silence is data; it shows how much the speaker had been running the partner-as-project frame.
- The coachee often defaults first to defensive language ("you do what you do, I don't care") -- the armored-pull-back variant of encouragement-withdrawal. Joe's follow-up move in the source session was to notice this: "There's a little bit of armor that just came up there." See [[name-the-body-defense-in-real-time]] for the response.
- The coachee then often finds something simple and warm: "I just feel like I love you no matter what." This is the wholeness frame in plain language. The work is to recognize this as the answer rather than the encouragement-form.
- The coachee may also discover that they cannot find the language because they have not been in the wholeness-frame with their partner for a long time. This is also data; the work is then to spend time in the wholeness-frame so the language can become available.

The question is not asking for the perfect sentence. The question is asking for the stance. Once the stance is found, the language flows from it.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the response is the warm-wholeness form ("I just love you no matter what"):** affirm it directly. The session source's exchange continues: Joe says "Which is what he gave to you. Which allowed you to do this." The point is named: the wholeness-frame the coachee just landed in is the same frame her husband had given her, which had been the structure of her own freedom. The recognition is doubled: the answer to the question is the same gift the partner had been giving the coachee all along.

- **If the response is defensive ("you do what you do, your life"):** name the armor. See [[name-the-body-defense-in-real-time]]. The defense is data; it shows the wholeness-frame is uncomfortable to take. Ask again, with a softening: "If it was open-hearted?"

- **If the response is silence:** stay with it. The silence is the partner-as-project frame collapsing without a replacement yet ready. Give time. The next thing said often emerges from a different layer than the previous statements were coming from.

- **If the response includes a new piece of vulnerability about what the coachee wants:** see [[own-your-want-instead-of-encouraging-them]]. The new statement often arrives in the four-move form -- affirm them as whole, name the want, name what the want is about, hand them back agency.

## Dialogue Examples

From the source session, the full exchange around the question:

- **Coachee** (after Joe has named the partner-as-project pattern): "I think I say, like, follow your dreams, babe. Like, you can -- you're going to be a New York Times bestseller before me... I'm like, just put it out there. Like, everyone's going to love it. Don't be afraid. Just do it."
- **Joe:** "Either way, I'm just perpetuating his negative self-talk."
- **Coachee:** "Even in the encouragement."
- **Joe:** "Right? Why? Who would you say that? Would you say that to me? No. Right. So the fact that you're taking care of him and you're agreeing that he's somewhat of a victim in this situation and that he needs some caretaking is ensuring his position as a victim. That fucking sucks. That sucks. How would you talk to him if you knew he was good enough?"
- **Coachee:** "It doesn't matter. Yeah, that's right. You do it. You do what you do."
- **Joe:** "It's your life kind of a thing. Yeah. There's a little bit of armor that just came up there."
- **Coachee:** "Okay. Like, you do what you do. Rare."
- **Joe:** "So if it was open-hearted?"
- **Coachee:** "I just feel like I love you no matter what. Yeah."
- **Joe:** "Which is what he gave to you. Which allowed you to do this."

The arc shows the question doing its work. The first response is armored (defensive withdrawal). Joe names the armor. The reframed question ("so if it was open-hearted?") produces the actual answer. The actual answer is the gift the coachee had been receiving from her husband for years. The recognition closes the loop.

## Tone

Direct, slightly slow, with space after. The question is short and lands hard. The space after is necessary because the answer often takes a moment to find. Do not fill the space with elaboration. Let the coachee work for the answer.

The question can be delivered with a slight smile -- the softening that signals "I am asking a real question, not testing you." The softening is the difference between the question landing as an invitation and landing as a corrective.
