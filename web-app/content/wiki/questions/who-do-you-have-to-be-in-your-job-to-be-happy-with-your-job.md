# Who do you have to be in your job to be happy with your job?

## The Question

The question is offered as a reframe when someone presents a stay-or-leave decision about their job. The original question (should I quit?) is rejected as the wrong question. The reframed question is about who they would need to be in the job for it to work.

"They said, 'I'm thinking about quitting and I'm wondering, what do you think? Should I keep my job or not keep my job?' And I said, 'Oh, I don't think that's the question. You know, I think the question is, who do you have to be in your job to be happy with your job?'" -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The reframe contains an implicit teaching: the stay-or-leave question keeps the decision oriented toward the job. The who-do-you-have-to-be question reorients the decision toward the person. Once the person is being who they have to be to be happy in the job, the stay-or-leave question often answers itself (the job either accommodates the person and the staying makes sense, or it doesn't and the leaving makes sense, but the person has shifted before the leaving rather than waiting until after).

Variations:
- "Who do you have to be in this job for it to work?"
- "What would you have to stop doing in the job to be happy there?"
- "What would you have to start doing in the job to be happy there?"
- "What's the version of you that this job could be home for?"

## When Joe Uses It

When someone is in a stay-or-leave deliberation about a job and is asking the coach to weigh in on the decision. The deliberation is typically a sign that the person has not yet asked the inner-stance version of the question. They are weighing external factors (salary, security, opportunity, hours) when the deciding variable is the inner-stance variable.

The question is also useful when:
- The person has been in the job a while and the discontent has crept up gradually
- The person is asking the question often (signal that the stay-or-leave deliberation is not actually about staying or leaving, it's about a misalignment that the person hasn't surfaced)
- The person has had similar discontent across multiple jobs (signal that the work to be done is about who they are in jobs, not which job to be in)

The question generalizes to other stay-or-leave deliberations. The job-version is the canonical because work-context has the highest cost-to-test ratio (changing jobs is materially expensive). The same move applies to relationships, communities, geographies — see [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]] for the broader pattern.

## What It Produces

The question often produces a brief silence because the person hadn't been thinking about the job that way. They had been thinking about the job as a thing-to-evaluate and themselves as the evaluator. The question reverses the relationship: the person becomes the thing-to-evaluate and the job becomes the context that will respond.

Once the person engages the question, they typically produce specific behaviors they have been suppressing — things they haven't said, things they haven't asked for, dissents they have been swallowing. The list is the operational answer to "who do you have to be."

The follow-up move is then to do those things. See [[just-be-yourself-and-see-what-happens]] for the experiment. The doing produces the data about whether the job can accommodate the person.

## What Joe Does With the Response

If the person identifies specific behaviors they have been suppressing: Joe encourages the experiment. Do the behaviors. See what happens. The willingness to act on the answer is the work; the answer alone is just intellectual.

If the person says "I don't know what I'd be doing differently": Joe sometimes asks them to feel into the discontent more specifically. Where in the job does the discontent show up? What would alignment look like in that specific situation? The specificity is what makes the question operational.

If the person treats the question as inviting them to design a perfect-job-version of themselves: Joe redirects to alignment-with-actual-self. The question is not "who could you become for this job to want you?" It is "who would you have to be (as yourself) for this job to be a place you want to be?"

If the experiment runs and the job rejects the person (firing, demotion, exclusion): Joe treats this as good information, not as a failure. See [[the-woman-fired-for-being-herself]] for the worked example. The next job will accommodate who they are.

## The Built-In Promise: The Next Job Will Accommodate You

The reframe carries an implicit promise that makes the experiment available:

"If you answered that question and you act that way, you might get fired, you might leave, who knows? It's okay either way. But what I do know is the next job that you find will be one that accommodates who you are. It'll be one that accepts who you are, and you will walk into that job being who you are rather than being what you think people want from you." -- Joe Hudson, "I Don't Belong Here"

The promise is structural: once you have been who you are in this job, you bring that same self to the next opportunity, and the next opportunity self-selects for someone who shows up as themselves. This is the [[world-reorganizes-around-self-possession]] principle in the work-context application.

The promise also addresses the most common objection to the experiment: "but I might get fired." The promise reframes: yes, you might. The firing is not the catastrophe. The catastrophe was the continued self-suppression in a job that couldn't hold the real you.

## Tone

Direct, slightly redirecting. The "I don't think that's the question" opening is a friendly but firm correction. The question that follows is offered as the better question, not as a softening. Joe's delivery suggests certainty that this is the more useful question to be asking.

The redirect-without-reassurance is part of the move. The person is asked to step into a new question rather than to be soothed in the old one.
