# What's required from you to love them as they are?

## The Question

The full form Joe uses:

"What do you have to do to love them as they are, even when you're walking away, even if you choose to leave them? What's required from you to love them as they are? To not look for them to change." -- Joe Hudson, You Only Get the Love You Can Let In

The question has two phases. The first phase asks what would be required. The second phase, "to not look for them to change," names the operational form of "love them as they are" so the person cannot quietly redefine it as "love them while still hoping they change."

The question presupposes: the person can do this. The asking does not check whether the person is capable. It assumes they are, and asks what would be required of them to operationalize the capacity.

## When Joe Uses It

After the person has presented the [[even-the-resistant-partner-is-your-perfect-match|non-cooperating partner]] objection: "my partner won't do the work / wants to blame me / won't grow." Joe has just reframed the objection as "wow, that's your perfect match, isn't it?" The person now faces the choice: stay or leave. The question is asked at that pivot.

The question is for both possible futures. Whether the person stays or leaves, the work is to love the partner as they are. The question collapses the difference between the two paths into a single common requirement.

The question is also asked when:

- A person has been trying to change their partner for years and is exhausted
- A person is in the [[inner-work-then-they-change]] middle phase where they are doing their own work and watching the partner remain unchanged
- A person is grieving the partner they wished they had married
- A person is in a [[relationships-as-personal-growth-path]] frame and has hit the part where the partner won't share the frame
- A parent is struggling to love a child whose behavior they want to change

## What It Produces

The question redirects energy. Until the question is asked, the person is spending energy trying to change the partner, either overtly or covertly. The question stops the change-project and reroutes the energy to the self-work that would be required to love the partner as they currently are. The person typically:

- Pauses. The question disrupts the change-project they have been running for years
- Begins listing what they would have to give up: the fantasy of who the partner would be, the hope that the partner will change, the secret bargain that they will love the partner more once the partner is different
- Touches grief. The grief is for the partner-as-they-actually-are versus the partner-they-have-been-trying-to-make
- Identifies the part of themselves that has been organized around the change-project: the part that has been waiting, the part that has been resenting, the part that has been performing love conditionally
- Often arrives at a self-work item: "I would have to love the part of me that wanted them to be different" or "I would have to stop needing them to validate me" or "I would have to actually let myself feel my own loneliness rather than blaming them for it"

The answer is rarely "I would have to do X to them." It is almost always "I would have to do Y inside myself." The question reroutes the work from the partner to the self by the structure of the question itself.

## What Joe Does With the Response

**If the person identifies an internal item:** Joe works that. The item is the entry point to the actual work the relationship was for. The person has just located the freedom-piece the relationship was meant to surface. The relationship-as-freedom frame becomes operational.

**If the person says "I can't" or "I don't want to":** Joe stays with the not-wanting. The not-wanting is data. Often the not-wanting is about a specific cost the person is not willing to pay -- giving up the hope, giving up the resentment, giving up the identity of the one who was wronged. Joe names the cost and asks if the person would rather stay in the cost or pay it.

**If the person tries to redirect to the partner:** "but how can I love them when they..." Joe redirects back. The question is not about what the partner does. It is about what is required from the person. The redirection is the work.

**If the person identifies that they would need the partner to stop the harmful behavior first:** Joe distinguishes loving-them-as-they-are from staying-in-the-situation. The principle is that the love is yours and is not contingent on the partner's behavior. The decision about staying in the situation is separate. Both can be held at the same time. See [[even-the-resistant-partner-is-your-perfect-match]] on the staying-or-leaving choice from the worked place.

**If the person reaches the place of actually loving them as they are:** Joe usually stays quiet. The arrival is its own event. The person can feel what just happened. From there, the staying-or-leaving decision becomes clean. The love is not what is making the decision -- the love is now available regardless.

## The "Even If You're Leaving" Clause

The clause is structurally critical. Without it, the person can interpret "love them as they are" as "agree to stay in the relationship." The clause makes clear that the love and the staying are separate. The love is required even if the person is leaving. The leaving from a non-loving place is a different leaving than the leaving from a loving-them-as-they-are place. The latter is clean. The former is the next configuration's setup.

"Even when you're walking away, even if you choose to leave them" -- Joe Hudson, You Only Get the Love You Can Let In

Most people walking away from a long relationship cannot love the person they are walking away from. The unmet-ness, the resentment, the change-project that failed are all in the way. The question, asked at the door, is the work that makes the leaving clean. The work is the same whether you walk or stay.

## What This Distinguishes From

This is not [[what-do-you-want]]. The wants question is upstream and broader. This question is specific to the configuration of a relationship the person wants to either accept-as-is or leave-cleanly.

This is not "do you love them." The asking-if is not the question. The asking-what-would-be-required is the question. The asking-if collapses into yes-or-no; the asking-what opens the actual work.

This is not "should you stay." The staying question is downstream of this one. This question is upstream. Do the work to love them as they are; then the staying or leaving choice becomes available from a clean place.

## Tone

Joe asks this with steadiness. There is no pressure in the asking. The question is offered as if the person has a genuine choice and the asker is curious about what they will discover. The implication is not "you should be able to do this." The implication is "this is what is available; what would it cost you."

The tone is also not gentle in the soft-around-the-edges way. It is direct. The clause "to not look for them to change" is delivered without softening, because the softening would let the person hold onto the change-project.
