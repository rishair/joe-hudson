# Training Coaches and Facilitators as a Domain

## The Application

Training coaches and facilitators is a distinct domain of the work. It overlaps with the broader teaching but differs in two structural ways. First, the foundation being built is the trainee's own self-awareness, not a skillset. Second, the trainee will be holding space for others, which means their own unresolved patterns will show up in that holding and have to be metabolized as part of the training itself.

The frame is not "here is how to coach." The frame is: "here is the long apprenticeship in being a person who can coach. The coaching is a downstream expression of that being-ness."

"What you're going to learn here is self-discovery and that self-discovery, that understanding of yourself, that's what's going to be the foundation of any kind of coaching practice that you have. And eventually, that expression of coaching is going to be yours. It's not going to be mine." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## What Disqualifies the Standard Sense of Qualification

The credentialed model of qualification does not predict good coaching. Multiple degrees do not predict a good therapist. Certifications do not predict a good coach. The same standard produces excellent practitioners and harmful ones in equal measure. The only qualification Joe accepts is demand: people watch what someone does and ask to learn it. Demand from people who have experienced the work is the signal that the work is real.

"On the qualified part, I don't really believe generally in qualifications. I've had people who have been extremely qualified with multiple degrees as my coaches or therapists in my life. And some of them are just amazing and some of them are horrible. And the qualification, as far as like standards go, is the same." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

This is structurally aligned with [[the-clients-wisdom-not-the-teachers]]: if the wisdom does not transmit through credentials, then credentials are not the right diagnostic. What transmits is presence. Presence cannot be certified.

## The Gate: Self-Awareness Over Coaching

The first gate, before any training, is which the person is here for: their own development or to become a coach. If the answer is "to become a coach," it is not a fit. The training requires the person to be primarily oriented toward their own self-discovery, with the coaching as a downstream expression of that orientation, not the goal itself.

"The first thing is, are you here for your self-development or are you here to coach? And if it's you're here to coach, it's not a fit." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

See [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]] for the full version of this principle.

## The Dangerous Motivations for Wanting to Coach

The reason for the strictness of the gate: coaching is one of the "rockstar careers" -- a role people are drawn to for reasons that will eventually hurt them and the people they coach. Joe names three specific dangerous motivations, each of which produces predictable harm if not metabolized before the person starts coaching.

1. **Wanting to save people.** Often a remnant of a childhood role (the kid who managed the alcoholic parent's emotions, the kid who held the family together). The pattern transposes onto coaching: I will make a career of saving people. The problem: coaching from this place positions the client as a victim who cannot save themselves, and depletes the coach. See [[coaching-as-savior-role]].

2. **Coaching as surrogate intimacy.** Coaching produces deep intimacy without the risk of an actual intimate relationship. The coach gets the connection without the vulnerability of a partnership. The problem: the coach is using the client to meet intimacy needs that should be met elsewhere, and the intimacy is structurally one-sided. See [[coaching-as-surrogate-intimacy]].

3. **Wanting to be seen as the one who knows.** The wisdom-holder role. The problem: positioning yourself as the knower trains your clients to look to you instead of to themselves, which is the opposite of what the work is for. See [[coaching-to-be-seen-as-wise]].

These three motivations are not unique to bad people. They are common, often subconscious, and can coexist with genuine interest in the work. The training process surfaces them so they can be worked rather than acted out.

## The Four Foundational Ingredients

Joe names four ingredients required to coach in this lineage. Skills come after these are in place.

1. **Commitment to self-awareness over coaching.** The primary commitment is to the person's own understanding of themselves and their own movement toward unconditional love. Coaching is downstream of this. See [[coaches-foundation-self-awareness]].

2. **Presence over skills.** "How do I listen? How am I with you? How open is my heart?" The VIEW mindset is the foundation. The skills are a thin layer on top of presence. Without presence, the skills become dangerous. See [[presence-over-technique-in-coaching]] and [[view-as-technique]].

3. **Listening to the internal call moment-to-moment.** The capacity to hear "the thing that wants to be said even if it's scary," and to follow it. Many of the best coaching moves arrive as "holy shit, am I about to say that?" The capacity is more than impulse-acting; it is the trained ability to register the call, feel the risk, and proceed anyway. "It's a very rare coaching session where there's some point where I'm just like, holy shit, am I about to say that? And then I say it. And usually those are some of the best things to say." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice. See [[pucker-and-still-say-it]] and [[listening-to-impulse]].

4. **Either understanding there is nothing special about you, or owning your narcissism.** Two paths to the same posture: being able to occupy the authority and influence without putting yourself above the people you work with. See [[own-your-narcissism]].

"There's some technique, you know, there's a little bit of technique, but without all that other stuff, the technique can just be dangerous." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

## The First Year Is Presence

The first program in the coaching path is entirely about presence. Not skills, not techniques, not exercises -- just the question of how the trainee shows up. The frame: how the coach is determines more about how the client shows up than anything else the coach does.

"The first program is all about just being like the presence. The first year is like all about presence. It's a dedication to just how do you show up? How do you listen?" -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The sushi metaphor: the famous story of the apprentice who spends ten years just making the rice perfect. Presence is the rice. The fancy cuts come much later. The premium on presence-first is not arbitrary discipline; it is the recognition that everything else is a refinement of this base, and the base is what produces the result.

## The Coach's Style Is the Coach's Own

The training does not produce coaches who coach like Joe. It produces coaches whose own coaching style emerges from their own self-awareness work. Each coach's expression is their own. The lineage is the orientation (presence, self-awareness, the VIEW mindset, the client's wisdom), not the surface style.

"I'm the only person who's going to coach the way I do. You're the only person who's going to coach the way you do." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

See [[coach-personality-determines-style]] for the full version. This is also why the path is not a ladder: "one coach has almost everything figured out except they're still trying to save people. Another coach has almost everything figured out except they keep not saying the risky thing." The work is shaped by personality, not by a fixed sequence of competencies.

## The Coaching Commitment

When Joe commits to coaching someone (executive or coach-trainee), the commitment does not end with money or job or status changes. As long as the person continues to do the work, Joe stays.

"If I coach you, I don't stop coaching you as long as you do the work. You run out of money, I still coach you. You get fired, I still coach you. As long as you're committed, as long as you continue to be committed to discovering yourself and doing the inner work, I'm with you." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The reciprocal: the coach is not here to please the trainee. The coach is here to mirror, to point out what is missing, to follow the read even when it is uncomfortable. See [[coach-as-mirror-not-customer-service]].

## The Dumpster Fire as Asset

When something goes wrong in a facilitation -- a group falls apart, an exercise lands badly, a dynamic explodes -- the standard response is to manage it back to working order. Joe's response is closer to: this is great. The blowup is exactly the conditions in which trainees learn the most.

The principle the trainees inherit: "this dumpster fire is not going to stoke itself." They are not here to make it work. They are here to be present with what is happening. The result-orientation has to release for the work-orientation to take over. See [[welcome-the-dumpster-fire-in-facilitation]].

## The Facilitation Layer Versus the Coaching Layer

Coaching one person is one challenge. Facilitating a room is exponentially harder. The trainee has to do everything the coach does -- presence, listening, internal call -- while also holding the agenda, being the authority, running the exercise, and being aware of the group dynamic.

"You need to do all that other stuff and be able to be there present, owning all that stuff. And you need to be able to say, okay, this is what we're doing. Okay, I'm the authority in the room. Okay, here's the exercise." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

The facilitation training comes later in the path. After presence is solid, after the trainee can hold their own without losing themselves, after the work of seeing they are not special is metabolized. Then the multi-person dynamic layer gets added.

## The Vision: A Community of Practice, Not a Lineage of Disciples

The vision is not a school that produces certified Joe-style coaches. It is a community of practice in which each coach develops their own expression of the work, holds the platform together, and supports each other in their own self-discovery. Different coaches bring different things -- one brings action sports and fear work, one brings physical health, one brings martial arts. The platform is enriched by the variety; the variety is enabled by each coach holding the same orientation (self-awareness, presence) while expressing it through their own life.

"My hope is more of a contained open source environment." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

This sits on top of [[community-over-teacher]] and [[become-unnecessary]]. The platform is structured so that the work continues without Joe. The teacher's tools are seeds, not canon. The community holds the practice.

The hard part of this vision: saying no to people who want it. Limited spaces mean some people get told no. Saying no breaks Joe's heart in a way he did not expect. The structural integrity of the platform requires it -- a coach who is not deeply committed to their own self-awareness contaminates the platform -- but the cost is real.

## Who Holds the Mirror for the Teacher

A live question for any teacher: who points out the teacher's blind spots? If the structure is teacher-at-the-top, no one holds that role, and the teacher's dysfunction propagates through the work.

Joe's answer is structural: many people. A coach. Tara. The teenage daughters who will readily call out anything they see. The office team who tells him what he's missing. Long-term coaches in the community who, after years of work together, are positioned to say "this feels wonky, what's going on?" The intention is to set up the work so that the teacher cannot become the unaccountable center.

"I'm lucky enough on the path at the point where like getting triggered, I can't go -- it doesn't go unnoticed. It's not like, oh, I got triggered, I'm just going to ignore that. It's like, no, I got triggered. That's work. Maybe not today, maybe in a month, but it's work and it's going to be addressed." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice

This is the personal expression of the structural principles: [[community-over-teacher]] at the personal level, [[downward-spiral-between-teacher-and-students]] avoided through ongoing real feedback, [[spiritual-narcissism]] interrupted by the people who will call it out.

## When Someone Says "I Want to Be a Coach"

If a coaching trainee or aspirant is presenting with the question "how do I become a coach?" or "I want to learn what you do," the coaching path:

1. **Surface the impulse.** What is driving the want? Is it the wanting to save, the wanting of intimacy without risk, the wanting to be seen as the knower? Or is it a genuine call that the person cannot not follow? The first three are the dangerous motivations and need to be worked before training, not after. The fourth is the actual signal.

2. **Redirect to self-discovery.** If the person has not done the foundational work -- the connection course, the longer programs, sustained inner work -- the coaching question is premature. The answer is not "let me teach you to coach," it is "do the work, and let's see what emerges."

3. **Reflect the pattern.** The aspirant who wants to start facilitating groundbreakers two years in, before any coaching or facilitation training, is often repeating a known pattern from earlier in their life. The coaching is not to refuse them; it is to ask what the hurry is, what they are after, and how the pattern from before is showing up here. See the example move below.

The example move (from the transcript): a person two years in wanted to start facilitating their own groundbreakers. Joe's response: "What's the hurry? What are you after?" and then naming a pattern from earlier: "in your company you got this thing where a whole bunch of people were dependent on you and then you were resentful. How, if at all, is that pattern repeating here?" The coaching does not refuse the want; it surfaces what the want is built on top of.

## "I Want to Do That" Is Itself Coachable

When someone watches rapid-fire coaching and feels "I want to be able to do that," the impulse is data. The honest move is to trace it back. What is the underneath want? Sometimes the underneath is "I want that level of self-awareness, I want the emotional fluidity, I want to feel what's going on in a room." That underneath is the actual call, and it points toward the self-discovery path, not toward technique acquisition.

The move is not to suppress the wanting. Joe is explicit: "I'm not suggesting by any chance any way to like sublimate a want, but just like be in it for the self-awareness and be self-aware in that want." The wanting remains; the relationship to the wanting changes. See [[rapid-fire-coaching-impulse-check]] for the full version of this move.

## The Honest Motive: Teach From Pleasure

A specific principle that lives inside the coaches-foundation-self-awareness gate: the most honest teaching comes from a teacher whose answer to "why are you doing this?" is some version of "I love it." Not "the world needs this." Not "to save lives." The pleasure-motivated teacher is structurally cleaner than the mission-motivated teacher because the pleasure motive is not extracting anything from the students' need.

Brett Kistler, having matured past his initial savior framing for his Welcoming Fear retreat, articulates the shift:

"A few years ago, I would have said a lot of what I'm doing here is about saving lives. And interestingly, I feel like that's kind of fallen away. It's like, I don't believe that I -- that anyone needs me to save them. But what I do enjoy is being with people in this exploration." -- Brett Kistler, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

Joe affirms the principle:

"I love the idea that you're teaching for your own pleasure. I think that's so critical. Not to be there to save the world, not to be there because the world needs you, not to be there to save a single person. It feels to me like the most honest teachings are the ones where the teacher's like, I'm here because I love it." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

This principle screens for one of the three dangerous motivations: the savior pattern. The trainee who has not yet metabolized the savior motivation will teach from it whether or not they want to; the metabolization is what allows pleasure to be the actual motive. See [[teach-from-pleasure-not-mission]] for the full treatment.

## The First Iteration

A specific principle for trainees about to run their first retreat, workshop, or course: the first iteration has a quality the later iterations cannot reproduce. In the first iteration, the teacher is wrestling with the material themselves, in real time, as they teach it. The teaching is alive in the teacher's nervous system because the teacher does not yet have the comfort of repetition.

"What I've noticed is when I do a retreat for the first time, there's a way in which it's like, the people are guinea pigs and they're getting the first version of it, which is not going to be in some ways as good as the future versions of it, but in some ways it's going to be better than any version, you know, because there's something that can happen on the first one that can't happen anywhere else." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

This principle counters the "wait until you're ready" instinct that keeps trainees from running their first thing. The not-yet-ready activation is part of what makes the first iteration valuable. The trainee's own work-in-real-time is the transmission. Subsequent iterations smooth and refine but lose the live aliveness. See [[first-iteration-is-irreplaceable]] for the full treatment.

## The Pre-Game Frame: Feeling Yourself Beats Going to Plan

A specific instruction Joe gives trainees the night before they run something for the first time:

"If everything goes to shit and you're feeling yourself, it's way better than if everything goes according to plan and you're not." -- Joe Hudson, A Workshop for Welcoming Fear

Brett, who received this advice the night before his first retreat, named it as the single most useful thing said to him going in. The frame released the result-orientation that would have otherwise governed his attention. With the result-orientation released, his attention was free to be with what was actually happening in the room.

The frame works best as a pre-game move -- said the day before, the morning of, or the moment before stepping into the difficulty. Said in this context, it releases the gripping that would have otherwise been carried into the session. See [[feeling-yourself-beats-going-to-plan]] for the full treatment and its companion relationship with [[welcome-the-dumpster-fire-in-facilitation]].

## Boundary Conditions

This application is for people training in this specific lineage of coaching, which is built on the orientation of self-awareness as the foundation. Other lineages -- skill-based coaching, accountability coaching, technique-oriented coaching -- have different structures and produce different outcomes. The principles here do not claim those other lineages are wrong; they claim that this lineage is built on this foundation, and people seeking technique training should seek it elsewhere.

"There's other schools of coaching that are nothing like that. There's other schools of coaching which is like, here's the skill set, go out and do it. And it's all about creating accountability and it's all about having just a very clear set of techniques. That is not at all what we're doing. And nobody should come and learn coaching from us if that's what they want." -- Joe Hudson, Coaching as a Practice
