# The Corporate Stack of Mutual Misreading

## The Application

The doing-to-be-valuable installation, when it operates in a corporate hierarchy with multiple management layers, produces a specific compounding dysfunction: each layer is trying to be valuable to the layer above by performing what they imagine the layer above wants — but they do not actually know what the layer above wants, because the layer above is also too busy trying to be valuable to *its* layer above to clearly communicate. By the time the cascade reaches the CEO, the work everyone is doing has very little to do with what the CEO actually wants.

This is why Joe stopped going into companies several layers down. The work cannot be done at those layers because the dysfunction is structurally produced by what is happening above them.

"This is why if you go into a corporation — I used to do this, I used to go in to work on culture, but I would go in maybe like 3 levels down in an organization. I refuse to do that now. I will only work with the CEOs of organizations. I won't go into a company because what I noticed was this level was worried about what the next level would think and was trying to manage it, but they didn't really actually know what the next level thought because they needed to be valuable. And then this level did the same thing, and then this level did the same thing. So by the time I got to the CEO, what that CEO wanted compared to what everybody thought their bosses wanted or what the CEO wanted were completely different things. They were just trying to be valuable." -- Joe Hudson, You're Not Giving People the Chance to Love You

The application is the corporate-context expression of the broader principle [[value-is-intrinsic-not-earned]]. The same doing-to-be-valuable engine that produces individual stress, when stacked across organizational hierarchy, produces collective misalignment. The dynamics are the same; the consequences scale with the number of layers.

## The Mechanism

At every layer, the manager is asking two questions:

1. **What does my boss want?** (Imagined, not known.)
2. **What can I do to look valuable to them?** (Optimized for visibility, not actual contribution.)

Both questions have wrong shapes. The first assumes the boss has a clear, stable, articulable preference set — which is rarely true (see [[job-interview-3d-chess]] for the same point in the interview context). The second assumes that looking valuable is the same as being valuable — which is false (see [[doing-things-for-love-is-prostitution]] for the structural account of why performing-for-value does not produce value).

When every layer is operating on these questions, the cascading effect is:

- **Layer N+1** is doing what it imagines layer N+2 wants
- **Layer N** is doing what it imagines layer N+1 wants (which is what N+1 imagines N+2 wants, filtered through N+1's misreading)
- **Layer N-1** is doing what it imagines layer N wants (which is N's reading of N+1's reading of N+2's wants)

By the time the cascade reaches the bottom or top, the actual work being done has very little to do with what anyone actually wants. The output is the layered product of multiple consecutive misreadings, each compounded with performance-optimization rather than truth-pursuit.

## The Meeting Symptom

A specific visible signature of this dynamic: questions in meetings that are asked not because the asker cares about the answer, but because the asker wants to be seen as someone who asks good questions.

"I've sat around team meetings where people are asking questions not because they care, not because it's going to move the business forward, is because they want to be seen as valuable by asking good questions. None of this gets the job done." -- Joe Hudson, You're Not Giving People the Chance to Love You

The questions are technically appropriate. They are sometimes even insightful. But the motive is wrong. The asker is performing question-asking for the value-signaling. The answer to the question is incidental.

The meeting is the venue where the cascade becomes visible in real time. The same dynamic that produces misaligned work also produces meetings full of performances rather than collaborations. The body language of the room often reflects this: people are watching how their contributions land more than they are listening to what others contribute. The room is a stack of performers, each performing for the next layer up.

The diagnostic for whether a meeting is operating in this mode: how much of the time is being spent on actual problem-solving versus value-display? The ratio is the diagnostic. A meeting that is mostly value-display can run for hours and produce nothing because no one is actually trying to move the work; everyone is trying to be visible as a mover.

## Why Bottom-Up Culture Work Fails Here

Cultural intervention at lower layers cannot fix the dynamic because the lower layers are responding to a signal from above. Even if the lower-layer manager somehow drops the doing-to-be-valuable engine in themselves, they are still embedded in an environment that is responding to the engine. Their authentic contribution gets read by the layer above as either insufficient (because it does not include the expected performance) or as defiance (because the absence of performance is read as misalignment).

The lower-layer manager who attempts the inversion alone gets punished by the system. The intervention has not changed the system; it has only made the individual visible as out-of-line. They either revert or leave.

This is the structural reason Joe refuses to work at lower layers now. The work he is being asked to do (improve the culture) cannot be done from the layer he was being placed at. The intervention has to start at the top — where the signal originates — or it produces casualties without changing the dynamic.

## What Working With the CEO Looks Like

Working with the CEO directly addresses the source of the cascade. The CEO is the only layer where the imagined-boss problem does not operate (or operates only at the level of the board, which is a different and usually weaker signal). If the CEO can drop the doing-to-be-valuable engine — can stop performing the CEO role for whatever audience they imagine — the signal cascading down the organization shifts.

The shift at the top is felt at every layer below, because each layer was responding to its reading of the layer above. When the reading material changes (the CEO becomes more authentic, more direct, less performative), the layers below have new material to respond to. The cascade can become a different cascade.

The work is not "fix the CEO's performance issues." It is "help the CEO drop the doing-to-be-valuable engine that the CEO is running, often without knowing it." Many CEOs are running the engine intensely — performing the CEO role for the board, for the press, for the team, for themselves. The performance is exhausting and produces the same misalignment cascade their reports are producing. The work is the same work that would be done with anyone running the engine; the difference is that the CEO's release of the engine has organizational consequences that no one else's release does.

See [[fix-the-team-by-fixing-the-leader]] for the related principle that the team's dynamics are downstream of the leader's state. See [[consciousness-is-the-product]] for the broader claim that the leader's consciousness is the actual product of the organization.

## The Validation-Misalignment Result

The end-state of the uninterrupted cascade is a specific organizational condition: everyone in the company is convinced that they are valuable (because they have been performing for valuability), and the actual work the company needs is not getting done. The team meetings are full of intelligent contributions that do not move the business. The strategy documents are well-written and unimplemented. The OKRs are met by metrics that do not reflect the actual customer value being produced.

This is also the condition that no amount of internal complaint can fix, because the complaint is also being performed for valuability. The person complaining "we are too focused on the wrong things" is also performing — for the value-signal of being the one who sees the problem. The complaint joins the cascade rather than interrupting it.

The exit from the condition requires someone willing to drop the performance entirely. In Joe's framework, that someone has to be the CEO (or at least a senior leader with enough authority that the drop is felt by the layers above as something other than misalignment). The drop is the discontinuity that allows the cascade to break.

## Joe's Words on Personal Misery Within Visible Value

Even when the layered cascade has installed everyone as "valuable" from the outside, the people inside it are not necessarily okay. The doing-to-be-valuable engine does not deliver felt well-being even when it delivers external recognition.

"None of it gets the job done inside of us. Everybody can think you are valuable and you can still be miserable." -- Joe Hudson, You're Not Giving People the Chance to Love You

The structural fact: the engine was supposed to produce the feeling of being valuable, which was supposed to produce well-being. The external recognition cannot install the felt-sense of value, because the felt-sense of value comes from inside, not from the outside-validation channel. The person who has gotten every external mark of value can still be deeply unwell because the engine was always pointing in the wrong direction. See [[chase-your-own-approval]] for the inversion that addresses this.

## What This Application Implies for Practitioners

If you are a manager (not the CEO):

- Notice when your work is being shaped by what you imagine your boss wants rather than by your own read of what the business needs. The first question is producing the cascade; the second question is interrupting it.
- Notice when your meeting contributions are optimized for visibility rather than for truth. The visibility-optimization is the performance; the truth-pursuit is the work.
- Notice that you do not actually know what your boss wants. The honest assumption is "I don't know." From that honest place, you can ask. Asking the boss directly what they actually want is more efficient than guessing.
- See [[speak-truth-twice-a-day]] for the operational practice that interrupts the cascade in your own contributions.

If you are the CEO:

- Notice that you may be running the same engine — performing CEO for whatever audience you imagine. The performance is shaping the signal everyone else is reading and amplifying.
- The work is to drop the performance, which is the same work as anyone else dropping the engine. The leverage is higher because the cascade is operating around you.
- See [[value-is-intrinsic-not-earned]] for the foundational principle.

## Tone

Joe delivers this with the practical conviction of someone who has tried both approaches and chosen the more effective one. The "I refuse to do that now" line is delivered as a professional decision, not a moral one. The story functions as evidence that the lower-layer intervention is empirically less effective, not that it is wrong to try.

The diagnosis of the meeting dynamic is delivered with mild exasperation. "None of this gets the job done" is the sober reading of what is actually happening in many meetings most of the time. The exasperation is the appropriate response to seeing a recurring pattern that is structurally fixable but rarely fixed.
