# Reducing Overwhelm in Organizations (Driving Flow Instead)

## Definition

The standard model: when a company is overwhelmed, accept it as the price of getting things done, or manage it with more processes, more management, more pressure.

The actual model: overwhelm degrades organizational output. The work of leadership is not to manage overwhelm down to a tolerable level. The work is to design for flow, so that overwhelm is not the default operating state. Done well, this produces both higher output and a workforce that wants to be there.

The reframe: do not ask "how do I reduce overwhelm in my organization?" Ask "how do I drive flow?"

"For me, the bigger thing is, oh, he was driving flow in his company. And so I would think about it less as how do I drive, how do I reduce overwhelm, and I would think of it more as how do I drive flow? How do I have people calm, relaxed, focused, on game?" -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## The Leader Is the First Lever

Before any organizational change, the leader has to be clear on overwhelm in themselves. A leader who is personally in overwhelm will produce an overwhelmed organization, regardless of stated values. See [[dont-reflect-your-patterns-as-culture]] for the underlying dynamic.

"The first thing is they have to be clear on it in themselves. I think if somebody's become a CEO of a successful company, when they're clear on it themselves, the company will be clear on it." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The successful examples Joe cites are leaders who have figured out how to get themselves out of overwhelm and protect their own flow:

- **Bill Gates:** Took one week per quarter alone in a cabin, away from the operation, to return to center, get out of overwhelm, and think at a higher level.
- **Alan Greenspan:** First 30 minutes of every day in contemplation.
- **Jeff Bezos:** Wandered until 10am, made decisions only between 10 and 5, did nothing related to work after 5. His whole job was to make good decisions, and protecting the conditions for good decisions was the work.

Each of these is a personal practice that protects the leader's capacity. None of them is about pushing harder. All of them are about preserving the state from which good decisions can be made.

"There are multiple people who have been very successful who have figured out that this is true: that their overwhelm doesn't serve and doesn't serve their company and doesn't serve them." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

## The Standard Counter-Argument (And Why It Fails)

The standard objection: "Those are stable companies with money. We're a startup. Everything is urgent. We don't have the runway to think about flow."

Joe's response is direct: "I don't want to buy from that attitude and I sure as hell don't want to invest money in that attitude."

The reasoning underneath: an overwhelmed startup is a startup making worse decisions than it could be making. The runway is shortened by every bad decision the overwhelm produces. The story that "we have to be overwhelmed because we're a startup" is the kind of story that turns into the post-mortem of why the startup failed. Burn-out, error rates, attrition of good people -- all of these compound under overwhelm.

The startup case is, in some ways, the strongest case for flow. The fewer decisions you have, the more each one matters. The more each decision matters, the more it should be made from a state where decision-making works. That state is not overwhelm.

## Where the Argument Is Harder

Joe acknowledges the case is harder for certain kinds of work. A company with 20 people mowing lawns may genuinely make $500 more per day if the workers move faster. The flow argument is less obvious there than in decision-intensive work.

But even there, the data tends to go in the same direction once measured. The [[buurtzorg-nursing]] example is the case Joe uses: a nursing company in the Netherlands rejected the standard efficiency metrics (minutes per patient, tight scheduling) and built a model around having coffee with patients, helping them become independent, treating the work as relational. The company became 20-30% more efficient than competitors and attracted nurses who would not work elsewhere. Both metrics improved together. The slow, relational model out-competed the rushed, transactional one.

The lesson generalizes: even in operationally-intensive work, the assumption that rushing equals efficiency is often wrong on the actual numbers. The optimal pace tends to be slower than the "common sense" pace, and the data shows it once measured.

## The Diagnostic Phase

Before changing anything, measure how overwhelmed people actually are.

**Concrete metrics:**
- Attrition rate of good people (a stronger signal than overall attrition)
- Attrition rate of mediocre people (sometimes also informative)
- Survey question: "Are you looking forward to coming to work on Monday?" The answer is a strong overwhelm proxy.
- Open-ended question: what is overwhelming you?

**The structural cause to look for:** most overwhelm in companies (where it is not the person's innate pattern) comes from responsibility without authority. People are responsible for outcomes but cannot make the decisions that determine those outcomes. The mismatch produces sustained anxiety. The anxiety hardens into despondency.

"Most of the time people are overwhelmed in companies, if it's not just kind of their innate pattern, is because they feel like they have the responsibility, but they don't have the authority to actually make it work. And so it starts with overwhelm and ends with despondency." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

This connects to [[structure-and-empowerment]]: empowerment requires both responsibility and the authority to act on it. Splitting them produces the overwhelm Joe is describing.

## Interventions That Drive Flow

**Productivity experiments.** Run actual experiments on pace. The paddleboard company example: a CEO took the company to 30 hours per week and consistently out-performed competitors on both productivity and sales. He reframed the company as "an online marketing company that happens to sell paddleboards" and protected the conditions for the marketing work to be done well. The 30-hour cap was not soft. It was strategic.

**Find the optimal pace for each kind of work.** Different work has different optimal paces.
- A novelist: most great novelists work about 3 hours per day. Pushing to 10 produces burnout or worse writing.
- Programming: probably more than 3 hours per day is possible.
- Sales: there is a level beyond which the next call hurts the overall numbers.

The work of leadership is to find the pace for each kind of work, not to assume that more hours equals more output.

**Open a channel for the team's input.** Slack channel for "reducing overwhelm" or similar. Define what overwhelm means and what it does not mean. Ask the team for ideas and examples of how they have moved out of overwhelm in the past. The team's intelligence on this is usually larger than the leader's.

"Reduction of overwhelm is like a team sport. So it's not going to come down through a policy. It's going to come with a discussion." -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

**Be precise about definitions.** Overwhelm means spinning, being non-productive in the spin, being non-relaxed. Overwhelm does not mean slacking off, wandering, or thinking. The leader who blurs this distinction will inadvertently encourage low effort while trying to reduce overwhelm.

**Accept some attrition.** Some people in any organization are overwhelmed by nature and are not willing to do the work to change it. The functional move is to help those people find a place that fits them better, not to let the chronic overwhelm drag the rest of the team down.

## When the Team Is Too Frozen to Ideate

A team that has been in overwhelm long enough sometimes enters collective freeze. The prefrontal cortex is too suppressed to come up with solutions. The team is in collective helplessness.

Joe's first move: give them a break. Get them out of the office. The freeze state is not going to be solved by more meetings. It is going to be relieved by removing the conditions that produced it.

"Give them a break. Get them out of the office. If they can't think, what you're basically saying, they're so overwhelmed they can't think. So now maybe you don't need them to think if they're cutting lawns or picking apples, but you need them to think for most modern jobs. And so why even have them there unless they can think?" -- Joe Hudson, What Can I Do About Overwhelm?

The implication: in any work that requires thinking, the time spent in a state where thinking is impossible is net-negative time. Sending the team home is not a cost. It is the move that restores the resource the work depends on.

## The Email Diagnostic

Joe describes how he can tell which executive assistants are overwhelmed just by their emails. The overwhelmed EA writes 20 emails, each a small novel, trying to catch every detail. The non-overwhelmed EA writes 3 emails, fewer than 5 sentences each. The work gets done either way. The state of the worker is visible in the form of the work.

The diagnostic generalizes. Overwhelm shows up in the form of the work being done, not just in self-reports. Watch the form. The form will tell you.

## See What's Working, Not Just What's Wrong

The standard organizational diagnostic looks for what is broken. A complementary move: look for where the team is in flow, where work is being done well, where energy is high, and try to scale those conditions to the rest of the organization. See [[see-whats-right-to-build-on-it]]. The data on what is working is often more useful than the data on what is failing.

## Tone

Practical, slightly impatient with the cultural assumption that overwhelm is just how business works. Joe is direct about the cost of the assumption and concrete about the alternatives. The energy is closer to "look at the actual numbers" than to "soften your culture."
