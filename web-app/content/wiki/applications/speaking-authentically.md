# Speaking Authentically as a Coaching Domain

## Definition

Speaking is one of the domains where the inner work is most visible. The performance is on the outside; the cause is on the inside. What you see on the stage — the freezing, the over-preparation, the manufactured persona, the lost flow — is symptom. The root is the relationship the speaker has with themselves, with their nervous system, with their fear of being pushed out of the tribe.

"Thinking of everything that's happening on the outside, a lot of what you can see are just symptoms. And what's interesting to me is figuring out what the root is. And if I can solve the root, then the symptoms solve themselves." -- Tristan de Montebello, Speaking Authentically with Tristan de Montebello

Joe agrees: the modern pursuit of communication training is not technique. It is "finding yourself and learning how to be more of yourself in all kinds of environments where you tend to be guarded or hiding or avoiding." That is the same description Joe uses for the work in any other domain. Speaking is one entry point.

This article treats speaking as a coaching domain — the patterns that show up, the moves that work, the underlying mechanisms that connect the speaking work to the broader work on emotional fluidity, presence, and listening.

## The Evolutionary Layer

The fear of speaking publicly is not irrational. From an evolutionary standpoint, standing in front of the tribe and saying something the tribe disagrees with risked being pushed out, which meant death. The body knows this. The fear of public speaking is at root the fear of social annihilation.

"From an evolutionary standpoint, if you stand up in front of your tribe and you say something dumb or you say something that nobody agrees with, you're going to get pushed out. If you get pushed out, that means death, isolation, and that's the worst thing that could happen to a human." -- Tristan de Montebello

This is not the whole picture. Humans also want to be extraordinary, to stand out. The simultaneous desire to fit in and to be exceptional produces a particular kind of internal friction Joe names directly: "We have these two competing wants. And so they want to go somewhere, but they don't ever get there because they have these two competing wants." The speaker who wants to be extraordinary but is afraid of being pushed out cannot fully arrive in either direction. The result is a managed, filtered performance that delivers neither outcome.

## The Two Roads After the First Hard Experience

A pattern Joe and Tristan describe: a person stands up in front of the class once. Something happens. From that moment, two roads open.

**The naturals.** Not people who did better than anyone else the first time. People who *liked the experience*. Liked the adrenaline. Liked the nerves. Liked how alert they were in that moment. After the first time they think "I want more of that," and they go looking for more opportunities to speak. The naturalness is not innate skill. It is the embrace of the emotional experience.

**Everyone else.** A different first experience, or the same first experience with a different orientation: humiliation, anxiety, hatred of the lead-up. From that moment, avoidance. Say no to opportunities. Put filters up so what comes out of the mouth is pre-checked. Build a different persona for every environment. Over time the system overloads and the person loses contact with who they actually are. They get trapped in a lie they themselves constructed.

The two roads are produced by the same kind of event with different interpretations. This is the core teaching: it is not the speaking that determines who can speak. It is the relationship to the emotional experience of speaking. See [[the-emotional-experience-decides-the-channel]] for the general form of this principle. Speaking is one instance. The same dynamic shuts down dating, art, leadership, intimacy, and any other channel where there is a first hard emotional experience available to either embrace or avoid.

## What Coaching Speaking Actually Targets

If the root is the relationship to the emotional experience, the work is not on the speech. It is on:

- **The fear underneath the avoidance.** Why is speaking scary? What feeling is the speaker preventing themselves from having?
- **The filters between impulse and speech.** What is the speaker pre-checking? What persona is operating? What is being managed?
- **The capacity to be in flow.** Can the speaker discover what they are about to say at the same time as the audience? Or are they reading from a pre-written script in their head?
- **The pause.** Can the speaker stop and not fill the silence?
- **The relationship to the subconscious.** Does the speaker trust that the words will arrive, or do they have to construct each sentence?

These are not communication skills. They are inner-work skills that happen to manifest as communication. A person who develops them speaks better as a side effect.

## State, Not Technique

Speaking well is a state, not a framework. The state is one of presence, listening, and trust in the subconscious. When the state is in place, the words arrive. When the state is not in place, no amount of technique compensates. See [[state-not-framework]] for the general principle.

"Speaking is about state. It's about tapping into forgetting about the fact that you're speaking. You should be discovering the words as they appear out of your mouth at the same time your audience is, because you're so in tune with the concept that you're trying to convey... The moment you start thinking about it and trying to hit a framework or trying to get the words right, you're out of the flow." -- Tristan de Montebello

The implication: a person who has memorized a speech is in worse shape than a person who has internalized the essence and trusts themselves to find the words. The memorizer is one broken chain away from full panic. The essence-holder has a tree of branches available; any branch can break without the tree falling. See [[chain-vs-tree-thinking-for-content]].

## The Body Cannot Be Bullshitted

A specific note Tristan makes that applies far beyond speaking: the subconscious knows whether the speaker has earned the right to feel confident. You cannot read books about speaking and then walk into a situation telling yourself "I've got this" and have your subconscious believe you. The subconscious has access to the actual evidence. If there have been no reps, no real experiences, no testing, the subconscious calls bullshit.

"If you say, I got this because I read a bunch of books, your subconscious is going to say, bullshit, you've read a bunch of books, but we've never tested this out. This is probably going to fail. And it's probably right." -- Tristan de Montebello

The fix is reps in conditions that approximate the real environment. The reps build evidence. After 100 reps in a similar environment, when you walk in and tell yourself "I've got this," the subconscious can check the record and confirm: yes, we've done this, it tends to work out. That is when confidence becomes operative rather than performed.

This is the speaking-domain version of [[practice-cannot-fool-itself-in-real-conditions]]. Inner work has to be tested in conditions that cannot be fooled. Speaking practice is one of those conditions.

## The Pause as the Hardest Part

In speaking coaching, the most underrated and most difficult move is the pause. Tristan describes a simple instruction: "I'm going to put a timer on. This is your space. Now you're going to hold that. You're going to be in this space. And anytime during this timer, as you're speaking, I just want you to stop and do nothing. Just take a pause. Ideally breathe, but I don't care. Just pause."

Most people genuinely cannot do this. The reason is not technical. The reason is emotional: I'm not worth it, I don't have my seat at the table, somebody else should be using this space, if I pause I'll look stupid, I'm deathly afraid of blanking. The pause exposes everything the speaker is using the talking to avoid feeling.

Joe extends this into the broader principle. In every domain, people focus on the doing and ignore the receiving. They are concerned with what they will say, what the presentation looks like, what the graphics are. They are not concerned with what they will listen to, what they will allow, what they will receive. The whole pause-receiving-listening half of communication is unaddressed.

"What are you going to allow to raise the money? What are you going to listen to to raise the money?" -- Joe Hudson

This connects directly to [[listening]] as the foundational practice. The speaker who cannot pause cannot listen. The speaker who cannot listen cannot speak from the subconscious, because the subconscious only delivers when it is being listened to. The pause is the open channel through which the words arrive.

## The Butler Arrives When You Listen

The somatic experience of speaking from flow is described as the butler — the subconscious — bringing you what is needed when you stop trying to produce it. See [[the-butler-metaphor]].

The pause is what makes the butler available. The trying-to-produce is what makes the butler stay quiet. A speaker in a flow state is not generating the speech from effort. They are discovering it the same way the audience is — for the first time, as it comes out of their mouth.

"I'm very curious about what the fuck's going to come out of my mouth. It's like I'm experiencing it for the first time. It's an unfolding that delights me." -- Joe Hudson, [[listening]]

This is the goal-state for speaking work. The speaker is the first listener of their own speech. The audience is the second.

## You Don't Remember What You Said

A reliable signature of the flow state: the speaker has very little memory of what they said. After a great session, after a great rapid-fire coaching, after a great podcast, the speaker walks out and has no idea how it went. They might remember the people. They might remember a story or two. But the words themselves are gone.

"I'll remember stories of other people. I'll remember— it's the same. You've seen me do the rapid-fire coaching. I'll remember them as a person and how they present themselves and the trauma that's in them. But I have a hard time remembering words." -- Joe Hudson

Tristan reports the same: in coaching sessions he gives 2-minute monologues that he knows are profoundly true and he is unable to access them again afterwards. The state that produced them is not available except in the state itself. "It's like cat trying to catch a dream."

This is normal. It is in fact a diagnostic. The speaker who can perfectly recall everything they said was probably not in the state — they were managing, performing, executing a plan. The speaker who cannot recall was in flow, in adrenaline-heightened presence, where memory is not the function being performed.

The practical implication for content creators and coaches: record yourself. The state will deliver content the trying-state cannot deliver, and the only way to retain it is to capture it externally.

## The Coaching Path (Speaking Specifically)

When the presenting concern is "I'm bad at speaking" or "I freeze when I have to present" or "I want to be more authentic when I speak":

1. **Locate the avoidance.** What part of the experience is being avoided? Often it is the nerves themselves, the adrenaline, the unpleasant feeling in the body before speaking. The avoidance of the feeling is what produces the cascade of pre-management.

2. **Reframe the nerves.** The same physical sensation can be called "nerves" or "alertness" or "aliveness." The story is the difference. The naturals get the same body-state as everyone else — they just like it. Can the body-state be welcomed rather than fought?

3. **Build the reps.** Confidence is earned by the subconscious. It cannot be installed cognitively. The work requires real reps in real conditions, ideally with supportive feedback.

4. **Train the pause.** Can the speaker stop and not fill the silence? If not, what is the silence threatening? That is the work.

5. **Stop memorizing.** Work from essence, not from script. Hold the one thing you want to convey. Hold the bookmarks along the way. Trust the tree of branches. See [[chain-vs-tree-thinking-for-content]].

6. **Listen while talking.** The advanced move. Speaking from inside the listening rather than alternating between listening and talking. See [[listening]] for the full development.

7. **Let go of authorship.** The speaker is not generating the speech alone. The speech arrives through them. Treating the words as one's own personal product produces anxiety about the words. Treating them as channeled produces freedom. See [[dont-claim-the-wisdom-as-yours]].

## The Connection to the Larger Work

Speaking is meta-skill. Tristan's frame: "Speaking's a meta skill. It improves everything and spreads out." You cannot change something in how you show up to speak without it propagating to the rest of your life. The same is true in reverse: changes in the rest of your life show up in how you speak.

This is why students of speaking and students of emotional work end up at each other's programs. The pursuits overlap. Both are about the relationship to the self that produces presence. Both are about state more than technique. Both are tested in conditions that cannot be fooled.

Joe's frame: "It's not just in speaking, it's in like so much of life is lost because people aren't doing the pause, the receiving, the allowing part of it." Speaking is one instance of the missing-receiving-capacity pattern. The whole rest of the toolkit applies.

## Tone

When Joe talks about speaking, he is informal, exploratory, often laughing at himself for finding things he didn't expect in mid-sentence. The texture of his speaking-about-speaking is itself the demonstration: he is in the state he is describing. He swears casually. He follows tangents. He is delighted when something new arrives. He is not performing competence about speaking — he is doing the thing while describing it.
