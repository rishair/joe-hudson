# The Covey Subway Story (The Dad Whose Wife Just Died)

## The Story

Joe attributes the story to "Kobe" -- almost certainly meaning Stephen Covey, the Mormon self-help author of the 1980s who built the Franklin Planner business and wrote *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*. Joe acknowledges the story as the one that stuck the third-kind-of-limiting-belief frame in his system.

The setup: Covey is on a subway. There is a man with three kids. The kids are acting out. Covey is getting agitated and pissed. "Man, control your kids. You're bothering everybody. You're a bad parent."

At some point the father looks over and says:

"I'm so sorry, they lost their mom just the other day."

Covey describes an instantaneous shift in his attitude. Suddenly there is understanding. Suddenly there is compassion.

"Same series of events, totally different understanding because he went from thinking he knew what was up to realizing that he did not have the whole story." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## The Point It Illustrates

This is the canonical example of the third kind of limiting belief: thinking you know the whole story. See [[three-kinds-of-limiting-beliefs]].

Nothing on the subway changed. The kids were still acting out. The father was still failing to control them. Every observable was identical before and after the father's sentence. What changed was the frame from which Covey was observing. He went from "I have a complete read on this situation" to "I had a partial read and missed the central fact."

The frame itself was the limit. Not any specific belief about the kids or the dad. The closure of the read was the limit. The new information could not enter as long as the closure was in place. The father's sentence forced the closure open by introducing a fact the existing read could not absorb.

"The difference is kind of what I know, what I know I don't know, what I don't know I don't know. And that's what it is. Like this one, it's very much about just thinking that you understand it when you don't." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## The Donald Rumsfeld Echo

The "what I know, what I know I don't know, what I don't know I don't know" frame is a direct echo of the famous Rumsfeld formulation about intelligence. Joe is using it for a different purpose: not as a theory of warfare but as a theory of perception. The whole-story limiting belief is the belief that there are no unknown unknowns in your read of a situation. There always are.

## What This Shows About Iteration

Joe extends the example to make a broader point about iteration. Two architects see the Chrysler Building differently. An atomic scientist sees it differently again. A photograph from one angle captures different information than a photograph from another. None of these views is the building. All of them together are not the building either. Each one is a partial photograph.

Progress in any domain requires this. You build iPhone 1.0 with one set of perspectives. You see what is missing. You build iPhone 2.0 incorporating what you missed. You build iPhone 3.0. The product gets better because the closure on "we know what this should be" keeps opening.

"So as we see new perspectives, we can iterate and we can create new things. And the old things always have some limits. And if we believe that the old things are the way to go and we're not integrating the new perspectives, then we are limited in our belief systems." -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

## When Joe Reaches for This

When teaching the third kind of limiting belief. This is the example for whole-story closure.

When a person is delivering a definitive read on someone else's behavior or motives. The story interrupts the certainty with a vivid demonstration of how much can be missing from a read that feels complete.

When the conversation has become locked between two people each defending their right read. The story illustrates that better solutions usually come from admitting both reads are partial and looking for what neither has seen.

## What It Does Not Mean

Brett raises the concern: if you always assume there is more to the story, you end up giving everyone a pass. "I could walk down the street and somebody could be being an asshole, and I can tell myself, 'Oh, they must have had a really hard day.'"

Joe agrees this is also a limit. The point is not to install a new closure ("everyone is innocent of everything because there's always more to the story"). The point is to stay aware of the partiality. Sometimes you respond firmly. Sometimes you respond gently. The freedom is in seeing the choice; the limit is in not seeing it.

"The truth is there's always something you don't understand. There's always a justification to be gentle about it or to be mean about it. There's all those things are always there. The difference is, are you in an awareness of that? Are you making choice around that?" -- Joe Hudson, Limiting Beliefs

This is also why the story pairs with [[using-limiting-belief-frame-to-judge-others]]: the whole-story trap is most likely to install itself when a person is judging someone else. The corrective is to remember that the partial read is partial, not to swing to forced compassion.

## Why the Story Stuck

Joe explicitly names this story as the one that stuck the concept in his system. The shape is part of why. The setup is short. The pivot is one sentence. The shift is instantaneous and visible. There is no abstract framing; the experience teaches the point.

This is the shape Joe favors in his own teaching: a small specific scene, a turn that comes from inside the scene, an insight that lands because of the scene rather than because of any explanation laid over it.
