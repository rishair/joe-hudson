# Joe's Famous CEO and the Right-Time Intuition

## The Story

Joe was introduced to a pretty famous CEO. The CEO wanted to work with him. Joe's logical brain ran the standard calculation: this is a great opportunity, this is the kind of engagement that should be said yes to. Joe did not say yes. He did not have a reason — he just knew it was not the right time.

> "I was introduced to a pretty famous CEO and he wanted to work with me, and I wasn't saying yes even though my mind was like, 'Yeah, this is a great opportunity. This is something you should do.' But I knew enough at that point to really listen when my intuition was talking. And though I couldn't describe what made it, I just knew that it wasn't right. It wasn't the right timing." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

A couple of years passed. The CEO had a big public fallout — a significant failure that played out in public. Joe reached out immediately.

> "I immediately reached out because I knew at that point there was gonna be enough humility where that person knew that they could be wrong, that they didn't see everything, and therefore the work that I was gonna do with them was gonna be more meaningful to me and more effective for them. And so that's when we started working together, and it was so much more rewarding than it would've been otherwise." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

## The Point Joe Uses This Story to Make

The story illustrates [[procrastination-is-wisdom]] in its purest form. The intellectual brain had a clean case for yes. The intuitive brain had a felt sense of no. The intuitive brain was right — for a reason the intellectual brain could not have generated in advance. The reason (the CEO's pre-failure lack of humility would have made the work ineffective) was visible in retrospect once the failure occurred. It was not visible in prospect from logic alone.

The story makes three structural points at once:

**The intuition was reading something the intellect could not articulate.** The right side of the brain perceives patterns the left side cannot describe. Demanding a rational explanation forecloses the intuition before it can deliver its signal. See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] for the somatic test that distinguishes intuition from avoidance.

> "The left side of the brain, the way it works is that it can describe stuff, but the right side, it doesn't describe things. You get it on an intuitive hit. You kind of know it, but you can't explain why you know it. And if you look for a rational explanation, then you're gonna actually forego some of the intuition that's actually happening in the right side of your brain." -- Joe Hudson, When You Know What To Do But You Just Don't Do It

**The action that emerged after the wait was better than the action that would have come from the bully-yes.** Had Joe forced himself to accept the original engagement, the work would have been less meaningful and less effective for both of them. The wait was not a missed opportunity; it was the precondition for the right version of the engagement.

**Patience with intuition is itself a discipline.** Joe could have rationalized himself into the yes ("don't be ridiculous, this is a great opportunity, your intuition is just fear, just take the deal"). He didn't. He held the no without needing to defend it. That holding is what made the right-timing version possible.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

This is the canonical case study for [[three-reasons-for-procrastination|reason one: wrong timing]]. When teaching about procrastination as wisdom, Joe uses this story to make the timing reason concrete. It is also the model case for [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] — the intuition arrived with no explanation, the rationalization-against pressed for the yes, the intuition was right.

The story is also useful in coaching contexts where someone is in the bully-yourself-into-action mode and needs to see an example of the wait paying off. The fact that Joe is the protagonist matters: the story carries the implicit credibility of a working coach who knows the cost of saying no to a famous client and does it anyway when the timing isn't right.

## The Risk Joe Acknowledges

The story is not a license for indefinite avoidance. The intuition test is real but it is also subtle. People in chronic avoidance can mistake their fear for intuition. The somatic discriminator matters: intuition arrives with the heart open or opening; conditioned avoidance arrives with the heart closing. See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] for the diagnostic.

Joe's story works precisely because Joe is someone who had developed the discrimination. By the time of the famous-CEO offer, Joe knew the felt difference between his intuition and his avoidance. For a person who has not yet developed that discrimination, the same hold-out posture is risky — the held-out no can be intuition or can be the avoided emotion of doing something hard. The work is in building the discrimination, not in unconditionally trusting any no.

## What the Story Does Not Say

The story does not say that all intuitions point at delay. Intuition can equally well say "do this now" against rational caution. The signature is the same — felt sense, can't fully articulate the reason, the heart open — but the content can be yes-now as easily as wait-two-years. The story is illustrative of the wait case because that is the case Joe needed to make about procrastination. The broader principle is that intuition carries information regardless of what direction it points.

The story also does not say that Joe sat passively for two years. He did other work, lived his life, and reached out the moment the readiness condition (humility from public failure) became visible. The wait is active, not passive. The intuition is asking for the right moment, not for indefinite delay.
