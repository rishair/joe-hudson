# The CEO Who Rode Horses to Quiet the Voice

## The Example

Joe describes a CEO he invested in long ago. She was a hard driver: someone who had been pushing relentlessly for years at the top of a company, with no obvious source of rest. Joe once asked her how she could drive so hard for so long. She brushed off the question.

Then one day, she said it was her time to ride horses. He went to watch. She had learned to ride and jump horses at age 35 and was now 50-something. While she was on the horse, for 45 minutes straight, she could not think about anything else. Not work. Not herself. Not the next thing to do. The horse required her full attention. The 45 minutes was the entire rest she got.

"I noticed when she was on that horse, she couldn't think about anything else. About the horse. 45 minutes straight, she didn't think about work, she didn't think about herself, she didn't beat herself up, she didn't drive herself. And it gave her enough of a break so that she could be revitalized at the end of it and then go back to work." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

## What the Example Illustrates

The example makes visible a specific form of rest that is not the obvious form. For most people, the picture of rest is being still: sitting on the couch, lying in bed, sitting on the porch. For this CEO, that form of rest was not available, because the moment she stopped moving, the inner voice started running. The stillness produced no rest because the voice was loud during the stillness.

The horse-riding solved this. Not because horse-riding is meditative in any traditional sense — it is not. It solved it because horse-riding is so demanding of attention that the voice has nowhere to land. The body is fully occupied. The mind is fully occupied. The voice cannot get a word in. The 45 minutes is therefore 45 minutes of actual rest from the voice, even though it is physically taxing.

The principle: for some people, real rest is not the absence of activity. Real rest is an absorbing activity that the inner critic cannot accompany. See [[inner-critic-prevents-rest-and-integration]] for the structural reason this matters: the body cannot get the rest that integration requires while the voice is running, so anything that quiets the voice — including high-attention activity — counts as rest.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

Joe uses this example when a person:

- Reports that they cannot rest because the voice fires the moment they stop moving.
- Has tried meditation, stillness, vacation, and found no relief because the voice followed them.
- Is dismissive of "rest" because their experience of rest is "lying down with the voice yelling at me."
- Is a high-driver type who would benefit from finding a form of rest that does not require them to be passive.
- Asks "what counts as rest if I can't stop thinking?"

The example expands the definition of rest. Rest is whatever quiets the voice for long enough that the body can actually settle. That can be stillness. It can also be horse-riding, rock-climbing, surfing, playing a demanding instrument, intricate cooking, or any other activity that requires enough attention to crowd out the inner critic.

## What Joe Doesn't Say

Joe does not say:

- "Everyone should take up horse-riding."
- "Hobbies are the answer to burnout."
- "You can avoid inner work by doing absorbing activities forever."

The example is not a prescription for the activity. It is an illustration of the mechanism. The mechanism is that the voice prevents rest, and an activity absorbing enough to silence the voice can be a form of rest. The activity has to be discovered. Horse-riding worked for this person. It might not work for the next.

The example is also not a substitute for working on the voice directly. The CEO got 45 minutes of rest from the voice each day she rode. She did not address why the voice was running the other 23 hours and 15 minutes. The horse-riding was a coping strategy that worked, not a resolution of the underlying pattern. For the long-term work, see [[say-ouch-to-the-inner-critic]] and [[menu-of-responses-to-the-self-critical-voice]].

## Different Kinds of Rest

Joe pairs this example with the broader observation that different forms of rest serve different functions:

"Some rest is best done as exercise, and some rest is best done as being still, and some rest is meditation, and some rest is hanging out with friends. Different kinds of rest get you different results, and sometimes you need one kind of rest and sometimes you need another kind of rest. So identifying the rest that gives you the best results is a wonderful thing to do." -- Joe Hudson, Working Every Day Is Why You're Behind

The implicit experiment: try several kinds of rest. Notice which ones actually leave you rested. The form is personal. The form also varies by what kind of depletion is being addressed:

- Physical depletion may want sleep and slow movement.
- Mental depletion may want stillness or nature.
- Emotional depletion may want connection with friends.
- Voice-depletion may want an absorbing activity like the CEO's horses.

See [[quality-of-downtime-matters]] for the related distinction about which forms of "rest" actually deliver recovery and which forms are stimulation pretending to be rest.

## The Connection to "Find a Healthy Replacement"

The CEO's horse-riding is structurally adjacent to [[find-a-healthy-replacement]] — the principle that bad habits are giving you something, and the work is to find a healthier vehicle for the same function. The CEO needed a break from the voice. Without horse-riding, she might have found that break in alcohol, scrolling, TV, or some other less-restorative substitute. The horse-riding provided the same break (45 minutes without the voice) in a form that was also restorative for the body and the spirit.

The cross-reference: when a person has a habit that quiets their voice (drinking, scrolling, smoking), the question is not "how do I stop the habit" but "what is the habit giving me, and what would give me the same thing in a healthier form?" The horse-riding is an example of what the healthier form can look like.
