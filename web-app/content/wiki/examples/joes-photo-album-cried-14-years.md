# Joe's Photo Album: Discovering Why He Hadn't Cried in 14 Years

## The Story

Joe is 7 years old. He is crying. His parents take a picture of him to make fun of him. They put the picture in the family photo album.

Joe is now 23 or 24. He is flipping through the photo album and comes across the picture of his 7-year-old self crying. He looks at it and says: "Oh. That's probably why I haven't cried for 14 years."

Before this moment, from roughly age 20 to 24, he had been trying to figure out how to have the life he wanted, why he and his girlfriend were always fighting, what was going wrong. The figuring had not produced movement. The picture produced the answer the figuring could not.

He decides to learn how to cry again. He has no help and no method. He goes out into the woods and pretends to cry. Then he pretends to cry. Then he pretends to cry. After enough repetitions of the pretending, the actual crying starts.

When it starts, things change. "There was so much clarity in my life. There was so much less resistance, so much less self-talk of shame, so much less fight." -- Joe Hudson, After 14 Years of Coaching

## The Point It Illustrates

Allowing feelings creates clarity that figuring cannot. The 4 years of figuring (20 to 24) produced no resolution to the relationship problems, the life questions, the sense that something was off. The first months of crying produced more clarity than the 4 years of analysis.

The mechanism this demonstrates is what later became [[feel-over-figure]] and [[decisions-are-emotional]]. The intellect cannot solve what was always an emotional question. The substrate the intellect is trying to work with is corrupted as long as the underlying feeling is being avoided. The feeling has to come back online for the intellect to function cleanly.

The story is also the origin of one of Joe's foundational coaching insights: when a person has been trying to figure out a stuck problem for years, the move is not better thinking. The move is to find what feeling has been walled off and let it come through.

## The Decision Was Made at 7

The structural piece worth seeing: Joe did not decide consciously at any point to stop crying. The decision was made by a 7-year-old in response to the parental mockery. The decision then ran silently for 14 years, organizing everything downstream — the inability to cry, the fights with the girlfriend, the spinning on life questions, the figuring that did not produce clarity. None of this was attributed to the original decision, because the decision was made before Joe had the cognitive apparatus to remember making it.

This is the form most [[childhood-imprinting]] takes. A young child encounters a situation, makes an adaptive decision (stop crying so the parents don't mock me), and that decision quietly runs the system for decades afterward. The adult does not know the decision is running until something — a photo, a conversation, a coaching session — surfaces it.

The picture worked because it was the literal artifact of the moment the decision was made. Most clients do not have such a direct artifact. Often the surfacing happens through felt body memory, through a question the coach asks that produces an unexpected emotion, through a current situation that re-stages the original one. The mechanism is the same: the decision-made-young becomes visible, and once visible, can be unmade.

## The Pretending-to-Cry Method

The practical piece of the story that often gets missed: Joe taught himself to cry again by going out alone and pretending. He had no method, no teacher, no framework. He just went into the woods and did the physical motions of crying — the face, the sounds, the breath — until eventually the real thing started.

This is the body's path back to the feeling. The body holds the form of crying even after the access to the feeling has been blocked for years. Running the form re-activates the access. The pretending is not a trick to fool the system; it is the deliberate use of the body to find a feeling that has gone offline.

The practice generalizes. A person who has not felt anger in years can do the body of anger — the breath, the sound, the stance — alone in a safe place, until the actual anger comes through. A person who cannot feel grief can do the body of grief. The form precedes the feeling. The feeling returns through the form.

This is the felt-experience precursor to what later became Joe's teaching on tactical ways to feel emotions (see [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]). The story is the proof-of-concept that the method works, discovered by Joe in his own life before there was a method.

## When Joe Reaches for This

- When someone has been trying to figure out a stuck problem for years and the figuring is not producing movement. The story models the move from figuring to feeling.
- When someone says "I never cry" or "I don't get emotional" in a way that signals access has been blocked. The story normalizes the blockage and points at the path through.
- When someone wonders whether the body can re-learn a feeling that has been gone for decades. The 14-year gap in the story is the empirical answer: yes.
- When someone is in early stages of emotional work and is afraid the feelings will be too much. The story shows the worst that happens: a lot of clarity, less resistance, less self-attack, less fight.
- When the conversation needs an origin story for why Joe trusts the feel-over-figure principle so completely. The principle was not learned from a book. It was discovered in the woods.

## The Connection to the Coaching Career

This story is one of the seed events of Joe's coaching work. The 14-years framing in the "After 14 Years of Coaching" video is not arbitrary; the work began with this discovery. Everything Joe later built — the connection course, the masterclass, the AOA — rests on the empirical fact that allowing feeling produces clarity that figuring cannot. The proof of concept happened in his own life first, then was reproduced across thousands of clients, then became the framework.

The pairing with [[joes-mill-valley-list]] (the wrote-list, found-six-months-later, the shoulds-didnt-change story) is structural. Both stories are seed events. Both are about Joe discovering by accident what later became core teachings. Both involve a young Joe doing something for himself that turned out to be the right thing — without a method, without a teacher, just from following the impulse.
