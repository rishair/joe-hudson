# Wingsuit as Awakening Path (Joe's BASE Jumping Story)

## The Story

Joe's first BASE jump was off an 800-foot bridge in West Virginia. A quarter-million people were watching. He had not jumped before. He had roughly seven seconds of free fall before he needed to pull the parachute. He decided to count to five — giving himself two seconds of fudge.

In the moment he pushed off, something happened that he describes as a system crash of his consciousness. The blue screen of death.

"The moment that I pushed off the edge, I felt like an inversion. There was like the space around me was just much bigger. The actual space was more dense. Like there was more granularity to the space. There wasn't a sense that I existed. It was almost like a blue screen of death or like some kind of system crash of my consciousness, of the operating system. And it was just pure awareness. And it was like I switched off and then switched back on again. I had to reboot and then came back, deployed my parachute, landed, and then immediately called my sister. And I was just like, 'I don't know what just happened, but something happened.' And I'm not talking about jumping off a bridge. Something happened." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

He somehow counted the five seconds accurately. He could not say how. He deployed, landed, and called his sister to report that something had happened that was not about the jump.

## What This Was

This was a taste of the gut awakening — the nervous-system version of the recognition that the bounded self isn't the actual organization. It arrived through the mechanism that extreme proximity to death produces: when the bodily self is about to potentially end, the activity of holding the bodily self together (which is constant and usually invisible) becomes obviously absurd. The release happens because the structure that was being maintained is about to be ended anyway.

This is one of the documented pathways to the gut awakening. See [[inherent-safety-as-gut-awakening]] for the full concept and [[three-paths-of-awakening]] for the framework that places this in context.

"For those who like it a little spicy, there's the wingsuit base jumping path." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The spicy path. It works, but it works through a mechanism (proximity to death) that is not particularly recommendable as a deliberate practice.

## The Habituation Problem

The taste did not persist. Joe started chasing the same experience by jumping more, and the same intensity at the same risk level stopped producing it. The nervous system habituated. To get the same depth of release, he had to go deeper into the experience — not more dangerous exactly, but always at his actual edge, where survival required full presence.

"Then I habituated to, like, I had to go deeper into the experience. I had to make it not necessarily more dangerous, but I had to keep it at my edge where I felt like I actually was at risk... Which ultimately what was really happening, what seemed to really be happening with me was that I was entering an experience where I really needed to be present and there wasn't space for an I anymore. And if I wanted to survive, I needed to be really present." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The mechanism became clear retrospectively: the survival pressure of the activity forced presence. The bounded self that usually runs as background commentary could not run because there wasn't bandwidth for it. The absence of the bounded-self commentary produced the recognition that the bounded self was not the actual operator.

## The Translation

The eventual recognition Joe arrived at: the presence the BASE jump forced is the same presence available in ordinary moments. The jump was not what produced the recognition. The presence was what produced the recognition. The jump was just one (extreme) way to get to that level of presence.

"It turns out you can be that present here having a podcast." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

"You don't need to be nearly dying." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes

The translation insight is what made the BASE-jumping practice unnecessary. The presence the activity required could be cultivated in ordinary conditions through different means. The BASE jumps gave Joe the taste; the taste eventually pointed at the territory; the territory turned out to be available without the activity.

This is also one of the structural pieces that informed Joe's pleasure-as-gut-path teaching. The spicy path works through pushing into proximity with death. The pleasure path produces the same recognition through pushing into deep capacity for ordinary enjoyment. Both reach the same territory. The pleasure path doesn't require near-death conditions to do it.

## Connection to Flow States Generally

This same pattern shows up in many activities that require sustained presence under high stakes. Artists report it. Athletes report it. Certain business people report it.

The structure is the same: an activity that requires the person to be fully present in order to perform produces, as a side effect, the absence of the bounded-self commentary. The absence of the commentary produces the recognition.

"It's also really common for versions of it to happen in flow states. You know, artists often love their art because in their art, they're — if they're in their judgmental mind, then the art sucks. So on some level, what they're doing is they're training themselves to be in these flow states, and these flow states have less me in them. Whether you're using heroin to get to the flow state or whether you've learned how to get it yourself, getting into that flow state has a lot less me. There's less critic, there's less second-guessing, less judging, less doubt, all those things." -- Joe Hudson, Awakening: Why We Waited 150 Episodes (the second speaker, in conversation with Joe)

The artists, the athletes, and the BASE jumpers are all producing the same condition through different means. The "less me" in the flow state is structurally what the awakening recognition is, just temporarily and produced by specific conditions.

## The Risk This Example Carries

The example carries a danger of being misread as advocacy for extreme sport. It is not. Joe is clear that you don't need to be nearly dying. The spicy path works but is not preferred. The pleasure path produces the same recognition with vastly less risk.

The example is included because it is informative about the mechanism, not because it is recommendable. The same mechanism (presence stripping the bounded-self commentary) is the operative ingredient in all the safer paths. The example makes the mechanism visible because the conditions are extreme.

## Why Joe Tells This Story

The story serves three teaching purposes:

**It demystifies the awakening recognition.** Joe is not describing a meditation hall experience or a mystical opening that came after years of practice. He is describing a moment on a bridge. The recognition is structurally the same as what shows up in meditation halls, but the framing here strips the spiritual scaffolding and leaves the recognition itself.

**It illustrates the post-experience pattern.** The taste arrives, the person chases the taste, the chase produces habituation, the habituation forces depth, the depth eventually reveals the mechanism, the mechanism turns out to be available without the activity. This pattern is the same regardless of how the taste arrived (BASE jumping, retreat, near-death, psychedelic, spontaneously). See [[joes-first-meditation-retreat]] for the same pattern with Joe's 8-second Vipassana experience that started the 7-year chase.

**It points at the structural condition.** The translation insight ("it turns out you can be that present here having a podcast") is the point. The recognition is not located in extreme conditions. It is located in presence. The presence is available wherever it is cultivated.

## Connection to Joe's Larger Arc

This story sits alongside [[joes-first-meditation-retreat]] (the 8-second Vipassana oneness experience) and [[joes-bathroom-realization]] (the eventual landing while peeing). All three are tastes of the same recognition, arriving through different channels and at different times in Joe's life. The BASE jump was an early gut-channel taste. The Vipassana retreat was an early head-channel taste. The bathroom moment was the integrated landing years later.

The pattern across all three: Joe did not produce the recognition through effort. It arrived. He then sometimes chased it through the activity that had produced it (more BASE jumps, more meditation), and the chasing produced its own teaching about why the chase doesn't work. The actual landing came later, after the chasing had quieted, in ordinary conditions.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with enthusiasm and self-aware humor. He acknowledges the spicy framing. He is not embarrassed about the BASE jumping period of his life; he is also not glorifying it. The story is told as data about a mechanism, with the experiential color that makes the mechanism vivid.

The most charged moment is the description of pushing off the edge — the inversion, the granular space, the absence of self, the system crash. Joe lets the description be vivid without overdramatizing. The "blue screen of death" framing keeps the description grounded by giving it a recognizably ordinary metaphor.
