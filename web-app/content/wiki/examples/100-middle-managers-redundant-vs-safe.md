# The 100 Middle Managers Story

## The Story

Joe was training a group of 100 middle managers at a large company. It was clear AI was going to come in and take half of their jobs. There was an opportunity for any of them to become the person who implemented AI in their role. That person would have a job for the next 10 years implementing AI in this company or in others.

That move would have produced safety.

What most of them were doing instead: making sure they were doing the tasks their boss would be happy with. The boss-happy productivity was the engine. They believed it was producing safety. It was producing the opposite -- redundancy.

"I was in another company and a whole bunch of middle managers. We were training 100 middle managers, and it was clear that AI was going to come in and take half of their jobs. And that means for all of them, there was an opportunity to be the person to implement AI in this role, and then they would have a job for the next 10 years implementing AI, either within this company or within other companies. That would make you safe. But what most people were doing was making sure that they were doing the tasks that their boss would be happy with. Not making them safe, making them redundant." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

## What the Example Illustrates

The example is the cleanest version of the inversion at the heart of [[productivity-is-the-trees-purpose-is-the-forest]]. The implicit forest of the middle managers was safety. The productivity they were running -- pleasing the boss -- was making them less safe, not more.

Several principles converge in the story:

1. **[[productivity-is-the-trees-purpose-is-the-forest]]:** the managers had not consciously identified the forest. Their productivity had been auto-pointed at "do what the boss wants" as a proxy for safety. The proxy and the goal had decoupled.

2. **[[ask-how-much-safety-plays-a-role]]:** the second-order question Joe pairs with the example is "how is your productivity aimed at actual safety?" In the managers' case, it wasn't. The aim was wrong.

3. **[[redundant-vs-safe]]:** the story names the specific failure mode -- the strategy that promises safety produces redundancy. The same shape recurs across many domains.

4. **[[productivity-as-fear-based-engine]]:** the engine driving the boss-pleasing was almost certainly fear. The fear was correctly identifying the threat (job loss) and incorrectly identifying the solution (boss-pleasing).

5. **[[do-the-work-thats-harder-for-you]]:** the leveraged task (figure out AI in this role) was the unknown task. The boss-pleasing tasks were known and procedural. The known tasks were the wrong ones; the unknown task was the high-leverage one.

## How Joe Uses the Example in Coaching

When a person presents corporate productivity as "I just have to do what my boss wants," Joe reaches for this example. The example works because it makes the inversion concrete. The person can see the structure in someone else's situation more easily than in their own. Once seen, the structure becomes visible in their own situation.

The follow-up question Joe pairs with the example: "How safe is your productivity actually making you?" The question lands harder after the example has been told. The example has primed the inversion. The question pushes the person to apply the inversion to themselves.

## The General Pattern

The 100-middle-managers shape recurs across contexts:

- **The employee who does what the boss wants and gets laid off in the reorg.** Their resume reads "good at pleasing my last boss," which is not a transferable skill.
- **The founder who does what investors want and builds a company that no one needs.** The investor-pleasing was supposed to produce funding-and-safety. It produced product-market-fit-failure.
- **The student who does what the teacher wants and develops no original capacity.** The teacher-pleasing was supposed to produce credentials-and-safety. It produced a person who cannot think outside the rubric.
- **The partner who does what the relationship wants and loses themselves.** The relationship-pleasing was supposed to produce intimacy-and-safety. It produced resentment, then collapse.

The pattern is general: doing what the external authority wants, in order to produce safety, tends to produce the opposite. The authority's wants are not the same as the conditions that would actually produce safety. Confusing them is one of the most common errors in adult life.

## Joe's Companion Story (The VP and the 3-Day Offsite)

Joe pairs this example with a related one in the same teaching:

"I was recently with a vice president of an organization, big organization, and she was missing the 3-day offsite of where the CEO and all the vice presidents sit down. I said, whoa, how do you feel about that? Her response was, I could ask Claude what the meeting summary is and it would be 80% accurate, and I would have gotten 80% of what I was meant to get out of it. And everybody who went will only also get 80% of what they were meant to get out of it. She's like, I could literally tell you what's going to happen, how it's going to happen. It's all, it's all a known quantity." -- Joe Hudson, Productivity Is Making You Unproductive

See [[vp-and-the-3-day-offsite]] for the full extraction. The two examples together illustrate the same principle from opposite ends: the middle managers were doing the wrong work because they had not asked "what is the productivity for?" The VP was opting out of work that wasn't producing anything because she had asked the question. Both stories point at the cost of unexamined productivity at scale.

## The Coaching Use

When using the example, Joe does not moralize. The middle managers are not stupid. The pattern is structural and common. The example exists to make the structure visible, not to judge the people inside the structure.

The use is: "Here is a clean version of the pattern you're describing in your own life. Notice what they were doing. Notice what would have made them actually safe. Now look at your own pattern through that lens."

The person almost always sees the parallel. The parallel does the work the direct question could not do alone.
