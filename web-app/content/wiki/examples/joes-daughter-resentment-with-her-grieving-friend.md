# Joe's Daughter and Her Friend with the Heart-Attack Father

## The Story

Joe's teenage daughter had a dear friend whose father had a heart attack. Out of the genuine care in her heart -- because she wanted to -- she started helping the friend by being there every evening. The friendship was real; the care was real; the help was offered freely.

Then resentment arrived. Joe saw it.

"She came home and I saw the resentment. I saw that she started getting tired of doing it. And she felt horrible about it because what she wanted was to not take care of her." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

The daughter could not allow herself to want a break. The friend's father had had a heart attack; the friend was grieving; wanting a break from helping a grieving friend felt wrong. The not-okay-to-want stance locked the want in place, which produced the resentment. The daughter then felt horrible about the resentment, which compounded the stuckness.

## Joe's Intervention

The intervention was minimal. Joe asked a single question and reflected the answer.

"I said, 'Hey babe, what do you want?' She goes, 'I want a break for a while.' I said, 'Hey, great. I want you to have a break for a while. And I bet your friend wants you to have a break too.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

Three small moves in the exchange:

1. **The want question.** "What do you want?" -- the standard locate-the-want move. See [[the-want]].
2. **The endorsement.** "Great. I want you to have a break for a while." -- the parent removing the not-okay-to-want stance by openly endorsing the want.
3. **The reframe of the friend.** "And I bet your friend wants you to have a break too." -- predicting that the friend also wants the break, which redistributes the imagined responsibility of asking.

The intervention is small because the work is internal. The daughter does not need a strategy. She needs permission. The question + endorsement + reframe gives her enough permission to make the ask.

## What Happened Next

"So she called up her friend. She said, 'Hey, I want a break for a while.' And her friend's like, 'Great, I want you to have a break for a while.' She had a break. I think it was like 2, 3 days and boom, she was back at it helping out her friend whose father had had a heart attack." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

The ask landed cleanly because it was the clean form of the ask. No shame, no explanation, no hedging. "I want a break for a while." The friend said the same words back: "Great, I want you to have a break for a while." The break lasted 2-3 days. After it, the daughter returned to the helping freely, because the caring was now clean caring rather than resentment-laced caretaking.

## What This Example Demonstrates

The example shows several principles operating in their cleanest form, with no complication:

**[[resentment-as-unspoken-want]].** The resentment was the form the unspoken want for a break took. The daughter was not following her want, and the resentment was the leak.

**[[resentment-as-caretaking-diagnostic]].** The shift from caring to caretaking happened at the moment the wanting-a-break was suppressed. Before the suppression, it was caring. After the suppression, it was caretaking. The resentment marked the transition.

**[[asking-dissolves-the-not-okay-to-want]].** The asking, not the getting, was the work. Even if the friend had said no, the asking would have dissolved the resentment because the asking was the contradiction of the not-okay-to-want stance.

**[[the-want]].** The single question "What do you want?" surfaced the actual want in one move. The want was already known to the daughter; it just needed permission to be named.

**[[boundaries]].** The 2-3 day break is the boundary in its specific form: not abandonment, not punishment, just "I am going to take a break, then return." The friend, not asked to change anything, received the boundary cleanly.

## Why This Example Is Worth Reaching For

The example has three properties that make it especially useful in coaching:

1. **The caretaking was real and good.** The daughter was not a bad caretaker. The caring was genuine. The example does not require the listener to identify with someone whose caretaking is suspect; it shows the resentment-leak happening even in caring that everyone would endorse.

2. **The friend's situation was acute.** A heart attack is a real crisis. The example removes the easy escape of "well, my situation isn't that serious so I can be selfish." If the daughter could take a break from a friend whose father just had a heart attack, the listener cannot defend their own resentment-producing caretaking on grounds of the cared-for person's need.

3. **The intervention was small.** Joe did not give a lecture. He did not work the daughter through her shame. He asked one question and endorsed the answer. The simplicity demonstrates that the work, when the principle is understood, is often small.

## The Parent Modeling Frame

A second-order point: this example also demonstrates what parenting from the principle looks like. Joe saw the resentment. He did not lecture the daughter on selflessness or on the importance of being a good friend. He did not shame her for the resentment. He asked her what she wanted, endorsed the answer, and predicted the friend would receive it well.

The daughter learned the move from the moment of being treated this way. The next time she finds herself resentful, she has a template: locate the want, ask for it, trust that the relationship can hold it. The teaching happened by being modeled in a moment of low stakes (the relationship between Joe and his daughter), which let the move generalize to higher-stakes situations later.

See [[esme-just-need-to-be-sad]] for the inverse story where the same daughter, at a different age, taught Joe to stop caretaking her by setting the boundary herself: "Dad, I just need to be sad. You don't need to take care of that." The pattern goes both directions in the family.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When teaching [[asking-dissolves-the-not-okay-to-want]] and wanting an example where the principle operates cleanly
- When a person is in [[i-caretake-people]] and is unable to identify what they want because the cared-for person's situation feels too serious
- When a person is resentful in a caretaking role and feels horrible about the resentment
- When the work is to demonstrate that the ask itself does the work, not the getting
- When parenting an adolescent is the topic and the move is showing how to coach a kid who is over-functioning for a peer
