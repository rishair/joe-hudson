# Joe's Daughter: 'I've Gotten Straight A's and You've Never Congratulated Me'

## The Story

Joe made a deliberate parenting choice: largely not to give compliments to his daughters. The reasoning was that he did not want to install himself as the third party in their evaluation of themselves. Instead of "good job," he would reflect what they were feeling: "I see how excited you are."

The intended outcome: his daughters would do their work without nagging because they were oriented to their own felt sense of doing the work, not to his approval. Joe reports this worked. The girls did their homework without being chased. The motivation was internal.

The unintended outcome arrived one day when one of his daughters confronted him. She said: I've gotten straight A's and you've never congratulated me. What the fuck?

Joe was caught off guard. He apologized. He told her how proud he was. He acknowledged the work she had put in. He acknowledged that he might have overcorrected.

"I literally had one of my daughters tell me, she's like, I've gotten straight A's and you've never congratulated me. What the fuck? And I was like, I'm so sorry. I told her how proud I was of her and how I realized the work that was put into it. But it's so important to me. So maybe I overcorrected there, but it was so important for me to have them be able to listen to themselves and not tell them what to do." -- Joe Hudson, Who Owns Your Authority?

## What the Example Illustrates

The no-compliments approach reflects a real principle: do not install yourself as the third party in your child's internal evaluation. The principle is sound. The example shows that the principle can be applied too rigidly, and that the child will eventually call out the rigidity.

There is room in the principle for explicit acknowledgment of pride and care. The error is not in any single instance of praise. The error is in the habitual, automatic praise that subtly orients the child's motivation toward earning the praise. The corrective is not to refuse praise entirely. The corrective is to be conscious about when praise installs the parent as the criterion and when it simply names what is true between parent and child.

A direct moment of "I am proud of you and I see the work you put in" does not necessarily install the parent as the criterion if the child's primary feedback loop is already running through their own felt sense. The risk is in the constant, low-grade evaluation — the "good job" that follows every task as a default — because that pattern teaches the child to perform for the parent's reaction.

## The Daughter's Authority in the Confrontation

What is striking about the moment is that the daughter operates her own authority in calling Joe out. She does not hint, withdraw, or perform; she names what she has noticed and asks for what she needs. The "what the fuck" is not a complaint — it is the daughter assessing her own situation, identifying a missing piece, and asking her father to address it.

The fact that she could do this is itself evidence that the deeper principle worked. A daughter trained to seek the parent's approval would have suppressed the complaint and continued to try to earn the missing congratulations. A daughter who has been given the space to develop her own authority calls the parent on the gap and asks for what she needs.

In a sense, Joe's parenting succeeded in producing exactly the daughter who could correct him. The success is visible in the form of the confrontation.

## The Apology as Part of the Example

Joe's response — "I'm so sorry" — followed by the explicit naming of pride and acknowledgment of the work, is the right response to the daughter's call. He did not defend the principle. He did not explain why he had withheld the compliments. He apologized, gave the missing acknowledgment, and named that he might have overcorrected.

This models for the daughter that her authority can change the parent's behavior, that the parent can be wrong about how to apply a principle, and that the relationship can be repaired through clean acknowledgment. All three of these are themselves things that develop the daughter's authority.

The apology and the moment together teach: principles are tools, not rules. A principle applied without responsiveness to the actual person in front of you becomes its own rigidity. The work is to hold the principle while staying responsive to the specific child.

## What This Example Pairs With

This example pairs with [[esme-step-by-step-project]], the inverse: a moment when Joe's principle of not telling his daughter what to do encountered a daughter who was asking for instruction, and Joe had to update his application of the principle in real time.

Together, the two examples describe the calibration. The principle of building internal authority requires holding back the default authority moves (constant praise, unsolicited instruction). It also requires responding when the child's own authority is asking for something specific (acknowledgment, instruction, presence). The wrong move is treating the principle as an absolute that overrides what the child actually needs.

## Tone

Joe tells the story with self-deprecation and warmth. The "what the fuck" delivered by his daughter is told with a smile. The apology and the explicit naming of pride are reported plainly, without self-recrimination. The tone is the tone of a parent who is doing the work, made a calibration error, was corrected by his child, and updated. There is no shame in the recounting and no defense of the original choice. Both the original principle and the correction are held as part of the ongoing work of parenting.
