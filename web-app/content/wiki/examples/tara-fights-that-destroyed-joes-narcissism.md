# Tara and Joe's Six Months of Vicious Fights

## The Story

About seven or eight years into the marriage, after years of what Joe described as amazingly healing fights, the vicious fights came back. Not low-grade. Vicious, with vengeance, for a six-month stretch. Joe says the early-relationship vicious fights had become a memory so faint he had stopped tracking them; the return was a shock.

For years afterward, both Joe and Tara wished the period had not happened. Joe describes it as "I wish that hadn't happened" -- not the whole period, but a specific incident inside it. They handled it imperfectly. They could have done it differently. The wishing-it-away energy lasted years.

The reframe came much later. Joe tells Tara, in the present-tense moment of the conversation with Brett, that he has just recognized what those fights actually did.

"I said, you know, I think that, that series of fights is what destroyed my narcissism. And it wasn't, I mean, there's no narcissism that isn't all completely destroyed ever, but the, there was a way in which I was subtly holding myself above Tara. And it was that set of fights that destroyed that. My brain can't conceive, compare myself to Tara anymore." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The recognition lands as Joe is telling it. He wells up describing it.

## What the Fights Actually Did

The subtle narcissism Joe is describing is not the loud, obvious version. It is the structural background hum of "I am slightly above" that ran in his mind during fights with Tara. Not stated. Not acknowledged. Operating.

The six months of vicious fights did what gentler fights could not. They went hard enough at the holding-above that the holding-above could not survive. The brain, after the period, was unable to construct the comparison anymore. Not that Joe suppressed the comparison; the comparison no longer arose.

"It was so painful to hold myself slightly above in even a fucking subtle way. And it was a horrendous set of fights, and I would not give it up for any -- the amount of peace and love that it brings to my heart on a daily basis today." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The peace is the daily payoff. Whatever the comparison was doing to him moment-by-moment for years, it stopped doing it. The structural background pressure was off.

This is the underlying claim of [[your-trauma-is-equally-matched-with-your-partner]] -- the recognition that the partner is not below you -- experienced from the inside as embodied truth rather than as an intellectual position. The fights produced the embodiment.

## The Lived Experience During the Fights

Joe is explicit about what the period felt like at the time. It did not feel like progress. It felt like the relationship might be over and like everything he believed about coaching might be wrong.

"I thought at the time, I was like, fuck, maybe our marriage is over. Maybe I fucking don't know shit about what I'm talking about. Maybe like, I questioned everything during that." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The questioning was total. The teacher questioning whether the teaching is real. The husband questioning whether the marriage is over. The practitioner questioning whether the practice works. None of this read as the system functioning. All of it read as collapse.

The reframe in retrospect: the questioning was the system functioning. The structural background certainties had to be broken for the deeper change to happen. The certainties he was losing were the same certainties the subtle narcissism was built on.

This is the [[feared-stage-of-relationship-deepening-can-feel-like-things-getting-worse]] dynamic in long-form. The thing that felt like the worst period was the period that delivered the deepest integration. Both can be true. The during-it experience does not predict the after-it value.

## The Survival Move

What got them through is named explicitly. It was not a technique. It was the basic practice they had been building for years.

"We just kept doing what we do as far as being there for each other. And it was in that moment, I remember in those fights, there came a place where I, I learned to just -- or we learned how to just sit there in loving attention with each other, just in the face of absolute bullshit from one another. 'Yeah, I gotcha. I'm right here.'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The move was sitting in loving attention with each other in the face of what felt like unrelenting hostility. Not arguing back well. Not refining their technique. Just staying. The staying is what allowed the destruction of the subtle narcissism to complete rather than producing the relationship's end.

This is the operational form of [[trauma-heals-in-relationship]] under maximum pressure. The capacity to stay loving while the worst material is firing is the medium of the integration. The medium produced the result.

## What Joe Reaches For This Example To Illustrate

The example serves several teachings:

**It illustrates that hard periods late in a relationship are not necessarily regressions.** A couple that has had years of good fighting can hit a six-month wall and have it be the most productive period of the relationship. The wall is the system asking for the next level of integration.

**It illustrates that the during-it experience is unreliable.** The "I question everything" inside the period does not predict what the period will turn out to have been. The period must be allowed to complete before its meaning becomes legible.

**It illustrates that the subtle forms of narcissism are the ones that take the most rigorous fights to destroy.** The loud version can sometimes be deconstructed intellectually. The "I am slightly above" version is body-level and requires body-level dismantling. The fights are the body-level dismantling.

**It illustrates that gratitude for the hardest periods often arrives years late.** The "I would not give it up for any" recognition came years after the period itself. Time was part of the medium. The integration needed to settle before its value could be felt.

## The Welling Up

Joe wells up while telling Brett this. The emotion in the telling is its own data. The recognition is not intellectual; it is felt. The destruction of the subtle narcissism produced a daily peace that has been present for years, and Joe's body still registers gratitude for it strongly enough to surface in tears.

"And, and, and I, like, as I'm talking about it, I literally just -- you can see I'm -- I, like, I well up. That was, like, such a fucking huge gift for me to feel, to have that fucking thing gone." -- Joe Hudson, How To Fight And Win

The welling up is offered transparently. The teaching includes Joe's continuing relationship to what those fights gave him.

## When Joe Reaches For This Example

This example is appropriate for:

- A couple in a hard period after a long stretch of relative ease, wondering if they have regressed
- A person doubting the work because their relationship has gotten harder, not easier, as they have grown
- Someone for whom the [[narcissism-as-incapacity-to-feel]] frame has landed and the question is what kind of work dismantles it
- Someone fighting Tara-and-Joe-style vicious fights and needing to hear that this period can be the most productive one
- The teaching of [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] when an example is needed of the principle holding under maximum pressure

## What This Example Is Not

This is not a claim that all six-month periods of vicious fighting destroy useful narcissism. Some periods of vicious fighting are just damage. The retrospective recognition Joe describes was specific to a couple that had a long practice of fighting well and had built the capacity to stay loving even in the worst of it.

This is not a recommendation to provoke vicious fights. The fights arose. The work was meeting them, not creating them.

This is not a claim that destroying narcissism feels good while it happens. Joe is precise: it was painful, it was unrelenting, it felt like the marriage might be over. The recognition that it was destroying narcissism was not available in real time. The example serves to show that the during-it experience and the after-it value are different categories.
