# Walgreens: Sales Per Customer, Not Sales Per Store

## The Story

When Walgreens was outpacing the rest of the drugstore market by a substantial margin, the distinguishing factor was not the obvious one. It was their choice of metric. Where other drugstores measured sales per store, Walgreens measured sales per customer. The metric was an indirect measure of customer experience. If a customer typically bought nine dollars of items, the experience was producing one level of value. If they bought eleven dollars, the experience was producing more.

"There's a book, I think it's Good to Great, and they talk about Walgreens when they were taking off and all the other drugstores weren't. They were beating the market substantially. And they talked about how their main metric wasn't sales per store, their main metric was sales per customer because they were measuring the customer experience. Did the customer buy nine dollars of things on average or eleven dollars of things on average? And so their whole metric was based on customer experience rather than just revenue." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The metric choice cascaded. Decisions about store design, staffing, inventory, and customer service were made in service of the chosen metric. The metric directed attention upstream of revenue. The competitors, measuring sales per store, were making decisions about store openings, square footage, and location. Both sets of decisions produced very different organizations.

## The Point Joe Uses It To Illustrate

What you measure determines what you produce. The Walgreens story is the case-in-point for the metric-choice principle in [[team-as-scientific-method]]. The same drugstore business, optimized against two different metrics, produces two different companies. The metric is not a passive measurement of what you are doing; it is an active determinant of what you become.

"What you measure and how you measure it is a huge -- I mean, it's an art form and a skill set." -- Joe Hudson, Building a Functional Team

The example is a counter to the assumption that the right metrics are obvious. "Revenue" looks obvious. "Sales per store" looks obvious. The leap to "sales per customer" required someone to ask what was actually being optimized for. The optimization target was customer experience. The metric was selected to track it.

## When Joe Reaches For It

Joe reaches for this example when:

- A leader is asking what metrics to use and has defaulted to the obvious ones
- A conversation about team design has gotten stuck at "we already measure that"
- A leader is choosing between two metrics and treating the choice as administrative rather than strategic
- A founder is selecting the first metrics for a new venture

The example shifts the conversation from "what should we measure" treated as a routine question to "what is the upstream variable that produces the thing we want" treated as a generative question. The right metric is often not the most direct measure of the outcome; it is a measure of the thing that produces the outcome.

## Tone

Joe delivers this as illustration, not as case study. The point is the principle, not the company. The example is recognizable to most people in business and lands quickly.
