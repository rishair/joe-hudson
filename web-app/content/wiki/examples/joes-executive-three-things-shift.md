# Joe's Famous Executive: From 60-Hour Weeks to "Three Things That Make Everything Else Easier or Redundant"

## The Story

Joe coaches famous executives. One of them, when they started working together, was constantly in the to-do list, going from meeting to meeting nonstop, working 60-70 hours a week. By conventional measures the executive was doing well -- the company was growing at 10-12% per year. By the standard of "is this person succeeding?" the answer was clearly yes.

By the standards of how the executive was actually living his days, the picture was different. The 60-70 hours were full of meetings the executive was running on adrenaline. The to-do list was the structure organizing the days. The growth was being produced, but at a cost the executive was paying personally.

The coaching shifted the question. Instead of "how do I get this done?" the question became: "what are the three things I can do that make everything else either easier or irrelevant?"

The executive started organizing his days around that question. Three things a day. The three highest-leverage things. Just those.

Two things changed. The first was structural: the to-do list became, in the executive's words, "the most high-leverage stuff." The second was felt: the executive felt better day-to-day. The dopamine of checking off many medium-leverage items is a kind of fulfillment, but the felt sense of doing something that you know will make a substantial difference is a different, larger kind of fulfillment. The executive got access to the second.

The downstream result: the company grew. Not just maintained -- grew. The shift to three high-leverage things did not slow the growth that the 60-70 hour week had been producing; it accelerated it.

"I had this one executive who was constantly in the to-do list, just going from meeting to meeting nonstop, working 60, 70 hours a week and doing pretty good, actually. The company was growing pretty well, like 10, 12% a year. We talked about a different way of doing time management, a different way of doing a to-do list. And he started thinking about his days as, what are the 3 things that I can do that make everything else either easier or irrelevant? And that's all he would focus on, just getting those 3 things done. And so all of a sudden his to-do list was the most high-leverage stuff. And there's a fulfillingness to like checking off a to-do list. But man, do you feel great if you've done something that you know will make a substantial difference?" -- Joe Hudson, Your To-Do List is Just Your Inner Critic on Paper

"Not only is it super effective at changing a company and his company grew, but it also felt better on a day-to-day basis." -- Joe Hudson, Your To-Do List is Just Your Inner Critic on Paper

## The Point Joe Uses It For

The story is the worked example for [[make-it-redundant-not-just-done]]. The general principle is "ask which three things would make everything else easier or redundant." The executive story is the demonstration that the principle works at the level of a person whose actual job is to run a growing company at high stakes.

The story also operates as a counter-argument to two common objections to the principle:

1. **"If I cut back on activity, the results will suffer."** The executive's results did not suffer. They improved. The 60-70 hours had been producing 10-12% growth. The three-things-a-day version produced more growth. The objection is empirically wrong in this case.

2. **"I'd feel like I wasn't doing enough."** The executive did not feel like he was doing less. He felt like he was doing more meaningful work. The fulfillment of doing three things-that-matter was larger than the fulfillment of doing many things-that-add-up. The objection assumes the wrong fulfillment metric.

## Why This Example Lands

The example lands because it covers both the productivity-result variable and the felt-experience variable. Most productivity advice optimizes for one and damages the other. Either it produces more results at higher cost (the 60-70 hour version) or it produces less results at lower cost (a vacation, a sabbatical). The executive story is the rare case where both improved -- more results and lower cost. The structural reason is in the principle: high-leverage work is also high-meaning work, and high-meaning work is also high-energy work. The three variables move together when the leverage is real.

The credibility of the example also matters. The executive is a real person with a real growing company. Joe is not theorizing; he is reporting on a coaching engagement with an observable outcome. The "10-12% growth" datum is specific. The "60-70 hours" datum is specific. The "company grew" datum is specific. The example is empirically grounded in a way that distinguishes it from generic productivity wisdom.

## How This Connects to the Larger Teaching

The story is positioned in the to-do-list-as-inner-critic teaching. The conventional to-do list the executive started with was the inner critic's list -- many items, all "have to" framed, organizing the days from meeting to meeting on adrenaline. See [[to-do-list-as-inner-critic-on-paper]]. The reframe to three high-leverage items is the move toward a list that works for the person rather than against them. The list was still a list; the items in it had changed; the relationship to it had changed.

The example also implicitly demonstrates [[plan-how-not-just-what]]. The shift was not just from many items to three items; it was from "execute the items the critic listed" to "select the items that will produce real leverage." The selection is part of the how. The how-shift is what produced the experiential improvement on top of the productivity improvement.

The example also connects to [[how-could-you-do-this-and-gain-energy]] -- the executive's days became energy-producing rather than energy-draining once the work was focused on high-leverage items. The body's reading at the end of the day shifted from depleted to invigorated. See [[efficiency-as-enjoyment]] for the structural reason this matters as a productivity metric.

## What the Story Does Not Say

The story is not an argument that all executives should work shorter hours. The point was not the hours; the point was the leverage. If the three highest-leverage things take 60 hours, the executive should do those for 60 hours. The shift was about what filled the hours, not how many hours there were.

The story is also not an argument that the executive's prior 60-70 hours were wasted. The hours had produced real growth. The reframe found a way to produce more growth with the same or fewer hours, but the prior hours were not nothing. They were just configured wrong.
