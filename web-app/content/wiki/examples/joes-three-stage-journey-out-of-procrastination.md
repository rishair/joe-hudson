# Joe's Three-Stage Journey Out of Procrastination

## What This Example Captures

Joe describes himself as someone who used to procrastinate all the time and no longer does — to the point where Brett, who has known him for most of a decade, has never observed Joe procrastinating once. The transformation was not produced by discipline, scheduling, or productivity systems. It was produced by understanding the structure of procrastination across three successive insights, each of which dissolved a different layer of what had been holding the pattern in place.

> "The more I understood it, the less procrastination happened. So for me, the journey of not procrastinating anymore wasn't under — was just simply to understand it." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The example is useful as a coaching reference because it stages the work. People who try to end procrastination by doing all three insights at once usually fail at all of them. Joe's actual sequence shows that each insight enables the next: the emotional-avoidance reframe makes iteration available, iteration makes welcoming judgment possible, and the welcoming of judgment closes the loop on the perfectionism that the iteration alone could not fully resolve.

## Stage One: Procrastination Is Emotional Avoidance

The first insight Joe arrived at: procrastination is not about the task. It is about avoiding the emotional state of doing the task. The body refuses to engage with work that feels like crap, and the felt-state is what is being avoided, not the work itself.

> "It is really the avoidance of an emotional state. That's actually what procrastination is. It's not not doing something. It's not wanting to feel a particular way." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The intervention that followed from this insight: change the emotional state of the doing rather than try to force the doing. The operational question became "what do I have to do to make this enjoyable?" — usually some form of non-judgmentalness, playfulness, or letting go of perfectionism. See [[redirect-from-doing-it-right-to-enjoying-it]] for the canonical form of this move and [[importance-makes-the-task-un-fun]] for the deeper principle on why the importance-loading produces the dread that makes the task feel like crap.

The structural unlock: once procrastination is seen as emotional avoidance, the question of "how do I make myself do it?" is replaced by the question "what emotion am I trying to avoid, and how do I shift the felt-state of doing it?" The new question is answerable. The old question was a trap.

## Stage Two: The Iterative Mindset

The second insight built on the first. Even after Joe understood procrastination as emotional avoidance, certain kinds of work still produced dread — particularly work where the standard for what "good" looked like was loaded with importance, perfection, or stakes. The iterative mindset dissolved this layer.

> "The job isn't to get it done on a timeline, the job is to iterate on it. So knowing that I can't particularly make a mistake, where I can't particularly fail, where this is just an iPhone 1, then there's an iPhone 2, this is the Connection Course 1, this is the Connection Course 2, we're just constantly iterating to make it better and better and better and better and better and never an expectation that it's supposed to be perfect or good enough." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The shift: do the first pass like a three-year-old playing. Become the exacting editor only on subsequent passes. This is how Joe learned to be consistently creative without procrastination. The first pass is not where the quality lives; the iteration is where the quality lives. Refusing to make the first pass because it might be bad is the mechanism that prevents the quality from ever arriving.

> "You do your first pass of something like you're a 3-year-old playing. And then you become like an exacting editor of every pass after." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The structural unlock: the iterative frame removes "failure" from the experience entirely. The first pass cannot fail; it's an iteration. The second pass cannot fail; it's an iteration. The accumulated iterations produce the quality outcome by the same mechanism that prevented the standalone first attempt from being achievable. See [[iterative-mindset]] for the full concept.

Joe's note on the podcast itself as an example: the early Connection Course episodes are not what they would do now. The accumulated iterations produced the current quality. Without the willingness to release the early ones, there would be no current ones.

## Stage Three: Look Forward to Being Judged

The third insight closed the remaining gap. After emotional-avoidance was understood and the iterative mindset was in place, what was left was the fear of judgment. Not the imagined critic in the head — that was a downstream feature — but the actual judgment of other people that would arrive when work was shipped. The unlock here was to reframe judgment not as a threat but as the door to freedom and refinement.

> "You can get a mindset shift, but you can't stop people from judging you. However, what you can do is you can actually look forward to the judgment. And that's like, that's amazing." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The mechanism Joe describes has two parts. First, if a particular judgment triggers him, that trigger reveals a place where there is freedom to be found — by feeling what is underneath, the tweak releases. Second, judgment that doesn't trigger him is still useful as discernment: it shows him where his communication isn't landing, where he's missing his audience, where his thinking isn't reaching the people he wants to reach. Either way, the judgment becomes information he wants rather than a danger he avoids.

> "If somebody triggers me with their judgment, then there's some freedom that I know I can find. So somebody judges me and I have no problem with it. Somebody else judges me and it tweaks me. Because if I get triggered, I'm going to learn something. Then what on earth would make me scared of being judged? Like, freedom." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

> "It's like hitting bad notes when you're practicing the guitar... I can't actually do an art form without judgment and get better. Either my own or somebody else's. Discernment, I think, is better than judgment. But yeah, so I look forward to it." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

See [[look-forward-to-judgment]] for the move that operationalizes this in coaching and [[letting-judgment-in]] for the related teaching that uses heartbreak as the door to dissolving the fear of judgment.

The structural unlock: the fear of judgment was the residual lock on shipping. Once judgment became something to look forward to — as either trigger-revealing-freedom or discernment-revealing-refinement — the cost of shipping dropped to near zero. The iterative work could iterate publicly, which is the only way the iteration actually produces refinement.

## The Final Layer: When the Critical Voice Dissolved

A fourth element Joe names, less as a separate stage and more as the resolution that the first three converge toward. When the negative self-talk in his head simply stopped being his dominant inner voice, the experience of procrastination disappeared entirely as a category.

> "When I stopped beating myself up... as that negative self-talk in my voice, the voice in my head with that negative self-talk, just did not become this repetitive negative self-talk in my head, as that disappeared, all of a sudden, like, there was just no idea of procrastination. And when that occurred, then all of a sudden I could see things and go, oh crap, I'm not doing that. What makes me not do that?" -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The first three insights converge here. Emotional-avoidance work softened the dread. The iterative mindset removed the failure category that was feeding the inner critic. The welcome-judgment move dissolved the fear of external judgment that was feeding the internal critic. With all three operating, the inner critic had no source material. Without source material, the voice quieted. Without the voice, "procrastination" as an experience no longer happened, because procrastination requires self-abuse and the self-abuse was no longer present (see [[procrastination-requires-self-abuse]]).

What replaced the procrastination experience: clear perception of what was and wasn't being done, with quiet investigation about why. Not "what's wrong with me?" but "what makes me not do that?" — asked from curiosity, not from attack. From that question, the actual first domino became visible. People who do not procrastinate, Joe observes, are better at picking the right first domino — not because they are more disciplined, but because the absence of the inner critic frees them to perceive where the actual leverage is.

## Why This Sequencing Matters for Coaches

The three insights are not interchangeable steps that can be taken in any order. The sequence Joe describes is structural:

1. **Emotional-avoidance reframe** has to come first because without it, the person is still trying to use willpower against the not-doing. Recognizing the dread as the actual obstacle makes the right kind of work possible.

2. **Iterative mindset** comes second because without it, the emotional-avoidance work has nowhere to land. If the standard remains "do it right the first time," even the felt-state work cannot save the task. The iterative frame removes the standard the emotional dread was protecting against.

3. **Welcome judgment** comes third because the first two get the person to the point of producing work; the third gets them to the point of shipping it. Without this insight, the iteration stays in the drawer.

Coaches working with chronic procrastination can use this sequence as a diagnostic. Where is the client in the sequence? If they are still seeing procrastination as a discipline problem, they have not done stage one. If they have done stage one but cannot iterate because every attempt feels final, they need stage two. If they iterate fine but cannot ship because they fear external judgment, they need stage three. The intervention is whichever stage is currently the lock.

## The Frame That Sits Around This

Joe's framing of his own arc is non-pathologizing. He does not describe his past procrastination as a defect he overcame. He describes it as a structure he came to understand. The framing matters because the alternative framing — "I used to be broken, I fixed myself" — would itself install the self-attack that the work was supposed to dissolve. The arc is a story of understanding, not of repair.

> "So that really is how it all unfolded for me. But yeah, you're right. I never feel like I'm procrastinating. I feel like I prioritize all the time, but I do not feel like I'm procrastinating really ever." -- Joe Hudson, I Don't Procrastinate Anymore

The replacement experience is not "I am disciplined now." It is "the category of procrastination no longer applies to my experience." The same person might still drop balls, delay things, decide not to do something — but without the self-attack, the experience of those events is prioritization, not procrastination. See [[prioritization-vs-procrastination]] for the distinction.

## When to Use This Example

- When a coachee is trying to solve procrastination with discipline-side interventions and needs to see that the problem is structural, not motivational
- When a coachee believes they have to "fix" all of procrastination at once and needs the staged framing
- When a coachee has done stage one work (felt the emotion) but is still stuck because they have not yet done stage two (iteration) or stage three (welcoming judgment)
- When a coachee is despairing that "this can't be done" and needs an autobiographical reference that it can be
- When a coachee has internalized the procrastinator identity and needs an example of someone who no longer holds it

## Tone

Reflective, observational, autobiographical without being indulgent. Joe describes his own arc the way one describes a path one has walked — useful as reference for others rather than as a claim about specialness. The lightness in the delivery matters: the journey did not require any heroic effort or breakthrough, just successive understandings that each made the next one possible.
