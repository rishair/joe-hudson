# Savannah: "How Do I Stop Being Misunderstood?"

## The Session

A live coaching session that arcs through every move in the self-listening teaching in one continuous demonstration. Savannah arrives with the surface concern: "I would like to do better with feeling misunderstood. When I make all attempts and use the best communication hygiene I possibly can, and the other party seems determined to take different meaning than what I'm giving... I struggle a lot with that." Joe runs the full arc — surfacing the cost of chasing, inverting the responsibility, demonstrating state with the [[say-the-same-sentence-five-ways]] move, naming the second layer of hurt, the 10% self-listening shift, and the inner-ear hack — and the session lands with the childhood origin of the pattern.

The session is also a clean demonstration of the meta-principle: a coachee who came in asking for communication advice was given no communication advice. The work happened entirely in her body, in the room, through small somatic instructions. The advice she did not get is what would have prevented the work from happening.

## What She Came In Saying

"I would like to do better with feeling misunderstood. When I make all attempts and use the best communication hygiene I possibly can, and the other party seems determined to take different meaning than what I'm giving... I struggle a lot with that in many areas of my life, whether that's professionally, in my relationship, in interpersonal relationships."

The signature phrases: "I tend to fall all over myself to try to correct that misunderstanding." "If I'm falling all over myself, I'm at least still trying. So that still gives me the illusion of control."

She used the word *illusion* unprompted in the first few minutes. The pattern was already partly visible to her. She just did not know what to do with it.

## The Arc Joe Ran

1. **Question the goal.** "What's the problem with being misunderstood?"
2. **Surface the cost of the chasing.** "What would happen if you didn't fall all over yourself?" — produced her own naming of "a panic room of concrete blocks of anxiety."
3. **Invert the responsibility.** "What makes it your job to be understood? What makes it not my job to try to understand you? Clear to who?"
4. **Confront the lack of control.** "How much control do you have over people's capacity to understand you?"
5. **Stage the dynamic live.** "Make it my responsibility to understand you" — she immediately produced the chasing pattern in the room and could feel it.
6. **Name the loop.** "The way it's being received is, someone's trying to control me. And when someone controls you, how do you respond?"
7. **Name the two hurts.** "There's almost two levels of hurt when someone doesn't understand you. The first level of hurt is that you don't feel understood. The second level of hurt is like... you don't feel like the goodness in which you bring is being recognized." Her response: "Bro, how can you see me like that?"
8. **Run [[say-the-same-sentence-five-ways]].** Five deliveries of the same exact words — armor, ego-protected, not protected, vulnerable, upright. The same words landed five different ways.
9. **Pivot to self-listening.** "How deeply are you listening to yourself?" She immediately produced the diagnosis: "I have a tendency to fully abandon myself when I'm interacting with someone else."
10. **The 10% instruction.** "Listen to yourself just 10% more. Same thing. Just, but listen to yourself 10% more as you're saying it." The shift was immediate. See [[listen-to-yourself-ten-percent-more]].
11. **Name what makes the listening hard.** "What makes you talk about things you don't care about?" — surfacing that self-listening is easy with things you love and hard with things you do not. The hardness is data.
12. **The principle.** "You can't fucking be seen or heard when you're not in yourself. How could I ever see you?" See [[you-cant-be-seen-when-youre-not-in-yourself]].
13. **Show that being-heard is already available.** "Focus on my listening to you. Notice how it feels a lot like listening to yourself." She noticed: "There's not like an outcome in mind. It's just like you pay attention to the details more."
14. **Catch the "how do I do it better" move.** When Savannah asked "how do I listen to myself better?" Joe caught it: "Better is just a way to please. It's just like, maybe it's not even how do I listen to it, it's just like, oh, you know, I'm listening." See [[dont-make-listening-a-morality]].
15. **The inner ear hack.** "Put your attention in your inner ear. I'll talk and that's it. That's all you have to do. Super easy." See [[put-attention-in-inner-ear]].
16. **The childhood origin.** "You think you got taught sometime in an early time that your happiness was dependent on other people's reactions. And the person whose reaction you thought your happiness was dependent on was having reactions for reasons that were completely not about you." See [[self-abandonment-to-manage-the-other]].
17. **The dialogue between listening and the brain.** "I want your listening to speak to your brain." Her response: "Bro, my listening is exasperated, turns out. Just a brief check-in lets me know that my listening is exasperated with my brain. Brain goes brrr all the time." Including her own question — "is that why people can't understand me? Am I exhausting?" — and Joe's honest answer: "A little bit."
18. **Acknowledge the natural return.** "You just, without even trying, without even the thought, I need to listen to myself, you just naturally tuned in, checked in with yourself, felt yourself." The body had already learned what to do.

## What Made the Session Land

The session relied on three things that Joe consistently does in this teaching:

- **Refusing the communication-advice frame.** Savannah came in asking for a tool. She did not get a tool. She got a redirection of attention. Every time she tried to convert the teaching back into a technique ("how do I do this in writing?" "how do I listen better?"), Joe caught the conversion and redirected.

- **Demonstrating everything in the room.** Nothing was explained that could be felt. The chasing was demonstrated. The state-shift was demonstrated. The listening was demonstrated. The seeing was demonstrated through Joe naming what he saw. Each demonstration produced a felt recognition that an explanation could not have produced.

- **Naming the childhood origin only at the end.** The deeper teaching about the pattern's origin was given after the body had already felt the return. If it had come at the start, it would have been an intellectual frame. Coming at the end, it landed as recognition.

## The Memorable Moments

- "Bro, how can you see me like that?" — the moment the second-layer hurt was named. The crack in the defense.
- The five deliveries of the same sentence. "Both of those two make my skin want to crawl. I'm fucking out the door." — the somatic feedback that made the state-difference audible.
- "Better is just a way to please." — Savannah's spontaneous insight when Joe declined to give her a "how to listen better" instruction.
- "Bro, my listening is exasperated, turns out." — the moment self-listening became spontaneous and produced its own diagnosis.
- "It's not me." — Savannah recognizing the experience of being out of herself, in three words.
- "Am I exhausting?" "A little bit." — the moment of clean honesty that integrated rather than wounded.
- "Frasier Crane. I'm listening." — the cultural reference she reached for to give the listening practice a memorable shape.
- "It's like helium." — her body description of what tuning into herself produced in her chest after the practice had been internalized.

## What This Is Not

This is not a script for handling the misunderstanding concern. Every coachee is different. The moves Joe ran with Savannah landed because they matched what was happening in her body in real time. With a different person, the order would be different, the moves would be different, the timing would be different.

What is consistent across the teaching is the orientation: do not give communication advice. Bring the speaker back to themselves. Trust the body to know what to do once it is back.
