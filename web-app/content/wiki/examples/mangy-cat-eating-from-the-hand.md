# The Mangy Cat Eating From the Hand

## The Story

Joe's daughter encountered a mangy cat living in the compost pile. Over time, the cat became part of the family. She did not get there by picking up the cat and squeezing it and saying "I love you so much, why aren't you different." She got there by spending three weeks getting the cat to eat out of her hand.

Joe's compressed form:

"My daughter, she had this cat. It was a mangy-ass, living-in-the-compost-pile cat, and it became a part of the family. She didn't pick it up and say, I love you, and squeeze you and hug you so much, and why aren't you different, and da da da da. She spent 3 weeks getting the cat to eat out of her hand." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

## What Joe Uses This For

The example is deployed when explaining how to be with a wounded part of the self. The person has identified a part that is scared, traumatized, or shut down — often the inner child, often the part that learned to dissociate or mask. The standard impulse is to love-bomb the part: hold it, reassure it, tell it everything is fine, demand that it open up because love is here now.

The cat example reveals the standard impulse as the problem. The mangy cat did not need to be squeezed. The squeezing would have made it run. What it needed was time, patience, and a hand held out without demand. The wounded part of a person is the same.

The example does this work in one image:

1. **It identifies the wounded part as legitimately wounded.** The cat is mangy and lives in the compost pile. The person's scared part is similarly not pretending. The pretense that the part should already be okay is what fails.

2. **It identifies the standard "love" response as the problem.** Picking up and squeezing is not love when delivered to a creature that has been wounded. It is the wounder's preferred form of love — fast, satisfying to the giver, retraumatizing to the receiver.

3. **It provides the actual move.** Three weeks of patient hand-extending. The hand is offered. The cat decides when to approach. The cat's pace governs the timeline.

4. **It removes the "have to" frame from self-love.** The daughter did not have to do this. She got to do this. The cat was an amazing creature. The patience was an act of relationship, not duty.

## When Joe Uses This Example

The example lands when:

- The person has identified a wounded internal part (often the inner child, the part that learned to dissociate, the part that masks)
- They are asking "how do I love this part" and the implicit framing is "how do I produce love quickly so the part heals"
- The person's instinct is to demand the wounded part change rather than to be with it
- The person is using the language of obligation about self-love ("I need to," "I have to learn to")
- The work requires a model of patient, non-demanding presence

In the source session, the example arrived in response to the client's question: "So as I disassociate with my body, in mind and in tune, like, I need to sit with all those emotions that arise?" The "need to" was the entry point. The example replaced the "need to" with "get to."

## The "You Don't Need To, You Get To" Reframe

The example is paired with a specific reframe that completes its function:

"You don't need to. You get to. My daughter, she had this cat..." — Joe Hudson, Watch him learn to feel his emotions in real-time

"You get to — you get to be with that, like, amazing cat."

The reframe converts the work from obligation to opportunity. The wounded part is not a burden the person has to manage. It is an amazing cat the person gets to be in relationship with. The shift is structurally similar to [[i-get-to-vs-i-have-to]] — but applied specifically to the relationship with the wounded internal part.

The reframe matters because the obligation frame produces the same dynamic the cat example warns against. The person feels they have to make the wounded part heal, which produces the squeezing energy, which makes the wounded part retreat. The "get to" frame removes the urgency. The wounded part can take whatever time it needs.

## What This Example Is Not

- It is not a teaching about cats or about the daughter. The example is structural. The cat is the wounded internal part. The daughter is the present-day self. The compost pile is wherever the wounded part has been living. The three weeks is the timeline the wounded part requires.
- It is not a recommendation to ignore the wounded part. The cat got fed every day. The hand was extended every day. The daughter was present with the cat. The patience is not absence; it is sustained presence without demand.
- It is not advice to wait for healing to happen on its own. The daughter was active in the relationship. She showed up daily. She held out her hand. The work was patient and consistent. What she did not do was demand a specific timeline or outcome.

## The Mechanism the Example Illustrates

A wounded part of the self has been hurt by demand and force. When demand and force show up again — even disguised as love — the wounded part recognizes the energy and retreats. The wound is in part the memory of being demanded of when the system was not ready.

Patient presence is structurally different. The wounded part can scan it and not detect demand. With nothing to retreat from, the part can begin to approach. The approach is on the part's timeline, not the present-day self's.

This is why love-bombing the inner child does not work. The love is real. The bombing is the problem. The inner child has been bombed before. The body recognizes the bombing energy and retreats, even though the bomber's intent is love.

The example makes this mechanism legible in the simplest possible form. A cat and a hand. Anyone can imagine the cat that runs from the squeeze and approaches the patient hand. The recognition is instant. The application to the inner part follows on its own.

## The Story Is About Joe's Daughter Specifically

The detail that the cat was Joe's daughter's cat — not Joe's, not a generic example — matters. The daughter is a child. The cat is being met by a child. The simplicity of the child's approach is part of the teaching: the work does not require sophisticated technique. It requires the simplest possible action — show up, extend the hand, don't grab — repeated daily.

The adult who is trying to repair their relationship with their inner child often imports adult complexity into the work. Therapy modalities, parts language, ritual structures. The cat example deflates the complexity. A child with a cat and three weeks of patience. That is what works.
