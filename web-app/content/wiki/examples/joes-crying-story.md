# Joe Learning to Cry

## The Story

In his early twenties, Joe was looking through a photo album and saw a picture of himself as a child crying. The same photo showed him looking dumbfounded that someone was taking a picture of him while he was upset.

He realized: his parents had been uncomfortable with his emotions because they had been taught to be uncomfortable with their own. One of the ways they handled his crying was to tease him about it. Taking the photo was part of the teasing.

"I was like, ah, that's probably why I haven't cried in 14 years." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

He put the photo on his desk and decided to learn how to cry.

## How the Parents Handled the Crying

In the longer retellings Joe specifies the specific moves his parents used. They were the family-folkway form of training emotional containment into the kid:

- They made fun of him for crying.
- They threw "pity parties" — sarcastic small-group reactions to his tears.
- They invoked the "world's smallest violin" gesture.
- They took photos of him while he was crying to use as ongoing material for the teasing.

The photo Joe later found on his desk was an artifact of move four. The face in the photo — at once disgusted and surprised that someone would take a picture of him while he was crying — is the visible record of a child registering that the family was making the tears unsafe in a new way.

The structural detail Joe later adds: his parents were uncomfortable with his tears because they had been trained to be uncomfortable with their own. The shaming was the inherited form being passed through. The transmission mechanism: kids cry, kids' tears bother adults who were not allowed to cry, adults shame the kids' tears, kids learn to shame their own tears, kids become adults bothered by the next generation's tears.

"My parents were bothered by crying, so they made fun of me. They threw pity parties, the world's smallest violin. They even took pictures of me to make fun of me while I was crying." -- Joe Hudson, The Physical Price of Emotional Control

The specificity matters for coaching use. A client whose family ridiculed their crying recognizes the pattern in this version of the story more readily than in the abstract "parents who were uncomfortable with emotion." The "world's smallest violin" lands as a familiar move for many adults whose tears were shamed in similar ways.

## The Airplane Test: How to Spot Who Wasn't Allowed to Cry

Joe surfaces a sharp diagnostic for the same pattern at scale. On an airplane, when a baby is crying, you can stand up and look around. The adults whose faces are tightening are almost always the adults who were not allowed to cry as children.

"You could just tell when somebody wasn't allowed to cry, because if you were in an airplane and a kid was crying, you could stand up, turn around, look at everybody's faces, and whoever was bothered was a kid that wasn't allowed to cry." -- Joe Hudson, The Physical Price of Emotional Control

The test works because the adult's discomfort with the baby's tears is the somatic memory of the adult's own tears being unsafe. The crying baby's sound is an unmediated emotional expression. The adult who learned to clamp their own version of that sound cannot hear the baby's version without the clamping re-engaging. The discomfort registers in the face, the posture, the impulse to make the parent do something.

The conscious adult version is often heard as "that kid needs to be quiet" or "those parents should have prepared better." The actual content is the unfelt childhood material activating. The other adults — the ones who were allowed to cry as kids — usually look at the baby with sympathy or neutrality. The sound does not threaten them because their own version of it was not shamed.

The test is useful operationally in two directions:

- **Self-diagnostic.** Notice your own reactions to babies crying in public. The intensity of discomfort is the rough measure of how much your own tears were suppressed.
- **Reading others.** The observable face-tightening, eye-rolling, throat-clearing reactions in adults around the crying baby are reads on their own emotional history. This is a public-context instance of the broader principle [[emotions-live-in-muscles]] — the body broadcasts the suppression history continuously.

## The First Attempt

A year passed. He had not cried. The photo had not worked.

So he committed to a more deliberate effort. He drove to a remote trail, walked in, then walked three miles off-trail so that nobody could possibly see him. He was carrying that much shame about crying.

Then he faked it. He just sat there and faked crying. He did this for three months.

"I would just fake it. And I did that for like 3 months." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

## The Turn

Eventually it became real. The fake crying gave the body a track to run on, and the real material found its way out.

When it became real, the relief was so great that he let it continue for four days. He cried while brushing his teeth. He could have stopped it, but his body had been waiting fourteen years and was not about to stop now. In one of the later retellings, the duration is given as "almost like it was maybe 4 or 5 days straight."

"My body was just like, years of tension just evaporating in days." -- Joe Hudson, Feel over Figure

The agency point matters and shows up in the later retellings explicitly: Joe was not taken over by the crying. He checked in with himself after the first hour, asked whether he was getting lost in it, noticed he felt better than an hour earlier, and decided to keep going. The decision to continue was a present-tense choice he kept renewing, not a loss of control.

"I remember after the first hour, I was like, 'Oh my gosh, I'm going to get lost in this.' And then I stopped and I was like, 'Oh no, I feel better than I did an hour ago. I'm gonna keep doing this.' And so I just kept crying. And every couple hours I check in, I'm like, 'Yep, I'm still feeling better.' And so I just cried for days." -- Joe Hudson, The Physical Price of Emotional Control

The check-in detail is operationally useful as a teaching artifact. The story preempts the predictable worry — "if I let myself cry, I'll never stop" — by showing what the relationship-to-the-emotion looks like from inside. The crying was happening; Joe was also there, aware, capable of stopping, choosing to continue because the body was getting better. This is what the third option ([[feel-emotions-without-being-taken-by-them]]) looks like when an emotion is allowed to move all the way through.

The public-facing video version of the same arrival, told in plainer language, lands the emotional payoff for an audience that may not yet trust the work:

"And when I was finished, I felt so much relief. I felt so good. And you can ask anybody that you know who cries easily how good they feel after tears. It's an amazing discovery to feel that actually crying makes you feel better. And by learning not to cry, you're basically learning how not to feel better." -- Joe Hudson, 4 Ways To Feel Your Emotions

The "learning how not to feel better" inversion does specific work for a beginner. The cultural picture treats crying as the bad outcome (weakness, loss of control) and not-crying as the good outcome (composure, strength). The inversion names what the body actually does: stopping the tears stops the relief. The person who has spent decades not crying is not protecting themselves from sadness; they are protecting themselves from the relief that comes after the sadness has moved. Once the body knows that crying produces relief, the resistance has less to do.

## What Followed

Once he had access to tears, that access deepened. Then it deepened again. Eventually he arrived at: "Every single heartbreak of mine increases my capacity to love. I cannot wait for more heartbreak."

The thing that he could not access at all in his twenties became something he sought.

## What Changed in the Body

In the later retelling, Joe adds a specific report about what his body did after the four days of crying — and the broader observation that this is what happens to most people when they begin expressing emotions for the first time.

"My whole world changed after that. How I held my body changed, how I related to people changed because I was allowing myself to feel an emotion instead of holding it back. I had so much more energy. So oftentimes when people start to feel these emotions for the first time or express these emotions for the first time, they're just like, 'wow, my hands feel different. My arms feel different. My head feels different' because they're not holding anymore. Chronically holding." -- Joe Hudson, The Physical Price of Emotional Control

The body-change report is its own data. The fourteen-year cry-suppression had been a chronic muscular project the whole time — not just felt as inability-to-cry but distributed across the whole body as holding. Four days of crying interrupted the project. The muscles that had been doing the holding stopped doing it. The energy that had been going into the holding became available. The relating-to-other-people changed because the body that does the relating was now in a different posture.

This connects to [[emotions-live-in-muscles]] as the structural mechanism. The hands-feel-different / arms-feel-different / head-feels-different observation is what the unlocked body reports when chronic holding releases. It is a common report from people new to this work and useful as a marker that confirms the work is landing.

## What This Story Illustrates

1. **[[childhood-imprinting]]:** Parents who could not handle their own emotions taught him to hide his. The photo was a visible artifact of the programming.
2. **Faking can prime feeling.** In the early stages, especially when an emotion has been suppressed for years, the body may not know the shape of the motion. Faking gives it the shape.
3. **[[daily-emotional-yoga]]:** The three months in the woods was effectively daily emotional yoga. Time. Privacy. Permission. Repetition.
4. **The shame around emotion is itself programmed.** Joe walked three miles off-trail because of the shame, not because of any real risk of being seen. The shame was the programming asserting itself.
5. **[[stages-of-emotional-development]]:** Stage 1 to stage 2 in action. From "I don't even know I have these emotions" to "I can let them move."
6. **[[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]]:** Fourteen years of unfelt material released in days once the door opened.

## The Condensed Live-Coaching Form

In a live coaching session with a person whose grief has been frozen for decades, Joe sometimes deploys the story in its most compressed form — a single sentence offered when the person predicts the cry will be very long. The compression preserves the operative element (long shutdown produces long release) while removing everything that would slow the moment down.

- **Person:** "I think it's going to be like a very long period of crying, like so many things."
- **Joe:** "Yeah. When I first cried after 14 years, I cried for 4 days straight."

*(Source: I Can't Feel Anything (Coaching Session With Master Coach))*

The sentence does specific work in the moment. The person was predicting the cry would be long and was using the prediction as part of the reason not to start. Joe's reply confirms the prediction is reasonable — long shutdowns do produce long releases — and removes the implicit fear that the length would mean something is wrong. The example is offered as a fellow-traveler note, not as a teaching. The brevity is part of the warmth.

Note also that Joe does not pivot to the longer story (the photo album, the woods, the three months of fake crying). The longer story would shift the focus from the person's about-to-arrive cry to Joe's biography. The single-sentence version stays focused on the person's experience and just gives one piece of evidence that what is coming is safe.

## The Parallel: Tara's 10-Day Cry at Vipassana

The same shape shows up in Tara Hawley's account of her own first deep somatic exploration. She was at a Vipassana retreat. She started bringing attention to the body. The held grief released — and the release lasted ten days.

"This was my experience when I started doing this, it was at a Vipassana meditation and I started doing this, just exploring the body through awareness, and all I could do was cry. I cried for 10 days nonstop." -- Tara Hawley, I started listening to my body and cried for 10 days

Joe's was four days after fourteen years of suppression. Tara's was ten days. The two stories taken together establish the structural pattern: extended grief release of multiple days is a normal phenomenon when held material that has been carried for years finally has access. The duration is not a problem. It is the body metabolizing what it has been carrying.

For the practitioner predicting the cry will be very long and using the prediction as a reason not to start, either story can serve as evidence that the long release is safe. Joe's is more compressed and works in live coaching. Tara's is operational for the person who is committing to a meditation retreat or another container where they will have days to give.

## When Joe Reaches for This Story

- When someone says they cannot access an emotion they want to feel.
- When someone reports they have not cried in years.
- When someone is embarrassed about feeling weird or fake while trying to feel.
- When the shame around emotional expression is itself the obstacle.
- When someone needs evidence that faking it is a legitimate starting point.
- When someone is predicting the cry will be very long and the prediction is being used as a reason not to start. The compressed form (just the 14-years/4-days line) is the form for this moment.

## Tone

Self-deprecating, warm. Joe tells this on himself. The three miles off-trail is the kind of detail that makes the story humane. He is not presenting himself as having arrived emotionally fluent from the start. He had to fake-cry alone in the woods to get here.
