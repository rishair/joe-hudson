# Two Three-Year-Olds Meeting

## The Example

Two three-year-olds meet each other and start playing. No sentences are exchanged. There is no need for them. The connection happens entirely without language — in the body, in the eye contact, in the shared attention to the play. The kids do not check whether they are being understood. They are simply with each other and the play emerges.

"Go check out two 3-year-olds meeting each other and playing. There's like, there's no sentences need to be exchanged. Most of our communication is non-verbal." -- Joe Hudson, I'm Not Responsible For How You Feel

The example is short, concrete, and verifiable. Anyone has seen it. The image of two small children meeting and playing wordlessly is in everyone's memory and can be summoned immediately. The example does not require explanation.

## The Point It Illustrates

The intellectual-connection-strategy is a learned behavior, not a structural requirement of connection. Humans connected before they had words. Children still do. The adult who says "I need to make sentences that make sense for everyone to connect with you" is not describing how connection works; they are describing the apparatus they installed to make connection feel safe.

The corollary: dropping the intellectual frame does not break the connection. It often is the connection. The two-year-olds are not less connected for being wordless; they are more connected, because the channel is direct (body to body, eye to eye) rather than mediated through the careful production of acceptable sentences.

The example is also a precise rebuttal to a specific story the person tells themselves: "I need words to be understood." The kids are understood. They understand each other. Words were not part of the operation. The story is not factually correct.

## When Joe Uses It

- When the person opens the session with an intellectual frame ("I need to make sentences that make sense for everyone to connect with you") and the intellectual frame is the very apparatus that is preventing the work.

- After [[whats-the-part-of-yourself-you-disconnect-to-connect-with-me]] has been asked and the first answer was the intellectual-connection strategy. The example refuses that answer as a valid description of how connection actually works.

- When the person is heavily verbally oriented and treats verbal articulation as the precondition for being received. The example shows them they were always receivable in other channels.

- When the work needs to make space for non-verbal communication and the person resists ("but how do I communicate without words?"). The example is the proof of concept the person already has internalized but has not been letting themselves use.

- Often as the bridge between the diagnostic question and the live wordless experiment. Joe asks the disconnection-for-connection question, the answer is "I need to use my logical intellectual mind," Joe deploys the two-three-year-olds example to refuse that frame, and then proposes the wordless experiment ("let's just be together without words for a couple minutes").

## What It Produces

**An immediate visceral recognition.** Everyone has seen two children meet wordlessly. The image arrives whole. The person does not have to reason their way to the recognition; the recognition is already in their body.

**A small smile, often.** The image is light. Two small kids meeting and playing is a happy image. The smile breaks the seriousness of the apparatus's claim that this is hard.

**The intellectual frame loses its load-bearing status.** The story "I need words to be understood" is no longer the operating principle. The example is the counter-evidence. The person can no longer use the verbal channel as the only access to connection without noticing that they are choosing to do so.

**An opening for the wordless invitation.** Once the example has done its work, the next move is often the live wordless experiment. The person has just been shown that wordless connection works; the invitation is to try it now.

## What Joe Does With the Response

- **If the person receives the example and softens:** Joe moves to the wordless experiment. "Let's just be together without words for a couple minutes and see if we can actually communicate."

- **If the person tries to qualify the example ("yeah but adults are different"):** Joe holds the frame lightly. The qualifier is the apparatus defending. He may simply re-ask the original question without engaging the qualifier.

- **If the person agrees verbally but does not soften in the body:** Joe may name the disconnect. The verbal agreement is the apparatus pretending to receive the example without actually receiving it.

- **If the person uses the example to relocate the question ("how do I learn to be like the kids?"):** Joe refuses the technique-frame. The kids are not doing a technique. They are just being. The relearning is releasing the apparatus, not acquiring a new skill.

## Dialogue Example

From the source session:

- **Person:** "I always try to connect first on a level, like on a logical intellectual level, because I need to make sentences that make sense for everyone."
- **Joe:** "No, you don't. That's not how the world works. Like, go check out two 3-year-olds meeting each other and playing. There's like, there's no sentences need to be exchanged. Most of our communication is non-verbal."

*(Source: I'm Not Responsible For He Feel)*

The structural detail: the example is delivered in two sentences. There is no expansion, no explanation, no elaboration. The example is its own complete argument. The brevity is part of its power — long explanations would themselves be the intellectual frame the example is meant to refuse.

## Why It Works

The example works because it is verifiable in the person's memory. Joe is not making a claim that has to be accepted on his authority. He is pointing to something the person has seen, something they can confirm by recalling. The recall does the work.

The example also works because it bypasses the apparatus the person is operating through. The apparatus is verbal: it makes sentences that make sense. The example is pre-verbal: it points to a form of connection that the apparatus cannot produce. The apparatus has no defense against an example that operates outside its frame.

The example is also generationally true. It is not a metaphor about a special case. It is descriptive of how connection actually works for most of human history and most of every adult's life. The verbal mediation the apparatus insists on is the exception, not the rule.

## Tone

Light, slightly amused. The example has a small smile in it. Joe is not lecturing about communication theory; he is pointing to something obvious. The tone is "look at this thing you've already seen."

The example often arrives in a slightly faster cadence than the surrounding conversation. The two sentences come out quickly, like a small punch — not heavy, not weighted, just direct. The speed prevents the person from re-engaging the apparatus to defend against the example before it has landed.

## What This Example Is Not

- It is not an argument that all communication should be wordless. Words are useful for many things. The example refutes the claim that connection requires words, not the claim that words can be a channel for connection.

- It is not a recommendation to act like a three-year-old. The example is about the structural fact of pre-verbal connection, not about regressing to childhood behavior.

- It is not a critique of intellectual people. The point is not that intellectualism is bad. The point is that the verbal channel is one of many, and treating it as the only channel for connection installs the apparatus that this concern is asking how to dismantle.
