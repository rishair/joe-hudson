# Lottery Winners Go Bankrupt

## The Point It Illustrates

When a person achieves something they could not own as a want, the system rejects the achievement. The thing arrives but cannot be held. The classic empirical illustration: nearly all lottery winners of large sums are bankrupt within a couple of years. The pattern generalizes — anyone who gets something they have an internal war with will lose it through "mistakes they were way too smart to have made."

This is the achievement-stage form of [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]] operating in reverse. When the want is owned, the achievement integrates. When the want was disowned, the achievement is structurally incompatible with the self that received it. The system corrects the incompatibility by ejecting the achievement.

## The Story

"It's something, I don't know exactly the thing, but it's 90, 100% of lottery winners of over $100 million are bankrupt inside of 2 or 3 years or something. Crazy like that. Right? It's because they were at war with having that billion dollars or $100 million or whatever it was. The war was in them. So of course it went away." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The lottery winner had to want money to buy the ticket and to want the win when it arrived. But for most lottery winners, the wanting was the kind that craves money from the outside, not the kind that owns it from the inside. The win lands on someone whose identity was structured around not-having. The new identity (having $100M) is foreign. The system corrects the foreign installation by returning the person to the identity that fits.

The mechanism is not stupidity, not bad luck, not undeserved windfall. It is the war with the want, manifesting after the want has been technically met. The internal "I am not okay to have this" produces the operational sequence that returns the person to a state in which they are not having it.

## The Pattern Beyond Lotteries

The pattern is not specific to lotteries. Joe sees it routinely with people he has worked with in business:

"I can count a dozen situations where somebody I know is worth over $100 million and, you know, went down to $5 million out of just mistakes that they were way too smart to have made, just because they've had an internal war with that level of success." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The marker is the phrase "mistakes they were way too smart to have made." When a person with deep business intelligence makes a series of decisions that an outsider could have spotted as catastrophic, the cause is rarely the decisions themselves. The cause is upstream of the decisions — in the war with the want that the success has triggered.

The same pattern appears in:
- **Famous-people implosions.** "Read People magazine and you'll see it. Oh look, that guy's worth $100 million and famous and has a beautiful wife and just imploded, right? It happens all the time."
- **Relationship blowups.** "I can't tell you how many people I know who have blown up a, what would be a great relationship because they don't feel like they deserve the love because they can't actually own the want."
- **Career self-sabotage** at the moment of arrival.
- **Promotion declined or hollowed out** when the new role exceeds the identity the person could own wanting.

In every case the structural shape is the same: the person achieved the surface thing, the surface thing exposed the underlying war with the want, and the war was won by the side that was rejecting the achievement.

## The Distinction From the Stable Famous Person

The pattern has a contrast case. Some people stay famous and happy across decades. The interview signature is different:

"The people who have been famous and happy for over 2 or 3 decades, they're pretty, like, when I see their interviews, they seem pretty clear on their wants as compared to the people who haven't, who like implode." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The stable-famous person can articulate their wants directly. They wanted the work, they wanted the success, they wanted the platform. The wanting was owned. The fame is therefore integrated rather than foreign. There is no war with the want for the achievement to expose.

This is the empirical version of the principle: clean owning of the want produces integration of what arrives; war with the want produces ejection of what arrives. The famous-and-happy and the famous-and-imploded are the two outputs of the same achievement input, sorted by the want-state of the receiver.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a client is approaching a level of success they have not owned wanting
- When a client is in the post-achievement phase and the achievement is starting to feel wrong
- When a client is describing wealthy or famous people who imploded
- When [[owning-the-want-dissolves-it]] needs an example that operates at the achievement-stage rather than at the want-statement-stage

## The Lottery Winners Example Also Appears In Money-Must-Flow

The lottery winners example appears in [[money-must-flow]] in a different framing — there it is about stagnant money's destructive quality and the structural problems of unrequested help (the lottery winner did not earn or request the money in a way that integrated it into their relational network). The two framings are compatible and add different dimensions.

In the war-with-the-want framing, the lottery winner is the demonstration that the want-state of the recipient determines whether the resource integrates. In the money-flow framing, the lottery winner is the demonstration that the relational structure of the resource (earned vs. given, requested vs. not) determines whether it can be held. Both are operating in the same case.

## The Coaching Implication

When a client is approaching success that exceeds what they have owned wanting, the work is to surface the want before the success arrives, not after. Once the success has arrived and is starting to leak away, the work is harder — the system is already in the implosion phase and the client is often in a defensive crouch about it.

The cleanest version of the work is preventative: as the client approaches the next level, check whether the want for the next level is owned. If it is not, do the want-work first. The win that arrives at a person who has owned the want for it is the win that integrates. The win that arrives at a person at war with the wanting is the win that leaks away.

See [[identity-must-shift-when-wants-are-owned]] for the structural reason this is the case — wants and identity are entangled, and an owned want at a new level is the same operation as a willingness to become a person who could have it.
