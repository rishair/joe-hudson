# Scottie Scheffler on Winning

## The Example

A reporter at a press conference asks Scottie Scheffler, the world's number-one golfer, why he seems so comfortable at the top when most of the players around him have been uncomfortable being number one. Scheffler answers without pretense:

"Sometimes the feeling only lasts about 2 minutes... and then it's like, okay, now you got to go do all this other stuff." -- Scottie Scheffler, golf press conference

He goes further. He won the Byron Nelson, hugged his family, then immediately wondered what to eat for dinner. The euphoria he had worked his whole life for evaporated in minutes. Asked about his hardest pushes -- the Masters, the Open Championship -- he says plainly:

"Why do I want to win this golf tournament so badly? Why do I want to win the Open Championship so badly? I don't know... if I win, it's gonna be awesome for about 2 minutes, and then we're gonna get to the next week... we work so hard for such little moments." -- Scottie Scheffler

He then says the line that names the structure exactly:

"It's fulfilling from the sense of accomplishment, but it's not fulfilling from a sense of like the deepest places of your heart." -- Scottie Scheffler

And asks aloud, in front of the cameras:

"I love being able to play this game for a living. It's one of the greatest joys of my life. But does it fill the deepest wants and desires of my heart? Absolutely not." -- Scottie Scheffler

The quote sequence is unusual because it comes from the person at the absolute top of his profession, on camera, while still actively chasing the next tournament. Most people who name the disguise have either stopped chasing or have not yet won. Scheffler is naming it from inside the chase.

## The Point It Illustrates

The example is a clean public demonstration of [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]]. The top performer in a domain is reporting the structure himself:

1. The joy is real.
2. The joy is short. Minutes, not days.
3. The chase resumes immediately. Reporters ask "what's next?" before the celebration is even cold.
4. The achievement does not fill the place it was supposed to fill.
5. The chaser knows this, names it publicly, and keeps chasing anyway.

This is the structural picture the principle describes, told from the inside by someone whose external position is exactly what most people use as the imagined finish line. The example removes the cope "well, when you actually get there, it will be different." The person who actually got there is saying it is not different.

The example also illustrates [[seen-through-the-disguise-of-success]]: Scheffler has noticed that the disguise is a disguise. He has not yet found what is on the other side. He sits in the in-between, articulating the trap publicly while still operating inside it. This is a recognizable stage. Many high-performing clients arrive in exactly this position.

## What Joe Does With This Example

In the source episode, Joe uses the Scheffler clip as a reaction-video teaching. The pattern of how Joe deploys the example:

1. **Plays a piece of the quote.** Lets it land. Does not interpret first.
2. **Names what is being said underneath.** "He's seen through the disguise of success." Then: "what he's saying is that no matter what, if you've succeeded in anything, the question is, well, then what are you gonna do next?"
3. **Generalizes the structure.** Connects to "this is how our head works, this is how people treat us from the outside." Pulls the example out of golf into the general human pattern.
4. **Names the joy-mechanism specifically.** Quotes Eckhart Tolle's framing: "the reason that winning feels so good is because for 1 or 2 minutes or 1 or 2 days, you're not thinking about what you have to do next. You're not thinking about what's the next thing that I have to do so that I can blah, blah, blah, blah."
5. **Connects to freedom.** Names that the structure being visible -- that everything comes and goes -- is what makes freedom possible. "Everything's futile is one way to say it... if that's true, then you have freedom."
6. **Names the moment-after.** What does the person do when they have seen the disguise but the next chase has not yet been chosen? Joe's answer: go fall in love with the big empty space that the chasing was trying to fill. See [[the-aloneness-is-the-quickest-route-to-freedom]] and [[fall-in-love-with-the-empty-space]].

The Scheffler example is particularly useful in coaching because it does the destabilizing work without Joe having to do it. A high-performing client who has trouble believing that "the achievement won't fill it" can hear it from a contemporary peer who is, by any metric, winning the game they themselves are trying to win.

## When Joe Reaches For This Example

- When a client is in the chase phase and intellectually rejects the principle ("but I haven't actually gotten there yet, you don't know what it will be like").
- When a client is post-achievement and is gaslighting themselves about why their joy is small ("I should be happier than this; what's wrong with me?").
- When teaching the joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants principle to an audience and needs a public-figure demonstration that the structure operates at the top.
- When a client is in [[seen-through-the-disguise-of-success]] territory and needs to know that this stage is real, recognizable, and is not the same as defeat.
- When teaching the structural difference between achievement-joy and building-joy -- Scheffler's "I'm kind of a sicko, I love putting in the work" line is the example of [[intrinsic-drive-to-build]] operating alongside the seen-through achievement-joy.

## Why It Lands When It Does

Most examples of "achievement doesn't fulfill" come from people who have walked away from the game. The CEO who quit. The artist who burned out. The billionaire who became a monk. These examples can be dismissed by the active striver: "those people stopped trying." Scheffler is actively trying. He is still in the chase. He is winning. And he is naming the structure on camera, mid-tournament, in front of reporters.

The example is therefore non-deniable in a way that retired-from-the-chase examples are not. The chaser cannot say "you only think that because you stopped." Scheffler did not stop.

A second feature of why this lands: Scheffler is not performing depth. He is not selling a book on inner work. He is not pivoting to a spiritual teaching career. He is a golfer answering a reporter's question and saying the obvious truth about what winning feels like. The lack of agenda is part of what makes the words land.

## The Building-Joy Alongside the Achievement-Joy

Scheffler also says, in the same press conference:

"I love the challenge. I love being able to play this game for a living. It's one of the greatest joys of my life... I love putting in the work. I love being able to practice. I love getting out to live out my dreams." -- Scottie Scheffler

This part of the quote is doing a different piece of teaching. The achievement-joy is short and disappointing. The building-joy -- the actual playing, practicing, engaging -- is real and renewing. The two are structurally different. See [[joy-of-accomplishment-is-the-absence-of-wants]] for the full distinction.

Scheffler is reporting both. The chase doesn't fulfill. The doing is one of the greatest joys of his life. The two coexist. This is the picture of someone whose engine is not entirely the achievement-engine; the building-joy is also running, which is part of what allows him to keep playing without collapsing into the disillusionment of the achievement-joy alone.

When using the example in coaching, do not flatten this. The Scheffler quote is not "achievement is hollow, walk away." It is "achievement is hollow, the doing is alive, both are true, the meaning has to be located in the right place." This is more useful and more honest than the renunciation version.

## What the Coach Listens For

When a client mentions Scheffler or the press conference or "that golfer who said the feeling only lasts two minutes," the move is to honor the recognition. The client has done part of the work just by importing this example into their own awareness. Do not over-explain. Sit with the recognition. Then ask the question Joe asks at the end of the example: what does it look like to enter the big empty space the chasing has been trying to fill, instead of finding the next thing to fill it with?
