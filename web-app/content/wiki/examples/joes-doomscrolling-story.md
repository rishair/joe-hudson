# Joe's Doomscrolling Story

## The Point It Illustrates

Doomscrolling fits the same shame-driven cascade as any other addictive behavior, and the way out is the same — self-forgiveness through recognizing the pattern as universal rather than uniquely yours. The story also illustrates the operational secret of why doomscrolling is so much harder to release than earlier avoidance substances: the phone has no built-in limit, so the body can avoid the underlying feeling all day rather than a few hours.

The story is the personal foundation underneath [[the-secret-to-the-bad-habit-is-thinking-youre-uniquely-broken]] and parallels [[joes-pot-habit]] structurally — Joe's own walked-through arc of an addictive behavior dissolving when the shame around it released.

## The Story

Joe was raised by an alcoholic father and a mother who catered to his father's needs rather than his. The arrangement left him with a chronic felt sense of not-enoughness — "oh man, I'm not good enough."

He tried various avoidance behaviors over the years. Television. Pornography. Drugs. Drinking. Each one had a structural limitation. You couldn't use porn all day. You couldn't eat all day. You couldn't drink all day. The body or the substance imposed a cap. Some amount of the not-enoughness had to be faced, even if just a little.

"There was this way in which I had to face that shame, even if it was just a little bit, instead of avoid the shame." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

Then the internet arrived. Then the phone arrived. For the first time the avoidance substance had no cap. The body could be in the avoidance for hours and hours and hours.

"And luckily I was just born in a generation where doomscrolling was just coming in online and you could kind of do it with a computer, but then the phone showed up. And we are calling it iPhone. And you could doomscroll all day long. You could just be in there doomscrolling, avoiding for hours and hours and hours at a time. And so I got pretty good at it. You know, I could do 2, 3 in the morning, but then I'd fall asleep and I could start, you know, as soon as I came home from work." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

## The Epiphany

The shift came not through willpower or technique but through a single moment of recognition. While scrolling, Joe noticed that other people were also scrolling. This wasn't unique to him. This was what humans were doing with phones. In that moment something released:

"One day I was just doom scrolling and I realized, oh, I'm not the only person who's, you know, doom scrolling. There's other people. This isn't, this isn't personal to me. This is something that's like, this is just the way humans are built on some level. And in that moment there was some forgiveness. And then I didn't want to doom scroll." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

The mechanism is the same as [[its-a-human-thing-redirect]] but self-administered. The universalization dissolved the shame. The shame had been the engine. Without the engine, the want to scroll diminished.

"That's when I recognized that if I actually am not in shame around it, then I'm less likely to doom scroll. That the whole thing of like what really drives me to doom scroll is that I don't want to feel like bad about myself. And then the more I doom scroll, the more I feel bad about myself. So the more reason I have to doom scroll so that I don't have to feel bad about myself." — Joe Hudson, The Secret to Elite Doomscrolling

## What This Story Establishes

Several principles converge:

1. **[[shame-drives-behavior]]**: the not-enoughness was driving the avoidance behaviors throughout. Shame was the engine the whole time. The substance changed (TV, porn, drugs, drinking, phone) but the function did not.

2. **[[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]** in its substance variant: scrolling produced more shame, the new shame increased the want to scroll, the cycle tightened. Joe names the exact downward spiral: "the more I doom scroll, the more I feel bad about myself. So the more reason I have to doom scroll."

3. **[[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]]** and the universalization test: the shame about scrolling was the installed version masquerading as natural. Recognizing that hundreds of millions of others were doing the same thing exposed the unique-to-me distortion as false.

4. **[[its-a-human-thing-redirect]]** delivered to oneself: the move that Joe uses with coaching clients in the moment of self-attack is the same move that released his own scrolling — but applied internally and not in a coaching context. The universalization works whether it comes from outside or from inside.

5. **[[bad-habits-are-the-avoidance-of-essential-self]]** in its phone-shaped form: the doomscrolling is Stage 4. The shame thinking is Stage 3. The not-enoughness is what is being avoided at the top of the cascade. The phone is what made it possible to stay in the avoidance all day rather than facing the not-enoughness for at least the hours when the other substances ran out.

## The Cap-Less Substance Insight

A specific feature of this story that the pot-habit story does not foreground: the structural difference between pre-phone and phone-era avoidance.

Pre-phone avoidance substances had natural caps:
- TV ended when you fell asleep or when other people needed the room
- Porn ended when the body could not produce another response
- Drugs ended when the supply did or when the body crashed
- Alcohol ended when the body could not absorb more or when you passed out

In each case, some hours of the day required facing the underlying feeling. The body got at least intermittent contact with the not-enoughness.

The phone removed the cap. The body can be in the avoidance from waking until sleep, every day, indefinitely. The intermittent contact with the underlying feeling that the older substances forced is no longer structurally required.

This is part of why the doomscrolling concern is so widespread now and so resistant to standard willpower interventions. The body of someone with chronic doomscrolling may have gone weeks or months without sustained contact with the underlying not-enoughness. The thing that finally has to be faced has been undisturbed for a long time.

## How Joe Delivers This Story

The story appears in a short satirical video framed as "how to become an elite-level doomscroller." See [[satirical-anti-advice-as-teaching]] for the format. The personal arc is the foundation — Joe gives his own progression as the credentialing for the satirical instructions — but the inversion is what carries the teaching. The literal instructions are the opposite of the actual teaching, which is what makes the actual teaching legible.

The reverse-form: "First of all, I had to stop exercising... I had to stop like any kind of exercising and meditation and anything like that. I had to let that go." The literal claim is that exercise and meditation interfere with elite doomscrolling because they produce competing dopamine. The actual teaching: exercise and meditation provide dopamine that competes with the phone, and re-introducing them is part of the work.

The same inversion runs throughout the story. "I had to feel like I was broken" is the literal claim. The actual teaching: feeling broken is what produces the scrolling, and recognizing that you are not uniquely broken is what releases it.

## The Anti-Experiment at the End

The video closes with an "experiment" — write down at least 50 specific ways you are broken. See [[dont-list-whats-wrong-with-you]] for the full anti-pattern this surfaces. The satirical claim is that this is how you become elite. The actual teaching, by inversion, is that the brutal-honesty-list practice (see [[joes-26-year-old-list]]) only works when applied to noticing what is here, not when applied to cataloging what is wrong with you. The two practices look formally similar — both involve writing a list about yourself — and produce opposite outcomes. The difference is whether the list is data or indictment.
