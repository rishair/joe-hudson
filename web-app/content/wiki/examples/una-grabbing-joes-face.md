# Una Grabbing Joe's Face to Restore the Field

## The Story

Joe's younger daughter Una was small. Joe describes moments when he was not present — distracted, in his head, somewhere else. Una would come over, climb into his lap, grab his face with her small hands, and physically turn it toward hers. She would make him have eye contact. Sometimes she would say, "I love you, Daddy."

"I remember Una, like, if I was not present, and it would come over to my lap, grab my face, and like point it towards me and make me have eye contact with her. And maybe she would say, 'I love you, Daddy.' It was like she was like, 'This, our field is not coherent right now. Our field is not relaxed right now.' And so my job as a kid is to get us back into connection." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

## What the Toddler Is Doing

She is not asking for attention in the way adults usually frame "asking for attention." She is repairing the field. The connection between the two of them has gone out of coherence — Joe has drifted into his head, somewhere else, not present — and she has registered this and is taking action to bring it back.

The physical move is precise. She does not just call to him. She does not just climb into his lap. She grabs his face. She turns it toward her. She forces the eye contact that her body knows is the channel for the kind of connection she wants. The verbal layer ("I love you, Daddy") is, if anything, a finishing move. The work is in the body — the hands, the redirection of attention, the demand for the gaze.

## What This Tells Us About How Children Operate

This is the principle of [[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]] in reverse. The child is not just receiving the field. The child is monitoring the field, registering when it has gone out of coherence, and acting to repair it. They have the felt sense for when the parent has drifted and they have somatic tools for bringing the parent back.

"I see that with kids all the time. Like they're constantly looking, they're like, they feel safe when there's that connection between them and their parents. And if they don't have it, they freak out a little bit and then need to regulate through emotions." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The child's behavior — the demand for face contact, the climbing, the requests, the tantrum if the bid for connection fails — is often the child's attempt to re-establish field coherence. The parent who reads these behaviors as "the kid is acting out" misses what is actually happening. The kid is reaching for the parent who has drifted.

## The Implication for the Parent

The parent who has internalized this knows that the kid's bid is information. The bid says: I noticed you went somewhere. Come back. The information is useful to the parent regardless of whether the parent had noticed the drift. The kid is calibrating the parent's state in real time, on a sensitive instrument, and is willing to provide feedback.

This is a small example of [[parenting-as-honest-mirror]]. The drift is data the parent did not have until the kid surfaced it. The kid did not analyze the drift. The kid felt it. The kid acted to repair it. The parent who can receive this data with gratitude rather than annoyance has access to information about their own state that very little else in their life will provide.

## What It Looks Like When the Parent Receives the Bid Well

The parent meets the eye contact. The parent does not deflect. The parent does not say "not now, sweetie, daddy's busy." The parent comes back into the field. The kid relaxes. The connection is repaired. The kid will, in most cases, then go back to whatever they were doing, because the bid was for connection, not for sustained attention. Connection re-established, the kid is free.

If the parent keeps deflecting the bids, the bids escalate. The child does not give up on the field. The child does what they have to do to get the parent back. Acting out, tantrum, regression to younger behaviors, physical illness — at the limit, a child whose bids are chronically deflected develops larger and louder strategies to get back the field they have lost.

## What This Example Illustrates

- **[[kids-respond-to-emotional-presence-not-words]]**: The child reads the parent's field. The principle running in reverse — the child is also monitoring the field.

- **[[connection]] as the bottom-line metric in family life**: The child knows when connection is missing. The child works to restore it. The child's behavior often makes more sense when read through this lens.

- **[[hand-in-hand-parenting]]**: The methodology is built on the recognition that children are constantly bidding for connection and that the parent's job is to be available to receive the bid.

- **[[parenting-as-honest-mirror]]**: The child's bid is data about the parent's drift. The parent who receives the data gets to keep working on their own presence.

- **The reverse-direction co-regulation**: Adults often frame co-regulation as the parent providing regulation to the child. This example shows the other direction. The child is providing regulation feedback to the parent. The relationship is bidirectional from the earliest ages.

## When To Use This Example

- A parent presenting that their child "constantly wants attention"
- A parent who thinks their child is being needy or clingy
- A parent who has not realized the kid is monitoring their state
- A coachee who is the kid in the story — who learned to bid for connection and now has the adult version of the same pattern
- A discussion of how children operate emotionally and what the parent is missing
- A parent who is being told to "not give in" to their child's bids for closeness

## When Someone Experiences This

- A parent whose toddler is grabbing them, climbing them, demanding face contact
- A parent who is distracted and noticing the child's behavior change in response
- A parent realizing in retrospect that the "clingy" period was actually the child responding to the parent's own distraction
- An adult who is starting to notice their adult partner doing a version of the same — bidding for connection when the partner has drifted

## Boundary Conditions

The example is about a healthy bid for connection from a young child who has the capacity to make it. Not every demand from a child is a bid for connection. Some are bids for autonomy, some are real needs (food, sleep, the bathroom), some are tests of the structure. The skill is reading what the child is actually asking for. The example points at one common type of bid that often gets misread.

The example also does not say the parent should always be infinitely available. The parent who genuinely cannot be present in a moment should say so honestly — "I am not available right now, I will be in 10 minutes" — rather than perform presence while internally absent. The kid will read the actual state regardless.

## Tone

Tender. The image of a small child grabbing a parent's face and turning it toward her is intrinsically tender. Hold that. This is not a clinical observation. It is also a small story about love. The teaching is in the precision of the child's somatic intelligence and in what it asks of the parent.
