# We, Not Me Became Connect, Connect, Connect

## The Story

Johannes Langgraf's company (Ona, formerly Gitpod) initially adopted a principle called "We, Not Me." The principle pointed at putting the company first, treating ego as not particularly helpful, prioritizing the collective over the individual. The principle was true in a narrow sense -- some teams genuinely need to be pulled out of individual-first behavior -- but the principle also produced a side effect: people did not show up fully empowered, because the principle implicitly suppressed the legitimate self-orientation that empowerment requires.

The iteration: the principle was replaced by "Connect, Connect, Connect." The new principle kept the connection orientation (which was the heart of what "We, Not Me" was reaching for) and dropped the suppression of self that the old wording was producing.

"We had a principle that was called like, we, not me. Which was about, you know, like, yeah, always kind of put the company first. It said like, ego is not important or not helpful. There is some truth to it. But what it also did was that people did not show up fully empowered. Because yes, it's also about yourself. It's not only about others. It's like about yourself. It's about having end-to-end ownership of things, right? Really, really, you know, driving those results and thinking also what you want as a person. Yeah. And say that. So we changed that and iterated that to Connect, Connect, Connect, which keeps the, you know, connection part and both with our customers and similarly connecting with each other, but also, and we spoke about that previously, connecting with yourself." -- Johannes Langgraf, 6 Operating Principles That Make 80% of Decisions Automatic

The new principle covers three directions of connection: with customers, with each other, and with yourself. The third direction is what the old principle had suppressed. The iteration restored it without losing the relational orientation the team needed.

## What This Example Illustrates

This is the canonical example of [[wrestle-with-your-principles]] producing a real change in the principle set. The team did not retire the underlying orientation; they refined the language that operationalized it. The principle was alive enough to notice its own side effect, and the team was willing to update it.

The example contains several teachings about principle iteration:

**Principles can be partially right and partially harmful.** "We, Not Me" was not a bad principle. It pointed at a real thing. But the framing had a cost the team could observe in their actual behavior. The framing of the principle is not the same as the orientation the principle is trying to install. A poorly-framed principle can produce the opposite of what it intended.

**Side effects are the diagnostic.** The team noticed that people were not showing up fully empowered. The not-showing-up-empowered was not random; it was traceable to a principle that implicitly suppressed self-orientation. The willingness to trace observable team behavior back to a principle in the set is what makes iteration possible. Without that diagnostic loop, the principle stays in place forever, side effects and all.

**The replacement principle is more compressive.** "Connect, Connect, Connect" includes "We, Not Me" as a special case (connection with each other), plus customer connection, plus self-connection. The new principle covers more ground with the same word count. This is often what iteration produces: a more general formulation that includes the prior principle as a sub-case.

**The verb-shape matters.** "We, Not Me" is a description. "Connect, Connect, Connect" is a verb. The verb form gives people something to do in real time. See [[open-the-drawer-as-shared-verb]] for the related design pattern. The shift to the verb form was not just semantic; it changed what the principle could do in daily work.

## What the Old Principle Was Trying to Do

The original "We, Not Me" framing emerged from a real concern: in some company cultures, individual ego destroys collective work. Engineers who optimize for their own visibility at the expense of the team. Salespeople who close their deals at the expense of customer relationships. Executives who play politics rather than building the company. The "We, Not Me" principle was trying to install the orientation that prevents these dysfunctions.

The orientation was correct. The framing was the problem. The framing taught the team that self-orientation was a vice, when in fact self-orientation is required for empowerment, ownership, and the kind of personal accountability that makes a high-performing organization possible. The team that was supposed to be empowered ended up not-quite-empowered because the principle pointing them at the team had also pointed them away from themselves.

This is the specific failure mode that some collectivist framings produce: the team becomes a collective in which individual selves have been quieted, which produces dependency rather than empowerment. See [[empowered-vs-power]] for the underlying distinction. The empowered person can be both for themselves and for the team. The "We, Not Me" framing accidentally treated those as in tension.

## How "Connect, Connect, Connect" Solves the Same Problem Without the Side Effect

The new principle treats connection as the orientation. Self, others, and customers are the three legitimate directions of connection. The principle does not privilege one over the others. The team that connects with each other is doing the work the original principle was trying to install. The team member who connects with themselves is doing the empowerment work that the original principle was suppressing. The customer connection is the orientation that drives the business.

The principle also resolves a tension that the old principle could not handle. When a team member's self-orientation is in service of the work (the engineer who wants the credit they deserve, the salesperson who needs to take care of themselves so they can take care of the customer, the executive who needs to honor their own intuition about what is right), the new principle does not require them to suppress it. The connection with self is part of the principle, not in opposition to it.

The result is a team that can both pull together (connect with each other) and stay self-aware (connect with yourself) and remain customer-oriented (connect with customers). The three directions support each other rather than competing.

## Why This Example Belongs in the Compendium

For anyone teaching or coaching on operating principles, this example is the cleanest demonstration of what wrestle-with-your-principles produces over time. The principle was not abandoned. The orientation was preserved. The framing was refined. The new framing is observably better at producing the outcome the original was trying to produce, plus a third direction the original was missing.

The example also models a willingness to revise that most companies do not have. Many companies treat their principles as fixed artifacts that cannot be touched without admitting failure. The actual practice (described in [[wrestle-with-your-principles]]) is the opposite: the principles get touched continuously, and the touching is what keeps them alive. The Ona iteration is what that practice looks like in real time.

The example is also a worked instance of how the verb-form upgrade works. "Connect, Connect, Connect" can be said in a meeting in a way that "We, Not Me" could not. The triple-repeat makes it sayable as a reminder ("connect, connect, connect" in the middle of a tense conversation). The verb form makes it actionable in the moment. The design lessons travel to other principles.

## What This Example Does Not Demonstrate

It does not demonstrate principle invention. The new principle was not invented from scratch; it was iterated from the old one. The story is about refinement, not creation.

It does not demonstrate that all principles should be revised. The Ona team has been mostly stable on their principles for several years. The revision of "We, Not Me" was a specific instance triggered by an observed side effect, not a general rule that principles always need updating. The principles that are working keep working until they stop working.

It does not demonstrate that all collectivist principles produce empowerment suppression. The suppression came from the specific framing of "We, Not Me" -- the explicit naming of ego as not helpful, the explicit privileging of the collective. A differently-framed collectivist principle might not produce the same side effect. The example is specific.
