# The Wingsuit Bail in France (When a Thought Breaks Contact With Reality)

## The Story

Brett was flying a wingsuit down a mountain in Europe. There was a section where, if he was flying at a good enough glide ratio, he could fly over a second mountain and have another several thousand feet of flight available to him.

He was on track. He thought he was going to make it. He was pretty sure he was going to make it. Kind of.

Then a thought flashed through his head: an image of his girlfriend at the time getting a phone call.

The thought broke his contact with the flight. The flow dropped. He decided he was not going to make it. He panicked, pulled his parachute, and landed in someone's backyard in the middle of France.

He survived. He also did not do it gracefully.

"I had this imagining of my girlfriend at the time getting a phone call, and that all of a sudden took me out of it. I was like, I'm not gonna make it. And I kind of panicked, pulled my parachute, ended up landing in some, uh, someone's backyard somewhere in the middle of France. And like, I bailed and I survived, and I also did not do it very gracefully. Like, I let in information that dysregulated me and yeah, constriction occurred, and then I had to bail." -- Brett Kistler, But... Is It Safe?

## What the Story Shows

The story illustrates the cost of letting a thought dissociate you from the contact-with-reality state that flow requires.

The thought was not wrong in any moral sense. It was about something real (his girlfriend, who would be the one getting the phone call). But it was the wrong thought at that moment. It pulled him out of the present-tense reading of the flight and into a future-tense story about his own death. The state shift was instant. The flow that had been sustaining the flight collapsed. The body went into constriction. The decision became binary: bail or die. He bailed.

The "information that dysregulated me" phrasing is precise. The thought was a piece of information that the system was not equipped to integrate in that moment. Integrating it would have required dropping out of the flight to feel through what the phone call would mean -- not something that could be done at terminal velocity over a mountain.

## When the Thought Belongs and When It Doesn't

In Brett's normal preparation, exactly this kind of visualization is part of the work. Before the jump, he allows himself to feel what each possible outcome would feel like, including the girlfriend's phone call, including the impact, including the failure to recover. This is the practice that produces grounded readiness. See [[safety-that-connects-vs-disconnects-from-reality]].

The same thought, in the same body, in a different moment, is either:

- Integration (during preparation): contact with reality
- Dissociation (during execution): break from contact with reality

The thought is not the variable. The timing is the variable.

Once the jump begins, the preparation is done. The state needed for execution is not feeling-through; it is full presence in the act. A thought that belonged in the preparation phase, arriving during the execution phase, is the wrong thought at the wrong time.

## The Difference From Stepping on the Signal

This is not [[dont-step-on-the-signal]]. Brett did not override a fear signal. He let in a thought he should not have let in during execution, and the thought produced the constriction. The fear that arose was reactive to the constriction, not informative about the conditions.

The discrimination: a fear signal during execution that says "something is actually wrong here" is information. A constriction during execution that follows a dissociating thought is the thought's effect on the system, not a read on the actual conditions.

Brett's read on his own moment: it was the second. He was going to make it. He let in the wrong information at the wrong time. He bailed unnecessarily, and unrgracefully.

## What Joe Reaches For This Example For

This is the canonical illustration of how letting in the wrong information at the wrong moment breaks the contact with reality that flow requires. The story is useful when:

- A person is trying to think their way through a high-presence moment
- A person is letting in catastrophic future-stories during execution of a present-tense task
- A person is conflating preparation work with execution work
- A person is in a difficult conversation and is being pulled out by thoughts about what will happen after
- A leader is mid-decision and is being pulled out by thoughts about how the team will react

The discrimination the story teaches: the preparation work is done before. During execution, full presence is the work. A thought that belongs in preparation, arriving during execution, is the thing that breaks the contact.

## What Brett Learned From It

The lesson Brett takes is not "never have that thought." The lesson is about the timing of where different states belong:

- During preparation: feel-through, visualize, integrate the worst-case
- During execution: be present, read the body, respond to what is actual

A jumper who can sustain present-tense awareness through the full flight has done the feeling-through in advance and is not doing it during. A jumper who hasn't done the feeling-through in advance ends up doing it mid-flight, with predictable results: the constriction, the bail, the ungraceful landing.

## The Generalization

The structure applies in any domain where execution requires sustained presence:

- A difficult conversation: feel-through the possible reactions in advance, then be present during the conversation
- A high-stakes decision in a meeting: feel the consequences of each path in preparation, then be present in the meeting itself
- A creative performance: do the doubt-work in private, then be present on stage
- A boundary in a relationship: feel the possibilities in advance, then be present when you set it

In each case, mid-execution incursions of preparation-state thinking break the presence required for execution. The work is to know which state belongs when, and to keep them in their proper phases.

## Tone

Brett tells the story without making himself wrong. The bail was the system's best response given the state he was in. The lesson is structural, not personal: this is what happens when execution-state and preparation-state get mixed.
