# I Help People Die Better

## The Story

Joe was at a Summit at Sea event with one of the better coaches he had ever met — a world-famous coach. Both of them were overwhelmed by the connecting-event energy of the gathering. They had retreated to the deck chairs of the boat to escape the intensity.

Someone "important" came along and asked the famous coach: "What do you do for a living?"

The coach answered: "I help people die better."

"That was, that was his answer. And this famous person is like, immediately sat down and like, and like they started a conversation and he became a client. I just help people die better. And he never had answered the question like that before. But he was coming from his space. That is the gift. What you're calling the gift, I would just call his nature." -- Joe Hudson, What Do I Offer The World?

The answer worked. The important person became a client. The coach had never given that answer before. Something about being in his space, off in the deck chair, overwhelmed, not performing, produced the answer that landed.

## What This Example Illustrates

The example illustrates [[your-gift-is-your-nature-not-your-skill]]. The coach was not deploying a marketing strategy. He was not using a tested elevator pitch. He was coming from where he was actually standing — from his space — and the answer that emerged was the answer that converted a stranger into a client.

Three structural points:

**The answer came from the space, not from the strategy.** The coach had not pre-crafted "I help people die better." It came out because that was the space he was in. His nature, in that moment of overwhelm and retreat, produced its own articulation. The articulation worked because it was sourced from his actual location, not from his strategic intent.

**He had never answered that way before.** This is the tell that it was not technique. Technique gets repeated. Nature varies in expression because nature is alive. The novelty of the answer for the coach himself — "he never had answered the question like that before" — confirms it was being spoken from inside, not retrieved from a script.

**What landed with the listener was the nature, not the words.** The important person did not become a client because "die better" was a clever positioning statement. The person became a client because they could feel that the coach was speaking from somewhere real. The feel of someone being-in-themselves is what converts. The words are downstream.

## Why This Worked Specifically

Several conditions made the moment possible:

- Both Joe and the coach were overwhelmed. Their normal performance-modes had been temporarily disabled by the intensity of the event.
- They had retreated to the deck chairs. The retreat was a return-to-self move, away from the connecting-event energy.
- The question "what do you do for a living?" was asked in this context, not in a networking context. The defenses were down.
- The coach was not trying to win the conversation. He was just answering from where he was.

These are not strict requirements. The general principle: when the performance-mode is off and the person is in their space, what comes out is what is there. What is there is the nature. The nature, spoken plainly, lands.

## The Contrast With Crafted Self-Description

Compare the coach's answer to what most people produce when asked the same question in a networking context:

- "I'm a transformational leadership coach helping high-performing executives achieve their full potential."
- "I work with founders on aligning their personal growth with their business strategy."
- "I help people across industries unlock their unique value proposition."

These are crafted. They are designed to be received. They are positioning statements that have been worked on. They tell you nothing about what the person is actually like to work with. They are the framework-fit answer.

"I help people die better" is different. It is short. It is specific. It is mildly unsettling. It does not optimize for being palatable. It is just what is true about what the coach does. The listener has to deal with what was said rather than slide past a familiar formulation.

The crafted answers do not land. The space-answer lands. The example surfaces the difference.

## What the Story Does Not Promise

The story does not say "be quirky in your elevator pitch." That would be missing the point. Quirky-on-purpose is also performance, just a different style of performance. The point is to be in your space, and to let what comes from your space come.

The story does not say "you must use unconventional language about your work." The unconventionality of "die better" was a feature of this particular coach's nature. Another coach in their space would produce a different sentence — maybe completely conventional in language but spoken from a real location. The locating is the work; the wording is downstream.

The story does not say "this will work every time." The same coach giving the same answer in a different context, to a different person, after rehearsing it — might land badly. The original landing was a function of the unprepared, in-his-space quality. Repeating it as a technique would lose the quality.

## When to Use This Story

The story is useful when:

- A coachee is asking how to articulate what they do, what their unique value is, what their superpower is
- A coachee is trying to fit themselves into a business-framework for self-description
- A coachee is preparing for a networking event or pitch and is over-engineering their introduction
- The principle [[your-gift-is-your-nature-not-your-skill]] needs a concrete illustration
- A coachee is dismissing simple, plain answers about themselves in favor of more sophisticated framings
- The conversation needs to shift from skill-search to nature-recognition

## When Not to Use This Story

The story can be misheard as "be unusual." Avoid telling it as a model to be imitated. The teaching is about the location of the speaking, not about the content of what gets said. If the coachee hears "I should come up with my own clever short phrase," the story has been received as technique-instruction, not as nature-pointer.

The story can also be misheard as "the answer will arrive if you just retreat enough." The retreat is part of why the answer came out, but the retreat is not the practice. The practice is being in your space, which can be done anywhere with enough internal disposition.

## The "Coming From His Space" Phrase

The phrase "he was coming from his space" carries the whole teaching. The space is the felt-location the person operates from when they are themselves. The coach was in his space when he answered. The space produced the answer.

What "his space" was, specifically, in the coach's case: a quiet, grounded, slightly-melancholy, real place where the work of helping people die better lives. The space has emotional texture. The space has values. The space has presence. The answer "I help people die better" was an articulation of the space, spoken from inside it.

The space is the same thing as the nature ([[your-gift-is-your-nature-not-your-skill]]). It is the felt-quality the person carries when they are not performing. It is what their actual gifts are sourced from.

For an AI coach: when a user asks how to be more "in their space" or how to access their nature, the practical instruction is usually [[go-to-your-heart-not-your-brain]] or another felt-attention practice. The space cannot be located by analysis. It can only be returned to by attention shifting from the head to the felt-location of the self.

## The Companion Story: She Motivates People

In the same coaching session, the guest tells a complementary story. Years ago, a friend introduced her by saying "she motivates people." It was "one of the most singular moments of feeling seen" she had ever had.

The two stories together make the structure clear: the coach was speaking his own nature ("I help people die better"); the guest's friend was reflecting her nature back to her ("she motivates people"). The nature can be expressed from inside or named from outside. Either way, what gets named is the nature, not the skill.

The guest's response — "one of the most singular moments of feeling seen" — confirms that nature-naming has a specific landing-quality. It is felt as being-seen. The skill-naming does not produce the same response. The contrast is the data.

When Joe tells this story, the tone is light, slightly impressed, slightly amused. He is recounting something that happened, not delivering doctrine. The "I just help people die better" delivered with mild emphasis on "just" — as if to say, that was it, that was all he said, and that was enough. The story is offered as data, not as scripture.
