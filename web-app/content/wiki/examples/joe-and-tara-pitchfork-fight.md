# Joe and Tara's Pitchfork and Miata Fight

## The Story

In the year after Joe and Tara got married, the relationship entered a particularly chaotic period. The safety of the vows had unlocked deeper material in both of them. Tara describes it: "All of my shit could come out."

The lowest point of that year, by Tara's own account, was a specific fight. The details are sparse and the visual is what carries the example.

"Oh yeah, picture a car and a sledgehammer. What was the other? A car, pitchfork, pitchfork, cars. One person using a car as a tool— me, it might have been a cute little Miata— and one person holding a pitchfork over it. One person threatening the other with the car, vroom vroom. The other threatening That might have been like the penultimate, like, bottom, actually the pit." -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

Joe confirms: "Yeah, for that particular dip. There's been other dips in our marriage economy. That was definitely the lowest."

The fight involved Tara in a Miata (using the car as a tool / threat), Joe with a pitchfork. The specifics of what was being said are not given; what is preserved is the image of two people who would later become known for teaching how to fight well at one point threatening each other with a vehicle and a farm implement.

## Why This Example Matters

The example does a specific kind of work in coaching. Most people, encountering Joe and Tara's current state -- 30 years married, public-facing, teachers of relationship work -- assume they must have always been like this. The pitchfork fight is the data point that breaks that assumption. They were not. At one point they were at the bottom of the bottom, in a fight whose form was so primitive that the visual is almost cartoonish.

The implication: where two people end up in a long marriage is not predicted by where they were in year one. The current Joe and Tara are not the year-one Joe and Tara. The year-one Joe and Tara could not have done the current work. The work made the current possible.

For someone in the early years of a marriage who is in the form of fighting that Joe and Tara were in at this point, the example is permission. The current state is not predictive. The work, if both are willing, produces a different couple over time.

## The Timing of the Fight in the Larger Arc

This fight came after the marriage vows, in a year-long period of "especially chaotic" behavior. Tara explicitly connects the timing to the safety the vows provided:

"After marriage, there was a safety there, like, oh, we're in it, we're like locked into something. And so really, all of my shit could come out. And so it got really bad, literally, that fight, the pitchfork fight happened after we got married, there was like a year period after marriage that was especially chaotic. And I think it was because there was the safety of these vows we'd taken that really, um, so that was particularly bad." -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The pattern is structural: the safety of the commitment makes the deeper material accessible. The deeper material is exactly what could not come out before the safety was in place. It comes out now, in a form that looks like the marriage failing, but is actually the marriage doing its work of surfacing what needs to be processed.

This is also the [[every-major-life-change-resets-the-marriage]] principle in its first application -- the pre-marriage to marriage reset. The transition from "two people dating" to "married couple" is itself a major life change. The pitchfork fight is what the reset looked like in Joe and Tara's case.

## What the Fight Was Actually About

The transcript does not give the surface content of the pitchfork fight. What the period was actually about is named elsewhere in the same interview. The general structure of Joe and Tara's fights at that stage:

"Wherever we had both had shame that were perfectly interlocking and feeling unseen. That was a huge part of it." -- Joe Hudson, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

And the deeper layer of what every fight was about:

"In a way it's, do you love me? Do you love me? Do you love me? Are we safe? Am I safe? Am I safe?" -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The pitchfork fight, like every fight in that period, was almost certainly processing the interlocking shame and the do-you-love-me / am-I-safe inquiry. The surface content -- whatever specific thing precipitated the car-and-pitchfork escalation -- was the cover. The underlying processing was the same processing that all the fights in that period were doing.

The point is not that the fight was justified by what it was processing. It was not. The behavior was, by Joe and Tara's own account, what they call "reenacting our parents' trauma on each other." See [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] for what makes a fight productive vs. retraumatizing. The pitchfork fight was on the retraumatizing side at the time. The progression to productive fighting was the work of the years that followed.

## What Joe and Tara Did After

The pitchfork fight did not end the marriage. Both Joe and Tara continued their dual commitment to their own transformation. They went to couples therapy. They went to individual therapy. They went to retreats. They learned Nonviolent Communication. They learned about verbal abuse and how to de-escalate.

"After we worked through that and we were in therapy, we were doing couples therapy, individual therapy, we were doing a lot of different retreats, like we worked it and really worked, um, Nonviolent Communication and all of these other, like, all everything that goes along with that, verbal abuse and escalation and how to de-escalate instead of escalate. And after that year, it got much better and we had a great period." -- Tara Howley, How Joe and Tara Became Joe and Tara

The recovery sequence:
1. The chaotic year happens, including the pitchfork low point.
2. They commit to the work. Therapy, retreats, communication training.
3. They learn the specific skills (NVC, recognizing abuse, de-escalation).
4. After that year, the marriage enters "a great period."

This is the [[are-you-willing-to-do-the-work]] principle demonstrated in extreme form. The willingness held even at the pit. The work, applied, produced the transition out.

## When to Use This Example

- A couple in the first year or two of marriage in a chaotic period that feels like the marriage failing
- A person who is comparing themselves to a public couple they admire and concluding their own relationship is uniquely bad
- A person doubting whether the work can produce real change in a relationship that started badly
- A person in [[we-have-built-up-resentment]] who needs evidence that the bottom is not predictive
- A person who is using the post-marriage shit-coming-out as a reason to leave rather than as the structural reset to be worked through
- A discussion of [[every-fight-can-bring-you-closer]] that needs the contrasting low-point example
- A discussion of [[every-major-life-change-resets-the-marriage]] needing the specific marriage-vows-as-reset example

## What This Example Is Not

This is not a claim that all early-marriage low points lead to good marriages. Some couples reach the pit and cannot find their way out. The example is not a guarantee; it is a demonstration that the pit and the eventual long marriage are compatible, and that the variable is the dual commitment to the work, not the absence of pits.

This is not an excuse for the behavior. Both Joe and Tara name the period's behavior as toxic. Their friends, who were telling them they should get divorced, were correct about the behavior; what the friends could not see was the commitment to growth that was operating alongside the behavior.

This is also not a claim that holding a pitchfork over someone's car is the work of the relationship. The pitchfork fight was the bottom; it was what they were trying to grow past, not part of the growth itself. The growth was the dedication to learning how to not do that anymore.

## What This Example Demonstrates About the Pre-Tools Phase

Joe and Tara were in their late 20s / early 30s. They had not yet developed the tools that would later make them teachers of relationship work. The behavior at that stage was the behavior that two trauma-carrying young adults default to under pressure. The pitchfork fight is what that default looks like when both parties have heavy material and no tools.

This is structurally consistent with what most couples experience in early marriage. The honeymoon phase ends. The vow-safety unlocks the deeper material. Neither partner has the tools to meet what is being surfaced. The behavior gets primitive. The relationship feels like it is failing.

The implication for the AI coach: when a couple presents in this phase, the right response is not horror at the behavior. It is the recognition that this is what unprocessed material looks like when it gets unlocked by commitment. The work is to develop the tools. The first step is naming what is happening and offering the path through it -- therapy, communication training, mutual commitment to growth.

## Tone

Brief, almost matter-of-fact, slightly amused by the absurdity in retrospect. Tara delivers the visual ("picture a car and a sledgehammer") with the comic detachment of someone who has been through the thing and can now see it from the outside. The "actually the pit" makes it clear that even with the comedy, the period was serious. Joe's "definitely the lowest" confirms the seriousness without escalating it.

Use the same tone when telling this story. The visual carries the point. The over-the-top quality (a Miata, a pitchfork) is what makes the example memorable. The retrospective tone is what makes it usable -- a story of a low point told by people who got out of it.
