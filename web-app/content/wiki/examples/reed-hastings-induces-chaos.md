# Reed Hastings Induces Chaos at Netflix

## The Story

Reed Hastings, the founder of Netflix (and Netscape before it), explicitly induces a certain amount of chaos in his organization. The reasoning is structural: smart people leave companies that have been made idiot-proof, and when the real changes come, the company that has optimized for stability cannot actually change quickly enough to adapt.

Joe references the story in the perfectionism conversation as the organizational counterpart to the personal teaching. The same dynamic that operates inside a person -- perfectionism produces stagnation, which produces the failure perfectionism was trying to prevent -- operates inside a company. The corrective at both scales involves a deliberate refusal to organize everything around the avoidance of mistakes.

"He talks about how he induces a certain amount of chaos in the system because smart people leave if you make a company idiot-proof. And then when the real changes come, they can't actually handle the innovation. They can't really change quick enough. And so he makes sure there's a certain amount of chaos so that there can be change. And that's what he accredits going from shipping DVDs to online streaming to probably now gaming or whatever it is that he's doing. So there's an intelligence to that to say, oh no, I'm gonna make sure there's a certain amount of chaos here that it's not gonna be all systematized." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism Creates the Chaos it's trying to Prevent

The story is told to make a specific point in the perfectionism teaching: the orientation toward "make everything perfect, make everything systematized, prevent every mistake" is not just personally costly. At the organizational scale it produces the inability to evolve. Netflix's evolution across three businesses (mail-order DVDs, online streaming, gaming) is offered as the cumulative evidence that engineered organizational chaos is the precondition for the kind of change that the businesses actually went through.

## What the Story Illustrates

Two structural points the story makes operationally:

**Smart people are repelled by perfectionist organizations.** The people who can actually drive change need room to make mistakes, to try things that don't work, to operate outside the established procedure. An organization that has systematized every process has implicitly told the people who would try things that there is no place for trying. The smart people leave. The remaining people are the ones who are good at executing established procedures, which are the people who cannot lead the next wave of change. The perfectionist organization recruits its own inability to evolve.

**Change requires the capacity for chaos.** A company that has eliminated chaos has eliminated the developmental space inside which change happens. When the market shifts (DVDs to streaming; streaming to gaming; etc.), the company has to be able to change shape. The chaos-capacity is what makes shape-change possible. The systematized company tries to change while keeping all its existing systems, which produces neither the new shape nor a functional version of the old one.

The general principle the story illustrates: organizational stability and organizational adaptability are in tension. Past a certain point, optimizing for stability undermines adaptability. The leader who knows this introduces enough chaos to keep the adaptability online. See [[transformation-happens-in-uncertainty]] for the broader principle.

## The Mechanism Behind the Story

The chaos Hastings induces is not random disruption. It is the deliberate non-elimination of certain kinds of unpredictability. The systems that would be added to make life smoother for the bureaucratic mind are not added, even when they could be. The processes that would be tightened to eliminate the last 5% of variation are left slightly loose. The procedures that would specify every employee's behavior in every scenario are not written.

What this preserves: the space where individual judgment is required. The space where someone has to figure out what to do without a procedure to follow. The space where a smart person can spot something the system would have missed. The space where iteration on the work is part of the work, not an exception to it.

What this prevents: the false sense of control that comes from having a system for everything. The illusion that the organization has been made resilient by being made rigid. The dependency on procedure that produces the inability to act when the procedure does not cover the situation.

This is the organizational equivalent of [[change-the-approach-not-the-target]] applied at the system level. The bureaucratic mind would change the approach from "operate the business" to "perfect the business's procedures." The leader who has internalized the principle changes the approach from "perfect the procedures" to "preserve the conditions under which the business can evolve."

## How to Use This Example in Coaching

For leaders who are running organizations that have become rigid, the Hastings story is a permission structure. It demonstrates that a successful leader has deliberately kept things from being fully systematized, and that the strategy has produced one of the more visible organizational transformations of the last 30 years. The example gives the leader cover to do the same thing in their own organization without having to defend it from first principles.

The questions the story opens:

- What in your organization has been systematized in ways that would benefit from being un-systematized?
- Where are the smart people leaving because there is no room to try things?
- What's the next transition your company will need to make, and does the current operating mode allow for that transition?
- Where have you been treating mistakes as failures to be prevented rather than as the cost of innovation?

For founders and CEOs the example also reframes the developmental task of the company. The task is not "build the perfect set of procedures." The task is "build the kind of organization that can evolve when it needs to." The two tasks are different, and they pull in opposite directions at the limit. Hastings is offered as the proof of concept that the second task is achievable.

## What This Example Is Not

It is not a recommendation to introduce chaos for chaos's sake. The chaos Hastings preserves is functional -- it preserves the space for human judgment and iteration. Random disruption that does not preserve those spaces is not the same thing and does not produce the same result.

It is not a blanket argument against operational discipline. Netflix runs operations, has processes, ships product. The argument is about which kinds of systematization are growth-promoting and which kinds are growth-killing. The growth-killing kind is the kind that eliminates the space for the people inside the system to try things, evolve, and adapt.

It is not a one-time intervention. The same dynamic operates continuously. Each year of organizational growth tends to systematize more. The work of preserving chaos has to be ongoing. The leader who introduced chaos five years ago and then stopped will eventually have a systematized organization again.
