# Jesse and Generous Admiration

## The Story

Joe attended a private conference of people in a similar business to his own. The organizer, Jesse, displayed a generosity with admiration that Joe had not seen before. In every interaction, Jesse openly admired qualities he saw in the other person. The effect on the room was visible and direct.

"Recently I was in this little private conference and it was full of people in a business that was similar to mine. And the person who organized it, Jesse, was so generous with his admiration. Every single person that he interacted with, you just saw him openly admire things in the other people. And because of that, they all shared those gifts. They all taught him how to do it, and his business was growing because of it. But not only that, he had all these people in a room together, all helping each other, all because of his admiration." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

The chain of consequences was concrete:

- Jesse admired specific qualities in each person
- Those people, recognized, shared the gifts that produced those qualities
- They taught Jesse how to do what he had admired
- Jesse's business grew from what he learned
- The same dynamic spread through the room: people started helping each other because the admiration had made the room safe to be seen

## Joe's Self-Recognition

The story matters less for what Jesse did than for what Joe noticed in himself by contrast.

"I realized to myself at that moment, like, 'Wow, I'm a little stingy with my admiration. What does it feel like to actually be really generous with my admiration?'" -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

The recognition has the structure of [[pointers-to-unseen-parts]] in reverse. Watching another person do something generously made Joe see where he had been doing it stingily. The seeing was not moral judgment; it was diagnostic. The next move was the experiment: what would it be like to do what Jesse did?

## The Experiment and the Result

Joe started being generous with his own admiration. The result was the same pattern Jesse had been producing:

"When I started being generous with my admiration, all of a sudden other folks started opening up and they started handing me their gifts. They were like, 'Oh, you admire this? Let me share with you how that happened, how that was created.' And then I could learn from that and I could get that." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

The mechanism: when admiration is generous, the admired person feels safe enough to show how the thing was built. The admirer gains both the relationship and the knowledge of how to develop the same quality themselves. The "stinginess with admiration" Joe had been carrying was protecting nothing; it was just blocking the flow.

## The Point Joe Uses This Story For

The story illustrates [[admire-what-you-crave]] as a general practice rather than only as a resentment antidote. The admiration move works in two registers:

- **Defensive register:** when resentment of a specific person's quality is present, the admiration releases the resentment and gives permission for the disowned want.
- **Offensive register:** as a sustained practice of generosity with admiration in all interactions, it produces the room Jesse produced -- one in which people share their gifts because they feel seen.

The Jesse example anchors the second register. The reframe from stingy to generous was not for a specific resentment; it was a way of being. The compounding effect was that the surrounding network became one in which knowledge moved, business grew, and people helped each other.

## The Compressed Lesson

"It was such a mind blow for me that it was as simple as just being generous with your admiration." -- Joe Hudson, How To Stop Being Resentful

The simplicity is the teaching. The thing that produced the compounding effect was not a technique, not a skill, not a strategy. It was a choice to be generous with a faculty Joe already had. The fact that the same intervention works at the small scale (single resentment) and the large scale (a whole network of relationships) is part of why the practice is worth installing.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When teaching [[admire-what-you-crave]] and wanting to show that the move is not just a one-off resentment fix but a way of being
- When working with a person who is "stingy with admiration" without recognizing it as a constraint on their own life and relationships
- When working with leaders whose teams are not sharing their gifts; the admiration practice is often a more direct path than any other team intervention
- When the person is in a competitive or comparison-heavy field and the resentment of peers is a barrier to learning from them
- When a person wants to build community or network and is approaching it as extraction rather than as appreciation

## The Jesse Example as a Form of Generosity

Joe returns to the Jesse story (under the name Tiago Forte in a later conversation -- the same kind of teacher-of-Building-a-Second-Brain organizer figure) when teaching the broader concept of [[generosity]]. The point in that context: generosity is not only money. Admiration is one of its forms. Jesse/Tiago is the canonical example of generosity-of-admiration.

"There's a guy, Tiago Forte. He runs Building a Second Brain, and he is one of the most generous people as far as admiration goes. He's so generous in his admiration. He looks at somebody, finds something to admire about them. What you see is that the people around him, when he admires them, they light up. It's like they've been given a gift. Way better than a couple hundred bucks, right?" -- Joe Hudson, How Generosity Improves Your Well-Being

The framing "way better than a couple hundred bucks" is the key teaching. Specific, openly-named admiration is received as a gift on the same scale as money -- often larger. This is why generosity-of-admiration belongs in the same category as generosity-of-money, and why a person who believes they "have nothing to give" because they have no money still has access to a form of generosity that produces visible effects in the receiver.

The Jesse/Tiago example anchors the multi-form teaching of generosity. See [[generosity]] for the four forms (money, admiration, seeing, being-with) and the principle that the form follows what the giver has access to.
