# The Subway Kid

## The Story

When Joe lived in Los Angeles, his neighbors were a first-generation family from Central America. Their 18-year-old son got his first job at Subway. He worked the counter with pizzazz: friendliness, joy, a kind of singing energy. He was one of those people you encounter behind a counter who is clearly enjoying themselves.

He worked there for about three weeks. Then a customer walked in, watched him work, and said, "Hey, I have a restaurant and I need someone." Within six months he was a sous chef. Joe lost track of him after that, but the pattern was set: he kept moving up because of how he showed up.

"He just did it with pizzazz. He did it with friendliness. He did it with a joy. He did it with a bit of a, like, a singing. He was just one of those characters that you've had the experience, you've gone into someplace and there's somebody on the other side of the counter who's enjoying themselves and they're maybe singing or they got a little pizzazz. He was one of those people. And he worked there, I think, for 3 weeks until somebody walked in and was like, 'Hey, I have a restaurant and I need someone.' And then within another 6 months, he was the sous chef. And then, you know, it just kept on going." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment

## The Point It Illustrates

It does not matter what you are doing. If you do it with artistry, you are creating the world that you want.

The objection that the [[art-of-accomplishment]] is a luxury only available to people in the right kind of work is wrong. The Subway counter is a more revealing test than the executive suite. The job is small, the wage is small, the constraints are large. None of that prevents artistry. The artistry is in the *how*. The how creates opportunities that did not exist before the artistry began.

The kid was not strategizing. He was not networking. He was not engineering a career path. He was being himself fully at the Subway counter. That is what was visible to the restaurant owner. That is what produced the next opportunity. The consciousness he brought to the work was the product, and the product attracted what came next. See [[consciousness-is-the-product]].

## When Joe Reaches for This

When someone says they cannot apply this work because of their circumstances: their job is too constrained, their boss is too controlling, their wage is too low. The Subway kid had all of those constraints and applied the art anyway, and the constraints dissolved as a result.

When someone is in a transitional job and is grinding through it waiting for the "real" job to arrive. The story shows that the way you do the transitional job determines whether the real job arrives.

When responding to the concern "[[i-dont-have-time-for-this]]" -- the kid did not have time either. He just was who he was while doing the work. That is not extra time. It is a different relationship to the time he was already spending.

## The Broader Frame

Joe pairs this story with Mandela's. See [[mandela-empowerment]]. Mandela was in prison. The Subway kid was at a sandwich counter. Different scales, same principle: the situation does not determine whether you can bring your full self to it. The full self is brought regardless of situation, and the situation changes as a result.

"To think that you can't do it because you're in a bureaucracy is, again, it's just as limited of the thinking as a businessman saying we can't be whole humans and do business." -- Joe Hudson, The Art of Accomplishment
