# Joe's Transition From Art to Venture Capital

## The Story

Joe is in his mid-30s. He had been doing large-scale video art installations. The buyers of large-scale video art were almost exclusively very wealthy people. One of these collectors and Joe were talking. The conversation drifted into the economy. Joe had been thinking a lot about gold and oil at the time, and he gave the collector some advice. The advice was specific and well-timed; both commodities subsequently spiked. The collector made a lot of money.

The collector said: come work for me. Joe said: nah, I'm doing art, I'm good.

The collector tried a different question: "Well, if you could do anything, what would you want to do?"

Joe took the question home and sat with it for three months. He came back with two answers. The change he wanted to see in the world was, basically, helping humanity heal itself through consciousness work. The other thing he was deeply interested in was the environment.

The collector gave him some capital to do both.

Joe spent the next 12 years doing both in parallel: starting organizations for helping kids with consciousness work, and doing venture capital in environmental — specifically agricultural — investing. Agriculture because that was where nobody was playing and where most of the resources were being used.

## Why Joe Was Even Available For This

The conversation with the collector happened because Joe was, in his mid-30s, ready to start making money. Until then, he had no interest in making money. The shift came from his family. He and Tara were going to have kids. The structural fact of needing to provide for a family installed the should that became "I should be making money." See [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] for the related arc of how that should later resolved.

The underlying orientation was preserved: Joe wasn't trying to make money for its own sake. He was trying to buy time. The framing he gives himself in this period was that he wanted enough money to be able to meditate. The art career, the business decisions, the eventual VC pivot — all of it was instrumental to having space to keep doing the inner work.

"And so I wasn't trying to make money, I was just trying to buy time. And, um, and so, but so I always kind of had one thumb in business. I, you know, did international stock lending at a young age, and I did legal and compliance for a bank at at a young age. And so I just — I always have my hand in it. And then again, my family came along, and it was time to have a family. Like, I was clear that it's time to have a kid, and you got to make some money." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The biographical detail to note: Joe had been doing business activity all along, even while ostensibly being a meditator and artist. International stock lending, legal compliance for a bank. The hand was always in business; it was just not the primary work yet. The mid-30s transition was less of a pivot than it might appear from the outside; it was a re-prioritization of activity that had always been part of his life.

## The Three-Month Sit With "What Would You Want To Do?"

The collector's question — "If you could do anything, what would you want to do?" — is one of the standard questions in coaching. Joe sat with it for three months before answering.

The three-month sit matters. The standard failure mode with this question is the snap answer that reports the consciously-held identity ("I'd be a writer," "I'd start a nonprofit") rather than the actual desire. The three-month sit was the time required for the actual desire to surface past the consciously-held identity.

The two answers Joe arrived at — consciousness work for healing humanity, and environmental work — became the structure of the next 12 years. The structure held because the desire was actual, not declared. The sit was the practice that let the actual desire be found.

## Why It Worked Structurally

Joe is explicit that the collector's funding was not transactional in the standard VC sense. The collector gave Joe capital to pursue both directions. The structure was permissive: Joe was being trusted to figure out what to do with the resources, with the implicit agreement that the work would matter to the collector.

The consciousness side became the nonprofits for kids. The environmental side became agriculture VC. The two ran in parallel for 12 years. The integration came later, when Joe realized that the bottleneck in environmental work was not technology but human ego — which collapsed the apparent separation between the two threads. See [[capitalism-can-be-force-for-healing]] for the principle Joe arrived at from this 12-year arc.

## The Eventual Integration: "You Can Evolve Consciousness and Make Money"

The 12 years produced a specific recognition. Joe had been carrying — and the broader culture had been carrying — an implicit assumption that consciousness work and business were separate domains. The environmental movement had carried the same assumption a generation earlier: you could be an environmentalist or you could be a businessman, but not both. The 1990s collapsed that distinction in environmental work. People realized you could be both, and that being both was sometimes more powerful than being either alone.

Joe's intuition during the 12 years was that the same collapse was going to happen with consciousness:

"That same thing is gonna happen with consciousness, that you can become a conscious, like you can evolve consciousness and make money at the same time, or capitalism can be a force for helping humanity heal, become themselves, understand themselves." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

This intuition is the connective tissue that explains everything that came after. The coaching work, the workshops, the podcast, the books — all of it sits on the proposition that capitalism can be a force for human consciousness work. The 12 years of running both threads in parallel was the lived test of the proposition. See [[capitalism-can-be-force-for-healing]] for the developed principle.

## What Led Out Of VC Into Coaching

Joe was a bad investor. The specific reason: his orientation as a person — installed by an alcoholic father he tried to be valued by — was to be needed. The pattern showed up in his VC work as a tendency to invest in companies whose entrepreneurs needed him.

The actual structural requirement of good venture investing is the opposite: invest in entrepreneurs who do not need you, who find you to be a nuisance. Joe was making the wrong selection. The investments were underperforming.

The only thing he could do was coach the entrepreneurs to upgrade their consciousness. He started coaching them. The coaching worked. Word spread. More people wanted to be coached. Eventually, more people wanted to be coached than Joe had time for. The small groups began. Then the workshops. Then the calling to teach. See [[joes-bad-investor-realization-led-to-coaching]] for the developed story.

The arc that started with the collector's "if you could do anything" question ended with Joe being a teacher. The teaching is what the question's answer was always pointing toward, but the path went through 12 years of VC and consciousness nonprofits to get there.

## The Recognition That Capitalism Made Him

Joe summarizes the entire arc in a self-aware joke late in the conversation:

"So in short, capitalism has made me what I am today." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The joke compresses a real claim. Joe did not get into coaching as a calling first and then look for a way to fund it. He got into coaching because his VC investments were not working, because he had been put in the position of VC by the collector's question, because that question came because he had been doing art that wealthy people bought, because he had been doing art at all because he had been trying to figure out what he wanted to do. The chain runs through capitalism the whole way. Without the structure of moneymaking activity, without needing to make his bad investments work, the coaching work would not have been generated.

The deeper claim under the joke is that the practitioner who positions themselves as too pure for business is missing the actual mechanism by which consciousness work gets done in the world. The work has to live inside the structures that move resources. Joe's career is one specific instance of how a person whose primary orientation is consciousness work can do that work while inside, and partly because of, the structures of capital.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When someone is asking how Joe ended up doing what he does
- When the conversation is about whether business and consciousness work can be the same thing
- When a client is artificially separating their inner work from their business work and the artificial-ness of the separation needs an example to make it visible
- When a client is in their 20s or 30s and asking "should I follow my interests or get serious about making money" and the answer is that the dichotomy is false
- When a coach or facilitator is asking how Joe came to coach the kind of clients he coaches — the answer is that he was inside their world as one of them for 12 years
- When the principle that capitalism can be a force for human healing needs a biographical anchor

## Tone

Direct, with the self-aware humor of someone who notices how unlikely the arc looks in retrospect. Joe tells this story without the spiritual-life-coach piety that would frame it as "I followed my calling and things worked out." The actual arc was messier: the family needed money, the collector asked a question, the answer led to 12 years of two parallel careers, the VC career failed in a way that produced the coaching career. Joe tells it that way.
