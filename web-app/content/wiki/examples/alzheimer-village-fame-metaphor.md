# Fame Is a Small Village With Alzheimer's

## The Metaphor

Joe attributes this to a famous comedian he heard talking about the experience of fame. The image:

"Being famous is like walking around with Alzheimer's. Everybody's very sweet and they know you, but you know nobody. Like you're in a small village with Alzheimer's and everybody's like, oh hi, you're like, I have no idea who you are." -- comedian, recounted by Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The structural feature: the asymmetric knowing. The famous person is approached as if they are known. They are approached by people they cannot recognize, holding histories they do not share, with familiarity they have not earned together.

The Alzheimer's frame is what makes the metaphor land. The famous person is not failing to remember; they never knew. But the social field around them treats them as if they should know. The pressure of repeated unrecognized recognitions produces the felt experience of being demented in a village of acquaintances.

## What the Metaphor Captures

The metaphor compresses several features of the fame experience that are otherwise hard to communicate:

- **The unfamiliarity of familiarity.** Strangers approach with warmth and pre-existing knowledge. The famous person has no matching warmth or knowledge to offer back.
- **The performance pressure of recognition.** Every encounter requires the famous person to manage someone else's expectation that they will be recognized in return. The performance is exhausting and depleting.
- **The dissolution of normal social calibration.** In a small village, you know who knows you. In a fame field, you do not. Every encounter is a fresh negotiation with someone you cannot place.
- **The character/person split.** "All these people relating to some character that you — that they think you are." The famous person is met as the projected character, not as the person. The projection runs the encounter; the person inside is invisible to the encounter.

## Why Joe Reaches for It

Joe uses this metaphor when:

- A coachee describes the experience of being in an authority position where they are approached differently than they were before
- A leader is starting to feel the weight of being recognized everywhere they go in their organization
- A CEO is describing the loneliness of the top in terms that suggest the asymmetric-knowing dynamic
- A teacher or coach is starting to be projected onto by students and is struggling with the projection
- The conversation needs an image that lets the listener feel the texture of the dynamic without long explanation

The metaphor is especially useful because it short-circuits the common dismissal "well, fame must be nice." The Alzheimer's frame makes vivid what is structurally lonely about fame in a way that the listener can feel rather than just acknowledge.

## How This Connects to Authority Issues

The metaphor is one specific instance of the broader [[authority-issues]] dynamic. The famous person is in the authority slot for the strangers approaching them. The strangers are running projections — the character they think the famous person is — and relating to the projection rather than the person.

The same structure runs at smaller scales. The CEO walking through their own company is approached by people running their own projections of the CEO. The teacher in a workshop is approached by students running projections. The parent in their own home is approached by children running projections. The fame metaphor is the high-volume version of a dynamic that runs whenever an authority position exists.

This is also why [[visualize-the-reality-of-leadership]] uses the same texture. The leader is asked to feel the social field that the projections produce. The fame metaphor is one way to evoke that field for someone who has not been in it.

## The Comedian Source

Joe attributes the metaphor to "a comedian" without naming them. The attribution carries weight because comedians have made a particular study of fame; the comedian's job often involves being publicly known in this asymmetric way. The image likely comes from a comedian articulating their own felt experience, which is why it lands with such precision.

The unnamed-comedian attribution is part of how Joe uses the example. The point is the image, not the source. The image stands on its own.

## What This Example Illustrates About Power and Loneliness

The metaphor is one piece of evidence for a broader observation Joe makes repeatedly: people in positions of significant authority or fame are often deeply lonely, and the loneliness is structural rather than personal. The position itself produces the asymmetric-knowing dynamic. The dynamic produces the loneliness regardless of who is in the position.

The example is useful in coaching because it lets the coachee locate their own experience inside a structural pattern rather than inside a personal failing. The famous comedian is not lonely because they are broken; they are lonely because the social field they inhabit produces loneliness as its output. Naming the structural production makes the loneliness less personal and therefore more workable.

## When to Use This Example in Coaching

- A coachee in a public role describing the disorientation of being recognized everywhere
- A leader struggling with the loneliness of authority who needs the loneliness to be normalized
- A coach explaining the structural features of authority dynamics to a coachee who is in a position of formal power
- A conversation about [[projections]] that needs a vivid example of projection running at scale

The example is also useful as a corrective when a coachee is dismissing their own struggle ("I'm so lucky to be in this position, I shouldn't complain"). The Alzheimer's image makes vivid that the position has real costs, regardless of its other features. The dismissal often softens once the listener can feel the texture of what is being dismissed.

## Tone

Joe delivers the metaphor with a small laugh. The comedian's framing is funny and Joe lets it be funny. The seriousness underneath — that the asymmetric-knowing is genuinely costly — comes through the laughter rather than against it. The metaphor lands harder because it is delivered with the lightness of recognition rather than the weight of analysis.
