# Joe's Expensive Way to Be Poor

## The Point It Illustrates

A life structured around what you don't want is just as much a constructed life as a life structured around what you want — and often more costly. The "I don't want money" stance felt principled. It was structurally a way of not owning a different want (his father's love). The rejection of the system was not freedom from the system. It was a sublimation of the actual want into an opposition.

This is the canonical illustration of [[dont-want-is-a-want]] operating across years of a person's life. It is also a companion piece to [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] — the same surrogate dynamic operating from the opposite stance. First Joe rejected money because his father pursued it; later he chased money because his father's approval came through it. Same underlying want; opposite surface behaviors.

## The Story

Earlier in his life, Joe was rejecting money. He thought it was bad. His father had wanted money; Joe had felt rejected by his father because of that pursuit; Joe concluded money was bad and refused to participate.

"When I was younger, I was living — I was rejecting money. I thought it was bad. Dad wanted money. I felt rejected by my dad because of his pursuit of money. So I thought money was bad. And I was living on very little money and I was very proud of that. And I was rejecting the system." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

He lived on roughly $10,000 to $12,000 a year. The frugality was a stance, not just a circumstance. He was proud of it. He believed the rejection of money was a rejection of the system that had hurt him.

A friend punctured the stance with a single observation:

"My friend said to me one day, came up to me and said, 'You know, it's really expensive the way that you're poor.' And I'm like, what? What are you talking about?" -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

## What the Friend Saw

The friend saw that the rejection of money was not actually producing freedom from money. It was producing a different relationship with money — one in which money's absence shaped the entire life, often more thoroughly than money's presence would have.

"I saw that I wanted the freedom from money, but to have freedom from money, I had to work really hard. Like I had to change my own transmission out of the van. I had to — somebody else didn't have to do it. I had to — I wanted to travel, so I had to work for like 10 hours to find that like one ticket to save me the $20 to get to the place. There was just like all the things that I had to do to live off of whatever it was, $10,000, $12,000 a year." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The freedom-from-money required constant labor in the service of staying free from money. The transmissions, the deal-hunting, the ten-hour ticket searches — all of it was a life shaped completely by money, just from the opposite stance than the people who pursued it. The rejection had as much grip as the pursuit would have.

## The Recognition

The realization arrived gradually rather than as a single moment:

"And I realized like, oh, there's no more freedom here than if I buy into the system. What I thought was buying into the system. And now that I'm on the other side of that and I'm, I'm like successful in the system, I no more agree with the system than I did back then. And I'm no more buying into it than I did back then. Like the whole thing was a construction of me not owning my wants." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

Two recognitions sit inside this passage:

1. The rejection-of-the-system phase had not produced more freedom than working inside the system would have.
2. The current successful-in-the-system phase was not a betrayal of the rejection. His relationship to the system had not changed. Only the surface behavior had changed.

The conclusion: the entire rejection phase was a construction. Not the system that was being rejected, but the rejection itself was constructed. It was a life built around a don't-want that was hiding a want.

## The Actual Want Underneath

"Which was really, I want my dad's love, which is what I, what was really what I wanted, had nothing to do with money. And so, so all of that was just, was just this weird constructed life built around not fully owning my wants." -- Joe Hudson, Ending The War With Your Wants

The actual want had nothing to do with money in either direction. It was the want for his father's love. The rejection-of-money was a sideways way of staying loyal to that want without exposing it (since admitting wanting his father's love would have required acknowledging it had not been given). The pursuit-of-money in his later VC career (see [[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]]) was a different sideways way of staying loyal to the same want.

Both stances were constructed around the unowned want. The actual integration came not from changing which surface behavior he ran but from owning the underlying want directly.

## When Joe Reaches For This

- When a client describes their identity through rejection of something (the system, money, fame, success, status)
- When the rejection looks principled but is producing as much grip as the pursuit would
- When [[dont-want-is-a-want]] needs an example to make the principle land
- When a client is in an early phase of rejection-of-something where the rejection is the identity
- When a client is doing the "expensive way to be poor" version of any other don't-want (rejecting fame is also expensive, rejecting power is also expensive, rejecting connection is also expensive)
- When the conversation needs to slow down and recognize that the don't-want is also a constructed life

## The Pattern Generalizes

The principle is not specific to money. Any life built around a "I don't want X" is a life shaped completely by X, just from the opposite stance. The person who built their life around rejecting religion is as shaped by religion as the religious person. The person who built their life around rejecting status is as shaped by status as the status-chaser. The person who built their life around rejecting their father is as shaped by their father as the person who built their life around pleasing their father.

The exit is the same in every case: find the want underneath the rejection. Own it directly. The shape the life takes after that is usually neither the rejection-shape nor its inverse. It is a third thing — the shape that comes from the actual want being the organizing energy instead of the avoidance of its surrogate.

## The Two-Phase Arc as the Full Picture

[[joes-pleasing-dad-money-story]] tells the second half of the arc. This story tells the first half. Together they illustrate the full pattern of a single unowned want producing two opposite life-phases that look unrelated from the outside.

Phase 1: Reject money because Dad pursued money. Build a life around the rejection. Notice it is expensive to be poor this way.

Phase 2: Pursue money in venture capital. Build a life around the pursuit. Notice in El Salvador that the pursuit was for Dad's love, not for money.

The two phases share the same underlying want (Dad's love) and the same surrogate (money). What changed across the phases was just the surface valence — first rejection, then pursuit. Neither phase was the actual want being owned. The third phase, after the hammock realization, is the one where the actual want gets named directly. From that point on, both the rejection of money and the chase of money lose their grip. Money becomes a tool because the want it was sublimating is no longer routing through it.

This two-phase shape is generalizable. Many people live a long phase of rejecting something they once felt rejected by, then a long phase of pursuing the same thing through a different surrogate, before they ever locate the actual want underneath both phases.
