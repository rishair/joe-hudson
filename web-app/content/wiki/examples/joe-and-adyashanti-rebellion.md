# Joe and Adyashanti

## The Story

Adyashanti became one of the most important figures in Joe's spiritual development. The relationship almost did not happen because Joe's rebellion against authority was strong enough to nearly disqualify the teacher before any actual contact.

The specific thought that almost prevented the relationship:

"Oh dude, look, look at you. You need your face on a book." -- Joe Hudson (recounting his earlier thought), Authority Issues

The dismissal was about Adyashanti having published books with his picture on them. Joe's rebellion-against-authority pattern reached for whatever justification was available to keep the teacher out. The picture on the book was the justification.

"I literally had that thought process around Adyashanti, who ended up being one of the more important figures in my life. 'Oh dude, look at you. You need your face on a book.' I just — whatever I did, whatever was required to make sure that I was better than the authority so I didn't have to feel my own longing for their approval or my own hurt for their disapproval or whatever the emotions were underneath." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

## What the Example Illustrates

**The rebel-position scans for disqualifiers.** When the authority issue is rebellion (Position 1 in [[the-four-authority-positions]]), the person's perception is selectively tuned to find reasons to dismiss anyone who might function as an authority. The reasons do not have to be substantive. The picture on the book is enough. The mansion is enough. The accent is enough. Whatever is available gets used.

The rebellion is not interested in the actual person being dismissed. It is interested in maintaining the disqualifier so the rebel does not have to encounter the longing underneath.

**Underneath the rebellion is longing.** Joe names this explicitly. The dismissal was a defense against "my own longing for their approval or my own hurt for their disapproval." The rebellion is not the absence of caring about the authority's regard; it is the presence of so much caring that the only way to manage it is to disqualify the source.

This is why "I don't care what they think" delivered with heat is often diagnostic of caring intensely. The energy of the dismissal is the size of the caring it is defending against.

**The cost of the rebellion is what gets missed.** Adyashanti became one of the most important figures in Joe's life. If the disqualifying thought had succeeded, the relationship would not have happened. The cost of authority issues is not abstract; it is specific. Each teacher dismissed is a relationship not had, a learning not received, a development not enabled.

Joe's broader version of this cost in the same conversation:

"I went through life not trusting any authority. There was, like, so, and therefore, like the idea of having mentorship, I couldn't even conceive of it until I was well into my 30s that somebody would mentor me. So, so I lost that... I just didn't trust any teacher. So I, I could read the book, but I wouldn't actually go and hang out with any kind of spiritual teacher because I couldn't trust them." -- Joe Hudson, Authority Issues

The Adyashanti near-miss is one instance of the broader pattern. The decades of pattern-running cost Joe access to the entire category of mentors and teachers. The recovery of even one teacher was a hard-won exception to the rule.

## What Almost Stopped It

The thought "you need your face on a book" worked as a disqualifier because it functioned as if it were a substantive critique. The thought has the form of an observation about Adyashanti. It is actually a move by Joe's psyche to keep Adyashanti at a safe distance.

The structural feature: the rebel position generates thoughts that look like assessments but function as defenses. The thoughts are not lies; they may even point at something real (Adyashanti did publish books with his picture on them). But the function the thought serves in the rebel's perception is to keep the authority safely dismissed.

The work for the rebel is to learn to recognize the dismissal-thoughts as such. Not to override them — that produces submission, not empowerment. To notice them: "I am about to dismiss this person. What is the dismissal protecting me from?" The longing or the hurt that surfaces is usually the actual material.

## The Adyashanti Move That Worked: Test It Yourself

There is a related side of this story that explains how Adyashanti and other teachers eventually got through to the young Joe despite the authority pattern. The instruction many of them gave was: don't trust me, test it for yourself.

That instruction was structurally compatible with the rebellion. Joe could not trust the teacher, but he could agree to test it. The instruction met the authority issue at exactly its sticking point. The teacher who insisted on being believed could not be received. The teacher who insisted on being doubted could be received -- because the doubting was permitted.

"Eventually I heard enough teachers say, don't trust me, uh, try it out for yourself, test it. And in fact, Adyashanti, the guy who, who who was a huge influence on me that I got over the picture clearly, uh, would, was one of those people who would say that. And so I did, and I took their advice. I, I said, okay, that's, that's something I can do. You want to put the power in my hands, that's where I can trust it. And so I started testing everything that I read." -- Joe Hudson, How Experiments Will Change Your Life

This is the origin of Joe's experimentation practice and the principle [[dont-take-my-word-for-it]]. The Adyashanti opening was the beginning of an experimental orientation that became one of Joe's central operating modes. The rebellion got Joe to the experiments. The experiments got Joe to everything that followed.

The pedagogical implication: a teacher working with a rebellious student should not try to be trusted. The instruction is the opposite. Tell them to doubt you and to test it. The trust will not come through being convinced; it will come through their own testing producing results they can verify themselves.

See [[evolution-of-the-experimenter]] for the broader developmental arc -- Joe's rebellion was Stage 1 of an experimentation practice that matured over decades into surrender.

## What Made the Relationship Possible Anyway

Joe does not explicitly describe what made the Adyashanti relationship happen despite the rebellion. The most likely candidates from his other teaching:

- The recognition of his own awakening through someone else (see [[case-the-consultant]] for the parallel story with Joe's mentor Case)
- The bathroom realization (see [[joes-bathroom-realization]]) creating an internal opening that softened the external dismissal
- The accumulated cost of the rebellion eventually outweighing its protective function

What Joe does name is that the relationship became one of the most important of his life, which is the after-the-fact recognition that the rebellion was about to cost him something irretrievable.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

- A coachee who is dismissing teachers, mentors, or potential authorities and not seeing what the dismissal is protecting them from
- A coachee in the rebel position (Position 1 of [[the-four-authority-positions]]) who needs the specific dynamic named
- A coachee whose intellectual critiques of authority are masking longing or hurt
- A coachee who is starting to recognize their rebellion as a pattern and needs to see the cost made concrete
- A coachee who has rejected someone they might benefit from and is asking whether to reconsider

The example is especially useful because Joe was wrong. He almost missed Adyashanti, and Adyashanti turned out to be one of the most important figures in his life. The example is not "be open to authority because it might be valuable." It is "the rebellion pattern is specifically designed to make you miss what would be most valuable." The rebel is not a bad reader of authority; the rebel is a person whose reader is calibrated to dismiss what they most need.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with humor and a touch of self-mockery. The "look, look at you" delivery is theatrical — he is performing the dismissive thought, not endorsing it. The recognition that follows is delivered with the warmth of someone who knows how close they came to missing something important and now teaches from that recognition.
