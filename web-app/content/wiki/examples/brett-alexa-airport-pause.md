# The Alexa Airport Pause

## The Story

Brett and his partner Alexa had been traveling. Brett realized he had left something at the airport. He immediately went into productivity mode: phone out, opening the customer service app, scanning for the support number, mentally rehearsing what to say to whoever picked up. The body had jumped to action. The action was anxious. The activity was fast.

Alexa interrupted. Her intervention was a single move: "What if you just paused for a moment and felt that anxiety for a second?"

Brett paused. He collapsed onto her shoulder for about five seconds. Whatever was in his chest discharged. Then, calmly, he sent a single message to the customer service app and put the phone down. Later, the response came back.

"It was a bunch of struggling just got saved by stopping to feel the anxiety." -- Brett Kistler, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

## What This Illustrates

The story makes one of Joe's structural points concrete: the productivity that runs on top of anxiety produces more work, not less. Brett's first move (the frantic phone-out, customer service app, rehearsing the call) was a pile of fear-driven motion. The motion was about to generate phone calls, message threads, follow-ups, frustrations, and a likely escalation -- all in service of "being productive about the lost item."

The pause did two things at once. It gave the anxiety a moment to be felt rather than acted on. And it gave the actual situation a moment to be assessed from outside the fear. Once the anxiety was felt, the appropriate action was small: one message, then put down the phone. The struggling that the anxiety would have produced did not happen.

Joe's response captures the generalization:

"That's such an incredibly useful productivity hack -- feel the fear behind the production that's happening, which is also something that's really ties back into where we started the conversation, which is what is the forest through the trees of productivity?" -- Joe Hudson, Your Obsession With Productivity Is Killing Your Productivity

## The Generalization

The story illustrates a move that applies far beyond lost items at airports. Any moment where productive activity is being generated by anxiety underneath, the structural shift available is the same:

1. Notice the body is in productivity mode.
2. Notice the productivity has an anxious signature -- fast, shallow breath, pushy.
3. Pause.
4. Feel the anxiety that the productivity is being generated from.
5. Once the anxiety is felt, the actual right action becomes visible. It is usually smaller, slower, and more effective than the anxious version would have been.

See [[stop-to-feel-the-anxiety-underneath-the-productivity]] for the move as a coaching instruction. See [[fear-is-about-feeling]] for the broader principle.

## Why the Five-Second Collapse Worked

The duration of the pause matters. The feeling did not require a meditation session or a long inquiry. Five seconds with the felt sense of the anxiety was enough. The discharge happened quickly because nothing was suppressing it -- Alexa's invitation was permission to feel, not instruction about how to feel. The body did what bodies do when given a moment of permission: discharged, integrated, and returned to a less reactive state.

This is consistent with what Joe describes elsewhere about how short the actual feeling of an emotion takes when not resisted. The story of being chased by waves of emotion comes from resisting, not from the emotion itself. Held in a moment of presence, the wave passes. See [[wisdom-of-each-emotion]] and [[meet-with-tenderness]].

## When To Use This Story

The story is short, concrete, and approachable. It works well as a teaching example when:

- A person is reporting that their productivity is generating more work, not less
- A person is in the middle of describing a recent moment of frantic activity
- The coaching context is professional or productivity-focused and the person needs an entry point to the "feel the underneath" move that does not feel too emotional or therapeutic
- The person needs to see that the pause is brief (five seconds), not a major intervention
- A partner-relationship example would land better than a solo-practice example
- The teaching is about the gap between activity and effectiveness

The story does not work as well when the underlying emotion is something other than anxiety, or when the situation is much higher stakes (an active crisis, a grief, a major loss). The lost-item-at-airport scale is part of why the story is useful -- it normalizes the move at the level where most productivity-anxiety actually happens.

## The Implicit Coaching Move Alexa Made

Alexa's intervention is itself worth examining. She did not:
- Tell Brett to calm down (which usually escalates)
- Take the phone from him
- Solve the problem for him
- Diagnose his pattern
- Offer advice

She asked one question: "What if you just paused for a moment and felt that anxiety for a second?"

The question is small. It is delivered without judgment. It assumes Brett is capable of doing the thing it suggests. It contains the entire move: pause, feel, stay with it for "a second" (not forever). The brevity is permission. The question itself does the work.

This is a useful template for the coaching move. See [[pause-feel-then-act]] and [[stop-to-feel-the-anxiety-underneath-the-productivity]] for the more general forms.

## The Forest Connection

The deeper layer the story connects to: the question of why the productivity is happening at all. Brett's anxiety about the lost item was about something. What was the lost item supposed to protect? What did its loss threaten? Most productivity-anxiety, when felt, reveals an underlying assumption about safety, identity, or loss that is much larger than the immediate trigger.

Joe makes this connection explicit by pointing back to the conversation's opening question: "what is the forest through the trees of productivity?" The anxious productivity is always about something deeper than the visible task. The pause is the way in to that deeper thing. The deeper thing, felt, releases the surface productivity from having to carry it. See [[what-is-the-productivity-for]].

## Tone

The story is told warmly and briefly. Both Brett and Joe relate it without making it precious. The lesson is small and the delivery is small. The lightness keeps the teaching available to people who would resist a more elaborate framing.
