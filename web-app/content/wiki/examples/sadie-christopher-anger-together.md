# Sadie and Christopher: Getting Angry Together (at the Illness, Not Each Other)

## The Story

A married couple came on the podcast in two parts. The first session was Joe coaching Christopher about his chronic illness. The second session, a month later, was Christopher and his wife Sadie together.

Sadie's stated concern was vague: she just wanted deeper connection with Christopher and more empathy for him when he was processing. She had "no end goal," she was "just open." This itself was the diagnostic. The vagueness was the symptom of years of putting her own wants in second place.

The session unfolded through five distinct moves.

## Move 1: Locate Sadie's Actual Experience

Joe asked Sadie how Christopher's chronic illness had been for her. The answer surfaced quickly: "It's more exhaustion. It's not every once in a while that things come up. It's all the time, every day, for hours every day. And it's just been so much for so long that I think I'm just tired."

The exhaustion was years long. She felt hopeless that it would always be this way. She felt guilty for being exhausted because Christopher's situation was harder than hers.

## Move 2: Find the Should

Joe asked: "What's the right amount of time that you should be holding space for him?"

The question surfaced the structural constraint. Sadie believed she should have "unlimited time, unlimited space." She felt selfish for being at her limit. The exhaustion was being compounded by the guilt about the exhaustion.

When Joe asked what created the sadness she was feeling as the question arrived, she said: "I feel like a really bad partner, because part of me feels like I should, I should have unlimited space for him." See [[im-exhausted-holding-space-for-my-partner]].

## Move 3: Trace the Voice to Its Origin

Sadie said she "gaslights herself" -- the voice in her head says "you're fine, toughen up, deal with this."

Joe: "Whose voice is that?"

The childhood origin surfaced: her parents. "Just toughen up and deal with it on your own... emotions were never welcome."

Joe then had her speak directly to the source. He stood in for the parents: "I'll be that whoever those people are... you speak to me directly." Sadie expressed for the first time what she had needed to express for years: "You tried your best, but you ultimately failed me. And I needed you. And you were never there... I'm mad at you for that." See [[become-the-authority-figure]].

## Move 4: Have Christopher Voice the Welcome

Joe then turned to Christopher: "Just to, in your own words, if it's true for you, can you say something to Sadie like, 'I never want you to deal with my emotions. I only want you to be with me when you feel like you can.'"

Christopher: "Baby, I never want you to deal with my emotions for me... I love all of you, every part. There's nothing you can show me that I won't be able to love. Any emotion, anything. You can say anything to me, you can feel anything with me. I love you for everything."

Joe watched the reception: "You let that in. Usually when this happens, the person doesn't let it in, but I saw you fully let that in. You had like one little flash maybe where you didn't believe something." See [[have-partner-state-the-welcome-aloud]] and [[letting-it-in-vs-deflecting-the-welcome]].

## Move 5: Agree With Christopher's Depression

Joe noted that Sadie believed Christopher needed her to be strong. He challenged the belief: "He's the one that's actually handling all of his emotions and going through his trauma. That's some strength. What does he need?"

To test it, Joe had Christopher voice his depression and asked Sadie only to agree, not argue him out of it.

Christopher: "Yeah, fuck, I'm never gonna get better. I'm just always gonna fucking release trauma... it's never gonna get better. Yeah, it just fucking sucks."

Sadie: "Yeah, shit."

Christopher then went deeper than he had been able to before: "Sometimes I don't even want to live anymore, and it's scary. It feels good."

See [[agree-with-the-depression-dont-talk-them-out]].

## Move 6: Anger Release Together

Joe noticed both partners smiled when he said the word "anger." The smile was the body's recognition that something had been held back. He asked: "How often have you guys just gotten fucking angry about this?"

Christopher: "Never, I think."

Joe then directed them through anger release at the situation, with both partners moving through both forms -- victim anger and dominating anger -- in turn.

**Sadie's victim form:** "You have ruined my life. You have taken over every part of me. I hate you. You have crushed my spirit. You have changed my goals. My dreams are shattered, and I fucking hate you."

**Sadie's dominating form (after Joe modeled it):** "You're not gonna ruin any more of my life. I am done taking your shit. I'm gonna do what I want to do and when I want to do it... you can stay in your dark wormy hole and die."

Sadie after: "It felt good. Like, I kind of wanted to, you know, stab something, maybe. Yeah, a little violent. Yeah, yeah, it felt really nice, actually."

**Christopher's victim form first slid into sadness:** "I'm so fucking over dealing with you all the fucking time... You're making me feel like I'm weak, but I'm not fucking weak. I'm just tired of feeling like this." Joe named that the anger was sliding into sadness and directed toward the dominating form.

**Christopher's dominating form:** "Yeah, no, I ain't going to stop. You can say whatever the fuck you want. I'm gonna lean back in my chair and savor this fucking moment because I'm strong. I don't need your bullshit."

See [[victim-anger-vs-dominating-anger]].

## The Result

Both partners reported feeling good. The mutual realization that landed: "We're bonding over -- because this could easily be the thing where it's directed, misdirected at each other, and then one person gets to express this, but the other person just feels pushed down."

Joe named what had shifted: "You can be together in the sadness of it, and you can be together in the anger of it, and you can be together in the victim of it. Nobody has to take care of each other here. You can just be together."

Christopher's closing: "If you think that showing your emotion is ever something that makes me love you less, this is not -- I love this and I love all of you... There's nothing you can show me that I won't be able to love."

## What This Example Illustrates

**Exhaustion was unexpressed anger.** Years of holding it together had presented as fatigue. The anger had been waiting. When the anger moved, the relief was immediate. See [[exhaustion-is-unexpressed-anger]].

**The strong-one belief was the trap.** Sadie's belief that Christopher needed her to be strong had organized her behavior for years. The actual evidence was the opposite -- Christopher's emotional work was the strength, and Sadie's suppression was costing her.

**Both anger forms are required.** The session demonstrated cleanly that victim anger and dominating anger move different layers. Both partners needed coaching to access the form that was harder for them. See [[victim-anger-vs-dominating-anger]].

**Anger at a shared situation bonds, not fractures.** The same anger energy that had been getting misdirected at each other for years moved cleanly through both bodies when directed at the chronic illness. The result was connection, not damage. This is the cleanest demonstration in the compendium of what becomes possible when [[dont-do-anger-at-your-partner]] is honored and the anger is allowed to move at the actual source.

**Agreeing with the depression opened the descent.** Sadie's "yeah, shit" let Christopher go deeper than he had been able to before. The agreement was the prerequisite for the anger work that followed. See [[agree-with-the-depression-dont-talk-them-out]].

## What the Session Did Not Do

It did not solve the chronic illness. Christopher's illness remained at the end of the session.

It did not fully unwind Sadie's voice in the head. When Joe asked if Christopher's welcome was enough to stop the voice telling her to toughen up, Sadie said no. The voice still needed its own work.

It did not produce a permanent shift in a single session. What it did was open a new repertoire: getting angry together at the situation became a thing they could now do.

## Joe's Closing Reflection

"It's totally okay to say, hey, I can't do the emotional work right now. I can't be with that big emotion right now. It's totally fucking okay. To tell yourself you should be with it is actually not being with it. So either you're capable in that moment or you're not, and that's fine. Like, whatever that is, that's totally fine. And the more you can honor it, the more you'll be able to be there the way that you want to be there." -- Joe Hudson, I Can't Hold It All Anymore

The closing teaching: capacity grows from honoring the limit, not from overriding it. The welcoming-the-other's-emotion capacity that Sadie wanted to grow into would come from her own emotional liberation, not from forcing herself past her actual limit.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

This example is reached for when:
- Working with couples where one is chronically ill, in recovery, or doing long-term trauma processing
- The partner holding space is exhausted and the relationship has become asymmetric
- The anger work is needed and the couple has been directing it at each other
- The both-forms distinction needs an illustration of how victim and dominating anger run together
- Demonstrating that anger work can be bonding rather than fracturing in the right setup
