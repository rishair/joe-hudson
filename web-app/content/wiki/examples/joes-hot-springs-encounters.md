# Joe's Hot Springs Encounters

## The Story

Joe is at a hot springs. Two encounters happen there. Together, they name the structural problem Joe is currently working with and the corrective move he is finding for it.

**Encounter one — the recognition.** Joe wakes up out of bed shirtless. He is wandering to the bathroom, half-asleep. Somebody says, "Joe Hudson?" Joe says yes. The recognition cascades. Six women. "I took your course." "I heard about your course." Joe's internal experience: "I just want to go to Vipassana alone."

**Encounter two — the man at Walmart.** Later. Joe is hanging out at the springs and a guy is there. They talk for a couple of minutes. The man tells a single story. He had a moment at a Walmart when he helped a woman interpret one of her dreams, and it made a difference in her life. As the man tells the story, he starts weeping.

Joe's recognition watching the man weep: "You can't do that if it's not personal."

## What the Two Encounters Together Demonstrate

The first encounter is the structural reality Joe is having to learn to hold: the focal-point experience. He is now recognized in private moments by strangers because of the reach of his work. The recognition is not violating — the women are not aggressive — but it does close off the kind of solitude in a bathroom that an unrecognized person can take for granted. The internal pull is toward more withdrawal. "I just want to go to Vipassana alone." The structural temptation, once a person has become a focal point, is to retreat from contact, to abstract the audience into "the people who know my work," to stop being personally available to anyone outside an inner circle.

The second encounter is the corrective. The man at Walmart had been deeply moved by helping one specific person. He had not abstracted her into "someone I helped." She was specific. The moment had stayed alive in him. He could not tell the story without weeping. Joe's recognition was that the man's personal contact with the woman was the thing that made the impact transmissible. Without that personal-ness, the story would have been a story about a thing he did. With it, the story was a transmission of why the work matters.

The same applies on the teacher's side. Joe can structurally protect himself from the focal-point energy by abstracting the people his work touches into "students," "the audience," "the people who took my course." The abstraction is safer. It also removes the personal-ness that the man at Walmart was demonstrating. Without the personal-ness, the teacher becomes a function. With it, the teacher remains a person.

## Why This Is The Distinguishing Example for Permeability

This pair of encounters is the canonical illustration of why the move from dissolution to permeability matters at the developmental phase Joe is currently working in. See [[dissolution-of-self-vs-permeability-of-self]] for the distinction.

Dissolution alone — "it's not me, it's coming through me" — would have served the first encounter perfectly. Joe could have nodded politely to the women, smiled, said something neutral, and walked into the bathroom. The encounter would have been managed cleanly. The dissolution frame would have held it.

But the dissolution frame would not have made the second encounter functional. The man at Walmart was not channeling anything. He was being personal. The contact he had with the woman he had helped was deeply personal contact, and the weeping was the evidence that the contact was still alive in his body. If Joe were to encounter that man from inside the dissolution frame ("the impact of my work moves through me, the personal is set aside"), Joe would not actually be in contact with the man. Joe would be performing presence while remaining structurally apart.

The permeability frame allows the second encounter. Joe can be moved by the man. Joe can let the man's weeping land in his own chest. Joe can let the larger pattern the encounter points to — the personal-ness as the substrate of impact — actually break his heart open. The frame restores the personal that the dissolution frame had set aside.

## The Quote That Compresses the Insight

"To be intimate with that experience is incredibly personal. To allow that to break my heart, I know that, that, that to allow that in on a personal level, on an intimate level, is gonna be required for me not to become dismissive of people, to not — there's this whole thing about power that like, as you have more power, you lose empathy. There's like a whole bunch of studies on it. For me not to lose that empathy, actually requires me to allow that to break my heart on a consistent basis, like this guy in this hot spring." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The phrase "like this guy in this hot spring" is the moment Joe is pointing back to the man at Walmart as the model. The man at Walmart is what staying in contact looks like. The structural challenge for Joe at the current edge is whether he can hold his growing platform while preserving the quality the man at Walmart had with the one woman whose dream he helped interpret.

## What the First Encounter Names

The first encounter — the shirtless half-asleep recognition by six women — names the structural condition that is now Joe's life. He is recognizable. Strangers in semi-public bathrooms know who he is. The privacy that an unrecognized person takes for granted is no longer available to him in the same way.

The honest internal response — "I just want to go to Vipassana alone" — is the honest reaction to the loss. It is not the response to act on. The response to act on is figuring out how to hold the focal-point energy without retreating from contact altogether.

The framing Joe arrives at is that the platform itself is "a dream come true that I never thought could happen. I never even imagined the possibility of it." He references saying this to Adyashanti (see [[joe-and-adyashanti-rebellion]]). The reach of the work is not something Joe wants to wish away. It is something he is having to learn to hold without it warping him. The two hot springs encounters together — the focal-point reality and the man at Walmart — show him what holding it well looks like.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When the conversation is about how to hold growing influence without losing the personal-ness that makes the work real
- When a coach or teacher is facing the focal-point experience for the first time
- When the structural risk of empathy decay with growing influence ([[power-erodes-empathy]]) needs a concrete example of the corrective
- When the distinction between dissolution and permeability needs an instance that makes both visible
- When a client is wondering how to remain in personal contact with the people their work affects as the number of affected people grows
- When the model is being made for what "staying touched by impact" looks like in a real life

## Tone

Reflective. Joe is naming his current edge. He is not delivering a settled teaching. He is describing the two encounters and what they made visible to him about the next move in his own work. The tone of using the story should match: the man at Walmart as a model, not as an instruction, and the first encounter as the felt structural reality rather than a complaint.
