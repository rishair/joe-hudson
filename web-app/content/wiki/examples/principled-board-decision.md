# The Principled Board Decision (You Build a Company to Build the Best Company, Not to Sell It)

## The Story

Early in Joe's venture capital career he sits in a board meeting that has the texture of a crisis. The company is in a merger and acquisition conversation -- the big anticipated exit. The deal is falling apart. The buyer is pulling back. Everyone on the board is freaking out, trying to figure out what to do to save it.

There is one person at the table who is operating differently. He is not freaking out. He is going through his principles, one by one.

One of his principles: you build a company to make the best company, not to sell it. The implication for the moment:

"If they don't want to buy the company, we don't chase. If we chase, the best case scenario, we get a lower price. If we're like, fine, best case scenario is they go, oh shit, and they come back. Great. But in the meantime, we're just going to continue to build the best company we can build." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

He moves through five principles. The decision drops out the other side. It is made quickly. He does not lose his center in the process.

## What Happened After

The decision turned out to be correct -- not in the sense that they got what they wanted in the moment, but in the sense that the longer arc validated the principle.

"It was just principally decided, bam. He had like 5 principles and he went through all of them. These are my principles. This is what I see works. And he just did it and he never lost himself. And it was, absolutely the right decision for the company. We made a lot more money on that company. And funnily, when they came back to buy it, it was that same company that came back to buy us and paid a lot more money a year later." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

The buyer that wanted to walk away a year earlier came back and paid more. The principled refusal to chase had been the better move on both axes -- both the principle was held and the outcome was better.

## What This Example Teaches

This is Joe's anchor story for [[principles-free-you]] in the decision-making context. Several things land at once:

**The principle handles the decision.** The board member does not deliberate. He runs through his principles, the action is implied, he acts. There is no internal political theater. The decision-feel never arises because the fear of consequence has nothing to grip.

**The principled stance preserves the center.** Everyone else at the table is losing their composure. The board member is steady. The steadiness is not stoicism or willpower; it is the natural consequence of having internalized the principles. The fear that would have destabilized him has been pre-handled by the principle.

**Outcomes are not the test.** The board member, Joe makes a point of saying, would not have cared if the principle had not paid off in this case. He would have stayed with the principle and continued to evaluate it over time. See [[decisions-as-portfolio]].

"They didn't work, I don't think it would have mattered to him at all, except for maybe he would have looked long-term, oh, are my principles wrong, right?" -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

**Principles include short-term ego cost.** Joe explicitly names that the principled decision feels like shit in the moment for the ego, the identity, the short-term gain. The principle is what lets the person do the thing anyway.

"It means I am going to do something that feels like shit right now for as far as my ego goes, as far as my identity goes, as far as like short-term gain goes, because I, I know these principles work, and so I'm going to live by these principles. And they worked." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

## What Joe Took From Watching It

Before this moment Joe had heard people talk about principled decision-making and not really understood it. The board room was where it became visible.

"I remember it was like -- And then what happened was I was working with somebody who worked exclusively on principled decision-making. And it was over this deal. It was early in my venture capital career... And I saw him do it. I saw him make a great decision really, really quickly, and I saw him not lose his center in the, in the process." -- Joe Hudson, How Can I Make Better Decisions?

The transmission was experiential. Watching it happen, watching the steadiness in the middle of the chaos, watching the speed of the decision and the long-term validation -- this is what made principled decision-making something Joe internalized as a practice rather than as an idea.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

- When teaching [[principles-free-you]] in the business or leadership context
- When a leader is wrestling with a high-stakes decision and trying to predict the outcome
- When someone is collapsing under the immediate pressure of a deal and losing their center
- When the question is how to act in crisis without becoming the crisis
- When teaching [[leaders-calm-becomes-the-teams-calm]] -- the board member's calm did not come from suppression; it came from the principles having pre-handled the fear

## The Pattern That Sits Underneath

This is one of Joe's stories that demonstrates the practical link between principles, decision-quality, and outcome. The principled decider:

1. Has fewer decision-experiences because principles dissolve most decisions before they form
2. Acts faster when decisions do arise because the principle does the weighing
3. Stays centered under pressure because the fear has been pre-handled
4. Often gets better outcomes anyway, both because the principle is well-calibrated and because the principled stance changes the relational dynamics

The board member illustrates all four in one scene.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with the slight reverence of recounting a formative apprenticeship moment. The detail of "I saw him do it" matters; the example is not abstract teaching but lived transmission.
