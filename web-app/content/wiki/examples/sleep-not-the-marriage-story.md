# The Hot Springs Story: It Was Sleep, Not the Marriage

## The Story

After the second child was born, Joe and his wife were in a period of constant fighting. They had not been sleeping enough — Joe says "geez, whatever it was" — and the relationship was tense. They were exploring the question: what is wrong with the marriage? Do we need counseling? Is this about me? Is this about her?

A friend offered to take the children so each of them could go to a hot springs to sleep. Joe went first. He slept 18 hours the first night and 14 hours the second night, "just dead to the world." When he came back, his wife went and slept the same amount.

When they were both back from the trips, the marriage problems were gone.

"We came home and there wasn't any problems in the marriage anymore. And it blew me away because we had always thought about if there's any problems in the marriage, it's because something's going on emotionally or psychologically. It was just about sleep. There was no actual problem in the marriage." -- Joe Hudson, You Need The Stress You Hate

## The Point

The named cause of the stress — "the marriage" — was not the actual cause. The actual cause was at a different layer entirely: sleep deprivation. No amount of work on the marriage at the cognitive, emotional, or relational level would have addressed the actual problem, because the actual problem was not at any of those layers.

The story is the canonical example of the principle that what we think we are stressed about is often not what we are stressed about. See [[investigate-the-actual-source-of-stress]].

The story is also the foundation of Joe's investigation move. After this experience, Joe started running the investigation in his own life systematically: "Oh, if I'm stressed, is it because of the thoughts that I'm having? Is it because I'm not having enough sleep? Is it because I am emotionally overwhelmed?"

## What Makes the Example Powerful

Several features of the story make it a clean teaching example:

1. **The named cause is plausible.** A new baby, constant fighting, marital stress — the inference "the marriage is the problem" is the natural one. The story is not about a wildly mistaken attribution. It is about a reasonable attribution that was nonetheless wrong.

2. **The intervention was minimal.** Joe and his wife did not do anything to address the marriage. They each slept. The marriage was not worked on. The marriage simply resolved when the underlying sleep deprivation was addressed.

3. **The result was immediate and total.** Not "the marriage got better." There were "no problems in the marriage anymore." The contrast is total.

4. **The discovery contradicts the assumed framework.** Joe and his wife had assumed marital problems must be emotional or psychological. The story is a direct contradiction of that frame. The frame had to be revised.

The story works as a teaching device precisely because it is so unflashy. The intervention is "sleep." The result is "the problem disappeared." The lesson is irresistible: maybe what I am stressed about is not what I am stressed about.

## When Joe Uses This Story

Joe reaches for this story in several contexts:

- When someone presents a relational or emotional stress that has not resolved through relational or emotional work.
- When introducing [[investigate-the-actual-source-of-stress]] as a move and needs a concrete demonstration.
- When teaching [[four-causes-of-stress]] and needs to show how the nervous-system layer can drive what looks like a thought-or-emotion-layer problem.
- When a person is doing extensive therapy or coaching on a relationship and the underlying sleep, nervous-system, or physical reality has not been examined.

The story functions as a permission slip. If even Joe — who has done the inner work — was completely wrong about what was causing his marriage stress, then the listener can also consider that they might be wrong about what is causing theirs.

## The Generalization

Sleep is not the only candidate for "the unexpected actual cause." The story generalizes to any case where the named cause and the actual cause are at different layers. Common patterns:

- Marital stress that is actually sleep deprivation. (This story.)
- Work stress that is actually unfelt emotion. See [[investigate-the-actual-source-of-stress]] for the parallel story Joe tells about the work-stress that dissolved when he had a big cry.
- Money stress that is actually identity protection ("I need to look successful").
- Parenting stress that is actually no unstructured time for the self.
- The stress about the stress being most of the stress. See [[stress-as-rejection-of-stress]].

The structural lesson: whenever stress is operating, the actual cause may be at a different layer than the named cause. The investigation is the move.

## What This Example Is Not

The example is not "all marriage problems are actually sleep problems." It is "what you think it's about is sometimes not what it's about." The story is anti-categorical, not categorical.

It is also not "you don't need to work on the marriage if you can sleep more." Real marital problems exist. The story is about a case where the marital problems were not real, and the investigation revealed that. The investigation might also reveal that there is a real problem alongside the sleep issue. Both can be true.

## The Connection to Stress Investigation

The story is the origin moment for Joe's broader investigation practice. Before the hot springs, he assumed stress had to be addressed at the level it presented. After the hot springs, he started running the multi-layer check on every stress that arose. The new layer he added — "is it actually about sleep / nervous system / something I haven't felt?" — became one of his standard moves.

This is what makes the story a foundational example. It is not just an illustration. It is the conversion experience that produced a methodology. The methodology — running the multi-layer investigation — became part of Joe's standard approach to stress in himself and in coaching. See [[investigate-the-actual-source-of-stress]] and [[four-causes-of-stress]].

## Tone

Plain, slightly amused, retrospective. Joe tells the story without dramatizing the marital fighting and without glorifying the resolution. The tone is "and then this surprising thing happened, and it changed how I look at this." The lightness is what makes the lesson accessible — if the lesson were delivered solemnly, it would not produce the small grin of recognition that the story produces.
