# Joe's International Childhood

## The Story

Joe grew up in five countries: Iran, Saudi Arabia, Wales, England, and Switzerland. He left Iran as the 1979 revolution was happening. He spent formative years in each of these contexts, watching cultures from inside them rather than from the outside.

The structural consequence of growing up that way installed an early intuition: most of what people in any one culture take to be "the way things are" is, on inspection, culturally specific. A child who has lived inside five cultures cannot easily accept any one of them as the natural form of things. The relative-ness is built into the perception before the conceptual capacity to articulate it arrives.

## What This Produced Cognitively

Joe describes the cognitive effect explicitly in the conversation with Brett:

"You go to another culture and you see how you learn so much more about your own culture by seeing it in contrast to another one." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The mechanism is comparison. A single culture, observed without contrast, looks like reality. Two cultures in contrast reveal that what looked like reality is, in part, the texture of one specific organization of human life. Five cultures in contrast make the relative-ness operational at the level of perception. The child does not have to argue themselves into seeing what is culture and what is human; the seeing is just there.

Joe adds the deeper recognition this produced:

"You get to see, oh, all of this stuff that's all choice. That's all culture. All of this stuff, that's humanity." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The distinction "this is culture / this is humanity" became operational early. Whatever varies across cultures is culture. Whatever is invariant across cultures is humanity. The exposure to enough cultures to make the distinction operational is, Joe is suggesting, one of the structural roots of his lifelong interest in what humans actually are underneath their cultural arrangements. See [[cross-cultural-exposure-reveals-the-arbitrary]] for the developed principle.

## The Continuation Into Adult Experience-Gathering

The intuition the international childhood installed continued to operate in Joe's twenties. He did not stop exploring cultures when he became an adult. He kept doing it, more deliberately. He hung out with tribes — at least 6 or 7 in America and at least 2 or 3 outside America. He drove with hitchhikers into cowboy bars (see [[joes-cold-beer-friday-cowboys]]). He worked at a fish cannery in Togiak, Alaska (see [[joes-togiak-fish-cannery]]). He hung out with the homeless and with billionaires in the same week.

The tribal exposure in particular extended the cross-cultural intuition. The child who has been in five countries plus the adult who has been in 10+ tribes has been inside enough versions of human social organization that the question "what is humanity essentially?" becomes a genuine investigation rather than a theoretical question.

"What's, what's human? What is the essence of who we are? What are we essentially?" -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The question is the genuine question. The international childhood gave it operational urgency. The adult experience-gathering kept feeding the investigation. The combined data set is one of the substrates Joe coaches from. He can speak to what is human across an unusual breadth of contexts because he has been inside an unusual breadth of contexts.

## The Specific Survival Skill That Pre-Dated The Childhood Exposure

The childhood reading-people skill came from a more painful source than the international moves alone. Joe's father was an alcoholic. The child who lives in a house with a volatile alcoholic parent has to read the parent's state very quickly to know whether the moment is safe. The reading skill becomes survival.

"I don't know particularly, like I can't think of a story as to how that happened. It just was always there for me. And I think if I had to guess, it would be like trying to read my father when, like, part of survival is that when your father walks in the room, he's an alcoholic, you better understand that shit really quick or you're gonna get in trouble." -- Joe Hudson, How I Learned To Stop Fighting Myself

The combination of "I have to read my volatile father" plus "I keep being moved to new cultures" plus "I am the family black sheep so I am not supposed to fit" produced an unusual orientation toward humans. The reading skill is operational. The cross-cultural comparison-frame is operational. The role permits exploration. Together: a child who is going to grow up to be the kind of adult who finds it natural to be in the room with whoever, watching humans be human, looking for what is true under what is culture.

## When Joe Reaches For This Story

- When someone is asking how Joe came to his orientation toward humans
- When a conversation requires an example of how cross-cultural exposure shapes the perception of what is culture and what is human
- When a young person is considering whether to expose themselves to multiple cultures and is wondering what the long-arc value of doing so is
- When a parent is asking whether moving their children internationally is worth the disruption
- When someone is asking what produces a person who can be at home in many different rooms
- When the conversation is about the early roots of vocational interest in humans (psychology, coaching, anthropology)

## Tone

Matter-of-fact, without exoticizing the international childhood. Joe does not present it as glamorous or as a privilege to be performed. He presents it as a fact about his formation that had specific cognitive consequences, and those consequences happen to be useful for the work he later did. The international childhood is one of the inputs to who he became, alongside the alcoholic father, the black-sheep role, and the early career of experience-collection.
