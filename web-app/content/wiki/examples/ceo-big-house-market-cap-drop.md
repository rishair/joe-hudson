# The CEO Big House Study

## The Study

Joe references a tax-record analysis (he believes European, possibly Dutch) that correlated specific CEO life events with subsequent market-cap impact on their companies. Many predictable life events affected company performance: death of a child, divorce, the death of a mother-in-law. Each of these registered.

One finding was unexpected. CEOs who built a house over a certain size — Joe recalls around 5,000 or 6,000 square feet — presided over an approximately 10% drop in market cap afterward.

"One of the things that affected businesses dramatically, like I think it was a 10% reduction in market cap, is if the CEO built a house that was over 5,000, 6,000 square feet, something like that." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

## The Mechanism

The size of the house is not the cause of the market-cap drop. The house is a signal of a shift in the CEO's orientation. Building a big house is one of the most concrete behavioral expressions of "I've made it." It marks the moment when the business has converted, in the CEO's internal world, from the work to the source of the rewards. The attention follows the orientation.

"It's the idea of like, yeah, I know, right? And it's like, oh, I've made it, or my attunement is towards something else. And because the business wasn't the thing. The business was a means to an end. And so my focus can be pulled away from it." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

The market notices the drift, eventually. The 10% is the financial signature of a shift the CEO may not even recognize they have made.

## What This Illustrates

The study is the data anchor for one of the core teachings in [[business-as-spiritual-practice]]: business as a means to an end produces a particular failure mode. The end-state arrives, and the business is then no longer the focus, and the business degrades.

The contrast is business as the practice itself — the means and the end together. Under this orientation, the question "have I made it?" does not arise because there is no it to make. The work continues to be the point. The attention does not drift because there is nowhere else for it to go.

"Whereas if business is a spiritual practice, it is the ends. It is the means and the ends. And so that's what allows you to fully be engaged in a way that doesn't burn you out and engaged in a way that you don't want to run away from it." -- Joe Hudson, Business as a Spiritual Practice

This connects to the broader pattern in [[improvement-mindset-vs-maintenance-mindset]] and [[epiphany-becomes-rut]]: the moment a person crosses into "I have arrived" is the moment the corrosion begins, in business, in marriage, in personal development. The arrival fantasy is itself the failure mode.

## When the Story Is Used

The study lands particularly well with leaders who have either reached or are about to reach a financial milestone they have been working toward. The natural assumption is that the milestone is the goal, and crossing it produces freedom. The study shows the opposite: crossing without re-orienting produces drift, and drift produces decline.

It is also useful when a coachee is operating from a means-to-end frame and has not seen that the frame itself is the problem. The study is data, not a story Joe invented. It is harder to dismiss than a moral about orientation. The data point becomes the entry into the deeper teaching about what business actually is when it is operating well.

## A Note on Sourcing

Joe attributes the study to European tax records, possibly Dutch, and notes some uncertainty about the exact details. The 10% figure and the 5,000–6,000 square foot threshold are approximate as he recalls them. The structural claim — that the CEO's investment of the rewards from the business correlates with subsequent decline — is what carries the teaching. The exact numbers are illustrative rather than definitive.
