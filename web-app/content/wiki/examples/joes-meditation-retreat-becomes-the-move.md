# Joe's Meditation Retreat Becomes the Move

## The Story

Joe had a 7-8 day meditation retreat planned. The timing overlapped with a household move that he and his wife Alexa had just signed a lease for. Alexa had a broken foot. Joe knew Alexa would attempt the move regardless of the foot. He saw two options.

**Option 1: Go to the retreat.** Spend 7-8 days meditating while knowing Alexa was moving with a broken foot. Return to the aftermath. Sit through the retreat with the constant background awareness of what he was not doing.

**Option 2: Cancel the retreat.** Stay and help with the move. Bring the meditation practice into the move itself.

He chose option 2.

"And I don't know, like, yeah, I'm confused because I wasn't expecting to enjoy moving so much. But I did because suddenly I was like, this is my intention. I'm doing my meditation retreat and I'm moving." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The outcome: he had plenty of time to move and also to be with himself during the process. The experience was extremely meditative. The move itself became the retreat.

## What the Example Demonstrates

The example surfaces several interlocking teachings:

**The false-vacation trap.** A common pattern in [[time-scarcity]]: working really hard to get to a vacation, retreat, or break, then spending the break preoccupied with what was left behind or with what is waiting on return. The break is consumed by the very stress it was supposed to relieve. The vacation never actually happens because the person never actually arrives at it.

"It's fascinating also how we can create so much time scarcity around creating space to not feel time scarcity, like work really, really hard to go on the vacation and then imagine that we will actually relax. And not feel stressed about everything we're gonna have to do when we get back." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The pattern is self-defeating. The energy expended to create the break, plus the energy lost during the un-restful break, plus the energy required to catch up after the break, is often greater than the energy that would have been needed to be present in normal life.

**Practice is not location-dependent.** The retreat was attractive because it represented a place where presence was available. But presence is not actually located in retreat centers. It is located in the person who is present. Bringing the practice to where the body already is removes the trip-cost and removes the artificial separation between "the practice" and "the life."

"Why not bring that practice into the moving process? And so that's what I did." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

**Intention restructures the activity.** The same boxes carried up the same stairs in the same time produces a different experience depending on the intention with which they are carried. With the intention "this is the retreat," each box is the practice. Without that intention, each box is a delay between the present and the destination. The work is the same. The relationship to the work is the variable.

**The short-feedback-loop bonus.** Joe also names a secondary insight from the experience: the move was satisfying in a way most of his work is not, because the feedback loop was short.

"The tasks I was doing while moving were very short timeline, like very quick feedback loop. I move this box from here and I move it over there and now it's done and I can see that it's happened. Which is very different from the very abstract form of work that many of us are embedded in in our daily life where we spin a plate and it comes back to us 6 months later." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The implication: some of the felt-time-scarcity in knowledge work comes from the long feedback loops. The system never gets the dopamine landing of completion because nothing actually completes in the day. A move provides hundreds of micro-completions, each one a small landing. The body knows the day was productive. The body knows the day finished. The body does not have to carry the unclosed loops into the night.

## Why This Pairs With the Time-Scarcity Teaching

The example is the lived form of the principle that time scarcity is about presence, not about hours. Joe did not have more time during the move than he would have had at the retreat. He arguably had less, because moving with a partner with a broken foot is more labor than sitting on a cushion. But the felt experience was of abundant time, because the attention was fully on what was actually happening.

The example also demonstrates the principle that the practice and the life are not separate. The frame "I have to get my life under control so I can do my practice" is the [[i-dont-have-time-for-this]] frame applied to spiritual development. The alternative frame is that the life is the practice, and the practice happens in the doing of the life. See [[business-as-spiritual-practice]] for the work-context version of the same teaching.

## The Counter-Move

It is worth naming what Joe did not do, because the alternative is more common.

He did not:
- Go to the retreat and hope to drop the worry once he got there.
- Try to delegate the move to a moving company at high cost to avoid the conflict.
- Tell Alexa not to move (which would not have worked).
- Push through both, attending the retreat half-attentive and helping with the move half-recovered.

He did:
- Recognize that the trip to the practice would defeat the practice.
- Cancel the trip.
- Set an explicit intention to make the move the practice.
- Stay present during what would normally be considered a logistical chore.

The recognition required is the most important part. Most people will go to the retreat. Few will see that going to the retreat is the form the retreat-avoidance is taking in that particular case.

## When This Example Is the Move

The example is useful when:

- The coachee is over-investing in a future state where they imagine they will finally be able to be present (after the project, after the move, after the kids are older, after retirement).
- The coachee is planning a vacation or retreat that the coach can see will not produce what they are hoping for.
- The coachee is in [[i-dont-have-time-for-this]] and the dichotomy between "the life" and "the practice" needs to be collapsed.
- The conversation is about [[time-scarcity]] and a worked example of the principle is needed.
- The person needs permission to make the practice fit their actual life rather than rearranging their life to fit a template of the practice.

## Tone

Direct and slightly self-deprecating. Joe tells this story on himself -- he was confused at how much he enjoyed moving. The confusion is part of the teaching. The instinct, even in someone trained in the work, is that the retreat is where the practice happens. Discovering that the practice was just as available during the move is itself the lesson.
