# Emile DeWeaver: Three Relationships With Fear

## The Story

Emile DeWeaver was sentenced at 18 to 67 years to life in California prison for murder and attempted murder. He served 21 years. During that time he became a writer and a father, refused prison politics, and used both his identity as a writer and the conditions of his sentence to reinvent his relationship to fear. He wrote his way out of prison and now describes his developmental arc with fear in three distinct stages.

This is one of the most compact accounts of a relationship-with-fear developmental arc, told by someone who lived through the most extreme version of the no-fear culture.

## Stage One: Cover Fear With Manufactured Anger

In Emile's pre-prison life, fear was forbidden by the hyper-masculine culture he grew up in. Fear meant weakness; weakness meant being prey. The strategy he discovered as a small child was to manufacture anger that overrode the fear.

The originating event he names: his older brother (four years older, much larger) was bullying him in a hotel room. Emile, who had "no chance in hell of standing up to him physically," reached a limit and threw a deodorant at him in rage. The deodorant shattered against the window. His father, who he expected to punish him, instead looked at him with pride and said, "Yeah, I bet you'll leave Emile alone now."

That moment installed the strategy. From that point, Emile would manufacture anger to push past fear, to cover the shame of being afraid, to act through what would otherwise paralyze him.

"From that moment, I was like, oh, there's this way that I can, one, cover up fear and cover up this sense of inadequacy I have through anger. Right? And like outbursts... I felt like after that moment, understanding that that was a source of like power and safety in a way that I could like not feel afraid and so not feel the shame, I would manufacture anger and feed it and stoke it and stoke it and stoke it until I could like drive myself to like move through this fear." -- Emile DeWeaver

The strategy ended in the murder he committed at 18. The murder is the case study of what happens when manufactured anger as fear-cover scales to its full destructive potential. See [[anger-as-cover-for-deeper-emotion]] for the broader pattern.

## Stage Two: Endurance Through Self-Loathing

In prison, the strategy that had produced the murder was no longer available to him. He could clearly see where it had led. He needed a different relationship to fear.

What replaced the manufactured anger was not yet courage. It was endurance powered by the conclusion that his own life did not matter. He decided he was not going to keep using the anger strategy. If the consequence of refusing to use it was that someone killed him, that was acceptable. He deserved it.

"It was like, I'm not going to do this other thing and I'm going to be afraid. But like, you know, I'm not -- I'm going to, like, still do the right thing and if that doesn't work out and someone kills me, okay. I mean, that's okay. Like, I deserve it, right? You know, I killed a man, so like, you know, someone should kill me." -- Emile DeWeaver

Joe's reading of this: "There's a freedom in what you've found that was basically like, I deserve it, which allowed you to be fearless. That just blows my mind a bit. What an interesting way to turn guilt into freedom, like to turn shame and guilt into freedom."

Emile is precise about this stage: "I wouldn't advocate that, like, that's the healthy way to deal with your guilt, right? That's what I mean by I got lucky. There's a way in which, like, the things that were happening for me coalesced in a way that allowed me to -- that gave me the space and the time to help myself and to expand my imagination because I wasn't doing the right thing exactly for the right reasons, but I was doing the right thing. And that showed me that more was possible."

The stage is unhealthy in its mechanism (self-loathing) but produced healthier action than the previous stage. Doing the right thing for the wrong reasons created the conditions for the right thing to eventually be done for the right reasons. The doing came first; the why corrected later.

### Joe's Coaching Question Inside Stage Two

In the conversation Joe asks the coaching-level question that the stage two account opens up: what distinguishes the people who, after the heinous act, abandon the manufactured-anger strategy from the people who keep using it?

"There's obviously some people who did that in jail and then they kept with that technique. They're like, I learned that to overcome fear with anger worked and then I created this heinous act and now I'm in jail and I'm gonna keep on with that strategy. Whereas you were like, no, I can't do that strategy anymore. What do you think is the difference between the people in jail who keep with the strategy and the people in jail who are like, that's not how I want to be?" -- Joe Hudson, Life After Murder

Émile's answer names two factors: luck, and the arrival of an operationally stronger identity than the old strategy could support. The luck was that his child was born during his trial, which produced a felt urgency he could not have manufactured for himself. The operationally stronger identity was father, and later writer. See [[refuse-to-be-defined-by-the-worst-act]] for the principle this answer expresses.

The coaching move embedded in the question: when someone has used a destructive strategy for years and then describes abandoning it, ask what made the abandonment possible. The answer is rarely willpower. It is almost always the convergence of conditions plus the arrival of an identity strong enough to make the old strategy intolerable. Naming this is the foundation for building the same conditions deliberately for people who have not had Émile's luck.

## Stage Three (Intermediate): Courage as Virtue

Reading Greek philosophy in prison -- Plato's dialogues, the classical virtues -- Emile arrived at the standard formulation of courage: not the absence of fear, but doing the right thing despite the fear.

"That became my relationship to fear for a very long time. That was my relationship to fear, I think, until the time I got out of prison. And it, like, changed, and it had its different manifestations. Like, there was a time where, like, I was afraid, I'm running straight towards it, right? Which I don't advise, right?" -- Emile DeWeaver

This is the version of fear-relationship that most adults consider mature. Feel the fear; do the thing anyway. It is functional. It produced years of right action. But fear remained a separate thing -- an obstacle, an opponent, something to overcome.

The note about "running straight toward it" is important. Even at this stage, the relationship was still adversarial. The fear was something to conquer through opposition. The strategy worked but carried cost. Émile names the caveat himself in the same breath: "There was a time where I was afraid, I'm running straight towards it, right? Which I don't advise." -- this is someone who has lived stage three reporting from inside it, and the warning is the warning of someone who paid the cost. See [[dont-confuse-embracing-fear-with-inviting-danger]] for the related anti-pattern.

## Stage Three (Mature): Fear as Roadmap and Partner

The final stage came from somatic therapy. The frame shifted from "fear is an obstacle" to "fear is a partner with information."

The critical move was learning that the body's protective mechanisms -- the things he was ashamed of, the things he wanted to get rid of -- were intelligent adaptations that had saved his life. They needed to be honored, not destroyed. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]].

"Something that I learned in somatic therapy is like, look, the body is smart. Think about where you learned that, right?... In that environment, all these things that you're ashamed of, can't you see how smart actually your body was? Like, do you think you could have survived that if your body didn't develop these mechanisms?" -- Emile DeWeaver

From honor came collaboration. The body's mechanisms could be updated, given choice rather than default. The fear that had been the enemy became the signal.

"Now fear isn't something I overcome. Fear is actually a roadmap for me. Fear is a signal that I need to pay attention right now. Fear is a signal that I'm avoiding something right now, and I may need to be careful about how I approach it... But it is signaling something very important to my growth and my spiritual journey that I need to pay attention to. And so now I use it like a road map. It's like, oh, I feel nervous about that. Okay, what's going on with that? What's beneath that? What's happening for you? What is it that's crying out to be healed in this moment?" -- Emile DeWeaver

This is [[fear-as-signal-of-care]] arrived at from a different door. The same destination Joe reaches from one trajectory, Emile reaches from another. The convergence is itself instructive.

The mature version is operationalized in a specific practice Émile names: actually talking to his body, heart, mind, and fears as parts that have their own intelligence and need to be respected. See [[talk-to-your-parts-and-ask-for-trust]]. The roadmap framing is the principle; the conversations are the daily expression of it.

## Joe's Mirror From His Own Meditation Practice

Joe describes the same arc in himself, located in meditation rather than in prison:

"At the beginning of my meditations, I would not want to feel the fear, and so I was meditating to manage that emotion. And by the time I was finished -- not, well, not that I'm ever finished, but the time that like where that journey ended for me of like of looking for something that was me -- when that ended, I was just following fear. It was like the direct line, like going right into the abyss every time and finding out that that thing that I was most scared of is actually where I find my deepest truth." -- Joe Hudson

Same final relationship to fear. Different developmental path. Joe's path went through meditation. Emile's went through prison and somatic therapy. The end state is the same: fear as the direct line to truth, not the obstacle to it.

## What This Example Teaches Coaches

1. **The relationship to fear has stages.** Not everyone is at the same stage. Coaching has to meet the person where they are. A person at stage one cannot be told to use fear as a roadmap. They will not have access to the somatic capacity required. The work is to move from where they are to the next stage.

2. **The strategies of earlier stages are not stupid.** The manufactured anger of stage one was an intelligent child's solution to an impossible environment. It needs to be honored before it can be updated. See [[honor-the-patterns-intent]].

3. **The path is not linear or fast.** Twenty years separated the murder from the fully integrated relationship to fear. The principle is the same; the timeline is real.

4. **The cultural setting matters.** Emile's path required first getting out of the no-fear culture (or at least getting some distance from it). See [[everyone-is-pretending]]. People still operating inside no-fear cultures often need help seeing the culture itself before they can shift their individual relationship to fear.

5. **Two-stage healing.** Stage one to stage two is "stop using the destructive strategy." Stage three is "make peace with fear itself." These are different moves. Skipping from stage one to stage three usually fails because the destructive strategy is too well-installed to release without an intermediate stage.

## What This Example Does Not Teach

- This is not a template. Emile's arc was Emile's. The specific shape is not universal.
- This is not an argument that prison produces growth. The conditions Emile transformed under were extreme suffering for him and irrevocable harm to the person he killed and to that person's family. Emile is explicit about this throughout.
- This is not a claim that everyone can or should arrive at stage four. The path described is one path. Other paths exist.

## Tone

Emile tells this with humor, precision, and the absence of self-pity. The murder is acknowledged. The accountability is unflinching. The growth is described without spiritual triumphalism. The tone is the tone of someone who has done the work and can speak about it cleanly.

Joe's tone is awe-tinged respect, with the willingness to push back on Emile's framing of stage two ("I deserve it") as a strategy he wouldn't recommend. The honoring does not preclude the disagreement.

## When This Example Is Useful

- When someone is at stage one (manufactured anger as fear-cover) and needs to see that the stage is real, not unique to them, and not permanent
- When someone is at stage three-intermediate (courage as overcoming fear) and is plateaued there
- When the developmental shape of relationship-to-fear needs to be named
- When the principle "fear can become your friend" is being delivered to someone who has only known fear as enemy
- When the connection between hyper-masculine cultures, anger-cover, and violence needs to be illustrated with a complete arc
