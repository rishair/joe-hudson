# The Hopi Three Sisters Planting

## The Story

When European colonizers came to the Americas and to parts of Africa with the goal of producing high crop yields, they observed how the local populations planted. The local approach often looked disordered to the colonizer's eye -- beans, squash, and corn growing in the same plot, mixed together, without the rectilinear separation the colonizer's farming logic expected.

The colonizer's instinct was to impose order. Separate the crops. Plant them in rows. Make the field look like a grid. The grid was the cognitive form of "well-organized" that the colonizer was bringing from their own culture.

The local approach -- often called "the three sisters" in Hopi tradition, where the three crops are corn, beans, and squash -- was not disordered. It was the product of many generations of close attention to the land. Corn grows tall and gives the beans a structure to climb. The beans fix nitrogen back into the soil, feeding the corn and the squash. The squash spreads its broad leaves across the ground, shading out competing weeds and conserving moisture for all three plants. Together the three crops produce more food per acre, with less soil depletion, than any of them grown separately in the colonizer's grid.

Joe references the example as one specific instance of a broader pattern, the same dynamic James Scott describes in *Seeing Like a State* (which Joe names directly). The colonizer mind, organized around legibility and control, sees chaos in any system whose order is not in the visual register the colonizer's culture has trained.

"There's a book I read a while back called *Seeing Like a State*, and it talks about how when colonizers came to Africa and they were trying to figure out how to produce yields for the resources that they were trying to grow, they saw the way that locals were growing plants and growing crops. And to them it looked like chaos because you've got like the beans mixed with the whatever mixed with --" -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The specific Hopi version (Joe credits the reference): "I think it's actually beans, squashes, and corn. ... In the Hopi reservation, it's those three things were planted together." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

## The Point It Illustrates

The story makes a structural point about the relationship between perceived order, actual order, and the narrowed mind of the fearful observer. What looks like chaos from a "very sharp, right-angled, boxy kind of logical mindset" is often the result of a deeper, more complex order that the narrow mind cannot see.

"There's certain things that are -- they didn't see that there are certain things that are planted together in certain ways that might look quite disordered from a very sharp, right-angled, boxy kind of logical mindset. But actually were really following the highly complex order of nature that had been discovered over many, many generations of really paying attention and attuning to the land." -- Joe Hudson, Perfectionism

The application to perfectionism is direct. The perfectionist mind, organized around the production of a specific visible standard, treats anything that does not conform to the standard as chaos. The work, the team, the project, the relationship that does not match the perfectionist's grid is read as disordered. But what the perfectionist is treating as chaos is often the higher-order arrangement that the actual situation requires. The grid is the wrong tool. The grid was generated by a narrowed mind that has lost access to the wider context. See [[fear-narrows-visual-field]] for the mechanism.

The same point operates organizationally. The leader who wants to impose grid-like order on a system that has been functioning through a more complex emergent order will often degrade the system's actual function while making it look more orderly. The looks-orderly metric is being optimized against the function. The colonizer's farming layout produced lower yields and depleted soil while looking properly organized; the perfectionist's standardized procedures produce lower output and depleted employees while looking properly managed.

## When Joe Uses This Example

The example surfaces when the teaching is about:

- How perfectionism narrows perception and treats higher-order arrangements as chaos
- The general principle that what looks like chaos from a narrow frame is often productive order from a wider frame
- The cost of imposing logical-grid order on systems that have been functioning through emergent or relational order
- The connection between perfectionism, loss of context, and the inability to see what is actually working
- The contrast between the colonizer mindset (impose the standard) and the indigenous mindset (attune to the system)
- The principle that what we see as chaos from our limited human perspective is often simply ignorance of the broader context that is actually there

The example is also useful as a frame for [[holding-contrary-beliefs-as-development]] -- the capacity to hold "this looks disordered" and "this is highly ordered" simultaneously is the cognitive capacity that allows the observer to actually see what is in front of them.

## The Generalizable Lesson

For any situation where a person or institution is judging another system as disordered, the example invites a particular question: is the apparent chaos a real lack of order, or is it order that requires a different cognitive frame to see? Often the apparent chaos is the latter. The work is not to impose the frame the observer is bringing; the work is to learn the frame that the system is actually operating under.

In coaching, this often surfaces when a person is judging their own life as chaotic. The judgment is sometimes accurate, but often it is the perfectionist mind reading any non-grid arrangement as disorder. The question "what's the order that's actually here?" can surface the structure that has been invisible because the perfectionist frame had no slot for it.

## What This Example Is Not

It is not a romanticization of indigenous farming. The three sisters approach is referenced because of its specific structural intelligence, not as a general claim that all non-Western approaches are wiser than Western ones. The point is structural: the colonizer mind narrowed and missed the order that was there, regardless of which culture is in either role.

It is not the claim that all apparent chaos is actually order. Sometimes chaos is chaos. The point is that the narrow mind cannot reliably tell the difference. The widening of the cognitive frame is what allows the distinction to be made accurately.

It is not anti-organization. Some imposed order is functional and necessary. The point is about the specific failure mode where order is imposed on a system whose function depends on a different kind of order. The grid is not bad. The grid imposed on the three sisters is bad. Discernment about which is which is the actual skill.
