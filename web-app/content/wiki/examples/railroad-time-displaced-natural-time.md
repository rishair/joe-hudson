# Railroad Time Displaced Natural Time

## The Story

Before clocks, time was tracked through natural cues: the sun's position, the time the birds called, how long it took rice to cook. Time was local and embedded. "Come home when the birds chirp" was a specific time.

The shift to clock time was driven by the railroad. A train needs a schedule. A schedule needs a shared time. The shared time needs to be the same across all the stations on the line. Local time -- the St. Louis time, the Chicago time -- became a problem because it could not be coordinated. The railroad standardized time across geographies for operational reasons.

The standardization was contested. People protested. Towns objected to losing their local time. According to a Radiolab episode Brett references, the protests were real:

"There was this moment of time where time was railroad time. You needed a clock to catch the train, and then the train needed the time to be the same in all the places so that there could be a schedule. ... A town used to have a time. And apparently, according to the podcast, when people were like, okay, well, St. Louis doesn't have St. Louis time anymore, there's going to be a time for the whole Midwest or whatever, people rebelled. People got upset. There was protests over the fact that... just imagine that. You're like, no, you're not taking away our time." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

## What the Example Demonstrates

The example surfaces a structural insight about the felt experience of [[time-scarcity]]:

**Clock time is a frame, not a fact.** The 24-hour grid that organizes modern life was installed at a specific historical moment. Before that installation, humans tracked time through different cues. The installation was sufficiently disruptive that people protested it. This is not a fact most people walk around knowing. The clock-time frame feels like reality. It is not. It is one possible relationship to time.

**The frame can be installed and the frame can be reframed.** If clock time was installed, it can be examined and partially uninstalled. Not by getting rid of clocks (the trains still need to run). By recognizing that the clock-mind is one mode of relating to time and that the body's mode (rhythmic, embedded, relational) is still available underneath. Recovering the body's mode does not require leaving civilization; it requires not letting the clock-mind be the only timekeeper.

**The protest was sensing something real.** The people who objected to losing their local time were not just being parochial. They were sensing that something embedded in their experience was being replaced by something abstract. The local time was tied to their relationship with the sun, the seasons, the rhythms of the place. The standardized time was tied to a national grid that did not know their place. The loss they were protesting was a loss of relational time in favor of mechanical time.

"If we took away clocks, if there was no such thing as you have to be here at 5 o'clock, like then what would we put all of this urgency on? Like, where would we put it? Because people lived without that concept for years and years and years, and we're living with it as if it's real. But it's really not real, which is a fascinating -- we're just describing a reality and then we bound ourselves to it, we chained ourselves to it." -- Brett Kistler, Why You Never Have Enough Time

## The Body's Surviving Timekeeping

Even after the installation of clock time, the body's other timekeeping has not gone away. It is still active. It is the mode that runs in flow states. It is the mode that lets a skydiver track 5 seconds to parachute deployment without looking at a watch. It is the mode that lets a cook know when the rice is done without a timer. It is the mode that the [[bomb-shelter-less-stressed-than-email]] example illustrates -- the friend in the shelter dropped into the body's time because the clock-mind had nothing to do.

Joe makes this explicit:

"What was happening is my body was tracking something other than a concept of time. It wasn't tracking clock time. It was tracking something far more like the moon or how long it takes to cook rice." -- Joe Hudson, Why You Never Have Enough Time

The body's mode is still there. The clock-mind has been installed on top of it, not in place of it. The work to reduce time scarcity is partly the work of demoting the clock-mind from its exclusive role and letting the body's mode contribute again.

## Why This Is Useful in Coaching

The example unlocks a different relationship to the problem.

For the person in deep [[time-scarcity]]: the framing "this thing you are suffering under is not a fact of nature; it was installed and it is contested" is itself a relief. The thing that has felt inevitable becomes optional. Not optional in the sense that you can ignore deadlines. Optional in the sense that the constant background tracking of clock time is not required.

For the person who believes they cannot escape the clock-mind: the example demonstrates that people have lived without it. The mode the body uses for time is still available. The work is to give it room to operate again alongside the clock-mind.

For the person who believes their stress is permanent: the example places the stress in historical context. The 80% time-starved statistic is a feature of a specific cultural moment, not a feature of being human. Other cultural moments produced other relationships to time. This one can be modified.

## What This Example Is Not

The example is not nostalgia for pre-industrial life. The point is not to abandon clocks. The point is to recognize the frame as a frame.

The example is not anti-civilization. The trains do need to run. The schedules do need to coordinate. The clock-time grid serves real functions.

The example is not a license to be late. Recognizing that clock time is constructed does not change the social contract that depends on it. The point is the internal relationship to clock time, not the external compliance with it.

## When This Example Is the Move

The example is useful when:

- The coachee believes time scarcity is an unavoidable feature of modern life.
- The coachee cannot imagine a different relationship to time and needs an existence proof that other relationships have existed.
- The coaching needs to interrupt the assumption that the clock-mind is the only timekeeper.
- The conversation has gotten into "this is just how things are" and needs a historical reframe.
- The coachee is heavy with the felt-experience of insufficient time and a moment of conceptual lightness is needed.

## Tone

Brett tells this story with curiosity and slight wonder. The protest detail is delivered as genuinely surprising -- the image of people in the 19th century protesting the loss of St. Louis time is meant to land as strange. The strangeness is part of the teaching: the thing that feels normal now once felt deeply abnormal to a lot of people.
