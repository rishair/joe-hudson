# The Makeup Artist and the Rock Star (Openhearted Naming of the Elephant)

## The Story

Brett Kistler opens the episode with a story from his time in commercial production. A famous musician was going through public personal difficulties. The production team had set up to welcome him — the right music in the makeup trailer, careful preparation, the right energy in the room.

The musician arrived looking like death. "Like they'd been hit by a truck." The crew greeted him cordially and pretended nothing was wrong. He walked through, sat in the makeup chair, and the room was silent.

The makeup artist came in. Walked right up. Said: "Oh wow, well you just look like shit, don't you?"

The room dropped. The musician relieved instantly: "Oh God, thank you. Yeah, yeah. I feel like absolute shit." The whole tension of the day broke. The shoot was now possible.

"And nobody said anything about it. They showed up and everybody was just really cordial, like, oh, hey, hey there, how are you doing? Welcome to set. And they just walked right through, came into the moho, they sat down in the chair and there's just silence in the room. And then the makeup artist comes in and she just walked right up and she just was like, oh wow, well you just look like shit, don't you? And immediately, immediately all the tension in the room just dropped." -- Brett Kistler, The Elephant in the Room

## What This Illustrates

The story is the canonical example of [[the-elephant-in-the-room]]: a room full of people seeing the same thing, no one willing to name it, the not-naming creating a low-grade catastrophe, and one person's openhearted speech act dissolving the entire field.

The story also illustrates [[speak-the-elephant-from-open-heart-not-fear]] in its purest form. The makeup artist's "you just look like shit" was delivered with [[view]]:

- **Vulnerable:** she could have been blamed for derailing the shoot
- **Impartial:** she was not trying to fix the rock star or move him to a different state
- **Empathic:** she was with him in his state, and the makeup artist's role offered the natural complement — "I'm here to help fix that"
- **Wondering:** she had no idea how he would respond

"It was very in view. It was vulnerable thing for her to say cuz she could have been blamed. There was an impartiality about saying it in the, in the fact that she was saying what was true. It wasn't that she was trying to get them into a, this rock star into a different place in that moment. There was empathy with it and there was wonder with it." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

## What Made the Delivery Land

Three things distinguish the makeup artist's delivery from the other available versions:

**1. Open heart, not fear of attack.** She was not protecting herself. She was not pre-apologizing. She was not hedging. She spoke from a place that was not defending against the response. Compare with the managed version Joe constructs:

"If you had the woman, the makeup lady, say to this rock star, oh goodness, you know, I really hate to say this, but geez, I'm gonna— oh, I mean, did something happen? You're not looking so— I don't know, please don't be mad at me for that. I would have far more likely gotten a shitty response than goddamn, you don't look good." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The managed version telegraphs the speaker's fear. The rock star would have read the fear and responded defensively. The open-hearted version offers nothing to react against.

**2. No shame attached.** She did not say "you look like shit" with the energy of "and you should be ashamed of yourself." Brett constructs the shame-loaded contrast version:

"Or, oh my God, you look like shit. This, this thing's gonna go horribly now."

Or the moralizing version:

"It wasn't like, oh my God, You fucking, you know, you're getting paid like a million dollars to be here and this is how the fuck you show up?"

The actual delivery was clean. The observation without the judgment. The fact without the meaning attached.

**3. She had the structural role to do something about it.** She was the makeup artist. Her job was, in fact, to fix appearances. Naming the elephant was not just observation; it was the necessary precondition for her actual work. Brett notes: "And I'm like, as a makeup artist, I'm here to like help you fix that." The naming and the offer were the same act.

## Why Strangers Can Sometimes Do This More Easily Than Intimates

Joe uses the story to make a counterintuitive point: it is often easier to name the elephant with a stranger or someone you have no long history with than with a parent, spouse, or long-term colleague.

"With an alcoholic father, for instance, meaning if the pattern has been entrenched over a long period of time and there are people who you want to have long-term lasting relationships with, where you're scared to lose love, where there's all this history built in, it becomes harder and harder to speak to the elephant in the room typically than if it's even a high-powered person that you've never met before." -- Joe Hudson, The Elephant in the Room

The makeup artist had less to lose than the rock star's family. The fear of losing love stacks the cost of naming until the not-naming becomes the rational move. This explains a recurring pattern: the people closest to the dysfunction are the last to name it; the newcomer or the outsider sees it immediately.

## What Made the Naming Possible

Brett notes that he was new to the industry and was specifically too junior to be the one to break the ice. The naming required someone with both the structural permission and the inner capacity. The makeup artist had more experience and was in a role that gave her the structural ground to name it.

"I really felt like we were all doing it wrong until, until it was, until that was spoken to. And it was somebody with much more experience in the industry than, than me that, that said it. And, and like you, like you pointed out earlier, it was said with love and not with shame." -- Brett Kistler, The Elephant in the Room

The "said with love and not with shame" is the operative phrase. The naming is not enough on its own. The naming-from-love is what does the work. See [[love-vs-nice]] for the underlying distinction.

## How Joe Uses This Story

The makeup artist story is Joe's canonical illustration for several teachings:

- **The elephant in the room as a concept.** The story gives the concept its felt sense — the silence in the trailer, the relief when it broke.
- **The openhearted delivery.** The story shows what open-hearted naming actually looks like in practice.
- **The structural ease of naming with strangers.** The makeup artist could do something the rock star's intimates could not.
- **The cost of not naming.** Before she spoke, the day was about to be a disaster. Naming made the day possible.
- **The dissolution of the false shame.** The rock star was carrying false shame about looking like death. The naming dissolved it instantly because his shame depended on the unspoken being unspoken.

## What Did Not Happen

The story is also instructive in the negative. The makeup artist did not:

- Apologize for the observation
- Frame it as a delicate matter to be handled carefully
- Try to fix the rock star's emotional state
- Make the conversation about herself
- Demand that the rock star explain or justify how he looked
- Express concern in a way that was actually criticism

She made an observation. She held it lightly. She offered the complement of her actual job. The rock star received the observation and offered his own. The conversation became real. The day became possible.

## When Joe Reaches For It

Joe uses this story when:

- Teaching the elephant-in-the-room concept and needing a concrete example
- Demonstrating that openhearted delivery is recognizable and different from defended delivery
- Explaining why strangers are sometimes more able to name what intimates cannot
- Contrasting the managed-fear delivery with the open-hearted delivery
- Illustrating how the shame around the elephant dissolves when the elephant is named openheartedly

## Tone

Joe tells this story (or hears Brett tell it) with appreciation for the makeup artist as a kind of natural exemplar. There is no agenda to make her a hero. The story is offered as a clean illustration of a posture that anyone can take. The lightness with which she delivered the line is the lightness Joe is pointing to as the teaching.
