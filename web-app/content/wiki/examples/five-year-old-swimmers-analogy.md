# The Five-Year-Old Swimmers Analogy

## The Setup

Joe's 15-year-old daughter told him she wanted to get into a great college and knew she had to do something special, and that she had been searching her whole life to find out what that special thing was. Joe asked how she had been searching.

Her method: think about something, wonder if it might be the thing, conclude it was not because she was not inspired, return to looking around for the next candidate, repeat.

The method is the standard mental-model search for purpose. The candidate is generated in the head, evaluated in the head, dismissed in the head. The actual activity is never investigated.

## The Analogy

Joe gave her the analogy:

"That's like looking at 10 5-year-old boys and saying, which one is gonna be the world champion swimmer? You can't know until they get in the pool, until you see how they swim, until they have a drive, until you see how, if they like water, if they're scared of water, if they have any passion about swimming. You actually have to go and investigate the thing to understand what the outcome is gonna be." -- Joe Hudson, Finding Your Purpose is Hard Until You Understand This

Ten five-year-old boys standing on the pool deck contain no information about which one will become the world champion swimmer. Their height, weight, build, even their stated interest — none of it predicts the outcome. The variables that determine the outcome are not visible from the deck.

The variables become visible in the pool: how they swim, whether they have drive, whether they love water, whether they fear it, whether they have passion for swimming. None of this is information available from looking. All of it is information available from doing.

## The Point

The analogy makes the abstract principle of [[mental-models-cant-tell-you-what-experience-feels-like]] concrete. The purpose-search by mental-modeling is structurally equivalent to predicting world-champion swimmers from kids standing on a deck. The prediction cannot work because the necessary information is not in the input. The input is the wrong kind of data.

The exit is the same in both cases: get in the pool. Investigate the actual activity. Let the doing produce the information that the thinking cannot produce.

## Why the Swimmers, Specifically

The analogy is effective because it makes the structural problem obvious. No one would seriously try to predict the world-champion swimmer from looking at five-year-olds on the deck. The intuition that the pool is required is immediate. By transferring that intuition to the purpose-search, the analogy reveals that the purpose-search-by-thinking has the same structural defect — a defect that is invisible when the search is operating but obvious once the analogy is applied.

The analogy also bypasses the defense that "but I have thought about it so much." The hypothetical analyst on the deck might have thought about each five-year-old very carefully and still have no useful prediction. The thinking is not the variable that produces the information. The pool is.

## How the Analogy Applies Beyond Children's Sports

The structure applies any time someone is trying to predict a felt outcome from constructed inputs:

- **Career choices.** Cannot predict whether being a doctor will fulfill you by imagining being a doctor.
- **Relationships.** Cannot predict whether a partnership will work by analyzing the partner's traits from the outside.
- **Cities.** Cannot predict whether moving to Lisbon will deliver the life you want by reading about Lisbon.
- **Spiritual paths.** Cannot predict whether a practice will produce what you want by reading about the practice.

In every case: the information is in the doing. The thinking can prepare, can plan, can sequence, but cannot deliver the experiential data the decision actually requires.

## When Joe Reaches for This

The analogy is reached for when:

- A young person (often a teenager or twenty-something) is doing the mental-model purpose-search and Joe wants to surface the structural problem
- Someone is asking how to evaluate a future activity from current information, and the analogy shows the question is unanswerable from where they are asking it
- Someone has been doing the mental-model search for years and needs a fresh frame that makes the futility obvious
- Someone is paralyzed by analysis and needs to be liberated from the requirement to know in advance

The analogy is particularly effective for young people because they have direct experience with not knowing what they like until they have tried it — they have done it with food, with sports, with friends. The analogy uses an existing intuition to dislodge an installed expectation.

## Tone

Light, paternal, slightly amused. Joe is teaching his daughter, and the framing matches: a clear example offered as a small kindness, not a heavy critique of her method. The analogy is meant to free her, not to embarrass her for having used the wrong method.

When delivered to an adult, the tone shifts only slightly — still light, still offered as a structural correction, with recognition that almost everyone uses the wrong method for years before noticing.
