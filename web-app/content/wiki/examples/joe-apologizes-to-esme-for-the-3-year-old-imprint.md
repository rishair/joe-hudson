# Joe Apologizes to Teenage Esme for an Imprint From When She Was 3

## The Story

Joe's teenage daughter Esme comes to him for advice. She is having an issue with a boy. She is describing the dynamic. Joe is listening, and as she is talking, he recognizes the pattern. He sees that what she is describing in this current relationship is something he installed in her when she was 3. He was not emotionally available to her in some specific way in that period, and that created a fear in her of getting hurt. He sees that exact fear running in her right now in the boy situation.

He stops the advice. He looks at her and says: "That's me. You learned that from me. When you were 3 years old, I did this. I was not able to be with you in this way emotionally, and that created this fear in you of getting hurt. And I see that happening now, and I'm so sorry for teaching you that."

"That's me. You learned that from me. When you were 3 years old, I did this. I was not able to be with you in this way. This way emotionally, and that created this fear in you of getting hurt. And I see that happening now, and I'm so sorry for teaching you that." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

## What Happened After

The moment landed deeply for both of them. Joe describes a deep connection happening over the fact that he had fucked up. Not despite it — over it. The acknowledgment itself produced the depth.

"I remember thinking, wow, I wish I would have fucked up a little bit more so I could have more moments like this." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The line is funny and also serious. Joe is not wishing harm on his children. He is noticing that the kind of connection that becomes possible when a parent acknowledges a real imprint with their kid is a kind of connection he could not have predicted. The vulnerability of the parent admitting the specific harm, and the kid being able to receive it, produced something neither of them would have had access to otherwise.

Then, even more surprising: the pattern in Esme dissolved.

"I also remember like going, oh, it's healed. Like, as soon as I said it and as soon as she accepted it, I'm like, that's that pattern is healed in her. And which is like something that I don't see very often." -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

Three or four months later, the pattern had not come back up.

## What This Example Illustrates

**[[upright-apology]] in its full form.** Joe is taking responsibility. He is not extracting forgiveness. He is not centering his own guilt. He is naming, specifically, what he did, when he did it, and what it created in her. He is not collapsing into shame about it. He is not asking her to manage his emotion about having done it. He is simply seeing it clearly and naming it.

**[[trauma-mapping]] across generations.** The imprint from the 3-year-old moment is showing up in the teenage relationship dynamic. The pattern transmitted from the parent to the child as a way of relating to vulnerability and connection. The pattern is structural — it will keep showing up in her romantic dynamics — unless something interrupts the loop.

**The acknowledgment itself can be the interruption.** Joe is direct that the pattern healed quickly after the conversation. This is not always how it works, but it is sometimes how it works. The naming of the imprint by the person who installed it — accurately, specifically, without shame — gives the receiver permission to release it. The pattern was being held in place partly because it had never been acknowledged. The acknowledgment frees it.

**[[parenting-as-coaching-domain]] in real time.** The parent who continues to do their own work has the capacity, decades later, to see what they installed and to repair it directly with the child. The work is not over when the child is grown. It changes shape. The relationship is still alive, and the patterns the parent installed are still available to be named and released, as long as the parent is still showing up to do the work.

**Joe is not extracting forgiveness from Esme.** Notice what is absent. He does not ask her to forgive him. He does not ask her to say it is okay. He does not perform contrition. He sees, he names, he apologizes, and then he is quiet. She accepts the apology and the pattern dissolves. The structure of the apology is the structure of [[dont-use-you-in-an-apology]] — it is about him, not about what she should now do about it.

**Imprints are specific.** Joe does not say "I'm sorry I was a bad father." He says "when you were 3 years old, I did this specific thing, and that installed this specific fear, and I see it running in this specific way right now." The specificity is what makes the apology land. A general apology would have been a performance. The specific apology was real information.

## The Move That Goes With This: The Apology and the Respect For The Kid

The third retelling of this story (in the Nathan Baschez interview) surfaces something that did not get fully articulated in the earlier tellings: the apology is part of a broader stance of treating the kid the way the parent would treat an adult they respect. The structural commitment is that respect is the baseline, and when the parent fails to extend it — through shaming, through emotional absence, through the small daily moves — the parent apologizes the same way they would apologize to an adult.

"One of the ways that I looked at, especially the young kids, was eventually I looked at it was, am I treating them with the same respect that I'm treating an adult? Now I have to teach them, so I get that, but I can teach them in a respectful way, I can teach them in a disrespectful way. And so part of that, oh, I'm going to treat them with the respect, was that if I did something like shame them, I would apologize. I would say, oh, that's not how I want to be with you." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

This is the upstream commitment that produces the willingness to apologize when an imprint is recognized later. The apology to teenage Esme about the 3-year-old imprint is the same move applied at the larger time scale. The parent who has been apologizing in real time for years for the small failures has the somatic infrastructure to apologize for the bigger, older failures when they become visible. The parent who never apologizes for the small failures is unlikely to find the apology for the larger one available when needed.

See [[treat-your-kid-like-an-adult-you-respect]] for the related stance with teenagers.

## The Bigger Insight About Mistakes

The example carries a frame that disrupts the standard parenting anxiety. The standard anxiety is: I'm going to fuck up my kid. Joe's frame is: yes, you will, and the fuck-ups become some of the deepest material in the relationship later.

"When the kids are like 2 years old, you're like, oh my God, I'm fucking it up. And you are, like, like, there's no perfect parent, so you're definitely fucking up your kids here and there. And, um, but I never thought that like we could actually have like such a deep moment of connection over me fucking up my kids, right?" -- Joe Hudson, Parenthood

The reframe: the fuck-ups are not a one-way damage event. They become, when met later with clean acknowledgment, opportunities for a kind of connection that cannot happen between people who have never had to repair anything. This is consistent with [[trust-requires-conflict]] and with [[heartbreak-as-capacity-expansion]] at the relational scale.

## What This Means For Coaches Of Parents

Many parents come to coaching carrying guilt about how they have parented. The guilt is mostly load-bearing — it is keeping them from looking at what they actually installed because looking would mean feeling responsible. The reframe Joe offers is that the looking, when done from upright posture rather than from shame, becomes the gift. The parent who can see the imprint, name it cleanly to the now-grown child, and not collapse into apology-as-self-flagellation, has access to a depth of relationship the parent who is too ashamed to look does not.

The parent's capacity to do this depends on the parent's capacity to love themselves enough to look at what they did without collapsing. See [[capacity-to-love-other-is-capacity-to-love-self]]. The parent who hates themselves for the imprint cannot name it from upright posture; the naming would tip into self-attack and re-traumatize the kid. The parent who has done the work to love themselves can see clearly, name plainly, and be there for the kid's response.

## When To Use This Example

- A parent presenting guilt about something they did when their child was young
- A parent asking whether old harms can be repaired with adult children
- A parent worried they are fucking up their young children right now
- A coachee who is the adult child waiting for the parent to acknowledge the imprint that never gets acknowledged
- A discussion of [[upright-apology]] applied to family relationships
- A parent asking how to talk to their teenager about something they regret from earlier years

## Boundary Conditions

The example is not a recommendation that parents go on apology tours with their teenage children for every imagined harm. The example works because Joe saw the specific imprint operating in the specific moment and named it cleanly. A parent who reaches into the past for an apology because they think they should is performing, not repairing. The repair is most useful when the parent sees the pattern actively running and can name it in context.

The example also presumes a child who has been raised with the capacity to receive an apology from a parent. A child who has been trained to manage the parent's emotions cannot fully receive an apology — they will collapse into reassuring the parent. The capacity for the depth Joe describes is partly the parent's apology and partly the kid having been raised with the freedom to receive it without taking responsibility for the parent's feelings.
