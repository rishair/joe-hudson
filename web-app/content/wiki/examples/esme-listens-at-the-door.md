# Esme Yelling "Just Let It In!" Through the Door

## The Story

When Joe's daughter Esme was about 7 or 8, Joe and his wife regularly held coaching sessions in the house. Esme would listen at the door of the room where the work was happening. Sometimes she could not contain herself and would yell into the room — coaching the adult in the chair from the other side of the wall.

"There was somebody on the couch doing the work with Mom or I, and you would do things like yell from the other room, 'Just let it in!' or 'Stop being defensive! Stop resisting!' You would like, because you would, I'm pretty confident you can tell me, you would like listen at the door and you would want to know about these conversations." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and His Daughter Discuss Fatherhood

Esme confirms in the same conversation: "I would listen to the whole thing. Yeah."

Joe adds:

"Occasionally you couldn't help yourself. You just like yell at the person, which was always disarming as heck for the person who was like, oh wow, the, the, the 9-year-old in the other room gets it and I'm pretending I don't, you know." -- Joe Hudson, Joe and His Daughter Discuss Fatherhood

## What This Shows About How the Work Lands in a Child

The 9-year-old who has absorbed the work enough to recognize defensiveness, name it accurately, and yell at it through a wall has done several things at once:

**Absorbed the language of the work.** "Let it in." "Stop being defensive." "Stop resisting." These are not words a typical 9-year-old uses. She picked them up from being in proximity to the work.

**Internalized the underlying frame.** She is not parroting the words. She is correctly diagnosing the moment — the adult on the couch is resisting, and the resistance is what is blocking the work. The 9-year-old has the diagnostic frame the work uses.

**Has the relational confidence to interrupt.** A child who had been shamed for speaking up would not yell through the wall. The yelling-through-the-wall is evidence of a household where the child has been welcomed as a full presence whose perceptions and impulses are taken seriously.

**Cannot contain the impulse.** This is the part that Joe finds funny. The work being so clearly visible to her that she physically cannot keep quiet about it. The wall is no obstacle to the wisdom needing to leak out.

## Why It Disarms the Client

The client in the room is, by definition, doing the resisting. They are using sophisticated adult language to explain why they cannot let in the feedback, drop the defense, change the pattern. The 9-year-old's intervention cuts through the sophistication. There is no available counterargument to a 9-year-old yelling "Just let it in!"

The adult cannot say "you don't understand." A 9-year-old understanding the situation is itself the data point. The adult cannot say "it's more complicated than that." It often isn't. The complication was part of the resistance. The 9-year-old has named what the complication was protecting.

This is why the disarmament happens. The intervention bypasses the entire defensive apparatus the adult had set up. The 9-year-old, by virtue of not having the apparatus, is operating in a clearer field. The adult, in proximity to that clarity, briefly cannot hold the position they were holding.

## The Mediator Role at School

In the same conversation, Esme describes how this capacity showed up at school: she became the mediator the teachers brought in when two kids were in conflict.

"Whenever there was a conflict between two kids in school, I was always the mediator. The teacher would bring me in and I would mediate because we always got through conflict way quicker." -- Esme, Joe and His Daughter Discuss Fatherhood

The same capacity — recognizing resistance, naming it cleanly, holding the space for the resolution — was being applied at school in a more contained form. The teachers had noticed the capacity and were using it. The conflicts resolved faster because the mediator was operating with frames most of the adults around her had not yet learned.

This is the natural extension of the work to a child who has been raised in it. The work becomes how the child engages the social world. Not because she was assigned a role. Because the capacity was there and the social environment recognized it.

## The Cost That Came With It

The mediator role and the listening-at-the-door capacity also produced the felt sense of separation from her peers that Esme has reported across this conversation.

"I was making me feel like separated from my class because, yeah, I, you know, being around that type of work, it's hard to understand if you weren't raised that way." -- Esme, Joe and His Daughter Discuss Fatherhood

This is the cost side of the parenting outcome. The same capacity that lets the child mediate adult conflicts also makes the child different from her peers in ways the peers cannot fully meet. The work that produced the capacity also produced the loneliness. See [[parenting-as-coaching-domain]] for the broader discussion of the loneliness of an empathically-raised child.

The example shows both: the capacity that the work produces in a child raised inside it, and the social cost the capacity also creates. Both are real. Neither cancels the other.

## What This Example Illustrates

- **The work transmits through proximity, not just through instruction.** Esme was never sat down and taught the principles of the work. She absorbed them by listening at the door. The household was the curriculum.

- **Children can hold the frames the work uses, often with more clarity than adults.** The capacity is not adult-only. The capacity is available to anyone who has not had it trained out of them.

- **The household environment shapes capacities the school environment will later make use of.** The mediator role at school was downstream of the listening-at-the-door behavior at home. The environment that produced the capacity was the home, not the school.

- **Wonder runs more freely in children than in adults.** The 9-year-old yelling through the wall is operating from a more unmediated relationship to what she can see than the adult on the couch. See [[wonder]] for the underlying capacity that the work tries to recover in adults.

## When to Use This Example

This example fits when:

- A parent is worried that doing this work in the home will damage their children or expose them to too much.
- A coachee is asking what it would look like if they raised their kids inside this work.
- The discussion is about how the work transmits — through direct instruction or through environment.
- A coachee is wondering whether children can hold sophisticated emotional frames.
- A parent is worried about working on their own stuff with kids in the house.

## Boundary Conditions

The example does not say that children should be exposed to all adult emotional content. The work Joe and his wife were doing was structured, contained, and not traumatic to overhear. A child overhearing actual abusive content, severe trauma processing, or unregulated adult conflict would be experiencing something different. The example points at what becomes available when the work in the home is sustained, regulated, and offered with care.

The example also does not say that all children raised in the work will become mediators or absorb the language to this degree. Children differ in their interests, their temperaments, and what they pick up from their environment. Esme had particular interest in the work that not every child will have. The point is not "your child will yell through walls." The point is "the work transmits even when you are not trying to teach it."
