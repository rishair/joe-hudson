# The Fired Head of Product Now Making $150K a Week

## The Story

A rapid-coaching call in the year-long program. The person is ashamed of something that happened in his past: he had a great job — head of product for a company — got involved with a woman, let the job slide, got fired. He has been beating himself up over this. He came to coaching with the shame as the presenting concern.

Joe asks: "After you got fired, you started your own app that's now making $150,000 a week. Why are you not fucking stoked that you got fired? How is that not the best choice that you ever made? How is it that you're beating yourself up for the thing that allows you to be independent and financially —"

The person starts laughing and smiling. The shame breaks.

"Oh, so all the shame was doing was preventing him from feeling how good life was." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

## What the Story Demonstrates

The structural point: shame is not always shame about something morally wrong. Sometimes shame is the apparatus the system uses to prevent itself from feeling how good things actually are. The shame holds the person in the bad-self framing so they cannot register the good-life framing. When the bad-self framing is held tightly enough, the felt aliveness of being in a good life cannot reach the body.

The man's shame was operating as a filter. It filtered out the fact that the firing produced the best outcome of his career. The filter was so effective that the man had been beating himself up for the thing that, looked at from the present, was the gateway to everything he now had. The shame was doing exactly what shame structurally does — keeping the emotional experience of aliveness from arriving.

Joe's intervention is not to reason the man out of the shame. The intervention is to surface the contradiction so clearly that the contradiction cannot be sustained. "How is the thing you're ashamed of not the best choice you ever made?" The man cannot answer the question without seeing the shame's function. The seeing is the release.

## The Generalization

Joe extends the example into a universal claim:

"Just by the way, that is with everybody. Somebody be like, there's no way that I would ever feel bullshit. Everybody does. Everybody beats themselves up for something that if they like draw the line out into the future, has made something great in their life happen today. Every single person is like, ah, I shouldn't have done this thing 3 years ago. I feel shame for the thing 3 years ago. But if they look at their life right now, they can say, oh, that shit wouldn't have happened if it wasn't for that. But they still feel ashamed of the thing from 3 years ago. It's an amazing thing." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The universal claim is the operational frame. The story is not unique to this man. It is the structure of how shame works in everyone. The person who insists they have no such shame is exhibiting the same pattern more thoroughly: the shame is so deeply doing its job that the person cannot even see what it is preventing them from feeling.

## What Shame Is Structurally For

The story makes a specific structural argument about what shame is. Shame is not a moral signal about wrongdoing. Shame is a mechanism that prevents the body from registering a particular emotional experience. The specific emotional experience it prevents in this case is the felt aliveness of being in a good life. The shame is in service of containment, not in service of moral correction.

This connects to the broader teaching that the deep positive emotions are scary (see [[fear-of-joy]]). Shame is one of the apparatuses by which the system prevents itself from dissolving into the deep positive emotion. The aliveness of "look at how good this is" would be expansive and threatening to the self-structure. The shame keeps the aliveness out. The self stays intact. The cost is that the life is not being felt.

"And that just by the way, that is with everybody... It just shows that the shame was all designed to stop that emotional experience from happening of feeling alive and the pleasure of what had happened." -- Joe Hudson, Pleasure

The framing of shame as designed-to-stop-aliveness rather than designed-to-signal-wrongdoing is the structural move that the story carries. The framing turns shame from a fact-about-morality into a mechanism-of-protection. The mechanism can be worked with. The fact-about-morality cannot.

## What Joe Did in the Session

Three moves, all small, all decisive.

1. **Surface the timeline.** Joe placed the past event (the firing) next to the present situation ($150K/week app). The juxtaposition is the entire intervention. The man had been narrating only the past event in isolation. Putting it next to the present made the shame's function visible.

2. **Ask the structural question.** "Why are you not fucking stoked?" The question is not rhetorical. It is asking the man to account for the contradiction between his shame and the actual outcome. The accounting requires the man to see what the shame has been doing.

3. **Let the laughter complete the work.** The man started laughing. Joe did not push further. The laughter was the somatic registration of the shame's release. The shame had been the apparatus; the seeing of the apparatus released the apparatus. The laughter is the body acknowledging what just happened.

The session was rapid because the structural insight is clean. The man could not maintain the shame after seeing what it was for. The maintenance required not seeing. The seeing made the maintenance impossible.

## What This Story Is Used For

In the pleasure episode, Joe tells this story to illustrate why shame is hard to release through fighting it directly. The way to release the shame in this man was not to convince him that the firing was fine. The way was to make the structural function of the shame visible. Once visible, the shame released on its own.

The story is also a worked example of [[what-we-fear-we-invite]] applied to inner experience. The man was avoiding the feeling of how good his life was; the apparatus he was using was self-flagellation about the past; the apparatus produced exactly the state it was designed to maintain (he could not feel how good his life was). Releasing the apparatus released the state.

The pleasure-episode use is specific: the felt aliveness of the present is the pleasure that the shame was blocking. The connection between pleasure and shame is structural. People who report they cannot feel pleasure are often using some form of self-flagellation about the past to keep the aliveness of the present from arriving. Naming the structure can release the apparatus.

## Tone

Joe tells this story with energy and a small profanity ("why are you not fucking stoked"). The profanity is part of the move. The man's shame is being met not with gentle reframing but with surprised vigor — the appropriate response to seeing someone beat themselves up for the thing that produced their flourishing. The tone makes the contradiction emotionally available, not just intellectually visible.
