# Aaron's Wife and the Fumble Coaching (First NFL Broadcast)

## The Story

Aaron Taylor was a CBS Sports broadcaster who had spent 14 years on television. One year (the story takes place during the COVID period), a colleague got COVID and Aaron was offered the chance to call his first NFL game -- the broadcast opportunity that he and everyone in his business wanted.

His response: "I ran downstairs and told my wife. She screamed, gave me this big hug. Like, this is the opportunity everybody in our business wants. And here it was in my lap. I was getting my shot."

Then he went into the bunker. Watched film. Called everyone he knew. Researched both teams' history back to 1911. This was his pattern: over-prepare, which creates anxiety, which justifies more preparation, which creates more anxiety.

His wife (a two-time Olympic beach volleyball player who grew up in communist Bulgaria and understood pressure) intervened.

"My wife, in all of her infinite wisdom, looks at me and kind of raises her hand. She's like, uh, are you, uh, are you open to some feedback? I was like, oh damn, every time she asked me that, here it comes, a fastball of truth is about to be thrown over the plate. And then she hit me with the follow-up of permission to speak freely. I was like, oh damn, like, this is a biggie. And I was like, yes." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

The double-permission structure ("open to feedback?" + "permission to speak freely?") is itself worth noting. It is the wife establishing that the next message is not standard reassurance but a substantive intervention.

The intervention:

"She said, I'm a little worried that you're so excited about this that you're going to squeeze it so tightly that you're going to fumble this opportunity. She said, I would offer you this. Every time that you're nervous or want to do something or thinking about how you're going to do X, Y, or Z instead of thinking about what it is you want to do, think about how it is you want to feel after the event is over." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

Then the visualization instructions:

"I want you to close your eyes when the anxiety comes up and imagine putting your computer and your game board, which is what every announcer uses to call games. What do you look over? What's the look on your partner's face when you guys high-five? What's the last thing the producer says as you take your headset off and go down underneath the stadium to the TV trucks? What's the, you know, who's texting you? What are they saying? And as you go to bed and put your head on the pillow, what's the predominant feeling? Is it pride? Is it relief? Is it gratitude? Like, play it all the way out." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

## The Result

"That was the best game I've ever called on the biggest stage in my biggest moment. I'm in the College Dang Football Hall of Fame. I've been on TV for 14 years, but that was the only time I never had performance anxiety and wasn't worried about what I was going to do, because I shifted my focus from what I feared to what I wanted to feel." -- Aaron Taylor, Feel Your Way to Freedom

"It led not only to the best game I've ever called, it's the best season I've ever had in television."

The story extends the practice from one moment to a sustained operating mode. The visualization that worked for one game became the basis for the rest of the year.

## Why Joe Spotlights What the Wife Did

Joe's response is not about the outcome. It is about the structure of the intervention itself:

"The amazing thing is, even in your wife's description of it, she's like, don't squeeze it too tightly. Like, she described it to you in physical terms. She literally was just like, 'You might fumble.' It's like the whole thing was like you could feel it in your -- like when you were describing your wife's words to me, I'm like, 'Oh, I can feel that in my body.' She is -- she was talking to your body. It's pretty cool." -- Joe Hudson, Feel Your Way to Freedom

What Joe is naming: the intervention worked because it was delivered to the body, not to the intellect. "Squeeze it too tightly" is a physical metaphor. "Fumble" is a physical metaphor. The visualization is a body-level instruction (eyes closed, sensory detail, felt aftermath). The whole thing bypasses the part of the system that produces and consumes the anxiety, and goes directly to the part that can release it.

This is a recurring pattern in Joe's teaching: interventions that work tend to engage the body, not the intellect. See [[exercises-need-body-voice-emotion]].

## What This Example Illustrates

This example illustrates several connected principles:

- [[visualize-the-felt-aftermath]]: the practice this story is the origin story for.
- [[fear-is-about-feeling]]: Aaron's anxiety was not about the broadcast performance. It was about the feelings he would have to feel if he performed badly. The visualization addressed the feared felt outcome by giving the body a desired felt outcome instead.
- [[embrace-vs-brace]]: the "squeezing it too tightly" is the bracing pattern named in physical language. The visualization released the brace.
- [[embracing-vs-creating-intensity]]: the wife's intervention did not manufacture confidence. It worked with what was already there -- excitement -- and reoriented its target.
- [[fear-vs-excitement]]: Brett's framing in the conversation: "your fear was transmuting into excitement." The same energy, redirected by the visualization.

## When to Use This Example in Coaching

This example fits when a coachee:

- Is over-preparing for a high-stakes event and the over-preparation is producing escalating anxiety.
- Is "squeezing it too tightly" -- gripping an opportunity in a way that will produce the fumble they fear.
- Has tried calming techniques that did not work because the techniques addressed the head, not the body.
- Is in a relationship where someone has tried to coach them and they are wondering whether the coach knows what they are talking about. The Olympic-athlete spouse detail is useful: she knew the felt sense of competition.

It does not fit when the coachee is genuinely under-prepared. In that case the story would be misapplied.

## The Implicit Teaching About Coaching

The story is also a quiet teaching about how to coach someone. The wife's intervention has several elements worth noting:

- Permission was asked twice before content was offered.
- The diagnostic ("you're going to squeeze it so tightly that you're going to fumble") was delivered in physical, not psychological, language.
- The intervention was offered, not imposed ("I would offer you this").
- The instruction was procedural and sensory ("close your eyes when the anxiety comes up and imagine...").
- The instruction included multiple sensory channels: visual, auditory, kinesthetic, emotional.
- The aftermath was the focus, not the performance.

These elements describe an effective coaching move regardless of whether the coach is a spouse, a friend, or a paid professional. The structure transfers.
