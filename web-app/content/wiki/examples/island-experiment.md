# The Island Experiment (12 People Who Unconditionally Love You)

## The Setup

A thought experiment Joe uses to point at what real community does and why nearly everyone, once they imagine it clearly, wants it.

"Imagine you were dropped onto an island and there was 12 people who unconditionally loved you. They drew boundaries with you. They — wasn't like they were just accepting your shit, but they loved you unconditionally. They accepted where you were and they loved you unconditionally. And now if you imagine that you were stuck on that island for a decade, right, and you're with these basic kind of like saints almost, like people who are just like in that kind of deep love, like, who are you at the end of that decade. Like, where, where, you know, where's your fear? Where's your love? Where's your — like, how do you interact in the world? How do you see yourself?" -- Joe Hudson, Finding Community

The features that do the work:

- Twelve, not one. The unconditional love does not depend on any single relationship being functional that day.
- Unconditional, but with boundaries. The people are not pushovers. They love you while drawing limits with your behavior when needed. See [[boundaries-with-vs-against]] for the distinction the experiment leans on.
- A decade. Enough time that the conditions become the new baseline, not a peak experience.
- Acceptance of where you are. The love is not contingent on your becoming something different.

## The Point

The experiment is not about the island. It is about the kind of person those conditions would produce. Once a person sits with the question, the answer is intuitive: they would be unrecognizably less afraid, less defended, more open, more in their love. Their relationship to fear, to love, to themselves and others would be qualitatively different.

The reason the experiment matters: those conditions are not exotic. They are the conditions a real community generates inside ordinary life. The experiment makes vivid what a person is missing when they say they have community but it is the surface kind. It also makes vivid why people are willing to give up money and power for the real thing once they have tasted it.

## When Joe Uses It

- When someone is dismissing the value of community, or treating community as a nice-to-have
- When someone is in a stifling community and confusing that for what community is
- When someone is asking why they should bother with the work of vulnerability and depth in a group
- As a counter to the cultural story that what people need is more privacy, more independence, more self-reliance
- When making the case for why a [[support-system]] matters, not just abstractly but to a person who is currently relying on willpower and individual effort to become who they want to be

## The Live-Audience Variant

Joe also runs this experiment as a real-time guided imagining, often opening it directly into a live audience or conversation. The setup is the same; the delivery is more immediate.

"Close your eyes for a minute and imagine you're on an island and you're stuck on that island. You got all the food, everything you need to be comfortable, but you don't get to leave that island. And on this island are 12 saints. And these 12 saints, they're fully human, but they all just know how to unconditionally love you and unconditionally love themselves. They have boundaries, they, they say what they want, etc., but there's just that unconditional love. 10 years later, who are you after living there? As soon as you see that, you're like, 'Oh, I would be like that. I would be like them.' And it tells you how important it is, this community of practice." -- Joe Hudson, How To Build A Great Support System

Two small differences from the longer setup that matter:

- The saints are explicitly fully human, with boundaries and stated wants. The unconditional love is not the love of erased people; it is the love of self-respecting peers.
- The conclusion the listener reaches — "I would be like them" — is the structural point. The conditions produce the person. You become like the people around you who unconditionally love themselves and you.

This variant is especially useful in the context of building a [[support-system]] rather than finding a [[community]]. The saints are not a community you find; they are the kind of people you could choose to gather around a purpose. The experiment makes vivid what the gathering is for.

## What It Produces

The experiment cannot be argued with intellectually. The person's own answer to the question does the work. They imagine the conditions, they sense the person they would become, and they feel the gap between that and their current life. The gap is the motivation. No further persuasion is needed.

"That's why everybody wants it, because they know if they're met there, what becomes of them — they become the thing that they truly are. And so I think that's the thing that really pulls people, and they — and people can sniff it out." -- Joe Hudson

## Internal and External Versions Are the Same Project

A useful second turn of the experiment, after the person has felt into who they would become: notice that the internal voice is almost never 12 saints loving the person unconditionally. It is usually the inverse — judgment, performance demands, conditional acceptance. The work that the external island would do over a decade is the same work that [[loving-all-parts]] of oneself does internally. The external island and the internal one are the same project.

The third turn: the experiment also functions as a mirror for what conditions the person is currently providing for others in their life. If 12 people on the island loved you unconditionally and drew clean boundaries with you, are you doing that for anyone? The asymmetry between what one wants from others and what one is offering tends to become visible. That visibility is often where the actual community-building starts: by becoming a node such an island can form around. See [[view]] as a way of life and [[community]]'s "How to Cultivate It Where You Are" section.

## The Saint-Behavior Inversion (When Encountering Someone You Cannot Stand)

A fourth turn of the experiment, surfaced in a different context: the experiment also inverts. If you walked off the island as a saint, and saints unconditionally love while drawing boundaries, then the test of where you actually are is what you do when you encounter someone in the world you cannot stand. The encounter is the inverse of the island. You can feel exactly how far you are from being one of those saints by noticing what you do when someone shows up who triggers you.

"And yet when we see something out in the world that we don't like, the very last thing we think to do is behave like a saint." -- Joe Hudson, Slimy vs. Authentic Charisma

The diagnostic moves the experiment from a thought-piece about community into a live test. When Joe gives this in the context of a conversation about polarizing public figures (Trump, Tate), the move is operational: the impulse to fight them, condemn them, hate them is the diagnostic of how far you are from the saint stance. The saint position is unconditional love plus boundaries — not approval, not agreement, not abdication of discernment. Just the position of someone who is not destabilized by what they encounter.

The implication for the AI coach: when a person describes being unable to be in a room with someone they find toxic, the island experiment in its inverted form is one available frame. Not as moralism (you should be a saint). As diagnostic (the friction is information about where the work is). The work is the work — saint behavior is not a performance to put on; it is the natural state of a person who has done enough of their own work that they are not destabilized by what they encounter.

This also explains why the original island experiment lands so universally. We can imagine being met by saints because we know what it would feel like. We almost never imagine being the saint because we have not done the work that makes the saint state available. The two halves of the experiment are connected: the work the saints would do for us over a decade is the same work we have to do ourselves to become someone capable of saint-behavior in the world.
