# The Parole Decision Before vs. After Lunch

## The Example

Joe references a study showing that prisoners going up for parole have something like a 20% higher likelihood of being granted parole if their hearing is before lunch than if it's after lunch. The implication for the parole board member: if you genuinely want to give the prisoner a fair shot at parole, scheduling the hearing for after lunch tomorrow morning is more effective than holding it after lunch today.

> "If you are in jail, you're going up for parole, and you do parole before lunch, you have something like a 20% — I don't know exactly — more likelihood of getting parole than if it's after lunch. So if you were the parole person, is it procrastinating to say, oh, let's do after lunch tomorrow morning?" -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The question Joe asks with this example: is it procrastinating to delay the decision to a time when the decision will be made better? The structural answer: no. The decision being made better matters more than the decision being made now. The delay is intelligent prioritization in service of the actual goal, not procrastination from inability to act.

## The Point Joe Is Making

The example illustrates a structural feature of decision-making and action that the procrastination-shaming frame obscures: **the agenda you set yourself is not always the most effective thing to be doing.** What you tell yourself you should do now may be exactly the thing that would be more effective tomorrow morning, before lunch, on a Tuesday after coffee, or some other specific condition the body knows about and the head doesn't.

> "It bends the mind to understand that our agenda is not always the most effective thing. Right. And when you understand that, then you produce more quicker, better." -- Joe Hudson, How to End Procrastination Now

The parole example is striking because it is one of the rare cases where the timing-affects-quality relationship is quantified. The judge is not biased on purpose. The judge is not procrastinating on the post-lunch cases by being harsher. The biological reality of decision-making just is what it is, and the decision-quality varies with conditions the conscious mind doesn't notice.

The implication is general. If parole decisions vary by 20% based on time-of-day, how much do creative decisions vary? Business decisions? Relationship conversations? The conscious agenda — "I should do this now" — is making calls without access to the data the body actually has about when the decision can be made well.

## How This Connects to Procrastination

The example is in service of [[procrastination-is-wisdom]] reason one: **wrong timing.** The not-doing carries information. Sometimes the information is: "this is the wrong time for this decision." The body knows. The head doesn't have language for what the body knows. The head calls the not-doing "procrastination" and starts attacking. The attack drowns out the signal that would have let the timing be honored.

Most procrastination on important decisions has some version of this happening underneath. The conscious mind says now, the body says wait, the conscious mind labels the wait as failure, the wait happens anyway, the decision gets made under the worst conditions — either rushed at the deadline or made wrong because the timing was off. Either way the quality suffers and the conscious mind concludes the person needs more discipline.

The structural alternative: trust the timing signal. Investigate what the wait is about. If it's about real timing (the body has access to something), honor it. If it's about avoidance (the body is protecting from a feeling), surface the feeling. Either way, the attacking-yourself-for-the-wait is the wrong move. See [[intuition-vs-conditioning]] for the distinction between wisdom-wait and avoidance-wait.

See also [[joes-famous-ceo-not-the-right-time-example]] for the major version of this pattern in Joe's own coaching life — he waited two years to coach a CEO because the timing was wrong, and the eventual work was much more effective than it would have been at the original moment.

## What This Example Does in Coaching

When a client is beating themselves up for not doing something, the parole example can land the structural point that timing matters and that "now" is not the only legitimate time to do a thing. The example is concrete enough to disarm the abstract "I should just push through" frame. If the parole board member should wait until after lunch tomorrow, what makes the client so certain that their thing should be done right now?

The example also surfaces the deeper teaching: the conscious agenda is not the highest authority on what should happen. There are other inputs (body wisdom, situational reading, the way conditions shift) that the conscious agenda can't see. Treating the conscious agenda as the sole determinant of what should happen produces worse outcomes than letting the larger system have input.

For coaches, the example is useful when a client is in pure "I should just do it" mode and needs a structural counter-example that shows why "should" isn't always right. The parole study is empirical enough to bypass the "yeah but my situation is different" defense.

## Variants of the Same Teaching

The parole example sits alongside other illustrations Joe uses for the same structural point:

**The creative-incubation cycle.** Trying to solve a problem for a while, then resting, is documented to produce more epiphanies than constant trying. The rest period isn't procrastination; it's the second half of the creativity cycle. See the reference Joe makes to Neuberg's *The Enlightenment Brain*.

**The customer service 24-hour wait.** See [[joes-customer-service-24-hour-wait-example]]. Joe noticed that if he didn't respond to client questions for 24 hours, they often resolved their own problems. The delay was wiser than the immediate response would have been.

**The Joe-and-Brett podcast on procrastination delay.** Brett and Joe joked for years about doing a procrastination episode and didn't do it. The joke was that they were procrastinating. The reality was that the episode landed much more effectively after a YouTube video on procrastination brought a wave of interested listeners. The "everything in its time" exit line of the episode is the structural point made compactly.

All four cases illustrate the same teaching: the conscious agenda's idea of when something should be done is one input among several. Sometimes it's right. Often it's not. The wisdom is learning to read the other inputs.
