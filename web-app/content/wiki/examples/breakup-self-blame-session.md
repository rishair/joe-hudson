# The Breakup Self-Blame Session

## The Setup

A short coaching session recorded in one of Joe's public Q&As. A physical therapist raises his hand. He has been through a breakup he attributes to his own behavior. He says he betrayed himself, was not authentic, was someone totally different. He is mad at himself. He does not know how to forgive that person he was, or how to move on. He has been in this state for a month.

The session takes about 30 turns and arrives at a structural shift with a take-home practice. Joe runs through several of his characteristic moves in compressed form. The session is a useful condensed example of how Joe coaches a post-breakup self-blame question.

## The Arc (Compressed)

1. **Surfaces the chronicity.** Joe does not engage with the specific breakup. He asks: "How often do you blame yourself? How often is it your fault?" The client confirms: "A lot, probably."

2. **Asks the inversion question.** "How is it not your fault that it's always your fault, that you're always blaming yourself?" Client: "I'm not sure." The wedge is in. See [[how-is-it-not-your-fault-that-its-always-your-fault]].

3. **Offers the explicit choice.** "If I could give you a choice at the end of this call, you could realize how to forgive yourself for fucking up this relationship, or you could realize how to stop blaming yourself for everything. What would you choose?" Client: "Forgive myself." See [[the-forgive-this-or-stop-blaming-everything-choice]].

4. **Asks the payoff question.** "What makes it important to you to make sure that you keep blaming yourself for shit?" First answer is surface ("because I'm mad at myself for my actions"). Joe pushes a second time: "What do you get from that?" Client lands the structural answer: "I can keep that alive a little bit, because if I let that go, then I kind of let go the relationship." See [[what-do-you-lose-if-this-belief-goes-away]] and [[self-blame-as-tether-to-the-lost]].

5. **Names the trap.** "Cool, cool. Yeah, you see it? Do you see the trap that you just set for yourself? Like, I see the emotion starting to come, so maybe you see it." The naming converts the structure from invisible to visible. The body confirms with arriving emotion.

6. **Surfaces the rumination-produces-the-behavior mechanism.** Joe asks how much of the original betrayal was caused by the client's rumination. Client: "A lot of it." Joe names the trap explicitly: "The thing you're ruminating, that kind of beating-yourself rumination, is the thing that's causing you to create the things that you ruminate about." See [[rumination-creates-what-it-ruminates-about]] and [[shame-drives-behavior]].

7. **Uses the child-shaming analogy.** "When you have a kid and you shame them for being naughty, it's the best way to make sure that they're going to be naughty in the future. So shame yourself for drinking and it's the best way to continue drinking." The general principle as a memorable form.

8. **Names the shame addiction.** "You're addicted to the shame more than anything else, I think, sir." Client recognizes: "That feels right." See [[addiction-to-shame]].

9. **Redirects to the body.** "Just stop, stop for a second. Get out of your head for a second. Go to your heart. Go to your gut."

10. **Asks for the antidote.** "What's the antidote to shame? What do you do when you've got like the shame hangover that you currently have?" Client lands it: "Support, love. Yeah, yeah, acceptance."

11. **Runs the somatic A/B.** "Let's do love here first. Let's do a visceral thing in your body. Feel the shame in your body. You hurt this woman, she left. And now, on a completely somatic level, I just want you to feel the exact opposite of that feeling." Client: "That's hard." Joe names the start: "Yeah, you started, man. I saw it. Your chest raised. You started to feel that love. I saw it." See [[feel-the-shame-then-feel-the-opposite]].

12. **Gives permission to cry.** "Yeah, so let's just call it love and just love yourself for a minute. Love her. Yeah, you're allowed to cry. That's part of love. So what you're going to want to do right now is shame yourself for crying in front of all these people."

13. **Uses the room in the compressed variant.** "Fuck that. Look at everybody. Look at me and, and love me. Love yourself. This is the antidote to your shame addiction. Yeah, see that I'm loving you. Look at the other faces, see that they're loving you right now, or in awe of you, or check them out. The shame is like, fucking, it's your matrix. It's not even true." See [[use-the-live-group-as-love-field]].

14. **Uses the client's profession as the metaphor.** "You said you're a doctor, did I hear that?" Client: "Physical therapist, Dr." Joe: "Physical therapist, right? Okay. So this is the physical therapy for the heart. You know, you're stretching that self-love muscle."

15. **Explains what surfacing means.** "When you do, what's going to happen is what just happened here. Like, so when you stretch a muscle, you get like some reaction, you get some tension, you get some shaking. That's all part of it. Same thing when you show up with love, everything that's unloved comes to the surface, like the sadness, and it's all going to move."

16. **Assigns the daily practice.** "Think of it just like physical therapy. It's like, um, 15 minutes a day, I'm coming in, loving myself, loving that shame. And whatever happens, happens." See [[15-minutes-of-self-love-physical-therapy-for-the-heart]].

17. **Catches the management framing.** Client says: "I'm struggling and managing all that." Joe interrupts: "The only reason you're managing, the only reason you're managing is for shame. If you're loving yourself, what do you have to manage?" See [[management-is-for-shame-love-needs-no-management]].

18. **Closes with the principle as a live question.** "Love yourself and see what there is to manage." Client: "Yeah, just can kind of be. Yeah. Yeah. Thank you."

The session does not end with advice about the breakup. The breakup itself is barely discussed. The work is on the self-blame structure, the shame addiction, the rumination cycle, and the somatic alternative. The grief about the relationship is left to dissolve downstream of the self-love practice.

## What the Session Illustrates

This session is a compressed example of several principles operating together:

- **[[shame-drives-behavior]]**: the self-blame is the lock that holds the behavior (the inauthenticity that ended the relationship) in place
- **[[rumination-creates-what-it-ruminates-about]]**: the post-event rumination produced the next-event behavior; the client confirms "a lot of it" when asked
- **[[addiction-to-shame]]** in its self-blame variant: the rumination is the activity; the shame is what makes it compelling
- **[[self-blame-as-tether-to-the-lost]]**: the self-blame is keeping the relationship alive; releasing the blame would mean accepting the loss as final
- **[[management-is-for-shame-love-needs-no-management]]**: the post-breakup management is being done for the shame; with love there is nothing to manage
- **[[the-shame-avoidance-loop]]**: the self-blame is the avoidance of the underlying grief; the avoidance produces more shame to avoid

## What Makes This Session Distinctive

The session is a parallel to [[do-i-stop-drinking-session]] and [[joes-pot-habit]] in structure: same shame-drives-behavior mechanism, applied to a different domain. Drinking → shame loop; pot habit → shame loop; self-blame after a breakup → shame loop. The structural moves are the same: refuse the surface frame, locate the shame, name the loop, find the somatic alternative, assign the practice.

What is distinctive about this session is that the "habit" is the self-blame itself — there is no external substance or behavior to dissolve. The pattern runs entirely as rumination and self-criticism. The body has wired shame into the act of doing the activity. The implication: the same mechanism that holds a drinking pattern in place can hold a chronic self-blame pattern in place, and the exit is the same — love the thing the shame is wired to, let the surfacing material move, build the muscle daily.

The session also makes explicit the move from intellectual concept (the antidote is love) to somatic experience (feel the exact opposite in the body, with the people in the room, with permission to cry). The client cannot leave with just the intellectual frame; the body has to have done it once in the session for the take-home practice to have a felt reference.

## What This Is Not

This is not an upright-apology session. The work is on the client's internal architecture, not on his relational repair with the ex. If there is repair to be done with her, that is a separate thread — see [[upright-apology]]. The self-blame work is what makes the upright apology possible (because the upright posture requires the shame to be loose). But the session focuses on the prior layer.

This is not a grief session in the conventional sense. The grief of the breakup will arrive once the self-blame stops blocking it — that is part of "what's going to happen" when the practice runs. But the session does not work the grief directly. The structure was the lock, and the structure had to be released before the grief could move.

This is not a session about whether the breakup was actually the client's fault. The question of literal responsibility is not engaged. The client's account ("largely my fault") is accepted at face value. The work is on what the self-blame is doing, not on whether it is accurate.

## Tone

Fast and clean. Joe moves through the structural moves in compressed form, takes the client at his word, and trusts the body to do the work once the structure is named. The matter-of-fact delivery prevents the work from becoming heavy. The humor ("you said you're a doctor, did I hear that?") keeps the session light without minimizing what is happening.

The use of the client's profession (physical therapist) as the metaphor for the take-home practice is one of the session's signature moves. The metaphor is not generic — it is the client's specific expertise applied to his own internal work. This kind of personalization makes the prescription stick in a way that a generic "do self-love practice 15 minutes a day" would not.
