# You Can Hire Someone to Speak a Language. You Can't Hire Someone to Have Your Will.

## The Story

Joe was in a stretch of comparing his daughters to his sister's kids. His sister's children knew three languages. His daughters knew one and had not really learned to read yet. He was talking to a friend and named the comparison: "Oh, you know, they don't speak these languages."

His friend said the line that changed his entire frame:

"You can hire someone to speak a language for you, but you can't hire someone to have your will." -- Joe Hudson's friend, How to Be a Parent

Joe immediately ran the test against the CEOs he knew. He went through the list mentally. The pattern held without exception. The thing that made each of them successful was not a skill set. It was their will — the way they are in the world.

"I just went through all the CEOs I know, and I was like, yep, absolutely. The thing that makes them successful is not a skill set. It's the way that they are in the world. And then they can develop the skill set, or you can hire the skill set." -- Joe Hudson, How to Be a Parent

The frame put him at peace about his daughters. The thing they were developing — will — was the variable that would actually matter. The languages and reading could come later if his daughters wanted them.

## What the Example Illustrates

**Will is not a skill.** Will is the engine that decides whether the skills get applied, deepened, or pursued at all. A person with skills but no will is brittle: they can perform what they know but cannot extend, adapt, or commit. A person with will but no specific skill can acquire whatever skill is needed because they actually want to.

**Skills can be outsourced. Will cannot.** This is the structural test. Anything that can be hired or delegated is in the category of skill. The thing that remains, that cannot be hired in someone else, is the will. The CEO can hire a translator, an engineer, an accountant, a designer. The CEO cannot hire someone to have their vision, to commit, to choose the next move from inside themselves.

**The developmental implication.** Since will is the variable that matters in adulthood, and since the developmental window for the will is the first eight years (see [[first-eight-years-are-for-the-will]]), the parenting priority during that window should be will-development, not skill acquisition. The standard parental anxiety about early academics, languages, music milestones, etc. is grading on the wrong variable.

**The anxiety-release.** The example is the lever that releases the parent who is comparing their kid unfavorably on intellectual milestones. The comparison is happening in a category that is not the one that determines outcomes. The frame change relieves the anxiety without requiring any change in the child.

## What This Lets a Parent Do

A parent who internalizes the example can:

- Stop comparing their child to other children on language counts, reading age, math levels
- Stop pushing for early academic acceleration
- Notice and value moments when the child is developing will (sustained imaginative play, embodied physical activity, expressing what they want, pushing against limits, choosing their own direction)
- Trust that intellectual content will be acquired later if the will is intact
- Relax into the developmental window the child is actually in instead of trying to make the child operate in a later window

## When To Use This Example

- A parent anxious about their young child being "behind" on academic milestones
- A parent comparing their child to a sibling, cousin, or friend's child on languages or reading
- A parent considering whether to push early academics
- A parent who has internalized that competitive early-childhood education is critical
- A coachee who is the adult version of an over-skilled-under-willed kid and is asking what was missing
- A parent navigating the kindergarten-vs-Waldorf-style choice

## What This Doesn't Say

The example does not say skills are unimportant or that intellectual development should be ignored. It says that skills are recoverable later and that will is not. The sequencing matters. Developing the will first does not preclude developing skills later; developing skills first at the cost of the will produces an adult who has the skills without the agency to use them well.

The example also does not say that any specific child cannot benefit from early language exposure, music, reading. Children absorb these naturally through immersion. The example is against the instrumental framing where early academic content is being installed against a developmental clock, not against the natural exposure that comes from being raised in a culturally rich environment.
