# The Electric Fence with the Dog

## The Example

To train a dog to stay in the yard, an electric fence is installed at the property line. The first few times the dog approaches the line, it gets a shock. The shock teaches the dog to stay away from the edge. Eventually the fence can be turned off entirely and the dog still will not approach the line.

The training has worked. The dog stays in the yard. The owner is satisfied.

What goes uncounted: on the other side of the line where the dog will not go, the world is open. There are smells, paths, fields, freedoms the dog will never encounter. The dog has been trained out of access to most of what exists.

Brett offers this metaphor in conversation. Joe extends it. The metaphor lands because it captures something about how avoidance patterns work in human beings.

"Each of our emotional movements is the system trying to find homeostasis. And to the extent that we avoid them, we're blocking it." -- Brett Kistler, Embracing Intensity

## How It Applies to Emotions

The shock is the early experience of an emotion being painful or punished. The dog is the body. The fence is the avoidance pattern that gets installed.

The avoidance pattern works. The person no longer goes near the emotion that hurt. The original pain is no longer encountered.

What goes uncounted: on the other side of the avoidance, most of life is happening. The wisdom of the emotion that was avoided. The information it carried. The capacities it would have unlocked. The joy that lives in the same channel. All of it sits behind a fence that is no longer electrified, defended only by the memory of a shock.

## The Difference Joe Names

The metaphor has a limit. With the electric fence, touching it actually does shock you. Even if you investigate, the fence is real. Emotions are different. The "shock" the person learned to avoid was probably not a feature of the emotion itself; it was a feature of the context (the parent's reaction, the social punishment, the overwhelm of feeling it as a child).

"What's different than with the emotions than electric fence is that the emotions actually become more and more comfortable, that they change." -- Joe Hudson, Embracing Intensity

Test the emotion now, as an adult, and the shock does not arrive. The fear that the feeling would be unbearable is the fence; the actual feeling, met directly, is not what was anticipated. It is often the opposite -- relieving, clarifying, informative.

See [[kinked-emotions]] for why the emotion expected was the kinked version (which is genuinely painful) rather than the actual emotion (which is not).

## Why the Fence Stays Up Without Voltage

Two reasons.

One: the body remembers the shock. The body does not know the fence has been turned off. The avoidance pattern continues to run because the system that learned the avoidance has not been given evidence to the contrary.

Two: testing the fence requires being willing to feel the shock. The dog cannot test the fence without risking the shock it learned to avoid. The person cannot test the avoided emotion without risking the feeling it learned to avoid. The willingness to risk the shock is what allows the discovery that the shock is no longer there. See [[embrace-intensity]].

## What This Implies for the Work

The work is not to find new ways to live within the yard. The work is to test the fence. To approach the line. To discover that the shock is no longer there.

The first approach is the hardest. The body is preparing for the shock. Every signal in the system is saying "do not go there." When the approach happens anyway and the shock does not arrive, the fence loses its power. Repeated approaches dissolve it entirely.

## When the Fence Itself Is Comforting

A complication. For some people, the fenced yard has become identity. "I am the kind of person who does not get angry." "I am the kind of person who does not need to feel that." The fence is no longer felt as restriction; it is felt as definition.

The release of this kind of fence is harder because the release also dissolves a piece of who the person thinks they are. The work then has to include grieving that piece. See [[feeling-the-unfelt-emotion]].

## Why Joe Liked the Metaphor

Joe responds warmly to Brett's introduction of this metaphor. It captures the structure of avoidance in a way that is immediate and visceral. The image of the dog circling near the line, knowing not to cross, is the image of most adults near their avoided emotions.

The metaphor also points at freedom directly. The other side of the fence is freedom. The work is what stands between the person and that freedom.

## The Shame Application (And the Dog That Runs Through Quickly)

The metaphor extends directly to socially-installed shame. Tribes install shame at the boundary the way owners install electric fences at the property line. The training functions identically: a few shocks at the boundary teach the child to stay inside, and the avoidance persists long after the original shocks are gone.

"You have this -- you have a road that has cars on it and dogs get hit by cars... So you put a fence around it... And then you train the dog by having it approach the fence and it gets the shock collar. And every time it gets a shock collar, it's like unexpected and it recognizes that something, something is wrong and it goes into this like, uh, there's like a nervous system shutdown and then it just doesn't do that thing anymore... And maybe it's not feeling shame about going there, but it's the same kind of nervous system response where we develop these habits of, oh yeah, I'm not gonna go there, I'm not gonna do that, that's gonna get me rejected." -- Brett Kistler, The Anatomy of Shame

The dark extension Joe adds: some dogs learn that if they run through the fence fast enough, the shock is brief and the freedom on the other side is worth it. Some people do the same thing with shame. They acknowledge the shame will come, accept the cost, and reach for the activity anyway. Over enough repetitions, the brief shock becomes part of the appeal. The dog has incorporated the shock into the route to freedom; the person has incorporated the shame into the appetite. See [[addiction-to-shame]].

"There's dogs that learn that if they just go through that fence really quickly, it's worth it. So they just like run really quickly and that like it shocks them, but it only shocks them for like a little bit of a time because they're out of the zone because it only shocks you within the zone. And that happens with people too as far as shame." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame

## When the Run-Through Becomes Catastrophic

Brett extends the metaphor again: sometimes the dog runs through the fence and gets hit by a car. The dog has correctly identified that the fence is not the threat, but missed that the fence was protecting from a real threat on the other side. This is the case where someone is releasing installed shame around something that has actual harm in it -- see [[natural-shame-vs-installed-shame]] for the distinction. The fence is conditioning; the road is real. Both are true, and the dog has to learn the difference.

Joe's answer: do not run through the fence. Turn the fence off instead. Investigate the shame thoroughly enough that it dissolves rather than getting bypassed. "I think running through the fence just starts wiring stuff together... I think it's just far better to turn off the fence, so to speak. Meaning that like really address the shame underneath it and really investigate it both emotionally and intellectually and watch it fall apart and then take your action." -- Joe Hudson, The Anatomy of Shame
