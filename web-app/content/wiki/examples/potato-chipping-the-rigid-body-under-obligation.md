# Potato Chipping (The Rigid Body Under Obligation Cannot Respond)

## The Example

Wingsuit fliers and skydivers have a term for the failure mode of a tense body in freefall: potato chipping. The body, instead of flying through the air with subtle control, flops around like a potato chip falling out of the sky. The form is unstable. The jumper cannot adjust. The same jump that would have been safe for a relaxed body becomes dangerous because the body cannot do what flying through air requires: relax.

Brett, a wingsuit and BASE jumping practitioner, uses this as the somatic analogue of obligation cutting off love. When you cut off love — for the partner, the work, the jump — the body has to constrict to hold the cut-off. The constriction is what produces the potato chipping. The same flight is now more dangerous, not because the air conditions changed, but because the body lost the relaxed responsiveness that flying requires.

"In air sports in particular, you're flying your body through the air. And if you are rigid, if you are tense, you're far more likely to be kind of flopping around in a form that we call potato chipping. Like you're dropping like a potato chip out of the sky. That's like the, you know, the worst case form of it... So, so to the extent that you have these tensions, whether they're conscious or subconscious, that does inhibit your capacity to feel and notice and be present in the moment and respond to what's happening." -- Brett Kistler, Love and Obligation

"And on, on a jump or wingsuit flight, that is extremely dangerous. And the same flight that might have been perfectly safe for you to make in a, in a relaxed state where you're, like you said, self-loving and also in like welcoming of all of the sensations in your body and not tensing against them, the same jump could become far more dangerous simply because there's obligation running in the back of your brainstem." -- Brett Kistler, Love and Obligation

## The Mechanism

The principle underneath: love is somatically expansive. The body in love is open, relaxed, receptive. The body cut off from love has to constrict the musculature to maintain the cut-off. Constricted musculature cannot respond to the environment in real time.

Joe formalized the mechanism:

"In general, cutting off your experience of love, like, love is like, if you — the feeling of love, which is, I would argue, is a natural state... very natural, like, expansive state. And when you cut yourself off from love, you have to constrict the musculature in the body. Like, it's less expansive, it's less open. And you also have to cut yourself off from other senses... if I am in that expansive state, then I can react to and adjust for and be responsive to things in my environment. That I can't be if I'm in a more contracted, more rigid state." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

This is why obligation does not just make tasks unpleasant. It makes them more dangerous and less effective. The body cannot perform a high-precision activity well from rigidity. Wingsuit flying makes this obvious because the consequences are immediate and physical. But the same principle applies to public speaking, sales calls, surgery, parenting under stress, leading a meeting, having a difficult conversation, asking for what you want.

## The Application Beyond Air Sports

Joe and Brett both noted that potato chipping is the perfect metaphor for non-physical situations:

"I love that potato chipping. It's like, it's, it's like, seems like such a great way to describe somebody who's like caught in their head too." -- Joe Hudson, Love and Obligation

"Or caught in a decision like, oh, should I buy the car or not buy the car? Should I do this? Like flip-flopping... You know, that's a, another expression of the same thing often." -- Brett Kistler, Love and Obligation

The flip-flopping decision-maker, the speaker who cannot follow the thread, the team member who cannot read the room — all are versions of potato chipping. The body and mind have lost the responsiveness that comes with the relaxed openness of love. The cut-off is producing the failure of performance.

## What This Adds to the Obligation-Love Teaching

The teaching that [[obligation-cuts-off-love]] is sometimes met with the objection: "Fine, but I can still do the action even if I do not feel the love. The action is what matters." This example refutes that.

The action is degraded by the cut-off. Not in a small way. In a significant, sometimes life-threatening way. The wingsuit example makes this concrete. A jumper who cuts off love to take the jump as an obligation has lowered the safety margin of the jump itself. The jump that was always survivable becomes one of the jumps where things go wrong.

Outside of air sports, the consequence is less immediate but the mechanism is the same. The CEO running on obligation makes worse decisions. The parent running on obligation responds worse to the child. The teacher running on obligation cannot meet the moment. The performance suffers because the body lost its responsiveness when it lost its love.

## The Specific Obligations in BASE Jumping

The example becomes more useful when the specific obligations are named. Brett identifies several common BASE jumping obligations:

- Obligation to jumping partners and the day's plans (you do not want to walk down and make them wait at the car)
- Obligation to a self-expectation (the version of yourself that should be jumping)
- Obligation to a parental or mentor figure from childhood (you are still trying to be the person they wanted you to be)
- Obligation to an imagined audience (society, Bond movies, social media)

Any of these can be operating when a jumper stands at the exit point. Each of them, if obeyed, will constrict the body and produce some version of potato chipping. The jumper who notices the obligation can either honor it consciously (and accept the elevated risk) or release it before the jump and recover the body's responsiveness.

## When Joe Reaches for This Example

Use this example when:

- A client is performing high-stakes actions under obligation and not seeing why their performance is suffering
- A client wants to argue that the felt state does not matter as long as the action is taken
- A client is in flip-flop decision mode and needs a somatic frame for what is happening
- A client is making BASE-jumping-level commitments (career change, relationship choice, large financial decision) and could be served by noticing what kind of body is making the decision
- The teaching that love is expansive needs a physical demonstration

The example is also a useful read for athletes, performers, and anyone whose work depends on physical responsiveness. It speaks their language.

## The Counter-Point: Loving the Sport Without Obligation

Brett gave the alternative: he stayed in love with the sport for years by treating opportunities as choices and agreements rather than obligations.

"Just because somebody starts writing checks doesn't mean that they have any — that they have control or management over me... And, and if I make agreements, it doesn't mean that I'm obligated in a, in the sense like you describe. Like it's a, like it's not a new reality for me. It's an agreement. And I'm choosing to be there." -- Brett Kistler, Love and Obligation

The flying stayed safe because the body stayed loved. The work continued. The sport stayed alive. See [[i-get-to-vs-i-have-to]].
